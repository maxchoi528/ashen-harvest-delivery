"Move it, corpse-carrier. The Dawn's patrol passes through in twenty minutes."
Kael pressed his back against the damp brick, the Mortician's Guild cart rattling past. Three bodies lay under tarpaulins, their spiritual signatures already fading from the world's memory. His fingers twitched toward them.
Not yet. Not here.
The voice belonged to a heavyset handler with a brass badge on his coat—the Mortician's Guild, legitimate collectors of the dead. They didn't know what Kael truly was. They never did, until it was far too late.
He counted the seconds as the cart disappeared around the corner into Ashworth Street. Twenty minutes. The Order of Dawn ran tight schedules through Fogmire District since the murders began, and Kael had learned their patterns the hard way. Three close calls in two days. His ribs still ached from the last near-miss, when a Dawn enforcer's spiritual senses brushed past his hiding spot in a collapsed tenement.
The night air stank of coal smoke and rotting cabbage. Somewhere nearby, a child coughed—thin, wet, the kind of cough that preceded death in the lower wards. Kael filed the sound away and refocused on his surroundings.
Fog curled through the alley like something alive. Gas lamps sputtered on the corners, their flames fighting against the damp. Two stories up, a windowframe hung empty, glass long since shattered. Perfect vantage point, or perfect trap.
Footsteps echoed from the main thoroughfare. Not the heavy plod of common folk, but the measured cadence of someone trained to move in formation. Kael counted: three sets, maybe four.
He slipped into shadow.
---
The Dawn patrol passed within fifteen feet of his position. Kael watched through a gap in the rotted boards, barely breathing.
Two enforcers in white cloaks walked abreast, their sanctified blades humming faintly at their hips. Behind them came a shorter figure—someone carrying a detection focus, a brass instrument that looked like a compass fused with a tuning fork. That device would sense spiritual residue.
Kael held his own power dormant, deep in his core where even the keenest Awakened would struggle to find it. The Soul Reaver ability sat coiled like a serpent, requiring no energy to maintain, only to use.
"—sigils appeared on the foundry wall again," one enforcer was saying. "Third time this week."
"Idiot cultists." The second spat. "Thin...
blood to the Veil." The shorter figure adjusted the detection focus. "Commander Voss wants the whole district swept by dawn. Priority targets: anyone operating without guild papers, anyone with recent Awakening markers, anyone—" He paused, tilting his head. "Anyone unusual."
Kael's jaw tightened. Unusual. That word would encompass half of Fogmire's population if the Dawn decided to interpret it loosely.
The patrol moved on, their footsteps fading into the fog. Kael waited a full three minutes before allowing himself to exhale.
Not a breakthrough chapter. The thought arrived unbidden, and he shoved it away. He wasn't Ascended. He wasn't close. The Soul Reaver had pushed him past certain thresholds, yes, but the fundamental stage remained Awakened. Somewhere in the distant future, power waited—but that power wasn't his to claim yet.
First, survival. Then, answers.
He emerged from the alley onto Widdens Lane, keeping his hood low. The street market had closed hours ago, leaving behind scattered vegetables and a dog gnawing at something unidentifiable in the gutter. Kael navigated around a drunk sleeping against a barrel, his boots splashing through puddles that reflected the sickly gaslight.
Lyra would be waiting at the safehouse. She would have information about the cult's movements, or she would have another argument ready about why they needed to leave Thornwick entirely. Probably both.
The thought of her brought a complicated sensation—not guilt, exactly, but something adjacent to it. Lyra had made her choice clear: she followed Kael because he offered purpose, because the alternative was confronting her past with the Shadow Court alone. That didn't make their alliance stable. It made it transactional.
Kael understood transactions. He preferred them to friendship.
A sound cut through the night—glass breaking, somewhere to his left. Kael froze, hand drifting toward the knife at his belt. Not a weapon that would stop an Awakened, but enough to discourage common thugs.
The second sound was worse: screaming. High, terrified, the kind that meant violence than pain.
He changed direction.
---
The alley behind the Brass Kettle tavern had become a killing ground.
Three bodies lay crumpled against the wall—one man, two women, their clothes torn and bloodied. A fourth figure stood over them, tall and skeletal, moving with the wrong kind of fluidity. Kael's eyes caught the spiritual energy radiating from it: faint but wrong, like sunlight filtered through poison.
Abyssal corruption. Not a full Entity manifestation, but something touched by the Abyss. A thrall, maybe. Or something worse.
The creature turned toward him, and Kael saw its face—or what remained of it. Flesh hung in strips, eye sockets empty, mouth stretched impossibly wide. It smelled of copper and decay and something else, something that made his Soul Reaver instinct scream.
*Fresh. The bodies are fresh.*
"Stay back." The words came from the alley's entrance—someone else had responded to the screaming. A young man in the stained apron of a tavern worker, holding a fire poker like a spear. "It's killed them, it's—"
The creature moved.
Kael didn't think. He stepped forward, placing himself between the thrall and the terrified worker. His hand found the knife, drew it, threw it in one motion.
The blade struck the creature's shoulder and burst into flames. Not from any spiritual technique— the grease coating the knife from Kael's earlier dinner. But the fire caught on the Abyssal corruption, spreading in hungry tongues across the thrall's chest.
The creature shrieked—a sound like metal tearing—and lunged.
Kael sidestepped, grabbing a loose cobblestone from the ground. The thrall's claws raked through the space where he'd stood, tearing through the tavern door like paper. Kael brought the stone down on its skull, the impact jarring through his arm.
The creature staggered. Kael grabbed its throat, feeling the wrongness of its flesh under his fingers—cold, rubbery, thrumming with stolen spiritual energy.
*Harvest.*
The thought came with a whisper of power, and Kael let it rise.
[Soul Harvest: Initiated. Target: Abyssal-touched Thrall. Age of remains: less than a few hours. Harvesting...]
The thrall convulsed. Kael's vision split—one eye seeing the alley, the other seeing something else entirely. Fragments of memory flooded through him: a dark chamber, chanting figures in black robes, the cold kiss of a ritual blade. Then pain, overwhelming and final, as something ancient and hungry tore through the victim's soul from the inside.
*Not a random attack. Directed. Someone sent this thing to kill.*
The thrall went limp. Kael released it, stumbling back as the harvest completed.
[Soul Harvest Complete: You extracted 12 spiritual essence units from the remains. Acquired: Fragment of Abyssal Resistance (lesser). Quality: degraded—thrall corruption reduced harvest efficiency by 40%.]
Lesser resistance to Abyssal influence. Not useless, but far from what a clean harvest would have yielded. The corruption had tainted everything.
"Are you—" The tavern worker stood frozen, eyes fixed on Kael's hand, which still smoked faintly from the contact. "What are you?"
Kael looked at the bodies on the ground. The man had been a dockworker, judging by his calloused hands. One of the women wore a wedding ring. The other carried guild papers—a seamstress, maybe twenty years old.
Kael thought about the child's cough he'd heard earlier. About the drunk sleeping in the gutter. About all the people in Fogmire who would never know that three of their neighbors had been murdered by something that shouldn't exist.
"Someone who can help." He crouched beside the bodies, pressing his palm to the dead man's chest. Another harvest. Another flood of fragments: a wife waiting at home, twin sons who liked to fish, a letter from his brother in the Ironworks District. The man's last thought had been confusion—what is that thing—and then nothing.
[Soul Harvest Complete: You extracted 7 spiritual essence units from the remains. Acquired: Memory Fragment (Occupational Skill - Dockwork). Quality: moderate.]
The woman's wedding ring caught the light. Kael ignored it, moving to the next body. A seamstress, younger than he'd thought. She had been running when the thrall caught her—he could see it in the way her arms were positioned, the momentum of her fall.
[Soul Harvest Complete: You extracted 4 spiritual essence units from the remains. Acquired: Memory Fragment (Skill - Basic Sewing). Quality: poor—death trauma fragmented recollections.]
The last victim was the worst. She had fought back. Her fingernails had torn at the creature's face, leaving furrows in the corrupted flesh. Her final moments had been pure, desperate courage.
[Soul Harvest Complete: You extracted 9 spiritual essence units from the remains. Acquired: Memory Fragment (Skill - Self-Defense Training). Quality: moderate.]
Kael stood. His hands were clean—the Soul Reaver left no residue, no evidence of its use. But the emptiness remained, as it always did. Four people had lived, had loved, had feared for their lives in their final moments. Now they were information and spiritual energy, nothing more.
"You killed it." The worker hadn't moved. "You touched it and it just... Stopped."
"Yes."
"The Dawn—they'll want to know. They'll need to examine—"
"No." Kael's voice came out flat. "They won't."
The thrall's body was already dissolving, Abyssal corruption consuming it from within. In moments, nothing would remain but a scorch mark on the cobblestones. Standard procedure for corruption casualties: the Orders preferred to clean up evidence of Abyssal activity before public panic could spread.
Kael understood the reasoning. He didn't agree with it.
"The Dawn's patrol will pass through here within the hour," he said. "You'll tell them there was an accident. A gas lamp exploded, started a fire. The bodies were already dead when it happened."
"But—"
"They'll believe you because the alternative is explaining how a thrall walked through Fogmire District without anyone noticing until people started dying." Kael met the worker's eyes. "The Orders don't like admitting failure. They'll take the simple story."
The worker swallowed hard. "You're not... You're not one of them, are you? The Dawn?"
"No."
"The Shadow Court?"
"No."
"Then what?"
Kael walked past him, pausing at the alley's entrance. "Someone who collects the dead. Make sure you remember that."

The safehouse was a condemned textile warehouse on the district's eastern edge. Kael climbed the external ladder to the third floor, where a window had been replaced with oiled canvas. Inside, a single lantern burned low.
Lyra sat at a makeshift table, surrounded by papers and a spread of mechanical components she was cleaning with practiced efficiency. She looked up when he entered, her silver-gray hair catching the light, her expression unreadable.
"You're late."
"The Dawn patrol runs late on Tuesdays. Extended sweep of the foundry quarter."
"I know." She set down a gear assembly. "I also know about the disturbance near the Brass Kettle. Three dead, one witness, unusual spiritual signature detected by the district monitoring focus. The Dawn's response team arrived six minutes after the signature vanished."
Kael pulled off his coat, examining the tear where the thrall's claws had grazed the fabric. "You monitored the district?"
"I monitor everything." Lyra's eyes narrowed. "What happened?"
Kael told her. The screaming, the thrall, the harvest. He kept his descriptions clinical—tactical information, not emotional processing. Lyra listened without interrupting, her fingers drumming once against the table when he mentioned the corrupted flesh.
"Directed attack," she said when he finished. "Not random."
"The harvest confirmed it. Someone summoned that thing specifically to kill those people."
"That's not how Abyssal thralls work." Lyra stood, pacing toward the window. "They require ritual circles, blood sacrifice, weeks of preparation. You don't waste that kind of investment on random murders in a lower ward."
"Then why here? Why now?"
Lyra turned. In the dim light, her face held shadows that made her look older, harder. "The cult has been growing bolder. Three weeks ago, they marked the foundry wall with sigils. Two weeks ago, a family's children vanished from their home—found two days later wandering near the sealed canal locks, no memory of what happened. One week ago, the Dawn's patrol discovered a ritual site in the abandoned sewer network. Empty, but recently used."
"Something's changed."
"Something's accelerating." Lyra returned to the table, spreading one of her papers—a map of Thornwick's district boundaries, marked with colored pins. "The Six Orders have maintained the seal network for three centuries. Seventy-two rifts, seventy-two Entities, all contained. But the seals degrade. They require constant maintenance, constant sacrifice of spiritual energy. The Orders have been fighting a slow war against entropy since the beginning."
"And the cult wants to win that war faster?"
"The cult wants to end it differently." Lyra tapped a pin near Fogmire District. "Zarek doesn't want to maintain the seals. He wants to break them. All of them, simultaneously. He believes the resulting cataclysm will transform humanity, elevate those with sufficient spiritual potential into something beyond Awakened, beyond Ascendant, beyond anything the current system allows."
"That's insane."
"It's also exactly what the Entities want." Lyra's voice dropped. "The seal network doesn't just contain the Abyss. It contains the Entities themselves—beings of pure spiritual energy, infinite power, alien desires. Zarek thinks he can control the transformation. He's wrong. When those rifts open, whatever emerges won't be human anymore. It won't be anything recognizable."
Kael stared at the map. Seventy-two pins. Seventy-two sealed rifts. The weight of that many potential catastrophes pressed against his thoughts like a physical force.
"The Dawn is searching for cult activity," he said slowly. "They're not finding it because they're looking in the wrong places. But someone is coordinating these attacks. Someone is moving the pieces."
"The cult has cells throughout Thornwick. Different neighborhoods, different social classes. They recruit from the desperate—people who have nothing to lose, people who think the current system has failed them. The Dawn's patrols catch the bottom level, the desperate converts, but they never get close to the leadership."
"Then we need to do what the Dawn can't."
Lyra's expression flickered. "What exactly are you suggesting?"
"The Soul Reaver harvests more than abilities. It extracts memories, fragments of knowledge, pieces of understanding. If I could get close enough to a cult member who's involved in the planning..."
"You'd need a target. Someone with access to the cult's inner structure."
"The thrall attack." Kael's mind raced. "The cult summoned that thing to kill those specific people. Which means they knew where those people would be, when they would be there. That's not general reconnaissance. That's specific intelligence. Someone gave them that information."
Lyra was silent for a long moment. Then she reached into her coat and withdrew a folded paper, sliding it across the table.
"I intercepted this from a Dawn courier yesterday. Encrypted, but their encryption is elementary for someone who knows the cipher."
Kael unfolded the paper. Inside was a list of names—sixteen, to start—and locations in Fogmire District. Beside each name was a small symbol: a circle with a line through it.
"These are the people the Dawn is investigating for cult connections."
"Keep reading."
The names continued onto the second page. And the third. And the fourth.
"This isn't a list of suspects." Kael looked up. "This is a membership roster."
"A list of people the Dawn believes are actively involved with Zarek's organization. Commander Voss has been building this file for eight months." Lyra's eyes glittered in the lamplight. "He shared it with his patrol captains, encrypted, because he doesn't trust the other Orders. Someone leaked it. Or someone intercepted it. Either way, we now have access to the cult's probable membership."
"You've been planning this."
"I've been preparing options." Lyra leaned forward. "The cult operates through trust networks—small cells that don't know about each other, only their direct superiors. Breaking one cell exposes nothing. But if we could harvest from someone high enough in the structure..."
"You'd learn the names of everyone above them. And everyone above those people. One harvest could unravel the entire organization."
"If you could reach them. If you could survive the contact. If you could avoid alerting the rest of the cult in the flow."
Kael examined the list again. The names meant nothing to him—they were common folk, merchants and laborers and minor guild members. The kind of people the Dawn would overlook because they didn't fit the profile of fanatics.
But that was the point. The cult hid in plain sight, using people no one suspected.
"This name." He pointed to one near the bottom. "Marcus Thorne. Dockworker. Last known address in the Ashworth Street tenements."
"The same docks where you encountered the thrall."
Kael's blood chilled. The dead man from the harvest. He'd been a dockworker, strong hands, a letter from his brother. Had he known Marcus Thorne? Had he been targeted because of that connection?
Or had Marcus Thorne been the real target, and the others been in the wrong place?
"Thorne is lower-level," Lyra said. "Probably a courier or lookout. But he might know someone higher. And unlike the cult leadership, he probably lives a normal life. Goes to the docks every day. Visits the same tavern after work."
"You want me to stalk a dockworker and harvest his memories."
"I want you to be careful." Lyra's voice hardened. "The Soul Reaver is powerful, but it's not invincible. The cult has killed before. The Dawn has killed before. And if Zarek learns that a Reaver is operating in Thornwick..."
"He'll come for me personally."
"He'll do worse than that. He'll use you."
The words hung in the air between them. Kael thought about the thrall's memories—the dark chamber, the chanting figures, the cold kiss of ritual steel. He thought about what Zarek had done to frame him, to ensure his execution, to erase any evidence of his crimes.
Zarek had resources. Zarek had followers. Zarek had three centuries of accumulated power and influence within the Orders themselves.
And Kael had a dead man's skills and a woman who might betray him if the price was right.
That was the math. That was always the math.
"Where is Thorne now?"
Lyra checked a small notebook, flipping through pages of cramped handwriting. "His shift ends at the docks at sunset. He takes his wages to the Golden Goose tavern on Millbrook Lane, drinks until he can't stand, then walks home. Alone."
"A routine."
"Perfect for surveillance. Or interception."
Kael folded the Dawn's leaked roster and tucked it inside his coat. "When does his shift end?"
"Tomorrow. Sunset."
He had hours to prepare. Hours to consider what he might find, what he might lose, what he might become.
"One more thing." Lyra caught his arm as he turned toward the back room. "The monitoring focus detected something unusual during the thrall attack."
"What?"
"Not the thrall. Something else. A second spiritual signature, present at the scene but not connected to the creature." She released him. "Someone else was watching. Someone who left before the Dawn arrived."
Kael thought of the fog in the alley. The shadows that had seemed deeper than they should have been.
"Could you identify them?"
"The signature was... Strange. Faint but organized, like a pattern cut into stone. I've never seen anything like it."
"Could it have been one of the cult?"
Lyra hesitated. "The signature didn't match any known cult methodology. It didn't match any known Order methodology either. It felt..." She paused, searching for words. "Old. Like something from before the current system was established."
Before the current system. Before the Six Orders, before the seal network, before the Abyssal Wars had ended and humanity had declared victory.
Something ancient. Something watching.
Kael filed the information away and continued toward the back room. He had preparations to make. A target to study. A harvest to plan.
And somewhere in the fog of Thornwick, something else was watching him.
