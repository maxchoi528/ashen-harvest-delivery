Blood dripped from Kael's fingers onto the cobblestones. Three bodies lay sprawled across the alley mouth—cultists, judging by the black sigils branded into their forearms.
"Another group," he muttered.
The Mortician's Guild would have cleaned this mess already in the richer districts. Here in the Undercroft, rats got to the dead before anyone asked questions.
Kael crouched beside the nearest corpse. Male, mid-forties, callused palms suggesting manual labor. The brand on his arm was fresh—still weeping serum where the iron had bitten deep. A summoning sigil, crude but functional. The kind a desperate man might carve into his own flesh after reading forbidden pamphlets.
The Abyss had been whispering louder since the Great Rift's seal started cracking. Kael heard the whispers too, but from a different source.
He pressed his palm flat against the dead man's chest.
The Soul Reaver sense unfolded like a flower blooming in reverse. Not sight, not sound—a pressure behind his eyes, a knowledge that bypassed his senses entirely. The corpse still held warmth. Less than an hour since death.
*Harvest available. Quality: High. Extract?*
"Yes."
His fingers tingled. The man's life-force flowed into Kael's reserves—not his soul, nothing so intimate, but the accumulated spiritual energy that every awakened being carried. Background radiation of power, the fuel that ran out when meditation couldn't keep pace.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 847 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Base vitality +a tiny fraction acquired.]
The notification flickered across his vision, invisible to anyone else. Kael had stopped questioning the system's existence months ago. It was, like breathing or bleeding.
He moved to the second corpse. Female, younger. Her fingers still gripped a length of iron pipe, knuckles scraped raw from impact. She'd fought back. Kael wondered what she'd seen in her final moments—the thing crawling up from the summoning circle, maybe, or the cultists who'd recruited her with promises of power.
Her death had been recent. Even better.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 1,204 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Combat reflex optimization acquired.]
*Combat reflex optimization.* Kael filed that away. Useful, if generic. The woman's body remembered fighting before her mind had caught up—muscle memory that persisted after death, now transferred to him.
The third cultist had been older. His brand was old, the skin around it scarred and puckered from repeated application. A true believer, not a recent recruit. Kael found less energy in the body, the reserves depleted by whatever rituals this man had performed.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 412 units of spiritual essence from the remains. No abilities acquired.]
Kael straightened, wiping his hand on his coat. The alley remained silent. No witnesses, no interruptions. Just the three bodies and the man who'd harvested them.
"Satisfied?"
The voice came from above. Kael's head snapped up as a figure dropped from the rooftop—landing in a crouch three meters away, rising to reveal a hooded cloak of deep charcoal. The fabric shifted with each movement, suggesting layers of armor beneath.
Not a cultist. The posture was too controlled, the landing too precise.
"The Shadow Court sends their regards," the figure said. A woman's voice, roughened by scar tissue or deliberate modification.
Kael's hand drifted toward the knife at his belt. "And what do they want?"
"Wanted. Past tense." She pulled back her hood, revealing a face marked by old burns, one eye milky white. "You're a difficult man to arrange a meeting with, Soul Reaver. We've been watching you for weeks."
Watching. Kael filed that away with all the other threats he'd accumulated since his execution and resurrection. The Iron Vanguard wanted him dead. The Arcane Spire wanted to dissect him. Several noble houses wanted to hire him, which meant they wanted to control him. And now the Shadow Court wanted something.
"I'm not in the habit of accepting meetings from people who lurk on rooftops."
"The habit of accepting meetings from anyone who asks nicely died when they put you in the ground." She gestured at his chest. "The execution scars haven't even finished healing. I can smell the sulfur and silver from here."
Kael said nothing. The woman wasn't wrong—his chest still ached where the execution blade had passed. A blade meant to end a Soul Reaver permanently. It had failed, as had every other attempt to kill him since his Awakening.
"Thornwick is changing," the woman continued. "The cult activity you've been... Cleaning up. It's not random. Someone is organizing them."
"I noticed."
"The Council of Six meets in eleven days. Every major Order will send representatives. The cults know this. They're planning something."
"And the Shadow Court wants to warn me? How charitable."
"We want to warn everyone. The cults threaten the seal network. If the Great Rift opens fully, there won't be enough Awakened in the world to hold the line." She paused. "Including those of us who operate in the shadows."
Kael studied her. The burns on her face looked self-inflicted—the pattern suggested someone who'd tried to remove tracking marks or identification scars. A former prisoner, maybe, or a defector from some less forgiving organization.
"You've got twenty seconds," Kael said. "Convince me this isn't a trap."
"Three days ago, a convoy left the old Duskmantle Warehouse. Destination: the Ashfall Caves, forty leagues north. Inside that convoy: forty-seven people marked for sacrifice. The cultists call it a 'tithe.' They're planning to offer the energy directly to the Entity behind the Great Rift's seal."
"Forty-seven."
"Thirty-nine confirmed dead. The rest are being held in cages until the ritual date. Three days from now."
Kael's jaw tightened. Children? The thought surfaced before he could stop it. His code barred killing children. It didn't bar saving them, if they existed.
"Children?"
"One family tried to hide. The cultists killed the parents in front of the youngest. He's eight years old now, alone in a cage, surrounded by people who will be dead before sunrise."
"Where exactly in the caves?"
The woman's scarred face twisted into something that might have been approval. "The convoy takes the Northern Road through Thornhallow. They rest at the Wayward Inn on the second night. That's where they're most vulnerable—camped out, guards on rotation."
"You're suggesting I ambush a convoy transporting prisoners."
"I'm suggesting you do what you do best." She reached into her cloak and produced a leather tube, tossing it across the gap between them. Kael caught it reflexively, the leather warm from her body heat. "Maps. Guard rotations. Cage specifications. Everything we have on their security."
"Why?"
"Because you're the only one who can get inside without raising the alarm. The cultists use blood-wards against the Orders—anyone with strong spiritual presence triggers the defenses. But you..." She gestured at the bodies on the ground. "You're invisible to their detection. They won't even know you're there until you've already cut their throats."
Kael opened the tube and unrolled the parchment inside. The map was detailed—topography, guard posts, entry points marked in red ink. Someone had done significant recon.
"If this is a trap—"
"Then you'll die surrounded by cultists who want to feed you to an Abyssal Entity. Hardly a pleasant outcome, but contained." She pulled her hood back up. "The Shadow Court doesn't make friends, Soul Reaver. We make calculations. Right now, you're the best tool available for preventing a catastrophe. Don't mistake pragmatism for altruism."
She turned toward the alley's far end.
"Wait." Kael's voice stopped her mid-step. "The name. I need a name."
"Call me Thorn." She glanced back, her good eye gleaming. "Though I suspect you won't need to. If the convoy reaches Ashfall, there won't be anyone left to report what happened."
Thorn disappeared into the shadows between buildings, moving with the fluid ease of someone who'd spent years practicing how not to be seen.
Kael stood alone with the map, the bodies, and three days to decide whether he trusted a Shadow Court operative who'd just handed him everything he needed to commit mass murder.
The smart play was to wait. Let the Orders handle it. He'd been framed once already for playing hero—Zarek's work, that trap had been—and the memory of iron collars biting into his wrists still haunted his dreams.
But forty-seven people. One child.
Kael rolled up the map and slid it into his coat.
* * *
The Wayward Inn materialized from the fog two hours before dawn—a squat building of weathered stone, its sign creaking in the wind. Lanterns burned in two windows, casting orange light across a muddy yard where horses stood dozing in the cold.
Kael dismounted three hundred meters out, tying his reins to a fencepost. He'd abandoned his horse two miles back, switching to the stolen mount that now carried his supplies. Identifying him through animal tracking was crude but effective. If the Shadow Court's intelligence was good, the cultists might be watching for a second rider.
He crossed the field in a crouch, staying below the hedgerow line. The fog helped—Thornwick's infamous river-mist had rolled inland overnight, turning the world into shades of gray and black. His Demigod senses stretched outward, feeling for spiritual signatures.
There. The compound behind the inn radiated faint corruption—the telltale signature of blood rituals performed repeatedly in the same location. Low-level, almost background, but present. The kind of thing an untrained Awakened might dismiss as nothing.
Kael wouldn't dismiss it.
The convoy had arrived. He could see six covered wagons in the yard, horses still harnessed and stamping at their posts. Armed figures moved between them—eight, ten, twelve. Some carried swords. Others bore the telltale bulge of firearms. One man walked with a staff topped by a skull—a Bone Singer, probably, their spiritual abilities useless against Kael but dangerous to anyone else.
The prisoners would be in the back, then. Wagons meant cages that couldn't be quickly moved. A tactical decision, or arrogance?
Kael circled the compound, mapping guard positions. The Shadow Court's intel proved accurate—three shifts, four guards each, rotating every four hours. The gaps between shifts were minimal but present. Two minutes of transition where attention fragmented.
Two minutes to kill twelve men and free forty-seven prisoners.
The math wasn't impossible. Difficult, but not impossible.
He found the prisoner wagons after another circuit. Three vehicles positioned at the compound's rear, away from the main buildings. Cages built into the wagon beds, iron bars welded to wooden frames. He could see shapes inside them—huddled masses pressed against the bars, faces turned toward the ground.
One face lifted as Kael watched. Young, pale, eyes too old for the body they occupied.
The boy from the intel. Eight years old, who'd watched his parents die.
Kael's chest tightened. He'd told himself this was a calculation, same as Thorn had said. Prevent a catastrophe, save the prisoners, harvest what strength he needed from the corpses. Clean. Professional. The kind of operation a Soul Reaver could justify without losing sleep.
The boy's stare said different things.
* * *
Midnight. The fog had thickened, turning the yard into a maze of shadows and half-seen shapes. Kael waited in the drainage ditch alongside the compound's eastern wall, counting heartbeats.
The Bone Singer had retired to the inn an hour ago. Two guards remained at the main gate, their conversation audible as low murmurs. The other two shifts were transitioning—watching for exactly this window.
Four guards patrolling. Four at the prisoner wagons. Four resting in the barn.
The Bone Singer would be the problem. Bone Singers could raise the dead—a handful of corpses animated with enough spiritual energy to overwhelm most Awakened. Kael could harvest them afterward, but not before they'd had a chance to kill someone.
He needed the Singer dead first. Or contained.
Kael moved.
The inn's kitchen door hung slightly ajar, admitting a blade of lamplight and the smell of old grease. He slipped inside, his footsteps silent on the packed-earth floor. A single cook worked at the far end, stirring something in a massive pot. The man's back was turned.
The stairs led up. Kael climbed.
The Bone Singer's room was on the third floor, door closed, a thin line of light visible beneath. Kael pressed his ear to the wood. No voices, no movement. The man might be sleeping.
Sleeping made things easier.
He tested the handle. Unlocked. The door swung inward without sound.
The room was small, dominated by a bed and a heavy wooden desk. The Bone Singer sat at the desk, head bent over a collection of yellowed bones spread across its surface. He looked up as Kael entered, and his face went pale.
"Soul Reaver," the man whispered. "They said you were a myth."
"They say a lot of things." Kael crossed the room in three strides. The Bone Singer tried to rise, tried to reach for the bones on his desk, but Kael's hand closed around his throat and slammed him back into his chair. "Stay quiet. Stay still. You'll die either way, but you might die peacefully."
The Singer's eyes bulged. He didn't fight—couldn't, probably, against a Demigod's grip. But his hand found Kael's wrist, not to push away, but to point at the bones on the desk.
"Trying to warn you," he rasped. "The bones—they told me you were coming. The dead talk to me. They told me about you."
"The dead don't talk. They just remember."
"Same thing." Blood vessels burst in the Singer's eyes, turning the whites red. "The first one—the first Soul Reaver—he never died. He opened the gate. He let them in. The seal network isn't holding because it was never complete."
Kael's grip tightened. "What do you mean, he never died?"
"The original. The one who created the Soul Reaver technique. He found a way to consume Entities. Became one himself. Still walks this world, wearing different faces. Looking for..." The Singer's voice faded to a gurgle.
"For what?"
"His blade. His weapon. The thing that made him." The Singer's body convulsed. "He'll find you. He'll take it back. Everything you are—everything you've harvested—it was never yours to keep."
Kael released the corpse. The Bone Singer slumped forward onto his desk, scattering bones across the floor. Dead, but not harvested. Kael needed to focus on the mission first.
Later. The man's secrets would keep.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 1,876 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Bone Whisper ability acquired. Warning: Bone Whisper may attract attention from Entities.]
Kael ignored the notification. The warning meant nothing—every ability came with costs. The question was whether the benefits outweighed them.
He retrieved the scattered bones and arranged them on the desk in a pattern the Singer had been studying. A map of the cave system, Kael realized. The convoy's destination. The cultists weren't just sacrificing the prisoners—they were opening a path.
The Great Rift's seal was failing. This ritual might be the final blow.
Time to leave.
* * *
The Bone Singer's death had gone unnoticed. Kael moved through the inn's upper floor like smoke, dropping from a window into the compound's shadows. The guard rotation had completed—fresh faces now patrolled the yard, still waking up.
He counted seventeen armed cultists now. More than the intel suggested. A reinforcements convoy had arrived while he was inside.
Kael's knife slid free of its sheath.
The first guard died without a sound. Kael caught the body, lowering it silently into the mud. A second guard walked past thirty seconds later, checking a horse's harness, unaware of the shadow that detached itself from the wagon wheel.
By the time the third guard fell, someone had noticed the absence. A shout echoed across the yard—sharp, alarmed, the universal sound of a plan falling apart.
Kael moved faster.
The cultists scrambled for weapons, shouting warnings to each other. One man drew a pistol, finger on the trigger, but Kael was already behind him. The blade entered below the ribs, angled upward, and the pistol fell unfired.
The Bone Singer's absence wouldn't stay hidden forever. Once they searched the inn, they'd find the body. And then they'd know a professional had arrived.
Two minutes, Kael estimated. Maybe less.
He reached the prisoner wagons as the fourth alarm sounded. The cages were secured with iron locks—mundane, not spiritual. His knife picked the first lock in seconds. The second took longer, the mechanism rusted and stubborn.
"Come on," he muttered.
The cage door swung open. The prisoners inside stared at him with hollow eyes—men and women in various states of terror, some weeping, others catatonic. They didn't move.
"Out. Now. Head for the trees."
They still didn't move. Too broken, Kael realized. Two days in cages, waiting to die. Fight or flight responses had calcified into paralysis.
He grabbed the nearest woman's arm, hauling her toward the gap he'd cut in the fence. She stumbled after him, and that movement broke the spell. Others began following—shuffling, stumbling, but moving.
The boy was in the third wagon. Kael ran for it, picking the lock in a fury of desperate speed. The cage door opened to reveal a small figure curled in the corner, knees drawn to chest, face hidden.
"Hey." Kael kept his voice low. "You need to come with me."
The boy looked up. Those too-old eyes met Kael's.
"Are you the one who kills the bad men?"
"I'm the one who gets you out."
The boy nodded slowly. He didn't reach for Kael's hand—he flinched away from contact, something in him too damaged for simple trust. But he stood, and he walked when Kael walked, and that was enough.
Twelve cultists remained between them and the tree line. Kael calculated distances, angles, the positions of the remaining guards. He'd killed five already. The numbers had shifted.
Impossible odds, his mind whispered. Just like the arena. Just like the execution.
Kael smiled, and it wasn't a pleasant expression.
"Soul Reaver!" The shout came from the compound's center—a massive figure in ceremonial armor, brandishing a serrated blade. "We felt your presence the moment you crossed the wards. The Faceless One warned us you would come."
The Faceless One. Kael's stomach dropped. The Entity that had planted fragments of itself in three individuals. One of those individuals was supposed to be him—had been him, before he'd burned the fragment out of his own soul.
But fragments could split. Divide. Spread.
"You're the host," Kael said. "The one carrying the Faceless One's piece."
The armored figure laughed. "Host. Such a clinical term. I prefer 'vessel.' The Faceless One sees through my eyes now. Controls my hands. Guides my blade." He raised the serrated weapon. "It finds you... Fascinating. A Soul Reaver who has harvested so many, yet remains untouched by corruption. It wants to understand how."
"Then come and try to understand."
Kael lunged.
The armored cultist moved faster than his bulk suggested—too fast, inhumanly fast, the Faceless One lending its own reflexes to the vessel's strikes. Kael blocked a downward cut, felt the impact rattle through his arm, and countered with a thrust that should have opened the man's throat.
The blade stopped an inch from skin. A ward, activated by proximity to the Soul Reaver's touch.
"The Faceless One protects its chosen," the cultist said. "Your harvest cannot touch me. Your blade cannot cut me. You are... Diminished."
Kael disengaged, circling. The cultist's speed was a problem. So was the ward. But wards had limits—they required energy, concentration, a sustained connection to the Entity that powered them.
Breaking that connection was simple. Difficult, but simple.
He reached into his coat and pulled out one of the items he'd harvested from the Bone Singer's room. A finger bone, carved with sigils that pulsed with borrowed power.
"What is that?" The cultist's voice carried a note of alarm.
"Something the dead remember."
Kael crushed the bone in his fist.
The spiritual feedback hit the cultist like a physical blow. His body convulsed, the Faceless One's connection severing in a cascade of fractured power. The ward flickered, failed, dissolved.
Kael's blade found the gap between armor plates.
The cultist fell.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 3,412 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Fragment of Faceless One awareness acquired. Warning: Fragment will attempt to influence host. Strong mental defenses required.]
Kael dismissed the notification without reading further. The influence could wait. The prisoners were still in danger, the remaining cultists still fighting.
He turned toward the tree line, where shadows moved among the escapees.
An arrow sprouted from his shoulder.
The impact spun him half around. Pain flared, sharp and immediate, followed by the colder sensation of spiritual damage. Someone had used a blessed bolt—a weapon designed to wound Awakened beyond natural healing.
Kael dropped behind a wagon wheel, checking his wound. The arrow had passed through, missing bone and major vessels. Painful but survivable. The spiritual damage would heal in an hour or two.
He had minutes, not hours.
More arrows thunked into the wagon's wooden frame. The cultists had archers on the rooftops now, using the distraction to pin him down.
Fourteen arrows in the first volley. More being nocked.
The prisoners had reached the tree line. Good. They would scatter into the wilderness, find help, tell someone what had happened here. The mission was succeeding, even if Kael died in this muddy yard.
But he wasn't planning to die.
He reached for the abilities he'd harvested over the past weeks—the combat reflexes from the alley cultist, the bone whisper from the Singer, the fragment of Faceless One awareness from the vessel. Power he'd collected, abilities he'd earned through the only currency Soul Reavers could spend: death.
The power flooded through him.
Kael rose from cover and moved.
The first archer fell before the man could nock his second arrow. The second archer's bowstring snapped as Kael's blade passed through the wood. The third archer screamed as his hands refused to obey, the bone whisper forcing his own skeleton to attack its owner.
Twelve cultists remained. Then eight. Then three.
Kael stopped counting when the last one ran.
He stood in the yard of the Wayward Inn, surrounded by corpses, bleeding from his shoulder, his coat torn and his blade dripping. The fog had begun to lift, revealing the first light of dawn creeping over the hills.
Thirty-nine prisoners had escaped into the trees. The convoy was destroyed. The cult's ritual would not happen.
And somewhere, in a place beyond mortal sight, the Faceless One was watching. Learning. Waiting for its moment.
Kael retrieved the arrow from his shoulder, biting down on his belt to muffle the scream as he pulled the barbed head free. The wound would heal. The fragment in his mind would try to influence him. The Shadow Court would want a report.
The Bone Singer's words echoed in his memory. The original Soul Reaver had never died. He walked the world wearing different faces, searching for his blade.
Kael touched the knife at his belt—the weapon that had been with him since his execution, the tool that let him harvest souls. He'd always assumed it was just a tool. A focus for his abilities.
Now he wondered if it was something more.
Footsteps in the fog behind him. Kael spun, blade raised, but it was only Thorn—the Shadow Court operative from the alley. She surveyed the carnage with an unreadable expression.
"You got them out," she said.
"I got most of them out. The rest didn't survive the night."
"We warned you. The convoy was well-protected."
"You could have sent backup."
"We don't do backup. We do information. What you do with it is your concern." Thorn stepped closer, examining the corpses. "The vessel is down. That's good. The Faceless One will need time to find another host, assuming it can't rebuild from the fragment you carry."
"It already has a fragment in me."
"Yes." Thorn's scarred face twisted into something like sympathy. "That's why we chose you for this mission, Soul Reaver. You're already compromised. We needed someone who could survive the attention."
Kael's grip tightened on his blade. "You used me."
"We used each other. You wanted to save the prisoners. We wanted the cult operation destroyed. The fact that you were the perfect tool for the job was only... Convenient." She turned to leave. "The Council of Six convenes in nine days. The Great Rift's seal will fail within the month. Whatever the cult accomplished tonight, it accelerated the timeline."
"How long do we have?"
"Before what?"
"Before the seal breaks completely. Before the Entities come through."
Thorn paused at the edge of the fog. "No one knows for certain. The first Soul Reaver was the only one who ever truly understood the seal network. When he... Ascended... That knowledge died with him. Or so we believed."
She disappeared into the mist.
Kael stood alone in the dawning light, surrounded by the dead, carrying fragments of Entities in his mind and a blade whose origins he didn't understand.
Nine days until the Council met. A month until the seal failed. And somewhere in the world, a being older than human civilization was searching for the weapon that had created him.
The fog finished lifting. The sun rose over Thornwick's distant spires, painting the sky in shades of blood and gold.
Kael began walking.
He had nine days to figure out who he really was.
