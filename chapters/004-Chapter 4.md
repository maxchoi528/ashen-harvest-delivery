Kael's fingers closed around the dead man's throat. The body still held warmth, barely—hours old, not days. Perfect.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted Tier 2 [Shadow Step] technique fragment from the remains. Precision acquired."
The assassin's memories flooded through him: hired blades circling the safehouse, silver coins silence, and somewhere behind them all, a name dripping like poison. Zarek.
Three figures emerged from the steam and shadow of the alley mouth. Leather armor, blessed blades, the crescent moon brand of the Order of Dawn stitched above their hearts.
"Drop the corpse, Reaver." The lead hunter's voice carried the bored authority of someone who'd killed dozens of his kind. "By decree of the Concordat, you are under arrest for unauthorized spiritual extraction."
Kael released the body. The hunter's blade came up, silver runes flickering along its edge. Behind him, his companions spread into a practiced arc, cutting off retreat.
"You understand what I am," Kael said. The Soul Reaver hungered at his core, a well of power that cost him nothing and cost everyone else everything. "You understand what I can do."
"We understand you're outnumbered." The hunter smiled. "The Concordat doesn't recognize Reaver rights. Kill you outright, no trial required."
Kael's eyes tracked the three positions. Lead hunter front, aggressive stance—wanted to end this fast. Left hunter favored his right leg, old injury slowing his pivot. Right hunter kept glancing toward the street, nervous about witnesses.
Nervous hunters died first.
The lead hunter lunged. His blade carved a silver arc toward Kael's neck, designed to sever the spiritual pathways that connected soul to body. Fast. Trained. Useless.
Kael stepped through him. Not around—*through*. The Shadow Step technique pulsed through his newly-acquired pathways, and for one heartbeat he existed between spaces, the hunter's blade passing through the ghost of where he'd been.
He materialized behind the lead hunter, one hand catching the man's collar, the other driving upward beneath the ribs.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted several hours of combat fatigue from the remains. Stamina debt transferred."
The hunter's body crumpled. Kael felt the drain—not spiritual, but physical. His own muscles screamed protest as the exhaustion of the dead man flowed into him. The Soul Reaver took, and the world paid the price.
The nervous hunter broke first. He turned to run, and that was the wrong choice. Kael caught him by the back of his armor and hurled him into the alley wall. Bone cracked. The third hunter hesitated, then charged.
She was better than the others. Her blade work showed years of training, and she didn't telegraph her strikes. Kael blocked two cuts, felt the third slice across his forearm, and tasted blood.
But she was fighting to survive. He was fighting to feed.
He let her open his cheek. Let the pain focus him. When she pulled back for another strike, his hand closed around her wrist and the Soul Reaver woke hungry.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted [Iron Will] technique fragment from the remains. Mental resistance increased."
She collapsed, alive but emptied. The technique would rebuild itself over days, but for now she was just a woman who would never again feel confident in her own mind.
Kael stood in the alley surrounded by bodies. Two dead, one breathing but broken, and the original corpse cooling at his feet. Blood dripped from his wounds. His muscles burned with borrowed exhaustion.
The nervous hunter groaned against the wall. Kael walked toward him.
"Who sent you?" He kept his voice flat. "And before you lie, understand that I can take what I need directly from your soul. Your memories, your skills—everything that makes you *you*. The Concordat says Reavers don't have rights. That means neither do you."
The hunter's eyes widened. "Please—I was just hired. Third party contract. They said you killed Dawn initiates in the Undercroft—"
"The Undercroft." Kael's jaw tightened. "That wasn't me."
"Then someone framed you, I swear! I just wanted the sovereigns—" The hunter choked on his own words. "They used a Shadow Court intermediary. That's all I know. Please."
Kael studied the man's face. Fear, genuine terror. This one hadn't known the truth.
Shadow Court. Third party contracts. Someone wanted him hunted and wanted it to look like he was killing Order members.
Zarek. This all traced back to Zarek.
He released the hunter. "Run. Tell your masters the Reaver sends his regards. Tell them if they come for me again, I won't leave witnesses."
The hunter scrambled upright and limped into the steam and gaslight, leaving a wet trail behind him.
Kael collected the Dawn initiates' blessed blades—worth at least fifty sovereigns each on the black market—and vanished into Thornwick's labyrinthine streets before the city watch arrived.

Three days passed before Kael risked surfacing again.
He found refuge in a dead man's apartment above a tannery, the smell of chemicals masking his own scent from spiritual tracking. The wounds healed slowly; his Awakened constitution accelerated the flow, but it couldn't erase the memory of combat.
The [Shadow Step] technique felt natural now, an extension of his will than a borrowed tool. He could feel other fragments settling into place—traces of the assassin's training, the lead hunter's tactical instincts, pieces of skill and knowledge that would take weeks to fully integrate.
But he needed more. He needed to understand what he'd become.
The Soul Reaver whispered in his mind, patient as hunger. It had no voice, no consciousness that he could sense. It *was*—a tool that took, and gave, and demanded nothing except use.
Kael stood at the grimy window, watching the street below. Steam-carts rumbled past, their engines coughing soot into the evening air. Workers trudged homeward, their faces blank with exhaustion. The city's perpetual haze caught the dying light, turning everything the color of old copper.
Somewhere out there, Zarek was planning his next move. Somewhere, the Shadow Court was hunting him. And somewhere, in the towers of the Arcane Spire, the Orders were deciding his fate.
He needed allies. Or at least, people who would take his coin without asking questions.
The Mortician's Guild dealt in death. The Veiled Market traded in forbidden goods. But the Arcane Spire—the Arcane Spire dealt in knowledge, and knowledge was the one currency the Soul Reaver couldn't harvest from the dead.
Kael pulled his hood low and descended into the streets.

The Arcane Spire rose from Thornwick's heart like a blade thrust into the sky, its black stone walls inscribed with thousands of years of magical theory. No other structure in the city matched its height; even the Cathedral of Dawn's Golden Light seemed modest by comparison. At its peak, a massive crystal focused sunlight into a beam that swept the horizon, monitoring for rift activity.
The plaza surrounding the Spire bustled with activity despite the late hour. Scholars in gray robes hurried between buildings, arms laden with scrolls. Apprentices argued theory in corners, chalk diagrams appearing and vanishing at their fingertips. The smell of old paper and alchemical reagents hung heavy in the air.
Kael paused at the entrance, studying the wards woven into the stonework. Detection arrays, designed to flag unauthorized spiritual signatures. His Soul Reaver presence would light them up like a bonfire.
"First time at the Spire?"
Kael's hand drifted toward his concealed blade. The speaker was a woman in her middle years, her gray-streaked hair pulled back in a severe bun. She wore the Spire's indigo robes, but the silver threads at her cuffs marked her as a senior researcher, not a student.
"Might be," Kael said carefully.

Kael hesitated. The name meant nothing to him, but her directness intrigued him. Most people avoided eye contact with someone wearing a hood in Thornwick's better districts.
"Kael."
"Just Kael? No noble house? No Order affiliation?" Martha's eyes sparkled with something between amusement and calculation. "That's refreshingly honest. Most people lie about their origins within the first thirty seconds."
"You asked about my intentions. Not my history."
"Fair point." Martha gestured toward the Spire's entrance. "Walk with me. I'm heading to the Restricted Archives anyway, and the wards respond better to pairs than individuals. Less like an infiltration, more like a scholarly discussion."
Kael matched her pace. Inside the Spire, the air changed—cooler, cleaner, humming with barely-contained energy. The walls were lined with shelves that stretched up beyond sight, each one packed with texts in languages he couldn't identify. Apprentices glanced up as they passed, their gazes lingering on his hood before sliding away with studied disinterest.
"You mentioned things that shouldn't exist," Kael said. "Like what?"
"Soul Reavers, for instance." Martha didn't look at him as she spoke, her tone conversational. "The theoretical frameworks say they shouldn't be possible. The Law of Soul Conservation is absolute—spiritual energy cannot be created, only transferred. Yet here you are, harvesting techniques and memories from the dead without depleting your own reserves."
Kael's hand tightened around his blade's hilt. "You're remarkably calm about this."
"I've spent forty years studying anomalies. Calm is a professional requirement." Martha stopped at an unmarked door, producing a brass key inscribed with complex runes. "Besides, if I wanted you arrested, I'd have triggered the wards myself than walking you through them."

"I keep odd hours," Martha continued, settling into a chair that looked like it might collapse at any moment. "The kind of hours where unusual individuals sometimes appear, looking for information they can't find through legitimate channels."
"And you help them?"
"I help *knowledge* find its proper context." She fixed him with a sharp gaze. "You're not the first Reaver to walk into my office, Kael. You're not even the first one I've helped. The trick is ensuring both of us benefit from the exchange."
Kael remained standing. His instincts screamed caution. This woman knew what he was, knew the danger he represented, and yet she sat there calmly, offering deals like they were discussing the weather.
"What do you want from me?"
"The same thing I want from all my research subjects: truth. The Soul Reaver phenomenon shouldn't exist according to current magical theory. Either our theories are wrong, or there's something we're missing. Given that no one has successfully studied a Reaver for more than a few hours before they were hunted down, our data is limited."

You've harvested techniques from each of them, accumulating skills that would take a normal Awakened decades to develop."
Kael's blood chilled. "You've been tracking me."

"Zarek."
"The name keeps appearing in certain Shadow Court circles. Care to tell me what you did to earn such dedicated attention?"
Kael hesitated. The office felt smaller, the walls pressing inward. He didn't know this woman. He had no reason to trust her.
But he needed information. And she had it.
"I didn't kill those initiates in the Undercroft. Zarek framed me for it before my execution. I've been trying to prove my innocence, but every time I get close to evidence, someone tries to kill me."
Martha's pen stopped. She looked up, and for the first time, her expression held something other than academic interest.
"Zarek framed you. Before your execution." She set the pen down carefully. "That means the frame job was planned in advance. Someone knew you would survive, knew you would be executed, and arranged for evidence to be waiting when you woke up."

"It means you're not just a Reaver, Kael. You're a *political* Reaver. Someone went to considerable expense to ensure you would be blamed for something you didn't do. The question is why."
Kael stepped closer to the desk. "What do you know about the Shadow Court?"
"Everything I can find that hasn't been burned, buried, or officially disavowed." Martha pulled another journal from the stack. Some want to control it. And some want to use the Abyssal Entities as weapons."

She opened the journal, revealing pages of interconnected names and symbols. "Zarek is interesting. He's been climbing the Shadow Court's hierarchy for years, but his methods are unusually violent even by their standards. He's made enemies within his own organization."
"And he's trying to kill me."
"Which suggests you know something he wants hidden. Or you're a threat to something he's planning." Martha studied him with new intensity. "What did you do before you became a Reaver? Before the frame job?"
"I was nothing. A convict. Someone who happened to be in the wrong place when Zarek needed a scapegoat."
"No." Martha shook her head. "There's no such thing as 'nothing' in the Awakened world. Everyone has connections, history, potential. If Zarek chose you specifically, there was a reason."
Kael's mind raced. The execution. The frame job. The impossible survival. Someone had known he would wake up. Someone had prepared evidence in advance. Someone had arranged for the Soul Reaver to find him.
"The Reaver power," he said slowly. "Where did it come from? How did I get it?"

They require a seed—a fragment of the original Soul Reaver's power, passed down through bloodlines or rituals. The original Soul Reaver died over three centuries ago, and the Orders have spent every moment since then hunting down anyone who inherited fragments of their power."

No logical pathway to Reaver manifestation." Martha's eyes glittered. "Yet here you are, using power that should have been impossible for you to access. Someone gave it to you, Kael. Someone went through considerable trouble to plant the Reaver seed in your soul before your execution."
The implications hit him like a physical blow. His entire situation—the frame job, the execution, the resurrection—all of it had been orchestrated by someone who knew he would become a Reaver.
Someone had *wanted* him to become this.

"I'm saying there's a conspiracy larger than Zarek, larger than the Shadow Court, possibly larger than the Orders themselves. And you're at the center of it."
Kael's hand moved to the concealed blade at his hip. Old instincts, the convict's paranoia that never faded. "Why are you telling me this? What's your angle?"
"Curiosity, mostly. Also professional pride." Martha smiled without warmth. "I spent my career studying anomalies. You're the biggest anomaly I've ever encountered. If someone is manipulating your existence, I want to understand why—and I want to document it before the Orders decide you're too dangerous to study."
"And if I refuse to be your research subject?"
"Then you walk out of here and continue running from hunters until someone finally succeeds in killing you. I don't control you, Kael. I just offer information. What you do with it is your choice."
The office fell silent. Dust motes drifted through the air, illuminated by the soft glow of crystallized soul-essence. Somewhere in the Spire's depths, something mechanical hummed, powering wards and rituals and the vast machinery of magical research.

"Teach me," he said. "Whatever you know about the Soul Reaver, about the original power, about how it works—teach me. I need to understand what I am before I can figure out what I'm supposed to do."

"I know the Concordat doesn't recognize Reaver rights. I know the Orders would happily execute you for what you are. But I also know that the truth is more important than politics, and the truth about Soul Reavers has been buried for too long."
Kael stared at the cuffs. Wearing them meant trusting this woman, trusting the Spire, trusting that this wasn't another trap designed to neutralize him.
But he couldn't keep running blind. He needed knowledge, resources, allies. And Martha Copperkettle had information he couldn't get anywhere else.
He picked up the cuffs and fastened them around his wrists. The metal hummed against his skin, dampening his spiritual presence until he felt almost normal.
"Welcome to the Arcane Spire, Kael." Martha rose, gathering her journals. "Try not to kill anyone while we're researching. The cleanup paperwork is dreadful."

Kael stepped inside.

"This is their journal," he breathed. "The original Soul Reaver."

*"The Reaver does not create power—it redirects. Every soul harvested returns energy to the Abyss, feeding the great cycle, but a fragment remains with the harvester. This is not theft. It is conservation. The Law is not violated; it is expanded."*

The Reaver didn't violate the Law of Soul Conservation. It worked *with* it, channeling energy that would otherwise be lost forever.
"The Orders misunderstood," he said. "They thought the Reaver was stealing, when really it's—"

Kael turned more pages. Diagrams of soul structures, charts tracking Reaver manifestations across centuries, notes on the 72 sealed rifts and their gradual degradation.
And there, near the back, a single paragraph that made his blood freeze:
*"The seal network was not built to contain the Entities. It was built to harvest them. The original Reaver understood this truth. The Orders fear not the Reaver's power, but the Reaver's knowledge. Those who know the purpose of the seals can undo them."*
"The seals are harvest sites," Kael said. "The Entities aren't prisoners—they're fuel."
"Now you understand." Martha's voice was soft. "The Orders maintain the seal network not out of necessity but out of greed. Every year, they drain a fraction of each Entity's power, converting Abyssal energy into soul-essence for their own advancement. The seals aren't protection. They're batteries."
"And if someone harvested an Entity directly—"
"They would have access to power beyond anything the Orders possess. Enough to threaten the entire balance of the Six Orders. Enough to reshape the world." Martha met his gaze. "Enough to make the Orders' fear of Reavers make perfect sense."
Kael closed the journal. His hands trembled, though whether from revelation or reaction he couldn't tell.
"The conspiracy," he said. "Whoever gave me the Reaver power. They want me to—"
"Harvest the Entities. Yes. That's my theory." Martha began shelving the documents with practiced efficiency. "Someone has been systematically weakening the seal network, creating opportunities for a Reaver to access power that should be impossible. You weren't chosen randomly, Kael. You were cultivated."
"For what purpose?"
"That, I cannot tell you. The documents don't say. But whoever orchestrated your existence has been planning for decades, possibly centuries. They chose you because you were expendable—a convict with no family, no connections, no one who would miss you if you died. They knew you would be framed, knew you would be executed, knew you would wake up hungry for souls."
"And now they want me to destroy the Orders."
"Or replace them. The original Soul Reaver walked a similar path. They ended the Abyssal War not by sealing the rifts but by harvesting the Entities themselves, absorbing their power until they became something beyond human. The Orders called it a victory. But in truth, they removed one god to make room for six smaller ones."
Kael's mind raced. The frame job. Zarek. The hunters. All of it was theater, distractions designed to push him toward a specific goal without giving him the choice to refuse.
He was a weapon. And someone had been forging him since before his birth.
"I need to find out who," he said. "The person or group behind this. I won't be anyone's puppet."

Zarek's operations have become increasingly aggressive, particularly regarding sealed sites. He's not just hunting you, Kael. He's hunting for something specific."

Kael stared at the folder. His entire existence, reduced to a quest for a word that someone wanted to use against him.
"Zarek framed me to draw me out," he said slowly. "He knew I would become a Reaver, knew I would start hunting for answers. He's using me to locate the tablet."

The Soul Reaver hungered at his core, patient and hungry. It knew nothing of politics or conspiracies. It only knew that there were souls to harvest, power to take, and enemies to destroy.

Kael followed her out of the Archives, the folder pressed against his chest. The weight of revelation pressed down on him, but beneath it burned something fiercer—the hunger that had never left, the Reaver's eternal demand.
He would find the tablet. He would find out who had made him. And he would make them regret ever believing they could control a Soul Reaver.

The Drowned Quarter lay on Thornwick's western edge, where the old canal system had collapsed decades ago and the sea had reclaimed the ruins. Buildings jutted from stagnant water at odd angles, their foundations long since rotted away. Only the desperate lived here—smugglers, fugitives, people who had fallen too far to climb back.

Kael pushed through the door. A dozen eyes turned toward him, assessed him, dismissed him or marked him as prey. He ignored them and approached the bar.
The woman behind the counter was tall and lean, her dark hair cut short and practical. A thin scar ran from her left temple to her jaw, and her hands bore the calluses of someone who had spent years handling blades. She watched him approach with the flat expression of someone who had seen everything twice and been impressed by none of it.
"Martha sent you," Lyra said. It wasn't a question.
"She said you might have information. About the Sepulcher Tablets."
