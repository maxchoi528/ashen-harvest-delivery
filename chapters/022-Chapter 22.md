The night air screamed past Kael's ears as he vaulted the rooftop gap, cobblestones thirty feet below. Behind him, the Order of Dawn's lanterns bobbed through the alley, their bearers shouting commands in clipped military cadence.
"Split! Cover the drainage tunnels!"
Kael's boots hit the opposite roof tiles, sending a cascade of loose slate clattering into the darkness. He didn't slow. Couldn't. Three blocks north, a church bell tolled the ninth hour, and every clang meant another patrol cycling into position. The Order had Thornwick locked down tighter than a miser's purse after the Ashborn incident.
His fingers found the rough edge of a chimney. He hauled himself up, chest burning, lungs raw from the smoke he'd deliberately set in the warehouse district to cover his escape. Twenty minutes ago, he'd been sharing information with a contact in the Weavers' Guild. Fifteen minutes ago, that contact had tried to kill him. Ten minutes ago, Kael had learned the hard way that Order agents had compromised the entire operation.
The contact's face surfaced in his memory—Jonas, with his nervous laugh and his habit of touching his left ear before a lie. The knife had come from nowhere, serrated and aimed at Kael's kidney. But Jonas had forgotten one thing: Kael wasn't just a former death-row prisoner. He was something far worse.
He dropped through a skylight, landing in a textile merchant's warehouse. Bolts of dyed wool surrounded him, their purple and crimson hues bleeding into shadow. Kael pressed himself behind a stack of shipping crates, controlling his breathing. Above, boots hammered across the roof. A voice called down:
"Descend and search! Standard sweep pattern!"
Kael counted to five. Then he moved toward the warehouse's rear exit, where a drainage grate led to the storm tunnels running beneath Thornwick's eastern quarter. The tunnels would buy him time, but the Order knew about them too. They always knew about everything.
He was three steps from the grate when he heard it—a sound that froze the blood in his veins. Not footsteps. Not weapons. Something worse.
A whisper, threading through the warehouse like smoke through a keyhole.
*Kael Ashborn. Soul Reaver.*
His hand shot to his chest, where the tattoo pulsed beneath his skin. The mark he'd woken with after his execution, the brand that marked him as something the Orders had spent centuries trying to exterminate. It burned cold now, reacting to something nearby. Something spiritual. Something wrong.
"You shouldn't be here," a woman's voice said from the shadows between the wool bolts. "Neither should they."
Kael spun, drawing the knife he'd taken from Jonas's corpse. The blade caught no light—the metal had been treated with a non-reflective coating, Order standard issue. That detail registered in his mind even as his eyes tried to find the speaker.
She stepped forward, and the darkness seemed to cling to her than retreat.
Mid-twenties, maybe. Tall, with a lean build that spoke of speed over strength. Her hair was black and cropped short, practical, and her skin had a faint pallor that reminded Kael of moonlight on old paper. She wore a long coat the color of charcoal, its cut suggesting mobility than fashion, and her boots were soft-soled—another person built for moving without being heard.
But it was her eyes that held Kael's attention. They were silver, flecked with darker spots, and they reflected nothing. No ambient light. No awareness of the world around them. Just an endless, depthless grey that made him think of empty graves.
"Order of Dawn," she said, tilting her head toward the roof. "Three Ascended, five standard Awakened. They're carrying suppression chains and enough sanctified oil to burn a block." Her voice was flat, almost bored, but something flickered in those silver eyes. "You ran into their cleanup crew. The one you killed was a lieutenant. They'll want his body back."
Kael kept the knife raised. "Who are you?"
"Someone who knows what you are. What you can do." She moved closer, and Kael's grip tightened. She stopped, reading his posture with the ease of someone who understood violence intimately. "Peace. I'm not with the Order. I'm not with the cult either, which is more than most people in Thornwick can claim tonight."
"Cult." The word tasted like ash. "Which cult?"
"The one that wants to crack open the seals. The one that's been accelerating their timeline." Her eyes narrowed slightly. "The one that knows a Soul Reaver just appeared in the one city where all six Orders have embassies. Coincidence?"
Kael's pulse hammered against his throat. The drainage grate was six feet behind him. The front exit, twenty feet ahead. Between him and the woman stood nothing but empty floor and a choice he didn't have time to make.
"The Pale Forest," she said, as if reading his thoughts. "Northeast, past the mill district. There's a place called Verdant Hollow—abandoned settlement, used to be a mining town before the Veil started leaking. The Order doesn't patrol there. Neither does anyone else." She reached into her coat, and Kael tensed— "Take this."
She threw something small and dark. Kael caught it reflexively. A folded square of black cloth, light as paper, with a symbol embroidered in silver thread. The symbol looked like a hourglass, but with the top half empty and the bottom half filled with what might have been stars.
"Sable Cross," the woman said. "We don't officially exist. But when the rifts start opening and the Entities crawl through, you'll want people who know what they're fighting."
"You expect me to trust a stranger who knows my name, my power, and exactly where I am?"
The woman smiled, and it transformed her face from cold to something almost warm. Almost human. "I expect you to trust that I could have killed you already and chose not to. The Order won't be so generous." She turned toward the warehouse's side window. "You have maybe two minutes before they finish their roof sweep and start the interior search. The Pale Forest is four miles northeast. Verdant Hollow is another mile beyond the tree line."
She paused at the window's edge.
"The Whisper King knows you exist, Kael Ashborn. When he sends his children for you—and he will—you'll want answers I can't give you on a warehouse floor with Order hunters closing in." She pushed the window open, letting in the chill of Thornwick's night. "Come to Verdant Hollow. I'll tell you what I know. What I've seen. What the first Reaver wrote in his final days."
Then she was gone, melting through the window frame like smoke through a crack in the door.
Kael stood alone in the warehouse, the black cloth heavy in his palm. His mind raced through the possibilities—trap, ambush, deception—but his body had already started moving. The Order's boots were thundering toward the warehouse entrance now, voices barking commands to breach and secure.
He didn't go for the drainage grate.
Instead, he went up—climbing the interior support beam, bursting through the skylight he'd entered, and sprinting across the warehouse roof toward the eastern district. Behind him, shouts of discovery erupted as the Order found an empty warehouse and a trail growing colder by the second.
The Pale Forest was supposed to be three miles beyond the city walls, where the Veil had grown thin decades ago and the trees had adapted to spiritual contamination. Most citizens avoided it. The Order posted warning signs at every road leading northeast. The merchant guilds paid hazard premiums to workers who had to travel through it.
None of that mattered now.
Kael ran until Thornwick's gaslit streets gave way to farmland, until the cultivated fields surrendered to wild meadows, until the wild meadows met the first pale trees of the forest that gave it its name.
The Pale Forest lived up to its reputation. The trees were silver-barked and branchless for the first twenty feet, their trunks rising like columns in a ruined cathedral. No leaves grew on any of them—instead, the branches ended in pale, claw-like projections that seemed to reach for the grey sky. The ground beneath Kael's feet was soft with moss that absorbed his footsteps completely, and the air carried a mineral scent, like limestone dust mixed with something sweeter. Something that reminded him of old blood.
The tattoo on his chest pulsed again, stronger this time. The forest was saturated with spiritual energy—not the clean, ordered power that the Orders cultivated, but something wilder. Rawer. The kind of energy that leaked through the Veil when the seals between worlds grew weak.
*Seventy-two rifts*, Kael thought. *Seventy-two Entities, each one sealed for centuries, and now they're all weakening.*
The forest path wound between the trees, barely visible beneath the moss. Kael followed it anyway, trusting his instincts and the fading traces of someone who'd walked this way before. The woman—Sable Cross—had been here recently. He could sense the disturbance in the spiritual energy, a wake in the water that hadn't closed.
An hour passed. Maybe more. The forest's dim light didn't change, and Kael had no way to track time except by the growing ache in his legs and the hunger gnawing at his stomach. He'd eaten nothing since the morning, and that felt like days ago.
Then the trees opened up, and Kael found Verdant Hollow.
The settlement had been built in a natural depression, a bowl of earth maybe fifty feet deep where a stream once ran. The houses were wooden, their walls grey with age, their roofs sagging under decades of neglect. Every structure was intact—nothing had burned or collapsed—but the village was utterly empty. No people. No animals. No signs of struggle or violence.
Just silence, heavy as a shroud.
Kael descended the slope carefully, his knife drawn, his senses extended. The tattoo on his chest burned now, a constant cold presence that made his teeth ache. Something in Verdant Hollow was triggering his Soul Reaver abilities, even though he hadn't activated them.
He stepped into the main thoroughfare—a dirt road, packed hard by feet that had walked it centuries ago—and the truth hit him like a physical force.
The village wasn't abandoned. It was a graveyard.
He couldn't see the bodies. Couldn't smell decay or rot. But the spiritual residue was overwhelming, layers upon layers of death compressed into the earth and wood and air. This place had been a killing field. A massacre so complete that the bodies had been removed—buried or burned—but the stain remained.
Kael forced himself to breathe. To think.
*Soul Harvest. You sense residual essence in the area. Quality: degraded. Age: approximately 200 years. Source: multiple sources, approximately 200-300 individuals.*
The notification flickered in his vision, unwelcome but informative. Two centuries ago, two or three hundred people had died here. Their souls had dissipated back into the Abyss, as all souls did, but their spiritual residue had soaked into the land itself.
The Soul Reaver abilities could harvest from corpses. Could they harvest from a site of mass death?
Kael didn't know. He didn't want to find out.
He moved through the village quickly, checking each building for signs of occupation or danger. Most were empty—furnishings rotted, goods scattered, memories long faded. But in the largest structure, which might have been a meeting hall or communal building, he found something different.
A symbol, freshly painted on the wooden floor.
The hourglass, with the empty top and star-filled bottom. Sable Cross's mark.
"You're early."
Kael spun, knife raised, and found the woman standing in the doorway. She'd entered without a sound, without a whisper of displaced air. Her silver eyes caught the dim light filtering through the hall's high windows, and she looked almost ghostly in that pale illumination.
"Order's probably still searching the eastern district," she said, leaning against the doorframe. "You have maybe an hour before they think to check the forest. Another thirty minutes before they reach the village boundary." She pushed off the frame and walked toward a table at the hall's far end. "Sit. You look like you haven't eaten in days."
"I haven't." Kael didn't move. "Who were they? The people who died here?"
The woman's hands paused over the table's surface. When she spoke, her voice had lost its flat quality, replaced by something harder. Older.
"Miners. Two hundred and forty-seven of them. They found something in the hills, a few miles northeast of here. A rift, supposedly sealed, but the seal had cracked." She set a satchel on the table, pulled out dried meat and a water flask. "The Entities came through. Not fully manifested—they couldn't sustain physical form—but enough to... Feed. The Order arrived three days later. They found the survivors and put them out of their misery."
"Put them out of—"
"The Entities had touched them. Corrupted them. There was no saving the miners who'd breathed the Abyssal air." She turned to face him. "The Order killed two hundred and forty-seven people to prevent the corruption from spreading. Then they salted the earth, burned the bodies, and pretended the village never existed."
Kael's stomach turned. He thought of the Order of Dawn hunters pursuing him, their righteous fury, their absolute certainty that they were the protectors of humanity. He thought of Jonas, his nervous laugh, his betrayal.
"They're not all like that," he said, though he wasn't sure who he was trying to convince.
"No. Some are worse." The woman gestured to the food. "Eat. You'll need your strength for what comes next."
Kael approached the table slowly, keeping the knife in his off hand. He took a piece of dried meat, chewed mechanically. The woman watched him with those depthless eyes, and he had the uncomfortable sensation of being evaluated.
"You're wondering why I helped you," she said.
"I'm wondering why I shouldn't kill you right now and take whatever information you have by force."
A smile flickered across her face. "Because I'm the only person in Thornwick who can tell you about the Soul Reaver lineage. The first one. What he did, what he learned, and why the Whisper King wants him dead." She gestured toward a crumbling stone near the altar, its hollow center concealed by creeping moss. "Look inside. You'll find a leather journal, its cover stained and cracked with age. It belonged to the second Soul Reaver. She died two hundred years ago, in the massacre I've just described. She came to Verdant Hollow looking for answers about her power, and she found only death."
Kael stared at the journal. His tattoo burned.
"The Whisper King," he said carefully. "Who is he?"
The woman hesitated. Then she placed the journal on the table and slid it toward him.
"He's not a man. Not anymore. He's something that used to be human, centuries ago, and he's been trying to complete a ritual ever since. A ritual that requires the deaths of every Soul Reaver who has ever existed." She met his eyes. "You're the seventh. The last. And when you die, the seals start to crack. The rifts open. The Entities return, not as shadows, but in physical form."
"Every Soul Reaver," Kael repeated. "The first one—"
"Died in a ritual gone wrong, or so the Orders claim. But the ritual wasn't meant to kill him. It was meant to bind him. To make him part of the seal network." She shook her head. "He refused. He escaped. And he's been hiding ever since, passing his power to successors, waiting for someone strong enough to finish what he started."
"And the Whisper King?"
"He was the one who performed the original binding ritual. He was the first Reaver's student, once. Brother, some say. They were close, before the Abyss changed everything." She paused. "The Whisper King has lived five hundred years on stolen time. The ritual that would end him—end the seals' decay—is the same ritual that would give him everything he wants. Immortality. Godhood. Dominion over both realms."
Kael's mind raced. The journal sat on the table, its leather cover worn smooth by centuries of handling. He could feel the spiritual residue clinging to it, echoes of the woman who'd carried it through her short, violent life.
*Soul Harvest. You sense residual essence in the object. Quality: moderate. Age: 200 years. Source: female, Awakened (Soul Reaver lineage). Memory fragments available. Extraction possible.*
"Why tell me this?" he asked. "What do you want from me?"
The woman's silver eyes flickered, and for a moment, Kael saw something behind them. Something that looked like hunger, or hope, or both.
"I want the ritual to succeed. I want the seals restored, the rifts sealed, the Entities banished back to the Abyss forever." She leaned forward. "And I want the Whisper King dead. Not imprisoned. Not bound. Dead."
"And you think I can do that."
"I think you're the only one who can." She stood, moving toward the hall's rear exit. "The journal contains everything the second Reaver learned about the original. How to use your power. How to advance through the stages. And how to find the place where the first ritual was performed—the place where the Whisper King will eventually have to face you."
She paused at the door.
"One more thing. There's a man in Thornwick named Dorian Vane. He works for the Order of Dawn, high ranking, trusted position. He's been feeding information to the cult—the same cult that wants the seals broken." She didn't turn around. "He was the one who ordered your contact's death tonight. Jonas. And another operative, a woman named Gwen."
Kael's blood froze. "What?"
"Gwen's squad went missing three weeks ago, outside the city. Official report says they were killed by bandits. Real report says Dorian sold them to the cult as test subjects for their unsealing rituals." The woman's voice was steady, but something cracked beneath it. "Gwen was my sister."
She left before Kael could respond.
He stood alone in the abandoned meeting hall, the journal heavy in his hands, the weight of revelation pressing down on him. The Whisper King. The original Soul Reaver. Dorian Vane and his betrayal. It was too much, too fast, and somewhere beyond the village boundaries, Order of Dawn hunters were still searching for him.
He needed to move. Needed to think. Needed to figure out what was truth and what was manipulation in the woman's carefully constructed narrative.
But first, he needed the journal's contents.
Kael sat at the table, opened the leather cover, and began to read.
The first pages were a personal account—handwriting cramped and hurried, ink faded to brown. The second Reaver had been young when she'd written them, barely more than a girl, and her entries alternated between terror and determination. She'd discovered her abilities at fourteen, when she'd touched her dying mother's hand and absorbed not just the woman's memories, but her final moment of agony.
*It felt like drowning*, she'd written. *Like being pulled under by something vast and cold. But when it was done, I knew things I had never learned. Her childhood. Her regrets. The name of the man who had wronged her.*
The entries continued, documenting her progression through the stages. Awakened, like Kael. Then Ascendant, then Transcendent. Each advancement required a choice—a deliberate harvest that pushed her closer to the Abyss and further from humanity.
*The higher you climb*, she'd written, *the easier it becomes to forget that you're supposed to be human. The souls you harvest don't just give you power. They give you perspective. Centuries of perspective, accumulated in fragments, until you start to see individual lives as flickers in a candle's flame.*
*I'm scared. I can feel myself changing. But I can't stop. Not until I understand what I am.*
Kael turned the pages, searching for practical information. There—a diagram of the soul harvesting process, drawn in careful detail. The technique wasn't instinctive, as he'd assumed. It required focus, intent, and a physical connection to the corpse. He read through the instructions twice, committing them to memory.
Then he found the final entry.
The handwriting was different here—shaky, uneven, written by a hand that trembled with exhaustion or fear.
*They're coming. I can hear them in the forest. The Order, or the cult, or something worse. I've hidden the tablet where only a true Reaver can find it, keyed to blood and intent. If you're reading this, you've earned the right to know.*
*The first Reaver didn't die. He transcended. He became something more than human—a bridge between the Material and the Abyss, neither fully alive nor fully dead. The seals aren't just stones and symbols. They're maintained by his sacrifice, his eternal vigil holding the rifts closed through sheer will.*
*But he's weakening. Five hundred years of holding back the Abyss, and he's finally starting to fade.*
*When he dies—when the last true Reaver falls—the seals will fail. Unless someone takes his place.*
*That's the secret. The ritual the Whisper King wants to complete isn't about power. It's about succession. He needs a new Reaver to replace the old one, and he needs that Reaver strong enough to maintain the seals for another five centuries.*
*He'll try to control you. To bind you. To make you his weapon.*
*Don't let him.*
*Find the tablet. Learn the truth. And whatever you do, don't trust anyone who claims to serve the light.*
The entry ended there. The remaining pages were blank.
Kael sat in the silence of Verdant Hollow, the journal closed in his lap, his mind reeling. The first Reaver was still alive. The seals depended on him. And the Whisper King—the man who'd once been his student—was hunting him to force him into a role he'd never asked for.
The tattoo on his chest pulsed once, cold and final.
Then the doors exploded inward.
Kael was on his feet before the splinters finished falling, knife in one hand, the journal stuffed into his coat with the other. Order of Dawn soldiers poured through the breach—five, six, seven of them, their armor gleaming with sanctified engravings, their weapons drawn and ready.
"Drop the weapon!" the lead soldier shouted. "On your knees! Now!"
Kael's eyes swept the room. The rear exit—blocked. The windows—too high to reach before the soldiers closed distance. The only way out was through.
He went through.
The first soldier came at him with a suppression chain, sanctified links crackling with binding energy. Kael ducked under the swing, drove his knife into the man's thigh, twisted. The soldier screamed and crumpled. Kael took the chain, whipped it around the second soldier's throat, pulled. Bone cracked.
*Soul Harvest: You extracted basic combat training from the remains. Quality: low. 3 combat techniques acquired.*
The notification flickered in his vision as Kael rolled behind an overturned table. Crossbow bolts sparked against the wood above his head. He counted three heartbeats, then burst from cover, using the table as a springboard to launch himself at the nearest crossbowman.
His hand closed around the man's throat. The harvesting was involuntary now, instinctive—a surge of cold through his palm, a fragment of the soldier's training flooding his mind.
*Soul Harvest: You extracted basic crossbow proficiency from the remains. Quality: low. 1 technique acquired.*
Three soldiers left. They circled him warily, their formations tight, their eyes calculating. The lead soldier from earlier had recovered, his sword drawn, his face twisted with hatred.
"Abomination," he spat. "You're not even human. You're a disease, feeding on the dead."
Kael said nothing. His knife was gone—lost in the second soldier's leg—and his left hand was bleeding from a crossbow graze. But his tattoo burned with cold fire, and the souls of his enemies whispered in his mind, urging him forward.
"Take him alive," the lead soldier commanded. "The Inquisitors want to study him before execution."
*Execution.* The word hit Kael like a physical blow. He'd already died once, on the gallows in Thornwick's public square. He'd woken with a brand on his chest and a power that shouldn't exist. He wasn't eager to repeat the experience.
One of the soldiers lunged. Kael sidestepped, caught the man's wrist, used his momentum to throw him into his companion. They went down in a tangle of limbs and armor. The third soldier swung his sword—a wide, brutal arc meant to take Kael's head.
He moved without thinking, his body responding to combat instincts that weren't entirely his own. The harvested training guided his movements, sharp and precise. He ducked under the swing, drove his elbow into the soldier's solar plexus, followed with a palm strike to the nose. Cartilage crunched. Blood sprayed.
*Soul Harvest: You extracted intermediate sword technique from the remains. Quality: moderate. 7 techniques acquired.*
Kael staggered backward, his vision swimming. Too much, too fast—the souls were fighting for control, their fragments asserting themselves against his own identity. He could feel them inside him now, dozens of echoes pressing at the edges of his consciousness.
*Soul Harvest: Warning. Soul integration threshold approaching. Recommend stabilization period. Harvesting at current rate may result in identity fragmentation.*
"Run," he gasped, not sure if he was speaking to the soldiers or to himself. "Run, before I—"
The wall exploded.
Not breached. Not collapsed. Exploded, as if something had torn through it from the outside, bringing stone and timber down in a cascade of debris. Kael threw himself flat, covering his head, and when he looked up, the meeting hall's eastern wall was gone, replaced by a ragged hole and the pale trees of the forest beyond.
And standing in that hole, silhouetted against the grey light, was a figure that hurt to look at.
It was tall—seven feet, maybe more—with limbs too long and joints that bent in wrong directions. Its skin was black, cracked like dried riverbed mud, and where its face should have been, there was only a smooth expanse of darkness punctuated by two points of sickly green light.
An Entity. Not fully manifested, but present enough to matter.
"Reaver," it said, and its voice scraped across Kael's mind like nails on slate. "The King sends greetings. The King sends demand. Come, or watch this village burn."
Behind Kael, the surviving soldiers scrambled to their feet, their weapons trembling in hands that had never faced anything worse than rogue Awakened and desperate criminals. The lead soldier found his voice first.
"Hold formation! Sanctified weapons to the front! We can banish this—"
The Entity moved faster than sight. One moment it stood in the breach; the next, it had crossed the hall and seized the lead soldier by the throat. The man's sanctified armor glowed white-hot, smoke rising from the contact points, but the Entity didn't flinch.
"Reaver," it repeated. "The King grows impatient. The King grows angry. Come, or more die."
Kael's mind raced through options, discarding each one as fast as it formed. He couldn't fight the Entity—not yet, not without knowing more about its capabilities. He couldn't save the soldiers, not without sacrificing himself. And the journal in his coat contained secrets that the Whisper King would kill to possess.

The Entity tilted its head, considering. Those green lights flickered, and Kael felt something probe at the edges of his mind—cold, curious, utterly alien.
"Reaver negotiates. Reaver delays. Reaver thinks himself clever."

The Entity's grip tightened. The lead soldier's screams cut off as his neck snapped. The body dropped, and Kael felt the harvest trigger automatically—combat training, fragments of leadership, a lifetime of devotion to an ideal that had ultimately meant nothing.
*Soul Harvest: You extracted advanced tactical command from the remains. Quality: high. 12 techniques acquired.*

Kael forced himself to smile, though his heart hammered against his ribs. "Is that what the Whisper King wants? Or would he prefer to stay hidden a little longer?"
The Entity stood motionless, those green lights fixed on Kael's face. Behind him, the remaining soldiers pressed against the far wall, their weapons forgotten, their faces masks of terror.

"The King remembers you. The King knows your name. The King will be... Pleased."

"Soul Harvest: You extracted shadow movement technique from the remains. Quality: moderate. 1 technique acquired.*

Behind him, in the silence of Verdant Hollow, the last soldiers of the Order of Dawn whispered prayers to gods who had never answered and would never start now.
The Pale Forest swallowed them all, pale trees reaching toward a sky that held no stars, and somewhere in the darkness, the Whisper King waited.
Kael Ashborn, seventh Soul Reaver, walked toward his destiny with borrowed souls screaming in his skull and a journal full of secrets clutched against his chest.
The night had only just begun.

The Pale Forest stretched endlessly before him, pale trees giving way to darker ones, the undergrowth thickening until Kael had to force his way through. The Entity had vanished somewhere ahead—he could still feel its presence, a cold spot in his awareness, but it wasn't leading him anymore.
It was waiting.

A tower, or something like a tower. Black stone threaded with veins of light—light that pulsed in rhythm with his heartbeat, light that hurt to look at directly. The tower turned slowly, impossibly, defying gravity and reason, and at its peak, a single window glowed with sickly green illumination.
"The first gate," a voice said behind him.
Kael spun, his harvested instincts screaming warnings. The woman from Verdant Hollow stood at the cliff's edge, her silver eyes fixed on the tower.
