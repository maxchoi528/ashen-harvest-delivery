Blood painted the cobblestones in patterns that defied geometry. Kael Ashborn stood in the wreckage of the old market square, his boots splashing through two inches of crimson water, and counted the bodies. Nineteen dead Awakened. The smell of charred flesh mixed with the sulfurous tang of destabilized spiritual energy, thick enough to taste.
Another rift had torn open. Not slowly, not with the gradual corruption that warned of decay, but in a violent spasm that had vaporized an entire city block and everything living on it.
The ground trembled beneath his feet. Not from aftershocks. From something beneath the earth, something vast and hungry, pressing against chains that had held for two hundred years.
Kael crouched beside the nearest corpse. A woman, her Order of Dawn insignia half-melted into her chest. Her eyes stared at the smoke-filled sky, frozen in an expression of absolute surprise. She hadn't known death was coming. None of them had.
"Soul Reaver." The voice came from behind him, sharp with barely contained fear. "Don't move."
Kael straightened slowly, keeping his hands visible. Three figures approached through the haze, their spiritual signatures blazing with combat readiness. Iron Vanguard. Heavy armor, rune-etched weapons, the disciplined formations that made them the most feared heavy fighters among the Six Orders.
"You have no authority here," Kael said. "The Concordat doesn't cover Abyssal incidents."
"Standard protocol applies to non-registered Awakened engaging in harvesting activities." The lead soldier, a woman whose rank insignia marked her as a Centurion, kept her blade raised. "Step away from the corpse."
"I'm not harvesting. Not yet." Kael turned to face them fully. "You want to waste time interrogating me, or you want to deal with the fact that another rift just tore open?"
The Centurion's jaw tightened. She knew he was right. Everyone in Thornwick knew what was happening—the seals were failing faster than the Orders could repair them. But the Concordat existed precisely to prevent chaos during crisis. If they let one Reaver harvest freely, where did it end?
"Kael Ashborn." She spoke his name like a curse. "We've been watching you."
"Good." He showed his teeth in something that wasn't a smile. "Stay watching. I have work to do."
He walked past them. No one stopped him. His spiritual signature flared, not with threat, but with the flat assertion of someone who had survived worse than three Iron Vanguard soldiers and had the body count to prove it.
The Centurion's voice followed him. "The Council will hear about this."
"Add it to my list of crimes." Kael didn't look back. "Right under 'being born with an inconvenient power' and 'refusing to die when I was supposed to.'"
He found a quiet alley three streets away, between collapsed buildings that had once been a textile warehouse. The ground still steamed where spiritual energy had superheated the cobblestones. Nineteen dead, and he could sense at least that many more trapped under rubble, dying slowly, their spiritual signatures flickering like guttering candles.
Every one of them was a harvest waiting to happen.
Kael pressed his back against cold stone and allowed himself exactly three seconds of weakness. His hands shook. His breath came in ragged gasps that had nothing to do with exertion. The Soul Reaver power thrummed beneath his skin, eager, hungry, indifferent to the moral calculus of its use.
He could save them. Not all of them—some were too far gone, their souls already departing the material plane—but enough. If he reached them in time, if he harvested the ones already dead, he could pull enough essence from their remains to stabilize the dying, to give their bodies the spiritual energy needed to survive.
The cost was simple: he would become even more of a monster in the eyes of the world. Every harvest was witnessed by someone. Every witness spread the story, distorted it, added their own fears to the reality of what he could do.
The Shadow Court had people everywhere. They would know within the hour that Kael Ashborn had harvested in the aftermath of the Great Rift's latest spasm. They would know because they were always watching, because the shadow operatives he sometimes glimpsed in his peripheral vision were persistent reminders that his power made him valuable to people who wanted to control it.
A groan from beneath the rubble. Someone still breathing.
Kael moved toward the sound.

The woman was pinned beneath a fallen beam, her legs crushed, her spiritual core cracked but not shattered. She was maybe thirty, her Order of Dawn insignia barely visible beneath layers of dust and blood. Her eyes, when they found his face, held the desperate hope of someone who knew she should be dead.
"Please," she whispered.
"Save your breath." Kael knelt beside her, assessing the damage with the cold efficiency of someone who had seen too much death to be overwhelmed by it. The beam was too heavy to lift. Her legs were beyond saving—crushed beyond any healing the Orders' best chirurgeons could repair. She would lose them regardless of what he did.
But her soul still burned. Diminished, flickering, but present.
"I'm going to take the pain," he said. "You'll feel cold for a moment, then nothing. The damage stays, but the pain goes."
She nodded, too weak to speak.
Kael placed his palm against her forehead. The Soul Reaver ability activated, and he felt her pain as a physical thing, a jagged pressure that tried to cut into his consciousness. He absorbed it, let it dissipate into the zero-cost transfer that made his power unique, and watched her face relax as the agony faded.
Her eyes closed. Not death—coma. Her body would redirect the energy meant for pain response toward survival. It might work. Probably wouldn't, but it might.
"Thank you," she breathed.
"Don't thank me yet." He stood. "Stay unconscious. It's the only advantage you have right now."
He left her there, a promise to send help, and moved deeper into the destruction zone.
The first corpse he found was fresh enough to harvest properly. A man, early thirties, Iron Vanguard by his armor. The explosion had torn him apart, but his torso remained intact, his face frozen in a snarl that suggested he'd died fighting.
Kael knelt, placed both hands on the cooling flesh, and activated the Soul Reaver.
The world inverted. Colors bled away. Sound vanished. He existed in the space between heartbeats, in the moment when a soul still clung to its vessel but had already begun to depart.
**Soul Harvest: You extracted 340 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Core Ability Acquired: [Iron Bastion] — defensive technique allowing short-term invulnerability at cost of mobility. Quality: High (fresh corpse, high-tier Awakened source).**
Kael gasped as the ability slotted into his consciousness, foreign but compatible, a new tool in an arsenal that grew with every death he witnessed. The man had been Sovereign stage. Strong. His defensive technique would have been worth a fortune in the Veiled Market, if Kael were foolish enough to sell his harvests.
But selling was the fastest way to lose them. The Soul Reaver abilities weren't meant to be separated from the Reaver. They worked because they were part of him, drawn from the dead but integrated into his living soul.
The notification faded, and Kael became aware of his surroundings again. The man's corpse had turned gray, drained of residual spiritual energy that would have dissipated naturally over the next twenty-four hours. Now it was just meat.
More bodies called to him. More opportunities. He moved from corpse to corpse, harvesting only the freshest, only those whose abilities would be useful, leaving the rest to the Mortician's Guild workers who would arrive eventually to collect what remained.
By the time he finished, he had acquired three more abilities: a minor healing technique from an Arcane Spire member, a sensory enhancement from a Shadow Court operative whose presence confirmed his paranoia about surveillance, and a combat technique he didn't recognize from a corpse that bore no insignia.
Five harvests. Five dead Awakened whose skills now belonged to him. The weight of their souls pressed against his consciousness, not heavy, but present. They would always be present.
Kael stood in the center of the destruction zone, surrounded by the grayscale remains of people who had died believing they were fighting something larger than themselves. He wondered, briefly, if they were right. If the Abyssal Entities truly represented an existential threat that justified the Concordat, the hierarchy, the entire system of control that kept people like him at the bottom of the social order.
The ground trembled again. Stronger this time.
He looked up and saw the sky above the old market square had turned the color of bruised fruit, the spiritual pressure from the destabilized rift creating visible distortions in the air. The seal was failing. Not slowly, not gradually, but in the way a dam fails when the water finally overwhelms its capacity to contain.
The Great Rift wasn't just cracking. It was preparing to explode open.

"You're late."
The voice came from everywhere and nowhere, the acoustics of the destroyed square creating an echo that seemed to come from the buildings themselves. Kael turned, his newly acquired senses spreading outward, and found the source.
A man stood in the center of the destruction, untouched by the chaos surrounding him, as if he existed in a bubble of stillness within the maelstrom. He was tall, gaunt, wearing robes that might have been white before the ash and blood of the battlefield had stained them. His face was hidden beneath a hood, but his presence radiated power that made Kael's newly enhanced spiritual signature feel like a candle beside a bonfire.
Transcendent. Stage three. Dangerous in ways that Demigods could only dream of.
"Zarek." The name came out flat, emotionless, but Kael's body coiled with tension he couldn't fully hide.
"Ah, so you remember." Zarek lowered his hood. His face was younger than Kael expected, barely older than his own, but his eyes held the weight of centuries. "I wondered if the execution had burned away some of your memories."
"You framed me for the Crimson Chapel bombing." Kael's voice didn't waver. "You killed four hundred people to create evidence that would get me executed."
"I killed four hundred people to create a distraction." Zarek's smile was thin, humorless. "You were convenient. A death-row prisoner no one would mourn, whose power made him the perfect scapegoat. It was elegant."
"It was eleven years of my life in chains."
"Fourteen, if you count the original sentence." Zarek examined his fingernails with theatrical disinterest. "You survived, didn't you? Most would have broken. The Mortician's Guild's harvesting chambers aren't known for their mercy."
Kael's hands clenched. The Soul Reaver power responded to his anger, pressing against his skin like something trying to escape. He could harvest this man. Zarek was powerful, but he wasn't invincible. If Kael could get close enough, if he could touch him long enough—
"Don't." The word came from a second figure, emerging from the shadows between collapsed buildings. "He'll kill you before you reach him, Ashborn. Zarek has been waiting for this moment."
Lyra. Kael recognized her voice before he saw her face, the years since their last encounter marked by changes he could barely process. She had been young when he knew her, a street thief with a talent for infiltration and a habit of getting in over her head. Now she stood beside the man responsible for Kael's nightmare, her presence a betrayal he hadn't expected.
"Lyra." Her name tasted like ash. "You work for him now?"
"I work for the Shadow Court." Her voice was steady, but her eyes betrayed something that might have been regret. "Zarek is a Council member. He gives orders; I follow them. That's how this works."
"That's how you choose to let it work." Kael's anger shifted, finding new targets. "The Shadow Court monitors me. They've been watching for months. Why?"
"Because you're interesting." Zarek answered before Lyra could. "One Soul Reaver in a world that claims only one can exist at a time. You should have been destroyed when we discovered your gift. Instead, you survived execution, awakened to full Demigod power, and began accumulating abilities at a rate that should be impossible. The Council has questions."
"I don't answer to your Council."
"No one answers to the Council. That's the point of having power." Zarek stepped closer, and the spiritual pressure increased, forcing Kael to brace himself against its weight. "But you will answer to me. The Great Rift is failing. The seal that has contained the Whisper King for two centuries is nearing complete collapse. We need your power."
"My power isn't meant to repair seals."
"No. It's meant to harvest from the beings that will emerge when the seal breaks entirely." Zarek's eyes glittered with something that might have been excitement. "The Entities that the Orders have spent centuries containing will pour through the rupture. The Six Orders will throw everything they have at containment. Thousands will die—Awakened and inert alike. And when the battle ends, when the Entities are finally pushed back or destroyed, there will be bodies everywhere. Powerful bodies. Ancient bodies. Souls that have been imprisoned in the Abyss for longer than human civilization has existed."
"You want me to harvest them." Kael understood. The horrifying clarity of it settled into his bones.
"I want you to become something more than a grave robber stealing abilities from the common dead." Zarek's smile widened. "Think of it. The 72 Entities. Each one contains power beyond anything a mortal soul can generate. Harvest even a fraction of that energy, absorb even a portion of their abilities, and you could advance to Divine. Beyond. Become something the world has never seen."
"And if I refuse?"
"Then you die here. The rift opens in—" Zarek paused, seeming to listen to something Kael couldn't perceive. "—approximately four minutes. The first Entities will emerge. The battle between Orders and Abyss will destroy everything within a mile radius. You can survive. You cannot win. The mathematics are simple."
Kael looked at Lyra. She stood motionless, her face unreadable, but her hands had curled into fists at her sides.
"You know what he's really planning," Kael said. "The Shadow Court doesn't work for anyone. They work for themselves."
"Everyone works for themselves." Zarek's voice hardened. "The only question is whether you choose to work with us or against us. I have no preference. Dead or allied, I get what I need."
The ground heaved. The trembling that had been constant for the past hour intensified, cobblestones cracking and shifting as something vast pressed against the membrane separating worlds. The sky above the market square had turned solid black, the color of void, and within that darkness, Kael could see shapes beginning to coalesce.
Entities. Already manifesting, even before the seal broke completely.
"Three minutes," Zarek said. "Decide."
Kael's mind raced through options. He could fight. His newly acquired abilities, combined with what he already possessed, made him dangerous. But Zarek was Transcendent. Lyra was Sovereign. Even if he managed to defeat them both, the Entities would pour through before he could escape.
He could run. The destruction zone was large enough that he might reach its edge before the rift fully opened. But running meant abandoning the dying woman he'd left behind, abandoning the other survivors who might still be trapped in the rubble.
He could surrender. Accept Zarek's offer, become a tool of the Shadow Court, and survive long enough to find another way.
Or—
A new tremor. Stronger. The ground split open ten feet to his left, a crack that ran for thirty yards, belching sulfurous smoke and the distinct scent of corrupted spiritual energy. Through the smoke, Kael glimpsed something moving. Not human. Not animal. Something that defied categorization, a mass of shadow and light and geometries that shouldn't exist in three-dimensional space.
The first Entity was early. The seal had held, barely, but it had cracked enough to let something through.
"Interesting," Zarek breathed. "This one is called the Hollow King. An old thing. Very old. Its soul fragment was imprisoned during the First Sealing, before humanity had developed proper terminology for such beings."
The Entity's attention shifted, focusing on the group of humans as if sensing their spiritual signatures. It moved toward them, not walking, but flowing, its form constantly shifting between states that suggested mass and then denied it.
Lyra stepped backward. Even Zarek's composure cracked slightly, his hand moving toward a weapon hidden beneath his robes.
"Your decision," Zarek said, but his voice had lost some of its certainty. "We need to fight or flee. Now."
Kael looked at the approaching Entity, at the crack in the ground that served as its portal, at the bodies of the dead Awakened scattered across the destroyed square. The Soul Reaver power thrummed beneath his skin, responding to the presence of something so fundamentally other that it felt like touching lightning.
He thought of the woman with crushed legs, unconscious in the rubble. He thought of the nineteen dead, whose abilities he now carried. He thought of the shadow operatives watching from distant rooftops, reporting everything to their masters.
He thought of what he could become if he harvested something that had been imprisoned since before history began.
"No," Kael said.
He moved toward the crack. Toward the Entity. Toward the impossible opportunity that the universe had, in its cruelty or its generosity, placed before him.
"Kael!" Lyra's voice, sharp with something that might have been fear. "You can't harvest a full Entity. The power differential alone will destroy you!"
Zarek said nothing. His eyes tracked Kael's movement with predatory interest.
The Hollow King sensed his approach. Its form shifted, the void-geometry condensing into something that almost resembled a face, hollow sockets where eyes should have been, a mouth that opened onto absolute darkness.
It spoke. Not in words, but in concepts, in raw spiritual frequencies that translated directly into meaning within Kael's mind.
**LITTLE REAVER. YOU DARE APPROACH THE THRESHOLD.**
Kael stopped three feet from the crack. Close enough to touch if he reached. Close enough that the Entity's presence made his skin crawl, made the air around him feel thick and wrong.
"I'm not here to fight," he said aloud. "I'm here to talk."
The Entity's almost-face twisted in what might have been amusement.
**TALK. LITTLE MORTAL THINGS ALWAYS TALK BEFORE THEY FIGHT. THE ORDERS SEND CHAMPIONS WITH SWORDS OF LIGHT. THE COVENANTS OFFER SACRIFICE. THE SHADOW MASTERS WHISPER PROMISES. WHAT DO YOU OFFER, REAVER?**
"The truth." Kael's voice didn't waver. "The seal is breaking. It's going to break regardless of what anyone does. The Entities will pour through, the Orders will fight, and thousands will die on both sides. But eventually, the battle will end. Eventually, the surviving Entities will establish themselves or be pushed back. And when that happens, when the dust settles, the world will need someone who can manage the aftermath."
**MANAGE.** The word dripped contempt. **YOU SPEAK OF MANAGING THE ABYSS?**
"I speak of making the best of a bad situation." Kael risked another step closer. The Entity's presence pressed against him, cold and ancient, testing his spiritual defenses. "You're the Hollow King. You've been imprisoned for two hundred years, watching through cracks in the seal, learning about the world that locked you away. You know how this works. The Orders will never accept coexistence. They'll fight until they're destroyed or you're pushed back into the void. But that doesn't mean extinction is inevitable."
**YOU PROPOSE ALLIANCE.** The concept was almost incredulous. **WITH AN ABYSSAL ENTITY?**
"I propose mutual benefit. The seal breaks completely in—" Kael calculated based on the tremors still running through the ground. "—two minutes, forty seconds. The Orders are mobilizing. Their response time is approximately fifteen minutes for full deployment. That gives you less than twenty minutes before you face the combined might of Thornwick's Awakened. You could kill dozens. Hundreds, if you're lucky. But eventually, they'll overwhelm you. They'll sacrifice enough soldiers to drive you back, and you'll find yourself imprisoned again, this time with no cracks to watch through."
The Entity's form solidified slightly, its attention sharpening.
**SPEAK PLAINLY, LITTLE REAVER.**
"The soul fragments. The pieces of Entities that the Orders have harvested and sealed over the centuries." Kael's voice was steady, his mind working through the implications even as he spoke. "You know they exist. The 72 Entities, each one fragmented, each piece locked away in some Order vault or foundation stone. The seal that contains you isn't just the Great Rift. It's all of them, interconnected, each fragment reinforcing the others."
**THE THEORY IS CORRECT. CONTINUE.**
"If the seal network fails completely, those fragments will be freed. Every Entity that has been fragmented will begin to reconstitute. The Orders know this. They're preparing to harvest those fragments themselves before the Entities can reclaim them."
The Hollow King's form rippled with something that might have been recognition.
**THE SHADOW COURT'S PLAN.**
"They're not alone in wanting that power. But harvesting an Entity fragment is dangerous. The energy differential alone could destroy a Demigod. It would definitely destroy anything below Transcendent." Kael met the hollow gaze that wasn't a gaze, speaking directly to whatever intelligence resided within that ancient form. "I can harvest from the dead. I've absorbed abilities from beings at my level and above. Theoretically, if I had access to a fragment, if I could take it slowly, carefully, I could absorb it without being destroyed."
**YOU PROPOSE TO HARVEST US. TO STEAL OUR ESSENCE AS YOU STEAL FROM THE COMMON DEAD.**
"I propose to negotiate. You have pieces of yourself scattered across the world, locked away in vaults and foundations. The Orders will try to harvest those pieces before you can reclaim them. But I'm not an Order. I'm not part of their system, their hierarchy, their politics. If you gave me access to your fragments, I could harvest them without the Orders' interference. Some of the power would come to me. Some would be returned to you. We both benefit."
Silence stretched for three heartbeats. The Entity's form remained motionless, its void-geometry frozen in what might have been contemplation.
**YOU ARE EITHER VERY BRAVE OR VERY FOOLISH, LITTLE REAVER.**
"Probably both." Kael allowed himself a thin smile. "Does it matter? You have less than two minutes before the choice is made for you. Fight the Orders alone and probably die. Accept my proposal and maybe, possibly, survive with enough power to matter."
**THE SEAL WILL BREAK REGARDLESS. YOU UNDERSTAND THIS.**
"Yes."
**THE OTHER ENTITIES WILL NOT ACCEPT COMPROMISE. THEY WILL DESTROY EVERYTHING.**
"Then I'll deal with them when they emerge." Kael's hand hung at his side, close enough to touch the Entity if he reached. "But you're different. You're the Hollow King. You've been watching, learning, adapting. You know this world better than the others. That's why Zarek came here personally. That's why the Shadow Court is involved. They want you specifically."
The Entity's form rippled again. This time, Kael sensed something that might have been surprise.
**YOU KNOW OF THE SHADOW COURT'S INTEREST.**
"I know they don't do anything without reason. Zarek didn't frame me for the Crimson Chapel bombing just to get rid of an inconvenient prisoner. He wanted me alive, on the streets, developing my power in ways that would eventually make me useful to someone." Kael glanced back at Zarek, still frozen in place, watching the exchange with an expression he couldn't read. "I've been a tool my entire life. At least this time, I get to choose which master I serve."
**AND IF I REFUSE YOUR OFFER?**
"Then I fight my way out and hope I survive the next few hours." Kael's voice hardened. "I'm not asking for charity. I'm asking for a chance. You want your fragments back. I want power to survive what's coming. We can help each other, or we can be enemies. Choose."
The ground heaved. The crack widened. The void-sky above pulsed with colors that had no names, and Kael felt the moment of decision pressing against him like a physical weight.
One minute thirty seconds.
The Hollow King's form contracted, concentrating into something almost solid, almost real. It extended a tendril of shadow-light toward Kael's outstretched hand.
**AGREEABLE.**
Contact. The Soul Reaver ability activated without conscious thought, and Kael felt the Entity's essence pour into him—not all of it, not even most, but a fragment. A piece small enough to survive the transfer, ancient enough to carry power that made his Demigod stage feel like playing at being a child.
**Soul Harvest: You extracted 2,340 units of spiritual essence from Entity fragment. Core Ability Acquired: [Hollow King's Sight] — perception of spiritual energy in all forms, including hidden layers. Quality: Legendary (ancient Entity source, direct contact harvest).**
The world snapped back into focus. Kael staggered, overwhelmed by the sudden influx of power, by the alien consciousness pressing against the edges of his awareness. The Hollow King had withdrawn, its form already beginning to fade as it prepared to face the onslaught of Order forces approaching from every direction.
**THIS IS NOT THE END, LITTLE REAVER. THIS IS THE BEGINNING. WHEN YOU ARE READY, WHEN YOU HAVE GROWN STRONG ENOUGH, YOU WILL COME TO THE GREAT RIFT. YOU WILL HARVEST WHAT REMAINS. AND WE WILL DISCUSS THE TERMS OF TRUE ALLIANCE.**
The Entity flowed back into the crack, taking its fragment of power with it, leaving Kael standing in the center of chaos with his hand still extended toward empty air.
The seal broke. Not completely, not yet, but enough. The crack became a fissure, the fissure became a gateway, and through it, Kael glimpsed the Abyss—vast, hungry, waiting.
Then the Orders arrived.
---
The next twenty minutes were a blur of combat and survival. Kael didn't fight the Orders directly—that would have been suicide—but he couldn't avoid conflict entirely. The Entities that poured through the partially opened rift attacked everything that moved. Kael found himself defending survivors, harvesting from the dead, accumulating abilities faster than he could process them.
By the time the battle reached its first pause, three more Entities had been pushed back, the rift had been partially stabilized by combined Order effort, and Kael had acquired eight new abilities from the corpses of Awakened who had died around him.
He found Lyra at the edge of the destruction zone, her robes torn, her face streaked with blood that might or might not have been her own. She stood alone, Zarek having vanished at some point during the chaos.
"You talked to an Entity," she said without preamble. "You made a deal with it."
"I made a deal." Kael's voice was flat, exhausted. "Not with the Shadow Court. Not with the Orders. With something that has existed longer than human civilization and wants to survive more than it wants to destroy."
"That doesn't make it right."
"Neither does watching thousands of people die because the seal network was designed by people who never expected it to fail." He met her eyes, seeing the conflict there. "You know what's coming. The other rifts are weakening. The 72 Entities will emerge, all of them, within the next few years. The Orders can't stop them. The Shadow Court can't control them. Something else has to change."
"And that something is you." Lyra's laugh was bitter. "The Soul Reaver who harvests from the dead, now allied with the imprisonedEntities who want to reclaim their fragments. You're going to fix everything with stolen power."
"I'm going to survive. Everything else is negotiable." Kael turned away, toward the distant lights of Thornwick proper, where the citizens who had escaped the initial destruction were huddled in fear of what the night might bring. "You can report this to Zarek. Tell him I've betrayed the Shadow Court, made unapproved contact with an Entity, violated approximately seventeen Concordat protocols. It won't matter. He knew what I was going to do before I did it. That's why he's been watching."
"You're his pawn."
"Everyone is someone's pawn." Kael started walking. "The only question is whether you play the game or let the game play you."
He heard her footsteps behind him, following at a distance. He didn't look back.
The night had just begun. The seal was damaged but holding, the Entities pushed back but not destroyed, the Orders mobilizing for what would be the first of many battles to come. And somewhere in the depths beneath Thornwick, in the place where reality grew thin and the Abyss pressed close, the Hollow King waited.
Waiting for Kael to grow strong enough.
Waiting for the next harvest.
Waiting for the conversation that would determine whether humanity survived what was coming or was swept away by powers beyond comprehension.
Kael walked through the burning streets, carrying the weight of a thousand stolen souls, and wondered how many more he would need before he was strong enough to matter.
The answer, he suspected, was more than he could count.
Footsteps echoed behind him. Not Lyra's. Someone else, moving with the careful silence of training that went beyond what any Order provided.
Kael stopped. Turned. Found himself facing a woman he had never seen before, her face hidden beneath a mask that seemed to absorb light than reflect it.
"Kael Ashborn," she said. Her voice was modulated, artificial, hiding something he couldn't identify. "The Second Soul Reaver."
His blood went cold.
"I think," she continued, "we need to talk about the paradox."
She gestured, and the shadows around them deepened, forming walls that cut them off from the surrounding chaos. A private space, in the middle of a battlefield.
"The First Soul Reaver created us both," she said. "You know this?"
Kael said nothing. His mind raced through implications, trying to fit this new information into the framework of everything he thought he understood.
"The original Soul Reaver existed four hundred years ago. He perfected the ability to harvest from the dead, accumulated power beyond imagination, and then faced a choice that no being should have to make." The masked woman stepped closer. "The 72 Entities were emerging for the first time. The seal network hadn't been built yet. The world was facing extinction. He could have harvested the Entities directly, absorbed their power, become something beyond Divine, beyond anything. But he knew that absorbing that much corrupted energy would change him into something that wasn't human anymore."
The story hung in the air between them, incomplete.
"So he divided himself," she continued. "Created a system that would split his power across multiple vessels, multiple Reavers, each one carrying a fragment of what he had been. Each one unable to reach full power alone, but together, if they could find each other, if they could learn to cooperate instead of compete—"
The ground trembled. Not from the rift, but from something else. Something approaching.
"You need to decide," the woman said, her modulated voice urgent now. "The Second Soul Reaver, the one who was supposed to balance you, died three years ago. I'm what's left of her vessel. And now the paradox is collapsing. The system the First Reaver created is trying to reconcile two fragments that should never have existed at the same time. You feel it, don't you? The way your power keeps trying to tear itself apart?"
Kael did feel it. A wrongness at the core of his being, a conflict between aspects of himself he had never understood. He had assumed it was corruption, side effects of harvesting, something to be managed than solved.
"The Hollow King's fragment accelerated the flow," she said. "You won't survive another major harvest. Your soul will shatter under the weight of incompatible powers."
"How do I fix it?"
"Find the First Reaver's remains. Find where he divided himself, where the original system still exists. You can either merge with me, stabilize the paradox by combining our fragments, or—" she paused, "—you can kill me, take what I have, and hope you can survive the backlash long enough to reach the solution on your own."
The choice hung between them, stark and terrible.
"I don't know your name," Kael said.
