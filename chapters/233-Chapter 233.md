The sewers beneath Thornwick offered no mercy to those who lingered. Kael pressed himself against the dripping stone, counting the footsteps echoing down the tunnel. Three sets. Maybe four. The gas-lamps flickered with each gust of stale air, casting long shadows that danced like hungry things.
"Come out, Reaver," a voice called from the darkness. "The Court knows what you are."
The Court. Kael's grip tightened on his blade. Not the Orders—something older, something that operated from the shadows. His investigation into the cult activity had led him here, through the rotting underbelly of the city where rats the size of dogs scuttled between the pipes and the smell of decay hung thick enough to taste. Three nights of tracking. Three nights of dead ends. Someone was feeding them information about his movements.
Or herding him.
"They're pushing me toward them," he thought. Every cultist he'd found died before they could talk. Every warehouse he raided held nothing but ritual chalk and dried blood. Someone wanted him to chase shadows.
The footsteps grew closer. Kael assessed his options. Fight meant revealing his position to whoever controlled these cultists. Run meant remaining the hunted. Neither appealed.
A torch flared around the corner, its light pushing back the darkness. Three figures emerged, dressed in black robes marked with symbols that hurt to look at directly. They moved in formation—two flanking, one center. Military precision. Not random cultists.
"I know you're there, Reaver." The lead cultist's voice carried certainty. "The Veil is thinning. The Whisper King calls."
Kael's heart hammered against his ribs. They had been tracking him, not the other way around. Every move he'd made, they'd known. This was a trap.
"Come willingly," the cultist continued, "and the Hierophant may show mercy."
Kael burst from cover.
His blade sang through the air, catching the first cultist across the throat before they could raise their ritual knife. Hot blood sprayed across the stone floor. Kael used the momentum to drive his shoulder into the second cultist, sending them both crashing against the tunnel wall. A third cultist lunged from the shadows—an ordinary knife in her hand, nothing spiritual about her strike.
Kael twisted, taking the blade across his ribs. Pain flared white-hot. He didn't stop. His elbow connected with the woman's temple, and she crumpled like wet paper.
The lead cultist stood firm, hands weaving patterns in the air. Spiritual energy gathered around her fingers, coalescing into a blade of pure light. An Awakened. Third stage, at least. Her technique was sloppy, but the power behind it was undeniable.
"The Court warned us about your kind," she said. "Soul Reavers think they're above the natural order. You're an abomination."
She struck.
Kael moved faster than thought. He'd learned this technique three days ago, harvested from a cultist's dying mind. Veil-Sight. The world shifted, and he could see the threads of spiritual energy connecting everything—the woman's blade, the ley lines in the stone, the fading essence of the corpses behind him.
He ducked under her strike, drove his palm into her solar plexus. The spiritual blade flickered and died. As she doubled over, gasping, Kael grabbed her forehead and pressed his hand against her skin.
The harvest began.
Cold flooded through his fingers. The woman's eyes went wide—then blank, then empty. Information poured into Kael's mind: locations, names, ritual schedules. The cult was organized. Hierarchies existed. Cells communicated through dead drops and coded messages. Someone called the Hierophant commanded them all.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted 3.2 essence units from the remains. Skill Acquired: Veil-Sight (basic)."
The woman's body went limp. Kael let it drop, his mind reeling from the sudden influx of stolen knowledge. The cult operated under strict organization. This wasn't random worship of the Abyss—this was a military campaign.
And he had walked right into their ambush.
The wounded cultist behind him groaned. Kael spun, blade raised, only to find her crawling toward the tunnel's exit. Her hand had found a knife again—ordinary steel, but her grip was determined.
"Don't," Kael said.
She laughed, blood on her teeth. "The Hierophant knows where you are. More are coming. You killed Brother Aldric and Sister Mira. The Court will—"
She died with a whisper as Kael's blade found her spine.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted 0.8 essence units from the remains. Fragmentary Memory Acquired."
More information flooded through. A meeting. Three days from now. Underground, somewhere beneath the old cathedral. Multiple cult cells converging for a ritual of significant scale. The Hierophant would be present.
The Hierophant. Vane. The name surfaced from the harvest like a hook in flesh.
Footsteps echoed from the tunnel ahead. More of them, then. Kael sheathed his blade and ran.
He found a drainage pipe barely wide enough for a man's shoulders. He squeezed through, stone scraping against his wounds, and emerged in a secondary tunnel that smelled of rust and stagnant water. Behind him, shouting. They had found the bodies.
Kael pressed himself into an alcove, controlling his breathing, and listened to the cultists pass. Their voices carried fragments of conversation.
"—said the Reaver would come here—"
"—Hierophant's orders. Alive if possible—"
"—doesn't matter. The Veil breaks in months. Once the Great Rift opens—"
They passed. Kael waited until silence returned, then emerged into the darkness.
Months. The Veil was breaking faster than anyone knew. The cult—organized, disciplined, well-supplied—was working toward something catastrophic. And they had been tracking him specifically.
Why?
The drainage tunnel emptied into a storm canal. Kael climbed the ladder, pushed aside the grate, and emerged into the night air of Thornwick. The city sprawled before him, its gas-lamps glowing like infected wounds in the fog. Somewhere out there, the Shadow Court watched. Somewhere, the Hierophant planned.
And somewhere in that chaos, Sera was waiting.

The safehouse in Drowning District had seen better decades. Kael patched his wounds with strips torn from his own shirt, the antiseptic burning worse than the cuts themselves. The cut across his ribs was shallow but needed attention. The bruise on his shoulder from the tunnel wall would stiffen overnight.
He spread the information from the harvests across the table. Maps of Thornwick, marked with locations from the cultist memories. Names. Faction designations. Ritual calendars. The more he studied, the clearer the picture became.
The cult wasn't random. It had structure. Leaders. Communication networks. A clear agenda.
Someone had built this organization with purpose.
"Mira Vance," he said aloud, testing the name. The Shadow Court operative who had tried to recruit him in the Ashfall district. She had known about the cult's organization. She had known about the Hierophant.
She had known about him.
Kael pulled out the cipher book he'd stolen from a cultist warehouse two weeks ago. Pages of seemingly random symbols, but the harvest had given him the key. He decoded the message he had found in the dead drop three nights ago.
THE HIEROPHANT SPEAKS. SEALED ORDERS FOR ALL CELLS. THE WARDEN PREPARES THE WAY. WHEN THE VEIL THINS SUFFICIENTLY, THE RITUAL BEGINS. SEVENTY-TWO WILL RISE. THE OLD KINGDOM RETURNS.
Seventy-two. The number of sealed rifts. The number of Abyssal Entities.
The cult planned to open all of them.
Kael's stomach turned. The Abyssal Wars had nearly destroyed the world three hundred years ago. The seals were the only thing keeping reality intact. If this cult succeeded—
"The Orders would stop them," he muttered. "The Six Orders maintain the seals. They would—"
But would they? Mira had said the Orders were fractured. Political infighting. Competing agendas. The Council of Six hadn't reached agreement on anything significant in fifty years. If the cult moved carefully, strategically, the Orders might not respond in time.
Or they might be compromised. The message spoke of the "Warden preparing the way." Someone inside the established power structures, helping the cult.
A knock at the door. Three sharp raps. Then silence.
Kael reached for his blade. His hand found the hilt. He waited.
"Kael Ashborn." A woman's voice, calm and measured. "I know you're awake. I know you're armed. I also know you're wounded and exhausted. We can do this the easy way or the hard way."
"Who's asking?"
"Someone who wants to help. Open the door."
Kael considered his options. Outside meant exposure to whoever waited. Inside meant siege. The safehouse had a back entrance—a window leading to the roof—but it would take time to reach.
Time he might not have.
He opened the door.
The woman standing in the hallway wore a long coat of deep blue, the color of the Order of Dawn. Her hair was silver, cut short, and her face bore the lines of someone who had seen too much and slept too little. She held no weapon, hands visible at her sides.
"Mira Vance," Kael said.
"You remembered." She smiled, but it didn't reach her eyes. "May I come in? What I have to say shouldn't be shouted in hallways."
"You tried to recruit me for the Shadow Court."
"I tried to offer you options. The Shadow Court isn't the enemy here. They're just another player in a game with too many pieces." She paused. "May I?"
Kael stepped aside. Mira entered, her gaze sweeping the room, noting the maps, the weapons, the blood-stained bandages. Her expression didn't change.
"You've been busy," she said. "The cult's activity in the lower districts. The disturbances near the old cathedral. You've been investigating."
"I've been surviving."
"Same thing, in your position." Mira sat uninvited, pulling a chair from the corner. "You killed three of their operatives tonight. They'll be looking for you now. More than usual."
"Good."
"It's not good. The cult has resources you don't. Manpower. Spiritual technology. And they have the Hierophant." Mira leaned forward. "You know the name now. Vane."
"You know who they are?"
"I know what they are. A figurehead. Someone powerful enough to coordinate cells across three cities, wealthy enough to fund operations for decades, and smart enough to avoid direct confrontation." She paused. "But not who. The identity changes. Masks within masks. The cult has survived three purges because the Hierophant is a function, not a person."
Kael processed this. "Why tell me?"
"Because you're relevant now. Your... Abilities have drawn attention. The cult wants to capture you. Alive. They're offering significant rewards to anyone who brings you in."
"Why alive?"
Mira's expression flickered. "Soul Reavers are rare. Maybe unique. The cult has theories about what your kind can do. Theories they want to test."
"What kind of theories?"
"The kind that involve dissection and spiritual experimentation." Her voice carried no emotion, but her eyes stayed fixed on his. "I don't recommend it."
Kael's grip tightened on his blade. "You're here to warn me. Why?"
"Because I was sent."
"By who?"
"By people who want Vane stopped. People who don't care about the politics or the power plays. People who just want to prevent the end of the world." Mira reached into her coat and produced a small leather folder. "This is for you. Maps of cult meeting locations. Guard rotations. Ritual schedules. Everything we've gathered in the last six months."
She set the folder on the table beside his maps.
"Why help me? I'm a Soul Reaver. The Orders execute my kind on sight."
"The Orders are broken, Kael. Six factions squabbling over scraps while the seals crack. The Council of Six hasn't reached consensus on anything meaningful in decades. When the Veil finally breaks, they'll point fingers at each other instead of the enemy." She stood. "I'm not here for the Orders. I'm here for the world."
"And if I don't trust you?"
"You shouldn't. Trust is earned. But right now, we have a common enemy. That's enough." She moved toward the door, then paused. "Three nights from now. The old cathedral. Multiple cult cells converging. The Hierophant will be there."
"You want me to crash their party."
"I want you to be there when they try to unseal a rift. What you do after that is your choice."
Kael studied her. "And if I refuse?"
"Then the cult succeeds. Or the Orders intercept them and kill everyone including potential witnesses. Either way, the Hierophant escapes and starts again." She opened the door. "You have my card. When you decide, send word."
She left. The door closed behind her with a soft click.
Kael sat in silence for a long moment. The leather folder sat on the table, unassuming, full of information that could change everything. He opened it.
Maps. Schedules. Names. The cult's organization laid bare in ink and parchment.
His hands trembled. Not from fear. From the weight of what this meant.
The conspiracy was real. The threat was imminent. And someone wanted him involved badly enough to hand him everything he needed.

The next two days passed in a blur of preparation. Kael mapped the cathedral's interior from stolen architectural records. He memorized guard rotations. He practiced techniques harvested from dead cultists until his muscles ached.
He also learned more about the Six Orders than he ever wanted to know.
The Order of Dawn maintained the eastern seals, their members trained in light-based spiritual combat. The Iron Vanguard guarded the northern fortresses, specialists in defensive formations and siege warfare. The Arcane Spire studied the theoretical—spirit energy, seal mechanics, the nature of the Abyss itself. The Verdant Circle worked the southern territories, their connection to natural ley lines making them invaluable for maintenance. The Storm Callers patrolled the coasts, their weather-sense allowing early warning of spiritual disturbances. And the Veiled Monks served as the eyes and ears of all six, operating in shadows where others could not.
Together, they had held the world safe for three centuries. But cracks had formed. Political divisions. Resource disputes. The Orders had begun fighting each other as much as the Abyss.
The cult exploited those cracks. Corrupted officials. Bought loyalties. Created chaos that distracted from their real work.
Now they were close to success. The Veil thinned. The seals weakened. And Kael stood at the crossroads.

Night fell on the third day. Kael moved through the shadows of the old cathedral's outer walls, staying low, staying silent. Mira's information proved accurate—two guards at the main entrance, three more patrolling the perimeter. He slipped through a service door left deliberately unlocked.
Inside, the cathedral stretched vast and dark. Pillars rose toward a vaulted ceiling lost in shadow. Candles burned at distant altars, their flames flickering with unnatural colors. The air tasted of ozone and something older.
The cult had been busy. Ritual circles covered the floor, some freshly painted, others worn by decades of use. Symbols Kael recognized from his harvests—the same patterns, the same horrors. This place had been desecrated long ago.
Voices echoed from deeper within. Chanting, low and rhythmic. Dozens of voices, maybe more.
Kael moved forward.
The main nave opened into a circular chamber that had once been a baptismal font. Now it held something far worse. A pit gaped in the center, its edges carved with seal-script that should have glowed with protective energy. Instead, the script flickered, dim and dying.
The rift. Alpha-7, if the harvests were accurate. One of the most dangerous.
Figures in black robes surrounded the pit, their chanting growing louder. At the center stood a tall figure in white robes, hands raised toward the dying light above. The Hierophant. Vane.
Not a name. A title.
Kael counted heads. Twenty cultists. Maybe more in the shadows. The Hierophant radiated power—third stage, maybe fourth. Spiritual energy crackled around their form like static before a storm.
The ritual was beginning.
Kael drew his blade and stepped from the shadows.
The chanting stopped.
Twenty heads turned toward him. Twenty pairs of eyes fixed on the intruder in their sacred space.
"One man," the Hierophant said, voice carrying effortlessly. "The Soul Reaver comes to us at last."
"You've been expecting me."
"We've been preparing for you." Vane turned, and Kael got his first look at the face behind the title. Old. Weathered. Eyes that had seen too much and forgotten none of it. "The Reaver who walks among the living. We wondered when you would find us."
"Find you? You've been leading me here."
"Leading you. Testing you. And now, you come bearing gifts." Vane gestured at the assembled cultists. "Twenty souls for the taking. Twenty essences to add to your collection. All you have to do is take them."
Kael's grip tightened on his blade. "What's the catch?"
"The catch is the rift." Vane smiled, and there was nothing human in it. "In moments, it will open. The Entity beyond will emerge. And you, Soul Reaver, will be consumed."
"Why tell me?"
"Because you're going to try to stop it anyway. You're that kind of fool." Vane raised their hands higher. "The ritual begins. Brother Magisters, restrain our guest."
The cultists moved.
Kael didn't wait. He launched himself at the nearest robed figure, blade singing through the air. The cultist parried with a ritual knife, sparks flying where steel met steel. Behind them, two more cultists charged with weapons drawn.
Kael duck under a sweeping blade, rolled between legs, came up driving his elbow into a kidney. One cultist fell. He parried a strike from the left, riposted, opened a throat. Another fell.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted 2.1 essence units from the remains. Skill Acquired: Rite of Binding (basic)."
Information flooded through. The cultists weren't just random believers. They were Awakened, each one, trained specifically for this ritual. Soldiers in a holy war.
Two more charged. Kael met them with cold efficiency, his blade finding gaps in their defenses. Blood sprayed across the ancient stones. Bodies fell.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted 1.8 essence units from the remains. Skill Acquired: Blood Offering (basic)."
"Soul Harvest: You extracted 2.4 essence units from the remains. Ability Acquired: Spirit Sight (temporary)."
The world blazed with spiritual light. Kael could see everything—the threads of power connecting each cultist, the ley lines flowing beneath the floor, the rift pulsing at the chamber's heart like a diseased heart.
And he could see Vane, standing untouched at the center, watching him with cold amusement.
"Enough," the Hierophant said.
Spiritual energy exploded outward. Kael was hurled backward, slamming into a pillar hard enough to crack stone. Pain flared through his shoulder. His vision swam.
When it cleared, the remaining cultists had formed a circle around the rift, their chanting resumes. The pit pulsed with sickly light. Cracks spread across the seal-script.
"It's already open," Vane said, stepping closer. "Alpha-7 has been weakening for months. Tonight, we complete the work. Tonight, we bring the Abyss into the Material."
"You're insane."
"I'm patient." Vane's hand closed around Kael's throat, lifting him off the ground. Spiritual energy burned where the Hierophant touched him, searching, probing. "You're more interesting than I expected. Stronger. More resilient. The theories were correct—you are something new."
"New?"
"Every Soul Reaver before you died within months of Awakening. Corruption. Degradation. The soul can't sustain what you've become." Vane's grip tightened. "But you survive. Why?"
Kael choked, unable to answer.
"It doesn't matter. Your secrets will be extracted soon enough." Vane dropped him, stepping back toward the rift. "Watch, Reaver. Watch as the world changes."
The chanting reached a crescendo. The pit's light intensified, blinding. Something massive stirred beyond the Veil, drawn by the cult's calling.
Kael forced himself to his feet. His body screamed in protest. Blood ran from his temple. But he was still standing.
He grabbed the nearest corpse—a cultist killed in the initial assault—and pressed his palm to the cooling flesh.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted 4.6 essence units from the remains. Memory Fragment Acquired."
Knowledge poured through: the ritual's focal point, the sigil carved into Vane's staff, the weakness in the seal that the cult had been exploiting. If Kael could destroy the staff, the ritual would collapse.
He had seconds to act.
Kael threw himself toward Vane, not at the Hierophant but at the staff. His blade arced downward. Steel met wood—and bounced.
Spiritual energy crackled around the weapon, protecting it. Vane laughed.
"Did you think it would be that easy?" The Hierophant's hand caught Kael's wrist, twisting. Bone ground against bone. "You were meant to die here, Reaver. Your essence feeding the rift. Your power completing what we started."
Kael's grip slipped. The blade clattered to the floor.
"Any last words?"
"Yes." Kael's free hand found the spectral knife at his belt—the weapon harvested from the cultist in the sewers. The blade hummed with stolen power.
He drove it into Vane's side.
The Hierophant screamed. Spiritual energy discharged in a blinding flash, throwing them both backward. Kael landed hard, the knife still clutched in his hand. Vane crumpled beside him, the staff rolling away across the stones.
The chanting faltered. The cultists looked at their fallen leader, uncertain.
Kael didn't hesitate. He grabbed the staff with both hands and *pulled*. Spiritual energy tore through him as he forced his will against the weapon's protections. The sigil blazed, resisted—then shattered.
The rift screamed.
Reality bent around the pit, the Veil tearing as something tried to push through. Kael saw it for a instant—a shape of pure darkness, vast and hungry—before the staff collapsed into dust.
The ritual broke. The cultists fled in panic, scrambling over each other to escape the collapsing circle. The rift pulsed once, twice—then sealed itself, the remaining light fading back to darkness.
Silence.
Kael lay on the cold stones, staring at the vaulted ceiling. His body felt like it had been trampled by horses. Every breath hurt. His wrist hung at an unnatural angle.
But he was alive.
And the Hierophant—
He forced himself to turn, searching for Vane's body.
Empty space. Blood on the stones, but no corpse. The Hierophant had vanished.
"Escaped," Kael muttered. Of course. Someone that powerful wouldn't die in a collapsed ritual.
Footsteps echoed from the cathedral entrance. Multiple sets, moving with purpose. Not cultists—the rhythm was wrong. These were soldiers.
Kael grabbed his blade and pushed himself upright. The world tilted. He caught himself against the pillar.
Torchlight flooded through the chamber doors. Figures in blue and silver entered, weapons drawn. The Order of Dawn.
"Hold," a woman's voice commanded. "I see him. He's wounded."
Kael recognized her. Mira Vance, standing at the head of a dozen armed Awakened. Her expression was unreadable.
"The ritual is broken," she said, approaching slowly. "The rift is sealed. Well done."
"You followed me."
"I followed the cult. You were a useful piece on the board." She stopped three feet away, studying him. "You're injured."
"I'll live."
"You'll live. For now." Mira gestured, and two soldiers moved forward with restraints. "I'm afraid I have to take you into custody. A Soul Reaver attacking cultists in a sacred site won't look good to the Council. There will be questions."
"Questions I can't answer."
"Exactly." Mira's voice dropped, meant only for him. "Come quietly. I have a plan."
Kael considered his options. He was wounded, exhausted, outnumbered. Fighting would mean death or worse. Trusting Mira meant walking into unknown complications.
But Vane was still out there. The cult was still organized. And Sera was still waiting.
"Fine," he said. "But if this is a trap—"
"It's not a trap. It's a negotiation." Mira accepted his blade as he surrendered it. "The Council will want answers. I suggest you have some ready."
The soldiers bound his wrists with spiritual suppression cuffs. They chafed against his skin, dulling his connection to the power that lived inside him.
Kael let them lead him away.

The cell was cold, damp, and deep beneath the Order of Dawn's fortress. Kael sat on the stone bench, staring at the wall, running through everything that had happened.
The conspiracy was larger than he imagined. The cult operated across multiple cities, coordinated by a Hierophant who wore masks instead of faces. The Shadow Court watched from the shadows. The Orders were fractured, compromised, too busy fighting each other to fight the real enemy.
And he was caught in the middle, a Soul Reaver in a world that would execute him if it knew what he truly was.
A sound at the cell door. Heavy bolts sliding. The door opened to reveal Mira, alone, carrying a tray of food.
"You look terrible," she said, setting the tray on the floor.
"I've had better nights."
"The Hierophant escaped. We found blood but no body."
"I know."
"Vane has been operating for decades. This is the closest anyone's come to stopping them." Mira sat across from him, her back against the cold bars. "You did well."
"I almost died."
"Almost doesn't count." She pushed the tray closer. "Eat. You'll need your strength."
Kael took the bread, tearing off a piece. "What's the plan?"
"The Council will convene tomorrow. They'll want to know how a Transcendent-stage Reaver disrupted a ritual that should have required an entire strike team." Mira's eyes glittered in the torchlight. "They'll also want to know why you didn't die when you should have. Why you can do what you do."
"And you'll tell them?"
"I'll tell them what they need to hear. That you're an asset, not a threat. That your abilities could help us fight the cult. That executing you would waste valuable resources." She paused. "It might work."
"Might?"
"The Council is divided. Some believe Reavers are abomination. Others see opportunity." Mira leaned forward. "Your survival depends on how tomorrow goes."
Kael finished the bread, chewing slowly. "What do you want in return?"
"Cooperation. Intelligence. Your abilities, when needed." She stood, moving toward the door. "And information about where you learned to do what you do."
"The Soul Reaver who came before me. The one who left the tablets in the Sepulcher."
"You know about the Sepulcher?"
"Everyone with half a brain knows about the Sepulcher. What's inside is another matter."
Mira smiled thinly. "The Council will want to discuss that as well."
She left. The bolts slid home. Darkness returned.
Kael lay back on the bench, staring at the ceiling, and thought about everything he had learned.
The conspiracy's scope was vast. The cult had been building toward this for decades, maybe longer. Vane commanded cells across multiple cities, coordinating toward a goal that could unmake the world. The Shadow Court watched everything, waiting for opportunities. The Orders squabbled while the Veil thinned.
And somewhere in that chaos, a Reaver had left a trail of secrets in the Sepulcher. Secrets about the first Soul Reaver. About what they truly were.
Kael closed his eyes.
Tomorrow would bring answers. Or execution. Or something worse.
He would be ready.

The Council chamber was vast, circular, dominated by a table where six figures sat in judgment. Kael stood in the center, unbound but surrounded by guards. His wounds had been treated, his clothes replaced with simple gray robes. He looked like what he was: a prisoner awaiting fate.
Mira stood among the observers, her expression carefully neutral.

"Kael Ashborn."

Murmurs from the observers. Thane's expression didn't change.

"The Council will now hear testimony from Grand Inquisitor Vance."
