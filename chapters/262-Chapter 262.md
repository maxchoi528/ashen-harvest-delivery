Kael's fingers tightened around the hilt of his blade. The corpse at his feet still radiated warmth, its eyes frozen in the final moment of terror. He crouched low, pressing his palm against the cooling flesh.
A pulse of golden light seeped from the dead man's chest into his palm, settling behind his ribs. A new instinct bloomed in his mind—alleyways, shortcuts, the hidden paths of Thornwick's undercity.
He rose, wiping his hand on his coat. Three days since his last harvest. The hunger never came, but the world remembered what he was every time his hand touched the dead.
"Kael." Sera's voice cut through the fog of the alley. She stood at the corner, her posture rigid, her face pale in the gaslight. "We have a problem."
He crossed the narrow street in four strides. "What kind?"
"The kind that gets us killed if we stay here." She grabbed his arm, pulling him toward the shadow of a collapsed awning. "Vael's been meeting with someone. I followed him last night to the old foundry near the Dockside district."
Cold sweat prickled along Kael's spine, his jaw clenching tight enough to ache. Vael. His most trusted scout, the man who had saved his life twice during the Ashwick affair. The ally who knew every safehouse, every route, every secret Kael had accumulated over the past months.
"Who was he meeting?" His voice came out flat, controlled.
"That's the problem." Sera's eyes darted to the street behind them. "I don't know. The person never showed their face. But Vael left with a satchel that wasn't his, and when he came back to his quarters, I saw him counting soul-essence vials. A lot of them."
The numbers clicked into place in Kael's mind. Vael's expenses had always been modest. He claimed to live on inheritances and small jobs. But Kael had never questioned it. He hadn't thought to question it.
"Show me."
They moved through Thornwick's streets like ghosts, Sera leading him through alleys that twisted and doubled back on themselves. The city had grown darker in the past months, the gaslights failing faster than the Guild could repair them, shadows pooling in doorways and under bridges.
The foundry emerged from the fog like a skeleton. Its brick walls were scorched black, windows shattered, the iron hanging open on one hinge. Kael had passed this place a dozen times and never thought to look inside.
"Here." Sera stopped at the entrance, her breath misting in the cold air. "He went to the basement level. That's where the meeting happened."
Kael drew his blade and descended. The stairs groaned under his weight. Below, the air grew thick with the smell of rust and something else—something organic, like old blood.
The basement had been converted into a command center. Maps covered the walls, connected by strings of red thread. Photographs, documents, a brass signal-box wired to cables that snaked through cracks in the wall toward the street above. Kael moved through the space, his eyes catching details.
A roster of names, each one circled in black ink. He recognized three: his own, Sera's, and a name he hadn't heard in months—Aldric.
"Looks like someone's been busy." Sera stood at the far wall, staring at something Kael couldn't see from his position. "Come look at this."
He joined her. The wall was covered in a massive schematic—Thornwick's layout, but marked with symbols he didn't recognize. Small circles at key intersections. Lines connecting them in patterns that suggested timing, coordination.
The symbols matched the glyphs Kael had seen on cultist corpses.
"They're planning something." Sera's voice was barely a whisper. "Something coordinated. Every marked location is a convergence point."
"For what?"
"I don't know yet. But look—" She pointed to a cluster of symbols near the city's center. "That's the old Cathedral district. The place where the Great Rift seal is located."
The pieces clicked together in Kael's mind with sickening clarity. The cult activity that had seemed random was organized. The timing of attacks, the locations chosen, the specific targets—all of it was part of a larger operation. An operation Vael had been facilitating.
"He's been feeding them information." The words tasted like ash. "Everything we know, everything we've planned, all of it goes straight to them."
The sound of footsteps on stairs froze them both. Kael shoved Sera behind a stack of crates, pressing himself against the wall. The footsteps were slow, deliberate. A single set, followed by another.
"Topside is clear," a voice said from above. "But the patrols are increasing. We should move the operation."
"Patience." A second voice, colder, more measured. "The seal weakens by the day. When it falls, we'll need everything in position."
Kael recognized that voice. He heard it in his nightmares, in the memories that bled through after every harvest. Prime Zarek.
The footsteps descended. Two figures entered the basement, their forms barely visible in the dim light filtering through the broken windows above. Zarek walked with the careful grace of a predator, his coat immaculate despite the grime of the city. Beside him stood another man—tall, gaunt, his face obscured by a hood.
"The Reaver is becoming a problem." Zarek examined the maps with detached interest. "His interference has delayed us. Every corpse he harvests is one less piece of evidence for the Orders to find."
"The Shadow Court will handle him." The hooded figure's voice carried no emotion. "They've been watching him since the Ashwick affair. When the time comes, they'll make their move."
The muscles across Kael's shoulders locked, his breath thinning to nothing. The Shadow Court. The organization that operated in the spaces between Orders, the deniable arm that every faction denied and everyone feared. They were watching him. They had always been watching him.
"Not yet." Zarek's voice dropped. "The Reaver serves a purpose. When the seals break, we'll need someone who can harvest the Entities. Someone who can take their power before it destabilizes reality."
"You want to control him?"
"I want to use him. There's a difference." Zarek turned toward the exit, his back to Kael's hiding spot. "The Sepulcher tablets mentioned a vessel. A Reaver strong enough to contain an Entity's essence without being destroyed. If our sources are correct, this one might be it."
The two men ascended the stairs. Their footsteps faded, leaving Kael and Sera in suffocating silence.
Sera's hand found his arm. Her fingers dug into his sleeve. "Kael. They think you're... They're going to try to use you as a container for an Entity."
He knew. He had known since the moment Zarek's voice echoed in the basement. The framing at Ashwick, the cultists that always seemed to know his location, the pattern of attacks that had kept him moving instead of staying to build strength—all of it had been manipulation. He was a piece on a board he couldn't see, moved by hands he hadn't identified.
"We need to leave." His voice came out steady despite the taste of copper at the back of his throat. "If they find us here—"
"They already know you're here."
The voice came from behind them. Kael spun, blade raised, and found himself facing Vael. His former ally stood at the basement's rear exit, a pistol in one hand and a knife in the other. His face was a mask of regret and determination.
"I hoped you'd stay away." Vael's voice cracked. "I tried to warn you off the Ashwick investigation. I fed you false information to keep you away from the foundry. But you just kept digging."
"Vael—"
"Don't." Vael stepped closer. "You want to know about the cult activity? About Zarek's plans? I'll tell you everything. But you need to understand something first." His eyes glistened in the dim light. "I didn't have a choice. They have my daughter. If I don't cooperate, she dies."
The confession hung in the air between them. Kael felt Sera tense beside him, her own weapon drawn but not raised.
"Where is she?" Kael asked.
"I don't know. That's the truth. They took her six months ago, after I refused their first offer. They send me proof of life every few weeks to remind me what's at stake." Vael's hand holding the pistol began to shake. "I've been feeding them information about you, about your movements, about your abilities. I know what you've been doing with the corpses. I know about the Soul Reaver."
"You've been reporting to the Shadow Court."
"Both of them." Vael laughed bitterly. "Zarek has his cultists, but the Shadow Court operates independently. They're the ones who approached me first. They said they wanted to protect you, guide you toward your true potential. I believed them because I was desperate."
Kael sheathed his blade. The motion was deliberate, a calculated risk. "If you wanted to protect me, why the deception?"
"Because the truth is worse than the lies." Vael lowered his weapons, his shoulders slumping. "The Shadow Court isn't trying to control you, Kael. They're trying to recreate you. The first Soul Reaver was a prototype, an experiment that succeeded beyond anyone's expectations. When he died, the Orders tore his body apart and scattered his remains across the continent. They thought destroying the physical vessel would end the bloodline."
"But it didn't."
"No." Vael met his eyes. "The Soul Reaver ability doesn't come from the body. Every few generations, a vessel is born with the potential to awaken it. The Shadow Court has been tracking those births for two hundred years. Waiting for the right candidate."
"And I'm the right candidate."
"You're more than that." Vael reached into his coat and withdrew a folded document. "The Shadow Court knows more than anyone realizes. And they know something else—something that Zarek doesn't understand yet."
He handed the document to Kael. The paper was old, brittle, covered in handwriting that looked decades old.
"The cult's mass unsealing isn't just about breaking the seals. It's about creating a conduit. Zarek wants to merge an Entity with a human vessel, but the ritual requires a specific type of energy. Soul-essence harvested from Awakened, specifically from those who've been Reaved."
Sera made a strangled sound. "They're hunting other Awakened. Killing them so their essence can be used to fuel the ritual."
"Worse." Vael shook his head. "They're hunting Awakened who have been harvested by a Soul Reaver. The combination of death-by-Reaver plus the harvested energy creates a specific resonance. Zarek needs dozens of those resonances to complete the ritual."
Kael stared at the document in his hands. Names. Dozens of names, each one crossed out with a date beside it. The most recent crossouts were from the past month—Awakened who had died under mysterious circumstances, whose bodies had been found with no signs of external trauma.
The pattern matched the rumors he had heard. Kael had assumed those deaths were the work of the cult, mindless slaughter in service of the Abyss. But this was different. This was precision. This was hunting.
"Why tell me this?" Kael's voice came out hard. "Why betray your handlers now?"
"Because they're going to kill my daughter anyway." Vael's composure cracked, tears streaming down his face. "I overheard them talking two days ago. They're not going to release her. They never were. She was leverage to ensure my cooperation, and once I served my purpose..." He couldn't finish the sentence.
Kael understood. He had been a weapon, and now Vael was a weapon with no more use. The Shadow Court would discard them both once the plan reached its conclusion.
"How many Awakened have been harvested by Reavers in the past year?"
"Forty-three that I know of." Vael's voice was hollow. "Most died in accidents, skirmishes with cultists, Order missions gone wrong. A few were executed by the Orders themselves for crimes they didn't commit. The Orders don't know what they're doing. They think they're protecting the world by eliminating Reavers, but they're just creating more fuel for Zarek's ritual."
The revelation reframed everything Kael thought he understood about his place in the world. The Orders hunted him not because of what he was, but because of what they feared he might become. The cult targeted him not because he was a threat, but because he was a necessary component of their plan. Even the Shadow Court, who claimed to want to protect him, viewed him as an object to be manipulated.
"I can give you the location of the Shadow Court's primary operations hub." Vael straightened, wiping his face with his sleeve. "I can provide you with names, schedules, security measures. Everything I know. In exchange, I want your help finding my daughter before they kill her."
Kael looked at Sera. She gave the faintest nod.
"You've been lying to me for months." Kael stepped closer to Vael. "You've been feeding information to people who want to use me as a vessel for an Abyssal Entity. You've helped them track my movements, predict my actions, set up the Ashwick frame job."
"I know."
"If I help you find your daughter, what guarantee do I have that you won't betray me again?"
Vael reached into his coat and withdrew a small vial. The liquid inside glowed faintly, a deep amber color that pulsed in time with his heartbeat.
"Soul-essence. Highly refined, worth a fortune on the Veiled Market." He pressed it into Kael's hand. "This is everything I have. It's also a tracker. The Shadow Court can locate anyone who carries refined essence within miles. If I betray you again, you'll know exactly where I am."
Kael studied the vial, then pocketed it. The weight of it settled against his hip like a promise.
"Show me the hub. We go tonight."

The three of them ascended from the basement, emerging into Thornwick's fog-choked streets. The city felt different now, its shadows deeper, its secrets closer to the surface. Every passing stranger could be a cultist, an agent of the Shadow Court, a hunter tracking Awakened for slaughter.
"Wait." Sera stopped at the mouth of an alley, her hand raised. "Someone's watching us."
Kael scanned the surrounding rooftops. Nothing moved in the gaslight. No figures crouched in the windows or lurked in the doorways. But his instincts screamed danger, a primal awareness that had kept him alive through countless harvests.
"From where?"
"Everywhere." Sera's voice was barely audible. "Can't you feel it? The air itself is watching."
Kael reached out with his senses, pushing past the surface layer of reality into the hidden depth beneath. The world shimmered, edges blurring, and for a moment he saw them—dozens of faint outlines, humanoid shapes that weren't there, clustered on every rooftop, pressed against every wall.
Entities. Or the echoes of Entities, remnants of their presence bleeding through weakened seals. They weren't attacking, weren't moving. They were watching, waiting.
"They're not here for us." Vael's voice shook. "They're waiting for something else. The seal is weakening faster than anyone predicted. Within days, the first rift will open."
Kael stared at the invisible watchers, feeling their attention turn toward him. The weight of it pressed against his consciousness like a hand closing around his skull.
"Let them watch." He started walking, pulling Sera and Vael with him. "We have work to do."
---
The hub occupied the lower levels of an abandoned textile mill, its true entrance hidden behind a false wall in what appeared to be a common laundry facility. Vael led them through a series of tunnels that twisted beneath the city's foundations, past sealed doors and checkpoints that opened at his touch.
"The main chamber is ahead." Vael's voice echoed off the damp stone. "They keep the most sensitive records there. Names, locations, the details of the ritual Zarek plans to perform."
"And your daughter?"
"Probably held in the cells below. I was never permitted to visit, but I heard screams from that direction during my last meetings."
They reached a heavy iron door, its surface covered in wards that pulsed with faint light. Vael produced a key and inserted it, then paused.
"There's something you should know before we go in. The Shadow Court answers to a figure called the Whisper King. He's the one who designed the plan, who identified you as the ideal vessel, who has been pulling strings from the shadows for longer than anyone realizes."
"The Whisper King." Kael had heard the name before, whispered in the darkest corners of Thornwick's underworld. A figure who supposedly commanded the Shadow Court, who had orchestrated the fall of three Orders and the rise of the cult movement. No one knew his true identity, not even those who served him directly.
"I don't know who he is." Vael turned the key. "But I know what he wants. He wants to become a god. And you, Kael, are the final piece he needs to make that happen."
The door swung open.
The chamber beyond was vast, its ceiling lost in darkness, its walls lined with shelves containing thousands of sealed jars. Each jar contained something that glowed faintly—soul-essence, refined and purified, enough power to fuel a small Order for generations.
At the room's center stood a single pedestal, and on the pedestal rested a tablet made of black stone. Ancient symbols covered its surface, pulsing with light that seemed to come from within the rock itself.
Kael stepped forward, drawn by a pull he could neither name nor resist. The stone hummed at his approach, its glow intensifying.
"The Sepulcher tablet." Vael's voice was distant, almost reverent. "The one the first Soul Reaver created."
"And the Whisper King?" Kael's eyes never left the tablet.
"Plans to take it for himself." A new voice echoed through the chamber, deep and resonant, carrying the weight of centuries. "Which is why I'm pleased you've arrived ahead of schedule."
Kael spun, blade drawn. A figure stood at the chamber's far entrance, tall and gaunt, dressed in robes that seemed to absorb the light around them. His face was obscured by a hood, but his eyes—his eyes glowed with the same amber light as the soul-essence vials.
"You must be the Whisper King." Kael's grip on his blade tightened, knuckles blanching white. "Vael's mysterious handler. The puppet master behind Zarek's cult."
"Guilty as charged." The figure inclined his head. "Though I prefer to think of myself as a collector of rare artifacts. And you, Kael Ashborn, are the rarest artifact of all."
Behind him, Kael heard Sera and Vael shifting into position. The exits were sealed, the walls too smooth to climb, the darkness pressing in from all sides. They were trapped.
"What do you want?"
"The same thing I've wanted for two hundred years." The Whisper King stepped forward, his movements fluid, almost boneless. "To finish what the first Soul Reaver started. To unlock the full potential of what you are. To become something more than human."
"The ritual. Using an Entity as a vessel."
"Using you as the conduit." The Whisper King spread his arms, and the shadows in the chamber surged toward him, drawn to his presence like iron filings to a magnet. "The Reaver ability is not only about harvesting from the dead. It is about channeling the fundamental energy of existence itself. With the proper vessel, the proper ritual, and the proper sacrifice, a single Awakened could contain the essence of an Entity without being destroyed."
"And then?"
"And then I would become divine." The Whisper King's voice carried no arrogance, only certainty. "The Entities are fragments of the Abyss itself, raw spiritual energy given form and consciousness. By merging with one, I would transcend the limitations of mortal existence. I would become eternal."
Kael's teeth clenched until his jaw ached. The Whisper King wasn't lying. He genuinely believed he could achieve godhood through the ritual. And worse, he might be right.
"The Awakened you've been killing. The ones harvested by Reavers before they died."
"Each one was a tuning fork." The Whisper King nodded. "Their harvested essence created a specific resonance, a frequency that prepares the world for the final merger. When Zarek's ritual completes, when the seals break and the Entities pour through, I will be waiting. And I will take what has always been destined for me."
The chamber trembled. Dust fell from somewhere above, and the glow of the soul-essence jars intensified, their light pulsing in synchronized rhythm.
"The seals are breaking faster than projected." The Whisper King glanced upward with something like satisfaction. "Zarek's cult has been busy. Within the hour, the first rift will open. The Abyss will begin bleeding into the Material, and the world as you know it will end."
"Then why tell me all this?" Kael demanded. "Why not just take me when I walked through the door?"
"Because you're more valuable alive and willing than dead and forced." The Whisper King smiled, and the expression sent ice crawling down Kael's spine. "A coerced vessel can channel an Entity's power, but it cannot contain it. The merger requires acceptance. Desire. The vessel must want to become divine, or the ritual will fail."
"And you think I want that?"
"I think you're tired of being hunted. Tired of running. Tired of watching the world fail while you struggle to survive." The Whisper King stepped closer. "I offer you power beyond imagination. The strength to protect everyone you care about. The ability to reshape reality itself. All you have to do is accept."
The air in the chamber grew thick, charged with potential. Kael felt the pull of the tablet, felt the whisper of the Abyss in his blood, felt the weight of two hundred years of planning pressing down on his shoulders.
He thought of Sera, of Vael's daughter, of every innocent who would die if the seals broke. He thought of the Orders, corrupt and complacent, too busy fighting each other to notice the apocalypse approaching.
He thought of the first Soul Reaver, the original vessel, the prototype that had birthed a bloodline.
"Why me?" The question escaped before he could stop it. "Why am I the right candidate?"
The Whisper King hesitated. For the first time since the conversation began, something flickered in his expression—uncertainty, calculation.
"Because your Awakening was triggered by proximity to a dying Entity, making your spiritual signature resonate with the Abyss. And because you carry something in your blood that shouldn't be there. A fragment, preserved across generations, waiting for the right vessel to activate it."
Kael's hand went to his chest, feeling his own heartbeat. A fragment. A piece of someone else, hidden inside him since birth.
"Accept my offer." The Whisper King extended his hand. "Accept your destiny. Or watch the world burn, knowing you could have stopped it."
The chamber shook again, harder this time. Somewhere above them, glass shattered, and the sound of screaming echoed through the tunnels.
Kael looked at Sera. She stood ready, blade drawn, eyes blazing with defiance. She shook her head.
He looked at Vael. The man was pale, trembling, but his eyes held a desperate hope.
Kael turned back to the Whisper King.
"I have a counter-offer."
The Whisper King's expression flickered. "I'm listening."
Kael's hand closed around the hilt of his blade. "The Shadow Court has been watching me for months. You know my movements, my habits, my abilities. What you don't know is what I've harvested. The skills, the memories, the fragments I've collected from every corpse I've touched."
He reached into himself, grasping for the power he had accumulated. The stolen abilities, the harvested essence, the instincts that had belonged to dead men and women.
Nothing happened.
The power slipped through his fingers like water through a cracked basin. His breath came faster, his heart hammering against his ribs. The chamber felt smaller, the walls pressing inward. He tried again, reaching deeper, and felt something twist inside his chest—a knot of resistance, as though his own body refused to obey.
The Whisper King tilted his head. "You're trying to channel. Interesting. You haven't learned how, have you? You've been hoarding fragments with no understanding of what they're for."
Kael's jaw locked. He reached again, forcing his will against the wall inside himself. Pain lanced through his temples, bright and sharp, and the fragments shifted—but refused to align. It was like gripping broken glass, every edge cutting deeper the harder he squeezed.
"Stop fighting it." The Whisper King's voice dropped to a murmur. "You're breaking yourself against a wall you don't understand. Accept the ritual, and I'll teach you to use what you carry. Refuse, and you'll die with those fragments rattling inside you like dead things in a box."
Blood welled at the back of Kael's throat. He spat it onto the stone floor. His vision swam, the chamber tilting, the amber glow of the essence jars bleeding together into streaks of fire.
Sera moved first. She lunged at the Whisper King from the side, her blade arcing toward his exposed flank. Shadows erupted around him in a defensive barrier, catching her strike with a sound like a bell cracking. The impact threw her backward, her body slamming into a shelf of jars. Glass shattered. Essence sprayed across the floor in a wave of amber light.
Vael raised his pistol and fired. The shot passed through the Whisper King's form as though it had struck smoke. The figure didn't flinch.
"Enough." The Whisper King raised one hand, and shadows surged outward in a flat wave. It struck Kael in the chest, lifting him off his feet and hurling him against the pedestal. His back struck stone. The tablet toppled from its surface and clattered to the floor.
The impact drove the air from his lungs. Kael slid down, gasping, his blade still clutched in one hand. The fragments inside him churned, unstable, sending lances of heat through his veins. He tried to stand and his legs buckled. The floor was cold against his cheek.
The Whisper King approached, his footsteps unhurried. "You see? You're not ready. You carry power you can't access, can't shape, can't control. You're a vessel with cracks running through it. One more push and you'll shatter."
Kael's fingers closed around the fallen tablet's edge. The stone was warm, almost alive, and the symbols on its face rearranged themselves at his touch. A pulse of recognition ran through his palm, up his arm, and into his chest—where it met the churning fragments and found purchase.
The knot inside him loosened.
Not all at once. Not cleanly. It tore, fiber by fiber, like rope fraying under strain. Each thread that snapped sent fire through his nerves. He gasped, his grip on the tablet tightening until his knuckles cracked. The fragments inside him shifted—not aligning, not yet, but moving, turning, responding to something in the stone that spoke the same language they did.
The Whisper King stopped. "What are you—"
Kael drove the tablet's edge into the floor and pushed himself up. His arms shook. Blood trickled from his nose, his ears, the corner of his mouth. His vision doubled, tripled, the amber glow of the chamber refracting into splinters of light.
He didn't try to channel. He didn't try to control it. He let the fragments move, let them find their own arrangement, let the tablet's resonance do what his will couldn't.
The Whisper King's eyes widened. "Impossible. You shouldn't be able to—"
Kael crossed the distance between them in a single stride and drove his blade through the Whisper King's chest.
The shadows around the figure screamed—a sound with no voice, no breath, a vibration that shook the jars on their shelves. The Whisper King's hood fell back, revealing a face that was aged, weathered, familiar in a way that made the ground tilt beneath Kael's feet. But he didn't recognize it. He had never seen this man before. The familiarity was something else, something that tugged at the fragment in his blood.
The Whisper King's form dissolved. Shadows poured from the wound in his chest, unraveling upward like smoke. His amber eyes dimmed, flickered, and went dark.
"This isn't over." His voice came from everywhere and nowhere, already fading. "The ritual will complete. The seals will break. And when they do—"
He was gone.
Kael stood alone in the center of the chamber, his blade dripping shadow. His hands were steady, but his breath came in ragged pulls. The fragments inside him had settled into a new configuration—not comfortable, not whole, but no longer fighting. The tablet hummed against his palm.
Sera pushed herself upright among the shattered jars, blood running from a cut on her forehead. Vael stood frozen, his pistol still raised, his face blank with shock.
The chamber trembled. A crack split the ceiling, running from wall to wall, and through it poured a light that wasn't light—a deep, pulsing violet that pressed against the eyes like a bruise. The sound that accompanied it was worse: a low, resonant hum that Kael felt in his teeth, in his bones, in the spaces between his thoughts.
The rift was opening.
Glass jars exploded across the chamber as the pressure intensified. Sera staggered. Vael dropped his pistol, his hands going to his ears.
Kael looked down at the tablet in his hands. The symbols had rearranged themselves again—new patterns, new configurations, lines of text that pulsed with the same violet light bleeding through the crack above.
He tucked the tablet inside his coat and turned toward the tunnel.
"The Cathedral district," he said. His voice sounded wrong in his own ears—layered, as though someone else spoke beneath his words. "That's where the primary seal is located. That's where Zarek will be."
Sera wiped the blood from her eyes. "How do you—"
"The tablet. It's showing me." He paused, his hand pressing against his chest. The fragments shifted beneath his ribs, restless, drawn toward the violet light the way a compass needle turns north. "It's showing me everything."
They fled through the tunnels, the Whisper King's hub collapsing behind them. Stone groaned and split. The violet light followed them, bleeding through cracks in the walls, pooling in the passages like something alive.
When they burst into Thornwick's streets, the city was already tearing itself apart. Citizens ran through fog-choked alleys, their screams bouncing off gaslit walls. Above them, the sky had turned the same impossible violet, pulsing in time with the hum that vibrated through every cobblestone, every gutter, every gaslamp.
Kael looked east, toward the Cathedral district. Through the gap between two leaning tenements, he could see it—a column of darkness rising from the old Cathedral's spire, thick as a tower, rotating with slow, terrible purpose.
The tablet pulsed against his chest. The fragments inside him pulled toward it.
Sera grabbed his arm. "Kael. Your eyes."
He touched his face. His fingertips came away wet—not with blood, but with something that glowed faintly amber in the violet light.
Vael pointed a shaking hand toward the Cathedral. "That's where it's starting. The Great Rift. The primary seal—"
A sound split the air. Not the hum, not the screaming. Something deeper. A tearing, like cloth ripped from end to end, but the cloth was the sky itself and the rip stretched from horizon to horizon.
The column of darkness above the Cathedral widened.
Something moved inside it.
Kael's hand found his blade. The fragments in his chest burned, pressing against his ribs, straining toward that darkness like a hound pulling against its leash.
He took a step forward.
Then another.
The tablet's symbols blazed against his skin, and the night tore open.
