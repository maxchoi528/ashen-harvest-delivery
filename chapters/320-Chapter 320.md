The flesh had already begun to stiffen beneath the torn surcoat, rigor locking the dead man's arms at awkward angles. Blood had pooled in the low hollow of the collarbone, dark and congealed, and when Kael's weight shifted the body, something inside—it might have been a rib, might have been something worse—grated against something else with a wet, crunching sound that vibrated up through his knees.

A swordsman's refinement. A dead swordsman's refinement, now his.
More enemies approached from the northern ridge. He stood and moved toward them.
The battlefield of Ashenmoor stretched for miles, a wasteland of ash and char where the Orders had made their stand against the Abyssal tide. The sky above hung low and sallow, bruised with clouds that refused to rain, and the air tasted of sulfur and burnt earth. Kael had walked into this conflict a few days prior, following rumors of mass casualties and abandoned defensive positions. The rumors proved accurate. The results exceeded his expectations.
An Abyssal creature broke from the treeline, all sinew and claws. Its hide glistened with a substance that caught the flat light and threw it back in oily rainbows, and its joints bent in directions that contradicted anatomy. The thing opened its mouth—a vertical split that ran from what passed for a chin to the base of its sternum—and shrieked. The sound drove needles into Kael's eardrums, high and thin and wrong, the frequency of cracking glass.
Kael sidestepped its charge, caught its trailing leg, and pulled. The creature's momentum carried it forward while its leg yanked back, and the result was physics, brutal and simple. It crashed sideways, its skull rebounding off a half-buried stone with a sound like a dropped melon. Kael's knee found its spine. The vertebrae separated with a thick, grinding pop that he felt more than heard, traveling up through his kneecap and into his hip. His hands found its throat. The hide was hot, feverish, pulsing with something that was not blood. He squeezed, and the creature thrashed beneath him, claws raking furrows in the ash, until the pulsing stopped and the heat began to drain.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 1,247 essence from the remains. Resist Corruption acquired.]

His hands were trembling. He flexed them, watching the fine tremor work its way through his fingers. Harvesting Abyssal essence left a residue, a faint wrongness that settled behind his teeth like the taste of copper wire. He wiped his palms on his coat, but the sensation clung.
The Sun Sect soldiers twenty yards distant watched him work. None intervened. Word had spread through the Order ranks about the Soul Reaver who harvested the fallen. Some called him monster. Others called him useful. Most kept their distance. He could see it in the way they held themselves—shoulders angled away, weapons gripped a fraction tighter, eyes cutting sideways toward him when they thought he wasn't looking. One soldier spat into the ash. The glob sizzled on contact with a patch of Abyssal residue, releasing a thin thread of acrid smoke.
A horn sounded from the eastern line. The Abyssal forces were regrouping.
Kael counted the bodies around him. Eleven corpses within easy reach. The orders would want burial details, sacred rites, proper respect for the dead. He had no such obligations.
His hands found the nearest corpse, and the cold, clammy flesh yielded beneath his fingers like wet dough pressed into a bowl. The stench hit him next—not the sharp, metallic tang of fresh blood he had anticipated, but something sweeter, more cloying, like rotting fruit left too long in summer heat. He forced himself to breathe shallowly through his mouth, but the taste of decay lingered on his tongue, thick and oily, coating the back of his throat with each inhalation.
The body lay twisted at an angle that spoke of violence and sudden stillness. The dead man's eyes were open, glassy and staring at nothing, reflecting the pale light that filtered through the shattered window above. Dust motes drifted lazily through the air, catching the weak afternoon sun, and he watched them settle like tiny gray snowflakes onto the corpse's pale cheek. A fly landed on the man's lower lip, crawled across the cracked and peeling skin, then lifted off again with a buzz that seemed obscenely loud in the stillness.
His heart hammered against his ribs, each beat a muffled drum in his ears. *Why did I come here?* he thought. *What was I hoping to find?* The questions offered no comfort, only a cold certainty that whatever answers existed lay somewhere in this room, hidden among the dead.
His fingers moved carefully across the body's jacket, searching pockets that might hold answers—or more nightmares. The fabric was damp, and when his hand brushed against something hard and rectangular, he felt a jolt of adrenaline spike through his veins. His throat tightened. He didn't want to pull it out. He didn't want to know.
But his hands moved anyway, driven by something deeper than reason—some desperate need to understand the horror that had brought him to this place, to this moment, with this corpse growing colder beneath his trembling touch.
The harvest took seconds. The essence flowed into him like cold water, filling spaces he hadn't known were empty.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 692 essence from the remains. Spiritual Sensitivity acquired.]
Another notification. Another piece of power that hadn't existed in his soul yesterday.
The Six Orders teach that spiritual advancement comes through meditation, discipline, and the slow accumulation of personal reserves. Kael had tried that method once. It had taken him years to reach the second stage through legitimate means. Four years of breathing exercises, of seated meditation in cold stone halls, of instructors scrutinizing his meridian flow with disapproving expressions. Four years of incremental, grueling progress that left him middling at best among his peers.
Three months of harvesting had carried him to Sovereign.
He moved to the next corpse.
The creature died without resistance. Kael harvested while the Shattered Coast soldiers retreated behind him, their formation broken, their numbers dwindling. The Abyssal offensive had struck their position with overwhelming force, breaching the defensive line in multiple places. He could hear the soldiers behind him—the scrape of boots on scorched earth, the clatter of a dropped shield picked up again with shaking hands, someone breathing in short, panicked bursts through their teeth.
They had not expected such coordinated assault.
The Shattered Coast commander's voice cut through the chaos. "Reaver! They're breaking through the center!"
He turned to face the breach. Three Abyssal creatures poured through the gap, their twisted forms adapted for burrowing, forelimbs thick with calloused plates designed for tearing through earth and stone alike. Behind them came human shapes—cultists in black robes, their eyes glowing with stolen light. That glow was wrong. Not the warm gold of legitimate spiritual cultivation, but a sick, pulsing violet that made Kael's eyes water when he focused on it too long.
Kael watched them come. The cultists moved with purpose, targeting not the soldiers but the corpses. They wanted the bodies intact. They wanted something from the battlefield.
The first cultist reached the nearest corpse and pulled a ritual blade. The metal was black, pitted with corrosion, and humming with a low vibration that Kael felt in his back teeth. The motion was practiced, efficient. This was not their first harvest.
Kael crossed the distance in three seconds.
His hand caught the cultist's wrist before the blade fell. The bones ground together under his grip, and the cultist's fingers spasmed open, then clenched again—refuse, resist, fail. The cultist's eyes widened, then narrowed with recognition.
"Kael Ashborn." The cultist spoke his name like a curse. "The Soul Reaver. We've heard of you."
"The Prime serves all." The cultist smiled, blood on his teeth. "We serve the resurrection."
Kael broke the wrist. The snap was sharp and clean, a dry twig sound, and the cultist screamed. The sound bounced off the ruined walls around them, multiplying, echoing, until it seemed like ten men were shrieking at once. He harvested the pain, the fear, the fragments of memory that surfaced in those final moments. The cultist's life unspooled in flashes: a cellar, candlelight, a voice reciting words in a language that burned to hear, hands pressing him down onto a stone table, something entering through the cracks in his resolve and setting up residence behind his eyes.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 341 essence from the remains. Cultist Memory Fragment acquired.]
The information crystallized in his mind. Organized cells. Coordinated attacks. A timeline measured in days, not weeks. Someone was preparing for something massive.
The remaining cultists fled back through the breach.
Kael let them go. The corpses remained.
Commander Voss of the Iron Vanguard arrived seconds later, her armor scorched, her blade still wet. The blood on the steel had already begun to oxidize, turning from bright arterial red to a dull brown that flaked when the wind caught it. She surveyed the scene—the dead cultist, the interrupted ritual, Kael standing over the harvest. Her jaw worked, the muscles in her cheeks bunching and releasing, and he could see the calculation behind her eyes. Triage. Priority assessment. What needed to be dealt with immediately and what could wait.
"You were supposed to be harvesting enemy corpses." Her voice carried no accusation. Only exhaustion. The kind that settled into the marrow and stayed.
She looked at the cultist's body, then back at him. "They want something from the dead. We found three similar scenes this morning. Bodies opened. Soul essence removed."
"Harvested." Kael corrected. "Someone else has the ability."
"Impossible." Commander Voss shook her head. "Soul Reavers are unique. The Orders confirmed it decades ago. Only one can exist at a time."
The commander's expression shifted. "You think the cult has a Reaver?"
"I think someone is trying to create one."
The implications hung between them. A cult-backed Soul Reaver would be catastrophic—unlimited harvesting, no moral constraints, no fear of consequence. The Abyssal forces already outnumbered the Orders. Adding a Reaver to their ranks would tip the balance permanently. Kael watched the understanding move across Voss's face like a shadow crossing open ground. Her grip on her blade tightened. Her stance widened, shoulders dropping into a defensive posture she probably wasn't aware she'd adopted.
Commander Voss reached a decision. "I need to report this. The Council of Six needs to know."
"The Council has ignored intelligence on cult activity for months." Kael moved past her toward the next cluster of bodies. "They won't listen now."
He didn't answer. He was already harvesting.

Another technique. Another piece of power.

"I know of him." The Order of Dawn's leader was famous for his anti-cult crusades, his public executions of Reaver sympathizers, his absolute commitment to destroying anything that threatened the seal network. His name alone was enough to make cultivators in three provinces check their conduct and lower their voices.
Kael had met him once, years ago, before his execution. Before his transformation. Thorne had called him a parasite then. Had predicted his downfall. The memory surfaced with the clarity of fresh pain: the cold stone of the judgment platform, the iron collar biting into his neck, Thorne's pale eyes looking down at him with the serene contempt of a man who had never doubted a single conviction in his life.
Now Thorne was coming to a battlefield where Kael stood harvesting the dead.
"Commander." A young soldier approached, breathless, his face streaked with ash and something darker that had dried in tracks from his temples to his jaw. "Scouts report a second wave approaching from the western quarter. Bigger than the first."
Commander Voss swore. The defensive line was already compromised. Reinforcements wouldn't arrive for hours. The only fresh troops in the area were Kael and whatever forces remained in the forward camps.
"Get word to the other units." She turned to Kael. "I know what you are. I know what you do. I also know you're the strongest Awakened on this field. Help us hold the line. When this is over, I'll make sure the Orders know you fought for them."
"Then we all die, and you can harvest whatever you want from the bodies." She met his gaze without flinching. "But you won't be able to explain the massacre to the other fronts when they ask why no one held the breach."
Kael considered the offer. Helping the Order meant exposing himself to their scrutiny. It meant risking discovery by Thorne's delegation. It meant potential capture or execution.
It also meant three hundred fresh corpses for his harvesting.
The western assault came with the sunset, painting the ash-covered wasteland in shades of red and gold. The Abyssal forces crashed against the repaired defensive line like waves against a cliff, their coordinated strikes revealing intelligent leadership behind the horde. The creatures came in staggered formations, the weaker ones absorbing arrow fire while the larger specimens circled wide, probing for weakness. The sky above turned the color of an old bruise, purple and yellow where the smoke thinned, and the last light caught the ash in the air and turned it to floating embers that never burned out.
Kael stood at the center of the breach, harvesting between attacks. Every corpse that fell within reach became his. Every death fed his advancement. He could feel the essence pooling in his core, dense and heavy, pressing against walls that were already beginning to crack.

A focused attention, a calculated assessment. Kael had encountered their agents before. They operated in the spaces between Orders, selling information to the highest bidder, occasionally manipulating events to serve their own mysterious agenda. They were not allies. They were not enemies.

A message. A summons. An offer.

The second wave broke against the defensive line. The Abyssal forces retreated to regroup, leaving hundreds of bodies scattered across the Ashenmoor Wastes. The Orders had held, barely, sustained by the intervention of a Soul Reaver who fought among their ranks.
Commander Voss collapsed against a shattered barricade, her armor dented, her breathing ragged. Blood seeped from a gash along her temple, running down the side of her face and dripping from her jaw onto the scorched wood beside her. She didn't wipe it away. "We survived."

Tomorrow would bring Thorne and his delegation, the Order of Dawn's scrutiny, and the beginning of whatever new phase the cult had planned. Tonight offered answers in the form of corpses and cards and whispered revelations.
