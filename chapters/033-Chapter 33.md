Iron clanged against iron three cells down. Kael counted the rhythm, timing his breathing to match the guards' patrol pattern.
Cell 17 smelled of rust and unwashed flesh. The cot's thin mattress did nothing to cushion the bars digging into his spine. Through the narrow window, a gray dawn crept over Thornwick's skyline, painting the steam stacks in muted bronze.
"You're the Reaver."
Kael's eyes snapped open. The voice came from the cell across the corridor—an old man with hollow cheeks and white hair matted against his skull. His robes bore the faded insignia of the Order of Ashen Bones.
"Guilty as charged," Kael said. "Unless you've got a priest to absolve me."
"Absolution." The old man wheezed a laugh. "Boy, the Orders want your head on a pike. What you did at the harbor—" He stopped, studying Kael's face. "You don't even know what you are, do you?"
A guard's boots scraped against stone. Kael waited until the footsteps faded before answering. "I know what I can do."
"Do you?" The old man shifted, chains rattling. "The Reaver ability isn't just a gift. It's a key. And there are doors in this world that were meant to stay locked."
---
The midday meal arrived in a bowl that might have once held paint. Thin gruel. A heel of bread hard enough to double as a weapon. Kael ate mechanically, his mind cataloging every sound, every shadow.
Seventeen guards rotated through Blackgate's three tiers. Two at the main entrance. Four patrolling the upper levels. The rest clustered near the flowing center, where prisoners were catalogued, sorted, and—according to rumor—disappeared.
Blackgate Prison was ancient, its foundations dating back to the Abyssal Wars. The walls contained remnants of the original seal stones, though decades of neglect had left them cracked and discolored. Kael ran his fingers across the cold surface of his cell wall, sensing faint resonance beneath the stone.
"Soul anchors," he murmured. The prison had been designed to contain Awakened prisoners by draining their spiritual energy. Even now, he felt the slow leak from his core, like blood from a wound that wouldn't close.
The system notification pulsed at the edge of his awareness, a cold reminder:
[WARNING: Spiritual energy draining at a tiny fraction per hour. Ambient seal fields interfere with natural regeneration.]
Kael needed to move. The longer he stayed, the weaker he'd become.
A bell clanged in the distance. Shift change. He counted the voices—four returning, four departing. The guard rotation gave him a seven-minute window of reduced supervision.
Cell 17's lock was a masterwork of Thornwick engineering: iron and steel, keyed to the warden's seal. Kael had watched the guards enough times to recognize the slight hesitation when they inserted the key, the micro-adjustment required to align the tumblers.
He didn't have a key. But he had something better.
The corpse of a rat lay wedged beneath his cot. It had died sometime during the night, its small body stiff and cold. Kael reached beneath the frame, fingers brushing matted fur.
He hesitated.
Soul harvesting required touch. Touching a corpse meant more than extracting abilities—it meant witnessing the final moments of a life, feeling the echo of whatever that creature had experienced before death. The first time he'd done it, Kael had vomited until his throat burned.
Now, the hesitation lasted only a moment.
His palm pressed flat against the rat's side. Cold flesh. The faintest trace of warmth lingering near the heart.
The world went white.
---
Fragments exploded through his mind. A sewer tunnel, damp and endless. The smell of rotting grain. Hunger—vast, consuming hunger that made Kael's stomach clench in sympathy. Then light. A cage of wire and wood. Human hands reaching down.
Pain.
The harvest completed, leaving Kael gasping.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted basic spatial awareness from the remains. Enhanced perception of enclosed spaces acquired. Duration: several minutes.]
The ability slotted into his consciousness like a key turning in a lock. the cell transformed. Kael could sense the exact dimensions of his confinement—the width of the corridor, the height of the ceiling, the precise distance between bars. More than that, he could feel the patrol patterns as disturbances in the air, pressure waves created by moving bodies.
His prison had become a map.
Kael rose from the cot. His muscles ached from days of confinement, but the rat's borrowed senses sharpened his awareness to razor clarity. The corridor stretched ahead, lit by sputtering gas lamps. Two guards stood at the far end, their attention focused on a card game spread across a wooden crate.
Three cells to his left, the old man watched.
"You're insane," the old man whispered. "They'll cut you down before you reach the stairs."
"Maybe." Kael tested the cell door. Still locked. "But staying here means slow death. The seal fields will drain me dry within the month."
"There are other options. The Mortician's Guild has arrangements—"
"I'm not selling my soul to corpse merchants."
The old man's laugh was bitter. "Boy, you already have. Every Reaver does, one way or another. The question is only whether you pay in blood or in service."
Kael's fingers found the seam where the cell's iron bars met the stone wall. The mortar here was old, crumbling. With enough pressure, he could widen the gap enough to slip through.
He didn't have much time.
---
The plan was simple. Escape the cell, reach the flowing center, find evidence of who had framed him for the harbor murders. Zarek's name had appeared in the initial arrest report, but Kael needed proof—something the Courts would accept.
The warden's office would have records. But reaching it meant crossing the entire prison, past three checkpoints and a squad of Awakened wardens who patrolled the upper levels.
Kael pushed against the wall. The stones shifted, grinding against each other. Dust rained down from the ceiling. He gritted his teeth and pushed harder, feeling the muscles in his shoulders strain.
The gap widened.
One shoulder scraped through, then the other. Kael dropped to the corridor floor, landing in a crouch. The gas lamps flickered overhead, casting dancing shadows across the stone.
The guards hadn't noticed. Their card game had intensified, voices rising in argument over some minor dispute.
Kael moved.
His borrowed spatial awareness painted the path ahead in neon outlines. The corridor branched twice before reaching the main stairwell. A maintenance shaft opened to his left, leading toward the lower levels where supplies were stored.
He chose the shaft.
The darkness swallowed him whole. Kael climbed by touch, fingers finding handholds in the rough stone. The rat's senses painted a three-dimensional image in his mind—every projection, every ledge, every narrow passage.
He emerged in a storage room stacked with crates of confiscated goods. Weapons. Jewelry. Personal effects from prisoners long dead or transported. The air smelled of dust and copper.
A wooden desk sat against the far wall, its surface buried under stacks of parchment. Kael rifled through the documents, scanning for anything related to his case.
"Transfer order, dated three days prior to the harbor incident," he murmured. "Authorizing removal of evidence from the city morgue."
Someone had sanitized the crime scene before the official investigation began. Someone with access to both the city watch and the Order of Ashen Bones.
The name at the bottom of the document made Kael's blood freeze.
Magister Dorian Ashworth. Order of Ashen Bones. Deputy Warden, Blackgate Prison.
The signature was crisp, professional. The seal beneath it genuine.
Dorian. Kael had trusted him. Had shared drinks with him, discussed the corruption within the Orders, laughed at his dry jokes about the Council's endless meetings.
Dorian had betrayed him.
---
The revelation burned through Kael's thoughts like acid through metal. He forced himself to focus, to breathe, to keep moving. Shock was a luxury he couldn't afford.
The storage room door burst open.
Kael threw himself behind a crate as two figures entered, carrying a body bag between them. The bag was heavy, sloshing with liquid. Fresh corpses, then—the Mortician's Guild's daily delivery of research subjects.
"This one's still warm," one of the carriers said. A woman with close-cropped hair and the build of someone who worked with their hands. "Got here just in time."
"The Guild pays triple for warm ones. The essence doesn't start degrading until the body temperature drops below ambient."
They dropped the bag onto a metal table. The zipper hissed as it opened, revealing a young man—no older than Kael—with dark hair and vacant eyes. His chest had been torn open, ribs splayed like broken wings.
Kael's stomach heaved.
The Soul Reaver ability pulsed in his core, responding to the proximity of fresh death. The corpse's residual energy washed over him, a tide of impressions: fear, pain, the flash of a blade, a face twisted in rage.
Someone had killed this man specifically to harvest him.
The woman pulled a syringe from her coat, filling it with a pale blue liquid. "Hold him still. I need to extract the meridian traces before they dissipate."
The man grabbed the corpse's shoulders. The woman inserted the needle into the chest cavity, beginning the extraction process.
Kael watched from behind the crate, every muscle coiled tight. He could intervene—could use the harvesting ability to extract what this corpse still held. But doing so would reveal his presence, would alert the entire prison to his escape.
The woman's extraction finished. She capped the syringe, sliding it into a leather case. "Good quality. Should net us fifty vials once it's refined."
They zipped the body bag and hauled it back toward the door. Kael held his breath until their footsteps faded.
Fifty vials. At a hundred gold sovereigns each, that was five thousand sovereigns' worth of soul-essence. More than most families earned in a decade.
This was how the Orders funded their operations. Not through tithes or donations, but through a relentless harvest of the desperate and the dead.
---
The corridors of Blackgate's lower level wound through foundations older than Thornwick itself. Kael followed the walls, using the rat's spatial awareness to avoid patrols. The deeper he went, the more the seal stones concentrated, their faded glyphs pulsing with faint blue light.
A door stood at the end of a narrow passage. Iron-bound oak, reinforced with bands of cold steel. A warden's post, according to the layout Kael had memorized.
Voices drifted from within.
"—the Reaver's being transferred to the upper cells. Order of Ashen Bones wants him alive for questioning."
"Why? What's so special about him?"
"He touched something at the harbor. The cultists were screaming about it before we put them down. Said he'd activated one of the old seals."
Kael's heart stuttered. Activated a seal. The harbor—the place where he'd awakened, where he'd first discovered his ability to harvest.
What had he done without knowing?
The conversation continued, but Kael tuned it out. He needed to reach the upper levels. Needed to find answers about what had happened at the harbor, about what the cultists had been trying to accomplish.
The warden's post door opened. Kael flattened himself against the wall, barely breathing.
A figure emerged. Tall, gaunt, wearing robes of deep purple embroidered with silver thread. The insignia on his chest made Kael's blood turn to ice: an eye wreathed in flames.
Order of Dawn. The crusaders. The most fanatical of the Six Orders, dedicated to purging all Abyssal corruption.
The Warden looked directly at the shadow where Kael crouched.
"I know you're there, little Reaver." His voice was soft, cultured, utterly calm. "The seal stones sang when you entered the lower levels. They've been waiting for you."
Kael's hand went to his belt, searching for a weapon. He found nothing.
"You can come out," the Warden continued. "Or I can drag you out. The choice affects only how much pain you experience."
Kael stepped into the light.
The Warden's smile was thin, knowing. "Kael Ashborn. The man who defied death and walked away. The Order of Dawn has many questions about your... Unusual origin."
"And if I don't feel like answering?"
"Then we ask while you're suspended over a consecrated flame. Your body will heal. Your mind will break. Either way, we get what we need."
The Warden raised one hand. Golden light gathered at his fingertips, forming a shape Kael recognized from old texts: the Sunburst Seal, used to bind Awakened prisoners.
Kael moved.
He wasn't faster than the Warden—not by a long shot. But he'd spent days in that cell, watching, learning, preparing. His body knew the Warden's reach before the strike landed.
He ducked under the golden light, drove his shoulder into the Warden's chest, and drove them both into the stone wall. The impact knocked the wind from both of them.
The Warden recovered first. His palm struck Kael's temple, sending stars exploding across his vision. Kael staggered back, tasting copper.
"Your enthusiasm is admirable," the Warden said, straightening his robes. "But you're still Awakened. Still a child playing at powers you don't understand."
The golden light reformed, brighter this time. The Sunburst Seal descended toward Kael's chest.
He had seconds. Maybe less.
Kael's hand found the corpse bag the collectors had left behind. He ripped it open, tearing the zipper in his haste. The young man's body tumbled out, still slick with extraction fluid.
The Warden's eyes narrowed. "What are you—"
Kael pressed his palm to the corpse's chest.
---
Death exploded through him.
The young man—his name was Marcus, his memories whispered—had died fighting. Fighting something that wore human skin but moved wrong, joints bending at angles that hurt to watch. Marcus had been part of a strike team, sent to investigate rumors of cultist activity in the warehouse district.
The cultists had been waiting.
Seventeen of them. All Awakened. All wearing the mark of the Whisper King's followers.
Marcus had killed four before they overwhelmed him. His ability—a resonance sense that let him feel the emotional signatures of nearby souls—had been his undoing. The cultists had no emotional signatures at all. Empty. Hollow.
Marcus had realized too late that he was fighting something that shouldn't exist.
Kael surfaced from the memory, gasping. His palm burned where it touched Marcus's chest, energy flowing through him like lightning through a copper wire.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Combat Resonance (enhanced) from the remains. Damage output increased by 40% for several minutes. Spiritual signature detection range: 50 meters.]
The new ability slotted into place beside his existing powers. Kael felt it expand outward, a sphere of perception that brushed against the Warden's soul like fingers against silk.
The Warden's spiritual signature was blinding. Pure gold, shot through with veins of white-hot zealotry. It pulsed with each heartbeat, a drumbeat of righteous fury.
"Impressive," the Warden said. "You harvested him. I didn't think Reavers could do that to fresh corpses."
He wasn't afraid. The realization chilled Kael more than the stone walls.
"Why aren't you afraid?" Kael asked. "Soul Reavers are hunted. Killed on sight. And you just watched me harvest a man in front of you."
"Because I'm not afraid of you." The Warden's smile widened. "I'm afraid of what you might become."
The golden light struck.
Kael threw himself sideways, but the Sunburst Seal was fast—faster than anything he'd faced before. It wrapped around his chest, pinning his arms to his sides. Golden light seared into his skin, burning through cloth and flesh alike.
Pain. Immense, consuming pain that drove him to his knees.
The Warden walked forward, each step deliberate. "The Order of Dawn has studied Reavers for centuries. We know what you are. What you represent."
The seal tightened. Kael felt his spiritual energy beginning to leak, pulled toward the golden light like water toward a drain.
"A key," the Warden continued. "That's what the old texts call your kind. Keys that can open doors that were meant to stay sealed forever. The 72 rifts. The prisons of the Entities. All of them respond to Soul Reaver touch."
Kael's thoughts raced. The seal was draining him, but slowly. He had minutes before he lost consciousness. Maybe less.
"The cultists at the harbor," he managed. "What were they doing?"
"You tell me. You activated the seal." The Warden leaned close, his golden eyes boring into Kael's. "Something awakened at the harbor that night. Something that's been sleeping for three hundred years. And it spoke your name."
The words hit Kael like a physical blow.
Spoke his name.
The original Soul Reaver. The first of his kind. The one who'd created the Sepulcher Tablets, who'd sealed the 72 rifts, who had—
"Who is the original Soul Reaver?" Kael demanded.
The Warden's smile became a grin. "That's the question, isn't it? The one that will determine whether humanity survives the coming storm."
He raised one hand, golden light gathering once more.
"I think we'll keep you alive for now. The Order of Dawn has... Plans for keys."
The light descended.
---
Kael woke in darkness.
His wrists burned where the Manacles gripped them. Chains ran from the Manacles to the floor, bolted into ancient stone. The cell was small, barely large enough to lie down.
But he wasn't alone.
A figure sat in the corner, barely visible in the gloom. Old. Frail. Wearing the tattered remains of robes that might once have been purple.
"Welcome to the Warden's private collection," the figure said. "Where we keep the truly interesting prisoners."
Kael's throat was dry, his voice a rasp. "Who are you?"
"A friend. Or an enemy. That depends on what you do next." The figure leaned forward, and Kael caught a glimpse of his face—gaunt, weathered, marked by decades of imprisonment. "I'm the one who told the cultists where to find you. The one who set the harbor trap."
Kael's blood froze. "You're the Whisper King?"
"No." The figure's laugh was bitter, broken. "I'm what the Whisper King used to be. Before he became something else entirely."
He reached out, his skeletal fingers brushing Kael's chained wrist.
"I know what you are, Soul Reaver. I know what you can become. And I know how to save you from the fate that awaits."
The chains rattled as Kael tried to pull away. "Why would you help me?"
"Because the Whisper King killed everyone I ever loved. Because he's planning something that will destroy the world. And because—" The figure's eyes glittered in the darkness. "—because you carry a piece of the original Soul Reaver inside you. A piece that the Whisper King will kill to possess."
Footsteps echoed in the corridor outside. Heavy. Measured. Coming closer.
"You have one chance," the figure whispered. "When the door opens, run. Don't look back. Find the Sepulcher Tablets. Find out what the original Soul Reaver really sealed."
The footsteps stopped outside their cell.
A key scraped into the lock.
The figure smiled, teeth gleaming in the dark. "Good luck, Kael Ashborn. You'll need it."
