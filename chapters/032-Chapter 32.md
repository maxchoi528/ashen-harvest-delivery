Kael pressed his back against the rotting timber, breath shallow. Below, torchlight flickered through the drainage grate — three figures in Mortician's Guild white, their boots splashing through ankle-deep muck.
"You smell that?" one growled. "Rot and sulfur. The Reaver's hole is close."
Kael's fingers found the cold iron of his stolen blade. No spiritual energy to spare. The confrontation in Thornwick had stripped him to emptiness, left him running on marrow and spite. He'd found this abandoned tannery on the city's lower eastern edge, a place where the ley lines ran thin and the dead piled up faster than the Morticians could process them. Dangerous territory. Perfect refuge.
The grate shuddered. Someone was cutting through the lock.
Kael moved. He crossed the derelict floor in three heartbeats, sliding beneath a collapsed conveyor belt where the smell of old hide and lye masked his own scent. Above him, through gaps in the rotting floorboards, he watched the grate swing open. A rope dropped. The first operative descended, crossbow already raised.
"Clear the perimeter," the man ordered. "Standard sweep pattern. He's wounded — he can't have gone far."
Two others followed, their movements coordinated in that precise way that spoke of training. Not city watch. Not simple bounty hunters. Someone had sent professionals.
Kael counted to thirty, then moved toward the building's rear. His leg wound from the previous night had reopened, leaving a dark trail behind him. He needed shelter. He needed time. Most of all, he needed to understand what had happened at the Concordat hall — why the Shadow Court's operative had known exactly where to find him, and why she'd spoken his mother's name before trying to put a knife in his throat.
The tanner's cellar access lay ahead, a square of blackness in the corner. He descended into darkness.

The cellar stank of copper and decay. Kael's boots found slick stone, then something soft that squelched. He didn't look down. Didn't need to. The ambient spiritual pressure told him everything — a dozen dead rats in various stages of bloating, their residual energy so faint it barely registered. Not worth harvesting. Not worth the risk of detection.
He found a corner where the walls formed a natural alcove and lowered himself to the ground. His leg throbbed. His soul reserves hovered at empty, that terrible hollow feeling that came from spending too much, too fast. The Soul Reaver ability drew from others, not himself, but without corpses to harvest, he was just another wounded fugitive in a city that wanted him dead.
The Morticians above were thorough. Their footsteps passed overhead, paused, moved on. He heard them speaking — fragments about a containment protocol, about protocols regarding Reavers, about the Council's new standing orders.
"The Council's gone soft," one voice said, muffled by the floorboards. "A hundred years ago, they'd have torched this entire district."
"Orders from the Spire. They want him alive. Something about his... Connections."
"Connections? He's a death-row convict playing at being an Awakened."
"Ask the Spire. I just sweep floors and collect corpses."
Kael's mind raced. The Arcane Spire wanted him alive. The same Order that had founded Thornwick's entire magical infrastructure, that controlled the ley lines and the Awakening rituals and the research into the seal network. Why would they care about a Reaver?
The footsteps receded. He allowed himself thirty seconds of stillness, then forced himself upright. Panic wouldn't save him. Planning might.
He needed to understand his enemy. The confrontation at the Concordat hall had been a disaster — he'd gone in looking for answers about his mother's death and found only blood and betrayal. Lyra's warning echoed in his memory: the Whisper King's agents were everywhere. The cult had moles in every Order. The entire structure of the world's defense against the Abyss was rotting from within.
And he, Kael Ashborn, was standing in the middle of it with nothing but a stolen blade and an ability that terrified everyone who learned about it.
He began to search the cellar. The tanners had abandoned this place years ago, driven out by the same industrial expansion that had created the lower districts' poverty. What remained were broken tools, rusted vats, and the accumulated refuse of a trade that had fed on death — animal hides requiring chemical baths, the runoff from which had poisoned the local water table and driven away the living.
His fingers found something beneath a collapsed shelf. A leather satchel, waterlogged but intact. Inside: a map of Thornwick's eastern districts, marked with symbols he didn't recognize. A glass vial containing what looked like crystallized soul-essence — expensive, worth at least a hundred sovereigns. And a journal, its pages swollen with damp, the handwriting cramped and paranoid.
Kael lit a match.
*Day 47 of the new cycle*, the first entry read. *The Mistweaver colony has expanded into the old drainage tunnels. Three workers lost this week. The Guild won't acknowledge it — admitting Abyssal contamination would trigger a quarantine, and the tanner's consortium has too much money riding on this quarter's production.*
Mistweaver. Kael's stomach tightened. He'd heard stories in the prison camps — Awakened who wandered into Mistweaver territory rarely came back out, and those who did were hollow shells, their memories eaten away by whatever the spiders injected.
*Day 52*, another entry read. *Found evidence of a secondary colony in the tanner's cellar itself. The silk is everywhere. They're building something. The silk structures look almost like... Runes? No. Must be exhaustion.*
The journal ended abruptly on day 53. The writer's fate was unrecorded, but the implication was clear.
Kael looked up from the pages. The match burned low. Around him, the cellar's shadows seemed to thicken, taking on a quality that had nothing to do with the absence of light.
The silk was there. Thin threads crisscrossing the walls, catching the faint glow from above. He'd mistaken it for cobwebs. A rookie error that might cost him everything.

The Mistweaver emerged from behind a collapsed vat, its body the size of a hunting hound, its eight eyes reflecting the dying match-light in concentric rings. Mistweaver spiders were not true spiders — the Abyssal contamination that spawned them had fused insectoid physiology with raw spiritual corruption, creating predators that hunted through a combination of physical violence and psychic assault.
The one before him opened its mandibles, releasing a cloud of silver mist that drifted toward Kael with unnatural purpose.
He dropped the match and drew his blade.
The mist touched his face first. Cold flooded his sinuses, and with it came images — his mother's face, twisted with accusation. The execution platform. Zarek's smile. Lyra's knife descending. All the nightmares he'd carried since Thornwick's dungeons crashed through his mental defenses like water through a broken dam.
Kael screamed. Not from pain but from violation — the spider was pulling memories from his surface thoughts, reading him like a book.
His sword arm swung wild, cutting through silk strands that snapped like harp strings. The Mistweaver scuttled sideways, faster than its bulk suggested, and lunged for his throat.
He twisted. The mandibles closed on empty air. His offhand caught the creature's front leg, bony and slick, and he wrenched sideways with all his strength. The leg snapped. Black ichor sprayed across his sleeve.
The Mistweaver shrieked — a sound like tearing metal — and retreated two paces. Its remaining legs scrabbled against the stone floor. Silver mist poured from its spinnerets in thick clouds now, no longer drifting but actively pursuing, hunting.
Kael backed against the wall. His leg buckled. He fell to one knee, blade scraping stone.
The Mistweaver advanced. Through the silver cloud, he could see its eyes pulsing with stolen memories — his memories — cycling through its psychic assault. It had learned his fears. It was weaponizing them.
*You are nothing*, the spider's presence whispered directly into his mind. *Your mother died hating you. Your allies abandoned you. The world burned your body and celebrated.*
"Shut up." Kael's voice came out ragged.
*You will join the countless husks we have made. Your skills will strengthen our colony. Your memories will teach us new terrors.*
The mist closed around him. His vision blurred. His thoughts tangled. Somewhere distant, his body tried to rise and failed.
But Kael Ashborn had spent three months in a death-row cell waiting for execution. He had survived the awakening ritual. He had murdered his way through Thornwick's underworld. He had faced down the Shadow Court, the Morticians, and the combined might of the Orders' enforcers.
A psychic spider was not going to break him.
He reached inward. Not for spiritual energy — there was none to find — but for the place where the Soul Reaver's power resided. The ability that had no cost, that drew from others, that made him a monster in the eyes of every Awakened on the continent.
The Mistweaver sensed the shift. Its assault intensified, flooding his mind with visions of failure and despair.
Kael smiled through gritted teeth. "You want my memories? Come and get them."
He touched the spider's broken leg.

The harvest hit him like a wave of ice water.
*Soul Harvest: You extracted 12.7 units of spiritual energy from the remains. Memory Fragment acquired: Mistweaver Hunting Pattern.*
*Soul Harvest: You extracted 8.3 units of spiritual energy. Memory Fragment acquired: Colony Communication Frequency.*
*Soul Harvest: You extracted 15.9 units of spiritual energy. Memory Fragment acquired: Abyssal Resonance Signature.*
Kael's vision cleared. The silver mist dissipated around him, and he found himself standing over the spider's corpse, his hands buried in its thorax where the largest concentration of spiritual energy pooled. The Mistweaver had died instantly — its psychic assault had been its only real defense, and touching a Reaver mid-harvest had proved fatal to its own nervous system.
The memories poured through him. Not complete consciousness — Reaver harvests captured fragments, not personalities — but enough to understand.
The colony was not random. It had been placed here deliberately, seeded in the abandoned tunnels decades ago as part of a long-term project. The Mistweavers were connected to the drainage system, which connected to the old ley line network, which connected to something deep beneath Thornwick's eastern districts. Something that pulsed with the same resonance Kael now felt burning in his harvested memories.
A rift. Or, the residue of one. Seventy-two had been sealed after the Abyssal Wars, but seals degraded. Leakage occurred. And in certain locations, the contamination built up over generations, creating pockets where creatures like the Mistweaver could survive in the Material realm.
The spider's memories also contained something else: a map. Not of the tunnels — the colony navigated by scent and spiritual resonance, not geography — but of the drainage network's layout. And at its center, marked with a psychic impression that made Kael's stomach clench, a chamber containing something the colony feared.
A nest. Or a lure. The distinction wasn't clear in the fragmented memory.
Kael withdrew his hands from the corpse. His soul reserves hadn't filled — the harvested energy went somewhere deeper than his personal reserves, reinforcing the foundation of his Reaver abilities — but he felt stronger. Clearer. The mist's assault had left no lasting damage.
He retrieved the journal and the vial of crystallized essence. Then he followed the spider's silk threads deeper into the cellar.

The tunnel entrance was hidden behind a false wall of rotting timber. Kael ducked through the gap and found himself in a passage that sloped downward at a steep angle. The air grew colder with each step. The walls glistened with condensation — or something else.
His harvested memories warned him: the colony's tunnels branched in three directions. Left led to the drainage network and escape. Right led to the main nest, where dozens of Mistweavers waited in darkness. Center led to the chamber at the tunnels' heart, the place the spiders avoided.
Kael chose center.
The passage narrowed. His shoulders scraped against wet stone. The darkness ahead was absolute, broken only by the faint phosphorescence of fungal growths that clung to the walls. Their light cast shifting shadows that made him paranoid, convinced each dark patch concealed another Mistweaver waiting to spring.
After what felt like an hour but was probably ten minutes, the tunnel opened into a vaulted chamber. The ceiling arched fifty feet overhead, lost in shadow. The floor was covered in silk — not the hunting strands from the upper tunnels but architectural structures, vast sheets woven into shapes that suggested intentional design.
And at the chamber's center, suspended in a cocoon of silver thread that glowed with inner light, hung a human figure.
Kael approached slowly. His blade stayed raised. The cocoon was the size of a man, and through its translucent layers he could see features that had once been recognizable: a face frozen in an expression of terror, hands pressed against the silk as if trying to push free, clothes that might once have been fine quality now reduced to rotted rags.
An Awakened. The spiritual pressure radiating from the cocoon was unmistakable — this had been someone of significant power before the spiders caught them.
Kael reached toward the cocoon. His fingers touched silk.
*Warning: Cocoon contains active soul anchor. Harvesting may trigger colony response.*
The Reaver system — that strange fusion of ability and notification that had manifested after his execution — pulsed in his awareness. The cocoon wasn't just a trap. It was a battery. The Mistweavers had been feeding on this Awakened for years, drawing out their spiritual energy in slow increments, keeping them alive just long enough to maximize the yield.
A fate worse than death. Worse than the execution platform. Worse than anything Kael had imagined in his darkest moments.
He could harvest the Awakened. The energy would be substantial — years of accumulated power, refined by the spider's own spiritual metabolism. It would make him stronger. It would also kill whatever remained of the original consciousness, ending their suffering but completing an atrocity.
Or he could destroy the cocoon. Free the remains. Give them a cleaner death but lose the harvest.
The Mistweavers had been placed here deliberately. Someone had seeded this colony decades ago, knowing it would eventually catch an Awakened worth draining. The drainage network connected to ley lines. The ley lines connected to the seal network. And somewhere in that network, the Awakened in front of him had been meant to serve as a beacon, a marker, a piece in a larger puzzle.
Kael didn't know what the puzzle was. He didn't have enough information to guess. But he knew one thing with certainty: someone powerful had been planning this for a very long time, and the Awakened in the cocoon was bait.
He couldn't harvest them. Not because of morality — the Soul Reaver had no moral constraints, only practical ones — but because harvesting would trigger exactly the response the planner wanted. It would mark him. Identify him. Maybe even guide whatever tracking mechanism the spiders were running.
But he couldn't leave them either. The suffering was unconscionable, and if this Awakened had been meant as bait, their suffering had a purpose. Interrupting that purpose might be the only advantage he could claim.
Kael drew his blade and cut.
The silk parted with a sound like a plucked harp string. The cocoon's glow faded. The Awakened inside didn't move, didn't breathe — they hadn't been breathing for a long time, kept in a state of suspended dissolution by the colony's careful feeding. What fell from the broken cocoon was a body, not a person. A vessel emptied of everything that had once made them real.
*Soul Harvest: You extracted 0 units of spiritual energy from the remains. Insufficient life force to harvest.*
*Soul Harvest: You extracted Memory Fragment: Awakened Alliance Communications.*
The memories that flooded Kael's mind were old, fragmented, filled with echoes of a voice that spoke with authority.
*...the Concordat is failing. The seals weaken faster than projected. We need more Reavers in the field...*
*...the Whisper King moves his pieces. I have confirmation of at least three active moles in the Council...*
*...the Six Orders cannot be trusted. Their internal corruption runs too deep. Only the old methods will...*
The memory cut off. Kael staggered, his head pounding with borrowed impressions. This Awakened had been part of something. A faction within the Orders, maybe. Or something older. They'd known about the Whisper King. They'd known about the mole problem. They'd been trying to do something about it when the Mistweavers caught them.
And now they were dead, and their memories belonged to Kael.
Footsteps echoed from the tunnel behind him. Dozens of footsteps, clicking and scrabbling against stone.
The colony had noticed the cocoon's destruction. The harvest had been triggered. They were coming.
Kael ran.

The drainage network was exactly where the spider's memories had promised. He burst from the tunnel into a vaulted tunnel filled with rushing water, following the current until he found a maintenance ladder bolted to the wall. He climbed, his wounded leg screaming protest, and emerged through a grate into the pre-dawn darkness of Thornwick's eastern streets.
The air tasted clean after the tunnels. Cold and sharp, carrying the promise of winter.
Kael leaned against a wall and allowed himself thirty seconds of rest. The harvested memories pulsed in his awareness, demanding attention. The Awakened had been part of an alliance — a secret faction operating within and alongside the Six Orders, dedicated to fighting the Abyss directly than waiting for the seals to fail.
They called themselves the Vigil. And according to the fragmented memories, they had been recruiting Reavers.
*...the only ones who can truly fight the Entities are those who can harvest their essence. Standard Awakened are limited by the Law of Conservation. Only the Reaver...*
Another fragment, cutting off before completion.
Kael's mind raced. The Vigil knew about the seal degradation. They knew about the Whisper King's moles. They were trying to recruit people like him — people with abilities that frightened the establishment, people who could operate outside the Concordat's restrictions.
And they had been systematically eliminated. The Awakened in the cocoon had been captured, drained, used as bait. Others had probably met similar fates. The Whisper King wasn't just moving pieces against the Orders. He was hunting anyone who might stop him.
Kael pushed himself upright. His leg had stopped bleeding, though the wound would need treatment. The vial of crystallized essence in his pocket could be sold for enough to buy food, shelter, and supplies. The journal contained maps that might prove useful. The memories in his head contained contacts, locations, fragments of intelligence that could take weeks to fully parse.
He had resources now. Not enough, never enough, but more than he'd had an hour ago.
The sun crested the eastern horizon, painting Thornwick's smog-choked sky in shades of copper and rust. In the distance, the great spires of the Arcane Spire caught the light, their crystalline surfaces pulsing with barely visible energy — the ley line nodes that powered the entire city's infrastructure.
Somewhere in those towers, people were deciding his fate. The Morticians wanted him dead. The Shadow Court wanted him controlled. The Arcane Spire wanted him alive for reasons he didn't understand. And the Whisper King's cult was hunting him as part of a larger operation whose purpose remained opaque.
Kael Ashborn, death-row convict, Reaver, and now member of a dead faction's fragmented memories, limped down the empty street toward whatever came next.

The Veiled Market operated in the spaces between law and chaos — underground bazaars that appeared and vanished according to secret schedules, known only to those who moved in Thornwick's criminal shadows. Kael had learned the location during his weeks of running, from a fence who'd offered him work and been turned away with a warning.
The market opened at dawn and closed at the first bell. Kael arrived at the second hour, paying his entrance fee — a silver shilling to a blind woman who smelled of grave dust and incense — and descending into a cavern that had been transformed into a maze of stalls and tents.
Soul-essence changed hands openly here. Weapons lined tables alongside genuine artifacts. People whispered about the latest incursions from the Abyss, about seal failures and Entity sightings and the growing panic among the Orders' leadership.
Kael found a dealer who handled crystallized essence and produced the vial from his pocket. The dealer's eyes widened — standard essence from an Awakened source, high quality, worth at least a hundred sovereigns in the right market.
"Where did you get this?" the dealer asked, his voice low.
"Does it matter?"
"The Morticians control the supply. The Spire controls the licensing. This didn't come through official channels."
"I harvested it myself."
The dealer's expression shifted. Not fear — he'd seen too much to fear a claim — but calculation. "You're one of them. A Reaver."
"Yes."
"Word is the Council put out a kill order. Twenty thousand sovereigns for proof of death."
Kael leaned forward. "And what are you going to do with that information?"
"Nothing." The dealer pocketed the vial and counted out coins — gold sovereigns, heavy and warm. "Twenty thousand is a lot, but I've survived this long by knowing when to stay quiet. You want to do business, I do business. You want trouble, the tunnels beneath the market connect to places even the Rats don't go."
They exchanged goods for silence. Kael took his gold and moved deeper into the market.
The memories from the harvested Awakened guided him more than conscious thought. A stall selling alchemical supplies. A tent offering false papers. A back room where a man with a scarred face sat behind a desk covered in maps.
"The Vigil," Kael said without preamble. "I'm looking for the Vigil."
The scarred man's eyes narrowed. "Who sent you?"
"No one. I have memories that say I should be looking for you. Memories that belonged to someone the spiders ate."
Silence stretched. The scarred man studied Kael's face, reading something there that satisfied him.
"Sit down," he said finally. "We have a lot to discuss."
---
The scarred man called himself Corvus. He was one of the Vigil's recruiters, responsible for identifying potential allies and testing their loyalties. The Awakened in the cocoon — their name had been Mira — had been his responsibility. He'd failed to extract her before the Mistweavers took her.
"The colony wasn't random," Corvus explained, spreading maps across his desk. "Someone knew her route. Someone fed her location to the spiders. We've lost four operatives in the past year, all to what looked like accidents or bad luck. Someone is hunting us."
"The Whisper King," Kael said.
Corvus's expression flickered. "You've heard the name."
"I killed one of his cultists last week. She knew things she shouldn't have. About me. About my mother."
"Your mother was one of us." The words fell like stones. "Elara Ashborn. One of the Vigil's founders. She disappeared three years ago, presumed dead. The official story was an Abyssal incursion during a rift-sealing operation."
Kael's hands clenched. "And the unofficial story?"
"We don't know. But the timing suggests she learned something the Whisper King wanted buried. She went dark after a meeting at the Concordat hall — the same building where you were attacked. Someone used that location to draw her out."
"The cult has moles in the Orders. Multiple moles. You said three in the Council."
"Seven, by our count. Maybe more. The Whisper King has been planning this for decades. The seal degradation isn't natural — it's accelerated. Someone has been working to weaken specific rifts, to create entry points for a mass incursion."
"Why?"
Corvus shook his head. "We don't know. The Whisper King's endgame remains hidden. But whatever he wants, it requires the Abyssal Entities to cross into the Material in force. The seal network is the only thing stopping him, and the network is failing faster than anyone realizes."
Kael thought about the rifts in his memories — seventy-two seals, seventy-two Entities, a system designed to hold back the Abyss for eternity but not built to last forever.
"The Six Orders maintain the seals," he said. "They're supposed to rotate patrols, reinforce weak points, monitor for contamination."
"They do. Or they did. Now half the Order leadership is compromised. The remaining loyalists are fighting a two-front war — against the Entities bleeding through degraded seals, and against colleagues they can't trust. The Vigil exists because the Orders failed. Because someone needed to fight the real enemy while the Orders tore themselves apart with internal paranoia."
"And you want me to join."
"We want you to help us. There's a difference. The Vigil doesn't have leaders, not really. We have consensus, working groups, shared purpose. But we don't order. We ask."
Kael considered. The Vigil had failed to save Mira. They'd lost four operatives in a year. They were fighting an enemy with resources, patience, and decades of preparation. Joining them meant becoming a target, marking himself as an enemy of whatever the Whisper King was planning.
But staying alone meant dying alone. The Morticians were hunting him. The Shadow Court wanted him controlled. The Arcane Spire wanted him alive for reasons he didn't understand. He had no allies, no resources, no way to learn the truth about his mother or the conspiracy that had killed her.
The Vigil had memories. Intelligence. A network of operatives who'd been studying the Whisper King for years.
"What do you need from me?" he asked.
Corvus slid a map across the desk. It showed Thornwick's eastern districts, with a section marked in red.
"There's a rift beneath the old tanneries. Or, there's a seal over a rift, and the seal is failing. Our estimates give it three weeks before complete rupture. If that happens, the Entities inside will emerge directly into Thornwick's population center."
"The Mistweaver colony."
"Part of the leakage. The spiders have been feeding on ambient energy from the rift for decades. The seal's degradation has accelerated their growth and expansion. If we don't reinforce the seal or evacuate the district, we'll see an incursion event within the month."
Kael studied the map. "Why me? Why not send one of your own Awakened?"
"Because the seal reinforcement ritual requires a Reaver. The energy transfer has to come from harvested essence — the Law of Conservation doesn't allow direct spiritual channeling from living sources. Only a Reaver can draw from the dead without triggering the collapse mechanics that kill normal Awakened who try."
"You want me to harvest corpses and feed their essence into a failing seal."
"I want you to help save a city of half a million people."
Kael leaned back. The logic was sound. Uncomfortable, but sound. The Vigil needed him. The city needed him. And in exchange, he would gain resources, allies, and access to intelligence about the Whisper King's operation.
It was not a hard decision.
"Three weeks," he said. "That's not much time."
"No. But it's what we have. The Vigil will provide support — equipment, information, extraction if things go wrong. You'll have access to our safe houses and our contacts in the lower districts. In exchange, you handle the seal and you don't draw attention from the Orders until the job is done."
"And after?"
Corvus smiled without warmth. "After, you decide what comes next. The Vigil doesn't bind its allies. We ask. We don't order."
Kael stood. His leg ached, but the pain felt distant now, secondary to the plans forming in his mind.
"Show me the seal," he said. "Show me what I'm working with."

They descended into the tunnels beneath the old tanneries through an entrance that didn't appear on any map. Corvus led the way, a lantern in one hand, a blade in the other. The passage sloped downward for several hundred yards before opening into a chamber that stole Kael's breath.
The seal dominated the space — a circular formation of standing stones arranged around a central point where reality seemed to fold in on itself. The rift beyond was barely visible, a wound in the world that leaked darkness and light in equal measure. Spiritual pressure radiated from it in waves, making Kael's teeth ache.
"This is one of the original seventy-two," Corvus said quietly. "Sealed during the Abyssal Wars by the first generation of Awakened. It's been maintained by the Orders for centuries, but the maintenance has been... Incomplete. Someone has been sabotaging the reinforcement rituals from inside."
"Which Order?"
"All of them, at different times. The moles have been working together, despite their different loyalties. They serve something bigger than inter-Order politics."
Kael approached the seal. Up close, he could see the damage — hairline fractures in the standing stones, spiritual energy leaking from seams that should have been solid. The Entities beyond were pressing against their prison, testing for weaknesses.
And he could feel something else. A presence in the darkness beyond the rift. Something old. Something aware.
It was watching him.
"The seal needs fresh essence," Corvus said. "Normally, we'd harvest from the dead — there's no shortage of corpses in Thornwick's lower districts — but the transfer ritual requires someone who can draw without triggering feedback. We need a Reaver."
Kael knelt beside one of the fractured stones. His fingers traced the damage, feeling the spiritual energy bleeding away like blood from a wound.
"I can do this," he said. "But I need bodies. Fresh ones. And I need to understand exactly how much essence the seal requires."
"We can provide bodies. The Morticians have been stacking casualties in their cold storage — they don't check their inventory closely enough to notice minor losses."
"And the ritual?"
Corvus produced a scroll from his coat, ancient parchment covered in symbols that seemed to writhe in the lantern light.
"This was recovered from the Sepulcher archives," he said. "The first Reaver's personal records. It contains the original seal maintenance protocols, including the energy transfer formulas."
Kael took the scroll. The parchment felt warm in his hands, almost alive.
The Sepulcher archives. The records of the first Soul Reaver. The same archives that contained information about his mother's disappearance, about the Whisper King's identity, about the true nature of the seal network.
He looked at Corvus. "Why do you have this? The Orders guard the Sepulcher with their lives. No one gets in without authorization."
"The Vigil has its ways. And the Sepulcher's guardians are not all loyal to the compromised leadership. Some of them remember what the Orders were supposed to be."
Kael tucked the scroll into his coat. Tomorrow, he would study it. Tonight, he would rest. Tomorrow, the work would begin.
"Take me to the bodies," he said. "We have three weeks to save a city."
Corvus led him back through the tunnels, toward the surface and whatever came next.
Behind them, the Entities pressed against their prison. And somewhere in the darkness beyond the rift, something ancient and terrible smiled.

The cold storage facility occupied a basement level beneath one of Thornwick's mortuaries — a facade that allowed the Morticians to operate legally while conducting business in the shadows. Corvus navigated the back corridors with the confidence of someone who had done this before, pausing at intervals to check for guards or surveillance wards.
"Thirty-seven bodies in storage unit seven," he whispered. "Died within the past week. Good harvest quality."
Kael nodded. His leg was bleeding again, staining his boot with each step. He needed rest, treatment, time to process the information he'd gathered. Instead, he followed Corvus into the storage facility's depths.
The smell hit him first — formaldehyde and decay, a combination that turned his stomach. The Morticians were thorough in their preservation methods, slowing decomposition through chemical treatment, but death left its mark regardless. The air hung thick with residual spiritual energy, the echoes of departed souls clinging to their emptied vessels.
Corvus unlocked storage unit seven and stepped aside.
