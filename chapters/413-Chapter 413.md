The dead man's eyes stared at the gaslit ceiling of the morgue, and Kael felt his own pulse quicken as he knelt beside the corpse. Three days dead. Too long. But a Sovereign's remains—even degraded—could yield something worth the risk.
"You shouldn't be here." The voice came from behind, feminine and sharp with barely contained fury. Kael didn't turn. His fingers hovered an inch above the cold flesh, sensing the fading spiritual residue. "The Mortician's Guild has jurisdiction over—"
"Your Guild sold this one to the Iron Vanguard six hours ago." Kael pulled his hand back, examining the black residue clinging to his fingertips. Soul Reaver. Zero spiritual cost. Every other Awakened in Thornwick would kill for that freedom. None of them understood the price. "I checked with Brennan at the docks. Nice fellow. Drinks like a fish but talks like a clerk when you buy him enough gin."
Lyra stepped into his peripheral vision, her dark cloak doing little to hide the silver threading along its seams—Order of Dawn insignia, hastily concealed. Her jaw worked silently for a moment before she spoke. "You broke into a secured mortuary."
"I walked through the front door. The lock was decorative at best." Kael stood, wiping his hand on his coat. "I'm not here to cause trouble, Lyra. I'm here to do what you people should be doing already."
The morgue occupied a corner of the Underhallow district, where steam vents from the city's industrial sector created perpetual fog. Somewhere above, a factory whistle shrieked the midnight shift change. The smell of preservation chemicals and old death hung thick in the air.
"Do what?" Lyra's voice dropped, her hand drifting toward the blade at her hip. Kael noted the movement without concern. She wasn't dumb enough to draw on him. Not here. Not yet. "Steal from the dead? Desecrate remains for your hunger?"
"My hunger." A dry laugh escaped him. "Is that what the Order teaches these days? That the Soul Reaver is just another monster under the bed?"
"You're standing over a corpse you have no right to touch."
"A corpse that was a cultist facilitator three days ago." Kael turned to face her fully, letting her see the cold amusement in his eyes. "The Ironside butchery on Merchant's Row. The poisoning of the Thornwick water supply. The disappearance of seventeen children from the Ashwick slums. All facilitated by the man whose body you're so determined to protect."
Lyra's expression flickered. Surprise. Recognition. Then the mask returned. "The Orders are handling those investigations."
"The Orders are handling their own incompetence." Kael gestured at the corpse. "This man—Corvan Marsh—sold information to cultist cells throughout the eastern districts. He knew names. Locations. Ritual schedules. And instead of extracting that intelligence through proper channels, your people let him rot here until his soul fragments dispersed into useless static." He stepped closer to her. "I can get that information. Right now. Before the last traces fade forever."
The temperature in the room seemed to drop. Lyra's hand trembled slightly before she forced it back to her side. "And what would it cost you? What would you take?"
"Only what he's already lost." Kael held up his hand again, the black residue glinting dully in the gaslight. "His memories. His skills. The things that made him valuable. I don't need his soul—his soul is gone. I'm just... Recycling the leftovers."
"Recycling." Lyra spat the word. "That's what you call it."
"That's what it is." Kael shrugged. "The Law of Conservation. Soul energy can't be created or destroyed. When this man died, his energy didn't vanish—it started dispersing back into the Abyss. In twenty-four hours, it'll be irretrievable. All that information, all those skills, gone forever." He paused, letting that sink in. "I can save some of it. Not everything. Not the person he was. But the knowledge? The practical application? That's salvageable."
"And you just happened to be passing by a secured mortuary at midnight."
"I was passing by the Iron Vanguard's holding cells two hours ago, where they were processing this body for execution." Kael's smile turned sharp. "Funny how things work out. The Vanguard wanted him alive for questioning, but the Order of Dawn invoked Concordat rights and demanded jurisdiction. Seems there's a political dispute about who gets to interrogate a dead man."
Lyra's eyes narrowed. "You have contacts in the Vanguard."
"I have contacts everywhere, Lyra. That's what happens when you're an independent operator in Thornwick." Kael turned back to the corpse. "Now. We can continue this philosophical discussion about the ethics of necromancy, or you can let me harvest this body before the window closes. Your choice."
Silence stretched between them. Kael could hear the distant rumble of a steam tram passing through the district above, the hiss of escaping pressure valves, the muffled sounds of the city that never slept.
"Harvest it." Lyra's voice was barely a whisper. "I'll watch. And if you take anything more than you should—if you try to bring him back or corrupt his soul in some way—I will end you."
"Noted." Kael knelt again, placing both palms flat against Corvan Marsh's chest.
The sensation was always strange at the point of contact. Like pressing his hands against still water that inexplicably resisted, thick and viscous. Spiritual energy had texture, and corpse-energy had the texture of old wine left too long in the sun—fermented, souring, losing its original character.
He pushed.
The harvest was different from fresh kills. When someone died seconds ago, the extraction felt like drinking from a fire hose—overwhelming, dangerous, requiring careful filtering. This was more like siphoning dregs from the bottom of a barrel. Diminished. Fragmented. But present.
Images flickered through Kael's mind as he worked. Corridors. Faces. A woman with green eyes laughing at something he'd said. The smell of coal smoke and burning paper. Numbers scrawled on parchment—dates, locations, amounts of soul-essence traded like common spices.
And underneath it all, a constant humming. A resonance that Kael had learned to associate with Abyssal contact. Corvan Marsh had touched something dark, something connected to the 72 sealed rifts, and he'd done it repeatedly over a period of years.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted degraded memories from the remains. Fragmentary intelligence acquired. Map of cultist safe houses in eastern Thornwick partially reconstructed. Detection of repeated Abyssal resonance patterns in subject's spiritual residue.]
Kael opened his eyes, gasping slightly as the connection severed. His hands were shaking—not from exertion, but from the sheer *wrongness* of handling information that should have been impossible to obtain from a three-day-old corpse.
"What did you see?" Lyra demanded.
Kael wiped his palms on his coat, buying time to organize his thoughts. "Enough. Safe houses. Supply routes. The identity of at least two other facilitators still operating in the city." He paused, the Abyssal resonance still echoing in his skull. "And something else. Your cultist friend wasn't just passing information. He was feeding resources. Soul-essence shipments. Weapons. People."
"People?"
"Recruits, I think. Or sacrifices." Kael stood, his knees protesting the cold stone floor. "The resonance pattern I detected suggests regular contact with an Abyssal Entity. Not a rift—something at a remove. A fragment or a proxy."
Lyra's face had gone pale. "You're certain?"
"I'm reading the spiritual residue of a man who spent years communing with something that exists beyond the Veil. If you're asking whether I'm certain about the technical details of that communion, no. But the pattern itself is unmistakable." Kael moved toward the door. "The question is what your Order intends to do about it."
"Wait." Lyra grabbed his arm, her grip surprisingly strong. "That information—the safe houses, the routes—you said you reconstructed a map."
"Partially. The memories were degraded. I'm missing pieces."
"But you have something. Tangible intelligence that could help us locate and dismantle these cells."
Kael stopped, considering her tone. Calculating. "I have something. Whether it's yours depends on what you're offering."
"We're not negotiating with a Soul Reaver."
"Then we're not negotiating." Kael tried to pull away, but Lyra held firm. "Let go, Lyra. The harvest is done. Whatever happens next is between me and your superiors."
"You're making a mistake." Her voice cracked slightly. "Coming here. Doing this. The Shadow Court knows about you. They've been tracking your movements for weeks."
The words hit Kael like ice water. "Shadow Court?"
Lyra released his arm, stepping back. Her expression had shifted from anger to something more complex—fear, maybe, or reluctant respect. "They've been monitoring all Soul Reavers. There's some kind of... Project. Initiative. I don't know the details, but my sources in the Order of Dawn say they're gathering Reavers. Testing them. For what purpose, I can't say."
"And you decided to share this information because...?"
"Because if the Shadow Court acquires you, it won't just be bad for you. It'll be bad for everyone." Lyra's hand dropped to her blade, not threateningly, but with the burden of someone making a difficult decision. "Kael, listen to me. The six Orders exist to maintain the seals. To fight the Abyss. To prevent the 72 Entities from breaking through and consuming everything. That's not propaganda—that's survival. And Soul Reavers..." She hesitated. "Soul Reavers are wild cards. You're neither aligned with the Orders nor bound by their restrictions. That makes you dangerous to everyone, including yourself."
"You're warning me out of concern for my wellbeing?"
"I'm warning you because I don't want to be the one who has to kill you when the time comes." Lyra's eyes met his, hard and unflinching. "I've seen what you can do. What you've done. And I've also seen the reports from the Whispering Heights incident—seventeen cultists dead, all of them harvested, their bodies left in configurations that suggested experimentation. If you're becoming something worse than a cultist, something the Orders can't control—"
"Something the Orders can't control." Kael laughed, genuinely amused. "Lyra, I hate to break this to you, but the Orders can't control anything. Your Council of Six hasn't mediated a dispute in fifty years. Your Concordat is a joke that every faction violates through proxy warfare. And your intelligence networks?" He shook his head. "I'm standing in a secured mortuary that was supposed to be under Iron Vanguard protection. I walked in through the front door."
"The security—"
"Was laughable. Three guards, all of them drunk or asleep. One ward on the building that any stage-3 Awakened could bypass." Kael stepped closer to her, lowering his voice. "Your institutions are failing, Lyra. The seals are degrading. The cultists are organizing. And the Shadow Court is playing games that could end the world. You want to know what I think? I think you're more afraid of what I represent than what the cultists are truly doing. Because at least the cultists follow rules. At least they have a chain of command, a clear ideology, a predictable pattern of behavior."
"And you don't?"
"I'm a Soul Reaver in a city full of corpses. Predictable isn't exactly my style." Kael turned toward the door again. "You want my help? Bring it to the table. Actual resources, actual leverage. Not moral lectures about the sanctity of death, not warnings about organizations that are already tracking me, and not the empty promise of Order protection. Those things mean nothing."
"Where will you go?"
"Away from here. Somewhere I can process what I've harvested without your Order's sentinels breathing down my neck."
Kael pushed through the morgue's heavy oak door, stepping into the fog-laden street. The gaslights cast pools of sickly yellow illumination, barely cutting through the mist. Somewhere in the distance, a clock tower began to chime—one, two, three in the morning.
He made it three blocks before he sensed the presence.
Not spiritual energy—that would have been easy. This was something subtler. A wrongness in the pattern of the fog, a gap in the ambient sounds of the city. Someone was following him, and they were good.
Kael kept walking, hands loose at his sides, mind racing through options. His recent harvest pulsed in the back of his consciousness—the map of safe houses, the names, the faces. Information that could get people killed if it fell into the wrong hands. Information that certain organizations would kill to obtain or destroy.
The fog thickened as he turned onto Carver Street, a narrow lane lined with shuttered warehouses and abandoned tenements. Perfect for ambushes. Perfect for disappearances.
A figure stepped out of an alley ahead of him, blocking the path. Tall, gaunt, dressed in robes that seemed to absorb the gaslight fairly than reflect it. No spiritual signature—either a non-Awakened or someone masking their presence with practiced skill.
"Master Ashborn." The voice was soft, almost pleasant. "We've been expecting you."
Kael stopped, keeping his distance. "You've got me at a disadvantage."
"Names are unnecessary. What matters is purpose." The figure tilted its head, studying him with an intensity that made Kael's skin crawl. "You carry something. Information. Fragments of a mind that touched the Abyss. We require it."
"Requisition, huh?" Kael's hand drifted toward the knife at his belt. "And if I refuse?"
"Then we take it by other means." The figure raised one hand, and the fog around them began to move, swirling in patterns that defied natural airflow. "The Shadow Court does not negotiate, Soul Reaver. We just... Acquire."
Three more figures emerged from doorways and shadows, surrounding him in a rough semicircle. Still no spiritual signatures. Still no clear indication of their capabilities.
"The Shadow Court." Kael kept his voice steady. "I've heard about you. Deniable operations arm for Orders that want to violate the Concordat without leaving evidence. Assassinations. Sabotage. Information warfare."
"Impressive." The tall figure sounded genuinely surprised. "Most people know nothing of our existence."
"Most people don't survive encounters with your operatives."
A soft laugh echoed from somewhere in the fog. "Survival is only a matter of perspective. Some of our subjects survive entirely well, truly. Others..." The figure shrugged. "Others become subjects in a different sense."
Kael felt his pulse quicken as the implications settled in. Shadow Court experiments. The same organization that was supposedly monitoring Soul Reavers, testing them, acquiring them for purposes unknown.
"I harvested from a corpse three hours ago," he said carefully. "The information is in my head. It's not something I can just hand over."
"Then you will tell us what you learned. Every detail. Every fragment." The tall figure stepped closer. "And afterward, you will accompany us to a facility where your... Unique talents can be properly evaluated."
"And if I decline?"
"Then we will be forced to take more persuasive measures."
The fog churned around Kael, pressing in like a living thing. He could feel the spiritual pressure building—not from the figures themselves, but from whatever they were channeling. The Shadow Court had access to Abyssal techniques, it seemed. Or maybe they were using artifacts connected to the 72 sealed Entities.
"Here's the thing," Kael said, drawing his knife with deliberate slowness. "I've spent the last three years learning what I can do. What my limits are. What my options are when people try to force me into situations I'd fairly not accept." He let the blade catch the gaslight. "And I've found that the best way to handle threats is to eliminate them before they become problems."
"You're threatening the Shadow Court?"
"I'm informing you of my intentions." Kael's eyes swept across the semicircle of figures, cataloging positions, estimating distances. "You want information from my head? Come and get it. But understand—I'm not going quietly. And I'm not going to be your test subject."
The tall figure sighed, a sound almost like disappointment. "A shame. We had hoped for cooperation."
The fog exploded inward.
Kael moved.
Three years of combat training, two Sovereign-stage abilities harvested from fresh kills, and pure desperate instinct propelled him forward. His knife carved an arc through the nearest figure, biting deep into what felt like flesh but spurted no blood. The figure staggered, its robes parting to reveal skin that was pale, waxy, wrong.
Not a person. A construct. A puppet animated by some form of spiritual energy.
Kael twisted, using the figure's momentum to spin himself toward the second operative. His free hand came up, fingers extended, and he *pushed*—not with spiritual energy, which he couldn't afford to waste, but with raw physical force channeled through techniques he'd harvested from a brawler named Marcus the Unbroken.
The blow caught the operative in the throat, crushing cartilage with a satisfying crunch. The figure collapsed, twitching.
The tall figure was chanting now, words in a language that made Kael's teeth ache. The fog responded, solidifying into tendrils that reached for his limbs, his throat, anything they could grasp.
Kael bit back a snarl. Soul Reaver abilities didn't require spiritual energy—that was their primary advantage. But they did require contact with corpses. And there were no corpses here.
Only puppets. Only constructs.
Only things that had been alive once.
"Two minutes!" The tall figure's chant intensified. "Hold him for two minutes!"
Kael's knife found another target, severing a grasping tendril that hissed like a wounded serpent. Another operative came at him with a blade of its own—black steel, humming with contained energy. They exchanged blows, steel ringing against steel, each trying to find an opening.
The construct was fast. Stronger than any human of its apparent build had any right to be. But Kael had fought Demigods. He had survived encounters that would have killed lesser Awakened a dozen times over. He knew how to read movements, how to anticipate strikes, how to turn an opponent's aggression against them.
He let the construct lunge. Sidestepped. Brought his elbow down on the back of its skull with enough force to crack bone.
The figure crumpled.
Kael spun, looking for the next target, and found himself face-to-face with the tall operative. Close enough to see the thing that lived behind those eyes—not a person, but something that had consumed one. Something that wore human skin the way a man wore a coat.
"Fascinating," the figure whispered. "You've killed three constructs already. The others managed one at best."
"I'm not like the others."
"No. You're not." The tall figure smiled, revealing teeth that were too sharp, too numerous. "That's precisely why we want you."
The fog surged again, and this time Kael couldn't dodge. It wrapped around his ankles, his waist, his chest, pinning his arms to his sides. His knife clattered to the cobblestones.
"The evaluation will be painless," the figure continued, stepping closer. "only invasive. We need to understand how your abilities function. What makes you different from standard Soul Reavers. Why you can harvest without degradation while others crumble under the strain."
"I don't know," Kael managed through gritted teeth. "I've always been this way."
"Precisely what we hoped to hear." The figure raised one hand, and Kael felt something cold press against his forehead. "You're the first natural-born Reaver in recorded history. All others required the ritual, the corruption, the forced Awakening. But you?" The figure's smile widened. "You just *are*. That makes you invaluable."
Kael's vision began to blur at the edges. The cold was spreading, sinking into his skull, trying to find purchase in the meat of his brain.
"What are you doing?"
"Reading you. All your memories, all your skills, all your secrets—soon to be ours." The figure's voice seemed to come from very far away. "Don't struggle. It will only make the extraction more... Complicated."
Kael's lungs burned. His heart hammered against his ribs. He could feel the Shadow Court operative rifling through his mind like pages in a book, taking what it wanted, discarding the rest.
And underneath that violation, something stirred.
The Faceless One's fragment. The piece of the Abyssal Entity that Kael had carried since the incident at Whispering Heights. It had been dormant, quiet, waiting. But now, in the face of external intrusion, it was waking up.
"No," Kael thought—or spoke, or screamed. He couldn't tell anymore. "Not now. Not like this."
The fragment responded anyway. It unfurled inside him like a dark flower blooming in reverse, and Kael felt power flood through pathways he hadn't known existed. The fog-constructs shattered. The tall figure staggered backward, its mask of humanity slipping to reveal something terrible underneath.
"What—" The figure's voice became a shriek. "What are you?"
Kael couldn't answer. He was drowning in sensation, in energy, in a darkness that tasted like the space between stars. He could feel the fragment calling to its source—the Faceless One, somewhere beyond the Veil, watching through his eyes.
The fog cleared. The street was empty. Kael found himself alone among the wreckage of the Shadow Court's assault, his knife in one hand and no memory of how he'd gotten it back.
He was shaking. His nose was bleeding. The taste of copper filled his mouth.
"What just happened?" he whispered.
No one answered.
Kael stumbled toward the nearest wall, using it to support himself as his legs threatened to give out. The fragment had retreated, sinking back into dormancy, but its presence lingered like a bruise on his soul.
The Shadow Court knew what he was. They wanted to study him, understand him, use him. And worse—they had information about his abilities that he didn't even possess about himself.
Natural-born Reaver. The first in recorded history.
Kael laughed, a sound that echoed hollow in the empty street. Of course. Of course he was something impossible. Something that shouldn't exist.
He pushed off the wall, forcing himself to move. He needed shelter. Time to process. A chance to understand what the fragment had shown him, what the Shadow Court had learned from their partial extraction.
Behind him, the clock tower chimed four. Dawn was still hours away, but Kael could already feel the first hints of it—the subtle shift in pressure that accompanied the approaching sun.
He made it two streets before he collapsed in a doorway, darkness swallowing the edges of his vision.
The last thing he heard before losing consciousness was a voice. Not his own. Something speaking from inside him, using his mouth without his permission.
"Seven," it whispered. "Seven seals will break first. You must find them before they wake."
Then nothing.
Kael woke to the sound of gunfire.
Sunlight streamed through gaps in a boarded window, painting stripes across a room he didn't recognize. Dusty floorboards. Broken furniture. The smell of mildew and old smoke.
He sat up slowly, cataloging his condition. Headache. Nausea. A bone-deep exhaustion that made even breathing feel like effort. The fragment was quiet now, dormant, but Kael could still feel it—watching, waiting.
The gunfire continued outside. Not the sharp crack of personal weapons, but the deeper boom of military-grade firearms. Something big was happening in Thornwick.
Kael pushed himself to his feet, wincing as his joints protested. His knife was still strapped to his belt. His coat was torn, stained with blood that might have been his own. He moved to the window, peering through the gaps.
Smoke rose from the eastern districts. Flames licked at buildings near the harbor. And in the sky above the city, something was wrong with the air itself—a shimmer, a distortion, like heat rising from summer pavement.
The Veil was thinning.
"Seven seals will break first," Kael muttered, repeating the fragment's warning. "Find them before they wake."
But which seven? And where?
A door slammed somewhere downstairs. Kael tensed, hand moving to his knife, but the figure that appeared in the doorway was familiar.
Lyra looked worse than he felt—her cloak torn, blood on her face, wild desperation in her eyes. "You're alive."
"Disappointing, I know." Kael's voice came out rougher than expected. "What's happening?"
"Everything." Lyra stepped into the room, her breathing heavy. "The Great Rift isn't the only one destabilizing. We got reports overnight—six other sites across the continent showing increased activity. Seismic readings. Spiritual fluctuations. Cultist movements near at least four of them." She met his eyes. "The Shadow Court activated something. Some kind of cascade protocol. They're accelerating the timeline."
Kael's blood ran cold. "The Shadow Court. They attacked me last night. Tried to extract information directly from my mind."
"What did they learn?"
"I don't know. But they called me a natural-born Reaver. Said I was the first in recorded history."
Lyra stared at him. For a long moment, she didn't speak. Then she laughed—a sharp, bitter sound. "Of course you are. Of course that's what you are."
"What does that mean?"
"It means the Shadow Court has been searching for natural-born Reavers for decades. Trying to understand why some Awakened develop the ability spontaneously while others require the ritual." Lyra slumped against the doorframe. "The theory was that natural Reavers could access powers that ritualized ones couldn't. That they were inexplicably... Connected to the source. The original Soul Reaver."
"The original?"
"There are stories. Myths, really." Lyra's voice dropped. "About a being that existed before the Abyssal Wars. Before the Orders. Something that could harvest souls directly from the living, not just the dead. They called it the First Reaver. And supposedly, it didn't die—it fragmented. Spread itself across the world, waiting for the right vessels to emerge."
Kael's hand went to his chest, pressing against the place where the Faceless One's fragment resided. "And if the Shadow Court is trying to collect these vessels? These natural Reavers?"
"Then they're trying to reassemble the First Reaver." Lyra's eyes met his, haunted. "Or control it. Use it for whatever purpose they've been planning for two hundred years."
The gunfire outside intensified. Somewhere in the city, something screamed—a sound no human throat could produce.
"The cascade protocol," Kael said. "They activated it. That's why the seals are failing."
"That's why the seals are failing faster than projected." Lyra pushed off the doorframe, straightening. "We have hours, maybe less. The Entities are stirring. If we don't stabilize the primary sites—"
"We won't." Kael shook his head. "The Orders don't have the resources to respond to six simultaneous crises. Let alone whatever the Shadow Court has planned."
"Then what do you suggest?"
Kael considered his options. The information from Corvan Marsh's harvest was still fresh in his mind—safe houses, routes, names. Intelligence that could help identify cultist cells, disrupt supply chains, maybe even locate key players in the conspiracy.
But it wasn't enough. Not against an organization that had been planning for two centuries.
"The other natural Reavers," he said slowly. "If the Shadow Court is collecting them, that means there are others. People who can do what I do. People the Shadow Court doesn't control yet."
"Assuming any of them want to help."
"Some might. Some might be motivated by self-preservation alone." Kael moved toward the door. "I need to find them. Before the Shadow Court does."
Lyra grabbed his arm again—the same grip, the same stubborn strength. "And if you find them? What then? You form your own faction? Fight the Entities yourself?"
"I do what I've always done." Kael met her gaze. "I take what I need. I harvest what I can. And I survive."
"You can't survive this alone."
"I haven't been alone for three years, Lyra. I've had enemies. Allies. People who wanted me dead and people who needed me alive." He pulled free from her grip. "The difference now is that the stakes are higher. The playing field is bigger. And the people I'm playing against have been at this longer than I've been breathing."
Outside, the screaming stopped. In its place came a sound like reality tearing—a deep, resonant crack that made Kael's bones ache.
The Veil was breaking.
Kael moved toward the stairs, toward the door, toward whatever waited beyond. He didn't know if he could stop the cascade. Didn't know if he could find the other Reavers in time. Didn't know if he could trust the fragment inside him or the Order operative behind him.
But he knew one thing: the Shadow Court had made their move, and now the real game had begun.
Seven seals. Seven Reavers. Seven chances to prevent the end of everything.
Or seven opportunities for the enemy to finish what they'd started.
Kael stepped into the burning morning light of Thornwick, ready to find out which.
