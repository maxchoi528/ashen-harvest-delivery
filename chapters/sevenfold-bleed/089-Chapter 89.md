Chapter 89: Terms of Entry

The morning air carried the taste of iron. Not weather-iron, not the mineral tang of approaching snow.

Blood-iron.

Roan Vance crouched behind the collapsed dormitory wall, counting exits through the debris field. Three to the main courtyard. One through the service tunnel that smelled like mold and old cooking grease. Two above via the structural damage that no one had bothered to seal.

*Six ways out. Four of them funnel into kill-zones if House Greel's people are smart.*

They were smart. House Greel didn't recruit stupid enforcers.

[Inversion capacity: 3/3.]

The notification pulsed once at the edge of his awareness. Clean. Full. He'd slept well for once, or his body had decided that dying tomorrow was preferable to dying today.

Behind him, the other survivors had stopped crying. That worried him more than the crying had.

Seven students from the lower academy dormitories. They'd made it through the first night by pure luck: the attack had come from the east wing, and they'd been sleeping in the west corner like afterthoughts. By the time they'd stumbled into the corridors, the enforcers had moved on to "priority targets."

Which meant they had maybe six hours before those enforcers came back to clean up stragglers.

Six hours. Seven civilians. One Tempered-tier student who wasn't supposed to exist.

The tally was ugly. "Water," he said to the nearest one, a girl with ash in her hair and a split lip who looked like she was trying very hard not to throw up. "There's a storage cistern in the basement. The stairs are destroyed, but there's a rain pipe that connects."

She stared at him like he was speaking another language.

"Water," he repeated. "You need it. Go get it."

"You—you can't just—"

He turned away from her. Her survival wasn't his problem. Not technically. He had no oath to these people, no debt of honor, no reason to spend his single clean Inversion charge on someone who couldn't fight back.

*Brendt would.*

The thought arrived without invitation. Cold and irritating.

Brendt would spend hours organizing these survivors into defensive positions. Brendt would share his own rations, his own medicine, his own time. Brendt would get three of them killed by being too visible while being too kind.

But two would live who otherwise wouldn't.

Roan hadn't majored in Brendt's particular brand of stupid.

"The cistern," he said to the girl. "Thirty meters west. Don't make noise. If you see anyone in House Greel colors, you run back here. Don't fight. Don't scream. Just run."

She nodded. Finally. Action was easier than processing.

He turned to the others. Four boys, two girls. All Initiates or below. All with that particular blankness that came after witnessing violence they hadn't trained for.

*They're going to die anyway,* he thought. *The only question is whether they die quiet or loud.*

The loud ones attracted House Greel's people faster. The quiet ones might slip through the cracks if they were lucky.

Roan didn't believe in luck. He believed in mathematics.

*Three options. Run, hide, or fight.*

Running meant dispersing into the city. With street patrols active and the Sentinel Bureau paralyzed by the attack, they'd be picked off within hours. Individual targets were easier than groups.

Hiding meant finding somewhere the enforcers wouldn't check. The dormitories were compromised. The academy grounds were compromised. The only safe zone he knew of was the Veyrn Sanctum, and that was a fifteen-minute run in good conditions, twenty in combat conditions, and three of these students couldn't run for thirty seconds without vomiting.

Fighting meant turning seven civilians into a liability.

Not an option.

*Numbers were harder to bribe than people. Run and group, or hide and split. Those are the real choices.*

He looked at the girl with ash in her hair. She'd started toward the cistern.

Good. She could follow instructions. That made her marginally less likely to die.

Three hours passed.

The girl, her name was Tessa, apparently, though Roan hadn't asked, had organized the others into a loose formation behind the collapsed wall. They'd scavenged broken furniture, construction materials, anything that could block a blade or cushion a fall.

They weren't fighters. But they weren't panicked animals either.

Roan watched from his perch on a shattered beam, scanning the courtyard through a gap in the debris. Twice he'd seen enforcers moving in the distance. They hadn't looked this way.

Third time might not be the charm.

*The system says capacity is full, but that doesn't mean I should waste it.*

Every Inversion charge was a life he could save or a fight he could win. Using one to avoid a conversation with Brendt wasn't efficient.

Using one to save an Initiate civilian who'd stumbled into the wrong crossfire also wasn't efficient.

*What is efficient, then?*

That was the problem. Inversion wasn't a tool for optimization, it was a tool for survival. And survival had diminishing returns when everyone around you was already dying.

His Codex Shard pulsed.

[You're thinking too loud. I can practically hear it. — A.V.]

Roan suppressed the urge to touch the mark on his wrist. The Shard had been quiet for weeks after the last major incident. Now it was offering commentary like an unwanted advisor.

*Shut up.*

[You won't like what comes next if you keep calculating like this.]

*And what comes next?*

[Same thing that always comes next. You optimize. You survive. You win—]

The notification cut off. Incomplete thought.

Roan's jaw tightened. His father had been dead for over a decade. Whatever remained in the Codex Shard was an echo, not the man himself. Echoes could malfunction.

But that particular silence felt intentional.

The fourth hour brought the sound of fighting.

Not the sharp crack of controlled violence, the enforcers' preferred method, but the messy, desperate noise of people who'd already decided to die.

Roan moved to the gap in the debris. The courtyard was fifty meters wide. On the far side, near the main academy entrance, three students were backed against a decorative fountain. Two of them had weapons. One was a girl he vaguely recognized from Combat Theory lectures. Iris Vell.

House Vell. But not protected either, not Greel or directly threatened by the current purge.

The enforcers surrounding them were three. Professional spacing, weapon readiness, the stance that suggested they'd done this before. They weren't rushing. They were herding.

*They want her alive.*

Iris Vell was worth more breathing than unconscious. House Vell had resources, connections, blackmail material. Alive, she was a bargaining chip. Dead, she was a complication.

The enforcers hadn't noticed the collapsed dormitory wall. They were focused on their prize.

Tessa's group had gone silent behind him. Good. They'd learned the first lesson of survival: when someone else was the target, you made yourself invisible.

Roan calculated.

Three enforcers. All Tempered-tier at minimum. House Greel didn't send Initiates for high-value captures. Iris Vell was apparently Hardened-tier, which meant she'd lasted longer than most, but three-on-one against trained professionals wasn't good odds.

The two students with her were Initiates. Dead weight in a fight.

*I could help.*

The thought came without calculation. Raw. Irritating.

*Why?*

Because Iris Vell had never mocked him in the corridors. She'd never joined the whispered conversations about "the Vance boy who talks too much and fights too little." She'd ignored him, which was better than most.

Because House Vell was one of the three pillars of the Conclave, and helping their heir build goodwill that might matter later.

Because Brendt would.

*But Brendt isn't here.*

No. Brendt was across the city, coordinating with Warden Drogan or evacuating students from the unaffected dormitories. Brendt was where he was supposed to be, visible, helpful, doing the thing that mattered.

Roan was supposed to be hiding. Staying low. Preserving his position until the chaos passed.

The enforcers closed another step.

Iris raised her weapon, a short blade, not her preferred style. She'd dropped something better in the initial panic. Her stance was defensive. Predictable.

She was going to die in the next two minutes.

The figures said: let her.

The figures said: run when they turn their attention this way.

The figures said: this wasn't your fight, your obligation, your problem.

The figures said a lot of things.

Roan dropped from the beam. He came in from the enforcers' blind spot, the collapsed wall had created a shadow they hadn't bothered to check. The first one died before he registered the sound: a construction spike through the eye slit of his helm.

Roan didn't watch him fall. He was already moving.

The second enforcer turned too slowly. Tempered-tier strength met a point aimed at the gap in his armor, shoulder joint, where the plate didn't meet the chain. The spike went in high, somewhere near the collarbone.

Not fatal. Incapacitating.

The third enforcer was better.

She disengaged from Iris immediately, blade coming up in a defensive pattern that spoke of formal training. Her eyes tracked Roan's movement, calculating angles, range, threat level.

"Vance," she said.

Not a question.

*She knows who I am.*

Interesting. And concerning.

"You're supposed to be dead," she continued. Her stance shifted, offensive now, not defensive. "The Greel envoy put your name on the priority list three days ago."

The Greel envoy. The heir. The one who'd been quietly hostile since Roan had started climbing the academy rankings faster than expected.

*He's been watching me.*

Another complication in an equation that had too many variables already.

"I'm not," Roan said. "So your intelligence is outdated."

He didn't advance. The enforcer was holding distance, waiting for reinforcements or an opening. The incapacitated second was making sounds that would attract attention.

Thirty seconds. Maybe less before someone came to check.

The enforcer smiled. It didn't reach her eyes.

"The envoy wants you breathing," she said. "Something about 'questions.' I was supposed to bring you in before the rest of this mess." She tilted her blade toward the courtyard. "Iris Vell is secondary. You are primary."

Iris made a sound behind him. Surprise, maybe. Or offense that her capture had just been downgraded.

Roan's mind processed the new variable.

*They want me alive.*

This wasn't a purge anymore. This was a collection.

*What does the Greel envoy know?*

Not enough to target him specifically, not yet, but enough to have his name on a list. Enough to send enforcers with orders to capture rather than kill.

Enough to make this situation significantly more complicated.

"I don't think so," he said.

The enforcer's smile widened.

"No," she agreed. "I didn't think you'd come quietly."

She moved. The first exchange was a feint. High, fast, designed to force a parry and establish rhythm. Roan didn't take the bait. He gave ground, letting the blade whistle past his shoulder, and felt the air displacement on his neck.

Good distance control. Professional.

The second exchange was a thrust, low, targeting his thigh, where the gap in his borrowed armor was widest. He twisted, felt the blade score along the outside of his leg, and suppressed the flash of pain.

*Sidewound registered.*

The notification pulsed. A heartbeat of genuine damage before Inversion activated.

[Sidewound absorbed.]

[Conversion successful: +94 Vitalis.]

[Capacity: 3/3 → 2/3.]

He kept it. Later.

The enforcer pressed her advantage, blade dancing through patterns he recognized from House Greel family training archives. She was better than average. Significantly better.

But she was also alone, against an enemy who had a Codex Shard, a history of surviving things he shouldn't, and absolutely no intention of being "captured for questions."

Roan let her drive him back another step. Then another.

She was getting confident. Predictable. Every thrust came from the same angle, every feint preceded by the same shoulder movement.

*They're trained to standard patterns. I'm not.*

That was the advantage Drogan had drilled into him for months: opponents who learned formal technique expected opponents who learned formal technique. They couldn't account for someone who'd been taught to fight like the technique didn't matter.

He stopped giving ground.

The enforcer's eyes flickered, surprise, quickly masked. She adjusted, shifted her stance, prepared for something she couldn't predict because she'd never seen it before.

Roan hit her with the spike.

Not a thrust. Not a strike. A thrown weapon, from the hand that should've been defending. She'd read him for an offensive pattern; he gave her a desperate measure instead.

She twisted. The spike caught her shoulder instead of her throat. But it changed the angle of her blade by enough, not deep or fatal.

The impact took her off-balance for half a second. Half a second was enough. He closed, seized her wrist, twisted, felt the crack of bone and the spray of blood as the blade turned inward.

She screamed.

Roan let her fall.

Silence.

The courtyard felt smaller than it had moments ago. The fountain burbled. Somewhere in the distance, more fighting, but it was moving away, not closer.

Iris Vell was staring at him.

"You're the Vance boy," she said.

*Everyone keeps saying that.*

"Yes."

"They said you were weak."

"People say a lot of things."

She looked at the three enforcers. One dead. One groaning. One staring at the ceiling with empty eyes. Then back at Roan.

"The one who threw the spike," she said slowly. "That was—"

"Effective." He retrieved the spike from the enforcer's shoulder. She'd stopped bleeding, but not because she'd healed. Because her heart had stopped trying.

"Dirty," Iris corrected. "The instructors would have deducted points."

"The instructors aren't in charge anymore." He cleaned the spike on her cloak. "Someone is. The Greel envoy, apparently."

Iris's expression shifted. A slight tightening around the eyes, calculation, not fear.

"You know something," she said. Not a question.

"I know I'm on a list."

"So am I." She stood, swaying slightly. The two Initiates behind her were helping each other stay upright. "But you're primary. I'm secondary." Her eyes were sharp despite the circumstances. "What does the Greel envoy want with you specifically?"

*Good question.*

Roan didn't have an answer. That bothered him more than the fighting had.

"I don't know," he admitted. "Yet."

Iris studied him. Then she nodded, a small, businesslike motion that carried more weight than her injuries warranted.

"House Vell has resources," she said. "Intelligence networks. If the Greel envoy is making moves against you specifically, we should know why."

"We."

Roan noted the pronoun. Interesting.

"Why would House Vell help me?"

"Because House Greel is making moves against everyone. And because you just saved my life." She straightened her shoulders, wincing. "I'm not in the habit of owing debts—"

A slow, deliberate pattern of footsteps interrupted her. Not the frantic shuffle of survivors. Not the heavy boot of enforcers. The measured stride of a man who owned every inch of ground he crossed.

Kael Greel stepped into the rubble.

He was immaculate. Academy formalwear, untouched by dust or blood, as if he had dressed for a ceremony rather than a massacre. Behind him, two Forged-tier enforcers stood at perfect ease, scanning the debris for angles of attack.

His gaze swept the courtyard. It passed over Tessa and the huddled survivors like they were furniture. It rested on Iris. Then it settled on Roan.

"Three," Kael said, his voice carrying the lazy confidence of absolute power. "Three of my best enforcers, dead or ruined by a Tempered-tier nobody with a construction spike. The reports didn't do you justice, Vance."

Roan said nothing. He was calculating. Kael was Hardened. The enforcers behind him were Forged. Fight was not an option. Run was not an option. Talk was the only weapon left in the rack.

"I came here for her." Kael gestured at Iris. "A simple collection. Instead, I find a rat in the walls who thinks he's a wolf." He stepped closer, letting the silence stretch. "Tell me, Vance. Do you know the cost of what you just did?"

Roan met his gaze. "The cost of leaving a woman to die?" He let the question hang. "No. I don't have that number in my ledger."

The implication was clear. *Do you?*

Tessa made a small, terrified sound. Word would spread. Roan Vance had just insulted the Greel heir to his face in front of witnesses.

Kael's smile didn't waver. It turned colder. "Noble. And stupid. You just saved her. You chose a side. Do you know what happens to people on the wrong side of House Greel?"

"They get charming monologues from overconfident heirs?"

A flicker of genuine irritation crossed Kael's face. He stepped closer, dropping his voice so only Roan and Iris could hear. "You think this is a game. It isn't. Your mother lives in Greycross. Your sister studies at Aldermere. I will make their lives a long, slow testament to your foolishness. You traded their safety for Iris Vell. I hope she was worth it."

Roan's expression didn't change. Inside, he was cold, precise, the arithmetic of revenge and protection spinning through his mind. He had made the choice. He would live with the consequences. And he would make Kael Greel pay for every single one.

"You touch them," Roan said, his voice flat, "and I will make your death last longer than your father's reign. Empty threats from a dead man walking."

Kael laughed. It was a brittle sound, entirely divorced from humor. "I look forward to proving you wrong." He turned to Iris. "You're safe today, Vell. Because of him. Remember that. He chose you over his own blood. This debt has a face."

He walked away, the Forged-tier enforcers closing around him like a living shield.

The weight of his words hung in the air long after he was gone.

Iris let out a breath she'd been holding. "Roan… you just made an enemy for life."

"I've had enemies for life since I was eleven. At least this one has a face."

He turned to the group. "We're moving. Now. East to the Veyrn Sanctum."

As they gathered their wounded and their courage, Iris fell in beside him. She walked in silence for a long moment. Then:

"House Vell owes you. Utterly. This isn't a favor, Roan. It's a marker. Use it when you need it."

"I always cash my markers when the interest is highest." He didn't look at her. "Don't make me collect too soon."

"I won't forget."

"It's not about forgetting. It's about choice. You chose to offer this. I chose to accept. We both know how quickly alliances shift."

Iris didn't answer. The implication was clear.

The Codex Shard pulsed against his wrist.

[The board is set. The terms of entry are clear.]

[Capacity: 2/3.]

Roan counted the survivors. Nine. He counted the threats. Infinite. He counted the names on his father's list. Six.

*Kael Greel isn't on the list,* he thought. *But he's the gate guard. I just paid the toll with my family's security. Now I have to collect enough interest from this market to buy them back.*

He led the group east, into the shadow of the academy's outer wall, leaving the bodies and the promises behind.

Nine survivors. One debt. A hundred new equations.

The arithmetic was ugly. But it was finally adding up.
