Chapter 112: The House Table

The wind on the Crown Tier hit like a dare. Forty meters above the Black Spire's main structure, exposed to every current that rolled in from the Ironholm industrial district, the training platform offered no comfort and less mercy. Roan Vance stood at its center, arms loose at his sides, eyes tracking nothing.

The columns were plain. The execution wouldn't be.

Below, the Spire's rankings board cycled through names and numbers, most of them irrelevant. His own entry sat at position 247, showing a Vitalis reading that corresponded to a standard Hardened. The lie he wore like a second skin. Above that number, separated by precisely ninety-three positions, Cyrus Ashford's name glowed at 154. The gap was intentional. Calculated.

"Again," he said.

Brendt Holloway lowered his sparring staff, sweat darkening his collar. The gap between them had widened since the tournament. Not in skill. Brendt fought better now than he had three months ago, but in the fundamental disparity between someone training for placement and someone training for survival.

"You're going to tear something," Brendt said.

"Unlikely."

"You've been at this for six hours."

"Seven." The correction came without emphasis. "The Marrowforge Sutra repairs micro-fractures faster than I generate them. The tally favors continued exertion."

Brendt's jaw worked. He had learned, over the months, that arguing with Roan about the ledger was like arguing with a wall about architecture. The wall wouldn't move. Neither would Roan.

Fine. Seven hours it was.

Brendt reset his stance, staff horizontal, weight distributed for a sweeping parry. He was Tempered now, had broken through two weeks ago, catalyzed by the techniques Roan had passed along from the Veyrn Sanctum notes. His Vitalis reading hovered around six hundred. Still too low for the tournament's upper brackets. Not low enough for the camouflage Roan wore.

Roan moved.

The first strike came from an angle Brendt wasn't expecting, not because it was fast, but because it was slow. Deliberately, achingly slow, like watching ice form on a windowpane. The staff traced an arc that seemed to exist slightly out of phase with reality.

Brendt intercepted it. His parry was textbook, clean, executed exactly as Drogan had taught him.

It didn't matter.

Roan's follow-through became a pivot. The staff slipped past Brendt's guard not through force but through geometry, the angle of entry had been calculated before the first strike landed, a geometric inevitability disguised as a feint. His palm struck Brendt's sternum, not hard enough to injure, just hard enough to reset.

"Forty-three," Roan said.

"Forty-three what?"

"Degrees off-center. Your parry opens your left hip by forty-three degrees. Against an opponent who reads body mechanics, that's a kill."

Brendt straightened, rubbing his chest. "Against someone like you, everything is a kill."

"Correct." No false modesty. No deflection. Just acknowledgment of a truth. "Which is why we drill until the weakness isn't there."

The wind gusted, hard and cold, carrying the metallic tang of the Ironholm foundries. Roan breathed it in, catalogued the temperature drop, adjusted his stance by two degrees. The Codex Shard pulsed once against his seventh vertebra, warm, familiar, demanding nothing yet.

Six for six. He hadn't used his capacity since the envoy encounter. Two mortal-grade inversions remained, locked behind thresholds he didn't plan to touch unless the alternative was death. The balance of reserve was as important as the arithmetic of expenditure.

"Again," he said.

Brendt sighed and raised his staff.

Three exchanges later, the platform's access door opened.

Roan didn't turn. He knew the footsteps before they crossed the threshold, too even, too controlled, each impact separated by precisely less than a heartbeat regardless of the wind resistance. A fighter's gait. A survivor's gait.

Warden Drogan Hale stepped onto the platform, his Iron Hand catching the light like an accusation. The prosthetic fingers flexed once, twice, a tell Roan had learned meant the Warden wasn't pleased.

"Pell is here," Drogan said. "Early."

Brendt lowered his staff. "The Merchant? Now?"

"One hour ahead of schedule." Drogan's eyes found Roan. "He's brought something. Claims it's from Suthmere."

The name landed like a stone in still water. Suthmere. The southern continent. The Unbroken Peak, where Kaelen the Unbroken had made his home after the Founding Wars.

And where Aldric Vance had spent three years in exile before his return to North America.

Roan turned, finally, facing Drogan fully. The wind pulled at his hair, but his expression remained level.

"What did he bring?"

"That's the interesting part." Drogan's voice carried the weight of someone delivering news they wished they didn't have. "He brought a message. And a weapon."

"Classification?"

"An induction-class catalyst. Marked with the old seals." Drogan paused. "And a fragment of something the merchant won't describe. Says it's a key."

The math rearranged itself. Suthmere. The Unbroken Peak. The Founding Seven's only truly neutral member. The old signatory was Africa, mindrotted and declining. Sister the southern signatory ran the Southern European tribunals with an iron Gospel. Lord the eastern signatory controlled the Eastern territories through blood-pact tributaries. House Greel held the North. Lady the western signatory held Western Europe through softer means.

Kaelen held nothing. Wanted nothing. Trained nothing. Sent students away at the door.

Until now.

"He's not here for the catalyst," Roan said.

"No."

"He's here for me."

Drogan's silence was confirmation enough.

Brendt looked between them, staff still raised, confusion cutting through his practiced calm. "Would someone mind explaining—"

"The merchant has a standing arrangement with someone on the Unbroken Peak." Roan's voice remained level, but his pulse hadn't changed. That was the discipline talking, keeping his body from betraying what his mind was already calculating. "He carries messages. Rarely. Only when the sender considers the recipient worth the risk of exposure."

"Kaelen wants to meet you." Brendt's voice cracked slightly on the name. "The Unbroken. Why?"

"Unknown." Roan turned back toward the platform's edge, gazing out over the Ironholm sprawl. The foundries belched smoke into the grey sky, their rhythms as reliable as a heartbeat. "But the catalyst suggests he's aware of what I carry. The weapon fragment suggests he's aware of what I need."

"And the message?"

Roan shook his head. "The message is for Drogan."

The Warden's Iron Hand closed into a fist. The servos whined, high and sharp, cutting through the wind noise.

"Wasn't it," he said.

"Wasn't it," Roan agreed.

Brendt made a sound of frustration. "Would one of you please—"

"The message confirms something Drogan already knew." Roan's eyes remained fixed on the horizon, tracking a cargo airship as it descended toward the commercial docks. "Kaelen was part of the group that hunted Aldric Vance. The Black Squad. Drogan. Aldric. A third whose name was erased from every record the Conclave maintains."

Brendt's staff lowered another inch. "You're talking about—"

"I'm not talking about anything." Roan finally turned, meeting the Warden's gaze. Drogan's face had gone hard, the prosthetic hand trembling with a vibration that wasn't mechanical. "I'm stating facts and observing that the third member of the Black Squad is currently on the forty-seventh floor, waiting to deliver a package that will require a conversation I've been expecting for three years."

The wind screamed between them, carrying ash and the smell of hot iron.

"The package," Drogan said slowly. "It's not for you."

"No."

"It's for me."

"The weapon fragment. Yes." Roan's expression didn't change. "Your old partner wants his Iron Hand back, Warden. Or at least the design specifications that House Greel stole when he had you carved apart in the Svalbard debriefing."

Brendt stepped backward, as if physical distance could cushion the weight of what he was hearing. "Drogan. What is he—"

"An apology," Drogan said. "Seventy years late."

Roan watched the Warden's face cycle through something that might've been grief, or rage, or the exhaustion of carrying a weight for seven decades. The Inversion stirred against his spine, sensing the emotional turbulence, offering nothing. The Codex Shard didn't process feelings. It processed damage.

And right now, the damage was all around him.

"I'm going to meet the merchant," Roan said. "You're going to come with me. You're going to take the package. And then you're going to tell me what Kaelen wants."

"Roan—"

"Because the tally says a seventy-year-old apology doesn't arrive one hour early." He moved toward the platform's exit, not waiting for Drogan to follow. "Someone told him I was training here. Someone told him I've been using techniques that shouldn't exist. And someone—" he paused at the doorframe, one hand on the cold metal, "—told him that the Inversion is active."

Brendt caught up first, falling into step beside him as they descended the maintenance stairs. "You think someone inside the Spire is reporting to Kaelen?"

"I think someone inside the Spire is reporting to everyone. That's how information economies work." Roan's feet found each step with mechanical precision. "The question is who benefits."

"And if it's House Greel?"

The thought had occurred to him. House Greel controlled the Northern territories, ran the Sentinel Bureau through proxies, and had spent twenty years trying to acquire or destroy every artifact connected to the Inversion archive. If Kaelen was moving pieces on a board, House Greel would be watching.

"Then Kaelen is either an idiot or he's planning something that makes House Greel irrelevant." Roan hit the bottom of the stairwell, the air here warmer, heavy with the Spire's ambient heat. "Given that he survived the Founding Wars and has refused every alliance offer for seven decades, I'm betting on the second option."

They walked through the secondary training halls, past rows of initiates running forms and Tempered students exchanging controlled strikes. No one looked at Roan twice. The camouflage held. Position 247, Hardened tier, unremarkable.

Drogan's footsteps echoed behind them, heavier than the other two, weighted with something Roan didn't have enough data to calculate.

Forty-seventh floor. The merchant's suite.

Old Pell stood at the window when they entered, his back to the door, hands clasped behind him. He was smaller than Roan remembered, the years had compressed him somehow, pulling his shoulders inward, reducing his presence from "formidable" to "forgettable." A useful skill for a man who survived by being underestimated.

The case sat on the table between them. Gray metal, no markings, sealed with a clasp that required a specific Vitalis frequency to open. Induction-class security. The kind that could withstand a Paragon's direct assault.

Roan didn't touch it.

"Master Holloway," Pell said, without turning. "Young Mr. Vance. Warden Hale." The last name carried weight. History. "Thank you for making the time."

"You're early," Roan said.

"I am." No apology. No explanation. "Circumstances changed."

"What circumstances?"

Pell turned, and Roan saw the scar for the first time, a thin white line running from the merchant's left temple to his jaw, the kind that came from a blade held by someone who wanted to leave a message rather than a corpse. Recent. Within the last month.

"The kind that make a man reconsider his retirement plans." Pell's eyes found Drogan. "Warden. The package is yours. The instructions are separate."

Drogan didn't move. His Iron Hand hung at his side, servos silent, the prosthetic somehow more menacing in its stillness than it had been in motion.

"What does he want?"

The question came out flat. Brooking no deflection.

Pell's smile didn't reach his eyes. "The same thing he's wanted for seventy years. To fix what he broke." He gestured toward the case. "Inside, you'll find the original schematics. Every modification House Greel made after he took you apart. Every improvement Kaelen designed to compensate. It's yours, Warden. No strings."

"And the message?"

"Separate." Pell reached into his coat and withdrew a small envelope, unmarked, sealed with wax the color of old blood. "This is for the boy."

Roan took it. The paper was coarse, handmade, the kind that hadn't been produced in mass quantities for two centuries. Inside, a single sheet. The handwriting was precise, architectural, each letter drawn like it was being inscribed in stone.

*Vance boy—*

*Your father and I argued about the Inversion for three years. He thought it was a gift. I thought it was a weapon. We were both wrong.*

*It's a test.*

*The Founding Seven failed it. House Greel is failing it worse. The question isn't whether you'll break, the question is what you'll become when you do.*

*Come to the Peak when you're ready. Come alone. Come with your Warden if you must, but understand: what I have to show you can't be taken back.*

*You asked me once, through your merchant friend, whether I remembered your father. I remember everything. Including the name of the person who sold him to the Tribunal.*

*But that's a conversation for mountain air, not Spire halls.*

*K.T.*

Roan read it twice. Then a third time, parsing each sentence for hidden meanings, for traps, for the geometry of manipulation that characterized every interaction between power and information.

Kaelen the Unbroken was offering him something. A name. A truth. A weapon or a wound, impossible to tell which until he held it.

And he was inviting him to the Unbroken Peak, where Aldric had spent three years in exile, where secrets were kept like prayers, where the Founding Seven's only neutral member had been waiting for seventy years.

For what?

"The boy hasn't spoken in four minutes," Pell observed. His voice carried the amusement of a man watching someone else's calculations spin. "That's unusual, given what I've heard about him."

"Give him time," Brendt said. "He does this."

Roan looked up from the letter, his expression unchanged.

"The merchant who carried my first inquiry to the Unbroken Peak." His voice was calm. "That was you."

Pell inclined his head.

"You told me Kaelen didn't remember my father."

"I told you he didn't wish to discuss him." Pell's smile flickered. "Memory and discussion are different currencies, young Mr. Vance. The first is free. The second costs."

"And now?"

"Now he wants to spend." Pell moved toward the door, pausing at the threshold. "The Peak is a six-month journey from Ironholm if you travel by commercial routes. Faster if you don't. Kaelen won't wait forever. The invitation expires when the first snow falls on the upper terraces."

He was gone before Roan could respond, slipping into the hallway with the practiced silence of a man who had spent decades being overlooked.

The room felt smaller without him. The case sat on the table, unopened. The letter sat in Roan's hand, unread the fourth time.

Drogan hadn't touched either.

"He's lying," the Warden said.

"Probably."

"Then why are you considering it?"

Roan folded the letter along its original creases, his movements precise, deliberate.

"Because the name Kaelen is offering is worth more than the risk of the trap." He looked at Drogan, finally, seeing the old grief beneath the prosthetic's cold surface. "You know who sold Aldric to the Tribunal. You've known for seventy years. And you've never told me."

The room went quiet. "That's not a question," Drogan said.

"No." Roan tucked the letter into his jacket, against his chest, where the Codex Shard pulsed its quiet heat. "It's a data point. One of several I'm collecting."

"What are the others?"

"The case. The catalyst. The timing." Roan's eyes moved to the gray metal container, still sealed, still waiting. "Kaelen sent a weapon fragment that Drogan needs. A catalyst that I can use. And a message that makes no sense unless you've already heard the terms."

"The terms?"

"The Inversion is a test." Roan's voice remained level, but something in his posture had shifted, a tension that hadn't been there before, held in check by discipline and calculation. "A test of what, he doesn't say. A test administered by whom, he doesn't say. A test with what stakes—"

He stopped.

The Codex Shard pulsed against his seventh vertebra, warm and sudden, and for a moment, just a moment, he heard something that wasn't a voice and wasn't a memory.

*Be careful, boy. The mountain has teeth.*

Then it was gone, leaving only silence and the hum of the Spire's environmental systems and the weight of a letter that promised answers and demanded a journey.

"To the Unbroken Peak," Brendt said slowly. "You're considering it."

"I'm considering the ledger." Roan turned toward the case, finally, reaching out to rest his fingers on its cold surface. "Six months' travel. Unknown variables. A meeting with someone who helped hunt my father and now claims to remember everything."

"And House Greel," Drogan added. "If this becomes known, House Greel will move."

"Yes." Roan's fingers found the clasp, tracing its contours, reading the Vitalis lock's frequency with the ease of long practice. "But House Greel is already moving. The question is whether I'm moving toward something or running from something."

The clasp opened beneath his touch.

Inside, resting on gray velvet, was a blade.

Not a weapon. Roan had seen enough weapons to recognize the difference. This was a prototype. A fragment of something larger, incomplete, its edge still carrying the faint blue shimmer of experimental metallurgy. Work that required resources beyond any single workshop. Work that had been lost when the Inversion archive fell.

Drogan made a sound, something between a laugh and a sob, quickly stifled.

"That's—"

"Your design," Roan finished. "From before."

The Warden reached out with his flesh hand, stopping just short of touching the blade. His Iron Hand hung at his side, servos silent, the prosthetic suddenly seeming less like a weapon and more like a memorial.

"He kept it," Drogan said.

"He modified it." Roan pointed to the blade's core, where the metal's grain ran in a pattern that didn't match standard forging. "New alloy. Graphene lattice, probably. Work that only exists in three facilities worldwide."

"One of which is in Suthmere," Brendt said, catching up.

"One of which is in Suthmere."

Roan looked at the blade, cataloguing its dimensions, its weight, the frequency of Vitalis resonance it emitted. The balance unfolded automatically, combat applications, material properties, the cost of reverse-engineering the production process.

Then he closed the case.

"You'll take this," he said, pushing it toward Drogan. "The catalyst is mine. The message is mine. The blade is yours."

"Roan—"

"The Unbroken Peak isn't a decision yet. It's a possibility." Roan moved toward the door, his posture unchanged, his voice carrying the weight of someone who had already calculated seventeen steps beyond the current moment. "I need to know more before I commit. I need to test the catalyst. I need to review the tournament rankings for anomalies. I need to determine whether Pell is lying about the timing."

"And if you decide to go?"

Roan paused at the threshold, one hand on the doorframe, the letter pressed against his chest.

"Then I'll need someone to watch Greycross while I'm gone." His eyes found Brendt's. "Lyra needs a harder target than Kaelen is offering. Make sure she is."

Brendt held his gaze.

"And someone to remember why I'm coming back."

The door closed behind him.

- break -

The laboratory was cold. Not environmentally cold, the Spire maintained consistent temperatures throughout, but cold in the way that mattered: the cold of controlled conditions, of variables isolated, of truth stripped of context.

Roan sat at the workbench, the Suthmere catalyst in one hand, a standard analysis kit spread before him. The catalyst was small, a pill-sized sphere of compressed Vitalis crystals, induction-class, the kind that cost more than most students' annual tuition.

The kind that could catalyze a breakthrough if the user's foundation was strong enough.

The kind that could shatter a weak one.

Roan turned it slowly, watching light refract through its crystalline structure. Standard inventory notation classified it as a Tier-3 catalyst. Common enough to be affordable, rare enough to be regulated.

But this wasn't common inventory.

The shimmer running through its core wasn't standard Vitalis crystallization. It was something else, something he recognized from the Veyrn Sanctum notes, from the fragments of Inversion archive methodology that Aldric had left behind.

Resonance compound. The kind that synchronized with existing techniques rather than amplifying them.

The kind that Kaelen would send if he wanted someone to reach a specific threshold.

The Codex Shard pulsed against his spine, warm and curious. The Inversion stirred, processing the catalyst's composition, calculating the probability of successful reversal.

High. Higher than it should be for an unknown compound.

The figures said this was safe.

The arithmetic also said Kaelen was manipulating him.

The contradiction wasn't a problem. It was information. Every manipulation carried fingerprints, the tell of intent, the shape of desired outcome. If Kaelen wanted Roan to reach the Crowned tier, there was a reason. Understanding the reason was more useful than the catalyst itself.

Roan set the sphere down on the workbench, next to the letter he had read six more times since leaving Drogan's suite.

*The question isn't whether you'll break, the question is what you'll become when you do.*

The words looked different in low light. Sharper. More like a diagnosis than a warning.

He had broken before. The envoy fight. The House Greel suppression. The nights when the Inversion had pushed him past limits he hadn't known he had, leaving him shaking in his quarters with blood vessels burst in his eyes and the taste of copper on his tongue.

Each time, he had rebuilt. Tighter. Stronger. More aware of the costs.

But Kaelen wasn't talking about the physical breaks. He was talking about something else, the test the Inversion represented, the failure that the Founding Seven had supposedly committed.

The Founding Seven, who had destroyed an entire civilization to suppress the Inversion's spread.

The Founding Seven, whose current representative, House Greel, had spent twenty years hunting everything connected to that civilization.

The math suggested that Kaelen's "test" wasn't about power at all.

It was about choice.

Roan picked up the catalyst again, weighing it in his palm. Resonance compound. Synchronization catalyst. A tool designed to align the user's technique with something external, something that required compatibility to function.

Something that might require compatibility with the Inversion itself.

He could feel the Codex Shard's attention, the way it tracked the catalyst's proximity, the way it pulsed with something that might've been anticipation. The Inversion was old. Older than Aldric, older than the Inversion archive, older than the Founding Seven's memory of it.

And Kaelen had been alive for all of it.

The letter said the invitation expired when the first snow fell on the upper terraces. That gave him approximately four months, depending on Suthmere's weather patterns and the commercial routing Pell had mentioned.

Four months to reach Crowned. To investigate the anomalies. To determine whether Kaelen was an opportunity or a trap.

Four months to figure out what the Inversion was, before he climbed a mountain and asked a man who had helped destroy his father's world to explain why it mattered.

The laboratory hummed around him, climate control and lighting and the distant vibration of the Spire's core systems. Somewhere below, students were training for the next rankings. Somewhere above, the Conclave's representatives were calculating the politics of power.

And here, in the cold light of controlled conditions, Roan Vance held a catalyst that could change everything and a letter that promised answers that would change more.

He set the catalyst down.

Not yet. Not until he understood what he was taking.

The Codex Shard pulsed once, warm against his spine, patient. The tally was incomplete. Incomplete math was just another name for risk.

He would need more data. The catalyst's resonance signature. The cost of a secure route south. The timing of Kaelen's patience.

And he would need a plan for Lyra. Pell's fresh scar was a map of how far Greel's reach extended. If Kaelen knew Roan would come, they knew Lyra was the leverage. She had to be a harder target than the Unbroken Peak.

He would need to find out what Kaelen wanted.

And he would need to decide, before the first snow fell, whether the answers were worth the questions they would raise.
