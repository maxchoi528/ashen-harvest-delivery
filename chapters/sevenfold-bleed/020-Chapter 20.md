Chapter 20: The Caravan

The charm had stopped glowing.

Roan stared at the point on his palm where the warmth had withdrawn—not cold, exactly, but an absence where presence had been. The iron was darker now, its surface interrupted by new markings that hadn't existed before the sequence in the alley. He could still feel the weight of it, heavier than before, the way it pressed into his palm like it was trying to get somewhere.

His left palm still ached. Not from the fight, but from the place where the light had entered. When he looked at the skin, there was nothing. No mark, no wound. Just the phantom memory of heat.

The room was silent except for his own breathing.

He should have been exhausted. The adrenaline from the Bloodscourge had long since metabolized, leaving behind the flatness that came after the body had spent everything it had. His muscles ached in ways he could not fully account for—some from the fight, some from the absorption, some from the simple violence of being alive when your body was rewriting itself.

Instead, he was awake.

Wide awake.

He stood and walked to the window, holding the charm up to catch the thin winter light that made it through Greycross's perpetual overcast. The markings were clearer now—clusters of characters too small to read easily, spiraling out from a central point like some kind of notation system he had never seen. Some of the symbols looked almost familiar from theoretical texts. Others did not.

The charm pulsed when he held it closer to the glass.

The voice came without preamble, without tone, without anything that made it feel like communication rather than intrusion.

“You felt the awakening.” Aldric's cadence, but flattened. “The first layer of the Codex has opened.”

The room contracted. Roan looked at the charm in his hand, then at the empty air around him, then back at the charm. No glow. No visible change. Just his father's voice coming from the iron like it had every right to be there.

“How are you speaking to me.” Not a question. A demand for an explanation he did not have the framework to receive.

“I left a fragment of myself in the Codex before I died. A habit I developed, given my line of work. I would not recommend the practice—dying is harder when you are already partly gone.”

That was Aldric. The dark humor that made people laugh nervously before they realized he meant it. The shard pulsed in his palm like a second heartbeat.

“The Codex is a container,” the voice continued. “For my life's work. Every technique, every calculation, every dangerous thing I learned in forty years of running from people who wanted me dead. Most of it is damaged or locked. The Codex survived the transfer, but poorly. Think of it as a library where half the books were burned, and the other half are written in a language only I spoke.”

“Transferred from where.” Roan's voice was steady. His hand was not.

“That question has a long answer. You are not ready for it yet.”

The dismissal hit like a slap. Not harsh, not angry, just practical. Like being told he did not have the clearance to access a file. He had spent six years making himself into someone who could handle difficult information, and his dead father was telling him he was not ready.

“Try me.”

Silence. Then: “A locked answer. One you are not getting tonight.”

The words landed in his chest and stayed there. Roan tightened his grip on the shard. He wanted to push, to demand the full picture, but he knew his father well enough to recognize a boundary that would not move.

“Fine. Then explain what I have already seen. The Inversion. How it works. What it costs.”

A pause. When the voice came back, it was measured, clinical. “The Codex converts side effects into Vitalis. That is the core function. When you use a dangerous technique, the damage is real—but the Inversion catches that damage before it settles and converts it into usable energy. The conversion is instant. It is also permanent.”

“No lingering cost? No daily limit?”

“There is no cap on the conversion itself. The Codex does not count uses. It does not shut down after a fixed number of activations.”

Roan blinked. That was not what he had expected. He had braced for a constraint, a hard ceiling that would force him to ration his use. “Then what stops me from using it constantly?”

“The same thing that stopped me: exposure.” His father's voice sharpened, losing its flatness for a fraction of a second. “Every conversion leaves a ripple in the Vitalis field. Most people cannot feel it. But the old bloodlines have instruments—and instincts—that can detect anomalies. A single conversion in a fight is easy to miss. Ten in a day, especially if they are concentrated in one location, becomes a pattern. And patterns get noticed.”

Roan turned this over. Not a system limit. An external risk. It was almost worse—there was no clean number he could memorize, no clear line between safe and dangerous. He would have to calculate every time.

“How long does the ripple last?”

“It fades. Hours for a single conversion. Days if you push hard. But the detection range depends on who is listening. The northern war-lines maintain monitoring stations near every major Rift. The Spires have their own instruments. I spent forty years learning their coverage patterns and staying in the blind spots. You will have to learn them too.”

“You mapped them?”

“I died before I could pass the maps. But the Codex contains fragments of what I knew. As you grow, more will unlock.”

Roan sat on the edge of his bed. The iron was warm against his palm, and his mind was already working the problem. If the conversion left traces, then he needed to control not just when he used it, but where. A fight in a dead alley in Greycross was low risk. A match in the Spire arena would be high risk. He needed to know who was watching.

“The people who wanted you dead,” he said. “They are still around.”

“Probably. The enemies I made were not the kind who forget. And the transfer took most of my resources—I came here with nothing except the Codex and what I could carry in my head. I built quietly. I trained in secret. I made sure no one connected the Greycross dropout with the monster they were hunting.”

“And then you had me.”

A pause. When the voice came back, it had changed—not softer, exactly, but more careful. “And then I had you. And then I had to make a decision about what to leave you. The Codex was the only thing I had that mattered. The question was whether to leave it locked, or to give you the key.”

“You could have left it locked.” Roan's throat was dry. “I would have been safer not knowing.”

“Yes. You also would have been weaker. And the people who might still be looking are not getting younger. I weighed the risks. The figures said you needed the tools more than you needed the safety.”

Roan closed his hand around the shard. The iron pulsed faintly. He thought about his father sitting somewhere in this same city, making calculations about what his son would need, leaving behind a weapon disguised as a charm and a key disguised as a pendant.

“Give me one name,” Roan said. “One person who is still looking.”

The silence stretched. When his father answered, the voice was barely a whisper. “No. Not because you do not deserve one. Because a name without context becomes a knife with no handle. You will cut yourself before you reach anyone else. Learn the history first. Learn who profits when old records stay buried. Then ask again.”

“Then tell me what I need to know right now. The immediate risks.”

“Avoid northern war-line families until you know which questions are safe to ask. Do not use inherited techniques in front of anyone who might recognize the pattern. And do not trust an offer simply because it arrives politely.”

“That is broad.”

“Broad keeps you alive until you can afford specific. Specific gets you killed if you treat a name like a target before you understand the board.”

Roan absorbed that. The advice was practical, if frustrating. He pushed further. “Was the activation visible? Could anyone detect the Codex opening?”

“Possibly. To ordinary eyes, no. But if someone was already watching for the right kind of anomaly, they may have noticed a question worth asking. Not a clear signal—more like a shadow moving where no shadow should be.”

“Should I be worried?”

“You drank Bloodscourge, activated a restricted inheritance, and used the Sevenfold Bleed in a public alley within an hour of each other. Yes. The more productive question is what you will do next.”

Roan stood. He walked to the window again, looked out at the dark city. Somewhere out there, people might be asking questions. Might be looking. Might be coming.

The calculation had changed. He had known the techniques were dangerous. He had not known that every use of the Inversion broadcasted a signal—faint, but real. He was not a man with a secret weapon. He was a man carrying a beacon that he had to learn how to dampen.

“The techniques in the Codex,” he said. “How do I access the rest?”

“They unlock as you grow. The first layer is already open—the Inversion core. The Sevenfold Bleed is partially available. Pyrebrand Cycling is locked until you reach a threshold. Other techniques are stored deeper. The more you use, the more your Vitalis matures, the more the Codex will reveal.”

“And the people looking for the source?”

“Don't let them find you before you can choose your ground. That is the only rule that matters.”

His father paused. The charm pulsed once, warm against his palm. When the voice returned, it was more distant, like a transmission fading over distance.

“That is enough for tonight. The fragment I left can only sustain so much before it needs to restabilize.”

“Wait.”

The room went quiet.

“Will you speak again?”

A longer pause. “When the next layer opens, I will know. Until then—survive. That is the only message worth sending.”

The warmth began to fade from the shard. Slowly, like a fire dying down. The presence was withdrawing, retreating back into whatever fragment remained stored in the iron.

“Father.”

The word came out before he could stop it. He had not called Aldric “Father” in years—too formal, too distant—but right now, in this moment, it felt like the only word that fit.

The room went quiet. He thought maybe the fragment would not respond, that the energy had already dried up, that he was talking to empty air.

Then:

“I am sorry I left you like this. The figures said you needed the tools more than the safety. But I know the math does not capture everything. I know what it is like to grow up with a target on your back. I tried to give you a normal life for as long as I could. When that stopped being possible, I tried to give you the best weapon I had.”

The warmth was almost gone now. Just barely a whisper of presence.

“The Codex is yours now. Everything I learned, everything I survived, everything I built—it is all in there. Use it well. Be smarter than I was.”

The shard went cold.

The presence was gone.

Roan stood in the dark, holding the iron pendant that was somehow heavier than it had been an hour ago. His father had come back. Had spoken to him. Had told him things he had never known, things that changed everything.

And then his father had left again—retreating into whatever remained of a consciousness that was a fragment of what it used to be.

He sat on the bed. He thought about the external risks, the traces, the instruments that could detect him. He thought about the Codex, the techniques, the system that was supposed to make him strong enough to survive. He thought about the alley, the fight, the way the Inversion had felt like something reaching inside him and rearranging the damage into power.

He thought about his father—forty years of running, building a life in secret, knowing that people might be looking.

The Codex was warm in his hand. Not hot anymore, just warm. Alive in a way that iron should not be.

He would not sleep tonight. He knew that already. The information was still settling, still integrating into the framework he used to understand the world. His father had come from a sealed history, and the old houses had killed that civilization. The Codex was evidence of something that was supposed to have been erased.

And he was carrying it. Wearing it. Broadcasting traces every time he used it.

He needed to think. To plan. To calculate.

But first, he checked his palm again. Still no mark. Still no wound. Just the phantom memory of light entering his skin.

His father had left him a library of dangerous techniques and a key that could make them survivable. The key was active now. The first door was open.

All he had to do was walk through it—and learn how to not be seen while doing so.

The iron was still warm in his hand.

Outside, Greycross slept, unaware that in a small room in the city's oldest district, a seventeen-year-old was sitting in the dark, holding the last remnant of a buried war, feeling the weight of forty years of survival pressing against his palm like a heartbeat that was not his own.

He did not cry. That was not who he was.

But he held the charm a little tighter, and he watched the darkness, and he waited for morning—

And he began calculating which blind spots he would need to find first.
