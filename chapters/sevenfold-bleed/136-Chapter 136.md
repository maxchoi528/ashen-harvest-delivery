Chapter 136: Deep Rift Roster

By the third hour inside the Rift, Roan had learned three things.

First, the Hollowed City was larger than the survey maps claimed.

Second, the survey maps hadn't been inaccurate by accident.

Third, Drogan had been here before.

Not recently. Not with this team. Perhaps not through this entrance. But the old man moved through the dead streets with caution that came from memory rather than training. He didn't hesitate often, and when he did, he hesitated before intersections that looked no more dangerous than the previous six. He avoided certain doorways without checking them. He watched the upper bridges at angles no briefing had stressed.

Roan said nothing.

Information extracted too early often came out warped. Better to let Drogan accumulate pressure.

Pressure made honest men speak and dishonest men improvise. Drogan was honest in deeply inconvenient intervals.

The team moved along a sunken avenue between two rows of leaning towers. Ash muffled their steps. Gray mist gathered at knee height and parted around them with the delayed reluctance of an animal deciding not to bite. Somewhere above, unseen structures groaned in a wind Roan couldn't feel.

Ines marked their path every fifty paces.

Not with chalk. Chalk failed in Rift moisture. Her markers were small brass pins with a thread of stabilizing Vitalis sealed inside, each pressed into cracks in the stone. They gave off no visible light, but Roan could feel them once he tuned his senses properly: faint, orderly ticks against the surrounding wrongness.

Brendt carried the heaviest pack and complained less than expected.

That worried Roan.

"Your breathing is off," Roan said.

Brendt glanced back. His filter mask hid most of his expression, but offense traveled well through posture. "We are walking through a dead city inside a gray murder hole."

"Your breathing is still off."

"I hate that you can tell."

"You're favoring your left."

Brendt adjusted instantly, then sighed. "Pack strap is cutting."

"Switch load at next stop."

"I can manage."

"That wasn't a question."

Brendt muttered something rude and accurate.

Drogan raised two fingers.

Stop.

Everyone froze.

Roan listened.

At first, nothing. The city's silence. The distant groan. The faint hiss of filter masks. Then a clicking sound, far ahead and above, like nails tapping stone.

One click.

Pause.

Three clicks.

Pause.

One click.

Not random.

Ines's hand moved to her rifle case.

Drogan shook his head once.

Don't engage.

Roan tracked the sound. Upper bridge, perhaps eighty meters ahead. Mist and angles blocked line of sight. The clicking repeated, slightly farther away.

"Scout?" Ines breathed.

"Maybe," Drogan said.

"Human?"

"No."

That answer came too quickly.

Roan adjusted his grip on the strap near Sidewound. "Husk?"

Drogan paused. "Not the kind you've seen."

Brendt's shoulders rose.

The Husks from lower-tier Rifts had been animalistic in the way fire was animalistic: destructive, hungry, uncomplicated. Roan had killed enough to know their rhythms. They charged, clawed, bit and shrieked, then died if one opened the core seam correctly. Dangerous, but honest.

Anything Drogan described as "not the kind you've seen" was unlikely to improve on acquaintance.

They waited.

The clicking faded.

Drogan lowered his hand and led them left into a narrow street partially blocked by a collapsed tram.

The tram had no wheels. It floated six inches above its track, dead and tilted, its windows filmed with gray residue. Letters along its side had been scratched out except one: V.

Roan noted it.

The street beyond narrowed further. Buildings leaned overhead until the sky became a jagged strip. On the right, a storefront remained intact behind a wall of glass untouched by cracks or grime.

Clean.

Everyone stopped before Drogan signaled.

Inside the storefront, shelves lined the walls. Empty shelves. A counter. A chair. No dust.

On the chair sat a coat.

Black. Long. Folded neatly.

Brendt whispered, "Nope."

"Good analysis," Ines said.

"Thank you."

Roan studied the glass. No reflection. Not even theirs.

The Vance Pendant grew colder against his sternum.

[Ambient recognition risk increasing.]

[External anchor interference active.]

[Observation uncertain.]

Observation uncertain.

That was almost worse than a warning. The Shard liked categories. If it was uncertain, either the target didn't obey known rules or the pendant was interfering with more than signal bleed.

Drogan took one slow step back. "We go around."

"That costs time," Ines said.

"We go around."

No one argued.

They retreated, crossed under the floating tram, and took a service alley choked with ash. It forced single-file movement. Bad. Drogan knew it was bad and chose it anyway, which meant the clean storefront ranked higher on his private threat list than ambush corridor.

Roan updated his own list accordingly.

Halfway through the alley, something moved under the ash.

Roan drew his standard knife, not Sidewound.

The ash bulged near Brendt's left boot. Small. Too small for a Husk. Trap? Burrower? Rift vermin?

Brendt saw Roan's motion and froze.

The bulge moved again.

Roan flicked the standard knife down.

The blade pinned something pale and segmented to the ground. It thrashed without sound, six needle legs scraping stone. Not a creature. A finger-length construct made of bone slivers and wire.

Ines crouched. "Marker eater."

Drogan looked back sharply. "Don't touch it."

"I know."

Roan filed the identification. Ines had been an expedition specialist before the file reviews buried her. The transfer had been political. The skills hadn't faded.

Roan twisted the knife and broke the construct in half. It went still.

[Minor Rift construct neutralized.]

[No yield.]

Of course not. Nothing useful ever came in convenient sizes.

Ines pulled one of her route markers from the previous wall and inspected it. The brass surface bore tiny scratch marks.

"They're tracking our tags," she said.

"Eating them?" Brendt asked.

"Eventually. This one was early."

Drogan's expression darkened. "How many could there be?"

"In this city?" Ines replaced the marker with a different model from her pouch. "Enough."

Roan looked at the broken construct. "Natural?"

"No," Ines said.

Drogan said nothing.

That was answer enough.

They moved faster after that.

Not rushed. Rushing killed. But the route changed. Ines began placing decoy tags in visible locations and true tags under ledges, inside cracks, beneath loose stone. Roan watched the ground for more bone-wire constructs. Brendt switched his shield frame to the left arm after they redistributed load under an overhang that had once been a transit shelter.

During the stop, Roan checked the team.

Brendt: shoulder strap abrasion, minor fatigue, morale strained but functional.

Ines: alert, too interested in the constructs, likely to take risks for samples if not monitored.

Drogan: controlled, withholding, watching landmarks rather than the map.

Roan: Vitalis stable, no nausea, Shard responsive but cautious, pendant colder than surface baseline, Sidewound quiet.

Usable.

They continued toward the first planned camp location: a substation marked on old maps as reinforced and elevated, then defensible.

The maps lied.

The substation existed, but its upper floor had been peeled open like a tin can. The walls were intact up to waist height, then bent outward in long strips of reinforced concrete. Inside, ash covered everything except a circle in the center of the floor.

Clean stone.

No one entered.

Drogan stared at the circle.

Ines checked her map, then the structure, then the map again. "This was listed as intact."

"It was," Drogan said.

"When?"

"Last survey."

"That was eight months ago."

No one said the obvious.

Whatever had opened the reinforced substation had done so within eight months, perhaps much less, and left a clean circle behind. Not ideal.

Brendt swallowed audibly through the mask. "Alternate camp?"

Drogan nodded. "Old pump house. Six hundred meters north."

Ines frowned. "That puts us closer to the inner district."

"Yes."

"That's worse."

"Yes."

"But still better than sleeping beside that."

"Yes."

Brendt looked at Roan. "I'm starting to understand why he hates explaining things."

"It doesn't improve the content," Roan said.

"No. But the efficiency is impressive."

They turned north.

The city changed as they approached the inner district.

The outer streets had looked dead. The northern route looked interrupted. Doors hung open. Stone benches sat overturned. A row of statues in a plaza had been decapitated, not shattered, the heads removed cleanly and stacked in a pyramid at the base of a dry fountain.

Brendt stopped looking at them after the first second.

Ines took three.

Roan took five, because useful details sometimes hid in unpleasant arrangements.

The statues represented humans, or near-humans. Hard to tell under erosion. The heads had been stacked facing inward.

Not display.

Containment? Ritual? Warning? Storage?

He didn't know.

He disliked not knowing.

Drogan saw him looking. "Leave it."

"I wasn't touching."

"You were thinking."

"Hard to avoid."

"Try."

They crossed the plaza.

A whisper moved through the fountain.

Brendt's shield came up.

Ines pivoted.

Drogan's hand went to the short blade at his hip.

Roan didn't draw. He listened.

The sound came again.

Not words. Almost words. The rhythm of speech without language.

The Codex Shard pulsed.

[Hollow residue harmonic.]

[Translation unavailable.]

[Potential lineage overlap.]

Roan's skin tightened under his coat.

Lineage overlap. The Shard didn't parse hints. Overlap meant junction, not coincidence. Aldric's system. Aldric's blood. The heart of this dead city was ground his father had walked before him.

That phrase went onto the list of problems he would prefer to investigate from behind thicker walls.

The resonance collapsed into silence.

Drogan began moving again without comment.

This time Roan didn't let it pass. "You heard it."

"Yes."

"Did Aldric?"

Drogan stopped.

Ines and Brendt both went still.

The city's quiet pressed in.

Drogan didn't turn around. "Not here."

"That's an answer to a different question."

"Yes."

Roan waited.

Drogan's shoulders rose and fell once. "He heard things in places like this."

"Things?"

"Voices."

"Human voices?"

"No."

"Did they speak his language?"

A longer pause.

"I don't know," Drogan said.

Truth.

Roan kept it.

They continued.

- break -

The pump house stood on the edge of a canal with no water.

Its stone walls were squat and ugly, plus blessedly dirty. Ash coated the roof. Rust streaked the iron door. One corner had collapsed inward, but the main chamber remained enclosed. No clean circles. No upright cups. No folded coats. No glass without reflections.

By Rift standards, it was practically cheerful.

Drogan circled it twice before allowing entry.

Inside, old machinery filled half the space: pumps, pipes, cracked pressure gauges, valve wheels fused in place. The floor sloped toward a dry drainage channel. There were two entrances, one blocked with rubble. Three narrow windows. Bad ventilation but defensible. Brendt set camp anchors while Ines mapped the interior and placed layered markers under the machinery.

Roan checked the corners.

No bone-wire constructs.

No clean objects.

No whispers.

Acceptable.

They established camp with practiced efficiency, or at least three of them did. Brendt's work was competent but anxious. Ines's was competent and self-interested. Drogan's was competent and grim. Roan's was competent because incompetence annoyed him.

By the time the camp frame unfolded and the first ward line hummed across the door, the gray sky outside had darkened.

Not night. The Rift didn't have normal night.

But the light withdrew.

That was close enough.

Brendt removed his mask only after Drogan tested the air twice and nodded. His face was damp with sweat.

"I never thought I would miss Ironholm air," he said.

"Low standards develop under pressure," Ines said.

He pointed at her. "You're not allowed to be funny when I'm suffering."

"I didn't realize you scheduled it."

Roan sat with his back to a pump casing, Sidewound across his knees, and cleaned ash from his gloves. Drogan handed out ration bars. He gave Roan two again.

Roan looked at the second bar.

Drogan stared back.

Roan ate both.

Some battles had poor returns.

For ten minutes no one spoke. They ate. Checked gear. Logged route notes. Listened to the city settle.

Then Ines opened her map case and projected a low-light route model from a thumb-sized crystal. The map hovered above the floor, flickering where distortion disrupted recorded geometry.

"Our path from entry to pump house," she said. "Distance traveled: approximately four kilometers if space behaved, which it didn't. Effective displacement from gate: two point one kilometers north-northeast. Tag loss: three decoys consumed, one true marker damaged. Construct activity confirmed."

Brendt leaned closer. "How bad is that?"

"On a normal student expedition? Catastrophic."

He closed his eyes.

"On this expedition?" she continued. "Informative."

"That's worse."

Roan studied the map. Several streets didn't fit the survey records. The city had shifted. Or the maps had been altered. Or both.

"Inner district route?" he asked.

Ines rotated the projection. "Three options. West through the statue plaza, north along the dry canal, or east through residential blocks."

Drogan pointed at the canal. "We take that at first light."

"Why?" Roan asked.

"Fewer roofs."

That made sense. Roofs held scouts. Or worse.

Ines marked it. "Objective remains survey anomaly?"

Drogan looked at Roan for a fraction too long. "Yes."

Brendt caught it. Of course he did. Brendt missed technical details, but he could read people. That was why he caught it.

"Is this anomaly that gets us paid," he asked, "or the kind that explains why Roan's life keeps getting worse?"

"Both," Ines said.

Drogan said, "Sleep rotation. I take first watch."

"No," Roan said.

Drogan's eyes narrowed.

"You take last," Roan said. "You know what the morning approach should look like. You need sleep before route decisions."

"I don't need—"

"You're older, injured more often than you admit, and pretending your left knee didn't lock twice in the plaza."

Brendt stared at Drogan's knee.

Drogan looked like he might throw a ration wrapper at Roan.

Ines smiled into her notes.

Roan continued. "I take first. Ines second. Brendt third. You fourth."

"No," Drogan said. "Brendt doesn't watch alone."

"I can watch," Brendt objected.

"No," Drogan and Roan said together.

Brendt's mouth closed.

Roan amended, "Brendt third with me half-awake."

"That's not sleep," Drogan said.

"It's for me."

"That explains your personality," Ines muttered.

Roan ignored her.

Drogan held his gaze, then grunted. "Fine. First sign of distortion, wake me."

"I was planning to quietly die."

"Don't get clever."

"Then stop leaving openings."

The old man lay down near the far wall with his coat folded under his head. Ines settled near the map case. Brendt took longer, adjusting his shield twice, checking the door three times, then finally lying down close enough to the central machinery that he could reach the camp anchor in one motion.

Roan remained by the door.

Sidewound rested unsheathed across his knees.

The ward line hummed softly.

Outside, the Hollowed City didn't sleep.

- break -

First watch was quiet.

That wasn't reassuring.

Roan preferred obvious threats. Obvious threats could be ranked, manipulated, killed, or avoided. Quiet in a Rift was an invoice that hadn't yet arrived.

He logged observations in his notebook under low light.

1. Clean objects are active threat markers or observation nodes.

2. Bone-wire constructs target route tags. Artificial. Controller unknown.

3. Hollow residue harmonic in plaza caused Codex response: "lineage overlap."

4. Drogan has prior exposure to similar sites. Still withholding.

5. Pendant reduces recognition risk but may interfere with Shard certainty.

6. Sidewound alignment shouldn't be inverted until necessity. Pain provides calibration.

7. Brendt functional under fear. Monitor pack abrasion.

8. Ines may prioritize anomalous salvage. Contract penalties insufficient under discovery conditions.

He paused at the last line, then added:

Add social penalty.

Money didn't restrain Ines as much as reputation among competent criminals might. He would need wording.

A faint scrape sounded outside.

Roan stilled.

Not near the door. Above.

He raised his eyes toward the narrow window.

Nothing.

The scrape came again.

Stone against stone. Slow. Testing.

Roan didn't wake the others yet. One scrape wasn't enough. Two scrapes suggested presence. Three would establish pattern.

The third scrape came from the opposite wall.

Roan stood without sound.

Sidewound remained in his right hand. Standard knife slid into his left.

The ward line at the door didn't change.

Meaning whatever moved outside hadn't crossed the threshold. Or didn't register as crossing. Or was already inside and the ward line was decorative.

Rifts were generous with bad options.

Roan stepped toward Brendt and nudged his boot.

Brendt woke instantly, to his credit. His hand found the shield latch before his eyes fully opened.

Roan touched two fingers to his own ear, then pointed up.

Brendt's face tightened. He nodded.

Roan moved to Ines next.

She woke with a knife halfway drawn.

Good reflex.

He pointed to the window.

She nodded and reached for her rifle case.

Drogan opened his eyes before Roan reached him.

Of course.

The old man sat up, listened once, and mouthed a word.

Husks.

The scrape came again.

This time, from beneath the floor.
