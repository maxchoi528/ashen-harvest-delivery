Chapter 127: Unmarked Interest

The morning light through Selene's study window fell at an angle that made the room smell of dry paper and cold tea. Roan sat across from her, hands flat on the desk, posture a deliberate statement.

"You're running numbers," she said. "Your eyes go still when you do that. I read it as a habit of someone who trades options in his sleep."

"A fair read."

"I have a file on you, Vance. Your Academy placement test, your combat evaluations, your match records. They all say the same thing: you shouldn't be this good. The metrics don't add up."

"Then there's an error in the metrics."

"Or an error in the assumption that metrics mean anything." She leaned forward. "I'm not asking you what you're hiding. I'm telling you that I know you're hiding something, and I've chosen not to report it. That's a decision that costs me credibility if you're caught."

"Why make it?"

"Because House Ardent survives by betting on futures instead of fighting over presents. You're a future, Vance. I want a piece of it."

"At what price?"

"A favor. Named later."

"That's a loose end."

"It's an investment. Don't make me regret it."

The Shard hummed against his spine—not a warning, an acknowledgment. Selene Ardent had just drawn a line, and she'd done it in a way that made him complicit in her silence.

"Agreed," Roan said.

"Good. One more thing. The Headmaster isn't the only one asking questions. Cyrus Ashford has been watching your matches. So has someone I don't recognize—older, well-dressed, not a student or faculty."

"House Greel."

"I didn't say that."

"You just did. Three sentences ago."

Selene's smile flickered. "Be careful, Vance. Being smart keeps you alive until it makes you interesting. Interesting people in this city have a way of becoming dead people in the next news cycle."

The door closed behind her. Roan sat in the morning light, running the new calculation. The Shard pulsed—a deep resonance that felt less like a notification and more like a line being drawn in the sand.

- break -

The training hall was empty except for Drogan. The Iron Hand clicked once as Roan entered—a nervous tic, or a signal.

"You saw Selene."

"She made an offer."

"House Ardent always makes offers. Did you take it?"

"I agreed to a future favor."

Drogan's jaw tightened. "That's how they hook you. One favor, then another. Before you know it, you're a line item in their ledger." He leaned against the training rack. "But you didn't come here to talk about Selene."

"The Headmaster summoned me."

"I figured."

"What does he want?"

"To see if you're worth watching. He knew your father. He saw what Aldric became, and he's trying to decide if you're the same pattern or a different one. Don't lie to him. He can spot a Vance lie from across the Crown Tier. But don't hand him the whole truth, either. Give him the shape of it and let him fill in the gaps with his own conclusions."

"What shape?"

"That you inherited something you don't fully understand. That you're powerful because of it, but reckless because of it too. That you're a danger to your enemies and a liability to your allies."

"And if he doesn't buy it?"

"Then you learn why your father kept his safest records sixty feet under a bakery."

- break -

The Crown Tier was designed to intimidate. Roan counted seven ways it could become a kill box before he forced the thought aside. Edran Cole wasn't an assassin. He was worse: he was an institutionalist.

"Vance. Sit."

Roan sat.

"You've made my Academy interesting. I value order. Interest is a disorder."

"I haven't done anything."

"You've done plenty. You're breathing. You're winning. You're making old men nervous about debts they thought were settled forty years ago." Cole's eyes were flat. "Your father left behind a legacy that most people in this city would rather forget. You're the walking reminder."

"I didn't choose that legacy."

"No one chooses their inheritance. They just decide what to do with it. I need to know what you've decided."

"I decided to survive."

"Survival isn't a decision. It's a default state. What comes after survival?"

The question was a trap. Roan saw the edges of it. "Growth."

"Growth toward what?"

"Whatever I need to be to protect what's mine."

The Headmaster studied him. The silence stretched, long enough to make a less disciplined man fill it. Roan held.

"You're careful with your answers," Cole said finally.

"Precise. I learned the difference from my father's notes."

"Your father's notes. Now that's an interesting phrase." Cole leaned forward. "What else did his notes teach you?"

"That the world is built on seven pillars. And six of them are rotten."

"And the seventh?"

"The seventh writes himself."

The Headmaster's expression didn't change. But something in the room shifted—a gap in the pressure, a crack in the institutional mask.

"Go," Cole said. "I'll be watching. And Vance—"

"Yes?"

"If you're your father's plan, I want you to know that I see it. And if you're his mistake, I'll close the book myself."

- break -

In the hallway, Brendt was waiting. His face said everything—he had spent the last two hours watching the Crown Tier and imagining every way it could go wrong.

"Well?"

Roan didn't answer immediately. "I bought us time. That's all. Time and questions."

"Questions about what?"

"About what I'm going to become."

That night, a message arrived at his dormitory door. No sender listed. No routing information. Just three words delivered by hand:

*We know about the Shard.*

Roan burned the note, then burned the ashes. The Shard pulsed once—not warning, not approval. Something else. Something that felt almost like acknowledgment.

His father's voice surfaced, faint and final:

*Good. Now they know. Now comes the hard part.*

The hard part, Roan thought. The part where everything he'd built met everything his father had left behind.

The part where the math either worked or didn't.

Time to find out which.
