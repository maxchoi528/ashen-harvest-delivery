Chapter 102: The Error in the Readout

The morning bell for third-period combat theory had already rung when Roan slipped through the eastern maintenance corridor. Three days since the Hardened threshold had settled into his marrow. Three days since Drogan’s coded message had landed: *They know someone broke through. Unmarked signatures found the old relay. Move now or lose the window.* He’d prepared for this, mapped the route, calculated the gaps between Sentinel patrols. The arithmetic had resolved cleanly until this morning. Now it was just a race against a clock he couldn’t see.

The Codex Shard pulled behind his sternum, not a pendant anymore, but something fused to his spine. The Inversion system didn’t speak in words. It spoke in a low thrum, an ache that bloomed behind his eyes whenever he drew close to sources of old power. The Veyrn Sanctum was the source. And someone had already opened it with a key Roan didn’t have.

He rounded the final corner. The door hung open two inches. A vertical slit in the bedrock, sealed by a mechanism that answered to bloodline signatures. Whoever had entered had done so with legitimate clearance, or brute force sophisticated enough to mimic it.

The system prompted without being asked:

[Degraded Vitalis trace detected. Age: 72 hours.]

Not broken in. Opened. Someone had stripped the door’s security layers clean.

A scrape of boot leather behind him. He didn’t turn.

“You’re early.” Brendt’s voice.

Roan let out a measured breath. “Third bell passed six minutes ago.”

“And you weren’t in the hall. Mira asked me to check.”

Lyra asking. That was new. Unwelcome. “I have a meeting.”

“With the sealed door under the eastern foundation that wasn’t on any schematic until last month?” Brendt stepped into view. “Theon Mercer mentioned the maintenance workers disappearing near the old sub-levels. Didn’t think anything of it until I saw you moving through corridors that don’t exist on student maps.”

Theon. Loose tongue. Roan cataloged the problem and kept his eyes on the dark gap. “How much did you tell him?”

“Nothing useful. Just that I noticed the pattern.” Brendt paused. “I’m not stupid, Roan.”

Roan turned. Brendt had gray eyes and a build that would never tier past Initiate regardless of effort. Exactly the kind the Spire was built to grind down. But he was also exactly the kind who would follow a friend into a sealed hole without asking why, and that made him useful and dangerous in equal measure.

“This sanctum contains artifacts from before the Spire existed,” Roan said. “Before the Conclave families had power structures. Whatever is down there predates everything we know about Vitalis.”

“Okay.”

“Someone with high-level access visited seventy-two hours ago. The signature doesn’t match any database I have access to.”

“Okay.”

“This could be a trap. It could be a vault. I don’t know which, and I don’t like not knowing.”

Brendt nodded. “So why are you going in alone?”

“Because if something happens, the cost should be mine. Not Mira’s. Not yours.”

Silence. The sanctum breathed cold air through the gap, carrying the smell of oxidized metal and something older that made the Shard pulse against his spine like a second heartbeat.

Brendt stepped forward until he stood beside him. “You can control whether I come or not. That’s a variable too.”

Roan’s jaw tightened. He pulled a sealed note from his inner pocket and pressed it into Brendt’s hand. “If I don’t come back out, you give this to Drogan. No one else. Not Mira. Not the Spire administration. Drogan.”

Brendt looked at the note, then tucked it into his own coat without opening it. “Deal.”

“Stay behind me. Don’t touch anything carved. If I tell you to run, you don’t argue.”

They went in.

The shaft dropped forty feet before widening into a chamber. Emergency Lumens flickered to life, triggered by their Vitalis presence, pale blue strips that painted everything in shades of forgetting. The space was larger than it should have been, as if the rock had been stretched by old pressure.

Tables lined the walls, dense and cold, embedded with circular depressions that had held something spherical. All empty. Whatever had filled them was gone.

“Roan.” Brendt’s voice was flat. “These walls.”

The chamber’s walls were covered in script. Not standard Runedic notation, but shapes that hurt to look at directly, characters that shifted when viewed from the corner of the eye. The Inversion system processed them for half a second before returning a result:

[Pattern match: 11.3%. Inversion archive relay node. Pre-Conclave structural signature. Core records corrupted.]

Eleven percent. The system was guessing. Aldric’s civilization had used this place as a waystation, but the specific records had been scrubbed or degraded.

Roan approached the nearest wall and traced the characters with his finger. The stone was colder than the air, colder than anything he’d touched. The Shard responded with a vibration that traveled up his arm and settled behind his eyes.

[New data logging. Pattern: directional markers. This node designated: Ironholm.]

“The walls are a map,” he said quietly.

Brendt moved closer. “A map to what?”

“Other stations. A network.”

“If this was a relay, where’s the relay equipment?”

Roan looked at the empty depressions. “Gone. Whoever opened this door before us took the hardware.” He scanned the wall script again. “But they left the directions.”

[Partial network topology. Ironholm node: primary relay logged. Aldermere node: secondary confirmed. One node matches mountain-range geological signature. Two additional nodes masked.]

Three confirmed. Two hidden.

Footsteps. Light, deliberate. Someone descending.

Roan turned, pivoting to put his body between the sound and Brendt. The figure appeared at the edge of the shaft’s light. Female. Tall. Moving with the economical grace of someone trained since childhood to make every step look effortless. Senior student uniform, but the insignia on her collar marked House Ardent’s delegation, not Ironholm’s branch, but the parent house from Aldermere.

Selene Ardent. The girl who’d beaten him in their first-semester sparring through sheer technical superiority. The girl who watched too carefully.

She stopped at the chamber’s edge and looked at them. Her gaze moved from Roan to Brendt to the empty tables to the walls.

“You’re either very brave or very stupid.” She paused. “I haven’t decided which.”

“Same thing, usually,” Brendt muttered.

Selene’s eyes flickered to him for half a second before returning to Roan. “You activated the door three minutes after I did. The mechanism logged your signature. It also logged mine.” She waited. “We’re both flagged now. Whatever alarm system exists down here knows we were here.”

Roan processed this. She had legitimate access. She’d known about the sanctum before today. “Which means you have something to lose by reporting me.”

“Same as you.”

No admission. Just a mirror.

“Relay station,” Roan said, nodding toward the walls. “Your house records tell you that?”

Selene’s expression didn’t change, but something behind her eyes sharpened. “House Ardent sealed this door seventy years ago after the Burning. Our charge was to keep the network buried. Someone with a better set of override codes pried it open three days ago.”

“They took the regulators.” She moved past him toward the nearest table. “Core artifacts. You can’t activate a relay without them. They’re trying to wake the network from the inside out.”

“They took the hardware,” Roan said. “They left the walls.”

“They wanted a trail. Witnesses to follow.”

The Codex Shard pulsed again, stronger.

[Secondary resonance detected. Source: Ardent bloodline. Vitalis signature matches residual pattern on sanctum access logs.]

Selene’s family had been here before. Not three days ago. Seventy years ago, when the archive was destroyed. They were the gatekeepers.

Roan looked at her differently. The perfection that made her untouchable in sparring. The way she never joined factions. The way her eyes lingered on details.

“House Ardent has history with this place,” he said.

“My family wrote the sealing protocols. We were the ones who buried the access codes when the Conclave ordered the remnants destroyed.” Selene’s voice dropped. “We didn’t know someone else had kept a copy.”

“The third party.”

“They have the regulators. They have the override codes. But they don’t have the network map.” She gestured to the walls. “These symbols are directional. The relay architecture forms a transit pattern. If I can match the topology to geography—”

“The system decodes it,” Roan said. He kept it vague. “The Codex Shard. It recognizes the script.”

Selene’s eyes held his. “That’s not a small admission.”

“Neither is your family’s sealing protocol.”

[Partial topology. Ironholm node: primary. Aldermere node: secondary. One confirmed mountain range: Veyrn Massif. Final destination probable: Veyrn Sanctum proper.]

The system’s readout was dry, clinical, but Roan felt the weight behind it. The First Seat. The place where Aldric’s civilization had made its last stand.

“The map converges on Mount Veyrn,” he said.

Selene’s jaw tightened. “My family records call it the First Seat. Where the remnant council convened before the Burning. It’s the network’s origin point.”

“The third party has a seventy-two-hour lead,” Brendt said quietly. “They have the keys from this station. They’re heading north.”

Roan looked at Selene. “You have lineage clearance I don’t. You have infiltration routes and a family infrastructure.” He paused. “I have the decoding system. This is a provisional merger of assets or we both lose the trail.”

Selene studied him. “You want me to come with you.”

“I want you to clear our exit without triggering a campus-wide lockdown. After that, the decision is yours.”

She held his gaze for three full heartbeats. Then Selene Ardent smiled, the first genuine expression he’d seen from her since they’d met. It made her look younger. Dangerous.

“House Ardent has resources in Aldermere. Transportation. A safe house near the mountain approaches.” She glanced at Brendt. “For three.”

“I’m the thread back to the surface,” Brendt said. “If they vanish in the mountains, someone outside the network knows where they went.”

Roan looked at the walls one last time. Ironholm. Aldermere. Mount Veyrn. Partial data on two more nodes. Someone was running a game that had started seventy years ago when the Founding Seven burned the Inversion archive and scattered its survivors. Someone had just made the first real move, and they didn’t know Roan was already in the game.

“We go now,” he said.

The ascent took less time than the descent. Selene cleared their exit through a service corridor that bypassed the main Sentinel stations, her access codes precise. By the time the fourth-period bell rang, all three of them were in a private vehicle heading north on the Ironholm-Aldermere corridor, the Black Spire shrinking in the rearview mirror.

Selene sat in the passenger seat, one hand pressed against a crystal embedded in the dashboard, the Ardent family’s communication network patching them into intelligence Roan couldn’t intercept.

Brendt sat in the back, silent, watching the terrain roll past.

The Codex Shard pulsed against Roan’s spine.

*You have three nodes. They have artifacts. They have a seventy-year head start and you have a piece of a dead civilization stitched to your backbone.*

“I know,” he said, too quiet for the others to hear.

The campus war was a cold corpse he was leaving behind. Cyrus waiting for an opening. Iris’s grandfather casting a shadow. Lady Sirenne’s warning echoing in the hollows of his memory. Drogan, waiting for a message that might not come. They were all threads he had to trust would hold until he returned. If he didn’t return, the board didn’t matter anyway.

*So why are you going?*

Roan watched the mountains appear on the horizon. Faint blue shapes resolving into white-capped peaks.

*They think they have the only key. They forgot that the principle built the lock. A lock opens if you understand the geometry of the teeth.*

The vehicle crested a ridge, and Mount Veyrn filled the windshield. At its base, barely visible in the afternoon light, Roan could make out the ruins of a temple complex overgrown by forest and time.

The First Seat. The place where his father’s world had ended and something else had been left behind.

Seventy years of silence.

And now, three people racing toward it, with a fourth already ahead.

The dead were about to speak.
