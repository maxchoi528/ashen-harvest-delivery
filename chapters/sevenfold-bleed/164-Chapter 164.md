Chapter 164: Private Rift Work

A month of recovery didn't feel like recovery.

It felt like being audited by biology.

Roan disliked it.

His body had crossed into Forged during a retreat through a hostile Rift while low on quota, carrying unfamiliar knowledge, a blood debt to a dead civilization, and one injured friend whose continued existence had become everyone else's evidence that Roan wasn't entirely made of knives. That was dramatic language. It was also a poor training condition. Breakthroughs earned under crisis tended to leave uneven foundations, and uneven foundations killed practitioners who thought new rank meant new safety.

Forged wasn't a medal.

It was a structural renovation done while the building was on fire.

For the first week, Roan did almost nothing impressive. He ate. Slept at least three hours every twenty-four because Brendt had weaponized infirmary guilt into a condition. Submitted to Dr. Seth's scans. Endured Drogan's mobility drills. Let the Codex Shard map the changes in his organs and nerves, plus marrow without accepting any optional optimizations that came with words like "accelerated," "experimental," or "unlikely fatal."

His stable Vitalis settled at just above 4,000.

The number looked small after all the things that had happened.

Roan appreciated that.

Small numbers were harder for enemies to fear and easier for fools to underestimate. Forged standard. Nothing special, if one ignored his combat peak, Inversion Depth, Sidewound resonance, the Black Rite of Veyrn stabilizing his mental architecture, and the fact that the Codex Shard now occasionally translated fragments of dead language in his dreams.

Publicly, he was a newly advanced Forged student with tournament fame, expedition injuries, and an unhealthy interest in private contracting.

Privately, he had become a problem with paperwork.

The rest of the month built itself around restrictions.

Restrictions weren't the same as inactivity. Inactivity was passive. Restrictions were a box. Boxes could be measured, reinforced, escaped, rented, or used to store knives. Roan treated medical limits the same way he treated bad contracts: identify the exact words, define the forbidden actions, and spend the remaining space profitably.

No peak training meant technique work. Sidewound draw angles, off-hand retrieval, breath timing, seam recognition against sensor dummies set to Forged baseline. No self-inflicted blood cycling meant no Crimson Wake drills, but it didn't prohibit dry movement patterns, visualization, or watching archived fights frame by frame until Brendt threatened to throw a pillow at the display.

No Rift exposure meant research.

Research produced more problems.

The Black Rite of Veyrn had changed his internal architecture in ways the Codex could describe but not entirely explain. His Inversion Depth had increased, but not in the simple sense of more power. It was more like gaining a second angle on pain. Before the Rite, damage approached him as a force: burn, poison, rupture, fracture. The Shard caught it and, if permitted, reversed the line. Now some effects arrived with context. Direction. Intent. A wound created by a Husk claw felt different from a self-cut palm. Rift toxin felt different from botanical command toxin. The difference mattered because inversion wasn't a universal refund policy. It was interpretation under pressure.

Misread the meaning, and the reversal might reinforce the wrong thing.

Roan wrote that down in large letters.

Don't invert what you can't classify.

Then, because the universe had a poor sense of humor, the next three pages of Codex notes classified very little.

[Inversion Depth: stage 1 / unstable.]

[Current stability limit: 4 major inversions per cycle recommended.]

[Minor inversion tolerance: variable.]

[Exposure risk: low-moderate.]

[Identity anchor: improved by external pendant.]

[Warning: repeated emergency inversion around emotional anchors may distort priority weighting.]

Roan stared at the last line for a long time.

Emotional anchors.

Brendt, then. Lyra. Mira. Drogan. The Shard had a habit of phrasing insults as diagnostics.

He didn't cross the line out.

He underlined it.

A flaw ignored became a trap. A flaw measured became a cost center.

The difference was survival.

Paperwork mattered.

The Guild board also solved a visibility problem. If he hid, enemies could define the silence. If he stood in the open with a rate sheet, they had to decide whether attacking him was murder, politics, or interference with licensed commerce. Bureaucracy didn't stop knives. It made some knife owners hesitate long enough to be named.

On the eighth day after the Rift, Headmaster Cole summoned him to a side office beneath the administrative tower. Not the grand office with black glass and city views. This one had no windows, two chairs, a tea service no one touched, and walls warded heavily enough to make the Vance Pendant warm against Roan's chest.

Cole sat behind a plain desk, hands folded. He looked exactly as he had during their previous meeting: silver hair, calm eyes, old Vitalis compressed so well that weaker students mistook him for merely dignified. Roan didn't.

Old predators learned stillness.

"Your medical clearance arrived," Cole said.

"You summoned me to discuss paperwork."

"I summoned you because your paperwork is attempting to become political."

Roan sat without being invited. "Then stop it."

Cole's mouth curved slightly. "That's one option."

"The efficient one."

"Not always. You have filed for provisional Rift Explorer registration through the Student Guild."

"Correct."

"You filed under your own name."

"Correct."

"That's an unusual choice for someone who has spent most of the year avoiding attention."

Roan glanced at the untouched tea. Steam rose from both cups. Real tea, not cafeteria extract. Expensive. Meant either as courtesy or pressure. Cole had selected a kind associated with Greycross markets to see if Roan noticed.

He did. He didn't drink it.

"I already have attention," Roan said. "Unregistered work makes attention dangerous for free. Registered work makes it billable."

Cole nodded once, as if confirming an answer on a private rubric. "You want private commissions."

"I want controlled access to low and mid-tier Rifts, salvage rights, field data, and clients whose problems reveal what official channels omit."

"You could have said yes."

"You would've been disappointed."

"Mildly."

Roan leaned back. The chair was comfortable in a way that encouraged confession. He disliked it and shifted his weight to the edge.

Cole noticed.

Of course.

"The school can approve provisional registration," Cole said. "With restrictions."

"List them."

"No solo Tier Two or above. No private contracts involving House disputes without administrative notice. No unsupervised transport of anomalous artifacts through campus. No acceptance of extraction, assassination, coercive collection, debt enforcement, political sabotage, or romantic retrieval contracts."

Roan paused. "Romantic retrieval?"

"Runaway heirs. Elopements. Kidnapped fiances who aren't kidnapped. Kidnapped fiances who are kidnapped but prefer the kidnappers. It's more common than one would hope."

"I would refuse those anyway."

"Good. They pay badly and cause duels."

Cole slid a document across the desk.

Roan read it.

The restrictions were annoying but not crippling. Tier Two access with team requirement. Tier Three only with licensed supervisor. Salvage declaration within seventy-two hours. School claim rights limited to artifacts recovered using school equipment. Private fees taxable through Guild channels. Medical reporting mandatory after contaminated exposure. No sale of unidentified materials to off-campus buyers without assay.

He marked three clauses.

"These need adjustment."

Cole's eyebrow rose. "You have had the document for less than a minute."

"Clause nine defines anomalous artifact broadly enough to include my knife if I sharpen it after a mission. Clause fourteen gives the school emergency custody over materials deemed hazardous by any faculty member, which is a theft clause wearing gloves. Clause twenty-one prohibits independent client screening databases, which is unenforceable and stupid."

"Stupid?"

"Dangerous, if you prefer politeness. If students can't screen clients, clients screen students. That produces dead students and public scandals."

Cole tapped one finger against the desk. "You propose?"

"Artifact definition limited to objects producing autonomous Vitalis output, cognition-affecting signals, spatial distortion, or biological contamination. Emergency custody requires two faculty signatures, one external Guild witness, and an itemized receipt with valuation floor. Client screening databases permitted if shared with Student Guild safety board under sealed access."

Cole watched him for several seconds.

"You should study law."

"I prefer crimes with better margins."

The headmaster's mouth twitched.

He took the document back, made three notes, and initialed the margin. "Conditional approval."

"Fees?"

"Student Guild takes twelve percent. School takes six if equipment is used. Medical bond required."

"Too high."

"Correct."

"Guild eight. School four. Medical bond funded from first three contracts, not upfront."

"Guild will refuse eight."

"Guild will accept ten if the school waives equipment fees for sanctioned training contracts and gets priority access to non-sensitive field reports."

"You came prepared."

"You summoned me about paperwork."

Cole signed the bottom line. "Ten percent Guild. Four percent school only on paid external contracts using school equipment. Medical bond deferred across first four jobs. Field reports required, with sensitive redaction rights subject to my review."

"Your review creates trust dependency."

"Yes."

Roan looked at him.

Cole met his gaze calmly.

There it was again: the headmaster positioning himself close enough to help, close enough to watch, and close enough to be blamed. He had said he wasn't Roan's enemy. That didn't make him an ally. Ally was a word people used before invoices arrived.

But Cole had adjusted three clauses without pretending the original was fair.

That placed him above most adults Roan dealt with.

"Accepted," Roan said.

Cole handed him a black-edged provisional badge. It bore Roan's name, registration number and rank, plus the words RIFT EXPLORER - STUDENT PROVISIONAL.

The badge was heavier than expected.

Symbolic weight was usually an excuse to overcharge.

"One more thing," Cole said.

Of course.

"House Vell has filed a complaint alleging that you assaulted employees during an unauthorized Rift exit engagement."

"They were Greel mercenaries blocking an exit after a student expedition."

"Yes. Their complaint is poorly constructed."

"Then reject it."

"I did."

Roan waited.

Cole steepled his fingers. "House Greel hasn't complained."

That mattered.

Vell complained when embarrassed. Greel stayed silent when calculating.

"They're collecting," Roan said.

"Likely."

"Names?"

"Not yet."

"You will tell me when you have them."

"I will tell Drogan."

"Bad answer."

"Safe answer."

Roan stood. "Safe answers age poorly."

Cole looked up at him. "So do reckless students."

"Then we are both managing decay."

For a moment, Cole looked amused. Then the old calm returned.

"Be careful with public contracts, Mr. Vance. Registered visibility protects you from certain knives and invites better ones."

Roan pocketed the badge.

"Better knives are easier to value."

- break -

The first week after registration produced twenty-seven contract inquiries.

Nineteen were trash.

Three were traps.

Two were traps but paid enough to reread.

One involved romantic retrieval and was deleted with prejudice.

The remaining two were real work.

Roan reviewed them in the dorm common room while Brendt occupied the couch with a blanket, three pillows, and the offended dignity of a man whose doctor had forbidden stairs. Theon Mercer hadn't returned to the room since receiving his research placement, which left the space quieter and more useful. Roan had converted the abandoned desk into a contract board. Brendt had called it disturbing. Then he had asked for a better chair.

Progress.

"No," Brendt said from the couch.

Roan didn't look up. "You haven't read it."

"You made the same face you make before saying something like 'acceptable casualty parameters.' So no."

"This contract has no casualty parameters."

"Worse."

Roan slid the tablet across the low table.

Brendt read for twenty seconds. "A private warehouse owner wants a Tier One Rift seepage cleared under a loading dock. Payment: six thousand credits, salvage rights limited, hazard rating understated. This is... Reasonable."

"Yes."

"Why do I distrust it?"

"Because you are learning."

Brendt scrolled. "Client name is Ivar Doss. Logistics supplier. No House affiliation. Two prior safety violations. One dismissed contamination complaint. Three late payments."

"Four. The fourth used a subsidiary."

"Of course it did." Brendt tapped the screen. "Terms?"

"Half upfront. Medical liability waiver rejected. Salvage assay independent. If hazard exceeds Tier One, fee triples or withdrawal without penalty."

"Good. Who's team?"

"You aren't cleared."

"That wasn't the question."

"It was implied."

"I can coordinate."

Roan looked at him.

Brendt lifted one hand. "From a chair. With maps. Like a dignified invalid."

"Invalids are rarely dignified."

"I am pioneering."

Roan considered. Brendt needed involvement or he would become restless. Restless people did independent things. Independent things created new paperwork. Remote coordination carried little physical risk and moderate intelligence value.

"Accepted," Roan said. "Remote only."

"Good. Who is field support?"

"Drogan offered a senior student. I refused."

"Of course."

"Ines is unavailable unless salvage percentage is obscene."

"Also of course."

"So I do it alone."

"Roan."

"Tier One seepage."

"Your registration says no solo Tier Two or above. It doesn't say solo Tier One is always intelligent."

"It's within rules."

"So is eating expired ration paste."

"Not comparable."

"Correct. The paste has fewer claws."

Roan reached for the tablet.

Brendt held it away, which was bold for a man who couldn't sit without planning. "Take someone. Anyone competent."

"Competent people cost money."

"You have money."

"Spending money because I have money is how rich people become poor with better furniture."

"Roan."

"Fine. Suggest someone."

Brendt smiled.

Roan immediately regretted the opening.

"Selene Ardent," Brendt said.

"No."

"You asked."

"I rescind the question."

"She's Forged, trained, politically connected, hates incompetence, and owes you enough respect not to get herself killed showing off."

"She's House Ardent."

"Yes. That's why she owns armor that doesn't come with apology clauses."

"Her family has historical connections to my father's enemies."

Brendt's expression changed. He lowered the tablet. "Do you have evidence that Selene does?"

Roan didn't answer.

"That isn't the same thing," Brendt said.

"It's adjacent."

"A lot of things are adjacent. You live adjacent to common sense and never visit."

Roan gave him a flat look.

Brendt didn't retreat.

Annoying.

Also useful.

Selene Ardent had fought beside him before. She had pride and discipline, plus enough sense not to confuse Roan's manners with weakness. She had also inherited a House name tied to old structures Aldric distrusted. House Ardent had appeared in fragments of Roan's investigation, not as a confirmed enemy, but as a family with archives deep enough to bury inconvenient truths.

Taking Selene on a low-level contract would test her under controlled conditions.

It would also announce, publicly, that Roan Vance was willing to work with House Ardent.

That could be useful.

Or bait. Both.

"One contract," Roan said. "Controlled scope. She receives no sensitive information."

Brendt smiled like he had won something.

"Don't look pleased," Roan said. "It lowers the room's standards."

"Too late."

- break -

Selene Ardent agreed to the warehouse job in under three minutes.

That was the first warning.

She met Roan two days later in Training Hall C, where Black Spire's upper-year students practiced without first-years stopping to stare. Selene wore light field armor in House Ardent's dark silver and red, her blond hair tied back, a narrow spear case across one shoulder. No entourage. No display jewelry beyond the thin family ring on her right hand. Practical.

Suspiciously practical.

"Doss Logistics," she said after reading the contract. "Low prestige, low pay for your current profile, moderate contamination risk, likely underreported. You chose it because the client is too minor to be politically useful and too cheap to be worth elaborate assassination."

Roan took the tablet back. "Mostly."

"And because a bad client teaches more than a good one."

"Better."

"Is that approval?"

"Don't develop expectations."

Selene's mouth curved. "I wouldn't dream of it."

That was a lie. Not a hostile one. Selene had expectations the way swords had edges.

Brendt participated through a remote projection crystal propped on a bench, his face appearing pale and irritated above a blanket. "For the record, I approve this team-up."

"Your approval has been logged," Roan said.

"And valued?"

"Logged."

Selene glanced at the projection. "You look better than the rumors suggested."

"The rumors were started by cowards who exaggerate pain," Brendt said.

"You started three of them," Roan said.

"Preventive reputation management. If people think I almost died more dramatically, they ask fewer technical questions."

Selene gave Roan a look. "He's learning from you."

"Badly."

"Rude," Brendt said.

Roan ignored him and passed Selene the amended team agreement.

She read it carefully. Good. People who signed unread contracts deserved what happened next.

"Clause eight," she said. "No independent communication with client during operation without team notice. Sensible. Clause twelve: salvage rights split after assay. Fine. Clause fifteen: any House-origin item recovered must be disclosed to team before transfer to family custody. Interesting."

"Problem?"

"No. But transparent."

"Good."

"You want to see whether I choose House privilege over contract integrity."

"Among other things."

"And if I do?"

"Then I know cheaply."

Selene studied him for a moment.

Most House heirs disliked being treated as test subjects unless the test involved applause. Selene merely nodded.

"Fair. I have one condition."

"You can try."

"If the client misrepresented hazard level, we document before withdrawal. Not after."

Brendt frowned from the projection. "That increases risk."

"It increases leverage," Selene said. "If we withdraw without proof, Doss claims cowardice or breach. If we document first, we own the narrative and the fee adjustment."

Roan looked at her.

Selene met his gaze.

Yes. Competent.

Inconvenient.

"Accepted," he said. "With time limit. Ninety seconds evidence capture after Tier mismatch confirmed."

"One hundred twenty."

"Ninety."

"One hundred five."

"Ninety, and Brendt handles external log duplication."

Brendt lifted a finger. "The invalid accepts."

Selene considered. "Accepted."

Roan passed her a stylus.

She signed.

The contract board gained its first official team entry.

Roan Vance. Selene Ardent. Brendt Holloway, remote coordination.

It looked almost respectable.

That was dangerous.

Respectability was camouflage for knives with lawyers.

- break -

The warehouse contract took four hours and produced three useful things.

First, Doss Logistics had lied.

The seepage under the loading dock wasn't Tier One. It was a degraded Tier Two pocket forming around old Rift-contaminated coolant pipes. The hazard wouldn't have killed a competent Forged practitioner under normal conditions, but it would have eaten two Initiate laborers within a week and left enough residue to poison half the warehouse staff by month-end.

Second, Selene didn't break contract.

When Roan found the first House-marked storage case beneath the dock. Ardent alloy seal, twenty years old, officially listed as destroyed. Selene's face went very still. She didn't reach for it. She didn't deny recognition. She didn't invoke family privilege.

She said, "Log it."

Brendt, through the comm crystal, said, "Logged, copied and sealed, then now I am emotionally invested."

Third, Roan discovered that Selene fought exactly the way he expected and slightly better than he preferred.

The Rift-formed things in the pocket weren't true Husks. They were coolant-warped vermin, blind and fast, plus acidic enough to scar concrete. Selene handled them with disciplined spear work, no wasted flourishes, no House theatrics. She kept distance, controlled angles, and adjusted when Roan's Sidewound changed a target's movement instead of killing it outright.

Good combat partners anticipated effects.

Bad ones anticipated applause.

Selene anticipated consequences.

By the time the pocket collapsed, Doss Logistics owed triple hazard fee, medical reporting penalties, and a salvage disclosure to the Guild. Roan also had a sealed Ardent storage case, three contaminated alloy samples, and enough evidence to make Ivar Doss regret underpaying maintenance crews.

Publicly, the job looked like a minor success.

Privately, it opened another old door.

Back at Black Spire, Selene stood in the decontamination chamber while white vapor rolled off her armor. Roan watched through the glass from the adjacent bay, waiting for the scan to finish.

"You have questions," she said through the comm.

"Several."

"About the case."

"Among other things."

"It's pre-Consolidation Ardent field storage. My family used that seal for military supply caches before most private Rift holdings were nationalized. If the record says destroyed, either the record is wrong or someone wanted it wrong."

"Which is more likely?"

"In my family? Intentional error."

Roan appreciated the lack of hesitation.

"Will you report it to them?" he asked.

Selene's eyes met his through the fogged glass. "Not until our contract allows disclosure."

"And after?"

"That depends what is inside."

"Wrong answer."

"Honest answer."

Better.

Honest bad answers were easier to price than polished good ones.

The scan chimed green. Decontamination complete.

Selene stepped out, helmet under one arm, hair damp from vapor.

"Another contract?" she asked.

Roan looked at her.

A House heir asking to continue after a dirty warehouse job, a lied hazard rating, acidic vermin, and family evidence she hadn't hidden.

Not random.

Selene Ardent wanted something.

So did Roan.

Sometimes that was enough to begin.

"Maybe," he said.

She smiled faintly. "How generous."

"Don't develop expectations."

"Too late."
