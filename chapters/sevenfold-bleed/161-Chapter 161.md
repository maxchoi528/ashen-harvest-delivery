Chapter 161: Brendt Wakes

Brendt woke forty-one hours after surgery and immediately attempted to apologize.

That alone proved Dr. Mara Seth hadn't adjusted his personality during reconstruction, which Roan considered a missed opportunity but not a medical failure.

The forty-one hours before that had been less dramatic and more unpleasant, which was how serious injuries preferred to spend time.

Drama belonged to wounds opening, enemies advancing, gates collapsing, and people making decisions they could later pretend were heroic. Recovery belonged to numbers, fluids, temperature drift, graft acceptance, clot integrity, and nurses with the authority to remove visitors who looked too much like future complications. Roan learned this because Dr. Seth banned him from the surgical bay after the first hour, then found him in the observation alcove reading the reflected monitor data from a polished cabinet door.

She hadn't been impressed.

"You are either obsessive or medically trainable," she had said.

"Both can be useful."

"Both can be sedated."

He had moved to the approved chair.

From there, he watched surgery through a privacy-filtered window and reconstructed what he could from the healer team's movement. Brendt's left shoulder required structural grafting. Three ribs were cracked, one nearly cut through. The abdominal wound had missed a fatal channel by less than two centimeters, which meant luck had finally contributed something after months of freeloading. Rift contamination had settled around the injury margins and tried to make the tissue forget what species it belonged to. That part took the longest.

Dr. Seth removed the contamination with a technique that looked less like healing and more like arguing with biology until biology apologized.

Roan approved.

Drogan came by once during the first night, stood beside Roan for twelve minutes, and said nothing.

Roan didn't ask whether the old man had spoken to Cole. Drogan's left hand was wrapped. His jaw looked like it had been used to grind stone. That answered enough.

Ines visited at hour twenty-three with two pouches of copied evidence, a bruise along her cheek, and the information that Iris's data crystal contained three layers of decoys, one genuine operational brief, and a personal note from the Vell patriarch that was "either manipulative family affection or a crime scene with punctuation."

"Can you decode all of it?" Roan had asked.

"Given time and tools, plus adequate financial disrespect, yes."

"Adequate?"

"Enough to make me complain but not leave."

"Send the number."

"Already did."

At hour thirty, Mira called.

Roan took the call in a storage room after checking it for listening wards and finding only three, all medical and all crude enough to offend him. His mother's face appeared above the cracked screen, tired and sharper than it had been before he left Greycross. Distance hadn't made her less perceptive. It had merely reduced the number of things she could verify.

"You look pale," she said.

"Training accident."

"Yours?"

"Brendt's."

"That wasn't an answer."

"No."

Mira looked at him. Behind her, the Greycross apartment kitchen looked smaller than Roan remembered. Lyra's school notes were stacked on the table in neat piles. A cheap kettle steamed on the stove.

"Is he alive?"

"Yes."

"Will he stay that way?"

"Likely."

She closed her eyes for half a second. "Likely from you means more than yes from most people."

"Yes."

"Roan."

He waited.

"Whatever you are doing, don't turn everyone who loves you into collateral."

The sentence landed with unpleasant precision.

"I am trying not to."

"Try harder."

Then she hung up before he could answer, which was rude and deserved.

At hour thirty-six, the Codex Shard restored one inversion quota and immediately produced a prompt recommending no use for twenty-four hours. Roan mentally classified this as advisory rather than binding. The Shard produced a second prompt with stronger wording. He ignored that too.

At hour forty, Dr. Seth told him Brendt would wake soon and that if Roan interrogated him before initial neural stabilization, she would "remove his visitor privileges with tools."

Roan had said, "Define interrogate."

She had smiled.

He had stopped asking questions.

Then Brendt woke and apologized for being heavy.

Rumors reached the medical wing before Brendt woke.

That was one of the few reliable things about Black Spire. Information moved through the student body faster than official messaging and with more creative anatomy. By the end of the first day, Roan had received seven messages asking whether he had truly entered a Tier Three Rift to harvest rare void-metal, three asking whether Iris Vell had stabbed him in a lovers' quarrel, two sponsorship offers from weapon houses that had concluded he was suicidal but marketable, and one anonymous note reading: *Please stop making the rest of us look underprepared.*

He deleted none of them.

Rumors weren't merely noise. They were weather reports. Each false version showed which anxieties the campus already wanted to believe. The void-metal rumor suggested students understood ambition. The Iris rumor suggested they understood drama badly. The sponsorship offers suggested adults had worse survival instincts than teenagers when revenue entered the room. The anonymous note suggested someone competent enough to be worth identifying.

Roan sorted the messages by likely origin and forwarded three to Ines.

She replied: *If this is your idea of courtship, I understand why the Iris rumor works.*

He didn't dignify that with a response.

Drogan returned at hour thirty-eight, after speaking with Cole.

He looked worse.

Not physically. Physically, Drogan had received stitches, a brace under his coat, and enough pain suppression to make his expression slightly less murderous. The damage was elsewhere. Old report. Buried expedition. Cole's choice. Aldric's warning. All of it had been poured back into places Drogan had spent thirty years mortaring shut.

He stood beside Brendt's bed and looked down at the unconscious boy.

"He shouldn't have been there," Drogan said.

"No."

"I let him come."

"Yes."

"You would have gone anyway."

"Yes."

"So I made the least bad call."

Roan looked at him. "Do you want absolution or review?"

Drogan barked a laugh without humor. "Review. Always review."

"Then the error wasn't allowing Brendt to come. The error was entering with insufficient extraction redundancy, no independent medical transport, and too little information about Hollowkin command structures. Brendt's presence raised risk. It also prevented two failures before they matured. He carried the anchors, stabilized the camp, and noticed the false retreat vector before Ines finished mapping it. Without him, we may not have reached the temple."

Drogan stared at Brendt.

"That your way of saying he earned it?"

"No one earns being gutted."

"Close enough."

Drogan left after that. He didn't say thank you. Roan didn't expect him to. Some men apologized by continuing to stand guard while pretending they were only passing through.

At hour forty-one, the leftmost monitor changed rhythm.

Roan closed his notebook before Brendt's eyes opened.

"Sorry," Brendt rasped, eyes half-open, voice rough as scraped concrete. "For... Being heavy."

Roan looked up from the chair beside the bed.

The private recovery room smelled of antiseptic, vitalist sealant, and the faint citrus oil Black Spire's medical wing used to convince rich families that injury was happening in a respectable environment. The walls were pale stone threaded with soft wardlight. The windows were frosted. Three monitoring arrays hovered over Brendt's bed, rotating through pulse, Vitalis circulation, organ stability, graft integrity, blood replacement rate, and a dozen other categories Roan had learned to read because trusting doctors was less efficient than verifying them.

Brendt lay wrapped from shoulder to hip in layered bandages and translucent reinforcement film. His left arm rested in a suspension brace. One side of his chest rose slower than the other. His face had the waxy look of someone whose body had temporarily outsourced several functions to expensive machinery.

Alive.

That was the relevant status.

"Your weight wasn't the problem," Roan said.

Brendt blinked slowly. "That sounded almost kind."

"Your blood distribution was."

"There it is."

The familiarity helped more than it should have. Roan didn't examine that, which didn't make it disappear either.

"Where's Drogan?" Brendt asked, the words clawing out of his throat.

"Arguing with Cole."

Brendt's brow knit through the haze. "About us?"

"Mostly about thirty years ago. We are peripheral evidence."

A ghost of a grimace crossed his face. "Sounds right. Ines?"

"Detained by Dr. Seth for assisting surgery with illegal materials."

Brendt's eyes widened with genuine alarm. "Is she in trouble?"

"No. Seth hired her on the spot." I watched the relief wash over him, slow and heavy.

"That tracks... and also doesn't."

"Pellan?"

"Relocated. Paid. Terrified. Promoted to a basement department with no doors."

He absorbed that, a flicker of pity in his eyes before exhaustion banked it. "Iris?"

I closed the notebook on my lap, the hinge clicking sharply. "Alive. Gone. Temporarily useful."

His chest hitched—a half-laugh that immediately soured into a wince. "Those words..."

"Worry you. Yes."

"You're learning."

"And you?"

"Functional."

"You always say that when you're one bad hallway away from collapsing."

"Then the hallways have been acceptable."

"Roan."

There was an unusual weight in the name. Not fear. Not accusation. Brendt didn't do accusation well when injured. He did concern badly and persistence worse.

Roan looked at the monitors instead of his face. "Forged baseline stabilized. Vitalis currently four thousand one hundred and eighty-two. Peak inaccessible until recovery. Inversion quota still regenerating. Structural debt down by twenty-three percent after enforced sleep and food, plus Dr. Seth threatening to nail me to a bed."

The surgery had cost more than Roan's first three businesses combined.

Black Spire would cover part of it under student expedition insurance, assuming Cole's sealed protocol held. The rest would come from salvage credits, tournament reserves, and whatever assets Roan could extract from Valech's dead network before Greel froze the accounts. Money wasn't the immediate problem. Money was a solvable pressure. The immediate problem was that Brendt's recovery required time, and time was the one resource enemies could attack without entering a room.

Roan had already drafted three schedules.

Schedule A assumed six weeks of protected recovery, limited physical therapy, no external attacks, and Brendt obeying medical restrictions. It was optimistic to the point of fiction.

Schedule B assumed four weeks before the first serious attempt to exploit Brendt as leverage. It included safe-room rotation, communications screening, controlled visitor access, and one staged rumor implying Brendt had seen nothing useful in the Rift because he had been unconscious for most of it.

Schedule C assumed an attack inside the medical wing.

He had spent the most time on Schedule C.

Dr. Seth, upon discovering a partial copy of Schedule C written on the back of a meal voucher, had added three corrections, one insult, and a list of which walls were load-bearing. She had then confiscated the voucher on the grounds that "bad plans spread if left untreated."

Roan had revised from memory.

Brendt watched his face while the monitors cycled. "You're planning something."

"Several things."

"Are any of them legal?"

"One is adjacent."

"Proud of you."

"Don't be."

"Too late."

The attempt at levity trembled at the edges. Painkillers made Brendt slower, but not stupid. He knew, in the way people knew after almost dying, that the room wasn't merely a recovery room. It was a temporary pause between categories of danger. The problem was whether to tell him how many.

Roan had originally intended to wait.

Waiting had advantages. Brendt's body needed recovery. His mind needed fewer shocks, not more. Information could be released in layers, each tied to a concrete task. That was clean. Sensible.

It was also cowardly if delayed beyond utility.

Brendt had nearly died without knowing why the enemies had come. Keeping him ignorant preserved his comfort at the cost of his agency. Roan had no objection to manipulating enemies. He had more objection than expected to doing it to Brendt.

Annoying discovery.

He added it to the internal continuity record under liabilities.

"She would."

"Yes."

"Good."

Roan looked back.

Brendt's mouth twitched. The attempt cost him enough that the smile vanished quickly. "You look awful."

"You look worse."

"I was opened like a badly designed pack."

"Accurate."

"Did we win?"

Roan paused.

A simple question. A dangerous one.

They had survived. They had escaped the Rift. They had recovered Aldric's letter, the Black Rite, proof of the Inversion civilization, images of the Founding targets, Greel evidence, Iris's partial brief, Valech's dying word, and Headmaster Cole's old report. Roan had broken into Forged. Brendt lived. Drogan had recovered a piece of old memory. Ines had gained access to materials that would keep her happily criminal for weeks.

They had also alerted something in the Spire foundations, confirmed Greel wanted the Shard, left a Hollowkin sovereign alive or at least unresolved, exposed Brendt to a war older than the city, and placed Iris in a temporary double position that could collapse into betrayal with one bad message.

Winning was a word people used when they wanted consequences to stop at the scoreboard.

"Provisionally," Roan said.

Brendt closed his eyes. "I hate that I understand that."

"Exposure develops resistance."

"You used that line before."

"It remains true."

For a while, neither spoke.

The monitors hummed. Rain tapped the frosted window. Somewhere in the corridor, a nurse told someone they weren't important enough to ignore visiting hours, which Roan found professionally admirable.

Brendt opened his eyes again. "Did you use it?"

Roan didn't pretend not to understand.

"Yes."

"The last quota."

"Yes."

Brendt stared at the ceiling.

The silence lasted longer this time.

"That was stupid," he said.

"Yes."

"Objectively?"

"Situationally."

"Don't lawyer me."

Roan leaned back in the chair. "The last quota had been reserved for killing the Hollowkin command node or escaping a terminal engagement. You were in irreversible decline under Rift contamination, blood loss, and core shock. Without inversion-assisted stabilization, your survival probability dropped below useful thresholds before we reached the outer corridor. I chose to spend the quota on you. This created additional combat risk, which I partially offset by inventing a dangerous technique I shouldn't have tested under live conditions. The technique worked. Therefore the decision appears successful but shouldn't be generalized without review."

Brendt turned his head slowly and stared at him.

"That," he said, "was the most horrifying way anyone has ever said they saved my life."

"You asked for no lawyering."

"I asked for less lawyering."

"You didn't specify."

Brendt closed his eyes and breathed carefully around pain. When he opened them again, they were wet but not spilling. Roan looked away before the expression could become a demand.

Brendt knew him well enough not to make one.

"Thank you," Brendt said.

"Accepted."

"That's it?"

"What else should happen?"

"Normal people say something like 'you're welcome.'"

"You're welcome."

The words felt poorly fitted, like borrowed armor. Roan preferred exact language, and exact language would've been uglier: *I spent a resource I needed because your death created an unacceptable future.* It was true, but truth delivered without calibration was just blunt trauma wearing clean shoes.

Brendt would hear enough ugly truth later.

"No, you should keep it."

"Then retract it."

"Too late."

The monitor above him settled after the exchange, pulse dropping two beats. Humor as medicine. Inefficient, unsterile, impossible to dose, but apparently not useless. Roan made a private note and immediately disliked the category it belonged to.

"How bad?" Brendt asked.

"Your injuries?"

"My injuries, school, enemies, whatever category our life is now."

"Your injuries: severe but recoverable. Full combat restriction for six weeks minimum. Shield arm limited for longer unless graft integration exceeds projection. School: contained. Cole is suppressing official inquiries. Enemies: Greel confirmed active. The Vell patriarch uncertain. Iris unstable. Founding involvement upgraded from historical to current. Rift entity may have followed us. Drogan is hiding less but still hiding."

Brendt absorbed that with the expression of a man receiving an itemized bill after a fire.

"Cool," he said faintly. "Very manageable."

"No."

"I was being sarcastic."

"Yes."

"You could help."

"You need rest, not optimism."

"I need both. Humans are inefficient that way."

Roan glanced at the door. No one outside close enough to hear, assuming the room wards were Cole's and not someone else's. He had checked twice. He still didn't trust them.

Brendt noticed the glance.

His face changed.

"There's more."

"Yes."

"Is it worse than memory solvent and ancient murder families?"

"Related."

"That wasn't a no."

Roan placed the notebook on the bedside table and stood. The movement pulled at the stress line behind his eye. Less than before. He took the small warded case from his inner coat pocket.

Brendt watched it.

"Is that something that's going to explode?"

"No."

"You answered too fast."

"It won't explode under current conditions."

"Roan."

"It's a letter."

That silenced him.

Roan set the case on the table but didn't open it yet.

The letter from Aldric had been copied twice since they returned. Once by Cole's null-room scanner, under Roan's supervision and over Drogan's objections. Once by Roan himself, by hand, because machines created dependencies and dependencies became vulnerabilities. The original remained sealed in a lead-lined, ward-muted pouch beneath three layers of false inventory labels.

This was the handwritten copy.

Not all of it.

Roan had redacted technical sequences that couldn't help Brendt and might make him a target. He had also removed two lines that spoke directly to the Codex Shard's deeper structure. Trust had limits. Limits preserved relationships better than dramatic confessions did.

Still, the useful truth remained.

Aldric wasn't from Earth.

The Founding Seven had destroyed his civilization.

Inversion was older than the Spires, older than the Bureau, older than the public power system that pretended to have invented categories for everything that mattered.

The people hunting Roan weren't merely enemies.

They were heirs to a successful genocide.

Brendt looked from the case to Roan's face.

"You're deciding whether to tell me something," he said.

"I decided thirty minutes ago."

"Then why are you standing like that?"

Roan considered lying.

Not because the truth was dangerous. Because the truth was irritating.

"Because after I show you, you become harder to keep uninvolved."

Brendt's expression shifted again, slower this time.

"Roan," he said, "I was uninvolved up until the moment a Husk tore my leg open in our first Rift and you decided friendship was apparently a terminal diagnosis. Everything after that has been paperwork."

Roan stared at him.

Brendt stared back, pale, bandaged, absurdly stubborn.

The monitor over the bed beeped once, as if taking his side.

Roan disliked democratic machinery.

He opened the case.

The hinges made no sound. Roan had oiled them twice in the last hour despite the case being new. Brendt noticed, because Brendt noticed small human things Roan usually categorized as irrelevant until they stopped being irrelevant.

"You are nervous," Brendt said.

"No."

"Then the case is nervous."

"The case is well maintained."

"That's what I said."

Roan looked at him for half a second. "Do you want the truth or a discussion about hardware?"

"Both, but Seth will drug me soon, so truth first."

Before he pulled out the pages, he checked Brendt's pulse with two fingers.

Brendt looked offended. "I am not going to faint before the terrible secret."

"People make ambitious claims before terrible secrets."

"Fine. But if I faint, don't let Ines hear about it."

"She will infer."

"Then lie."

"Badly?"

"For me, try."

Roan considered the request and decided it wasn't worth refusing. "Fine."

Brendt blinked. "That worked?"

"Don't overuse it."

"I have a quota?"

"Everyone does."

The answer made Brendt smile, which made one monitor complain and forced him to stop smiling. That seemed like sufficient deterrence.

Roan removed the copied letter from the case and placed the pages face down first, in order. He had already decided which technical lines to redact. The visible version would be enough to change Brendt's model of the world without teaching him how to open doors better left shut.

That distinction mattered.

Trust didn't mean handing someone a loaded mechanism because honesty felt clean. Trust meant giving enough truth for agency and enough restraint for survival. It was more work than lying. That was why most people preferred speeches.

Roan hated speeches.

He opened his mouth, then closed it. Words assembled in the wrong order, then disassembled. He didn't have a script for this. The letter was the script. The letter was all he had.

Brendt's hand shifted weakly on the bed. "Before I faint, read it."

Roan read.
