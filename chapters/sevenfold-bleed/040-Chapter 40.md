Chapter 40: Greycross First

The training yard was quiet at four in the morning. Roan preferred it that way.

He stood at the edge of the sparring circle, bare feet pressed into the frost-hardened grass, and watched the Codex Shard's pale glow pulse faintly against his sternum. The Shard had been embedded for eleven days now. Still no second dose of Bloodscourge. Still no word from Drogan about the tournament qualification rounds.

The Shard pulsed.

[Sidewound capacity: 2/4.]

[Replenishes at dawn.]

The Inversion had been sitting at half quota for six days straight, which was exactly as comfortable as it sounded.

He rolled his shoulders. The Frostcough pills he'd taken yesterday were still cycling through his system. Their intended effect was supposed to be temporary immunity amplification, a defensive boost for tournament qualifiers. Their Sidewound, however, was a mild neural static that made his fingertips feel like they'd been dipped in ice water for the next six hours.

Roan had run this experiment a dozen times. The Inversion didn't care about intended effects. It only recognized the Sidewound.

[Sidewound detected: Cold Sensitivity (minor).]

[Conversion successful: +34 Vitalis.]

[Yield: 100%. Your fingers will thank me later. — A.V.]

He flexed his hands. The numbness was gone—not neutralized, but inverted. His fingers felt more responsive than before the Frostcough hit. Cold sensitivity had flipped into cold resistance. Neural static had become neural sharpening.

That was the mirror problem. Most cultivators took Bloodscourge and accepted the trade. The Inversion looked at that trade and asked why anyone would accept those terms. The damage was the resource. The scarring was the investment.

Math didn't lie.

Behind him, the training yard's east gate creaked open.

Roan didn't turn. He recognized the footsteps, uneven, slightly heavy on the left heel. Brendt.

"You're up early," Brendt said, coming to stand beside him at the circle's edge. His breath fogged in the pre-dawn chill. "Couldn't sleep?"

"Slept four hours." Roan watched the frost on the grass blades catch the first grey light. "Sufficient."

"Sufficient." Brendt shook his head. "You know that's not normal, right? Most people need—"

"Most people aren't Tempered yet."

Brendt's jaw worked silently for a moment. He was still Initiate, had been for three months since his breakthrough, still adjusting to the new baseline of his own Vitalis. Every time Roan mentioned his rank, Brendt's left eye twitched slightly. Not resentment, exactly. More like the frustration of someone standing at the bottom of a ladder while watching someone else sprint up it.

Which, Roan calculated, was an accurate summary of their situation.

"The qualification brackets got posted this morning," Brendt said, pivoting to safer ground. "Third-round prelims. You got matched against Delray Marsh."

Roan searched his memory. Delray. Third-year. Tempered, mid-stage. Specialized in defense-oriented techniques. Vitalis readout somewhere around eight hundred, maybe nine if he'd been training hard.

"I know him," Roan said.

"He broke two of Theon's ribs last month. Sparring match. Theon said it felt like hitting a wall."

"Heavy Vitalis focus. High bone density. Low Vitalis output but consistent." Roan's eyes tracked a bird lifting off from the dormitory roof, wings cutting through the fog. "His defense is his weakness."

"His defense is his—" Brendt stopped. "How is his defense his weakness?"

"Because he's optimized for not taking damage. That means he reacts instead of initiates. Predictable patterns." Roan finally turned to look at Brendt directly. "I won't be hitting a wall. I'll be walking around one."

Brendt blinked. Then, slowly, a grin cracked across his face. "You're going to toy with him."

"I'm going to win efficiently." Roan's expression didn't change. "There's a difference."

"Right." Brendt's grin faded into something more thoughtful. "What about you, though? The brackets had a lot of weird pairings this round. Did you see who else is in your bracket?"

"I saw."

"And?"

Roan looked back at the frost-covered grass. The qualification rounds were a funnel, sixty-four students down to sixteen, sixteen to eight, eight to the final bracket that mattered for tournament seeding. Third-round prelims were designed to separate serious contenders from hopefuls.

The bracket math had taken him less than a minute to calculate. He'd be facing Delray in round one, which meant he'd advance to round two. Round two would pit him against either Sera Vance (no relation, same clan name, different bloodline) or a first-year prodigy from the Southern territories whose Vitalis readout topped out at six hundred but whose technique scores were conspicuously high.

Tournament committees loved prodigies. They generated attendance revenue.

But the math didn't account for everything. The prodigy's technique scores suggested heavy investment in offensive forms, exactly the kind of cultivator who burned hot and fast and collapsed when someone refused to play their game.

Round three was where things got interesting.

Ines Carrow was seeded second in the bracket. Which meant, assuming she won her first two matches, she'd face the winner of Roan's bracket in the quarterfinals.

Ines Carrow. Rank: Tempered, late-stage. Vitalis: eighteen hundred, publicly. Higher. Specialty: Hollowstrike Doctrine, which Drogan had been teaching Roan for the past six weeks. The technique was a counter-system, reflective combat that turned an opponent's force back against them. The Sidewound was brutal: every reflected blow damaged the user's joints equally.

The Inversion loved Hollowstrike. Drogan hadn't yet understood why. Roan kept that information close. Disclosure meant revealing the Inversion's full scope—a card he wasn't ready to play. Trust was useful, but full transparency carried risks he couldn't calculate yet.

"You're thinking about Carrow," Brendt said.

Roan didn't answer.

"She beat Theon in twelve seconds last semester. Just stood there and let him throw everything he had, then turned it all back at him." Brendt's voice dropped slightly. "Theon said it felt like hitting himself."

"I know."

"So what's your play?"

Roan watched the bird disappear over the dormitory roof. "Carrow isn't my problem in round three."

"But—"

"Delray is my problem in round three. Everything else is speculation." He turned away from the training circle. "I need to eat. Then I need to review Delray's last six sparring matches. Then I need to find Drogan and ask about Hollowstrike's blind angles."

"Roan." Brendt grabbed his arm. His hand was warm, body heat running higher than normal, the lingering effect of his recent breakthrough. "What's going on with you?"

"What do you mean?"

"I mean—" Brendt released his arm, stepping back. His brow furrowed. "You've been different since the Bloodscourge. More focused. More calculating. And I don't mean that as a compliment. You used to at least pretend to care about things."

"I care about things."

"You care about math."

Roan paused. The accusation landed with uncomfortable precision. He thought about his mother, who worked fourteen-hour shifts at the Greycross refinery. He thought about Lyra, who sent him letters asking when he'd come home for the winter break. He thought about the Codex Shard, pulsing faintly against his chest, and the Inversion that had rewritten his understanding of what strength cost.

He pushed the images aside. Lyra's face, Mira's silence—they were weights he couldn't carry into the arena. Not yet. Maybe not ever.

"I care about getting stronger," he said finally. "Everything else is a variable I can't control."

Brendt stared at him. Then he shook his head. "That's not the same thing, man. That's not even close."

"Maybe not." Roan started walking toward the mess hall. "But it's what's available."

He left Brendt standing in the frost, his footsteps crunching on the frozen grass. Behind him, the training yard was beginning to fill with early risers, students who wanted to get in extra practice before the qualification rounds started. Roan ignored them all.

The mess hall was warm and loud, packed with students grabbing quick meals before the morning matches. Roan collected his tray—protein paste, fermented vegetable paste, a cup of something that claimed to be tea—and found a seat in the corner, back to the wall.

He pulled out his notebook. Delray's sparring records. He'd memorized the patterns weeks ago, but repetition was the foundation of preparation.

Halfway through his meal, a shadow fell across his table.

"You're Vance."

Roan looked up. The girl standing over him was young, maybe fifteen, first-year by the look of her academy robes. Her Vitalis readout was faint, barely above civilian baseline. Initiate, early stage. Nervous, but trying to hide it.

"Correct."

"I'm Yara. Yara Solt." She swallowed. "I just wanted to say, my brother competed against you last month. In the open sparring session. He said you were—" she hesitated, searching for the word, "—impossible to read."

"Your brother?"

"Davin. He said you didn't even look at him during the match. Just waited until he committed to a technique, then stepped aside like you already knew exactly what he was going to do."

Roan recalled the match. Davin Solt. Initiate, mid-stage. Aggressive starter, sloppy finisher. He'd lunged three times with the same punch. Roan had sidestepped twice and swept his legs on the third attempt.

"I remembered his pattern," Roan said. "That's all."

"But that's—" She stopped herself. She was visibly struggling with something. "Never mind. I just wanted to say good luck today. Delray's tough, but I think you can beat him."

She turned and walked away before Roan could respond. He watched her go. There was a tightness in her shoulders that didn't match her casual tone. He noted it without conclusion, then returned to his notebook.

The equation left little room for comfort. Delray would try to outlast him with superior defense. Roan would need to break through that defense without exhausting his own resources. The Inversion could handle any Sidewound that arose from Hollowstrike, but he'd need to land strikes to generate the openings.

And Hollowstrike's blind angles—

[Note: Hollowstrike reflects force, but force carries rhythm. Against an unstable Vitalis pulse, the reflection scatters. Aim for the off-beat. — A.V.]

Roan closed his notebook.

The qualification rounds started in two hours. He'd find Drogan first. Then he'd prepare.

The mess hall buzzed around him, full of students talking about bracket predictions and technique analysis and a dozen other variables that Roan had already calculated and discarded. They didn't understand what he understood.

The Inversion didn't just change how he processed side effects. It changed how he processed everything. Every fight was a system. Every opponent was a set of variables. And variables could be solved.

Delray Marsh would block. He'd wait for Roan to overextend. He'd counter with his signature brace technique, locking joints and grinding through defenses with pure bone density.

But bone density couldn't stop what it couldn't catch.

Roan stood, collected his tray, and headed for the door. Somewhere in this academy, Drogan was waiting with answers about Hollowstrike's blind spots.

And somewhere in the back of his mind, the Codex Shard pulsed with a faint warmth that might've been anticipation.

Or might've been a warning.

He didn't know which. He wouldn't know until he stepped into the arena.

Arithmetic had no incentive to flatter him. But sometimes the math was incomplete.

He pushed through the mess hall doors and walked into the morning light.
