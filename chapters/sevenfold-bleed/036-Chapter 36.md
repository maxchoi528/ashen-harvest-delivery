Chapter 36: Clean Hands

The summons arrived during second-period tactical theory, slipped under the door like a blade finding its mark between armor plates.

Roan didn't move until the proctor's lecture drifted into irrelevance, something about Rift incursion response protocols that the Sentinel Bureau would ignore anyway, and then he crossed the room in four steps. The parchment was heavy stock, embossed with the Ashford crest. Official. Deliberate.

Cyrus Ashford was calling him out.

Not the careless taunts of the training yard. Not the whispered insinuations that followed him through corridors. This was formal challenge protocol, witnessed by proctors, logged with the Student Affairs office. A duel with terms already written.

The text was concise, almost elegant in its contempt:

*You will present yourself at the Eastern Courtyard dueling platform at fourth bell. The matter of your father's debts will be settled publicly, as fitting for a Vance. Stakes: 500 Spire Credits each. You may forfeit and retain your shame. I recommend it.*

500 credits. That was more than Roan had accumulated in four months of careful maneuvering. More than enough to fund three months of Black Spire rations, or purchase two more Bloodscourge Draughts on the grey market, or. He set the parchment down. The proctor was still talking. Seventeen minutes until fourth bell.

The shape was simple, but the pieces he had to use weren't.

Cyrus Ashford was Tempered rank. That wasn't rumor. Roan had seen him punch through a training dummy reinforced with iron bands during last week's combat assessment. The Ashford family had funded their heir's advancement with every available resource. Vitalis reading hovered somewhere between 400 and 600, depending on how much genetic enhancement the family had pumped into their bloodline.

Roan's own reading sat at 247. Visible. Deliberately calibrated to look modest rather than pathetic.

The gap was significant. A straight fight would be unwise. Dangerous. Fatal if Cyrus decided to make an example.

But 500 credits was 500 credits. And the alternative, public forfeiture, would paint a target on his back that even his careful low profile couldn't hide. The Ashfords would smell weakness. Others would follow.

Drogan found him in the corridor outside the dueling platform, fifteen minutes early, running calculations.

"You received the challenge." Not a question.

"I received it."

"And you're going to fight him anyway."

Roan didn't answer. Drogan's iron hand caught his shoulder, warm metal, slightly too heavy, the weight of a man who'd killed things that most students at Black Spire had never seen.

"Cyrus Ashford has been Tempered for eleven months. He has formal training in the Ashford Spire technique. His family has connections to House Greel that go back three generations." Drogan's voice was flat, instructional. "You are Initiate. You have a knife that hasn't been properly blooded and a technique that makes you flinch every time you use it."

"The Sevenfold Bleed isn't—"

"The Sevenfold Bleed will tear your Vitalis channels apart if you haven't learned to control the feedback." Drogan's grip tightened. "And showing it here, in public, where every proctor and half the senior faculty will see? That's not a fight, boy. That's a confession. They'll know exactly what you are."

Roan met his mentor's eye. The ledger was already done, filed somewhere beneath conscious thought, surfacing as decision.

"What if I don't use it?"

"Then you lose. Get hospitalized. The Ashfords win, you lose your credits, and every opportunist in this academy decides you're easy prey." Drogan released him. "The math doesn't work, Roan. Not unless you're planning something I can't see."

Fourth bell rang. Three floors up, the Eastern Courtyard bells answered in metallic chorus.

"I always am."

The dueling platform was elevated stone, worn smooth by generations of students who'd settled grudges here. The Ashford crest had been stamped into the flagstones at the platform's center. Cyrus had paid for that, Roan knew. A message. *This is Ashford ground now.*

Forty students ringed the platform. Word had spread faster than arithmetic. Half of them wore expressions of anticipation, the hungry curiosity of spectators who'd come to watch someone get humiliated. The other half looked uncomfortable, aware that something wrong was happening but unwilling to intervene.

Cyrus Ashford stood at the platform's northern edge. Tall, blonde, built like someone who'd never missed a meal in his life. His training clothes bore the Ashford house colors, deep crimson and black, and his expression carried the satisfaction of a man who'd already won.

To his left, a proctor from the Combat Theory department. To his right. Roan's jaw tightened.

Selene Ardent. House Ardent's heir, watching with eyes that gave nothing away. She shouldn't be here. The Ardent family maintained careful neutrality in academy politics, and public appearances at Ashford-sponsored events were explicitly against that policy.

Unless she was here to observe him specifically.

"Roan Vance." Cyrus's voice carried across the platform, pitched to reach every spectator. "Thank you for accepting. I wasn't certain you had the courage."

Roan climbed the steps. His boots found the stone with deliberate weight, announcing each step. The Shard hung heavy against his chest—a weight no one else could see.

"I wasn't certain you had the credits to back your mouth," he said. "But here we are."

Laughter rippled through the crowd. Small, but audible. Cyrus's smile flickered at the edges.

"The stakes have been verified by Student Affairs." The proctor stepped forward, holding a sealed ledger. "500 Spire Credits from each combatant. Winner takes all. Standard dueling rules apply: first incapacitation or yield. Lethal force forbidden. Proctors will intervene only at the point of serious injury."

Standard rules. The kind that allowed broken bones and internal bleeding, just not death. The kind that let winners make examples of losers without technically violating academy code.

Cyrus drew his weapon, a longsword, not the training wooden models used in basic combat courses. The blade caught the afternoon light, edge gleaming with something more than polish.

"Mother's gift," Cyrus said, noticing Roan's gaze. "Ashford steel. I'm told it holds an edge even through bone." He shifted his grip, settling into a stance Roan recognized from tactical theory texts. The Ashford Spire opener. A thrust-focused technique that prioritized reach and timing over power.

Roan drew Sidewound.

The blade was short, unremarkable, not worth noticing from Cyrus's perspective. The visible Vitalis reading attached to Roan's wrist, 247, would do nothing to change that assessment.

"Let the record show both combatants have accepted terms." The proctor raised his hand. "On my mark."

Cyrus's eyes hadn't left Roan's face. Calculating. Enjoying this.

*He thinks this is a teaching moment*, Roan realized. *He thinks he's going to humiliate me, make an example, and walk away with everyone watching him prove the Vance line died with my father.*

The proctor's hand dropped.

"Begin."

Cyrus moved.

The first strike came fast, faster than most Initiates could track. The Ashford Spire technique prioritized speed over power, and Cyrus had been drilled in it since childhood. The blade swept toward Roan's throat in a horizontal arc designed to force a defensive retreat.

Roan didn't retreat.

He stepped *into* the strike, letting the blade pass close enough to feel wind on his neck, and drove Sidewound upward in a motion that served no defensive purpose whatsoever.

Cyrus's eyes widened. The blade continued its arc, would have opened Roan's shoulder if the proctor hadn't stepped in. But Roan wasn't aiming for the shoulder.

Sidewound found the gap between Cyrus's extended arm and his torso, a three-inch slice that would bleed freely but wouldn't incapacitate. Exactly enough to demonstrate that the Ashford heir had left an opening.

Not a teaching moment.

A message.

"You lead with your ego," Roan said, stepping back into range. "Your father encouraged that."

Cyrus's face went red. He reset his stance, faster this time, anger replacing calculation.

The second exchange was a blur of steel. Cyrus pressed forward with combinations that came from formal training, textbook techniques, optimized for dueling against other Tempered opponents. Each strike carried enough force to shatter bone if it landed clean.

Roan gave ground. Not much, but enough. Sidewound deflected where it could, slipped past guard lines where deflection wasn't possible. Every hit he took was shallow, placed where muscle and bone would absorb the worst of it without critical damage.

*247 Vitalis against 400+.* The figures said he couldn't win a sustained exchange. The figures said attrition would grind him down.

But the tally also said nothing about making every exchange cost *Cyrus* something.

Blood dripped from the gash on Cyrus's forearm. A slice across his thigh. A shallow cut above his ear that was already starting to clot. None of them were serious. All of them were embarrassing.

"You call this fighting?" Cyrus hissed, resetting again. "You're just running."

"I'm just not dying on your schedule."

The third exchange ended differently.

Cyrus feinted left, then dropped into a sweeping low kick that caught Roan's leading leg and sent him off-balance. The longsword came up in a rising cut that would have taken Roan's knee out if it landed.

It didn't land.

Roan twisted, letting the kick take his balance instead of fighting it, and fell *toward* Cyrus instead of away. His shoulder struck Cyrus's chest. His weight drove them both toward the platform's edge. And Sidewound, held in a reverse grip during the fall, found the inside of Cyrus's thigh and opened a line that spurted arterial bright.

The proctor's whistle split the air.

"Hold! Medical assessment required!"

Roan rolled away, coming up with Sidewound ready. His shoulder screamed where he'd landed on stone. His leg ached from the kick. The visible wounds on his arms and torso were shallow but bleeding freely.

But Cyrus was pressing his hand against his thigh, blood seeping between his fingers, face white with shock.

"Arterial," the proctor said, examining the wound with clinical efficiency. "Combat can't continue. Winner: Vance."

The crowd erupted. Not the satisfied roar of spectators who'd watched a confident victor prove himself, but something messier, confused, people realizing they'd misread the entire fight.

Roan walked to the platform's edge and looked down at Cyrus Ashford, who was staring at his own blood with an expression that suggested he'd never seen so much of it before.

"Your mother gave you a nice sword," Roan said quietly. "Maybe next time she'll hire you a real instructor."

He collected his winnings from the proctor's ledger, 1,000 credits, transferred instantly to his academy account, and walked down the opposite set of steps without looking back.

---

Selene caught him in the corridor outside the infirmary wing, forty minutes later. His wounds had been treated by the academy healers, standard procedure after sanctioned duels, and he'd spent the intervening time calculating what the Ashfords would do next.

The answer was ugly. Very ugly.

"You took unnecessary risks."

Roan didn't slow down. "You were watching."

"Someone needed to. The Ashfords have been building toward this for months. They wouldn't have issued a formal challenge without a backup plan."

"Everyone has a backup plan. The question is whether they have a tertiary plan when the first two fail."

Selene fell into step beside him. Her expression was unreadable, which meant she was calculating something worth hiding.

"You're not going to tell me what you're planning."

"You haven't asked what I'm planning."

"I'm asking now."

Roan stopped. The corridor was empty, afternoon hours, most students in training sessions or lectures. He turned to face her, noting the way her weight shifted slightly forward, the micro-tells of someone preparing for unexpected information.

"The Ashfords will claim I cheated. They'll say the wounds I inflicted were outside normal dueling parameters, that I targeted arteries specifically to force a medical stoppage rather than fighting to incapacitation." He paused. "They're right. I did."

"The proctor's assessment was—"

"The proctor's assessment was correct. Medical stoppage is valid. But the Ashford family doesn't care about validity. They care about optics. And right now, the optics are that their heir got cut open by a Vance he should have crushed."

Selene's eyes narrowed. "You're baiting them."

"I'm creating a situation where their response will tell me more than their words ever could."

"Roan." Her voice dropped. "The Ashfords have House Greel connections. If the white-bearded man decides you're worth eliminating personally—"

"Then my father was wrong about him, and we're all dead anyway." He resumed walking. "But that's not going to happen. Not yet. Not until they understand what I am."

"Which is?"

Roan smiled. It wasn't a pleasant expression.

"Ask your father. House Ardent has old archives. Old enough to remember what the Vance name meant before my grandfather made it famous." He paused at the junction where their paths diverged. "Or don't. Either way, the Ashfords will make their move within three days. Watch for it."

He turned left, toward the dormitory wing, and left Selene Ardent standing in the corridor with an expression that had finally cracked into something approaching genuine uncertainty.

---

The message from Brendt arrived that evening, delivered by academy courier with the urgency that meant he'd heard about the duel before the official reports had been filed.

*You fought a Tempered Ashford. In a public duel. For 500 credits. Are you trying to get killed, or do you actually have a plan I'm not seeing?*

Roan set the note aside without answering. His shoulder still ached from the fall, and the healer had been explicit about avoiding strain for at least twelve hours.

His Vitalis reading had dropped to 231, loss of blood, combined with the healer's treatment accelerating his body's natural recovery cycle. Sidewound's blade had absorbed some of the impact, its metal edge holding microscopic traces of the blood it had tasted, but that was a small comfort against the weight of what was coming.

The Shard pulsed against his chest. Warm. Almost alive.

[You took unnecessary risks. — A.V.]

Roan didn't respond to the voice in his head. He'd learned that much, at least, that engaging with his father's residual consciousness only made the episodes longer and stranger.

[I mean it. Cyrus Ashford is a minor nuisance. What you're doing is painting a target on yourself that even Drogan can't protect you from.]

"I'm aware."

[The Ashfords aren't the problem. They never were. The question is what you do when the Greels decide you're worth noticing.]

"That's a problem for future Roan."

[Future Roan is going to be very disappointed in current Roan.]

Current Roan closed his eyes and began running calculations for the scenarios most likely to unfold in the next seventy-two hours. The Ashfords would appeal to Student Affairs, claiming the duel had violated unwritten codes of conduct. The appeal would fail, because the proctor's testimony was unambiguous. Then they would escalate, social pressure, family connections, a formal complaint to the Sentinel Bureau about his father's alleged crimes.

The last option was the dangerous one. If the Ashfords convinced the Bureau to reopen the Vance investigation. Roan opened his eyes.

The Shard pulsed again. Warmer.

[You have questions. I can feel them circling.]

"I always have questions."

[Ask one.]

Roan considered the offer. The Codex Shard had been part of him for less than a month, but he'd learned its rhythms. Aldric Echo spoke in fragments, revealing information only when it served some purpose Roan couldn't see.

If he asked about the Greels, Aldric would give him a piece of the puzzle. A small piece, carefully selected to guide rather than inform.

He asked anyway.

"What did you do to make the white-bearded man hate you?"

Silence. The kind that felt heavy, pregnant with something that might emerge as words or might crush him under its weight.

[That's two questions. You're only owed one.]

"Fine. What did *I* do to make the white-bearded man hate me?"

The Shard's warmth flared, almost painful, and then subsided.

[You exist. That's enough. The Vance bloodline carried proof that their order was a toll system dressed up as law. They built a world where power *must* come at a cost. Pain, sacrifice, blood, years off your life. It makes the system stable. People accept their place because they believe the price is universal.]

[We proved the price was a choice. That suffering could be turned inside out, the cost refused, the ledger reversed.]

[They didn't kill me for vengeance, Roan. They killed me because I held the evidence. If you rise, you don't just avenge me. You resurrect the proof they spent forty years burying.]

Roan understood. The weight of it wasn't abstraction. It was a single concrete truth that had been crushed to keep the world orderly. The white-bearded man wasn't just an old enemy. He was the warden of that lie.

"They can't let me live."

[They can't let you *win*. Dying is straightforward. Proving them wrong is the part they can't survive.]

The Shard went cold against his chest. Aldric retreating, the conversation over, the voice fading back into silence.

Roan sat in the darkness of his room and listened to his own heartbeat and calculated the odds of surviving what was coming.

The shape didn't improve when he turned it over.

But math had never been the only variable that mattered. Not for him.

---

The Ashfords moved faster than he'd predicted.

A delegation arrived at his dormitory room the next morning, three proctors from Student Affairs, accompanied by a man in civilian clothes whose bearing suggested Sentinel Bureau affiliation. The summons was polite in its language and absolute in its implications.

House Ashford had filed a formal complaint regarding the conduct of the duel. Specific allegations: excessive force, targeting of vital areas, behavior unbecoming of a Black Spire student. The Bureau had opened an investigation.

Roan looked at the Sentinel, middle-aged, expressionless, a face that had seen too much to be surprised by anything, and recognized the game being played.

"You want to ask about my father."

The Sentinel's expression didn't change. "I'm here about the duel, Mr. Vance."

"The duel was clean. The proctor's report confirms—"

"The proctor's report is part of our investigation." The Sentinel produced a document, seal pressed with official stamps. "However, preliminary findings suggest that your combat methodology raises questions about training background. Techniques of the type you displayed aren't part of standard Black Spire curriculum."

*There it is.*

Roan saw the opening and the trap layered over it. If he claimed self-taught, they'd push harder, where had an Initiate from Greycross learned to fight like that? If he claimed Drogan as mentor, they'd investigate Hale, looking for connections to his father's past. If he claimed family training.

"The Vance family has combat techniques passed down through generations." Roan's voice was flat, factual. "My father taught me before his death. You can verify this with the academy archives—family combat methods are registered during enrollment."

A lie. Family methods had been registered, but Aldric Vance had registered nothing. The archive would show a gap, a deliberate absence that anyone digging would find suspicious.

But the Sentinel didn't know that. The Ashfords didn't know that. They'd moved too quickly, pushed too hard, and now they were standing in Roan's doorway without the evidence they needed.

"The Bureau will be in touch, Mr. Vance."

"I'm sure you will."

The delegation left. Roan closed the door and stood in the silence of his room, running new calculations.

The Ashfords had escalated to the Sentinel Bureau. That meant they were more scared than he'd estimated, or more connected. The Bureau didn't take cases based on wounded pride. Someone had pushed them toward this, someone who understood that Roan's existence was a threat that couldn't be answered with a simple duel.

*The white-bearded man.*

The name surfaced in his mind like oil on water, ugly and impossible to ignore. The head of House Greel. One of the old houses. A man who had apparently decided, forty years ago, that Aldric Vance needed to die, and who was now watching Aldric's son prove that the Inversion had survived.

Roan's hand found the Shard, pressing it against his chest until he could feel his own heartbeat through the metal.

The target was painted. The game had changed.

Roan crossed to his desk, retrieved the locked ledger from the bottom drawer, and opened it to the page reserved for debts. He drew a clean line through the Ashford entry—*500 credits, duel settled*—and pressed the pen hard into the paper below it.

He wrote a single name.

***Greel.** *

The white-bearded man wasn't just an old enemy. He was the warden of a lie. The lie that power required sacrifice, that the cost was universal, that suffering was the only path to strength.

Roan closed the ledger and slid it back into its hiding place. The Shard pulsed against his chest, warm and alive, carrying the evidence Aldric Vance had died to protect.

*Let him watch*, Roan thought, facing the window that looked north. *Let him see exactly what I'm going to do.*
