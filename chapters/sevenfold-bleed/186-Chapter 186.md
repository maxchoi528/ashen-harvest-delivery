Chapter 186: Reopening the Case

The morning before the statement, Roan spent nearly half an hour learning how not to look fragile in a medical chair.

This wasn't vanity. Vanity was an expensive misreading of mirrors. Optics were material. If he looked too strong, Greel would argue the duel injuries had been exaggerated. If he looked too weak, commentators would frame the statement as emotional instability from a traumatized student. If he leaned back, he looked managed. If he leaned forward too often, he looked aggressive. If he hid the splint, he wasted evidence. If he displayed it theatrically, he invited pity, and pity was a poor foundation for legal pressure.

Ines solved the problem with tape.

She marked three positions on the table: resting hand, document position, and water glass. "Don't move outside these unless someone asks about your mother or calls Aldric a traitor. If that happens, Cole cuts the question and Drogan scares the oxygen out of them."

Drogan said, "I can do that."

The communications officer looked distressed. "Please don't scare oxygen out of accredited press."

"Depends on accreditation."

Selene adjusted the line of Roan's jacket so it didn't hide the bruising at his collar. The gesture was precise and impersonal until it wasn't. Her fingers paused for half a second at the edge of the fabric.

"You can still postpone," she said.

"No."

"I know. It needed saying once."

"Now it has been."

She stepped back. "Your statement is stronger if you don't answer every question."

"I know."

"Refusal can look evasive."

"Only if the question was entitled to an answer."

"Good."

Iris entered with coffee and didn't offer any to Roan.

He looked at the cup.

"No," she said. "Your healer threatened me personally."

"Credible threat?"

"She had syringes and institutional support."

"Reasonable."

Iris handed him a water glass instead. "Last-minute hostile frames."

She projected five likely questions onto the wall.

*Are you accusing Vorath Greel of murder?*
*Why not release evidence now?*
*Did your unknown technique tamper with Kael's lawful oath?*
*Are you being used by Black Spire to strike House Greel?*
*Is this revenge?*

Roan studied the list.

The first four had clean answers. The fifth was the dangerous one because the honest answer was yes and no, and mixed answers made ugly headlines.

"Is this revenge?" Iris asked aloud.

"No," Selene said.

Drogan snorted.

Roan said, "Yes."

Cole, who had entered silently, looked like he regretted every educational philosophy that had led to this student.

Iris rubbed her forehead. "You can't say yes."

"Then don't ask questions with true answers."

"Public communication is the art of not doing that."

Roan considered.

Revenge wasn't the whole motive. It was one engine among several: survival, evidence preservation, enemy movement, debt to Aldric, threat to Brendt, risk to Mira and Lyra, and the simple fact that men who got away with murder tended to budget for more.

Calling it revenge would let enemies shrink the structure to emotion.

Denying revenge would be false enough to weaken his own internal map.

He wrote one sentence under the fifth question.

*Revenge is what people call accountability when the injured party is still alive to request it.*

Iris read it.

Then she looked at Selene.

Selene said, "Too quotable."

Iris said, "Much too quotable."

Drogan said, "He's going to say it now because you said that."

Roan crossed it out.

Everyone paused.

He wrote a replacement.

*I am requesting review, not permission to grieve.*

Cole nodded once. "Better."

"Less fun," Ines said.

"Fun isn't today's metric," Cole told her.

"Tragic."

At 13:40, the first feeds opened. Roan watched the viewer count climb on the internal monitor and made himself stop watching after ten seconds. Numbers past a certain point became weather. Weather mattered, but staring at rain didn't keep one dry.

The Codex Shard pulsed while technicians adjusted the table microphone.

[Witness field density: high.]
[Emotional resonance: unstable.]

Roan read the signals and made his own decision. Declarative structure. The room was volatile enough to twist ambiguity into liability. He didn't need an engine to tell him that. He had walked into courtrooms of public opinion before.

The press room doors opened.

Roan took one breath and used it to check the ledgers. Pressure: Greel, Vorath, public doubt, body damage. Rule: protected speech, preserved testimony, oath disclosure, review request. Resource: witnesses, archive mirrors, advisory shield. Enemy: visible Greel, hidden six, unknown listener. Next debt: force movement after the statement. Not safe. Safe was a word people used when they wanted to stop counting. But the structure held, and for the next twenty minutes structure mattered more than comfort, pain, fear, or the small private part of him that wanted to say his father's name without turning it into a weapon. He didn't give that part the room. Not today.

He checked the water glass, the microphone, the table edge, the route to the side door, and the position of every person who might move if the room turned ugly. None of that would stop Vorath. It might stop someone cheaper. Cheaper threats still killed people when ignored. Roan had grown up around cheap threats; their only virtue was honesty, which made them easier to price in advance, with receipts and dates, plus signatures, and witnesses, preferably.

The press room had been designed for victories.

That was its first flaw.

Black Spire's public communications hall sat on the west side of the administrative ring, close enough to the main gates for outside media access and far enough from student dormitories to pretend separation existed. Its backdrop displayed the Spire crest in muted silver. The lighting softened bruises. The podium had two height settings, both optimized for instructors who believed shoulders improved authority. Rows of seats descended toward the front in neat arcs, each with built-in slate ports and recording permissions restricted by event tier.

A room for tournament announcements, donor ceremonies, visiting lecturers, disciplinary reforms, and other occasions where institutions wanted humans to resemble policy.

Roan arrived eleven minutes early and ruined the room's intent by refusing the podium.

"No," he said.

The communications officer blinked. "Mr. Vance, the podium is standard."

"It hides my hand."

"That's generally considered a benefit."

"Not today."

She looked toward Cole.

Cole looked at Roan.

Roan sat at the plain table positioned beside the podium for panel questions and placed his splinted left hand on top of it.

The officer hesitated, then nodded at the technicians. Cameras shifted. Lighting adjusted. The room lost ceremony and gained evidence.

Better.

Drogan stood off to the left, officially security, visually a threat to anyone considering too much enthusiasm. Selene sat in the first row as Ardent witness, posture calm enough to cause diplomatic harm. Iris sat two seats away, representing no formal body but wearing a student finance badge that somehow granted her access. Ines stood by the technical booth and looked personally offended by every camera angle not under her control.

Brendt watched remotely from the medical annex. He had sent a message twenty minutes earlier.

*If you say anything poetic, I will get out of bed and make the healers blame you.*

Roan replied: *Unnecessary threat.*

Brendt: *Necessary for morale.*

The room filled.

Student press first. Then regional martial outlets. Then legal correspondents. Then House observers pretending to be analysts. Then three people with Sentinel Bureau posture and no visible tags. Finally, external feeds opened under controlled delay.

Delay mattered. It gave Black Spire seven seconds to cut the feed if Roan said something catastrophic.

He had no intention of giving them seven seconds.

Cole stepped to the podium.

The room quieted immediately.

"Black Spire has advised Mr. Roan Vance to postpone public comment pending internal review of the Kael Greel matter and associated legal disputes. Mr. Vance has declined that advice. He speaks personally. His words aren't institutional findings unless later confirmed by investigation."

Clean. Protective. Slightly irritated.

Cole stepped aside.

All cameras turned to Roan.

There were several ways to begin.

He had rejected most of them.

Not with *My father was murdered*. Too blunt, too easy to package.

Not with *I demand justice*. Vague words attracted vague allies.

Not with a recounting of childhood grief. True, perhaps, but grief had poor evidentiary value and worse taste in public.

Start with the current record. Rules before blood.

Roan leaned toward the table microphone.

"Yesterday, in the west arena, Kael Greel stated under preserved record that he came to Black Spire to kill me on Vorath Greel's order."

The room stilled. No one had expected him to open by repeating the sentence. That was the point. Repetition fixed shape. Greel wanted debate over admissibility. Roan wanted the original words heavy enough to bend every later answer.

"House Greel contests the validity of that statement. They're entitled to contest it. Black Spire has refused to transfer me to Northern Compact custody for inquiry. Lord Vorath Greel has announced personal attendance in Ironholm."

Second piece. Third piece. Pressure line.

Roan paused long enough for recording systems to mark separation.

"I am making this statement because the attempt to kill me didn't begin with Kael."

Pens moved.

Slates lit.

The room leaned forward.

Good.

"Six years ago, the world was told my father, Aldric Vance, died in a failed Rift operation."

Drogan didn't move. Roan saw him anyway.

"That wasn't the whole truth."

A camera clicked despite silent capture being standard. Someone in the third row whispered and was immediately shushed.

"Six men killed my father."

The sentence left his mouth cleanly.

Not painless. Clean.

Beneath the table, his splinted hand pressed against the chair's edge. Just pressure. Just enough to feel the frame protest.

The Codex Shard pulsed against his spine.

[Lineage exposure event initiated.]
[Witness record active.]
[exposure risk: rising.]

Roan kept his face still. He didn't need to hide the truth. He needed to control when it landed.

"I have their names."

The room changed.

This wasn't silence now. This was intake. An entire audience inhaling around a claim large enough to damage careers.

He continued before they could turn it into theater.

"One of those names is now part of a preserved record through Kael Greel's statement. I am requesting the Aldric Vance case be reopened under neutral review, with all sealed mission files, witness classifications, and recovery records examined by parties not connected to House Greel, Black Spire disciplinary command at the time, or Northern Compact security organs."

Specific. Procedural. Less satisfying than vengeance. More dangerous to bureaucrats.

"If the men who killed him believe I am wrong, they may say so under oath."

There.

The hook.

Not *come fight me*. That made him look reckless.

Not *I will kill you*. That made him easy to condemn.

*Say so under oath.*

Powerful men hated being asked to use the systems they built for smaller people.

The first question came before the communications officer could assign order.

"Mr. Vance, are you accusing Lord Vorath Greel of murdering Aldric Vance?"

Roan looked at the reporter.

"Kael Greel stated he came to kill me on Vorath Greel's order. I am saying the same network reaches back to my father's death."

"That isn't a yes."

"No. It's a statement precise enough to survive contact with lawyers."

A ripple moved through the room. Not laughter exactly. Tension venting through professional masks.

Second question, student press. "Do you have evidence of the six men?"

"Yes."

"Will you release it?"

"No."

The student blinked. "Why not?"

"Because evidence released before the correct people are forced to deny it becomes noise."

Iris's pen moved in the first row. Approving. Stealing the sentence.

A legal correspondent raised a hand. "Are you asking for a criminal investigation, an institutional review, or a civil inquiry?"

"Yes."

The correspondent paused. "Could you clarify?"

"Criminal if the evidence chain survives jurisdiction. Institutional because Black Spire has custody of relevant records and witnesses. Civil because discovery compels documents powerful people prefer to remember inaccurately."

That answer would bore casual viewers and alarm the correct ones.

Good.

A House-aligned analyst stood without waiting. "Mr. Vance, critics may say you are using your father's death to distract from serious allegations that you interfered with a lawful Greel oath. How do you respond?"

There it was. Greel's line, carried by a mouth with plausible independence.

Roan looked at him for two seconds.

"Was the oath disclosed to Black Spire?"

The analyst hesitated. "I wouldn't know."

"Neither would I. That's one of the problems."

"That's not an answer to my question."

"It's the part of your question you hoped no one would notice."

The room stirred.

Roan continued, voice flat. "If Kael carried a lawful command oath capable of compelling assassination, House Greel should produce registration. If it was unlawful, they should explain why they sent it into Black Spire. If it didn't exist, they should explain why asking him one question caused a visible oath mark to destabilize on global feed."

The analyst sat down slowly.

No permanent injury. No direct humiliation for pleasure. Just removal of standing ground.

Acceptable.

A Sentinel-looking woman raised her hand. "Mr. Vance, do you fear retaliation after this statement?"

Roan almost answered no.

That would be stupid. Fear had tactical relevance only when it changed behavior. Denying it made one sound young.

"Yes," he said.

The room seemed surprised.

Good. Truth worked better when unexpected.

"Retaliation is likely. That's why the statement is public and archived, then distributed. If something happens to me, several people will have stronger search incentives."

Drogan's jaw tightened at the edge of vision.

The Sentinel woman asked, "You are using your own safety as leverage?"

"Yes."

No one spoke for half a second.

Roan added, "It's one of the few assets my enemies haven't already tried to spend for me."

That would travel.

He disliked that, but some lines needed legs.

Another reporter asked about his mother.

Roan didn't answer. His fingers brushed the edge of the microphone. Not a gesture. An anchor. He found his center in the silence, then let it lengthen.

Cole stepped forward. "Family questions are out of scope."

Good.

Another asked whether he intended to meet Vorath.

Roan said, "Under conditions that make murder administratively inconvenient."

Ines coughed in the technical booth.

The communications officer looked like she wanted to die.

A young reporter from a regional practitioner outlet rose next. Not House. Not student. Working press, badly paid, jacket sleeves worn near the cuffs. He held his slate with both hands.

"Mr. Vance, your father was known for refusing House sponsorship. Some commentators are already suggesting this is an anti-House political statement. Is it?"

Good question. Not friendly. Useful.

"No."

The reporter waited.

Roan added, because one-word answers could look evasive when the question deserved structure, "House politics helped bury the case. That doesn't make every House guilty. It makes the burial mechanism worth examining."

Selene's posture didn't change, but something in the first row eased.

A woman from a northern outlet stood immediately after. "You say six men killed your father. Are all six associated with the Northern Compact?"

Unknown.

"No answer."

"Does that mean yes?"

"It means no answer."

"Why should the public trust a claim you refuse to substantiate?"

"They shouldn't trust it. They should preserve it, compare it against records, and watch who tries to prevent review."

That one landed differently. Roan saw two legal correspondents look down and write at the same time.

A student reporter lifted a hand, voice tight. "Are students in danger because of this?"

Cole shifted beside the podium.

Roan answered before Cole could cut it.

"Yes."

The room stopped again.

"Students were already in danger," Roan said. "Kael's transfer proved that. Hiding the danger doesn't reduce it. It only changes who gets surprised."

The student's face went pale, but she nodded.

Roan didn't soften the answer. Softening danger made people sloppy. He did, however, add a useful path.

"Black Spire should audit external transfer procedures, oath disclosures, House-sponsored medical waivers, and private training access. Students should ask whether any technique affecting command, compulsion, or bloodline obligation was disclosed before entering shared facilities."

Cole's expression became unreadable.

That recommendation would create administrative work. It would also make Greel's life worse. Acceptable trade.

A martial channel commentator leaned into her microphone. "Mr. Vance, you have repeatedly framed this through records and oaths, plus procedure. Are you personally angry?"

There it was. The emotional hook. Not revenge, but adjacent. A room full of people wanted a boy with a dead father to perform anger in a shape they could consume.

Roan looked at her for long enough that the silence became part of the answer. His pulse thudded against the inside of his throat. He noted it, filed it, and let it pass without permission.

"Yes," he said.

No elaboration.

The commentator waited.

Roan didn't feed her.

Finally she sat down.

Better. Anger acknowledged, not rented out.

Another reporter rose near the back. "If Lord Greel meets you tomorrow, what will you ask him?"

Roan looked directly at that camera for the first time.

"One question."

"What question?"

"If he wanted to answer it in public, he wouldn't have asked for coffee."

The room reacted despite itself.

Not laughter. Not exactly. A low current of recognition, the kind audiences produced when a sentence cut several layers at once.

The communications officer signaled that time was nearly done.

Roan ignored the signal for one final answer.

A gray-haired legal reporter had raised his hand and waited properly through the entire session. Respect for procedure deserved reward.

"Mr. Vance," the man said, "what outcome do you want from reopening the Aldric Vance case?"

That was the only question that mattered.

Roan thought of several answers.

Names. Blood. Records. Vorath on the floor with the Northern Glaive out of reach. Six to go.

None belonged here.

"Corrected record," he said.

The reporter looked surprised. "Only that?"

"No."

"Then why say that first?"

"Because everything after depends on it."

The question phase lasted twelve minutes. Roan answered nine, refused four, redirected three, and gave Greel no sentence longer than necessary. The statement's power lay in its incompleteness. Six men. Names. Oath. Reopen. Under oath.

Every unanswered detail became a room someone else would now search.

At the end, Cole returned to the podium.

"Black Spire will release procedural updates through official channels. This concludes the statement."

Feed delay ended.

Cameras cut.

Noise arrived all at once.

Not in the room. Online.

Roan felt it before anyone showed him the figures. Slates chimed. Message crystals pulsed. Ines swore from the booth in a tone that suggested success had created workload. Iris looked down at her slate and smiled like someone watching a market crash in the correct direction.

Selene stood, walked to the side of the table, and said quietly, "You didn't make him a slogan."

Roan looked at her.

She nodded once toward the room, toward the record, toward whatever door he had just opened.

"That was precise."

"Mostly."

"Don't ruin the compliment."

He accepted the correction.

Drogan approached last.

For a moment, the old man said nothing.

Then he placed one hand on the back of Roan's chair. Not pushing. Not restraining. Just there.

"Your father would have hated that," Drogan said.

Roan waited.

Drogan's mouth twitched painfully. "He would also have stolen three of your lines."

Roan looked down at his splinted hand.

Something in his chest shifted. Not grief exactly. Not relief. A locked mechanism moving one tooth.

"Efficient theft," he said.

Drogan huffed once.

That was enough.

The first hostile response arrived six minutes after the feed ended.

Not from Greel.

From an anonymous legal account claiming Roan's statement constituted defamatory escalation against noble families and calling for disciplinary suspension pending psychological evaluation. The account had been created two hours earlier and followed seventeen northern legal commentators.

Ines flagged it.

The second came from a Greel-aligned martial blog arguing Kael's confession had been induced by "unknown slum techniques."

Iris laughed at that one. "They're using poverty as mysticism now."

The third came from an old Black Squad memorial archive.

That one made Drogan stop moving.

The archive had been dormant for four years. Its profile picture was a black field with seven gray chevrons. It posted one line.

*Aldric Vance didn't die where they said he died.*

No evidence. No explanation. No name.

Within thirty seconds, the post had been copied, mirrored and screenshotted, then attached to every major discussion thread about Roan's statement.

Drogan stared at it.

Cole, who had remained in the press room, read it from the communications officer's slate.

His expression changed.

"Who controls that archive?" Roan asked.

Drogan didn't answer.

Roan looked at him.

"Drogan."

The old man swallowed once.

"Only Black Squad survivors had access."

"How many?"

"Three, after the memorial purge."

"You."

"Yes."

"Who else?"

Drogan's eyes stayed on the post.

"One is dead."

"And the third?"

No answer.

The Codex Shard pulsed.

[Old witness active.]
[Unknown vector entering field.]

Roan looked at the archive post again.

A dormant account. A single sentence. Perfect timing.

Someone had been waiting for him to say it first.

His slate chimed.

New message. Unknown sender. No metadata.

This one contained only six words.

*Stop counting. One of them listens.*

Roan read it twice.

Then he copied it to the evidence folder.

The room, already noisy, seemed suddenly too small.
