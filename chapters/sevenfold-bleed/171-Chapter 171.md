Chapter 171: The Ordinary Transfer

Kael Greel's first public mistake was that he made no mistakes.

Roan attended Professor Ilmere's political history lecture the next morning for reasons that were academically fraudulent and operationally necessary. He didn't belong in the class. His schedule already contained enough theory credits to satisfy Black Spire's administrative appetite, and sitting through ninety minutes of Unification-era treaty failures didn't advance his personal combat growth unless one counted increased resistance to boredom.

Cole had approved the attendance override anyway.

The official reason was cross-disciplinary enrichment.

The actual reason was that Kael had been assigned to Ilmere's lecture block, and Roan wanted to see how the assassin behaved when forced to discuss dead politicians with tired students at eight in the morning.

People revealed themselves under inconvenience.

Grand danger let trained men perform. Minor irritation made them forget which mask they were wearing.

Roan arrived two minutes before start and took a seat in the third row from the back, near the aisle. Not the best tactical position, but the best compromise between visibility, exit access, and appearing uninterested. Selene sat three rows ahead, because her official enrollment in the course provided a better excuse. Ines had tried to enroll under a false name and been rejected by Ilmere in a reply that read only: *No.*

Brendt listened through a contraband audio bead until Roan confiscated the receiving slate and left him with a written transcript delay.

The lecture hall filled slowly. Second-years, a few third-years making up credits, transfers clustered near the side, and House students who thought history was a negotiation with footnotes. Cyrus Ashford entered near the end.

The room noticed.

It tried not to. That made the noticing worse.

Cyrus looked thinner than he had during the tournament. Not physically weak. Vale resources would never allow visible deterioration that could be solved with nutrition and trainers. But something in his face had sharpened around the eyes, and his posture had become too rigid. Pride held up by splints.

He didn't look at Roan.

That was new.

Before the final, Cyrus had looked at Roan like a problem the world had mistakenly placed in his path. After the final, during the few times their routes crossed, he had looked at Roan like a wound with legs. Now he looked past him, jaw set, and took a seat on the far right.

Avoidance.

Not recovery.

Roan marked it.

Kael entered last.

Also deliberate.

Late enough to be seen. Not late enough to be corrected. He wore the Black Spire uniform without alteration, carrying a simple satchel and one old notebook. No entourage. No House colors. No visible Greel marker. He paused at the doorway, scanned for open seats with just enough hesitation to seem uncertain, then chose a place two rows behind Cyrus and one diagonal away.

Not beside him. The placement was deliberate—close enough to read, far enough to deny design.

Near enough to become available. Far enough to avoid intent.

Professor Ilmere shut the door exactly as the hour began.

"Today," she said, "we discuss the Northern Crown Accords and why everyone involved deserved worse lawyers."

Several students looked confused.

Roan almost respected the opening.

Ilmere wrote three words on the board.

*Power. Memory. Paper.*

"Civilizations like to believe wars end when people stop killing each other in large numbers," she said. "This is sentimental rubbish. Wars end when power agrees to be recorded in a way that future power finds convenient. The Northern Crown Accords didn't create peace. They created filing categories for violence."

Roan's attention sharpened.

Selene's shoulders remained still, but her pen stopped.

Kael looked attentive.

Cyrus looked bored with such intensity that it became performance.

Ilmere continued. "Mr. North."

Kael looked up. "Yes, Professor?"

"As our newest northern exchange student, explain the Crown interpretation of provisional adjudication authority."

There it was.

A pressure test, wrapped in lecture.

Kael stood. Not too quickly. "The Crown interpretation holds that provisional adjudication authority allowed temporary consolidation of disputed assets and records, plus personnel following breach events to prevent local retaliation cycles."

The answer was textbook, carefully neutral.

Ilmere's eyes narrowed. "And the non-Crown interpretation?"

"That it allowed the north to steal anything not bolted down and later define the bolts."

The class laughed.

Even Ilmere's mouth twitched.

Kael sat.

He had humanized himself in two moves: the safe answer, then the honest one. Won the room without seeming to try.

Roan disliked him more.

Cyrus glanced back once.

Only once.

Kael didn't notice, or performed not noticing.

Ilmere moved on to legal categories, archive displacement, and the way House-led stabilization committees had transformed emergency custody into generational ownership. It would've been merely interesting before Red Orchard. Now every sentence arrived with teeth.

At the forty-minute mark, Ilmere assigned a debate exercise.

Students were to form groups of three and argue whether emergency record seizure after a Rift bloom constituted preservation or occupation.

Roan didn't move.

A girl from Eastmere asked to join his group, saw his face, and chose life elsewhere.

Selene turned, met his eyes once, and remained with her assigned group. Good. Too much visible coordination would feed Kael.

Cyrus stood alone for six seconds.

Six seconds was longer in a classroom than in combat.

Long enough for people to see. Long enough for him to see them seeing.

Then Kael turned with mild uncertainty.

"Vale, isn't it?" he asked.

Cyrus's face hardened. "What?"

"I need a third. You look like you don't want to be here either."

Several nearby students pretended not to listen.

Cyrus looked at Kael. Then at the empty seat. Then away from Roan, deliberately.

"Fine."

They joined a third student, the mining boy who looked like he still didn't trust ceilings. His name, according to the attendance list, was Merrit Soll.

Roan listened.

Not with Vitalis. Too obvious. With ordinary hearing sharpened by Forged physiology and years spent in rooms where bad men spoke quietly.

Kael let Cyrus speak first. A clean recruitment technique. People who felt invisible mistook attention for respect.

Cyrus said something about record seizure being necessary if local authorities were compromised by Rift influence. It wasn't a bad argument. Angry didn't mean stupid. Kael nodded at the right moments, challenged one minor point, and let Cyrus correct him.

Kael didn't contradict. He expanded the frame, made himself the fair one. Cyrus looked rigid by comparison.

Merrit offered a cautious objection about occupation.

Cyrus dismissed it too quickly.

Kael didn't. He turned to Merrit. "No, that matters. If the authority taking records also controls later review, preservation becomes impossible to distinguish from theft."

Merrit blinked, then nodded.

Cyrus looked annoyed for half a second, then recalibrated because Kael hadn't contradicted him directly. Kael had built himself into the arbiter.

Roan wrote in his notebook:

*Kael social method: validation without subservience. Lets target feel recognized, then models sharper enemy category. Dangerous.*

Ilmere passed behind him and glanced at the page.

Roan closed the notebook.

"Mr. Vance," she said. "Since you have joined us for enrichment, enrich."

The room turned.

Roan considered several answers and chose the one least likely to waste time.

"Emergency record seizure is preservation only if custody and access, plus audit are separated. If one authority takes the record, seals the record, and later cites the sealed record as justification for continued authority, the emergency never ended. It became a business model."

Silence.

Ilmere stared at him for a moment.

Then she wrote *business model* under Power.

"Crude," she said. "Accurate."

Kael looked at him.

Cyrus did too, unwillingly.

Roan didn't look back at either.

Let them decide what that meant.

After class, Professor Ilmere detained him by the ancient academic method of saying his name as he attempted to leave.

"Mr. Vance."

Roan stopped near the door. Students flowed around him, some slowing in the hope of witnessing disciplinary entertainment. Ilmere waited until they realized she might assign written reflections to spectators and fled accordingly.

Selene left without looking back. Correct. Kael also left, speaking to Merrit Soll about the assigned reading with the exact amount of confusion appropriate to a transfer student who had already read it twice. Cyrus left alone. Also expected.

Ilmere stacked her notes. "You aren't enrolled in my course."

"Cross-disciplinary enrichment."

"I have taught here long enough to recognize administrative lies by font."

Roan said nothing.

Ilmere looked over her glasses at him. "If you intend to use my classroom as a surveillance blind, don't insult the subject by learning nothing."

That wasn't the reprimand he had expected.

"I answered your question," he said.

"You answered one question. Students answer questions by accident all the time. Learning is more suspicious."

Roan considered her. Professor Ilmere had no visible combat threat and therefore might be underestimated by people who confused violence with power. The lecture had already shown that she understood record seizure better than half the people involved in hiding it. Cole had placed Kael under her partly because she was harmless in the ways Greel expected and dangerous in the ways institutions forgot.

"What do you know about Northern Crown provisional review files?" he asked.

Her expression didn't change.

Not enough by itself. Good academics controlled their faces around student stupidity. But her hand stopped on the top sheet.

"More than is fashionable," she said.

"NC-14."

Now the pause was real.

Ilmere's eyes sharpened. "That isn't coursework."

"No."

"It's also not a file a student finds in a public library."

"No."

"House Ardent?"

Roan didn't answer.

Ilmere seemed to take that as confirmation, or close enough. She placed the notes into her leather case with precise movements. "Northern Crown files are less a collection than a disease. Once emergency custody became legal authority, every House learned to bury its embarrassments by calling them disputed assets. NC-14 was used for artifact correspondence, contested salvage, and certain categories of person who made committees nervous."

Person.

Roan kept his face still. "Can records be recovered?"

"Records can always be recovered. The question is how many people die embarrassed before admitting they kept copies."

That sounded like something Roan might write down later.

"Who has the best index?" he asked.

"The Crown claims it does. The Houses claim theirs are incomplete. Both are lying. The Spires have fragments because academics steal better than soldiers and file worse than criminals."

"Black Spire?"

Ilmere's gaze moved to the door through which Kael had left. "Perhaps."

That meant yes with conditions.

She snapped the case shut. "I won't hand restricted historical fragments to a student because he asks unpleasantly."

"I can ask pleasantly."

"I doubt that."

"Fair."

"If your interest is connected to Mr. North, tread carefully. Northern families don't send polite boys south because they value cultural exchange."

"I know."

"Yes," Ilmere said. "That's the problem with you. You often do."

She walked past him toward the door, then stopped.

"One more thing, Mr. Vance."

Roan looked at her.

"If a student in my class becomes a corpse because adults are playing old games through children, I will be very difficult."

"Define difficult."

Ilmere smiled thinly. "No."

Roan watched her leave and decided, not for the first time that week, that older women with paperwork were among the more dangerous species in institutional ecosystems.

He added Ilmere to the mental board as useful and watched, and armed with history. A dangerous combination.

Then he went to the training annex.

- break -

Kael's second public performance came in the training annex.

Transfers were required to complete a standard combat placement drill within twenty-four hours of arrival. The drill wasn't a duel. Black Spire used a rotating construct array to test movement, response control, and Vitalis discipline. Students who treated it as a duel usually left embarrassed and occasionally concussed.

Roan watched from the observation gallery with Drogan and Cole.

Selene had chosen not to attend. Correctly. Kael looking at her during arrival had made any repeated proximity too visible. Ines attended because the drill generated useful betting markets among students too stupid to realize the administration could read public odds.

Cyrus attended.

Not required. Not subtle.

He stood at the far side of the gallery, arms folded, eyes on the arena below. No entourage. No friends. Pride in exile.

Kael entered the ring when called.

The construct array activated: six jointed training forms, each blunted and ward-bound, then tuned to low Forged response limits. The test measured economy. Overpowering constructs reduced score. Taking hits reduced score. Excessive output triggered penalties.

A perfect place to hide.

Kael bowed to the examiner.

The drill began.

He moved like a competent student, carefully scrubbing every trace of the weapon underneath.

Step back from first strike, shoulder turn, palm check, low kick to disrupt. Controlled burst to avoid second construct. Half-second hesitation before choosing between two angles, just enough to seem like processing rather than planning. He took one glancing hit to the arm that he could have avoided.

Roan's mouth tightened.

Drogan saw it. "What?"

"He added a mistake."

"Which?"

"Third sequence. The arm hit."

Cole watched the arena. "Students get hit."

"Yes."

Drogan's eyes narrowed. "But not like that."

The hit had landed on the outer forearm, a safe zone. Enough to reduce score. Not enough to threaten function. More importantly, it happened immediately after Kael demonstrated he could read the construct's timing. A normal student made mistakes because perception lagged. Kael had perceived, then chosen to be late.

He wasn't merely hiding strength.

He was curating mediocrity.

The final score appeared.

Upper-mid placement. Unremarkable on purpose.

Polite applause.

Kael looked mildly pleased.

Cyrus watched with contempt that looked a little too hungry.

Roan understood why.

Kael's score was close enough to Cyrus's early second-year metrics to invite comparison. Not better. Not threatening. A peer. Someone Cyrus could approach without feeling beneath him.

This wasn't chance.

After the drill, Kael accepted brief congratulations from the coastal brothers and the Eastmere girl. He spoke to Merrit for thirty seconds, then left the arena alone.

He passed near Cyrus.

Not too near.

Cyrus said, "You held back."

Kael stopped.

The observation gallery noise thinned. Students nearby pretended not to listen.

Kael turned with an easy smile. "Did I?"

"Yes."

"Then I did it badly."

Cyrus's eyes narrowed.

Kael lowered his voice, but Roan still heard. "I was told Black Spire dislikes arrogance."

"They dislike losing control."

Kael's smile faded into something more serious. "Most institutions do."

There it was.

Not agreement with Cyrus directly. Agreement with the wound beneath him.

Cyrus looked away first. "Your footwork is northern."

"Some of it."

"Greel?"

Kael paused just long enough. "My mother's side."

Third lie. Or partial truth.

Cyrus's expression changed.

House blood recognized House blood, especially when both had reasons to pretend otherwise.

"Vale," Kael said, as if remembering. "You were in the tournament final."

Cyrus went still.

A clumsy recruiter would have praised him. A cruel one would have needled him. Kael did neither.

"I watched the earlier rounds," Kael said. "Your semifinal was clean."

Cyrus looked at him.

Not the final. Earlier rounds. Clean. A memory of competence before humiliation.

Cheap poison. Expert dosage.

Cyrus's jaw flexed. "People remember the final."

"People are lazy."

Silence.

Cyrus almost smiled.

Roan updated Kael from dangerous to extremely dangerous.

Drogan muttered, "Damn it."

Cole looked like a man watching a legal disaster introduce itself politely.

Roan said, "We need Lysa moving today."

"Already assigned," Cole said.

"Faster."

Cole took out his slate.

Below, Kael stepped aside, letting Cyrus leave first. It was deference without submission, calibrated to the exact degree Cyrus's pride could absorb.

Cyrus walked away.

Kael watched him for one heartbeat.

Then he looked up at the gallery.

At Roan.

This time, he smiled.

Small. Polite. Ordinary.

The Codex Shard pulsed.

[Northern Crown vector: active hostility likely.]

Roan didn't smile back.
