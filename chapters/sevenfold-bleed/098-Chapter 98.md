Chapter 98: The File in the Clinic

The clinic sat on the Spire’s fourth floor, a narrow room of white tile and chemical quiet. Roan had chosen it for the sightlines—two exits, a window overlooking the central courtyard, and a door that clicked audibly when opened. The automed had already finished its work, sealing a shallow friction burn on his forearm with a strip of synthetic film. The visit was logged as routine maintenance. Clean. Untraceable to the half-dissolved knot of bruises beneath his ribs.

He sat on the edge of the examination table, sleeves rolled down, motionless. The file was folded in his inner coat pocket. Iris Vell. He had read it four times since breakfast. The data was thin: transfer from Aldermere Spire, mid-tier scores, a family background in trade regulation. Nothing that explained why a girl with her credentials had spent the last three weeks orbiting his schedule like a moon testing its tide.

The door clicked.

She entered without knocking, carrying two cups of tea from the commissary kiosk. Her smile was calibrated—warm, but not invasive. Concern, but not pity. She had practiced this.

“They said you were in here.” She set one cup on the counter beside him. “You missed the strategy meeting. Warden Drogan was looking for you.”

“I was here.”

Iris sat in the visitor chair, angling her body toward him. Open. Receptive. The posture of a friend. “You’ve been pushing hard. The qualifiers are in eleven days. You don’t have to do it alone.”

Roan picked up the tea. He didn’t drink it. He let the warmth bleed into his palm while his attention tracked the line of her jaw, the tension in her shoulder. She was wearing a lighter coat than the weather demanded. No concealed rig. No tactical adjustments. She wanted to look unarmed.

“You’re not the first person to offer help this week,” he said.

“I’m not trying to be the first. I’m trying to be the one you take seriously.”

He didn’t respond. The silence expanded. Iris held it longer than most, which meant she had been trained for it. Brendt would have cracked by now. Lyra would have filled the space with jokes. Iris let the quiet sit, letting him feel the weight of her patience.

“I know you don’t trust me,” she said, finally. “I wouldn’t either, if I were you. New transfer, no clear faction, asking too many questions. But I’m not asking because someone told me to. I’m asking because I see what you’re doing, and I want to understand it.”

A perfect lie. Or a dangerous truth. Either way, it was the same move: advance, offer vulnerability, see if the target mirrored it.

Roan set the tea down untouched. “What do you think I’m doing?”

“Surviving.” She leaned forward. “But not just surviving. You’re building something. A position. A leverage point. I don’t know what for. But I know you didn’t learn it from the Spire curriculum.”

Roan met her eyes. “You spend a lot of time watching me, Iris.”

“I spend a lot of time watching people who matter.”

The words hung in the sterile air. A door opening. Or a cage door, depending on which side you were standing on.

Roan stood. His thigh ached faintly, the old wound from the Factor District reminding him it existed. He didn’t show it.

“If you want to understand, keep watching,” he said. “Eventually, you’ll see something you weren’t looking for.”

He walked past her, close enough that his sleeve brushed her arm. A deliberate gap in his guard. A piece of bait.

He felt her eyes on his back as he left the clinic.

The file in his pocket had a new note to add: *Phase two initiated. She’s abandoned the passive approach. The love tactic is live. Countermeasure: let her believe it’s working.*

Roan turned down the corridor, already recalculating. Eleven days to the qualifiers. One spy playing a new game. And a man in the north turning over his family’s foundations, board by board.

The arithmetic was changing.

He was ready.
