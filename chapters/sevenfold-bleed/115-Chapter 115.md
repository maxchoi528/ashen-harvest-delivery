Chapter 115: The First Speech

The morning after Selene Ardent's semifinal loss, Roan found her in the eastern courtyard.

She sat on a stone bench beneath a twisted oak, her twin blades laid across her knees like an offering. The morning light caught the ember-like veins in the steel. The Twin Embers, the Ardent family heirlooms. She wasn't cleaning them. She was staring at them like they'd betrayed her.

Roan considered three approaches. He dismissed two. He had seen the way her gaze tracked Cyrus Ashford during the bracket draw—the same cold arithmetic he recognized in his own mirror. The calculation fit.

He walked to the bench and sat three feet away, close enough to speak quietly, far enough to leave himself an exit. The tally dictated this spacing. Too friendly and she might confide something he didn't want to know. Too distant and she'd dismiss him as just another gawking student.

"The semifinal wasn't your fault."

Selene didn't look up. "The tournament records disagree."

"The tournament records track outcomes. They don't track interference." He let that sit for two beats. "House Ashford's team physician. The one who examined you before your second match."

Her fingers tightened on the nearest blade. The knuckles went white.

Roan had spent the night cross-referencing. The Ardent family physician normally traveled with Selene. For the semifinals, he'd been "delayed." House Ashford had provided a substitute, a man whose credentials were genuine, whose affiliations were less so. The substitute had administered a standard pre-match stimulant.

Standard stimulants didn't cause the tremors Selene had displayed in the third round. Standard stimulants didn't leave metabolite traces that would dissipate within six hours.

"You noticed." Selene's voice was flat.

"I'm good at noticing things that shouldn't be there." Roan leaned back against the cool stone. "The question is what you noticed. Before the tremors started. Before your hands started shaking."

Silence settled. The oak above them creaked once. Somewhere across the courtyard, students ran drills, their shouts carried faintly.

Selene finally looked at him. Her eyes were the color of tarnished copper, and just then, they held something Roan recognized. Not gratitude. Not fear. Something colder.

"That examiner told me the stimulant was a new formula. Faster absorption, reduced crash. I believed him because I wanted to believe him." A muscle in her jaw twitched. "I felt the difference immediately. My meridians were responding wrong. Like my own Vitalis was fighting me."

Roan filed that. *Meridian interference. Targeted disruption, not simple poisoning.* The distinction mattered. Poisoning was crude. Meridian disruption required intimate knowledge of how Ardent family techniques channeled energy.

"Whoever did this knows your family's cycling method."

"Yes."

"That's not public knowledge?"

"The Ardent cycling method has been in our bloodline for four generations. We don't publish manuals." Bitterness crept into her voice. "House Ashford has wanted the Twin Embers technique for thirty years. This is the closest they've come."

Roan processed. House Ashford, the rival house that had been circling Selene's family for decades. The tournament was a perfect cover. A tragic accident, an ambitious young disciple who pushed too hard. No one would question it unless someone looked closely.

And no one looked closely at House Ashford's medical staff unless they already suspected.

"You didn't come here to tell me this."

"No." Roan tilted his head. "I came here to see what you'd do with it. Whether you'd tell your family. Whether you'd withdraw. Whether you'd go after Cyrus Ashford directly."

"And what conclusion did your *math* reach?"

"That you're too smart for the first option, too proud for the second, and too rational for the third." He met her gaze steadily. "Which makes you dangerous. To them. And to me."

Selene set the Twin Embers aside. She folded her hands in her lap with deliberate control. The morning light caught the calluses on her palms.

"You're wondering why I'm telling you this instead of handling it myself."

The directness surprised him. He let it show on his face.

"You're Vance." She said it like the name itself was an explanation. "Your father disappeared six years ago. Your family's resources evaporated within eighteen months. You're here on a scholarship with an equipment stipend that hasn't been updated since you enrolled." Her copper eyes didn't waver. "Someone wants your family gone. And you're still here. That means you're either stupid or patient."

Roan said nothing.

"I'm betting on patient."

The wind picked up. Dead leaves skittered across the courtyard stones. The drills across the way paused—the students had noticed them talking. Let them notice. Gossip was noise. Noise was camouflage.

"House Greel." Roan tested the name. "That's your theory?"

"My theory is that my father would rather let House Ashford take a piece of the Ardent cycling method than admit the tournament was compromised." Something underneath her voice trembled. "My father thinks strategic losses prevent larger wars. He's been buying that theory for thirty years."

"You want something different."

"I want House Ashford to know that their little trick didn't work." She picked up the Twin Embers, one blade in each hand, and held them up to the light. The ember-veins glowed faintly. "The technique they tried to steal—it's mine now. My adaptation. My version. And I'm going to use it to break their flagship fighter's spine in front of everyone who matters."

"Third-place match," Roan said. "Against whoever wins the other semifinal."

"Barring disqualification, yes." The corner of her mouth twisted. "The examiner's report lists the tremors as 'recurring fatigue symptoms.' Not doping. Not sabotage. Just bad luck."

The system was corrupt. Of course it was. The Sentinel Bureau was full of people who'd learned that justice and efficiency rarely shared the same address. But Selene wasn't asking for justice.

She was asking for revenge.

Roan considered the angles. Selene was good—genuinely good. He'd watched her matches. Her technique was fluid, aggressive, technically perfect. The tremors had cost her precision in the semifinal, but without them, she would have advanced.

She was also damaged. Emotionally, not physically. Damaged people made mistakes that careful people didn't.

"You're not asking me to help you."

"I'm asking you to watch." Selene sheathed the Twin Embers and stood. Her stillness came from years of controlling every unnecessary movement. "After everything that's happened in this tournament, I imagine you've developed certain observational skills. If something unusual happens during the third-place match, you'll be paying attention."

"And if I see something?"

"Then we'll talk again."

She walked away without waiting for his response. Her footsteps were silent on the stone. The morning light caught her hair as she rounded the corner and disappeared.

Roan stayed on the bench.

The oak above him creaked again. He counted the seconds between creaks. Wood settling, not wind. The tree had learned to flex under accumulated weight.

Selene Ardent was learning to flex. The question was whether she was flexing toward him or around him.

He pulled out his notebook. The margins were getting full—names, dates, connections drawn in dark ink. Selene's name was near the center of a web that included House Ashford, House Vell, and a half-dozen smaller families he'd flagged as compromised or susceptible.

House Greel was at the center. It always came back to House Greel.

He closed the notebook and put it away. Training in an hour. The third-place match was tomorrow. There were things to prepare.

He walked back toward the Black Spire. The armory was quiet at this hour.

Eckhart was bent over a workbench, his spectacles catching the lamplight as he examined a blade under magnification. The old equipment master had been here since before Roan's enrollment, longer.

"You're early." Eckhart didn't look up. "Training doesn't start for another forty minutes."

"I need information."

"Everyone needs information." He set the blade down and pushed his spectacles up. His eyes were grey, polished, unreadable. "The question is whether you're willing to pay for it."

Roan placed a small vial on the workbench. The liquid inside was amber, faintly luminescent. Hollow Marrow Essence. On the open market, twelve hundred credits.

Eckhart picked it up. Held it to the light. Turned it slowly. "Decent quality. Not military-grade, but decent." He set it down. His expression shifted, the professional mask softening for just a fraction. "Your father once did me a service. This settles part of that debt. Two questions."

Roan's breath caught, but he didn't let it show. "The Ashford family. Specifically, their connections to the Sentinel Bureau."

The silence had weight. Eckhart's eyes stayed on him.

"House Ashford has been a Sentinel Bureau contractor for forty-three years. They provide medical staff, equipment maintenance, and consulting services to the Bureau's combat evaluation divisions. In exchange, the Bureau provides favorable assessments of Ashford fighters during official tournaments. Recruitment advantages. Contract prioritization."

"That's documented?"

"Everything is documented. Not everything is published." He picked up the vial again. "This is enough for two questions."

Roan calculated. Two questions. He'd need to choose carefully.

"The substitute physician who examined Selene Ardent before her semifinal. His real affiliations."

Eckhart's expression didn't change, but something in the quality of his silence shifted. "The man's name is Aldous Venn. He was a junior physician at Ironholm General until three years ago, when he was 'transferred' to the Sentinel Bureau's combat division." A pause. "His transfer was facilitated by a letter of recommendation signed by Marcus Ashford. Head of House Ashford's medical subsidiary."

Roan absorbed that. A planted man. A deliberate operation. House Ashford had compromised the tournament months in advance.

"The second question." Eckhart's grey eyes met his. "Make it count."

"Who recommended Selene Ardent for early admission to the Black Spire?"

The lamp flickered. Outside, footsteps passed.

"Seven years ago, a letter arrived at the Black Spire's admissions office. It contained a full scholarship application for a twelve-year-old girl named Selene Ardent, along with transcripts from a private tutor, combat evaluation scores from the Sentinel Bureau's junior program, and a donation of fifty thousand credits earmarked for discretionary admissions resources." Eckhart set the vial in his pocket. "The letter was unsigned. The donation came through a shell company that traced back to a family foundation."

"Which foundation?"

"The Vance Memorial Education Trust."

The name hit him like cold water.

Aldric Vance had been dead for six years. The foundation should have dissolved when the family's assets were seized. Should have. But foundations didn't always die when their founders did.

Roan's father had set up a scholarship. Years before his death. For a girl Roan had never met until two weeks ago.

"Why?" The word came out flatter than he'd intended.

Eckhart shook his head. "That's your third question. I'm out of vials."

Roan walked out without saying goodbye. The corridor outside was filling with students. He pushed through them without seeing faces.

Aldric Vance had funded Selene Ardent's education. Seven years ago. When Roan was ten and Lyra was six and their father was still alive—still making plans Roan was only now beginning to understand.

The implications arranged themselves like names on a list. Selene was here because Aldric wanted her here. Selene was good—dangerously good—because Aldric had invested in making her so. Selene was an Ardent, which meant House Greel's rival, which meant...

The board was bigger than he'd thought. And his father had been playing on it longer than anyone knew.

He reached the training hall and found Brendt already there, stretching by the weapons rack. His roommate looked up as Roan entered.

"You look like someone walked over your grave."

Roan considered telling him. Considered the weight of the information, the danger of sharing it. Brendt was loyal. But loyalty was a blade that cut both ways.

"Bad dreams," he said. "Nothing important."

Brendt's eyebrow rose. He let it go, but his shoulders held tension. "You've had that look for three days. If you need someone to watch your back—"

"I'll tell you when I do."

They started warming up together. Stretches, strikes, the rhythm of bodies preparing for violence.

The third-place match was tomorrow. Selene was going to fight. And somewhere in the architecture of his father's plans, there was a piece Roan hadn't found yet.

He would find it. The tally didn't care.

But for now, there was training. There were capacities to husband—his system sat at full, untouched. No wounds to reverse today. There were opponents to assess and patterns to predict.

Tomorrow, Selene would face Garrett Holt in the third-place match.

Roan intended to be watching.

The arena was packed for the consolation match. Students packed the stands three deep. Faculty occupied the elevated viewing boxes. The Sentinel Bureau observers were stationed at the corners, their recording crystals gleaming.

Roan found a position near the eastern entrance. Good sightlines, easy exits. He wasn't the only one watching the watchers—a dozen students had positioned themselves in similar spots.

On the arena floor, the preliminary ceremonies concluded. The third-place match was between Selene Ardent and Garrett Holt. Hardened-tier, well-trained, no political significance.

Garrett was going to lose. That wasn't the interesting part.

The interesting part was who was watching Selene *win*.

Cyrus Ashford was seated in the competitor's box, his posture relaxed, his expression mildly interested. Beside him sat two older men in Sentinel Bureau uniforms—not physicians, but evaluators. Combat assessors. Men who wrote reports that determined promotions and family standings.

They were taking notes.

Selene walked onto the arena floor. She moved like water over stone. The Twin Embers gleamed at her hips. Her copper eyes swept the crowd and, for a moment, found Roan's position near the eastern entrance.

She looked away.

The match began.

Garrett came in aggressive, his Ironheart technique cycling through its opening sequence. He pressed forward with low strikes, testing Selene's guard.

She yielded. Let him advance. Let him build momentum.

Roan watched her Vitalis. It cycled wrong—he could see the stutter in her meridian flow left by the stimulant. Her body was compensating in real time, redirecting energy through pathways that weren't optimal. She was fighting injured.

And she was still winning.

Selene's bladework shifted in the third exchange. The Twin Embers blazed—not fire, but heat shimmering the air. Garrett stumbled back.

*New technique. Her adaptation.*

The crowd roared. Selene pressed forward. The compensations were gone. The stutter had smoothed out. She was running a modified cycling method that circumvented the damage.

Roan's breath caught. The energy patterns in her strikes—the way her Vitalis compressed before release—it wasn't Sevenfold Bleed. Not Inversion. But it reminded him of something from his father's notes: a Filter technique. Temporary energy isolation from damaged pathways. An echo of a method Aldric had described but never fully written down.

*She's using something he touched.*

The Twin Embers carved a pattern through the air. Garrett's guard shattered. He hit the arena floor hard.

"One point. Ardent."

Selene stood over her fallen opponent, barely winded, her copper eyes fixed on the competitor's box.

On Cyrus Ashford.

On the Sentinel evaluators who had stopped taking notes.

The second exchange was shorter. Garrett came in desperate. Selene met him with blazing blades and carved through his defenses.

Two points. Match to Selene Ardent.

The crowd erupted. But Roan wasn't watching the celebration. He was watching Selene's face—the set of her jaw, the way her eyes stayed locked on the evaluator's box.

She'd done exactly what she'd promised. She'd proven the sabotage didn't work. She'd shown them her adaptation.

And she'd done it using a technique that shouldn't have existed outside his father's locked chest—now running in the body of a seventeen-year-old girl who'd been a scholarship student for seven years.

Selene Ardent walked off the arena floor without looking at Roan. She didn't need to. Her message was written in every deliberate step.

*I know what I am. What do you know about what you are?*

The stands were still roaring. The evaluators conferred in urgent whispers. Cyrus Ashford's face had gone tight, his jaw working like he was chewing glass.

Roan stood in the eastern entrance and watched it all unfold.

His father had funded this girl. His father's techniques were running through her meridians. And somewhere in the architecture of the Vance legacy, there was a connection he was only beginning to see.

Brendt appeared at his elbow. "Hell of a match."

"Hell of a match," Roan agreed.

"You look like you're calculating something."

"Always." He turned away. "Come on. We've got training in twenty minutes."

They walked together toward the Black Spire's interior. Behind them, the crowd noise faded into a distant roar. Ahead, the corridor stretched into shadow.

As they passed the junction near the east arch, Roan caught a flicker of movement—a green-cloaked figure ducking into a side corridor, recording crystal still clutched in one hand. The man didn't look back. But the image stuck: a watcher who had been watching the watchers.

The third-place match was over. The final was tomorrow.

And the board was still expanding.
