Chapter 173: Three Boards

By morning, the training room incident had already become five different stories.

In the first, Cyrus Ashford had challenged Kael Greel and lost so badly that he had begged for instruction. In the second, Kael had insulted Roan Vance in absentia and Cyrus had laughed, which told Roan more about the storyteller's imagination than the event. In the third, Roan had been hiding in the walls like some academically motivated ghost, watching them both. That version was closest to the truth and therefore spread slowest. The fourth story claimed Selene Ardent had been present, which was correct, but only because everyone at Black Spire had decided Selene's name improved any rumor involving violence, old money, or romantic misinterpretation.

The fifth version said Iris Vell had arranged the meeting.

That one worried Roan.

Not because it was true. It almost certainly wasn't. Iris liked leverage, but her current position didn't favor clean campus manipulation. Since the Rift exit incident, she had moved through Ironholm like someone changing safehouses by instinct rather than plan. Her warnings arrived through flower shops, corrupted receipts, and one frankly insulting bakery account. She wasn't stable enough to build Kael's social web, and not suicidal enough to hand Cyrus directly to Vorath's grandson.

But the rumor existed.

Rumors didn't need accuracy to be useful. They exposed what people thought possible. If the student body could imagine Iris, Cyrus and Kael, plus Roan in the same conflict diagram without laughing, then the campus had already accepted the outline of a war it didn't understand.

Roan didn't like wars that advertised themselves before he controlled the terms.

He sat in the back row of Applied Rift Ecology with a clean notebook, a dull pencil, and Sidewound hidden beneath his jacket where the chair back wouldn't press against it. Professor Hainley stood at the front explaining predatory mimicry among low-tier Rift fauna, which was the first lecture topic in weeks that had arrived with decent timing.

"Classical mimicry models assume prey response," Hainley said, tapping a diagram of a pale lizard with too many eyes. "But higher Rift entities often mimic social signals. Names. Authority. Vulnerability. Familiar grief. Anything that causes hesitation."

Roan wrote: *Mimicry succeeds by selecting response, not appearance.*

Then, below it: *Kael mimics correction.*

Cyrus sat four rows ahead and three seats left.

He had come to class.

That was new. After the tournament, Cyrus had missed lectures with the precise discipline of someone too humiliated to perform normal life but too proud to vanish completely. He had drifted through hallways in a cold shell, flanked by former allies who didn't know whether proximity remained profitable. Now he sat upright, notes open, jaw set. But present, not healed or calm.

Kael Greel sat beside him.

The placement was either bold or lazy. Roan suspected boldness wearing laziness as a coat. Kael had chosen a seat public enough to prevent accusations of secret influence and close enough to Cyrus to make every witness register association. He took notes with his left hand, posture relaxed, attention apparently on the lecture.

Apparently.

Three times in twenty minutes, Kael's pencil paused half a heartbeat before Cyrus wrote down a concept. Not looking. Not instructing. Setting rhythm. Cyrus unconsciously matched it by the second repetition.

Small habits were dangerous. Grand declarations could be rejected. Habits became scaffolding before pride noticed.

Brendt leaned over from Roan's right. His injured shoulder had improved enough for light classes and bad decisions, which he considered equivalent categories.

"Is it illegal to stab someone for note-taking?" he whispered.

"Yes."

"Morally?"

"Depends on handwriting."

Brendt glanced at Kael's notebook. "Unfortunately clean."

"Then no."

"Shame."

Professor Hainley turned sharply. "Mr. Holloway, if you have a contribution?"

Brendt smiled with the pained dignity of a man who had survived a Tier Three Rift but not classroom exposure. "Only that hesitation remains the most dangerous opening in mimic encounters, Professor."

Hainley blinked, surprised into approval. "Correct. Especially when the mimic presents a target the victim feels responsible for."

Brendt's smile faded by a fraction.

Roan didn't look at him.

Hainley continued. "Protection impulse is among the easiest behaviors to exploit. The response is predictable, often faster than conscious analysis."

Kael's pencil moved.

Cyrus wrote the same line three seconds later.

Roan underlined his own note once.

*Third hand: Iris remnants.*

The board had three active forces now, though only one had shown its face cleanly.

Force one: Roan's group. Brendt, recovering but mobile. Selene, politically useful and still deciding how much family treason she considered recreational. Ines, unofficial and expensive. Drogan, who remained an instructor only because Black Spire hadn't found a word for armed institutional haunting. Cole, not an ally in the friendly sense, but a man with enough old guilt to stand in the right doorway at the right time.

Force two: Kael and Cyrus. Kael as hand, Cyrus as handle. Publicly harmless. Socially poisonous. Tactically developing.

Force three: Iris remnants. Not Iris herself, necessarily. Vell accounts, Greel contractors who had survived Valech, students once fit House Vell's influence network, minor families trying to guess which larger predator would win. Remnants were often more dangerous than organized factions. They lacked leadership, which meant they compensated with enthusiasm.

Roan had three competing threats and one school week full of mandatory examinations.

Black Spire had poor scheduling ethics.

- break -

The first test of the day wasn't academic.

It came in the corridor outside Rift Ecology, where a third-year from House Merrow stepped into Roan's path with two friends behind him and a smile designed by someone who had confused confidence with dental exposure.

"Vance," Merrow said. "Heard you've been calling yourself an explorer now."

Roan stopped.

Brendt stopped with him. That mattered. A month ago Brendt might have stepped forward or made a joke to diffuse the pressure. Now he angled his body so his injured shoulder was away from the strangers and his good hand remained free. Progress.

Selene, approaching from the opposite side with a folder under one arm, slowed but didn't intervene.

Kael and Cyrus emerged from the classroom behind them.

Excellent. Audience selected by providence or planning. Roan disliked both.

"I don't call myself anything," Roan said. "Other people are inefficient enough to require labels."

Merrow's smile flickered. "Then let me label you. Rift lucky. Tournament lucky. Greycross lucky. Lot of luck for one orphan."

The corridor quieted.

Not fully. Black Spire students had survival instincts, but gossip instincts were faster. Conversations died by radius. Phones didn't rise. Too obvious. But reflection charms activated in cuffs, earrings, belt buckles. Rich children had turned cowardice into jewelry.

Brendt inhaled once.

Roan's left hand moved two centimeters, not toward Sidewound but toward Brendt's wrist. A small contact. Restraint disguised as accident.

Merrow wanted a reaction. Not necessarily violence. Violence would help if Roan supplied it, but the insult's structure aimed wider. Father unknown, mother poor, sister vulnerable, Greycross origin, throw enough dirt and see which stain he protected first.

Crude.

Crude didn't mean harmless.

Roan looked past Merrow to Kael.

Kael watched without expression.

Cyrus watched Roan.

There it was. The real audience wasn't the corridor. It was Cyrus. Merrow was either stupid enough to start this on his own or encouraged by someone who wanted to see how Roan handled public provocation after Kael's first lesson.

Roan chose the least satisfying response.

He stepped aside.

Merrow blinked.

"Sorry," Roan said. "Were you blocking the hall deliberately? I assumed architectural failure."

A few students laughed.

Not loudly. Enough.

Merrow's face tightened. "You think you're clever?"

"No. That would be an emotional relationship with evidence. I have evidence."

More laughter.

Roan kept his eyes bored. Boredom was more insulting than anger and cheaper than violence.

Merrow's friend on the left shifted his weight. Aggression building. Roan marked his stance: amateur boxer base, reinforced knuckles, likely Tempered midline, proud of shoulders. Friend on the right: nervous, hand near charm bracelet, recording more than fighting. Merrow himself: Hardened, but soft around the decision making.

Kael took one step forward.

Not enough to intervene. Enough for Cyrus to see him as potential restraint.

Roan almost admired it.

Merrow said, "Maybe Greycross boys need reminders where they stand."

Roan reached into his notebook, tore out a blank page, and held it out.

Merrow stared. "What is that?"

"A list."

"It's blank."

"Yes."

"Of what?"

"People whose opinion changed my standing."

The laughter this time was sharper.

Merrow flushed.

Good. Not too far. Public humiliation beyond recovery created reckless retaliation. Roan needed him angry enough to expose the chain but not angry enough to force security intervention.

"Touch me," Roan said, mild as rain, "and Black Spire disciplinary law classifies it as assault on a sanctioned Rift returnee under medical restriction protocols. Touch Brendt and I stop being polite before security arrives. Touch neither, and you can pretend this was banter."

Merrow looked at Brendt's shoulder.

Roan's voice dropped. "Choose carefully."

The corridor tightened around them.

Merrow's mistake was looking sideways.

Not at Kael. At a fourth-year near the window alcove, a girl wearing a silver pin shaped like a folded fan. Vell style. Not Iris's close circle. Too minor. But the look carried query and resentment.

Force three had placed a finger on the board.

The girl's face closed when she saw Roan notice.

Merrow stepped back. "Not worth it."

"Correct."

Roan walked past him.

Brendt followed, quiet until they turned the corner.

Then he muttered, "Blank list was rude."

"Accurate."

"Still rude."

Selene fell into step on Roan's left. "The silver fan pin belongs to Vell auxiliary patronage. Minor scholarship circle. Iris used them for errands when she didn't want family channels logged."

"Name?" Roan asked.

"Marra Sile."

"Useful?"

"Ambitious, underfunded, frightened of choosing late."

"Then she will choose loudly soon."

Brendt looked back down the corridor. "Merrow?"

"Bait."

"Kael?"

"Observer."

"Cyrus?"

"Student."

Brendt grimaced. "That last one is the part I hate."

Roan agreed.

He didn't say so.

- break -

Lunch produced the second move.

Black Spire's central refectory had been designed by someone who believed students ate better under surveillance. Tiered seating circled a sunken central floor where official announcements, club recruitment pitches, and occasional sanctioned disputes could be staged without moving the furniture. Glass walls looked out over the west training lawns. Wards hummed in the ceiling, subtle enough for most students to ignore and strong enough to drop a Crowned-level suppression net if someone made the dining period memorable.

Roan took a table with Brendt and Selene near the outer ring.

Not his usual corner.

Corners were good for defense but poor for observation when the room itself became the event. Today he wanted sightlines across three entrances, the central floor, Cyrus's preferred table, and the south alcove where Vell auxiliary students gathered when pretending not to be a faction.

Ines appeared six minutes later with a tray she hadn't paid for and sat down without invitation.

"Your admirer from House Merrow is telling people you threatened him with legal language," she said.

"I did."

"He sounds offended."

"He should improve his risk assessment."

Ines stole one of Brendt's potatoes. Brendt looked at her hand, then at his plate, then apparently decided today's battles should remain metaphorical.

Selene opened a thin folder beneath the table. "Marra Sile received three payments last week through a shell scholarship fund tied to Vell accounts. Small amounts. Not enough for mercenary work. Enough for rumor distribution, errands, access purchases."

"Access to what?" Roan asked.

"Exam schedules, medical wing visitor logs, training room reservations."

Brendt's expression changed. "Medical wing?"

"Your visitor logs were requested twice," Selene said. "Denied both times. Cole's office flagged them."

Roan added Cole to the list of people being temporarily useful.

Ines leaned back. "So Iris remnants are looking for your recovery windows, Kael is building Cyrus, and someone is testing your public temper. Busy morning."

"It's almost flattering," Brendt said.

"No," Roan said.

"I said almost."

"You're injured. Their interest is logistical."

"That was my least favorite interpretation."

"It was the accurate one."

A hush moved through the refectory.

Kael Greel had entered with Cyrus Ashford.

They didn't sit at Cyrus's old table. Smart. Cyrus's old table carried too much defeat. They chose a middle ring table with enough visibility to look unafraid and enough distance from Roan to avoid direct challenge. Kael set his tray down first, but not at the head. He left the head position to Cyrus.

Cyrus hesitated only once before taking it.

Roan watched the room watch him.

Status didn't return all at once. It accumulated in permissions. Cyrus sitting at the head again, even of a new table, gave other students permission to imagine his recovery. Kael not taking the seat gave Cyrus permission to believe the recovery belonged to him.

It was well done.

Too well done for a simple assassin.

"Kael is patient," Selene murmured.

"Yes."

"Vorath isn't famous for patience."

"No."

Brendt looked between them. "Meaning?"

"Meaning Kael may have his own preferred method," Roan said.

Ines's eyes sharpened. "Or his own agenda."

"Both possible."

Across the refectory, Kael looked up.

Their eyes met.

For one heartbeat, the room's noise thinned.

Kael smiled politely.

Roan didn't.

Then Marra Sile stood from the Vell alcove and walked toward the central floor.

She held a small envelope.

That was too theatrical for Iris. Too public for simple Vell remnants. Too early for Kael if he had planned the sequence cleanly.

A third hand, then. Or a clumsy one pretending to be third.

Marra reached the central floor and spoke loudly enough for the refectory acoustics to carry her voice.

"Roan Vance."

Brendt closed his eyes. "Why do people keep saying your name like a curse at meals?"

"Marketing," Ines said.

Roan stood.

Not moving would concede stage control. Moving too far would enter hers. He remained at the table, one hand resting on the chair back.

Marra lifted the envelope. "A message from those who remember what Iris Vell forgot."

Interesting phrasing.

Not House Vell. Not Iris. Those who remember. Splinter faction or borrowed slogan.

"Send it by post," Roan said.

She flushed. "Coward."

Weak.

The insult had no teeth because she didn't believe it. She needed him to approach the envelope. The envelope mattered.

Roan looked at Ines.

Ines's gaze was already on the paper. "Charm-sealed. Image burst. Maybe toxin dust. Maybe both. Cheap work."

Marra's face betrayed the toxin.

Roan sighed.

He didn't want to spend lunch on amateur poisoning.

"Professor Hainley," he said without looking away from Marra.

The professor, three tables over, went still.

"Would you classify a toxin-sealed message delivered in a refectory as social mimicry, predatory baiting, or academic misconduct?"

The room turned.

Hainley adjusted his spectacles. He looked annoyed, which meant he understood the assignment. "All three, Mr. Vance. The third carries immediate disciplinary referral."

Marra's hand tightened around the envelope.

The paper cracked.

A faint green powder hissed out along the seam.

The ceiling wards flared.

No one moved.

Roan sat back down.

"Third option," he said.

Security arrived in twelve seconds.

Kael watched the entire exchange with mild interest. Cyrus looked unsettled, not satisfied.

Good.

Roan picked up his fork.

Brendt stared at him. "You're still eating?"

"The potatoes are already compromised by Ines, not toxin."

"Different kind of danger," Ines said.

Roan ate.

Across the room, Marra Sile was escorted out, pale and shaking, still holding the envelope as a containment charm wrapped her wrist.

The Vell remnants had exposed a courier and confirmed desperation.

Kael had watched Roan refuse the bait.

Cyrus had watched Roan win without standing up.

One move. Three audiences. Uneven returns.

Acceptable.

- break -

The third move came at night.

Not an attack. An invitation.

A folded note appeared under Roan's dorm door at 11:43 p.m. Which immediately made it suspect because anyone competent knew his door threshold was marked.

The note stopped two centimeters past the inner ward line and began smoking.

Brendt, seated on his bed with one arm in a medical sling and a textbook open upside down in his lap, looked at it.

"Should I ask why our floor is eating mail?"

"No."

"Good. I didn't want to know."

Roan lifted the note with tongs and dropped it into a glass tray. The outer fold had charred, but the inner page remained intact. Good paper. Expensive. Scentless. No visible powder. No charm activation after ward burn.

He opened it.

One line.

*He isn't here for Cyrus.*

No signature.

Brendt read over his shoulder. "Iris?"

"Possibly."

"The flower shop had more personality."

"Yes."

"So not Iris?"

"Or Iris under pressure. Or someone imitating her badly. Or someone imitating her badly so I conclude it isn't her."

Brendt shut his textbook. "I preferred when threats wore knives."

"Knives are honest."

"You say that like a man sleeping beside three of them."

"Four."

"Of course."

Roan placed the note beside the flower receipt under separate glass.

He isn't here for Cyrus.

Obvious, on the surface. Kael had been sent for Roan. But the sentence's value lay in what it chose not to say. Not: Kael is here for you. Not: Greel sent him. Not: beware Cyrus. The wording positioned Cyrus as decoy, not tool.

If true, Kael's work on Cyrus might be cover for another objective.

If false, the note wanted Roan to dismiss Cyrus's danger.

Either way, Cyrus remained on the board.

The ward under the door clicked again, softer this time. Not another note. Residue. The delivery charm had carried a second function and failed to survive crossing the threshold. Roan crouched beside the blackened line and scraped a fleck of ash into a sample tube. Blue-white grains clung to the glass. Cold signature. Northern. Too faint to prove Greel. Strong enough to make denial expensive later if matched to another source.

Brendt watched from the bed. "Please tell me you aren't collecting cursed door dust."

"Evidence."

"That's what people say before cursed door dust becomes our Tuesday."

Roan sealed the tube. "It's already Tuesday."

"I miss ignorance."

"No, you don't."

Brendt looked at the note again, humor draining. "If he isn't here for Cyrus, then Cyrus is a distraction."

"Or a trigger."

"For you?"

"For campus. For public opinion. For Kael's access. For whatever Vorath wants measured."

Brendt flexed the fingers of his good hand. The medical brace along his injured shoulder hummed faintly as it corrected a movement he hadn't realized he was making. "You know the worst part?"

"There are several candidates."

"Cyrus looks better."

Roan didn't answer.

Brendt leaned back against the wall. "I hated him after the tournament. Not nobly. I just did. He looked at you like you were dirt that had learned to talk, and he looked at me like I was furniture you had dragged in from a bad neighborhood. Then he broke in public and I thought good. He earned that. But today..."

"Today he looked like someone being repaired."

"Yes." Brendt rubbed his eyes. "That makes it harder."

"No. It makes it clearer."

"How?"

"Breaking him was easy. Repairing him in the wrong direction is the threat."

Brendt was quiet. "You are going to let Kael keep teaching him."

"Until stopping it helps."

"That sounds cruel."

"It's controlled."

"Not a denial."

Roan looked at Cyrus's name on the board he had begun building across the dorm wall. There were no strings yet. Strings were for people who wanted their conclusions to look inevitable. He used columns, dates and incentives, plus empty spaces. Empty spaces were more honest.

"Cruelty is pain for appetite," Roan said. "This is waiting for evidence."

Brendt studied him. "You hear yourself, right?"

"Unfortunately."

That earned a weak laugh. Good. Weak was still movement.

Roan wrote three columns.

Kael objective: kill Roan.

Kael secondary objective: identify Codex capabilities.

Kael hidden objective: provoke legal exposure? Isolate allies? Test campus wards? Access Aldric-linked records?

He stared at the last phrase.

Access records.

Kael didn't need Cyrus for that.

But he might need campus legitimacy, student proximity, and a socially acceptable reason to approach restricted spaces without looking like an infiltrator.

Cyrus gave him status cover. Vell remnants gave noise. Roan gave target fixation.

A clean assassin used distractions.

A family envoy collected intelligence.

Brendt said nothing for a while. Then, quietly, "You think he wants something before he kills you."

"Yes."

"What?"

Roan touched the Vance Pendant through his shirt.

The disk remained cool.

"Something my father left behind," he said.

Brendt's face changed. Not fear exactly. Brendt had learned fear and kept moving. This was anger arriving slowly because it had to pass through pain first.

"Then we don't let him have it."

"No."

Roan looked at the note again.

Three hands. Kael. Iris remnants. Unknown sender, Iris, someone using her shadow.

The board was getting crowded.

Crowded boards favored people willing to remove pieces.

Roan slept two hours that night.

It was enough to dream of a clean white cup in a dead city and wake with his hand on Sidewound.
