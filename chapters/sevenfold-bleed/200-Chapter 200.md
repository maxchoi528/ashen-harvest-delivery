Chapter 200: Six to Go

Black Spire's roof wasn't meant for students.

That was the first thing Roan liked about it.

The official access door sat behind three locked service corridors, two maintenance lifts, one pressure seal, and a polite warning plaque explaining that unauthorized roof entry would result in disciplinary review, expulsion proceedings, and potential civil liability in the event of death, dismemberment, wind shear, falling debris, ward exposure, or emotional distress experienced by witnesses.

The plaque had been written by a committee.

Roan bypassed it in twelve seconds using the temporary access token Cole had given him, a maintenance override Ines had once sold him for reasons she had called hypothetical, and a folded strip of wire Brendt had insisted wasn't technically lockpicking if the lock was already badly designed.

Before opening the final service door, he built three contingencies.

First, a delayed message to Drogan set to trigger if Roan didn't cancel it within ten minutes. The message contained the roof access route, the phrase *memory remnant*, and the instruction *don't let Cole convene a committee before checking for blood*. Drogan would understand the priority order and complain about it later, assuming later remained available.

Second, a slim ward tag from Brendt's repair kit went under the latch. It wouldn't stop anyone serious. It would tell Roan if someone opened the door while he was occupied. Most security devices failed because people wanted them to solve danger. Roan preferred devices that identified which danger had arrived.

Third, he drew a narrow chalk line around his boots once he reached the threshold, then scratched a gap in it with Sidewound's sheathed tip. Not a ritual. A measurement marker. If memory pressure moved his body without permission, he wanted proof. If something else moved his body, he wanted a direction.

Paranoia was a word used by people who had never watched a museum portrait find their sister.

The door opened into weather.

Winter struck him first. Cold air, sharp enough to cut through coat seams. Then wind. Then height.

Ironholm spread below in layered light: black towers, blue ward-beams, rail lines glowing like heated wire, district grids fading into industrial smoke and river fog. To the east, the sealed municipal Rift entrances pulsed faintly beneath their containment pylons. To the north, old terraces climbed toward the dark line of mountains beyond the city, where Suthmere waited behind a note, a door, and whatever Kaelen had decided not to explain.

Above, the clouds moved low and fast.

The roof surface was wet with melted snow. Puddles gathered around drainage grooves and the bases of antenna spines. Roan's boots sank ankle-deep in a shallow drift near the parapet before reaching bare concrete.

For a moment, the position struck him as familiar.

Not memory.

Shape.

A high place. Cold underfoot. City below. A list of names ahead. Something dead behind him, or not dead enough.

In the prologue he hadn't yet lived, blood had reached his ankles.

Here it was only snowmelt.

Progress, perhaps.

He walked to the edge and looked down.

Black Spire dropped beneath him in tiered terraces and glass faces, every level holding people who believed the institution was a shield, a ladder, a prison, or a marketplace depending on tuition bracket and personal trauma. The tournament banners had been removed. Kael Greel's duel clips hadn't. Somewhere below, students were still arguing online about whether Roan sparing Kael counted as mercy, insult, strategic brilliance, or proof that Vance was secretly building a revenge cult.

The fourth theory had disappointing evidence but strong engagement metrics.

Roan had not corrected it.

Reputation was a tool. Misunderstood reputation was often a better one.

He checked the phone.

Lyra: safe room. No mirrors. Mara angry at furniture.

Mira: Cole's office called. Are you hurt? Answer me in complete sentences.

Brendt: if you are doing something ominous on a roof, at least don't fall off before breakfast.

Selene: Ardent records copied. Sirenne's office has gone quiet. That's worse than loud.

Drogan: Don't make me climb stairs.

Roan answered Lyra first.

*Good. Keep Mara angry. Angry competent people notice details.*

To Mira: *I am physically intact. Lyra is protected. I will call after sunrise.*

He considered whether that counted as complete sentences. It did. Barely.

To Brendt: *Falling would be inefficient.*

To Selene: *Quiet means she's choosing venue. Track exits, not statements.*

To Drogan: *Use lift.*

Drogan replied almost immediately: *Brat.*

Roan put the phone away.

The Vance Pendant rested cold against his chest. Sidewound lay across the small of his back. The Codex Shard had been quiet since the cemetery, which meant it was either stable, damaged, or waiting to be dramatic.

The last option had become more likely recently.

He didn't choose the roof because it was dramatic.

Drama was a side effect other people confused for significance. The roof had practical advantages. Fewer reflective surfaces. Fewer witnesses. Clear horizon lines. Stronger sky-ward dissipation if the Shard released excess pressure. Most importantly, the Spire's upper ward lattice was designed to repel external probes rather than monitor internal student behavior. Cole trusted doors and offices. The roof trusted wind.

Wind had fewer conflicts of interest.

There was also the matter of memory geometry. The Shard had responded to elevation twice before: once during the tournament aftermath, when Vorath's pressure made the air feel indexed; once at the hidden bakery door, where Kaelen's old mechanism tied Ironholm's ward lattice to something buried below. Aldric's civilization seemed fond of vertical symbols, spires, mountains, cairns, thresholds, wounds running through layers.

If that was superstition, it was testable superstition. Testable superstition was just science with worse branding.

He recorded the hypothesis in a waterproof page torn from his field notebook and wedged it under the maintenance light. If he forgot writing it, that would be data. If the page moved, that would also be data. If the page vanished, he would owe the roof an apology and violence.

He stood facing the city and opened his Vitalis circulation one layer at a time.

Not enough to flare. Not enough to alert the masked hunt field still buried in Ironholm's ward lattice, though Kaelen's bakery-door mechanism had reduced it to a dormant echo for now. Just enough to let the Shard know he was listening.

The wind pulled at his coat.

Nothing happened.

The first thirty seconds produced nothing except cold fingers and the growing suspicion that the Shard enjoyed making him stand in weather.

He changed variables one at a time.

Vance Pendant uncovered: no response.

Sidewound drawn two inches from its sheath: faint pressure without prompt. Aldric's cairn phrase repeated internally: a single pulse, then silence.

The six suspected names written in his notebook and held open to the wind: warmth along the spine without activation. Roan paused before the blank seventh line. The pen hovered over it. He didn't write a question mark. A question mark was still a mark. The Shard had warned against external inscription, and if old systems treated symbols as handles, then leaving the blank untouched was the only clean test.

The moment he closed the notebook on the blank line, the roof lights flickered.

For comparison, he lowered Vitalis output to civilian-adjacent levels and waited again. The Shard cooled. The roof became only a roof: wet concrete, wind noise, distant traffic, cold fingers. No pressure. No remnant.

Then he raised output to ordinary Forged circulation. Warmth returned, but scattered, like a receiver catching static. Not enough.

He added the Vance Pendant by pressing two fingers against it through his shirt. The warmth narrowed.

That was the missing variable. Not power. Anchoring.

Useful, and irritatingly consistent with every lesson he hadn't asked his father to leave. Strength opened doors. Identity decided whether the door led somewhere survivable.

Good.

Not safe. Good.

Roan waited.

Patience wasn't passivity. Patience was applied violence against bad timing.

He reviewed the ledgers.

Vitalis: Forged, stable around ten thousand when measured conservatively. Peak output higher under Inversion and Sevenfold cycling, but every peak now increased recognition risk. Sidewound: functional, partially synchronized, still dangerous under overfeed. Vance Pendant: signal reduction useful but insufficient against direct Throne-tier attention. Codex Shard: cracked, stabilizing after inheritance refusal, sleep quota reduced to two hours, recovery pathways gated by Suthmere. Brendt: recovering abnormally, possible low grade resonance exposure. Lyra: tagged by a portrait, protected but now on the enemy's conceptual map. Mira: civilian shield requested, not enough. Selene: useful, exposed to Sirenne's notice. Drogan: withholding less, still unreliable around Aldric's ghost. Cole: protective asset with institutional leverage. Kaelen: unknown category, likely necessary. Vorath: marked, unresolved, politically damaged, personally hostile.

Enemies known: Vorath Greel. Maddox Vell. Lady Sirenne. House-level collaborators. Unknowns connected to Hesh, Vespa, Krell if the fragments in Aldric's memory meant what they seemed to mean. Seventh variable unresolved.

Objective: survive long enough to identify all six, kill or ruin in optimal order, prevent family collateral, access Suthmere, understand what the seventh writes himself means before it writes in blood.

Reasonable agenda.

For a lunatic.

The Codex Shard pulsed.

Once.

Then again.

The roof lights flickered.

Roan didn't move.

[Elevation threshold met.]

[Debt ledger stabilized.]

[Inheritance refusal recorded.]

[Host identity anchor: partial.]

[Memory remnant access: available.]

There it was.

Roan exhaled slowly. "Define pressure."

The prompt waited, which was rude.

[Pressure: memory access intensity.]

[Risk: increased recognition.]

[Cost: emotional contamination likely.]

Roan stared at the last line.

"Likely?"

[Confidence: 94%.]

"Pedantic thing."

The Shard didn't defend itself.

He considered refusing.

There were good reasons. Memory pressure could cause gaps. Recognition risk could alert Vorath's damaged hunt field, Sirenne's remote watchers, or any old world mechanism still listening through names and height. Emotional contamination was vague, which made it suspicious. Aldric's previous remnant had managed thirteen words and left Roan sitting on a dorm floor for three hours. Another appearance could destabilize judgment.

But delaying came with its own cost.

Vorath had moved through portraits. Lyra had been tagged. Suthmere waited. Sirenne was quiet. Kaelen had intervened once and vanished. The Shard had just offered a memory remnant after refusing unknown inheritance terms, which suggested the system respected boundaries only if they were stated before accepting power.

That rule mattered.

Test rule. Accept limited access. Refuse authority. Observe reward. Track cost.

Roan placed both hands on the parapet.

"Limited access," he said. "No memory overwrite. No unconsciousness. No external beacon beyond current risk. End on my command."

The Shard pulsed.

[Terms acknowledged.]

[External beacon limitation: partial compliance only.]

Honest, at least.

"Clarify."

[Memory remnant activation cannot be fully hidden from entities with prior direct authority contact.]

Vorath. Sirenne. Perhaps others.

"Duration?"

[Estimated exposure: brief but hazardous.]

That was either nothing or far too long, depending on who was counting.

Roan looked north.

The beacon was unavoidable. The names were not. Noise bought intelligence. He let the signal fly.

"Proceed."

The world folded inward.

Not darkness.

White.

Snow-white, bone-white, hospital-white, the white of light reflected off a blade before blood decided what color the scene deserved. The roof vanished. Ironholm became a smear of distant heat. The wind stopped touching his skin.

A man stood beside him at the parapet.

Aldric Vance looked less complete than before.

The first remnant had been translucent, damaged, a shape patched from regret and emergency protocol. This one had more detail and less stability. Dark hair threaded with gray. Tired eyes. Coat collar turned up against a wind that didn't exist. One hand resting on the parapet in the exact place Roan's left hand had been a heartbeat ago.

Not a ghost.

A message wearing a man.

Roan made himself remember that.

Aldric looked out over the city.

For one impossible second, father and son stood in the same posture.

Then Aldric smiled faintly.

"You refused the terms," he said.

The voice hit harder than the meaning.

Roan's fingers tightened against stone that was no longer there. He counted pulse, breath, Shard heat, six-point-seven-second exposure window. He didn't let the sound become anything useful enemies could exploit.

"They were badly drafted," Roan said.

Aldric's smile widened by a fraction. "Good."

The word landed with the weight of a hand on the back of the neck, brief and gone before anyone could decide whether it had been comfort.

Roan hated that it worked.

"Names," he said.

No greeting. No apology. No what happened to you. No why did you leave us with riddles and corpses.

Those questions mattered.

They also had poor timing.

Aldric turned his head.

His eyes were wrong in the same way the Codex prompts were wrong: too much structure behind human expression, old mathematics looking through grief.

"Vorath," he said.

The roof trembled.

A line of pale text opened in the air between them.

VORATH GREEL.

White beard. Gold-red eyes. Northern Glaive. Coffee cooling on a table. A portrait looking at Lyra. Kael bleeding on a legal stage. The name burned itself into Roan's memory ledger, no longer just an enemy but an axis.

"Hesh."

HESH.

No surname. A flash of black water under a stone bridge. A hand covered in rings, each ring holding a different eye. A laugh cut off by static.

"Maddox."

MADDOX VELL.

A counting room. Contracts stacked like bone. Iris's face, pale with fury and fear. A ledger closing around Aldric's name.

"Vespa."

VESPA.

A woman in a red veil standing in a room full of sleeping children. No. Not sleeping. Preserved. Roan's jaw tightened before he could stop it.

"Krell."

KRELL.

Steel hands. A furnace city. Men kneeling in ash with their mouths sewn shut by wire.

"Sirenne."

SIRENNE.

Lady Sirenne smiling in an old photograph beside Aldric. A glass raised. A promise made with eyes that didn't match the mouth. Drogan younger, laughing off-frame. Snow behind them. A mountain far away.

The six names hung in the white air.

Vorath. Hesh. Maddox. Vespa. Krell. Sirenne.

Six.

Roan had known some. Suspected others. The difference between suspicion and name was the difference between smoke and address.

"Seventh," Roan said.

Aldric didn't answer immediately.

The exposure window pressed against the back of Roan's skull.

[Exposure continuing.]

[Recognition risk increasing.]

Aldric looked at him.

The father's expression changed, not into sorrow, because sorrow was too simple for whatever fragment had survived. It changed into the look of a man watching a knife he had forged decide whether to become a key.

"The seventh," Aldric said, "wasn't one of them."

The white air split.

A seventh line appeared beneath the six names.

Blank.

No name. No image. No flash of memory.

Just an empty space shaped exactly like a slot waiting for a blade.

Roan felt the Shard crackle.

[Seventh position: unresolved.]

[Self-authoring vector detected.]

[Warning: do not permit external inscription.]

That was more warning than explanation.

Typical.

"Who writes it?" Roan asked.

Aldric's edges began to fray.

"You do," he said.

"That isn't an answer."

"It's the only one that keeps you alive."

The wind returned at the edges of the vision.

Roan stepped closer, or tried to The white space resisted movement.

"What is Suthmere?"

Aldric looked north.

For the first time, fear crossed his face.

Not fear for himself. Roan had seen enough dead men's recordings to know the difference. Fear for what survived him.

"A door," Aldric said. "And a debt collector."

"Kaelen?"

"Unbroken things still owe." Aldric's voice thinned. "Don't mistake him for safe."

"I don't mistake anyone for safe."

"You do."

The words cut cleanly because they were quiet.

Roan went still.

Aldric's fading hand lifted, not touching, stopping short of Roan's shoulder as if the remnant remembered boundaries even when memory itself was failing.

"You mistake yourself," he said.

[Recognition risk rising.]

[Recognition risk: high.]

Roan felt something turn toward him from very far away.

Not Vorath. Colder. Finer.

Glass over a blade.

Sirenne.

The name in the air flickered.

Aldric's expression hardened. "End it."

"Not yet."

"Roan."

His name in that voice was almost enough.

Almost.

"What did you mean?" Roan demanded. "Don't become me. What did you become?"

The remnant looked at the six names.

Then at the blank seventh.

"Necessary," Aldric said.

The word didn't sound like pride. It sounded like an autopsy.

Roan ended the access.

White shattered.

The roof returned with a violence that drove him to one knee. Wind hit his face. Snowmelt soaked through his trouser leg. His right hand had gone to Sidewound without conscious permission. The pendant against his chest burned cold enough to hurt.

The Codex Shard screamed once inside his spine.

Not sound. Pressure. Warning.

[Memory remnant closed.]

[Recognition event detected.]

[External attention: Sirenne — probable.]

[External attention: unknown — possible.]

[Ledger updated.]

He logged the term. Debt. Not an inversion cost. Something older. Something the Shard carried for his father.

Roan spat blood onto the roof.

It froze almost immediately.

The delayed message to Drogan vibrated in his pocket with a one-minute warning. Roan canceled it, then set a second one for five minutes. The act felt absurdly mundane after hearing his father's remnant list murderers in white light. That was useful. Mundane actions returned ownership of the body. Button, timer, breath, pocket. Real things.

He checked the chalk line.

His boots had moved half an inch forward. Not much. Enough. He marked the shift with the pen, added *memory pull vector: north*, and tore the page free. The waterproof paper trembled in the wind. The handwriting was his own. No missing time between strokes.

Acceptable.

The roof tag under the latch pulsed once: someone had entered the service corridor but hadn't opened the door. Drogan, likely. Or Cole. If it was Cole, he would knock because administrators believed doors were moral objects. If it was Drogan, he would open the door if worried and kick it if annoyed.

The door remained closed.

Drogan, then, waiting.

That was almost considerate.

That was inconvenient. Blood on the roof could be tested. He wiped it with a cloth, then burned the cloth with a controlled Pyrebrand spark and scattered the ash into the drainage groove.

Only after that did he stand.

Priorities.

The six names remained in his mind with unnatural clarity.

Vorath. Hesh. Maddox. Vespa. Krell. Sirenne.

The seventh remained blank.

Below, Ironholm's lights continued as if nothing had happened. People moved through streets. Trams ran. Students slept, studied, gossiped, trained, lied. Lyra sat in a mirrorless room at Aldermere with a senior warder angry at furniture. Brendt was awake despite needing rest, composing a message about breakfast and ominous roofs. Drogan was perhaps in a lift, swearing at architecture. Selene was tracing exits while Sirenne went quiet. Cole was building paperwork walls and pretending they were enough.

Life continued.

That was rude of it.

Roan looked down at his boots. Snowmelt pooled around them, shallow and dark in the roof light. Not blood. Not yet. But the posture matched the future enough to make the back of his neck tighten.

He crouched and touched the water.

Cold.

Real.

No vision.

Good.

His phone vibrated.

A message from Selene.

*Sirenne's office just requested travel clearance to Suthmere.*

A second message arrived from Drogan.

*Roof. Now. Something just looked at the Spire.*

A third from Lyra.

*The covered portrait laughed.*

Roan read all three twice.

"Wonderful," he said, to no one, flat the way he'd been saying it for years. The word came out tasting of copper, the way it always did when the day had stopped negotiating.

He also copied the sixth name onto a separate page and circled it once. Sirenne. The Shard's warning had classified her attention as probable, not confirmed. Probable was enough to alter planning, but not enough to justify direct movement. Direct movement was what people like Sirenne wanted. Quiet people didn't go quiet because they lacked plans. They went quiet because sound had become unnecessary.

So Roan wrote three countermeasures beneath her name: don't meet indoors, don't drink, don't accept reflected surfaces.

The last one looked absurd on paper.

So had ancient kettles. So had ordering Lyra to put her phone in her sleeve. So had refusing to look at museum portraits. Survival had a way of making absurdity respectable after the first corpse. Roan preferred not to wait for the corpse.

He added one more line beneath the three.

*Assume she knows which rule I just learned.*

That meant she might not chase the remnant itself. She might chase the boundary conditions: height, anchor, blank name, refusal terms. People who understood rules didn't attack the locked door. They attacked the hinge, the key, or the person stupid enough to believe the lock mattered.

Roan underlined *blank name* once. Not twice. Twice would imply anxiety, and anxiety made poor ink for permanent ledgers under sustained enemy pressure.

Then he opened his notebook to the page that had once held six question marks and wrote the names.

1. Vorath Greel

2. Hesh

3. Maddox Vell

4. Vespa

5. Krell

6. Lady Sirenne

7.

He left the seventh line blank.

For now.

The Codex Shard pulsed, no longer hot, no longer silent. Waiting.

Roan closed the notebook and looked north.

"Six to go," he said.

The wind took the words across the roof.

Behind his ribs, pale text unfolded one final time.

[The seventh writes himself.]
