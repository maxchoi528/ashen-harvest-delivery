Chapter 199: Aldric's Cairn

The Greycross cemetery sold silence badly.

Roan arrived before dawn and found two maintenance drones stuck in a snowdrift beside the eastern gate, their hazard lights blinking in mournful amber. A municipal salt spreader had broken down near the third row of memorial trees. Somewhere beyond the cremation wall, a caretaker argued with a vending machine that refused to dispense coffee despite accepting payment, which Roan considered an honest summary of city services.

Still, it was quieter than Black Spire.

That was enough.

He didn't take a campus car.

Campus cars had drivers, route logs, and polite ward tags designed by people who believed transparency improved safety. Transparency improved lawsuits. For safety, Roan preferred alleys, unpaid favors, and vehicles whose registration plates didn't correspond to the chassis.

Dren's courier brought him halfway across Ironholm in a bakery van that smelled of onions and engine oil. The van's heater failed twice. The rear door rattled whenever they passed over tram seams. The driver, a woman with a shaved head and a tattoo of a broken halo behind one ear, asked no questions after Roan handed over the envelope.

Good courier.

At the edge of Greycross, Roan got out three blocks early and walked the rest.

That wasn't nostalgia. Nostalgia was memory with bad accounting. He walked because Greycross still showed movement honestly. A tail from House Greel would avoid puddles and broken glass in ways locals didn't. Black Spire security would keep too much distance. Cole's people would watch rooftops. Drogan, if he was following, would choose the cemetery itself because he disliked wasting energy on theater.

At the lower bend, he found fresh flowers under a cracked stone that didn't belong to Aldric. Cheap white ones, wrapped in paper from a corner market. Someone had placed them carefully enough to clear snow from the name first. Roan read the inscription as he passed. Not the Black Squad boy Drogan had mentioned. A woman. Two dates. No epitaph.

The absence of an epitaph felt more honest than most. It also made him wonder who had decided silence was cheaper than a lie, and whether they had been wrong.

He kept walking. The detail mattered only because cemeteries were ledgers too. Some entries were paid in flowers. Some in names. Some in men standing before empty graves with burnt cards because the body had never been recovered and the enemy still owned too much of the truth.

By the time Roan reached the lower cemetery road, he had identified two municipal watchers, one bored Sentinel contractor, and a man pretending to smoke beneath a closed noodle shop while using the reflection in a dark window to track the street. The last one had decent discipline and bad shoes. Greel? Maybe. Journalist? Also possible.

Roan stopped beside a trash bin, removed a coin from his pocket, and flicked it into the gutter.

The smoker looked down for half a second.

Not Greel. Greel people had better training or more fear. Journalist, then, or private investigator. Roan let him keep breathing. A body before dawn created paperwork and distracted from the grave.

He did send Ines a photograph.

Her reply arrived thirty seconds later.

*Independent feed-chaser. Sells duel-location tips. Want him scared?*

Roan typed: *Later. Track buyer list.*

*Romantic.*

He ignored that.

Near the cemetery gate, the arguing caretaker finally defeated the vending machine by kicking it hard enough to trip its maintenance latch. A paper cup dropped into the tray and immediately overflowed. The caretaker stared at the mess with the weary betrayal of a man who had expected very little and received less.

Roan considered helping.

Then the man kicked the machine again and acquired a second cup.

Self-sufficient. Good.

Roan bought the second cup from him for twice its value and threw it away unopened. The caretaker stared, offended on behalf of both coffee and economics. That was fine. The purchase gave Roan a reason to stand beside the gate for seven seconds longer, and seven seconds confirmed no one followed him through the lower road.

He had slept two hours because the Codex Shard had determined that a full eight-hour cycle was an inefficient use of available time and had compressed his recovery into the minimum viable window. Just before the second hour of sleep could pretend to be restful, pale text appeared behind his eyes.

[Sleep quota nearing completion.]
[Prepare for conscious recovery.]

At the two-hour mark, he woke without grogginess and immediately began cataloging the implications.

The body felt functional. That was the problem. Functional wasn't the same as rested. The Shard had optimized the minimum recovery cycle by trimming whatever it considered waste. Dreams, perhaps. Long unconscious repair. The psychological luxury of not being himself for a while.

He had logged three tests before leaving: pulse stable, Vitalis circulation smooth without tremor under low output. Cognitive recall intact across number strings, legal clauses, and Lyra's emergency instructions. Emotional irritability elevated by twelve to fifteen percent, difficult to measure because his baseline was disputed by nearly everyone he knew.

Conclusion: usable, dangerous, not yet crippling.

The Shard had recalculated his physiology's downtime requirements and decided that deep recovery could be sustained on a leaner schedule. He disliked systemic optimizations mostly when they were applied without consent, even if they worked.

The cemetery gate recognized his student pass after two attempts. The first rejection cited restricted hours. The second opened because Cole had apparently updated his campus protection token to include after-hours municipal access, graveyard category.

Roan paused under the arch.

Cole was either thorough or interfering. Both.

Snow fell in thin, dry lines, catching on the shoulders of his coat. Greycross spread below the cemetery hill in dark blocks and dirty lights. This had once been the edge of the city. Now the city had grown around its dead, as cities tended to do, repurposing grief into real estate pressure and transport inconvenience.

Aldric Vance's cairn stood in the older section, where the stones leaned slightly and the names had weathered enough to require effort. Mira had chosen the spot because it overlooked the district where Aldric had supposedly spent his last sober months. Roan had later learned that was a lie, or at least a decorative simplification. Aldric's last months had involved secret contacts, a murdered squad, a civilization's corpse lodged in his blood, and six enemies powerful enough that men like Cole and Eckhart still spoke around them three decades later.

But the view was decent.

People made decisions with the information available. Then time mocked them.

Roan passed the memorial trees and took the narrow path uphill.

He wasn't alone.

He knew that before he saw the coat.

Drogan stood beside the cairn with a paper cup in one hand, breath fogging in the cold. He wore his old field jacket instead of Spire black. Snow had settled in his hair, which made him look more like a retired soldier than usual and less like an instructor pretending school politics didn't make him want to commit municipal crimes.

Roan stopped three paces away.

"You followed me," he said.

"Yes."

"Poorly."

"No. You expected me."

Fair.

Roan walked the last few steps to the cairn.

The marker was simple: ALDRIC VANCE. Beloved husband. Father. The rest had been Mira's choice, and Roan had never asked whether she believed it, needed it, or carved it because cemeteries punished honest inscriptions.

*Beloved husband* was true.

*Father* was true.

Everything else important had been omitted.

Drogan held out the cup.

Roan looked at it. "Coffee?"

"Tea."

"Why?"

"Coffee seemed too pointed."

Roan took the cup.

It was terrible tea. Bitter and over-steeped, then too hot. Drogan had bought it from the cemetery vending machine after the caretaker surrendered.

Roan drank it anyway.

For thirty seconds, they stood in silence while snow gathered on Aldric's name.

Then Drogan said, "Mira know you're here?"

"No."

"Lyra?"

"No."

"Cole?"

"Probably."

Drogan grunted. "He put access on your token."

"Yes."

"Interfering bastard."

"Useful interfering bastard."

"Those are the worst kind."

Roan looked at the cairn. "Agreed."

He removed Vorath Greel's card from his inner pocket.

The card had survived the café. Heavy cream stock. Greel crest in raised gold. A private number embossed below a name that history had permitted to remain respectable through either cowardice or careful editing.

Vorath Greel.

Northern Crown.

White beard. Gold-red eyes. Throne-tier pressure. A portrait that could look at Lyra from a museum wall and press *found* into her skull.

Roan had considered keeping the card as evidence.

Evidence had to be preserved when preservation increased future leverage. In this case, the information had already been recorded, photographed and copied, then stored in three places, one of them off-network. The physical card was no longer evidence. It was an anchor.

Not mystical, perhaps.

But perhaps was a dangerous word around men who weaponized portraits.

He handed the tea back to Drogan and crouched before the cairn.

Drogan watched the card. "You test it?"

"Seven ways. No active ward. No current sympathetic link. Minor Vitalis residue from direct contact. Possible passive image-memory."

"Possible."

"Yes."

"So you're burning it."

"Yes."

"Because possible is enough now."

Roan took out a matchbook.

"Possible has always been enough," he said. "People just prefer waiting until it becomes obituary."

The match struck on the first attempt.

Small flame. Ordinary. Honest.

Roan held it to the card's corner.

For one heartbeat, nothing happened.

Then the gold crest blackened.

The Codex Shard pulsed.

[Foreign authority residue detected.]
[Burn vector available.]
[Invert?]

Roan stared at the flame.

No.

The refusal came clean. Not because the gain was useless. Inverting foreign authority residue might convert some trace of Vorath's contact into resistance, insight, perhaps even a better defense against Northern pressure. Tempting. Mechanically elegant.

Also stupid.

Not all poison should become medicine. Some poison should be burned and left as ash, especially when the poison belonged to a man old enough to hide hooks inside gifts, portraits and bloodlines, plus polite invitations.

The Shard waited.

The registration-window window passed.

The card burned like paper.

Good.

Roan set it on a flat stone at the base of the cairn and watched it curl inward. Gold raised lettering became black ridges, then flakes. The number vanished first. The crest lasted longer. Vorath's name held until the final fold, stubborn as reputation.

Then that burned too.

Drogan said nothing.

Snow hissed where it touched the flame. The card became a small gray-black smear.

Roan waited until the last ember died.

Then he gathered the ash with two fingers and scattered it across the frozen ground before the marker.

"One down," he said. "Five to go."

Drogan's breath stopped for half a second.

Roan didn't look at him.

The sentence was inaccurate in the literal sense. Vorath was alive. Throne-tier. Unrepentant. Far from down in any physical, legal, financial, or strategic way that would satisfy a competent ledger.

But something had changed.

Vorath had come to Ironholm expecting an isolated boy under institutional pressure. He had left after being refused in public, blocked by Kaelen, legally constrained by Cole, politically exposed through Kael, and forced to move through indirect hooks like museum portraits. He remained dangerous, but his perfect shape had cracked.

Down didn't always mean dead.

Sometimes it meant named, seen and resisted, then entered into the account.

The Codex Shard warmed.

[Enemy anchor: Vorath Greel — marked.]
[Status: unresolved.]
[Classification conflict detected.]
[Host assertion: One down.]
[Codex ledger: Six remain.]

Roan's fingers paused over the ash.

"That looked like a prompt," Drogan said.

"It was."

"Bad?"

"Pedantic."

"Runs in the family."

Roan brushed ash from his gloves. "The Shard disagrees with my accounting."

"Smart Shard."

"Careful. It may start approving of you."

"Then kill me."

Roan stood.

For a moment the cemetery blurred at the edges. Not dizziness. Memory pressure. The kind that came from the Codex pushing old images against present perception without asking permission.

Aldric at this same cairn? No. Aldric had never stood at his own empty grave. Different snow. Different stone. A mountain in the distance. A woman laughing behind a camera. Six figures under aurora light.

Then nothing.

Roan blinked once.

Drogan's hand had moved near his elbow but not touched.

Good. Touching would've been inefficiently kind.

"What did you see?" Drogan asked.

"Not enough."

"That wasn't the question."

"It was the answer available."

Drogan looked at the cairn. "Aldric hated this place."

Roan turned his head slightly. "He came here?"

"Once. Before you were born. Before Mira. Different grave." Drogan took the tea back and grimaced as if surprised it remained terrible. "A Black Squad boy died in Greycross. No family. Aldric insisted on a marker. Paid cash. Lied about the surname so the Bureau wouldn't reclaim the plot."

"Why tell me now?"

"Because you keep treating the dead like a list."

Roan's eyes narrowed.

Drogan met them. "Lists are useful. They're not complete."

"I know."

"Do you?"

There were several responses available. The efficient one was to deflect. The aggressive one was to remind Drogan that incomplete sentiment hadn't saved Aldric, Black Squad, or the boy under the false surname. The cruel one was to ask how many graves Drogan visited before deciding which dead earned lectures.

Roan chose none of them.

Not kindness. Control.

"I treat the dead as lists," he said, "because lists can be acted on. Missing people can't."

Drogan's expression shifted.

The snow kept falling.

"Your father said something close," Drogan said.

"Was he right?"

"Sometimes."

"That's a useless answer."

"Most true ones are."

Roan looked back at the cairn.

Aldric Vance. Beloved husband. Father.

He had spent months turning the name into architecture: proof, motive, lineage, threat model, inheritance, technical system, enemy map. It was easier that way. A father who was architecture could be studied. A father who was a man asked for things that had no clean output.

Don't become me.

Too late? Not yet? Unclear.

The Shard remained warm.

[Classification conflict unresolved.]
[Ledger correction pending.]
[Additional data required.]

Roan almost told it to find better data. He suspected it would take that literally.

Instead, he crouched again and pressed two fingers to the edge of the stone.

Cold. Rough. Real.

"He came to you," Roan said.

Drogan's jaw tightened. "Who?"

"Kaelen. Before the café. Or before the bakery. At some point. He knew enough to arrive at the right second. He knew about Suthmere. He knew my father owed him a drink. He didn't learn that from watching my tournament fights."

Drogan took too long to answer.

"Yes," he said.

Truth.

"When?"

"After your public statement. Before Vorath arrived."

"And you didn't tell me."

"He asked me not to."

Roan stood slowly. "You accepted secrecy from a man strong enough to block Vorath."

"I accepted help from a man strong enough to block Vorath."

"Different sentence. Same risk."

"Yes."

That yes removed several sharper responses. Roan disliked when people acknowledged flaws before he could use them.

"What did he want?"

Drogan stared past the cairn toward Greycross, where the first dull line of dawn pressed against the buildings.

"To see if you were Aldric's son," he said.

"Genetically confirmed."

"Not what he meant."

"I know."

"He wanted to know whether you could refuse a Crown without becoming one."

Roan filed that phrase carefully.

Refuse a Crown without becoming one.

Kaelen liked tests. Or riddles. Both. Strong old men seemed incapable of using simple nouns when symbolic threats were available.

"Did I pass?" Roan asked.

"He left the note."

"That isn't an answer."

"It's the one he gave."

Roan looked north. Suthmere. The mountain. A door under a bakery. A second door elsewhere, waiting beneath stone, snow, or debt.

"The note said when I'm ready," Roan said.

"Yes."

"Ready for what?"

Drogan didn't answer.

The cemetery did.

Not in words. In pressure.

The Vance Pendant cooled against Roan's chest. The air around the cairn tightened by a fraction, too subtle for ordinary senses, too orderly for wind. Snow falling across Aldric's name slowed for half a breath, each flake hanging like a white pin in gray light.

Drogan's cup cracked.

Roan didn't move.

The Codex Shard opened.

[Lineage cairn recognized.]
[Physical remains absent.]
[Memory residue: insufficient.]
[Debt residue: present.]
[Request: acknowledge inheritance vector.]

Roan read the prompt twice.

Request. Not command. Not recommendation.

That was new.

"What is it?" Drogan asked.

Roan considered the question. The wrong answer was to say nothing. The worse answer was to say everything. He chose the useful middle.

"The Shard wants acknowledgment."

Drogan's face hardened. "Don't."

"Why?"

"Because old things ask politely when they can't force you yet."

Good advice.

Also incomplete.

The Shard had optimized his sleep without permission. It had recalibrated bone and blood, plus pain. It had embedded Aldric's voice in prompts and turned catastrophe into growth. It had never asked before.

A request was either weakness, respect, bait, or a rule.

Rule field: when a system changes interaction mode, test boundaries with minimal commitment.

Roan placed one finger on the cairn, not his whole hand.

"I acknowledge the debt," he said.

The air tightened.

Drogan swore softly.

Roan continued, because wording mattered. "I don't accept unknown terms. I don't accept inherited obedience. I don't accept any claim on Lyra, Mira, Brendt, Drogan, or anyone under my protection. I acknowledge only this: Aldric Vance was murdered. I will collect."

For a moment, nothing happened.

Then the snow fell normally again.

The cracked paper cup in Drogan's hand collapsed, spilling terrible tea into the snow.

The Codex Shard pulsed once.

[Inheritance vector acknowledged.]
[Unknown terms refused.]
[Refusal recorded.]
[Debt ledger stabilized.]

A small line of warmth moved along Roan's spine, not painful, not pleasant. The crack within the Shard shifted.

Not healing.

Aligning.

[Sleep quota stability improved: 2h 00m baseline confirmed.]
[Additional recovery pathways locked pending Suthmere access.]

There it was.

Reward. Optimization. Next gate.

Drogan stared at him. "What did you just do?"

Roan didn't answer immediately. He moved three steps aside and crouched, then pressed his fingers to the snow where the terrible tea had spilled. The liquid had spread in a lopsided fan, melting a shallow dark stain before freezing at the edges. No glow. No residual line. No prompt.

That mattered.

The cairn had responded to wording, not blood, not tea, not touch alone. The system hadn't activated when he arrived, not when he held Vorath's card, not when Drogan spoke of Aldric. It activated when Roan acknowledged debt and refused terms.

Rule: inheritance vectors weren't automatic.

Limit: they could be invited by language.

Cost: sleep remained capped, recovery pathways locked, Suthmere now mandatory if he wanted more than a two-hour baseline and a spine full of warnings.

Reward: debt ledger stabilized. Whatever that meant in combat or memory work remained untested.

He wrote the sequence in his notebook while kneeling in snow because relying on memory after accepting a memory-adjacent inheritance protocol was how arrogant people became cautionary plaques.

Drogan waited until the pen stopped moving.

"You taking notes at a grave?"

"The grave started it."

"That's a sentence I hate understanding."

"Then stop collecting context."

"Too late."

Roan closed the notebook. The line about debt ledger stabilization sat under Vorath's name like a nail hammered through paper. It didn't make him stronger in any immediate way. It made the board legible.

A reward that didn't change future choices was decoration.

This one changed choices.

Suthmere had moved from optional investigation to recovery gate. Kaelen's note had gained hard utility. Drogan's secrecy around the mountain had become less tolerable. Lady Sirenne's travel clearance, if it came, wouldn't be an interesting coincidence.

The next route was no longer north someday.

It was north before the sleep debt found interest.

"Accepted the useful part. Refused the rest."

"Of course you did."

"It worked."

"That doesn't make it sane."

"It rarely does."

Roan looked down at the ash of Vorath's card. Wind had already scattered most of it. No hostile pulse remained. No hidden thread, at least none the Shard or pendant could detect.

But something else had settled at the cairn.

A ledger had closed. Another had opened.

He picked up the empty matchbook and put it in his pocket. Leaving trash on a grave was inefficiently disrespectful.

Drogan watched him do it.

"What now?" the old instructor asked.

Roan glanced toward the city, where Black Spire's top vanished into low cloud.

"Now I go higher."

Drogan looked up too.

He understood.

The roof.

Aldric's second appearance had come once under pressure, once in a dorm room, once through artifacts and fragments. If the Shard's debt ledger had stabilized, if Lyra's danger had forced the family line into open recognition, if Vorath's name had been burned and refused, then the next prompt wouldn't arrive in a quiet cemetery at dawn.

It would arrive where the city couldn't press close.

Roan started walking.

Behind him, Aldric Vance's empty cairn held its silence a little better than before.
