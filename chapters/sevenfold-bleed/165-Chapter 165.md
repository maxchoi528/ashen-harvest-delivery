Chapter 165: Selene Joins

Selene didn't ask to join Roan's team the next day.

That would've been too direct, and Selene Ardent had been raised by people who considered directness a tool best used after the blade was already in.

Instead, she sent him three things.

The first was a clean copy of the Doss Logistics hazard report, filed through the Student Guild, with every clause properly cited and no attempt to reduce Roan's salvage claim. The second was a separate note identifying two legal pathways by which Ivar Doss might try to contest the tripled fee, both of which she had already blocked by attaching timestamped evidence from Brendt's remote log. The third was an invitation to tea at the Ardent study hall, phrased politely enough to be a trap and specific enough to be useful.

Roan read all three before breakfast.

Brendt, still confined to the dorm couch by medical order and personal betrayal, watched him over a bowl of fortified porridge.

"You made the contract face," Brendt said.

"I have several."

"This is the one where someone competent did something inconvenient."

"That's most competent people."

"Selene?"

Before he went, Roan built Selene a file.

Not because he lacked one. He had begun building it the day she first spoke in class and every House student within ten meters adjusted their posture. People who changed rooms by entering them deserved files. Selene's file had been thin at first: House Ardent main line, second daughter, elder brother in military procurement, younger brother at Aldermere preparatory track, mother on three philanthropic boards and two defense trusts, father officially retired from field work but still appearing in budget signatures where retired men didn't belong. But the margins had filled quickly.

Selene had refused two patronage offers from House-aligned student societies. She had challenged Cyrus once in a private spar and lost on points but left him with a cracked rib he didn't report. She paid her servants directly during school terms despite House accounts handling such expenses, which suggested either vanity, guilt, or a desire to know who was loyal to her. Her archive terminal access had spiked in the past month. Always legal. Always skirting the edges of old Rift management.

She wasn't rebelling.

Not yet.

She was mapping the walls of the cage.

That was more useful than rebellion. Rebels made noise. Cartographers found doors.

Roan added three risk markers.

Family pressure: high.

Personal pride: high.

Contract integrity: provisionally high.

Susceptibility to truth presented without theatrics: unknown.

Brendt, watching from the couch, said, "You know that if she ever sees that file, she will stab you."

"She will object first."

"With the spear?"

"Possibly."

"At least add a flattering note."

Roan looked at the file.

After a moment, he added:

Competent under pressure.

Brendt squinted at the screen. "That's what you consider flattering?"

"Yes."

"You are going to die alone surrounded by very organized enemies."

"Unlikely. You keep joining."

Brendt opened his mouth, closed it, and pointed his spoon at Roan. "That was almost nice."

"No."

"It was adjacent to nice."

"Finish your porridge."

"Coward."

Roan passed him the tablet.

Brendt read the files, his spoon forgotten halfway to his mouth. "She did our legal cleanup for free."

"Nothing from House Ardent is free."

"She did it without billing us."

"Different sentence."

"Fine. What does she want?"

"Access."

"To what?"

"Me. The team. The recovered case. Whatever she thinks I know about her family's old operations."

Brendt scrolled back to the invitation. "Tea at the Ardent study hall. That sounds less like access and more like ceremonial ambush."

"Yes."

"Are you going?"

"Yes."

"Why?"

"Ceremonial ambushes have rules. Random ones are messier."

"You say things like that, and then people wonder why I worry."

Roan took the tablet back. "You worry because it fills time between bad decisions."

"Exactly. My schedule is packed."

The Ardent study hall occupied the western wing of Black Spire's old library, behind glass doors etched with House donors' names and wards subtle enough to avoid insulting people while still counting them. Roan arrived five minutes early. Early enough to observe. Not early enough to suggest eagerness.

The hall smelled of polished wood, old paper, and expensive restraint. Tall shelves lined the walls. Study alcoves sat under brass lamps. A pair of Ardent students looked up when Roan entered, recognized him, and immediately decided not to stare. Good training. On the far side of the room, Selene sat at a rectangular table with two cups of tea, three documents, and no visible guards.

No visible guards mattered less than people thought.

Roan counted four probable watchers before he reached the table. Two students pretending to read. One older librarian with posture too military for books. One reflection in the window that didn't match anyone in the room.

Selene rose. "Roan."

"Selene."

"Tea?"

"No."

"Poison concerns?"

"Temperature."

Her mouth twitched. "Of course."

He sat with his back to the wall, because some habits weren't paranoia if people kept proving them correct.

Selene remained standing for one moment longer than necessary, then sat across from him.

"You brought watchers," Roan said.

"I am a House heir meeting a politically inconvenient Forged student with a habit of surviving murder attempts. My security would resign if I didn't."

"Will they interfere?"

"Not unless you attack me."

"Define attack."

"Physical harm, direct mental intrusion, poison, or structural collapse of the study hall."

"Verbal?"

"I assumed that was inevitable."

"Good standards."

She slid the first document toward him. "Final Doss report. Fee adjustment confirmed. Guild payment expected tomorrow. Your share after deductions is seven thousand four hundred and twelve credits."

Roan checked the tally. Correct.

Selene slid the second document. "The recovered storage case is currently held in Guild quarantine. House Ardent hasn't been notified through official channels."

"Unofficial?"

"My mother knows I found something. She doesn't know what."

Roan looked up.

Selene didn't pretend otherwise.

Useful.

"And the third?" he asked.

She touched the final document but didn't slide it across. "A proposal."

"No."

"You haven't read it."

"You waited until a controlled environment, created a courtesy debt by cleaning the Doss filing, placed watchers, established transparency about your mother, and kept the third document within your reach. It isn't a proposal. It's a negotiation designed to look like one. No."

Selene studied him.

Then, unexpectedly, she laughed softly.

The watchers didn't react. Well-trained.

"My father would hate you," she said.

"Efficient families delegate hate."

"You aren't wrong."

She slid the proposal over.

Roan read it anyway, because refusal before reading was posture, and posture without information was how idiots became statues.

The proposal was concise.

Selene Ardent requested provisional attachment to Roan Vance's registered explorer team for a trial period of three contracts. She would provide field combat support, House-grade gear access at cost, limited legal review, and sealed reporting discretion. In exchange, she would receive standard salvage share, operational transparency regarding contract risk, and equal say in withdrawal decisions during missions not involving Roan's personal enemies.

The last clause was interesting.

"You excluded personal enemies," Roan said.

"You have several."

"Most people do."

"Most students don't emerge from a Tier Three Rift and get ambushed by Greel-linked mercenaries at the exit."

"You heard about that."

"Everyone with access to the right rumors heard something. Most versions are wrong."

"Which version did you hear?"

"That Iris Vell was present. That Greel money hired the blades. That Drogan broke a captain's arm in three places. That you killed two Hardened practitioners before they understood you had advanced." Selene watched his face. "And that Iris walked away alive because someone let her."

Roan folded the document.

"Someone?"

"You. Drogan. Circumstance. I don't know. That's why I used the vague word."

"Careful."

"Yes."

The answer was simple and direct.

Selene had changed since the early semester. Or perhaps Roan had not been close enough to see the parts of her that mattered. At first she had looked like a polished House blade: proud, controlled, useful but expensive. Now he saw the cracks under the polish. Not weakness. Space where curiosity had been restrained too long.

House heirs weren't free. They merely had better cages.

"Why?" Roan asked.

Selene didn't ask what he meant.

"Because my family is lying about old Rift operations," she said. "Because the storage case you found bears a field seal that shouldn't have survived. Because every time your name crosses one of our old records, someone removes the page before I can read it. Because Iris Vell is unraveling in public and nobody in the Houses wants to admit why. Because you are building something outside the normal patronage channels, and that makes you dangerous to people I have been told are respectable."

Good answer.

Too good.

"And because?" Roan asked.

Selene's eyes narrowed slightly.

He waited.

Eventually, she said, "Because when Cyrus lost to you, half the school decided you were a brute with a trick. When I watched the recording, I saw restraint. Not mercy. Restraint. You could have humiliated him worse. You didn't because it would have given less return than letting him live with the correct conclusion."

"That isn't usually a recruitment motive."

"It's if one is tired of dramatic idiots."

Roan considered that.

Outside, rain touched the library windows in thin lines. Ironholm did rain properly: cold and organized, then economically depressing. Students moved through the stacks beyond the Ardent section, carrying books and gossip with equal care.

"You understand the risk," Roan said. "Association with me invites attention from people your family may not protect you against."

"My family protects assets, not people."

"Honest."

"I try."

"No, you don't."

Her mouth curved. "Not always."

Better.

Roan placed the proposal on the table.

"Trial period. Three contracts. Contract scope reviewed individually. No access to private files. No assumptions of House protection. No unilateral disclosure to your family. No romantic retrieval jobs."

Selene blinked. "No what?"

"Cole's restriction. Apparently common."

"That's horrifying."

"Agreed."

"Add no ceremonial House errands."

"Define."

"Recovering lost heirlooms from cousins who pawned them. Escorting relatives who call everything a security threat. Appearing at events so someone can imply I tamed the Greycross knife-boy."

"Knife-boy?"

"That one was from my aunt."

"Your aunt lacks imagination."

"She compensates with money."

Roan took out his pen. "No ceremonial House errands."

Selene looked at the pen. "You carry amendment ink to tea."

"You brought a negotiation disguised as one."

"Fair."

They amended the agreement for fourteen minutes.

Brendt joined remotely halfway through because Roan sent him a copy for review, and because Brendt had developed an alarming ability to sense contracts happening without him. His projected face appeared above Selene's document crystal with a blanket still around his shoulders.

"I approve clause nine," he said. "But clause ten needs a non-disparagement carve-out for private team morale."

Selene stared at the projection. "You want contractual permission to insult us?"

"Not insult. Maintain morale. Through selective disparagement."

"Denied," Roan said.

"Counterproposal: I get two insults per mission, unused insults roll over."

"No rollover," Selene said immediately.

Roan looked at her.

She shrugged. "My younger brother weaponized rollover dessert rights for six years. I know where that road ends."

Brendt pointed at her through the projection. "See? This is why we need her."

"For dessert jurisprudence?" Roan asked.

"Among other things."

Roan wrote: Private morale comments permitted within operational reason.

"There," he said. "Vague enough to be abused by everyone equally."

"A fair clause," Brendt said solemnly.

Selene signed first.

Roan signed second.

Brendt signed digitally as coordinator and witness, adding a tiny shield icon beside his name because medical confinement had apparently damaged his taste.

When the document sealed, the crystal flashed blue.

Team registration updated.

Roan Vance - Field Lead.

Selene Ardent - Combat Specialist.

Brendt Holloway - Operations Coordinator.

Roan stared at the lines.

The sparring test happened because Selene asked for it.

That improved his opinion of her more than the signed contract.

"If I am joining your team," she said after Brendt's projected shield icon finally stopped flashing, "I need to know how you move when you aren't performing for a tournament crowd."

"No peak techniques," Roan said.

"Agreed."

"No House techniques above public-school classification."

"Agreed."

"No injuries that require Dr. Seth."

Brendt's projection leaned into view. "Thank you for pretending I still have influence."

"No injuries that require Dr. Seth," Selene agreed. "Bruises acceptable. Pride damage not compensable."

"Good clause," Brendt said.

They used one of the small practice rings behind the library, a space too narrow for theatrical lunges and too public for anything secret. Selene fought with a training spear. Roan used a dulled knife and a short practice baton weighted like Sidewound's sheath.

The first exchange told him three things.

Selene preferred controlling centerline, not chasing openings. She used reach responsibly, which already placed her above half the spear users at Black Spire. She also watched Roan's shoulders instead of the weapon, which meant feints would require more investment than usual.

Good.

He stepped inside on the fourth beat.

She didn't retreat. She shortened her grip and struck with the butt of the spear toward his ribs.

Better.

Roan let the baton take the strike and allowed the force to rotate him half a step left. The obvious move would be a knife touch to her forearm. He aimed instead for the forward knee.

Selene lifted it before contact.

Not guessed. Read.

The spearhead stopped an inch from his throat.

His dulled knife rested against her inner elbow.

A mutual loss, depending on medical assumptions.

Brendt applauded from the projection. "Excellent. Everyone is dead. Very efficient."

Selene lowered the spear. "You target mobility first."

"People with reach dislike losing feet."

"You give ground less than expected."

"Ground is expensive."

"Again?"

They went again.

By the sixth exchange, Selene had adapted to his inside entries. By the ninth, he had adapted to her adaptation. By the twelfth, Brendt declared that if they continued he would begin assigning style scores, and both of them stopped because that threat was credible.

Selene was breathing harder, but not badly. Roan had one bruise along his forearm where her spear haft had found a gap. She had a red mark at the collarbone from the baton.

"Acceptable," Roan said.

"High praise," Brendt said.

Selene looked at Roan. "From him? Apparently."

She wiped the spearhead with a cloth and collapsed it back into its case. There had been no showing off, no attempt to prove House superiority without offended reaction when he struck low. She had tested and adjusted, then stopped at the agreed limit.

Contract integrity under physical pressure: improved.

Roan added the note mentally.

Selene saw something in his expression and shook her head. "You are updating a file in your head."

"Yes."

"At least be ashamed."

"Why?"

"Never mind."

Brendt's projection said, "Don't worry. He put something flattering in the written one."

Roan looked at him.

Brendt smiled the smile of a man safely distant and medically pitied.

Selene's eyebrow lifted. "Written one?"

"Operations file," Roan said.

"Does mine say terrifying?"

"No."

"Disappointing."

"Competent."

She paused.

Brendt pointed triumphantly. "See? Flattering."

Selene's expression did something small and controlled. "For him, perhaps."

"For anyone," Roan said.

Neither of them mocked that.

Interesting.

Then Iris's message arrived that evening and converted interesting into dangerous.

A poor boy from Greycross, a House heir, and an injured logistician with insufficient fear.

There was also the matter of reputation.

Selene didn't say it directly, which meant she understood it well enough to be dangerous. Roan's public profile after the tournament had become too sharp to hide and too unstable to spend carelessly. Poor students saw him as proof the House system could bleed. House students saw him as a threat, curiosity, or career opportunity depending on intelligence and family pressure. Faculty saw him as paperwork with a pulse. Clients saw fame and assumed competence. Enemies saw Aldric's shadow and began sharpening knives.

Adding Selene changed the shape of that profile.

With her attached, Roan looked less like a lone anomaly and more like the beginning of an independent pole. Not a House. Not a guild. Not a student club with matching jackets and delusions. A team that could take contracts, survive them, and cross social lines without asking permission from the old families.

That would irritate people.

Irritated people revealed priorities.

Selene knew that too. Her proposal included a line about "mutual reputational exposure" that most students would have treated as legal padding. Roan read it as a warning and an offer: use my name and let me use yours, but don't pretend either name is clean.

He appreciated clean drafting more than clean names.

"You understand this makes you visibly disobedient," he said.

Selene's fingers rested on the edge of her teacup. "To whom?"

"Your family."

"My family values useful disobedience when it succeeds."

"And when it fails?"

"Then it was never authorized."

"Convenient."

"Traditional," she said.

Brendt's projection made a soft noise. "House politics sounds exhausting."

Selene glanced at him. "It is. That's why we hire tutors for it."

"I was tutored by a Greycross landlord who once tried to evict a rat before a tenant."

"Did it work?"

"The rat had better legal standing."

Roan looked between them and decided the conversation had become unproductive in a way that might improve team cohesion.

Unfortunate, but tolerable.

"Three contracts," he said. "After that, either party may terminate without insult."

"Without formal insult," Selene corrected. "Informal insult seems inevitable."

"Accepted."

"Add a review meeting after each contract."

"Operational review already implied."

"Personal review."

"No."

"Then trust failure will hide behind operational success."

Roan paused.

Brendt pointed silently at Selene through the projection, as if presenting evidence in court.

Roan disliked both of them.

"Ten minutes," he said. "After each contract. No emotional language required."

"Allowed," Selene said.

"Not required," Roan said.

"Progress," Brendt said.

"You are enjoying this too much," Roan told him.

"I nearly died. I am owed hobbies."

Not a sensible structure.

But sensible structures had produced the world Roan was currently trying to dissect: Houses pretending virtue, schools pretending neutrality, Guilds pretending safety, and old murderers pretending history had no receipts. A structure didn't need to be sensible. It needed to survive contact with pressure and return more information than it consumed. By that standard, the three names on the registry weren't absurd.

They were an experiment with weapons. Effective.

The problem with experiments, Roan had learned, was that the successful ones attracted funding, imitation, theft and sabotage, plus people who called themselves mentors after arriving late. He intended to profit from the first and prepare for the rest.

Selene lifted her teacup. "To provisional disasters."

Brendt raised his bowl of porridge through the projection. "To billable disasters."

Roan didn't lift anything.

They both looked at him.

He sighed.

"To disasters with documentation."

Selene smiled.

Brendt grinned.

Somewhere behind the shelves, one of the watchers shifted, confused.

Good.

Confusion was a cheap defensive layer.

It wouldn't hold forever. Cheap layers rarely did. But cheap layers bought seconds, and seconds were the smallest currency Roan trusted. Useful enough, for now.

---

The first consequence arrived that evening.

Not from House Ardent.

From Iris Vell.

The channel was brittle—patched through a loyalty server she'd abandoned months ago. Sloppy. Or deliberate. The message contained no greeting.

*You are collecting Houses now?*

Roan read it once.

Brendt, watching from the couch, saw his expression. "Problem?"

"Iris."

Selene's name had already moved.

Good.

Information traveled quickly. Too quickly for ordinary gossip, which meant someone watched either Selene's study hall, the Guild registry, or Roan's message traffic.

He typed back:

*No. They're expensive to feed.*

Iris responded in under a minute.

*Ardent burns its allies eventually.*

Roan considered.

Then he wrote:

*Then I will invoice for heat damage.*

No reply.

Brendt stared at him. "Did you just antagonize a volatile House operative while she's emotionally destabilized?"

"Yes."

"Why?"

"To see whether she warns me or threatens me."

"And?"

Roan watched the blank screen.

Three minutes passed.

Then a final message arrived.

*Ask Selene about Red Orchard.*

The channel burned itself immediately afterward.

Roan didn't move.

Brendt leaned forward despite his ribs. "Roan?"

Roan opened the team agreement, added a private note under Selene Ardent, and wrote two words.

Red Orchard.

Then he sent Selene a contract inquiry for the next morning.

Not a question.

Questions gave people time to decide which lie suited them best.

Work gave lies terrain.
