Chapter 44: Departure

Week eight of the intersession break had settled into routine. The corridor outside the Forged-tier training hall still smelled like iron and old sweat. Not the fresh metallic tang of blood-forged Vitalis cycling—the dull, leached-out residue of a hundred failed cultivators who'd pushed too hard and quit.

Roan counted the scuff marks on the floor. Seventeen. He'd counted them four times now, waiting.

The door didn't open so much as shudder, releasing a wave of heat that made the air ripple. Inside, Brendt was visible through the observation slit, shirtless, his back a topography of healing burns from today's cycle. He was mid-breath on the Ninth Revival Form, spine curved wrong, weight shifted half a degree off. Wrong enough that three more seconds would snap something important.

Roan watched through the slit. Didn't knock.

Brendt's form corrected. Barely. He finished the cycle and sagged forward, catching himself on the wall.

"You saw that," Brendt said to the stone.

"Saw it."

"Helpful."

"I also saw you fix it."

"After you watched me almost die."

"That's when you fix it. Not before." Roan leaned against the corridor wall, arms crossed. "If I'd pulled you out early, you'd have filed it as *Drogan's disciple caught me* and done it again tomorrow."

Brendt's reflection in the polished stone doorframe was trying for annoyed. Settling for exhausted.

"Since when did you get pedagogical?"

"Since someone told me he wanted to catch up."

Brendt laughed, short, surprised. He toweled off his face and pulled a shirt over the burns. "Fine. What's the catch?"

"No catch."

"There's always a catch with you."

Roan said nothing for a moment. The arithmetic here was simple, and simple math didn't require justification.

The reality was less philosophical. Brendt was burning himself out trying to close a gap that had nothing to do with form quality or cycle speed. He'd been Tempered for two months. His Vitalis ceiling sat at around four hundred. His actual output, under stress, topped out at two-ninety. The remaining hundred *felt* absent even though it was mathematically present.

Some people's Vitalis didn't circulate freely after Breakthrough. It pooled. Got sticky. Refused to move until the body physically demanded it. Most cultivators called it a calibration plateau. The textbooks called it circulatory dissonance.

Roan called it fixable.

But the fix wasn't free, and he hadn't decided yet whether Brendt was worth the cost.

"What's your daily credit balance?" Roan asked.

"Forty-three."

"Dip it to ten. Buy three doses of Ironbile Resin from the discount shelf."

Brendt turned around fully. "Ironbile? That's—I know what that does. My organs would—"

"Would what? Melt a little. Heal stronger. That's the *point*." Roan pushed off the wall. "Ironbile's cheap because the market thinks the downside outweighs the upside. They're not wrong, for normal cultivators. For normal cultivators, the organ damage is permanent enough that the healing benefit barely breaks even." He paused at the door. "You're not normal. You're wasting a hundred Vitalis every time you cycle because your body's too comfortable to push. Ironbile forces the discomfort. Forces the circulation. Three doses, spaced five days apart, and your dissonance clears."

"Three doses costs thirty credits each. Ninety credits."

"Costs ninety credits if you pay retail."

Brendt's eyes narrowed. "What are you not telling me?"

"That the discount shelf moves slow because most students are idiots, not because the product is bad. Check the batch dates before you buy. The older stock loses potency at the rate of roughly two percent per month. The discount shelf sells anything past its nominal peak at forty percent off." A shrug. "The *actual* effective shelf life for Ironbile is about eighteen months past nominal peak. The shelf only stocks twelve-month-old maximum. You're not buying degraded product. You're buying product that's still eleven percent above what the label claims."

Brendt stared at him.

"I'm not going to ask how you know that," he said slowly.

"Good."

"I'm also not going to ask if you're planning to buy the discount shelf yourself."

Roan's expression didn't change.

"I'm going to ask if this advice comes with strings."

"No strings."

"Roan."

"I'm your friend." The word sat in the air, unremarkable. True. And exactly as complicated as truth got.

Brendt exhaled. Something in his posture loosened—not relaxation, but the relief of someone who'd been bracing for a trap and finding none.

"Three doses," Brendt said. "Five days apart."

"I'll check the batch dates with you. Show you what to look for."

"Why?"

The question wasn't about Ironbile. Roan heard the subtext.

*Because you're the only person at this institute who doesn't want something from me. Because the rest of them are counting my Vitalis, measuring my growth rate, waiting for me to become useful or dangerous. Because you asked me to train with you like it was a favor and not a transaction.*

"Because math doesn't lie," Roan said instead.

Right.

The corridor's ambient light shifted. Late afternoon bleeding into evening. The Forged-tier hall's heat was bleeding through the doorframe now, and somewhere deeper in the complex, a warden was running a cluster of newer students through foundation drills, the rhythmic *crack* of palm strikes against training posts carrying through the stone.

Brendt gathered his gear. They walked together toward the residential wing, not talking, which was its own kind of conversation. The dining hall was three tiers down and across the compound. Massive and loud, plus perpetually understocked this late in the academic term. Roan had mapped its crowd flows six weeks ago—peak density hit at 18:15, clearance by 19:00. It was 18:52. He calculated the odds of finding an empty table at two-person capacity.

Forty percent. Acceptable.

Brendt secured one near the eastern windows. Roan arranged his tray—protein, starch, mineral supplement, nothing that would interfere with evening cycle—and sat across from him.

The table had a view of the practice yards below. A pair of senior students were running partnered drills in the last grey light. One of them caught a strike wrong and went down hard. The other stepped back immediately, hands raised in the universal posture of *not my fault, instructors saw*.

The downed student pushed himself up on one elbow, shook his head, and waved his partner off before rising.

Roan recognized him. Kester Farrow. First-year Tempered, right knee ligament damage eight months ago that had never healed right. He'd been cleared for return, but the joint still torqued half a degree wrong on landing.

The math on Kester Farrow: a forty-three percent reduction in peak combat effectiveness that the institute's medical clearance system couldn't detect because it only tested static strength and dynamic range, not kinetic compensation under sudden load.

The institute would keep him in limited duty. Kester would keep pushing. The knee would eventually blow out entirely, within the next four months, if he kept landing on it wrong, and the institute would note it as an unfortunate training accident.

Roan looked back at his tray.

*Not my problem.*

"You're staring again," Brendt said.

"Assessing."

"People don't like being assessed."

"Kester Farrow isn't people. He's a data point." Roan chewed. Swallowed. "His knee will fail in approximately sixteen weeks. Medical will call it a re-injury. It won't be."

"How can you possibly—"

"The ligament healed at a two-degree inward rotation. Standard medical imaging doesn't catch that because they image the joint at rest, not under dynamic load. Every time he lands on it, the rotation introduces micro-stress on the medial side. The stress compounds. Eventually the scar tissue fails." Roan took another bite. "I watched him take six falls this week. The rotation's increasing. I'd put the window at twelve to eighteen weeks."

Brendt set his fork down.

"You've been tracking his falls."

"I watch everything."

"Does he know?"

"That his knee is going to blow out? No."

"Are you going to tell him?"

Roan considered the question. Not the answer, the question. Brendt was testing something. Looking for a line.

"Seventy-three credits," Roan said.

"What?"

"To fix Kester Farrow's knee properly—tier-appropriate ligament regeneration—requires a qualified bone-weaver and approximately seventy-three credits of bio-restorative compound. He doesn't have the credits. I do." A pause. "That's not charity. That's investment."

"In what?"

"He owes me a favor worth seventy-three credits. I collect when I need it." Roan shrugged. "His knee gets fixed. His career doesn't end. He becomes someone who owes me a significant favor from a position of gratitude rather than obligation. The tally works out."

Brendt studied him. Whatever he was looking for, he seemed to find it, because he picked his fork back up.

"You're exhausting," he said.

"I know."

"That's not a compliment."

"I know that, too."

They ate in silence. Below, Kester Farrow took another fall. His knee twisted. He got up, jaw tight, and waved his partner back into stance before the man could call a halt. Shook off the pain before it could settle.

Brendt watched Roan watching the yard.

"You're going to tell him," Brendt said quietly. It wasn't a question.

Roan said nothing.

*Sometimes the ledger is incomplete.*

He wasn't sure why that thought surfaced. It came with a faint sensation—not warmth, not pressure. Something with weight. The Codex Shard, set at the seventh vertebra, sat inert. No system prompt. No Aldric quip.

Just the thought, settling into place like a piece of a puzzle he hadn't known he was building. The evening cycle was scheduled for 20:00. Roan returned to his quarters at 19:34 to prep.

The room was small. Single bed, desk, weapon rack. Two windows facing the inner compound, currently open to the cooling air. He'd spent forty minutes arranging the space to minimize variables: moved the desk two inches left so the window's cross-draft wouldn't hit his face during seated meditation, angled the weapon rack so the ceiling-mounted Vitalis-reading lamp cast its glow behind him rather than in his eyes.

Small optimizations. Compound returns.

He stripped to a training singlet, sat cross-legged on the floor mat, and began the preparatory breathing. Standard sequence. Twelve counts in, eight counts hold, sixteen counts out. His lungs expanded fully on the third cycle. By the seventh, his resting heart rate had dropped from sixty-eight to fifty-four.

The Codex Shard hummed against his spine. Not active—no Inversion prompt—but *present*. Aware.

He pulled the evening's cycle from memory: Pyrebrand Cycling, seventh sequence. Not the full burn—too expensive this close to the tournament bracket announcements—but the preparatory form. The one that opened the secondary circulation channels and primed the body for higher-yield conversion.

The cycle was a modified version of what Drogan had taught him. Modified by Roan, based on seven weeks of trial-and-error data he'd collected on himself.

The balance:

Standard Pyrebrand Cycling, full burn, converted blood-oxygen debt into a fifteen percent increase in Vitalis output for approximately ninety seconds. Cost: blood-vessel micro-ruptures along the inner forearm and shoulder, healing time four to six days.

Modified Pyrebrand Cycling, half burn, converted the same debt at a nine percent output increase for sixty seconds. Cost: residual warmth in the palms for approximately two hours post-cycle.

Roan's modification—partial inversion preparation—added a third phase: a controlled intake of low-grade Wraithmilk that introduced mild neural inflammation, which the Codex Shard registered as a Sidewound and immediately began converting. The conversion cooled the inflammation *and* primed the Inversion pathways for faster activation during the Pyrebrand burn.

Net result: nine percent output increase, sixty seconds duration, *plus* the Wraithmilk's base Vitalis yield of eighty points, *plus* the Inversion pathway prep for the next cycle.

Cost: two hours of slightly impaired fine motor control.

He held the line and waited for the world to catch up. It always did.

He pulled the small vial from his desk drawer. Wraithmilk Tincture, third-tier diluted. He'd bought six doses from Old Pell last week at thirty credits each, below market, because the batch had a slight amber tint that most students associated with oxidation—but the tint was a mineral-content variance that increased absorption by about three percent.

Old Pell hadn't known. Roan hadn't explained.

He uncorked the vial. The liquid smelled like copper and wet stone. He drank it in one motion, set the empty vial down, and began the cycle.

*Sidewound detected.*

*[Sidewound type recognized: Neural inflammation, mild. Inversion standing by.]*

He breathed. The Wraithmilk hit his bloodstream. His neural pathways began the low-grade inflammation that the tincture was designed to produce—compensatory, the textbooks said. The body's response to sudden neurotransmitter density increase.

The inflammation spread. His fingertips tingled.

*Inversion triggered. Conversion successful: +80 Vitalis. Neural inflammation cleared. A.V.*

*[That last trick wasn't in the manual. I'm almost impressed. —A.V.]*

Roan's lips twitched. Not a smile. An acknowledgment.

He began the Pyrebrand sequence. The secondary circulation channels opened. His forearms flushed with heat as blood was redirected from the surface network into the deeper meridians.

The cycle lasted nine minutes. When it ended, his Vitalis reading sat at 1,247—solidly within the Tempered range, past the midpoint toward late-Tempered ceiling.

His palms were warm. Fine motor control was degraded by approximately eighteen percent for the next two hours.

Acceptable.

He rolled his shoulders and stood, then crossed to the window. Below, the practice yards had emptied. The compound lights were transitioning to night mode—amber glow from the warden's stations, blue-white from the high towers.

A silhouette moved along the outer wall. Quick, economical, at the exact pace that suggested someone who didn't want to be seen but also didn't particularly care.

Roan watched. Identified.

Drogan. Moving toward the western gate, not the official checkpoint.

Unusual. Drogan's quarters were in the inner ward. The western gate led to the training valleys—the restricted zones where students weren't permitted after dark.

Roan checked the time. 20:17.

He weighed options. The arithmetic was quick: Drogan's evening cycle should have ended an hour ago. Drogan's quarters should be two floors up and east. Drogan had changed out of training gear into dark civilian clothes, which meant this wasn't a routine check.

He was moving with purpose. Purpose that required discretion.

Roan pulled on a jacket and boots. His palms were still warm, fine motor impaired, but not balance, not vision, not Vitalis output.

He opened the window.

The compound wall had handholds. He'd noted them during his first week, filed away as a tertiary escape route. He'd never used them.

Tonight felt like a night for tertiary routes.

He climbed down in the dark, dropped the last eight feet into the shadow of the equipment shed, and followed Drogan's trajectory at thirty meters' distance.

Drogan reached the western gate. Didn't stop. Didn't show credentials. The gate's lock clicked open as he approached—biometric recognition—but the gate was supposed to be locked to students after 19:00.

Someone had made an exception.

Roan stopped at the gate line. Beyond it: the narrow valley path, the restricted zone, the dark.

He stood in the shadow of the equipment shed and calculated.

Following Drogan into a restricted zone he wasn't authorized to enter was a disciplinary violation. Caught: minimum sanction was suspension from sparring for two weeks. Maximum was expulsion from the junior sparring program.

Not following meant losing sight of data he didn't yet have. Data about what Drogan did after hours, in restricted zones, alone.

His palms were warm. His Codex Shard sat quiet against his spine. Somewhere beyond the gate, Drogan's silhouette was diminishing into the valley path.

The figures said don't follow.

The figures said a lot of things.

Roan crossed the gate line.
