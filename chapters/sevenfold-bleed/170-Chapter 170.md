Chapter 170: The Transfer Ledger

Kael Greel arrived under gray rain and good manners.

Roan watched from the second-floor overlook above Black Spire's east reception hall, where new transfer students were processed beneath banners that tried too hard to appear welcoming. The hall had been polished for the occasion. Black stone floors, silver ward lines, reception desks arranged in a crescent, student guides stationed near the doors. A large projection listed transfer names by arrival cohort.

Kael's name sat fifth.

No crest beside it.

That was the first lie.

House students almost always carried crest markers in institutional systems, if not out of pride then for convenience. Priority dining permissions, sponsor billing, restricted library access, medical waivers, dueling insurance. House identity made bureaucracy move. Kael Greel's file had entered the system as *external northern exchange, civilian sponsorship processed through compact authority.*

Technically possible.

Also nonsense.

Selene stood beside Roan with a tablet in hand and no visible reaction to the rain dripping from the coats of arriving students below. Ines occupied a bench behind them, boots up on a bag that didn't belong to her, pretending to read a campus brochure upside down. Drogan had refused the overlook and positioned himself near the western exit where he could be mistaken for a disciplinary problem until he became a tactical one.

Brendt wasn't there.

Brendt had argued.

Roan had countered with Dr. Seth.

Brendt had called that betrayal and then sent eight messages asking for live updates.

Roan had muted him after the fifth.

"You are enjoying this too much," Selene said.

"I am not enjoying it."

"You muted him with unusual efficiency."

"That's survival."

Ines turned a page of the upside-down brochure. "Brendt has started messaging me instead."

Roan looked back.

She smiled. "I am giving him worse updates."

"Define worse."

"I told him Kael is seven feet tall and carrying a ceremonial bear."

Selene's mouth tightened. "Why?"

"He asked whether anything interesting happened."

Roan considered unmuting Brendt solely to correct the bear, then decided against rewarding the channel breach.

Below, the first four transfers processed without incident. A girl from Eastmere with a storm focus. Two brothers from a minor coastal academy whose luggage contained more grooming products than combat gear. A quiet boy from a mining district who looked at the ceiling like he expected it to fall and therefore had better instincts than the others.

Then Kael entered.

He didn't look like a killer.

That meant nothing, but Roan noted it anyway.

Kael Greel was of medium height, lean, dressed in a dark travel coat cut well but not ostentatiously. His hair was a muted blond, rain-dark at the edges. His face matched the file: pleasant, forgettable, composed. He carried one duffel and one hard case small enough for books or knives. His posture had been deliberately softened. Not slouched. Not military. Student-neutral.

Too neutral.

People trained from childhood by violent families had resting habits. Weight placement. Door awareness. Eye movement. Hand spacing. Kael had removed most of them.

Most.

When the reception hall doors closed behind him, his eyes flicked once to the three highest vantage points.

Window. Balcony. Overlook.

He didn't look at Roan directly.

Good control.

Or good theater.

The Vance Pendant cooled by a fraction.

[Ambient recognition vector present.]

[Source: uncertain.]

[Recommendation: do not initiate contact.]

Roan agreed.

Kael approached the desk. The receptionist smiled with institutional warmth.

"Name?"

"Kael North."

Second lie.

Selene's finger paused on her tablet. "North?"

"Alias in student-facing register," Roan said.

"Approved?"

"Apparently."

Ines lowered the brochure. "That will make rumors worse."

"Yes."

"Do you think Greel intended that?"

"Yes."

A hidden heir with a thin alias created curiosity. Curiosity created approaches. Approaches let Kael choose contacts without appearing to initiate too aggressively. It was social bait disguised as privacy.

Clever.

Annoying.

At the desk, Kael presented documents, answered questions, and allowed his wrist to be scanned. The baseline ward flashed blue.

Forged.

Low Forged, according to the public readout.

Roan watched the ward lines rather than the number.

There.

A stutter.

Not a failure. A tiny hesitation in the scan's return, as if the ward had reached a surface, accepted it, and ignored the pressure behind it because the paperwork said not to look deeper.

Suppression.

Not definitive proof of Crowned. Could be artifact masking, disciplined cycling, or specialized Greel technique. But the readout was false.

Selene saw his face. "Cheated?"

"Yes."

"How badly?"

"Enough to matter."

Ines murmured, "That's your least comforting category."

"No. There are worse."

"Name one."

"Interesting."

Selene looked at him. "That explains too much."

Below, Professor Ilmere arrived to collect the transfer cohort.

Ilmere was sixty and thin, then carried a stack of papers like a weapon. Her gray hair was pinned so tightly it looked like a disciplinary measure. She had never fought a public duel in her life, according to records, and had once made a Crowned House scion apologize to a janitor because he had tracked mud across a memorial floor.

Roan respected her from a distance.

She looked at the transfer students over the rim of her glasses.

"Welcome to Black Spire," she said. "You are late in the term, behind in coursework, and statistically overconfident. We will address these failings in order."

The Eastmere girl blinked.

One coastal brother looked offended.

Kael smiled politely.

Ilmere's gaze landed on him. "Mr. North, your file contains a sponsorship irregularity."

Kael's smile didn't move. "I was told the compact office resolved it."

"I was told many things by offices during the war," Ilmere said. "Most were lies printed on quality paper. Bring me your original theory transcripts by sixteen hundred."

"Of course, Professor."

"And stop smiling like a hostage negotiator. This is a school."

Ines made a small delighted sound.

Selene whispered, "Cole chose well."

"Yes," Roan said.

Kael inclined his head with appropriate humility.

He gave nothing away.

Professor Ilmere marched the cohort toward the academic wing.

As Kael passed beneath the overlook, he lifted his gaze.

Not to Roan.

To Selene.

Only for a fraction of a second.

Then he looked away.

Selene's expression didn't change.

Roan's didn't either.

Inside, he moved Selene's threat rating up.

Not because she had done anything wrong. Because Kael had noticed her first.

- break -

Before the scan review, Roan spent forty minutes with the transfer ledger itself.

Cole didn't allow him into the admissions backend. Cole allowed him to sit in a records alcove while Deputy Registrar Senn projected selected screens onto a wall and looked increasingly as if retirement had begun to sound like a tactical extraction.

Senn was a narrow woman with silver-rimmed glasses, a severe bun, and the exhausted authority of someone who had spent twenty years preventing rich families from turning school forms into siege engines. She didn't like Roan. This recommended her.

"You may view," she said. "You may not copy, photograph, transmit, annotate, or ask me to violate compact privacy provisions."

Roan took out his notebook.

She looked at it.

He put the notebook away.

"Good," Senn said.

Drogan, seated near the door, looked amused.

Roan ignored him and memorized instead.

Kael's admissions path had been built with care. The transfer request originated from a northern academy with valid compact accreditation but minimal combat reputation. The sponsorship chain passed through a civic education trust, not House Greel directly. The medical waiver was clean. Too clean. The dorm request didn't ask for proximity to any target. The course selection looked like a normal late-term compromise between available seats and credit equivalencies.

Normality, Roan was beginning to think, should be regulated.

"Who touched the file after submission?" he asked.

Senn's mouth tightened. "That's annotation-adjacent."

"I am asking you to view an audit trail."

"You are asking me to summarize it."

"No. I am asking whether you see anything interesting."

Senn looked at Cole, who stood behind her with the expression of a man having a headache through posture alone.

Cole said, "Answer within policy."

Senn exhaled through her nose and opened the audit tab. "Automated compact validation. External observer certification. Housing assignment request. Medical file confirmation. No human override from Black Spire before headmaster notification."

"No human override from Black Spire," Roan repeated. "External?"

"Compact office."

"Which clerk?"

"That isn't—"

"Visible?"

She paused. "Initials only."

"Initials."

Senn looked as if every syllable cost budget. "K.G."

Drogan stopped looking amused.

Roan didn't move. "Kael Greel countersigned his own transfer?"

"No," Senn said sharply. "The compact office uses staff initials. They aren't unique across all jurisdictions."

"Convenient."

"It's inconvenient to registrars."

"Then I withdraw the insult."

Senn blinked.

Cole's mouth twitched.

Roan studied the dorm assignment. East transfer block, floor three. Cyrus on floor one of the adjacent dormitory, connected by the same dining hall bridge and evening training route. Not too close. Not too far. Exactly as they had discussed. Except the original algorithm had placed Kael in west overflow before a compact-side housing preference adjusted him east.

"Who requested east block?"

Senn stared at the screen. "The trust sponsor."

"What reason?"

"Dietary access."

Drogan said, "Dietary access?"

Senn's voice went flat. "The east hall has northern grain options."

Roan looked at Drogan.

Drogan looked back.

Then Senn said, with deep personal pain, "Yes. I know."

A murderer had moved himself closer to Cyrus through porridge.

Roan added another invisible note to the growing mental file: institutions could be killed through their catering accommodations.

"Can you reverse it?" Drogan asked.

Senn looked at Cole.

Cole shook his head slightly. "If we reverse without cause, Greel sees intervention. Leave it."

Roan agreed, though he disliked agreeing with people who had just informed him about strategic grain. "Flag dining hall routes and equipment rooms between east block and Vale's schedule."

Senn's eyes narrowed. "That sounded like an instruction."

"It was a recommendation with survival benefits."

"I don't take recommendations from students."

"Then take it from the pattern."

She held his gaze for a moment, then opened a security note and typed nothing Roan could see from his angle.

Useful woman. Irritating. The two categories overlapped often.

When the ledger review ended, Senn closed the projection with unnecessary force. "For the record, I dislike this."

"Which part?" Roan asked.

"The part where a transfer file makes me wish students still settled matters with ordinary fistfights."

Drogan stood. "They never did."

"No," Senn said. "But the paperwork was shorter."

Roan left the alcove with three memorized audit points, one new respect for registrars, and a revised estimate of Kael's planning quality.

The boy hadn't merely entered Black Spire.

He had entered its systems first and let his body follow.

- break -

The baseline scan review happened in a locked training room that smelled faintly of burnt dust and student failure.

Cole had pulled the raw ward data within ten minutes of Kael's processing. Not the public readout. The underlying resonance trace. That required either headmaster privilege or a willingness to commit administrative violence. Roan preferred not to ask which.

Drogan stood at the wall with arms folded. Selene sat at the table, tablet open. Ines perched on the edge of a supply crate. Roan studied the projected scan.

Brendt attended through an audio-only channel after making what he described as a legally compelling case for remote participation.

His case had consisted of: *If you don't let me listen, I will ask Ines for bear updates again.*

Roan had allowed audio.

The scan projection showed Kael's Vitalis profile as a layered spiral. The outer shell matched low Forged markers: organ reinforcement, stable internal circulation, controlled output without active injury. Beneath it, however, the trace warped inward around three pressure points.

"Suppression knots," Drogan said.

Cole nodded. "Likely."

"Can you prove intent?" Selene asked.

"No," Cole said. "Advanced control techniques aren't illegal. Neither is privacy."

Ines snorted. "Privacy for aristocrats is just concealment with nicer lawyers."

Brendt's voice crackled through the speaker. "I support this statement, though I object to Ines being right while I am lying down."

Roan ignored him.

"What does the actual rank look like?" he asked.

Cole magnified the trace. "If the knots are masking output rather than stabilizing injury, upper Forged at minimum. Crowned possible but unconfirmed."

"Crowned feels too early," Selene said.

"Why?" Roan asked.

"If Greel sends a Crowned assassin as a student and we expose him, the Compact fractures. House Greel would risk too much."

Roan looked at the scan. "Only if exposure can be proven."

Selene didn't like that.

Good. She shouldn't.

Drogan tapped one of the pressure points. "This isn't standard Greel suppression."

Cole looked at him. "You know standard Greel suppression?"

"I know old battlefield versions. This is cleaner."

"Modernized?"

"Or taught by someone older than the modern version."

House Greel.

No one said it. No need.

The Codex Shard pulsed once.

[Northern Crown signature: partial match.]

[Recommend avoiding prolonged direct Vitalis contact.]

Roan's eyes narrowed.

Direct Vitalis contact meant grappling, clash of techniques, barrier contest, or any exchange where Kael could feel the shape of Roan's circulation. Seeing strength was surface work. Kael needed to feel structure.

The Shard was worried about recognition.

Useful.

Annoying.

"Training restrictions?" Cole asked.

"Don't place him in my combat section," Roan said.

Drogan looked surprised.

Roan continued. "He expects proximity. Deny it without looking afraid. Put him in Ilmere's theory load and standard second-year drills. If he wants me, he must route through voluntary contact, challenge systems, or Cyrus."

"Cyrus again," Cole said.

"Yes."

Brendt's voice came through quieter. "How bad is Cyrus?"

Cole hesitated.

That was enough.

"Headmaster," Roan said.

Cole sighed. "Mr. Vale has complied with formal probation. He attends required review sessions. His grades are unstable but recoverable. He has withdrawn from former associates, refused two House counseling recommendations, and filed one appeal regarding tournament disciplinary notation."

"Grounds?" Selene asked.

"Claims excessive reputational harm."

Ines laughed. "He got beaten on camera and appealed reality."

Brendt said, "Ines."

"What? I said it efficiently."

Roan looked at Cole. "Who is near him?"

"Currently? No one reliable."

That was the answer Roan had expected and disliked.

Humiliated students didn't become dangerous because they were alone. They became dangerous because loneliness made the first person who named their pain sound like a prophet.

Kael wouldn't approach Cyrus with threats. He would bring recognition. He would say Roan had cheated. Or that the tournament had humiliated noble blood. Or that Cyrus had been abandoned because people feared his potential. The exact content mattered less than the shape: someone powerful sees you, agrees the world wronged you, and knows how to make them pay.

Cheap poison. Reliable market.

"We need a buffer," Selene said.

"Who?" Ines asked. "Anyone close to Roan makes it worse. Anyone close to House Vale reports upward. Anyone faculty looks like pity."

Silence.

Then Brendt said, "What about Lysa?"

Roan looked at the speaker.

Selene frowned. "Lysa Renn?"

"Cyrus's old team medic," Brendt said. "She quit his circle after the final, but she was the only one he didn't insult publicly. She also hates Roan only a normal amount."

Roan considered. "Define normal."

"Less than murder."

"That's a wide category."

"She's practical," Brendt said. "And she owes me a favor."

"No," Roan said.

Brendt made a tired sound. "You don't get to reject every option involving me knowing people."

"I can reject poor ones."

"Lysa isn't poor. She's not loyal to Cyrus, not loyal to you, and not impressed by House speeches. That makes her usable."

Roan disliked that Brendt had framed it correctly.

Drogan looked at him. "He's right."

"I know."

"Then stop making that face."

"This is my neutral face."

"It isn't."

Cole interlaced his fingers. "I can assign Ms. Renn to peer support review under medical pretext. Quietly."

Roan looked at Brendt's audio channel. "You contact her first. No mention of Greel. Say Cyrus is vulnerable to predatory mentorship."

"Predatory mentorship," Brendt repeated. "That's the most academic phrase for assassination grooming I've ever heard."

"Good. Use it."

"I am starting to worry you value my social instincts."

"Don't become expensive."

"Too late. Medical bills."

Roan ended the audio before Brendt could enjoy that.

Selene watched him. "You trust him with this."

"Yes."

"Without a written plan?"

"He's the plan."

For some reason, that silenced her.

Ines looked between them and wisely said nothing, which meant she would weaponize it later.

Cole dismissed them with instructions to report any contact between Kael and Cyrus immediately. Drogan stayed behind, likely to argue about security measures Cole couldn't publicly implement.

Roan left with Selene and Ines.

In the corridor, Selene said, "Kael looked at me."

"Yes."

"You noticed."

"Yes."

"Why?"

"Possibilities: he recognized House Ardent involvement in Red Orchard, he knows you are investigating, he intends to pressure you, or he wanted me to notice that he noticed."

"That last one is unpleasant."

"Yes."

Ines stretched. "He may also think you are pretty."

Selene looked at her.

"What?" Ines said. "Assassins can multitask."

Roan said, "Irrelevant unless he weaponizes it."

Selene's expression went very flat. "Comforting."

"I wasn't comforting you."

"I noticed."

They reached the main corridor overlook just as Professor Ilmere's transfer cohort crossed the courtyard below.

Kael walked in the middle, carrying his modest duffel, listening politely while one of the coastal brothers spoke too much. Rain fell around the group. Students glanced at them and moved on.

Ordinary.

That was the danger.

Public monsters invited resistance. Ordinary ones rented rooms.

Kael's head tilted slightly.

For one heartbeat, his eyes found the overlook.

This time, he looked at Roan.

No smile. No challenge. No recognition displayed.

Just a glance, clean and measured.

Roan held it.

The Codex Shard went cold.

[Northern Crown vector engaged.]

Kael looked away first.

Not submission.

Timing.

Roan watched him disappear under the archway toward the east dormitories.

Ines exhaled slowly. "That boy isn't low Forged."

"No," Roan said.

Selene closed her tablet. "What now?"

Roan looked toward the dormitory wing where Cyrus Ashford lived alone with a ruined reputation and an appetite someone else had already scented.

"Now," he said, "we let the knife choose its handle."

The first response from Brendt arrived before Roan reached the operations room.

*Ines says Kael smiled at you across the rain like a villain in a student romance serial. Confirm/deny?*

Roan stared at the message while walking.

Then he typed: *Deny genre.*

Brendt replied immediately. *That isn't a denial.*

Selene, beside him, glanced at the slate and made the mistake of looking amused.

"Don't encourage him," Roan said.

"I am not encouraging him. I am observing that he identified the public function of the gesture."

"Ines described it as romance serial villainy."

"Bad vocabulary. Not entirely bad analysis."

Roan slowed under the covered bridge between administrative tower and dormitory wing. Rain struck the warded glass overhead in silver streaks. Below, students moved between evening lectures and dining hall rotations, carrying bags, training cases, and the small private dramas that made them useful cover for larger crimes.

Kael's smile across the rain hadn't been private. It had been placed where enough students could see without understanding. Later, if Roan accused him, those same students would remember civility rebuffed. Polite transfer smiles at tournament champion. Champion stares coldly back. Rumor didn't need truth. It needed a clean image and enough bored witnesses to repeat it.

Roan typed again.

*Collect student rumors about Kael/Roan first impression. Filter terms: rude, cold, arrogant, afraid, jealous.*

Brendt: *You are outsourcing gossip to an injured man.*

Roan: *Yes.*

Brendt: *Good. I am bored.*

A second later: *Dr. Seth says if this affects my pulse rate, she will blame you.*

Roan put the slate away.

Selene looked toward the east dining hall. "You are turning gossip into surveillance."

"Gossip is surveillance with worse grammar."

"Brendt will enjoy that sentence."

"Unfortunately."

Ines, walking ahead of them, said without turning around, "He already sent me three possible rumor maps and one drawing of Kael with a bear."

Selene blinked. "Why a bear?"

"Prior misinformation campaign," Roan said.

"I leave you alone for one afternoon," Selene murmured.

The east dining hall doors opened below. Students poured out into the rain-dim courtyard. Somewhere in that flow, Cyrus Ashford would soon become visible to Kael in exactly the right accidental ways: same corridor, same training slots, same dining rotation justified by northern grain preferences and administrative cowardice.

Roan had grown up in streets where everyone could see the knife and no one intervened. Visibility wasn't safety. It was only evidence after the wound.

He didn't intend to rely on it.

"Controlled contact with me," he said. "Controlled distance with you. Brendt maps rumor. Ines maps logistics. Cole maps records. Drogan watches exits."

Selene looked at him. "And Cyrus?"

Roan watched the dining hall doors swing shut. "Cyrus chooses whether he's a person or a handle."

"That's a brutal way to phrase it."

"Yes."

"Is it fair?"

"No."

She accepted that with visible difficulty. Good. Anyone who accepted unfairness easily tended to create more of it.

They reached the dormitory entrance as Roan's slate chimed again.

Brendt: *Early rumor sample says you looked like you were planning murder.*

Roan typed: *Inaccurate. Planning was earlier.*

Brendt: *I am telling Dr. Seth your pulse rate is worse than mine.*

Roan muted him again.

Operational necessity could look like friendship if viewed from an unhelpful angle.
