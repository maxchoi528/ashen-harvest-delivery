Chapter 133: The Tournament Ends

Eckhart didn't hand Roan the knife immediately.

That was the first sign something was wrong.

The old weaponsmith had spent most of the afternoon pretending to be annoyed by the world in general and Roan in particular. He had muttered about students with tournament prize money and no understanding of maintenance. He had complained about Black Spire issuing procurement vouchers with more restrictions than sense. He had looked at Brendt's proposed shield plates and declared, without looking up, that "whoever designed this wanted you dead but lacked the courage to stab you personally."

That, Roan had decided, was normal.

The silence wasn't.

Eckhart stood behind the long iron counter with both hands wrapped around the weapon's scabbard, his knuckles white against weathered leather. The forge behind him had gone down to a deep red glow, heat breathing through the workshop in slow waves. Iron filings glittered on the floor. Half-finished spearheads hung from hooks overhead, rocking whenever the ventilation fans coughed.

The knife lay between them like a bad legal clause.

Too small to be a sword. Too long to be a proper dagger. Its sheath was matte black and undecorated, then slightly curved near the end. The handle had been wrapped in dark cord that looked plain until Roan noticed the fibers weren't woven around the grip but through it, vanishing into seams so thin they were almost imaginary. The guard was narrow. The pommel carried no gem, no House crest, no maker's mark.

A cheap-looking weapon, if one ignored the way his Codex Shard had been humming against the seventh vertebra of his spine for the last eleven minutes.

Roan didn't reach for it.

That was what Eckhart expected him to do. The old man's eyes had gone too still, waiting for contact, waiting for some reaction. Maybe a flare of Vitalis. Maybe a system prompt. Maybe Roan's hand burning off. All three. Weaponsmiths had strange hobbies.

"Is there a problem?" Roan asked.

Eckhart's mouth twitched. "Boy asks that while standing in my shop after making me work overnight on metal that shouldn't exist."

"You accepted payment."

"I accepted material cost."

"Your mistake."

Brendt, who had spent the last ten minutes examining a rack of armored gloves he couldn't afford, turned around and stared. "Roan."

"What?"

"You're haggling with the man who just made your knife."

"I'm clarifying liability."

Eckhart let out a sound that might've been a laugh if someone had stepped on it first. He looked down at the weapon again. His left thumb rubbed once over the scabbard's mouth, where a faint seam ran in a pattern too precise to be decorative.

Ines Carrow leaned against the wall near the front door, arms folded, boots crossed at the ankle. She had come along because she wanted to "verify procurement quality," which meant she wanted to watch Roan spend money and judge whether his paranoia had improved since becoming famous. She had said so directly. Ines didn't waste much effort pretending to be pleasant.

Drogan hadn't come. He had taken one look at the address Eckhart had written on the requisition slip and told Roan to buy what he needed, avoid unnecessary conversations, and not make the old man angry.

That had put Eckhart in the small category of people Drogan treated as dangerous without trying to kill.

Roan had adjusted accordingly.

"How much do you know?" Roan asked.

Brendt stopped breathing loudly behind him.

Ines's gaze sharpened.

Eckhart didn't look surprised. He looked tired.

"Enough to know that if I answer that question properly, every ward in this workshop starts screaming," the old man said.

There were wards, then. Roan had counted at least nine passive arrays, three visible load-bearing anchors, and a pressure plate behind the counter connected to something under the floor. He had assumed the old man valued his inventory.

Apparently, the shop also valued its secrets.

Roan considered the room again.

One exit behind them. Two windows, reinforced. A trapdoor under a crate near the left wall, likely cellar access. Ventilation shaft too narrow for a person but broad enough for gas. Eckhart's right hand within two inches of a hidden trigger under the counter. Ines carrying two knives and a folded chain garrote. Brendt with no shield yet, which was inconvenient but survivable.

Drogan had told him not to make the old man angry.

Drogan gave bad instructions when sentiment was involved.

Roan placed his left hand on the counter, palm down, several inches from the knife.

"I'm not asking for a biography," he said. "I'm asking whether you are about to become a problem."

Eckhart stared at the hand. Then at Roan.

"Your father had the same charming habit."

The workshop changed.

Not physically. The forge still breathed. The fans still coughed. Brendt still stood half-turned with one glove in his hand, caught between panic and the deep, helpless curiosity of someone watching a cliff edge approach.

But every possible line of conversation narrowed.

Roan didn't move.

His father's projection had stood in his dorm room seven nights ago, translucent and incomplete, and used thirteen words as if rationing them from a dying world.

I'm sorry. I'm proud. Don't become me.

Roan had sat for three hours afterward and done nothing useful.

This was worse in a smaller way. A dead man's regret was heavy, but it wasn't actionable. A living man with knowledge could be questioned, leveraged, followed, threatened, bribed, or killed. Information had handles. Grief didn't.

Eckhart had just given Roan a handle.

"Careful," Ines said softly.

It was unclear whom she was warning.

Roan kept his eyes on Eckhart. "You knew Aldric Vance."

The old man's expression tightened at the name. Not fear. Not surprise. Something older and more irritating. Debt, perhaps.

"That was what he called himself at the end."

End. Not when he died. Not while at the Spire. At the end.

Useful.

"You know what he called himself before?"

Eckhart's thumb stopped moving.

A ward near the ceiling clicked.

Very useful.

Roan let the question sit for two seconds, then withdrew it without apology. "Wrong question."

"Very."

"Then answer a safer one."

"Those exist?"

"Why did you forge this at cost?"

Eckhart looked past him, toward the barred windows and the gray Ironholm evening beyond them. The city's skyline was all black glass, steel bridges, and Rift-monitoring towers projecting thin blue pillars into the clouds. Tournament banners still hung from the buildings outside, Roan's name printed on some of them in fonts chosen by marketing departments that deserved budget cuts.

Seven days. It had been exactly seven days since the world watched him break Cyrus Ashford in under seven seconds.

Since then, strangers had offered him sponsorships, training contracts, interviews, House patronage, and one marriage proposal from a minor coastal family that had apparently mistaken his tournament record for a dowry negotiation.

He had refused all of them.

The refusals had taken roughly three minutes of his time. The follow-up commentary hadn't.

*@north_signal_news*: Vance R. Declines sponsorship offers from Vellmark Reinforcements, two Spire-affiliated training consortia, and an unsolicited contract from House Greer. Total declined value estimated at 4.7 million Spire Credits in the first week. No public statement issued.

*@BlackSpireStream*: HE TURNED DOWN A COASTAL HOUSE'S DAUGHTER WITHOUT OPENING THE PACKET I AM UNWELL

*@ArenaTechWatch*: For accuracy, the dowry packet was opened, scanned for warded triggers (none found), and returned to sender with the seal restored. Source: a Spire mailroom clerk who would like a raise.

[Public ledger: visibility — high.]

[Conversion to leverage: pending.]

A public reputation was useful only if it could be converted into leverage. Celebrity was a tax. Every hour spent smiling for cameras was an hour not spent preparing for the people who had killed his father.

And now an old weaponsmith in a warded shop knew something about that father.

Roan was beginning to dislike the knife on principle.

"Because your father paid me in advance," Eckhart said at last.

Brendt blinked. "Paid you for a knife?"

"No." Eckhart slid the weapon across the counter. "Paid me to still be the man who would make it."

That was a better answer than Roan expected.

Annoying.

He looked at the knife but still didn't take it. The Codex Shard pulsed again, once, like a finger tapping bone from the inside.

[Resonant implement detected.]

Roan's face didn't change.

[Name-anchor match: SIDEWOUND.]

That made the old man's choice of name either deeply sentimental or very stupid. Eckhart didn't look stupid. Sentimental, then. Dangerous in another direction.

[Compatibility: 71%.]

[Activation threshold: 1,000 Vitalis.]

[Full awakening threshold: insufficient local authority.]

Local authority. Not insufficient Vitalis. That distinction was going to bother him. He was well past the activation threshold. The real limit would reveal itself under strain.

"What did you put in it?" Roan asked.

"Metal."

"Try again."

"Expensive metal."

Roan looked at him.

Eckhart sighed. "A sliver of void-tempered alloy in the spine. Grave-iron laminate in the edge. Riftglass powder in the bonding layer. A stabilizing channel cut through the tang and seated against the grip. No poison reservoirs. No House signatures. No tracking rune."

"You considered tracking runes?"

"I considered billing you extra for not including them."

Ines smiled slightly.

Roan reached for the knife.

The moment his fingers closed around the grip, the world went sideways by half an inch.

Not visually. The workshop remained where it was. But his Vitalis shifted, pulled into a pattern so clean and narrow that his bones registered it before his mind did. The knife didn't drink from him. Drinking implied appetite. This was more like a lock recognizing a key and deciding, grudgingly, not to bite.

Pain flashed through the tendons in his hand. A heartbeat.

Roan let it happen.

At the edge of perception, the Codex Shard opened.

[Micro-Sidewound contact established.]

[Foreign channel attempting alignment.]

[Invert?]

No.

He didn't think the word. He shaped the refusal through the same inner mechanism he used to restrain Pyrebrand bleedover and Crimson Wake recoil. The impulse to reverse the pain into reinforcement was immediate and tempting. A clean gain. A permanent synchronization boost. Convenient improvement that came with a hook through the ribs.

The Shard withdrew.

Pain remained pain.

Good.

The knife settled.

Brendt released a breath. "Is it supposed to do that?"

"What did you see?" Roan asked.

"Your hand went white."

"Then yes."

Eckhart watched him with an expression Roan couldn't place.

Approval, maybe. Or relief. The distinction mattered less than the information behind it: the old man had expected an automatic inversion. He had expected Roan to let the Shard solve the contact problem for him.

Which meant Eckhart knew enough to anticipate the temptation.

Roan drew the blade.

Sidewound made almost no sound leaving the sheath. The edge looked wrong, not because it glowed or smoked or displayed any of the embarrassing theater House artisans liked to sell to teenagers with rich parents, but because it refused to catch light properly. The workshop lamps slid along the blade and vanished into the central ridge. A faint line ran down the steel, off-center, like a healed scar.

No. Not healed.

Open.

A wound held in place.

Roan angled it toward the forge. The red light bent.

"Ugly," Brendt said.

Eckhart glared at him.

"I mean that respectfully," Brendt added.

"No, you don't," Roan said.

"No, I don't."

Ines stepped closer. "May I?"

"No."

She stopped, then laughed under her breath. "Correct answer."

Roan tested the weight. Light in the hand, but not fast in the ordinary sense. Cheap knives moved quickly because there was nothing in them. Sidewound moved as if it had already chosen a path and expected his wrist to follow. That was useful and dangerous. A weapon with intent trained the user as much as the user trained the weapon.

Drogan would hate it.

Drogan would also recognize it.

Roan sheathed the blade.

"What does it cut?" he asked.

"Vitalis seams," Eckhart said.

Brendt frowned. "Armor?"

"Armor is just the part amateurs can see."

That sounded like Drogan, if Drogan had spent forty years inhaling forge smoke.

Eckhart tapped the counter. "Every body reinforcing technique leaves stress lines. Every barrier has circulation joints. Every Rift creature with a core has a feeding seam. Most weapons hit the surface and ask your strength to do the rest. This one looks for where the target already started breaking."

"Convenient."

"It isn't convenient," Eckhart snapped. "It's precise. There's a difference. Use it like a club and you'll get a very expensive piece of scrap. Force too much Vitalis through it and it will cut the wrong seam."

"Mine?"

"Eventually."

Roan nodded. That made sense. Good weapons had costs. Bad weapons pretended not to.

"What maintenance?"

"Clean it with alcohol after organic contact. No salt. No cheap oils. Don't let anyone below Hardened cycle Vitalis through the grip unless you dislike them. If the central line widens beyond a hair, stop using it and bring it back."

"If I'm inside a Rift?"

"Then stop using it and don't die."

"Useful."

"I'm not your mentor."

"No. Mine gives worse advice with more drinking."

Eckhart snorted. The old man turned away and opened a drawer beneath the counter. Not the trapped one. Different drawer. He removed a flat case wrapped in gray cloth and set it beside the scabbard.

Roan didn't touch that either.

"What is it?"

"The other half of the commission."

"I ordered a knife."

"No," Eckhart said. "You gave me materials, told me your estimated threat profile, and asked what would improve your survival odds in a Tier Three Rift. The knife was the loud answer. This is the quiet one."

He unfolded the cloth.

Inside lay a pendant.

At first glance it looked like a dark metal disk, slightly larger than a coin, threaded through with a chain of dull black links. Its surface bore no House crest, no visible rune without decorative edging. It was plain enough to sell badly in a street market.

The Codex Shard went still.

Not quiet. Still.

That was worse.

Roan looked at Eckhart.

The old man didn't meet his eyes. "Your father wore something similar."

"Similar."

"Not the same. I can't make the same."

"What does it do?"

"Mostly? Nothing."

"People keep giving me expensive objects that mostly do nothing. It's becoming a theme."

"In a direct strike, it spreads impact across your sternum and spine. It won't stop a real blade. It might stop shrapnel. It won't save you from a Crowned practitioner. It may keep a Husk claw from opening your chest."

"That's the advertised function."

Eckhart's jaw worked once.

Roan waited.

The old man finally said, "It's also a signal sink."

Ines's posture changed. Very slightly. Enough.

"For what kind of signal?" she asked.

Eckhart ignored her.

Roan already knew the answer, or part of it. The pendant wasn't interacting with his Vitalis. It was interacting with the absence behind it, the place where the Codex Shard made all ordinary sensing behave like a drunk accountant.

The Shard had gone still because the pendant hadn't knocked.

It had recognized the door and chosen to stand outside.

"Codex," Roan said quietly.

Eckhart closed his eyes for half a second.

There it was.

Brendt looked between them. "What's Codex?"

"A bad word," Roan said.

"Is it one of those bad words where knowing it gets me killed?"

"Eventually."

"Great."

Roan picked up the pendant.

No pain this time. No prompt. No flare.

The chain was cold.

He slipped it over his head and let the disk settle beneath his shirt, against the center of his chest. For a moment nothing happened. Then the pressure along his spine eased by a fraction, as if a background sound he had stopped noticing had been muted.

The Codex Shard pulsed once.

[External anchor detected.]

[Signal bleed reduced: 4%.]

[Compatibility pending.]

[Do not trust old debts.]

Roan almost smiled.

Aldric's old notes had a tone. Dry, inconvenient, often insulting. The Shard's system prompts weren't his father, but they carried enough of him to make annoyance feel inherited.

Eckhart watched his face too closely.

Roan adjusted his collar. "You knew what this would do."

"I hoped."

"Why?"

The old man's gaze dropped to the counter.

Outside, a siren wailed somewhere in the city. Not emergency. Tournament traffic control. Ironholm used sirens for everything because apparently one form of public irritation hadn't been enough.

Eckhart reached under the counter again and produced a small maintenance kit: oil vial, cloth, narrow whetstone, alcohol capsule, replacement grip cord.

"Because thirty years ago," he said, "I made a choice that kept me alive and helped get better men killed."

No one spoke.

Brendt set the glove back on the rack without looking away.

Ines's expression lost all amusement.

Roan placed the kit into his pack. "Aldric?"

"Among others."

"You betrayed him?"

Eckhart's eyes lifted sharply. "No."

That had been too fast.

Roan kept it. Not betrayal, then. Cowardice. Delayed warning. Refusal to help. Failure to hide someone. Something in that family. People liked to separate sins by flavor when consequences had already eaten the meal.

"Then what did you do?" Roan asked.

"I survived."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only one the wards will allow."

The ceiling rune clicked again, louder.

Roan glanced up. "Your shop is listening."

"My shop is the reason I'm alive."

"Sounds inconvenient."

"Being dead is worse."

"Debatable," Roan said. "Depends who handles the estate."

Brendt made a strangled noise that might've been protest. Ines laughed once.

Eckhart didn't.

He leaned forward, both hands on the counter. For the first time since Roan had entered the shop, the old man looked not like a craftsman protecting trade secrets, but like a soldier standing in smoke after the order to retreat had already been given.

"When you go into the Deep Rift," Eckhart said, "don't trust clean ruins."

Roan's attention sharpened.

"Define clean."

"No dust. No bodies. No corrosion where corrosion should be. If a place underground looks maintained, assume it's occupied by something that doesn't understand hospitality."

"Rift creatures?"

"Sometimes."

"Human?"

Eckhart didn't answer.

The wards didn't click either.

Interesting. Silence by choice, not restriction.

Roan placed one credit chit on the counter.

Eckhart frowned. "What is that?"

"One credit."

"I know what it's."

"Then why ask?"

"You already paid."

"No. You gave me a discount based on an old debt. That makes this personal. Personal transactions cause stupid behavior. This converts the pendant advice into paid consultation."

Eckhart stared at the single credit as if Roan had placed a dead rat on his counter.

Brendt looked pained. "Roan, that's the most insulting thing I've ever seen you do, and I watched you call Cyrus a poodle on global broadcast."

"It worked."

"That's not the point."

"It usually is."

Eckhart picked up the credit chit between two fingers. For a moment Roan thought he might throw it. Instead the old man slipped it into his apron pocket.

"Your father did that too."

"Insult craftsmen?"

"Turn sentiment into contracts."

"Then he had sense."

"No," Eckhart said. "He had enemies."

Roan sheathed Sidewound across the small of his back, where it would sit under his jacket without disrupting shoulder movement. The weapon settled more naturally than it should have. He disliked that.

The pendant remained cold against his sternum.

A signal sink. A defensive anchor. A connection to something his father had worn before the end.

Too many functions for a simple object. Too many questions for a shop full of wards.

He wouldn't get more today. Pressing Eckhart risked triggering protections, burning a useful contact, and exposing more of his own ignorance than necessary. Better to leave with the weapon, test it under controlled conditions, and send someone else to watch the shop for three days.

Ines. She liked professional suspicion.

Roan turned toward the door.

"Vance."

He stopped.

Eckhart's voice had dropped so low the forge almost swallowed it.

"Tell your father Eckhart still owes him."

Brendt went very still.

Ines's eyes flicked to Roan.

There were several acceptable responses.

Your debt is to me now. Too aggressive, and not entirely true.

My father is dead. Pointless. Eckhart knew.

Pay it properly next time. Tempting, but cheap.

Roan looked back.

The old man's face had folded inward around the sentence, as if saying it had cost him more than the weapon.

So Roan didn't press. Not because he was kind. Kindness was what people called restraint when they didn't understand the arithmetic.

Eckhart had information. He had guilt. He had made a useful weapon and a useful anchor. A man like that was worth more alive and uncornered, then thinking Roan understood things he hadn't yet confirmed.

Roan inclined his head once.

"I'll pass it along when he starts answering reliably."

Eckhart flinched.

Good. Not cruel. Calibrated.

Roan opened the door. Cold Ironholm air slid into the workshop, carrying the smell of rain and exhaust, plus distant Rift ozone.

Brendt followed first, still looking like he wanted to ask sixteen questions and had wisely chosen to live. Ines came last, pausing at the threshold to glance back at Eckhart.

Outside, the evening had gone the color of old steel. The Spire district rose above them in black tiers, the tournament banners snapping in the wind. Somewhere high overhead, a Rift-monitoring beam flickered once and steadied.

Roan touched the pendant through his shirt.

The Codex Shard remained quiet.

That wasn't comfort. Quiet systems were usually calculating.

Brendt finally broke. "So. Your father knew the terrifying weaponsmith."

Roan touched the pendant once through his shirt.

"And the terrifying weaponsmith owes him."

"Yes."

"And you didn't ask why."

Roan started walking. "He wanted me to ask why."

"Right. Obviously. Silly me."

Ines fell into step on Roan's left. "You're having the shop watched."

"Already decided."

"By whom?"

"You."

She gave him an amused look. "Bold."

"You came because you were bored. Now you're not."

"Payment?"

"Access to my Rift salvage priority list."

"First refusal on materials."

"Second."

"First on non-Codex materials."

Roan glanced at her.

She smiled. "I listen."

That was both inconvenient and useful. Like most competent people.

"Fine," Roan said.

Brendt looked between them. "Did I just watch a surveillance contract happen in eight seconds?"

"Nine," Ines said.

"You hesitated on Codex materials," Roan said.

"I was being polite."

"No, you weren't."

"No," she agreed. "I wasn't."

They walked through the rain-slick street toward the tramline. People recognized Roan now. Heads turned. A pair of first-years in Black Spire jackets whispered as he passed. Someone raised a phone, thought better of it when Ines looked at them, and lowered it.

Fame had made movement more difficult.

The pendant might make sensing less difficult.

Sidewound might make killing more precise.

Eckhart's warning made the upcoming Rift more complicated.

Roan reviewed the variables.

Deep Rift expedition: approved by Drogan, unofficially. Team composition: Drogan, Roan, Brendt, Ines. Objective: controlled exposure to Tier Three environment, salvage acquisition, practical training, investigation of possible Aldric-linked anomalies. Known threats: Husks, Hollowkin traces, unstable architecture, old military wards, rival scavengers, Rift fever, signal distortion, whatever Drogan wasn't saying because mentors apparently confused secrecy with pedagogy.

New variables: Sidewound resonance with Codex. Vance Pendant signal reduction. Eckhart's debt. Clean ruins warning.

Unknown: whether House Greel's recognition at the tournament had accelerated the hostile timeline.

Roan's hand brushed the knife hilt beneath his coat.

The weapon didn't hum.

It waited.

That was better than eagerness. Eager weapons got people killed.

At the tram stop, Brendt leaned closer and lowered his voice. "Are we in trouble?"

Roan watched rain bead on the black rail.

"Yes."

"How much?"

"Enough to justify your shield upgrades."

"That's not comforting."

"I wasn't comforting you."

"Right. My mistake."

The tram arrived with a hiss of brakes and a blue flash of wardlight along its doors. Roan stepped in first. Three passengers recognized him and immediately pretended not to. Better manners than expected.

He took the corner seat, back to the wall, clear view of both exits. Brendt dropped beside him. Ines remained standing, one hand on the overhead rail, already scanning reflections in the darkened window.

Roan closed his eyes.

Not to rest. Rest was a reward for finished work.

He opened the thin inner channel to the Codex Shard and waited.

For three breaths, nothing happened.

Then pale text unfolded behind his eyelids.

[Sidewound registered.]

[Vance Pendant registered.]

[External debt-web detected.]

[Warning: old allies are not equivalent to safe assets.]

Roan's mouth twitched.

No. They weren't.

But they were assets.

For now, that was enough.
