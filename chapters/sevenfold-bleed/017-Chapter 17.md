Chapter 17: The Hoarder's Expansion

The first customer of the morning was a nervous boy with shaking hands and a scholarship intake's hollow cheeks. He laid three unmarked vials on the counter and asked if the old man would buy them back.

Roan watched from two aisles over, pretending to read a maintenance manual for cleaning protocols. The boy's Vitalis reading came in at forty-two. Initiate tier, fresh from the academy entrance exams. He had that particular look, the one kids got when they'd bought something they couldn't afford and needed to recover the credits before their parents noticed.

Old Pell studied the vials through his monocle. "These are the ones. Pyretic Tonic. You drank half of one?"

"Some of it," the boy said. His voice thinned at the end—the sound of a boy suddenly aware of the depth he'd stepped into.

Roan had seen the batch this morning, standard issue from the academy's own dispensary. Pyretic Tonic was basic stuff, used to spike blood temperature for short term strength bursts during endurance training. The academy's version was watered down to hell. Half a vial would give you maybe twenty minutes of elevated heartbeat and nothing else.

The boy's Vitalis reading suggested he'd barely felt it.

Old Pell set the vials down. "I can give you thirty credits for all three."

"Fifteen each?"

"Twenty-five total. And I'm being generous."

The boy hesitated. His eyes went to the door. He was thinking about walking out, finding another buyer, doing the balance on whether his parents would notice the credits missing from his account. But three vials meant he had already tried to sell them once before and failed. He needed this done fast and quiet.

Roan made his decision in four seconds.

He stepped out from behind the aisle. "I'll give you forty."

Old Pell's hand flattened against the counter. The stillness that followed wasn't confusion—it was the kind of pause a man learned when he'd held a rifle too long. The boy flinched.

"I don't need them whole," Roan said. He was already walking toward the counter, pulling his wallet. "Forty for the lot. You can keep one if you want to finish it."

The boy stared at him. Then at Old Pell. Then back at him.

"Why?" the boy asked.

Roan shrugged. "I'm studying alchemy. I need samples."

It wasn't even a good lie. But the boy didn't push. He just took the credits, shoved his hands in his pockets, and walked out without looking back. Smart enough to recognize a decent offer. Not sharp enough to ask why a first-year was throwing forty credits at empty vials.

Old Pell watched the door close. "You're cutting into my margins."

"I'm doing your inventory a favor." Roan pocketed the three vials. "Pyretic Tonic is base-catalyst for Bloodscourge derivative work. You haven't stocked any in three months."

"I haven't had demand."

"I have demand."

Old Pell studied him through that monocle. The old man's Vitalis reading was somewhere around six hundred. Tempered tier, had been for decades, and showed no signs of breaking through to Hardened. Old eyes, old blood, old instincts. "You got a supplier now, boy?"

"That's my business."

"It's my shop. My rules."

Roan set the vials on the counter. "Pyretic Tonic. Standard academy dilution. Half-life of forty minutes. The residue in these vials has a Sidewound profile of approximately eight hours if you run it through a secondary extraction."

Old Pell's monocle stayed level. His thumb pressed against the counter's edge—a deliberate pressure, not a twitch. "Go on."

"The residue." Roan tapped the nearest vial. "When students drink half a dose and stop, they think they're saving money. They don't realize the body processes the catalyst in real time. Half a dose means half the filtering time. The blood picks up metabolic byproducts that bind to the compound. By the time they stop, there's roughly eight hours of side effect material sitting in the remaining liquid."

He paused. Let that sink in.

"I can extract those byproducts. Refine them into something useful. The students throw away maybe two thousand credits worth of usable base-catalyst every month." He pushed the vials forward an inch. "That's the business. That's why I'm cutting into your margins."

Old Pell picked up one vial. Held it to the light. Squinted.

"You're telling me kids are dumping profit down the drain because they don't know how to finish a dose?"

"I'm telling you I can turn their waste into my product." Roan retrieved his wallet. "Keep the credits. Consider it a consulting fee. Next time you get a batch of returns, call me first."

He turned for the door.

"Seventy-twenty."

Roan stopped.

"Seventy for you, thirty for me. On anything I supply. But I want exclusivity on academy returns." Old Pell set the vial down. "You're smart enough to have figured this out already. Which means you're going to find other buyers. I want to know I get first crack."

The arithmetic didn't require much. Fourteen hundred credits a month at seventy percent of two thousand—but that assumed extraction yield held at eighty, which it wouldn't, because the vials came in at different dilution levels. Call it twelve hundred after yield loss. Minus what he'd pay the students, maybe thirty per batch, which was still more than Old Pell would offer. Then subtract lab rent if he wanted to scale. The ledger looked clean on paper, but paper didn't account for the time cost of extraction or the risk that Ashford or someone else would notice the missing vials.

Doable. Not clean. Good enough was usually the most dangerous number in Greycross, but this one had a margin that let him breathe.

"Sixty-five," Roan said. "And I set my own buy rate with the students."

"Sixty-eight. Final offer."

He extended his hand. Old Pell looked at it, then clasped it. Dry, rough, the grip of a man who'd been doing business in Greycross since before Roan was born.

"Deal."

- break -

The academy dispensary ran from noon to two. Roan positioned himself in the corridor outside the alchemy annex at quarter to one, a notebook in hand and the practiced bored expression of a student killing time between classes.

The first batch came easy. A girl selling her afternoon's worth of Hollowmend Pills because she'd bought a better grade from a private supplier. She wanted sixty credits for four pills. Roan offered eighty. She blinked, said yes, and walked away before he could explain why.

The second batch was harder. A senior student who'd gotten ahold of something called Ironbile Resin, heavy-grade stimulant used for post-competition recovery. The side effects were documented and ugly, well known enough that the academy had a standing policy against selling it on campus.

Roan bought the vial anyway. His Codex Shard pulsed against his spine, three times, quick, recognizing the compound profile.

[Sidewound pattern logged.]

[Pattern: Ironbile Cycling. Yield: hepatic scarring.]

He kept his face neutral. The old math said Ironbile Resin was profitable because the academy's recovery market was starved for anything stronger than the standard-issue stuff. But the new math said something else: if the side effects were hepatic scarring, and if he could Invert them, then the profit margin wasn't just on the resale.

It was on the reversal itself.

He needed to test that. Soon. But not here, not now.

The third customer was the problem.

He came through the dispensary door at one-fifteen, walking with the loose confidence of a second-year who'd never failed an exam and never worried about money. Vitalis reading: eight hundred. Hardened tier. His academy robes had the stitched hem of House Ashford.

Cyrus Ashford.

Roan recognized the name from the rankings. Third in the year-group, which meant nothing on paper but everything in terms of social capital. He'd seen Cyrus in the dining hall exactly once—that was enough. Student who smiled when he shook your hand and remembered your weaknesses for later.

Cyrus wasn't here to sell. His Vitalis reading was too clean, his robes too pressed. He was here to buy.

Roan watched him approach the counter. Watched him request something called Veyrn-Bark Tincture. Watched the dispensary attendant, Old Ferris, sixty-plus, Tempered, shake his head and say the tincture required a medical waiver.

"I'm not asking for a waiver." Cyrus's voice carried. "I'm asking for a purchase order. My family's account is in good standing."

"Your family's account is flagged. You know this."

"My family also funds half the academy's equipment budget."

Old Ferris's expression didn't change. "Then your family can also afford to wait three days for the waiver approval. Which you'll submit. Today. Through proper channels."

Cyrus smiled. The skin around his eyes didn't move. A practiced shape, not an expression.

He turned to leave, and his gaze caught Roan across the corridor.

Roan didn't look away. Didn't adjust his posture. Didn't do any of the things a normal student would do when caught staring at someone above their station. He just held the eye contact for half a second, noted the slight narrowing of Cyrus's pupils—processing, categorizing, filing away—and then looked down at his notebook.

The notebook said nothing. He'd written down the word "pyretic" and then stopped.

Cyrus walked past him without a word.

But Roan felt it anyway: the slight hesitation, the glance back, the recalculation happening behind those clean grey eyes. A Hardened-tier student had just noted a nobody from the year-group below. That wasn't nothing.

That was a variable.

He closed the notebook. Stuffed it in his bag. Walked out through the east stairwell, the one that led away from the Ashford dormitories, and spent the rest of the afternoon running numbers that suddenly looked a lot less clean.

Cyrus Ashford wanted Veyrn-Bark Tincture. That compound wasn't used for recovery. It was used for one thing and one thing only: temporary neural pathway expansion, which let a student brute-force their way through techniques they hadn't fully mastered.

It was also the primary precursor for The Black Rite of Veyrn.

Roan pulled his Codex Shard, turned it over in his palm. The black iron was warm, always warm now, a steady pulse against his skin that he'd started to associate with his own heartbeat.

"Aldric," he said quietly. "You know what this is."

[Sometimes. Not always. — A.V.]

"Black Rite. The one that ate your civilization."

[It didn't eat anything. It was a tool. Tools don't eat. People do.]

He tucked the Shard back under his shirt. The corridor was empty. The afternoon light was going amber through the high windows, and somewhere in the building a bell rang the quarter-hour.

He ran the sequence again. Veyrn-Bark expanded neural pathways—that meant Cyrus was trying to force a technique he couldn't handle. But Black Rite wasn't a technique. It was a ritual. A binding. The codex entry from his father listed it as one of the practices that had hollowed out their civilization from the inside. Not because the ritual was flawed, but because people used it to skip the costs they didn't want to pay.

If Cyrus was chasing Black Rite, he wasn't just ambitious. He was connected to something older than the academy. Something that had already killed one civilization.

Cyrus Ashford was three tiers above him. He had a family name, a funded account, and apparently ambition that made him walk into a dispensary and demand restricted compounds without paperwork.

That wasn't a variable.

That was a threat vector.

Roan pulled out his notebook again. Started a new column. Wrote "Cyrus Ashford" at the top and then sat there for a long time, watching the light shift across the page, running math that had nothing to do with credits.

The figures said he was still three tiers away.

The figures said that didn't matter.

The figures said the first thing he had to do was figure out what Cyrus knew about Veyrn-Bark Tincture, and whether he knew enough to be dangerous, or just enough to be stupid.

The bell rang again. Five o'clock. The academy's public corridors would lock in ninety minutes.

He had time to find out.
