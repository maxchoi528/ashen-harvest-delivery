Chapter 26: The Five Trials Announced

The Iron Spire's third-tier refectory smelled of synthetic protein and institutional regret. Roan slotted himself into a corner table, positioning his back to the wall and his sightlines covering both exits. Old habit. The tray in front of him held three dishes he hadn't ordered, someone had delivered them while he was climbing the stairs, but the proportions were right. Someone knew his preferences.

Someone was playing games.

He catalogued the meal without touching it. Grilled whitefish, raw grain bread, a broth that steamed with ginger and something medicinal. The Ginger-Scallion Vitalis Broth, specifically. A common enough restorative, sold in every dormitory vending unit. But prepared at this concentration, with this particular blend? Someone had paid attention to his recent training logs.

Brendt slid into the seat across from him without invitation. His roommate had acquired a black eye overnight and wore it like an apology.

"You weren't in the room," Brendt said. "Couldn't sleep. Figured you were—"

"Training." Roan pushed one of the unmarked dishes toward his roommate. "Eat. You look like you lost a fight with gravity."

"Theon Mercer." Brendt picked up the bread, tore it without looking. "Junior year. Cornered me after curfew in the east corridor. Wanted to know why a Tempered-rank freshman had authorization to requisition Hollow Marrow Elixir."

Roan's fork stopped halfway to his mouth.

Hollow Marrow Elixir. A Tier-2 catalyst, restricted to Hardened-rank students and above. He'd filed the requisition under a forged authorization code, one he'd generated three days ago and scheduled for automatic deletion. Someone had traced it anyway. Someone had talked to Theon Mercer. And someone had sicced Theon Mercer onto Brendt before coming for Roan directly.

"Theon Mercer," Roan repeated. The name matched a face in his memory: lean, sharp-jawed, a permanent expression of someone who'd been told he was talented and believed it absolutely. "What's his angle?"

"He's part of the Ashford contingent." Brendt chewed, swallowed. "Got a scholarship. Plays the loyal soldier for House Ashford's junior representatives. Heard he's angling for a permanent spot in their training circles once he hits Hardened."

House Ashford. Cyrus Ashford's family. The same house that had funded Garrick's expedition into the Hollow Reaches. The same house whose heir Roan had humiliated in front of the entire first-year cohort during orientation.

Cyrus hadn't forgotten. He'd been patient.

Roan set down his fork. "What exactly did you tell him?"

"Nothing." Brendt's jaw tightened, defensive reflex, not deception. "He asked. I said I didn't know what he was talking about. He hit me. I kept saying I didn't know."

Reasonable. Brendt was many things, but disloyal wasn't one of them.

"He'll come for me next," Roan said. "During afternoon combat drills. Standard escalation pattern, establish dominance through the friend, then challenge the target directly."

"So we skip drills."

"No." The word came out harder than intended. "Running marks you. Makes you prey." He picked up the fork again, speared a piece of fish. "We go to drills. We do everything exactly as scheduled. And we let them think they're in control."

Brendt stared at him. "That's insane."

"That's strategy." Roan chewed, swallowed, calculated. "Theon Mercer isn't the threat. He's a messenger. A pressure tactic. The question is what House Ashford wants."

The broth was good. The medicinal kick was clean, slightly sweet underneath the ginger heat. Whoever had prepared this knew what they were doing.

"They want the elixir, obviously," Brendt said. "That's a Tier-2 catalyst. It's worth, what, eight thousand credits on the open market?"

"Twelve thousand, if you know the right buyers." Roan finished the broth, set the bowl down. "But that's not it. If House Ashford wanted to confiscate contraband, they'd have gone through the Sentinel Bureau directly. They sent Mercer to rough up a freshman instead. Which means they want something other than the elixir itself."

"Which is?"

"Information." Roan's fingers drummed once against the table. "They want to know how a Tempered-rank student obtained authorization codes for Hardened-rank requisitions. They want to know who helped him. They want to know if there's a pattern, a network, a source, something they can leverage."

Oh.

Oh, that was elegant.

House Ashford wasn't trying to punish Roan. They were trying to recruit him. Or failing that, discredit him in a way that made him dependent on their protection. Rough up the friend first, demonstrate reach. Then offer the target a way out, conditional on cooperation.

Cyrus Ashford had grown a brain since orientation. That was inconvenient.

"There's a complication," Brendt said. "Mercer mentioned something else. Said the Ashford representatives want to meet you. Formally. Tonight, after dinner."

"Formal meeting means witnesses. Witnesses mean they're building a case for something." Roan pushed the empty bowl aside. "What did they say, exactly?"

"Wanted you at the east courtyard. Third bell. Said it's about 'academic collaboration' and 'resource sharing.'"

Academic collaboration. Resource sharing. In the Ashford dialect, that translated to: join us or we bury you.

The arithmetic was doing itself now, running scenarios, mapping probability trees. Join the Ashford contingent and gain protection, but lose autonomy. Refuse and face escalating harassment until he broke or got expelled. Fight back and transform a social problem into a legal one, exactly the trap House Ashford was laying.

There was a third option, but it required resources he didn't have yet.

"We need to see Drogan," Roan said. "Now."

The Warden of Practical Combat went by Drogan Hale, though nobody called him that to his face. He occupied a cluttered office in the Black Spire's sub-basement, sandwiched between the weapons vault and a tunnel that led to the academy's private training chambers. The sign on his door read WARDEN HALE. OFFICE HOURS BY APPOINTMENT, which everyone interpreted as a suggestion rather than a rule.

Roan knocked twice, then entered. Brendt followed on his heels.

Drogan sat behind a desk that had seen better decades, his Iron Hand resting on a stack of combat forms. The prosthetic caught the light from a flickering overhead lamp, old design, but functional, the joints still moving with mechanical precision despite the rust creeping along the seams. His real eye studied them with the flat assessment of someone who'd stopped being surprised by anything thirty years ago.

"Vance. Holloway." He didn't stand. "You look like you have a problem."

"We have a problem," Roan agreed. He closed the door behind them. "House Ashford has decided I'm interesting. They're sending proxies to make formal contact tonight. I'm here to ask what the academy's position is on external houses recruiting students during term."

Drogan's expression didn't change, but something shifted in the air, a stillness that hadn't been there before.

"Formal recruitment or informal pressure?"

"Pressure dressed as formal recruitment. They've already roughed up Brendt to establish credibility. The meeting tonight is designed to force a response."

"And you came to me because?"

"Because you're the Warden of Practical Combat, and House Ashford's recruitment tactics fall under your jurisdiction if they involve coercion or disruption of scheduled training." Roan kept his voice level. "I'm not asking for protection. I'm asking for clarification of the rules. Specifically: what constitutes legitimate recruitment versus harassment, and what recourse do I have if an external house violates those boundaries?"

Brendt shifted uncomfortably. Drogan's single eye moved from Roan to Brendt and back.

"You've done your homework," Drogan said finally. "Most students don't know the difference between formal and informal recruitment until they're in too deep to ask."

"Most students don't have enemies who fund expeditions into the Hollow Reaches."

The Iron Hand moved, not threatening, just repositioning. Drogan leaned back in his chair. The only sound was the flickering lamp and the distant hum of the academy's heating system.

"There's a process," Drogan said. "External houses can recruit students, but only through the Office of Student Affairs. Any contact made outside that channel, including 'formal meetings' in off-campus courtyards, technically violates academy policy. Technically."

The word hung in the air, weighted with implication.

"Theoretically," Roan said, "if I documented such an unauthorized contact, I could submit it to the Office of Student Affairs as evidence of harassment."

"Theoretically."

"And if I documented retaliation following such a report, that would strengthen my case."

"It would."

"And if House Ashford attempted to interfere with my training schedule or access to legitimate academic resources in response to my report—"

"You'd have a much bigger problem." Drogan's voice hardened slightly. "Because then it wouldn't be about recruitment anymore. It would be about a recognized house attempting to undermine an enrolled student through institutional channels. That's the thing that triggers a formal inquiry."

Roan nodded slowly. The math was resolving itself.

He had options now. Not good options, the Ashfords would adapt, find new angles, escalate in ways he couldn't predict. But he wasn't trapped. He had leverage, narrow as it was, and leverage was always better than nothing.

"What about the Hollow Marrow Elixir?" Roan asked. "That's the bait they're using. If I can legitimize my requisition retroactively, their angle collapses."

Drogan's eye narrowed. "You want me to approve a Tier-2 catalyst for a Tempered-rank student."

"I want you to tell me what I need to do to make that happen."

Silence. Brendt was holding his breath. The lamp flickered again.

"The Hollow Marrow Elixir is restricted for a reason," Drogan said. "It rebuilds bone marrow, but the process is agonizing. Most students who take it without proper supervision end up with permanent nerve damage or worse. You're asking me to authorize a catalyst that could kill you if mishandled."

"I'm asking you to authorize a catalyst that I already have, that I intend to take regardless of approval, and that will be significantly safer under your supervision than under my own."

Drogan stared at him.

Then, unexpectedly, the corner of his mouth twitched, not a smile, but close.

"Your father had the same talent for making reasonable requests sound like threats." He reached into his desk, pulled out a form. "There's a waiver. You sign it, you acknowledge the risks. I witness the signature, and your requisition becomes legitimate. That also means I become responsible for monitoring your progress."

"Acceptable."

"Don't be hasty." Drogan slid the form across the desk. "The elixir requires a minimum of three days' observation after consumption. You'll be assigned to the medical wing for monitoring. That means no combat drills, no sparring without missions. You sit in a bed and let the marrow rebuild while I make sure you don't die."

Three days in the medical wing. Three days where he couldn't train, couldn't investigate, couldn't prepare for whatever House Ashford was planning next.

Three days where they would think he was running scared.

"That also means," Drogan continued, watching him closely, "that you won't be able to attend that meeting at the east courtyard tonight."

The implication landed like a thrown knife.

Drogan knew. Drogan had known before Roan walked through the door, had seen the situation and had already calculated the same moves Roan was making. The waiver wasn't just about legitimizing the catalyst. It was about buying time, removing Roan from the board before House Ashford could make their play.

And it meant trusting Drogan, as an administrator, and as something more. Someone who'd known Aldric. Someone who might have reasons to protect the son for the father's sake.

Roan picked up the form. Read the first line. Signed at the bottom without reading further.

"Three days," he said.

"Minimum." Drogan witnessed the signature, initialed the margin. "The elixir will be delivered to the medical wing within the hour. You'll be checked in by noon." He stood, and the Iron Hand caught the light again. "Don't make me regret this, Vance."

"I make no promises I can't keep."

Drogan's single eye studied him for a moment longer. Then he turned away, dismissing them both.

The walk back to the dormitory was quiet. Brendt matched his pace, uncharacteristically subdued.

"You're really going to do it," Brendt said finally. "The elixir. You know what they say about the pain."

"I know what they say." Roan turned the corner into the east residential wing. "I also know what they say about students who get expelled for academic fraud versus students who recover from a badly documented catalyst reaction."

"They'll still come after you. Three days isn't that long."

"No." Roan stopped at the dormitory door, keyed his authorization. "But three days is long enough for House Ashford to realize their play failed. Long enough for them to wonder why. And long enough for me to come back with documentation of every violation they committed in the meantime."

Brendt followed him into the room, closed the door behind them.

"And if the elixir kills you?"

Roan considered the question. The elixir would burn through his nervous system, force a false marrow collapse. To a normal body it was a gauntlet of agony with a high failure rate. To the Codex, the collapse was leverage. The Inversion would meet the damage at the point of contact, reverse the degradation into a density his frame could never have achieved through conventional training.

"The elixir won't kill me," Roan said. "The elixir is the cheapest part of this transaction."

Brendt didn't look convinced. He didn't need to be.

Roan crossed to his desk, pulled out a fresh sheet of paper, uncapped his pen.

Brendt watched him write, names, dates, authorization codes, requisition numbers. The skeleton of a complaint, structured for maximum institutional damage.

"What are you doing?"

"Building a case." Roan's pen moved without hesitation. "The Ashfords want to play by Conclave rules? Fine. Let them try playing by academy rules."

The answer came fast, when you stripped away the politics. House Ashford had committed violations. Those violations could be documented. Documentation could be submitted. Submission could trigger an inquiry. Inquiry could expose House Ashford's recruitment methods to institutional scrutiny.

It wouldn't destroy them. Houses like Ashford had survived worse than academic investigations. But it would hurt them. It would cost them resources, prestige, political capital. It would make them think twice before targeting a Tempered-rank student with nothing but a borrowed name and a stack of forged requisitions.

It would buy time. And time was the only resource that mattered.

Roan finished the first page, started on the second.

"Brendt."

"Yeah?"

"I need you to do something for me. Tonight, after third bell."

"What?"

Roan looked up. His roommate's face was pale, bruised, determined, the expression of someone who'd already decided to see this through regardless of personal cost.

"I need you to go to the east courtyard. Not to meet them. To watch. Document who shows up, how long they wait, what they do when I don't appear. Then come back here and write down everything you saw."

Brendt swallowed. "They'll know I was there."

"They'll know someone was there. That's different." Roan's pen resumed its movement. "The difference between 'witness' and 'target.' If they hurt you again, it confirms a pattern. A pattern gives me more to work with. So really, the safer you stay, the more dangerous you become to them."

"That's twisted."

"That's logic."

Brendt was quiet for a moment. Then: "You really think this will work?"

"I think," Roan said, setting down his pen, "that House Ashford has been operating under the assumption that I'm alone. That I have no allies, no resources without institutional support. They're wrong on all three counts. I have you. I have Drogan. And I have enough documentation to make their lives complicated if they push too hard."

He stood, crossed to the window, looked out at the Spire's lower ring. The Black Spire rose in the distance, a black needle against the pale sky.

"They're not used to being challenged," Roan continued. "House Ashford has been riding their name for three generations. They've forgotten what it feels like to fight someone who doesn't care about their reputation."

"Or someone who cares more about winning than about looking good doing it."

"Exactly." Roan turned back. "That's the edge. They want respect. I want results. Respect is fragile. Results are bulletproof."

Brendt nodded slowly, the logic settling into place.

"Okay," he said. "I watch the courtyard. I document. I come back. What happens if they try to talk to me?"

"They won't. Not yet. They want me, not you. You're the leverage, not the target." Roan picked up his bag. "When they realize I'm not coming, they'll try to figure out why. That's when they'll send someone to check on my room. You should be gone by then."

"Gone where?"

"The library. The main branch, not the academy wing. Stay in the public sections. Read something boring. Look like you're killing time, not hiding."

Brendt's mouth twitched. "Kill time. Read something boring. I can do that."

"Good."

Roan checked the time again. Three hours and forty minutes until the medical wing check-in. Three hours and forty minutes until he surrendered the visible part of his control to Drogan and the academy's schedule.

Three hours and forty minutes to finish building his case, secure his dead drops, and make sure that when House Ashford came looking for him they found a locked room and a paper trail that led directly back to their own front door.

The Codex Shard pulsed against his sternum, patient and steady.

[Sidewound status: negligible.]

He was entering the elixir's domain now. The Hollow Marrow Elixir would burn through his nervous system, force a false collapse through his marrow. To a normal body it was a gauntlet of agony with a high failure rate. To the Codex, the collapse was a fixed transaction. The Inversion would meet the damage at the point of contact, reverse the degradation into a density his frame could never have achieved through conventional training. The outcome wasn't a gamble. It was the price of the next step.

Tonight, the Ashfords would learn that Roan Vance didn't show for meetings he hadn't agreed to attend.

Tomorrow, the Warden's office would file the documentation of a Tempered student surviving a Tier-2 catalyst under supervised observation.

And in three days, when he walked out of the medical wing with rebuilt marrow, a formal complaint in motion, and an Inversion that had turned their pressure tactic into a permanent upgrade, House Ashford would understand exactly what kind of arithmetic he was running.

The variables were stable. The solution just needed time to settle.
