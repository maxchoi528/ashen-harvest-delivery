Chapter 168: The Father Case

Roan discovered, four hours after placing Selene Ardent under Protected and Useful, then Watched, that those three columns were already insufficient.

The problem wasn't Selene herself. Selene had behaved with the irritating competence that made her useful in the first place. She hadn't sent messages through House channels. She hadn't asked follow-up questions in writing. She hadn't tried to prove sincerity with theatrical declarations about loyalty, truth, or justice. By morning, the only evidence of the balcony conversation was a single card delivered to Roan's rented operations room by a student courier who looked nervous enough to be innocent.

The card contained three lines.

*Public archive access granted under Red Orchard liability review.*

*House Ardent central repository, west annex, fourteen hundred.*

*Bring no weapons they can see.*

Roan considered the last line for a full second.

Then he placed Sidewound into the concealed sheath at the back of his belt and selected two additional knives that were shorter and flatter, and therefore less likely to be seen by people who thought visible danger was the only kind worth counting.

Brendt watched from the operations couch with the expression of a man trying to disapprove while wrapped in two medical blankets and holding a mug of broth.

"You are going to the House Ardent archive," Brendt said.

"Yes."

"With Selene."

"Yes."

"After telling her you might kill members of her family if they helped murder your father."

"Accurate summary."

"And you are bringing knives."

"Also accurate."

Brendt stared into his broth, hoping it would contain another friend. "I want you to understand that most people would consider this a delicate social situation."

"It is."

"You selected knives for it."

"I selected small knives."

"That isn't the distinction you think it is."

Roan checked the fastening on his sleeve. The Vance Pendant rested against his sternum, cold but not pulsing. Since returning from the Deep Rift, its behavior had become more variable. Sometimes it muted the Codex Shard enough that Roan could almost forget the artifact in his spine was listening. Sometimes it went cold for no visible reason and made him reconsider every person in a thirty-foot radius.

This morning it was quiet.

Quiet was useful. Quiet was also never proof of safety.

He looked at Brendt. "If I don't bring weapons, they learn that House invitation protocols can make me disarm. Bad precedent."

"If you bring too many weapons, they learn you expect to stab them."

"I don't expect to stab them."

"That sounded reassuring."

"I expect to avoid stabbing them because stabbing House archivists rarely improves access."

"There it is."

Brendt shifted against the pillow and grimaced. The movement was small, but Roan noted the delayed breath afterward. Ribs still unstable under twisting strain. Brendt had improved enough to resent bed rest, which wasn't the same as being recovered. A fact Brendt knew and ignored whenever possible because being sensible apparently caused him spiritual pain.

Roan took the mug from him.

"Medical limit," he said.

"That's broth, not a tablet."

"You are using it to stay awake."

"I am drinking."

"You are performing wakefulness at broth."

Brendt opened his mouth, closed it, then pointed at him. "That sentence was terrible."

"Sleep."

"I am part of operations."

"You are part of upholstery until your ribs stop threatening independence."

Brendt smiled despite himself. "You stole my phrase."

"It was useful."

"That means you liked it."

"It means I stole it."

"Roan."

The name came out differently that time. Less joking. More careful.

Roan stopped at the door.

Brendt looked down at the blankets, then back up. "Selene is walking into her own family's machinery because of you."

"Because of Red Orchard."

"Because of you," Brendt said again. "Red Orchard gave her a reason. You gave her a direction."

Roan said nothing.

Brendt had a talent for making statements that weren't tactically useful but were still difficult to dismiss. It was one of his worst habits, after self-sacrifice and optimism.

"She chose," Roan said.

"I know." Brendt's fingers tightened around the blanket edge. "That doesn't lift your share of it. It just makes the carry harder."

Weight. Another word people measured imprecisely.

If Selene was killed because Roan withheld information she needed, that was his failure. If she was killed because she chose to investigate House Ardent's old sins and underestimated her own family, that was partly hers. If House Ardent killed her because old men preferred buried crimes to living heirs, that would be theirs, and Roan would treat it accordingly.

The arithmetic wasn't clean.

Most real arithmetic wasn't.

"I have contingency paths," Roan said.

Brendt looked at him for a long second. "For extracting her?"

"Yes."

"And for avenging her?"

Roan didn't answer.

Brendt nodded once. That had been answer enough. He leaned back. "Try extraction first."

Roan opened the door. "Sleep first."

"Terrible friend," Brendt murmured.

"Alive friend."

"That too."

Roan left before Brendt could turn the exchange into a conversation.

- break -

House Ardent didn't place its central repository in a castle.

That was almost disappointing.

The west annex occupied the lower five floors of a smooth white tower on the edge of Ironholm's old administrative district, a building of glass, pale stone, and carefully maintained restraint. It had no banners across the front, no gold lions above the doors, no ancestral statues glaring at visitors with the smug patience of inherited violence. The only visible sign was a narrow silver plaque beside the entrance.

*ARDENT CIVIC FOUNDATION. HISTORICAL RECORDS AND LEGAL REVIEW.*

Roan disliked it immediately.

Gaudy buildings announced ego. Minimalist buildings announced budget discipline, professional security, and confidence that everyone important already knew the name on the plaque. They were worse.

Selene waited inside the first security hall, dressed in a gray coat with Ardent silver at the cuffs and no visible jewelry except a signet ring turned inward against her palm. That small choice mattered. House identity present but not displayed. Family access invoked, not celebrated.

Good.

Two security officers stood behind a curved white desk. Both wore civilian suits tailored to conceal armor. Both had Tempered baseline readings suppressed to administrative levels, which meant either expensive control or expensive implants. Roan assumed both.

Selene's eyes flicked once across him.

"No visible weapons," she said.

"You requested that."

"I also requested restraint."

"I brought that too."

One of the guards failed to hide a reaction.

Selene didn't. "This is Marin Doss and Cale Ardent."

The woman at the desk inclined her head. "Mr. Vance."

Marin Doss. Not related to Doss Logistics by coincidence. Her file, assembled in Roan's head from Guild records and Red Orchard shells, suggested an old service branch with ties to Ardent procurement. Dangerous in the way competent administrators were dangerous: not because they could kill you personally, though sometimes they could, but because they could make paper decide you had never existed.

The man beside her smiled.

Cale Ardent had the family look: pale eyes, clean jaw, posture trained by instructors who thought leaning was a moral failure. Late twenties. Tempered peak or low Hardened, hidden under a civic foundation badge. Cousin, maybe. Not main-line heir. Too polished to be harmless.

"Tournament champion," Cale said. "We were sorry to miss the final in person."

"No, you weren't," Roan said.

Selene closed her eyes briefly.

Cale's smile paused and recalculated, then returned. "Pardon?"

"You would have had priority seating if you wanted it. House Ardent chose not to appear publicly for either finalist after the Vell dispute became unstable. Sensible. But don't call it regret."

For half a second, the security hall was quiet except for the low hum of the scanning arch.

Marin Doss's mouth twitched.

Cale's smile became thinner. "Selene said you were direct."

"She was being polite."

"I was being concise," Selene said.

"That too."

Cale's gaze moved between them, collecting details. The exchange had given him more than Roan liked. Familiarity. Tolerance. A rhythm. People built assumptions from rhythm faster than from declarations.

No help for it now.

Marin tapped the desk. "Access has been granted under Red Orchard liability review. Public archive, sealed index references by request, no removal of materials, no live recording without personal projection crystals. Mr. Vance will be logged as external mission analyst."

"Not consultant?" Roan asked.

"Consultants invoice."

"Correct."

Marin looked at Selene. "You brought him here and didn't warn me enough."

"I warned you accurately."

"No," Marin said. "You warned me legally."

Selene didn't apologize.

Cale gestured toward the scanning arch. "Standard procedure. Place all metal and crystals, plus active foci in the tray."

Roan stepped toward the arch.

It didn't alarm.

Cale blinked.

Selene didn't.

Marin looked at the display, then at Roan's coat. "Interesting."

"Is it?" Roan asked.

"The arch detects weapons."

"No," Roan said. "It detects categories your procurement team listed as weapons."

Marin's expression sharpened into something close to professional appreciation.

Cale's didn't.

"That distinction usually matters only to smugglers," he said.

"And security designers," Roan said.

Selene stepped between the sentence and whatever Cale wanted to do with it. "We are here for archive references. Not a compliance debate."

"Compliance is never merely debate," Cale said.

"No," Selene agreed. "It's also liability. Which is why the visitor has now passed your listed procedures and any discretionary escalation must be justified in writing."

Cale looked at her.

There was family in that look, and not the pleasant kind. A reminder that people who shared blood often learned exactly where to press.

"You are invoking formal process against me?" he asked.

"I am preventing informal process from creating exposure."

"Because of him?"

"Because of Red Orchard."

Good.

She had used the true reason without giving the deeper one. Roan adjusted her classification upward by a margin too small to deserve a new column.

Cale held her gaze, then turned aside with a controlled smile. "Of course."

No one in the room believed him.

They entered the archive.

- break -

The public archive smelled of paper and stone, with the quiet scent of money spent to make age look dignified instead of inconvenient.

Rows of shelves stretched under white light. Glass reading rooms lined the inner wall. At the far end, sealed lifts led downward, each marked by a silver line and a security sigil. Not public, then. Public archive above. Real archive below. Obvious enough that only institutions could pretend otherwise.

Selene signed in at a terminal. Marin Doss accompanied them, which was expected. Cale didn't, which was worse.

People who disappeared from view after being annoyed tended to reappear as complications.

Roan took the first reading table with his back to a wall and sight lines to both exits.

Selene noticed and chose the chair facing him. "We have two hours before someone asks why the Red Orchard liability review requires pre-Unification field records."

"Someone already asked."

"Cale doesn't count."

"Why?"

"Because he asks questions to establish hierarchy, not acquire answers."

Roan nodded. "Annoying."

"Yes."

"Useful?"

"Sometimes. Unfortunately."

Marin placed three flat archive slates on the table. "Public index references for Red Orchard, Doss Logistics, Ardent Foundation rural grants, and dissolved shell trusts. Anything sealed must be requested by collection code."

"That's the problem," Roan said. "We don't have collection codes."

Marin looked at him. "Then you will enjoy the public archive."

"Unlikely."

Selene tapped one slate awake. "We aren't requesting sealed contents. We are requesting metadata references from index-level catalogs."

Marin's eyes moved to her. "That distinction is legally accurate."

"You disapprove?" Selene asked.

"I admire it. I also recognize when someone has read rules specifically to walk along their edge."

Selene didn't look at Roan.

Roan didn't smile.

Marin lowered her voice. "Red Orchard was ugly, Selene. If this is a family cleanup, keep it clean. If it's something else, decide early whether you want protection or deniability. You may not get both."

Selene's hand stilled on the slate.

Roan studied Marin again.

Not hostile. Not loyal in the expected way. Concerned, perhaps, though concern inside House infrastructure usually came wrapped around ten hidden priorities.

Selene said, "Noted."

Marin left them with the slates.

For the next forty minutes, they worked without speaking except for necessary terms.

Roan liked archives more than he liked most people. Archives lied, of course. They omitted. They misfiled. They sanitized murder into "incident," bribery into "facilitation," and failed experiments into "limited adverse outcomes." But they lied with structure. Once a person learned the structure, absence became legible.

Red Orchard appeared twenty-seven times in public records. Most references involved agricultural grants, soil rehabilitation, public health screenings, and a discontinued youth education initiative. None mentioned buried stone, Rift roots, or Hollow residue.

Expected.

Doss Logistics appeared everywhere, usually as transport provider, occasionally as emergency contractor. Its old filings were too clean. No organization handling rural medical supply chains for sixty years had that few irregularities unless someone had burned them or everyone involved was dead and boring.

Roan doubted the second.

Selene found the first useful fracture.

"Here," she said.

Roan leaned closer.

The slate showed a funding pathway from Ardent Civic Foundation to a shell trust, then to a Red Orchard "soil vitality study" fifty-eight years ago. The trust had dissolved twenty-nine years ago. They already knew that much.

But the dissolution order referenced a legal review file.

File code: NC-14 / provisional crown matter.

Roan looked at the letters.

NC.

Northern Crown. Coincidence.

Coincidence was what lazy investigators called a pattern before billing ended.

"Collection?" he asked.

Selene checked. "Sealed."

"By whom?"

Her fingers moved across the slate. "Not listed in public metadata."

"Request index-level authority chain."

"That may flag."

"Already flagged."

She considered, then submitted the query.

The slate hesitated.

Not loading. Hesitating.

A small red line appeared.

*INDEX AUTHORITY CHAIN RESTRICTED. HOUSE SECURITY REVIEW.*

Selene's jaw tightened.

Roan looked toward the lifts at the far wall. One of the silver sigils had changed brightness by a fraction.

Downstairs had noticed.

Good. That meant downstairs existed in the relevant chain.

Bad. Same reason.

Selene withdrew the query before it could lock. "We have confirmation something is there."

"We had that before."

"Now we have a file code."

"And a watcher."

She followed his gaze to the lift.

The silver sigil steadied.

Selene's face remained controlled, but the signet ring turned once under her thumb. "If I push deeper here, Cale intervenes."

"Yes."

"If I don't, the record may disappear."

"Yes."

"What would you do?"

"Depends whether I value the record more than the person holding access."

"That isn't an answer."

"It's the answer."

She understood after a second. "Marin."

"She knows the system. She warned you. She may be reachable."

"She may also report everything."

"Likely."

Selene looked at the slate. "You trust no one."

"Incorrect."

She looked up.

Roan turned the slate toward himself and copied the file code by hand into his notebook. Paper, not digital. "I trust incentives."

For some reason, she laughed softly.

Not amusement exactly. Recognition.

"We need Marin's incentives," she said.

"Yes."

The second fracture came thirteen minutes later.

It wasn't in Red Orchard records. It came from a list of old archive relocation logs, a document no one read unless paid poorly or motivated by revenge.

Seventy-one years ago, during a period labeled *post-breach municipal stabilization*, House Ardent had transferred several sealed field caches from temporary custody to "northern adjudication." Most cache names were code strings. One wasn't.

VANCE, A. Wound-line artifact correspondence.

Selene went still.

Roan felt the Codex Shard pulse against his spine.

The Vance Pendant went cold enough to hurt.

[Archive lineage reference detected.]

[Signal discipline recommended.]

Roan exhaled once through his nose and did nothing else.

Selene looked at him. "A. Vance."

"Yes."

"Could be another Vance."

"Yes."

"You don't think so."

"No."

The relocation log listed destination authority: Northern Crown provisional review, with receiving witness signatures redacted. The date was wrong for Aldric as Roan understood him. Seventy-one years. Too early if Aldric had been merely his father, a man who had married Mira and left behind a son and daughter.

But Aldric had never been merely that.

The Deep Rift temple had already broken normal timelines. The projection hadn't explained enough. The Codex Shard carried old prompts, old lineage markers, old debts. Seventy-one years wasn't impossible. It was inconvenient.

Selene watched him carefully. "This changes something."

"It confirms something."

"What?"

"That my father's history entered House records before he should have existed in them."

She absorbed that.

To her credit, she didn't ask the obvious question.

Instead, she asked, "Can I know why that's possible?"

"No."

"That was fast."

"It was an easy no."

"Because it endangers me?"

"Because it endangers me."

That honesty cost less than a lie and more than silence.

Selene nodded slowly. "Fair."

The word had become a strange bridge between them. Not warm or soft, but weight-bearing.

Roan copied the relocation string.

The archive slate flickered.

For one second, the VANCE entry blurred.

Then it disappeared.

Selene's hand snapped to the slate. "No."

Roan's pen had already stopped moving.

He had the code.

Not the full entry, but enough.

Selene stared at the blank space where the line had been. "They deleted it while we were reading."

"Or hid it."

"That isn't better."

"No."

Across the archive, the sealed lift opened.

Cale Ardent stepped out.

He walked toward them with two security officers and the smile of a man who had found process and intended to use it as a knife.

"Selene," he said. "There appears to have been an unauthorized query."

Roan closed his notebook.

Selene didn't stand. "Then your system should file a report."

"It has."

"Good."

Cale's eyes moved to Roan's notebook. "External analysts aren't permitted handwritten extraction of archive metadata."

Marin hadn't said that.

Roan looked at Selene.

Selene looked at Cale. "Cite policy."

Cale's smile thinned.

Beautiful.

Not emotionally. Procedurally.

Cale had moved too early and without Marin. He had authority, but not clean authority. Selene had forced him to name the rule. If he couldn't, every escalation became personal interference with a liability review.

Cale said, "House security may restrict—"

"Cite policy," Selene repeated.

The nearby archivists pretended not to listen with the intensity of people who would remember everything.

Cale's eyes cooled. "You are making a mistake."

"No," Selene said. "I am documenting one."

Roan stood.

Both security officers shifted.

He didn't reach for any weapon. That would have been rude and unnecessary.

Instead, he placed a single credit chit on the table.

Cale looked at it despite himself.

"Copying fee," Roan said.

Selene's mouth tightened in a way that suggested she was either suppressing anger or laughter. Both.

Cale didn't appreciate the gesture. "You think this is amusing?"

"No."

"That wasn't convincing."

"I don't care."

Cale stepped closer. "You are in a House facility, Mr. Vance. Tournament applause doesn't follow you here."

Roan met his eyes. "Good. Applause is noisy."

Cale wanted to order the guards forward.

Then Marin Doss appeared behind him.

"Cale," she said, pleasant and deadly. "You are obstructing a logged review without written security basis."

Cale didn't turn. "Marin."

"You may either produce the policy Selene requested, or you may leave my archive."

*My archive.*

There were the incentives.

Cale turned enough to look at her. "Your archive exists under House authority."

"And survives under legal defensibility." Marin smiled. "Try not to confuse the two in front of witnesses."

Silence spread.

Cale's face smoothed. "Of course."

He looked back at Roan. "Enjoy your review."

"I won't."

Cale left with the guards.

The lift closed behind him.

Marin approached the table and picked up the single credit chit. "This isn't our copying fee."

"Then keep the change."

"It's one credit."

"I know."

She looked at Selene. "Your analyst is a problem."

"Yes," Selene said.

"Useful?"

"Yes."

"Unfortunate."

Marin pocketed the credit, which told Roan more than several speeches would have.

Then she lowered her voice.

"You have two minutes. Write nothing else. Ask nothing else. Leave separately."

Selene went very still. "Marin."

"No. You wanted to know whether the house has old rot under the floorboards. It does. Now decide whether you are lifting boards or burning the room. Those are different operations."

Roan studied her.

Marin looked at him directly. "And you. If you drag her into a private war and call it truth, I will become tedious."

"Define tedious."

"No."

Wise woman.

She turned and walked away.

Selene stared at the blank archive slate.

Roan placed the notebook inside his coat.

After a long moment, she said, "I will check the family vault."

"No."

She looked up.

"Not yet," Roan said. "They know you're looking. Your next move is being predicted."

"My next move is expected to be retreat."

"By Cale, maybe. Not by Marin."

Selene's eyes narrowed slightly. "You think Marin wants me to continue."

"I think Marin wants you to choose a method before you make her choose a side."

"That isn't the same thing."

"No."

She looked toward the sealed lifts. "I can get an internal map."

"Don't steal it through House systems."

"I know."

"Don't use a servant."

"I know."

"Don't ask a cousin who dislikes Cale unless you want Cale informed in an hour."

She gave him a flat look. "I said I know."

"Good."

"Do you do this to Brendt?"

"Yes."

"Does he tolerate it?"

"He complains."

"I may borrow that."

"Expected."

Selene stood. Her face had become very calm, which meant the anger had gone somewhere productive.

"I said I would check old archives," she said. "I meant it."

"I know."

"No," she said. "You calculated it."

Roan paused.

A fair correction.

Selene buttoned her coat. "You gave me limited trust. I am giving you limited access. If I find something that proves House Ardent helped kill your father, you hear it from me before anyone else weaponizes it."

"And if it proves they didn't?"

"Then you hear that too."

Fair.

Roan nodded once.

They left separately.

Outside, rain had turned the white tower's reflection into a pale wound across the pavement.

Roan walked three blocks before turning into an alley, checking for surveillance, and opening his notebook beneath the cover of a dead awning.

NC-14. Northern Crown.

VANCE, A. Wound-line artifact correspondence.

Entry removed during live access.

Cale hostile. Marin useful but unclassified.

He added one more line.

House Ardent isn't a wall. It's a room with people fighting over the locks.

The Codex Shard pulsed once.

[Archive thread established.]

[Warning: Northern Crown proximity increasing.]

Roan looked north through the rain.

Somewhere beyond the city, old crowns were moving.
