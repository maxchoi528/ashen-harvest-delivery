Chapter 34: Selene Ardent

The Five Trials were behind him. He had passed. Now came the first real month of the Spire grind. The training yard at Black Spire had four zones, and Roan had mapped each one.

Zone One held the initiates doing basic forms, slow, deliberate, embarrassing. Zone Two housed the tempering-phase students running combat drills under instructor supervision. Zone Three was reserved for hardened-phase warriors running live exercises. Zone Four existed on no official schedule, but Drogan had given him a key and told him to use it before dawn or after midnight.

He chose after midnight.

The forge yard's heat hit first. Then the smell, ore, charcoal, the faint copper tang of quenched steel. Old Pell's workstation occupied the far corner, and the old man was already there, hunched over a half-finished blade with a grinder that screamed through the silence like a dying thing.

"You're late," Pell said without looking up.

"Two minutes under acceptable threshold."

"Threshold you invented."

"Mathematics doesn't require consensus."

Pell made a sound that might've been a laugh. "Your father said the same thing. Sit down. We have work.". The work was metallurgy.

Not combat, not technique refinement, not the endless loop of forms that most initiates spent their nights grinding through. Pell had sat him down at a workbench and handed him a piece of pig iron and told him to describe it.

"Not the blade. The metal itself. What's it made of. What's wrong with it. What it wants to become."

Roan had answered all three questions in four minutes. The iron contained too much phosphorus, impurity from the ore source. It would make a brittle blade that would shatter on impact. What it wanted was carbon, and a long, slow heat to drive the phosphorus out and allow the grain structure to align properly.

Pell had stared at him, then pulled out a second piece of metal and repeated the exercise. And a third. And a fourth.

By the fifth piece, Roan realized this wasn't metallurgy training.

It was assessment training.

"You're not learning to forge," Pell said, confirming the thought. "You're learning to see. A blade tells you everything it's in the first three seconds of contact. Most fighters don't bother listening. They feel what the blade does and react. You—" The old man gestured at him with a pair of tongs. "You want to know why the blade does what it does. That's useful."

"Useful for what?"

"Keeping you alive when better fighters die.". Three weeks of this. Three weeks of midnight sessions with ore samples and finished blades, learning to read the story metal told about its creation, its flaws, its potential. Pell talked less and less as the sessions progressed. By week two, he stopped asking questions and handed Roan a blade, then waited. By week three, Roan was examining new pieces without instruction, running his fingers along the grain, testing the edge with his thumbnail, holding it up to catch the light.

"Stop."

Roan looked up.

Pell was standing over him, arms crossed, expression unreadable. "You found it."

"Found what?"

"The thing your father had. The reason he was dangerous because he could fight, and because he could see what others couldn't." The old man nodded toward the blade in Roan's hands. "That piece had a fold in the metal. Three inches from the tip, running along the spine. Most smiths would miss it. You didn't."

Roan turned the blade, found the fold, traced it with his fingernail. He could feel it now, a slight discontinuity in the metal's structure, invisible to the eye but present to touch. "It's a stress concentration point. Impact here would propagate the crack along the fold line."

"Exactly. Which means—"

"The blade is compromised. Not from damage. From manufacture. Whoever made it didn't control their temperature gradient during fold-over."

Pell picked up the blade, examined it, set it down. "Bought it from a market stall in Greycross. Cost me eight silver. The vendor swore it was forged steel, top quality, worth fifty at least."

"Vendor lied."

"Vendor lied. But you didn't have to take my word for it. Or anyone else's. You determined the truth yourself." Pell sat down on a stool across from Roan, his knees popping audibly. "Your father could do this with people. See the fold lines in their thinking, the stress points in their arguments, the places where they'd crack under pressure. Made him a hell of a negotiator. Also made him a hell of a fighter, because he could see exactly where to apply force to break what others thought was unbreakable."

Roan set the blade down. "You're saying I inherited something."

"I'm saying you've been training your body for six weeks. Your father trained his mind for twenty years before he ever picked up a weapon. You're ahead of schedule."

"By how much?"

Pell considered this. "Maybe six months. Maybe a year. Depends on whether you keep showing up at midnight.". Three days later, Roan walked into the Spire's equipment requisition office during business hours and requested a complete weapons inventory for his student profile.

The clerk, a bored-looking woman with ink-stained fingers, pulled up his file on the board. "Initiate Vance. First-year, Zone One. You're limited to training equipment. No live blades until you pass your tempered-phase certification."

"I understand. I'm requesting a complete inventory list, including discontinued models, workshop surpluses, and instructor overrides."

The clerk's eyebrows rose a millimeter. "That's... Not a standard request."

"I'm aware. I have a research purpose. Materials science correlation with combat applications."

"Uh-huh." She tapped something into the board, waited, shook her head. "Your authorization level is Zone One. I can't pull discontinued inventory without supervisor approval."

"Who's the supervisor?"

"Senior Instructor Marsh. But she's in the eastern wing with the second-years right now, won't be back until after lunch."

Roan checked his internal clock. Lunch was in forty minutes. Forty minutes was acceptable. "I'll wait."

The clerk stared at him for a moment, then shrugged and went back to her previous task. Marsh turned out to be a tall woman with close-cropped grey hair and bearing that suggested she'd spent decades in combat zones before settling into an administrative role. She listened to Roan's request with the patient expression of someone who had heard every possible justification for equipment access and found none of them compelling.

"Complete weapons inventory," she repeated. "Including discontinued models and workshop surpluses. For a first-month initiate."

"Affirmative."

"For materials science research."

"Correlation with combat applications, yes."

Marsh leaned back in her chair. "You're the Vance boy."

"Yes."

"Your father was here. Ten years ago, maybe eleven. Different supervisor then, but I was around. Aldric Vance. Good fighter. Strange methods, but effective. He used to request equipment inventories too."

"Did he get them?"

"Sometimes. Depends on what he was trying to prove." Marsh studied Roan with eyes that had seen enough to develop sharp judgment. "What are you trying to prove?"

"I'm trying to understand what I'm working with. Every weapon tells a story. The better I understand the story, the better I understand how to break—" He caught himself, recalibrated. "How to handle opposition."

"Break. You almost said break."

"Handle opposition," Roan repeated, expression unchanged.

Marsh's lips twitched, not a smile, but something close. "You remind me of him. Same way of talking around things. Fine. I'll approve a partial inventory. Discontinued models and workshop surpluses, but not the full archive. Some of that stuff is classified for security reasons, special issue weapons, experimental pieces, things that belong to specific instructors."

"That works."

"Thirty-minute viewing window. Document what you need by hand, no copying equipment without photographs. After hours without takebacks. Clear?"

"Clear.". The surplus room smelled like oil and old leather.

Shelves lined three walls, stacked with weapons in various states of repair. Training swords, broken practice staves, blades with cracked edges and handles wrapped in faded tape. A few pieces looked genuinely old, not ancient, but old enough to have seen use, been retired, forgotten.

Roan started at the left wall and worked his way methodically through the racks.

Training swords first. Standard pattern, carbon steel, balanced for forms practice. Average weight a little over two pounds, edge retention fair, impact resistance adequate. Nothing special. The Spire used these by the thousand and replaced them when they wore out.

Practice staves. Fiberglass core, polymer coating. Essentially indestructible for training purposes but useless for anything requiring fine edge work. Students used these for learning to manage reach, nothing more.

Then he reached the discontinued models.

These were different. Someone had stopped manufacturing them for reasons Roan could guess at, the handles were non-standard, the balance points shifted in ways that made standard grips awkward. He picked up a short blade with a curved handle, tested the weight distribution, found the center of gravity two inches further forward than expected.

Designed for someone with longer arms. Or for a fighting style that favored draw cuts over thrust work.

He set it down and moved to the next piece.

A longsword with an unusual crossguard, not the standard Spire pattern but something with extended quillons, designed to catch opponent blades. Defensive configuration. Someone had modified this for parrying emphasis.

Then a short axe with a double bit and a grip too short for two-handed use. One-handed axe, but weighted for chopping rather than slashing. Different philosophy than standard combat axes.

Each piece told a story. Each story was a window into how someone had thought about fighting at a time when the Spire's standard curriculum wasn't meeting their needs.

He was taking notes, dimensions, materials, modifications, anything that might be useful, when the door opened behind him.

"Looking for something specific, or just browsing?"

Roan turned.

The man in the doorway was in his thirties, wearing Spire instructor insignia but not the formal uniform. Leather jacket over training clothes, calluses visible on both hands, a scar running from his left temple to his jaw. His posture was relaxed, but something in his eyes suggested he wasn't.

"Instructor Marsh authorized my access," Roan said. "Research purposes."

"Marsh authorized partial access. Most of what's in here isn't research, it's scrap waiting to be melted down." The man stepped into the room, letting the door close behind him. "Though I suppose scrap has its own value, if you know what you're looking for."

"I do."

The instructor walked along the shelves, examining pieces with the casual expertise of someone who'd handled hundreds of weapons. He stopped at a rack near the back, pulled out a blade Roan hadn't examined yet.

"This one's interesting. Spire prototype from fifteen years ago. Reinforced spine, extended grip for two-handed use, blade geometry optimized for thrust work. They made fifty of them, tested them against standard longswords, and discontinued the entire line."

"Why?"

"Too expensive. The manufacturing process took four times as long as standard blades, and the performance improvement was marginal. Better, but not better enough to justify the cost difference." The instructor set the blade on a nearby table. "Most people who come in here look for the flashy stuff. Special editions, experimental pieces, weapons with famous histories. You went straight for the discontinued models. The ones nobody else wants."

"I'm interested in why things don't work. That tells me more than studying things that do."

The instructor studied him for a moment. "You're Vance's kid."

"Yes."

"Thought so. Name's Garrett. I was a junior instructor when your father was here. Didn't work with him directly, but I saw him around." Garrett ran his thumb along the prototype blade's edge. "He used to say something in the common room sometimes. About how every problem was a math problem, and every math problem had a solution if you found the right equation."

"Numbers were harder to bribe than people."

"There it is." Garrett smiled slightly. "He used to say that too. Never thought I'd hear it again." He set the blade down. "You here for a reason, or just curious?"

"Both. I'm trying to understand what I'm working with. The Spire has certain approaches to combat training. I want to know the edges of that approach, what's inside the box, what's outside, and why."

"Most initiates don't ask that question until they're halfway through their second year. Some never ask it at all."

"Most initiates aren't me."

Garrett laughed, a short, sharp sound. "Fair enough. You want my advice? Don't spend too much time in this room. The scrap here is interesting, but it's not the real education. The real education is out there—" He gestured vaguely toward the main campus. "Fighting people. Losing. Winning. Figuring out why. The weapons are just tools. The balance is in the combat itself."

"Noted. Thank you."

"Don't mention it." Garrett moved toward the door, then paused. "Almost forgot. Heard a northern buyer came through last week, asking about old Spire prototypes. Specifically a broken blade from twenty years ago. Said it belonged to a Vance. Could be a coincidence—could mean someone's taking an interest." He shook his head. "Anyway. One more thing. You're at Zone One, which means you've been classified as having no combat specializations. Standard curriculum without exceptions. But if you want to learn something the standard curriculum doesn't teach, there's a sparring circle in Zone Three. Unofficial, off the books. Happens every week, after hours. Interested?"

"Location?"

"Third sublevel, old maintenance tunnel. They don't advertise it. You find out if you find out." Garrett opened the door. "Show up if you want. Don't if you don't. But if you do show up, don't expect them to go easy on you just because you're an initiate."

"I'll take that under advisement."

Garrett nodded and left.

Roan stood alone in the surplus room, surrounded by the remnants of ideas that hadn't worked. He picked up the prototype blade again, tested its balance, set it back on the shelf.

The sparring circle was an unknown variable. Unknown variables required data. And data required observation.

Time to find out what Zone Three was hiding. The maintenance tunnel was exactly where Garrett had said, third sublevel, behind a door that didn't appear on any official floor plan. The room beyond was large, reinforced concrete walls covered in scuff marks, a circle painted on the floor in faded red paint.

Twenty people inside. Some in Spire uniforms, some not. All of them watching the two figures in the center of the circle, where a match was in progress.

Roan found a spot near the wall and observed.

The fighters were both hardened-phase at minimum. Roan could read it in their movement economy, the way they transferred weight and energy without wasted motion. One was using a staff, long reach, aggressive tempo. The other had twin short blades, defensive positioning, waiting for openings.

The staff fighter pressed forward. The dual-blade fighter gave ground, absorbed a strike on his guard, counterattacked. The staff moved to intercept, too slow. The dual-blade found an angle, struck once, twice. Staff fighter stumbled back, raised his guard.

The match ended without a clear winner. Both fighters stepped back, nodded, exited the circle.

"Next!" someone called.

A different pair entered. Same intensity, same focus. Roan watched three more matches, building a mental database of technique patterns and movement signatures.

The sparring here was nothing like the formal training yards. More brutal. More honest. People came here because they wanted to fight, not because they were supposed to The skill variance was enormous, some fighters were experienced, others were taking lumps and learning fast.

One of the observers—a scarred man missing two fingers on his left hand, called Brand by the others—caught him watching. "You here to fight or just watch?"

Roan considered the question. "I could do both. Watch first."

"Smart. Most initiates show up thinking they can hang with the hardened-phase crowd. They can't. They learn the hard way." Brand gestured toward the circle. "Matches are voluntary. You step in when you're ready. Nobody forces anyone. But once you're in, you fight until someone calls it or can't continue."

"Any rules?"

"Only one. No permanent damage. Everything else is acceptable." Brand grinned. "Tooth's missing, ear's cauliflowered, ribs cracked, all acceptable. Death isn't. This isn't a killing circle. Just a fighting circle."

Roan nodded. "I'll watch for a while."

"Suit yourself.". He watched for three hours. Catalogued forty-seven matches. Identified seven distinct technique families. Calculated that the average fight in this circle lasted four minutes, compared to eight in formal sparring at Zone Two.

Faster pace. Higher consequence. People here weren't practicing, they were testing.

As midnight approached, the crowd began to thin. The hardcore regulars remained, but the curious observers filtered out, back to their quarters, back to whatever lives they lived outside the circle.

Brand approached again. "You ready to try?"

Roan checked his internal inventory. Quota: three for the day. He'd used zero so far, these were observation matches, no combat, no Sidewound triggering. Available capacity: three.

"Tomorrow," he said. "I'll be back tomorrow."

Brand shrugged. "Your loss if you wait too long. Some people show up once and never come back. Others show up every week and never step into the circle. Both groups are wasting their time."

"I'm not wasting my time. I'm gathering data."

"Data doesn't win fights."

"Neither does instinct. Not against someone who's already done the arithmetic."

Brand stared at him, then laughed. "You're Vance's kid, aren't you?"

"So I've been told."

"Fair enough. See you tomorrow, Vance. Or don't." He walked away.

Roan stood in the emptying circle, surrounded by the residue of violence, scuff marks, stains, the faint smell of sweat and blood. As he turned to leave, a flicker of movement caught his eye. In the shadow of a support pillar, a young woman stood watching him—dark hair, sharp features, posture that suggested she was ready to move. Their gazes locked for exactly one second. Then she stepped back into the darkness and vanished, as silent as she'd appeared.

Roan filed the sighting. Unknown variable. Noted.

He had forty-seven matches worth of data to analyze. Skill distributions, technique frequencies, outcome correlations. All of it mapped against official Spire rankings and performance metrics he'd pulled from the requisition database.

The math was taking shape.

Tomorrow, he'd run his first analysis. Then he'd decide whether the circle was worth the risk.

He walked out of the tunnel, back up through the sublevels, into the cold night air of the Spire grounds. Stars overhead without moon. Darkness that made you feel small.

Forty-seven matches. Seven technique families. The numbers were locked. Tomorrow he would decide whether to enter the circle—and what it would cost.

The next morning, a message appeared on his board: *Instructor Marsh requests your presence. Equipment requisition office. Urgent.*

Roan read it twice. An urgent summons from Marsh meant something had changed. Either his research requests had raised flags he hadn't anticipated, or someone else had taken an interest in his activities.

Either way, the tally had just become more complicated.

He got up and dressed, then went to find out what variables had shifted.
