Chapter 187: The World Notices

By nightfall, Aldric Vance was alive again in every way except the inconvenient physical one.

The student boards moved faster than the professional channels and with worse spelling.

That didn't make them less important.

Student rumor had a particular kind of force. It didn't need confirmation. It needed repetition, emotional contour, and a place to land between classes. Within an hour, Black Spire students had created timelines, speculative suspect lists, annotated duel clips, edited compilations of Aldric's old matches, and one deeply inaccurate chart connecting Roan, Selene, Kael, Cyrus and Vorath, plus the cafeteria's recent shortage of citrus pudding.

Roan stared at the pudding chart.

"Why am I connected to dessert logistics?"

Ines leaned over. "Because you bought all the cheap ration bars last term and someone thinks that proves supply chain influence."

"That isn't how supply chains work."

"It's a student forum. That's barely how thought works."

Brendt, attending through a medical projection hovering over the far chair, said, "For the record, the citrus pudding shortage was real and suspicious."

"You are medicated," Roan said.

"Doesn't make me wrong."

"It reduces evidentiary value."

Brendt looked offended and slightly transparent. "When I can walk again, I am investigating pudding."

Cole didn't look up from his slate. "No one is investigating pudding."

"That sounds like something a guilty institution would say."

The exchange was absurd. It helped for exactly four seconds.

Then panel nine updated with a clipped compilation titled: ALDRIC VANCE WARNING SIGNS: WHY WAS HIS FINAL YEAR SCRUBBED?

The room returned to its real shape.

The compilation contained old footage of Aldric in three public appearances during his final year. In the first, he exited a Rift training facility with a bandage around his throat and refused to answer questions. In the second, he stood behind Drogan during a Black Squad memorial event, eyes tracking someone off camera. In the third, he laughed at a reporter's question and said, "If I vanish, start with the people who call it procedure."

Roan paused the clip.

The room went still.

Drogan looked away.

Roan replayed the line.

"If I vanish, start with the people who call it procedure."

Aldric had said it lightly. Too lightly. The reporter had laughed. The old comment thread below the archived clip showed people treating it as another Vance joke.

Six years later, it was no longer funny.

Roan saved the clip.

"Yes."

Truth, and worse for being true.

Roan didn't press. Not yet. The day had enough open wounds without digging fingers into all of them simultaneously.

On panel six, Greel-linked accounts began pushing a counter-hashtag.

#ProcedureMatters

Iris looked at it and made a sound of professional disgust. "They're going with the exact word Aldric mocked. That's either arrogance or no one on their side watched the clip."

"Both," Ines said.

Roan made a note.

Greel media response not fully coordinated. Pressure causing speed errors.

Speed errors were useful. Enough speed errors became map.

His file resurfaced first.

Not the sealed file. The public one. The sanitized obituary Black Spire had released six years earlier, the short martial bulletin from the Northern Compact, three student memorial posts, one grainy training clip, two photographs from a tournament decade before Roan entered the Spire, and an old interview where Aldric had been asked about "the future of independent practitioners in a House-dominated field" and answered, with visible boredom, that futures were usually owned by whoever survived the invoice.

The line began circulating within the hour.

People loved dead men with sharp quotes. They required less maintenance than living ones.

Roan watched the feeds from Cole's secure conference room and hated almost all of it usefully.

A wall-sized display had been divided into twelve panels: major martial forums, legal commentary, House channels, student boards, Sentinel-adjacent rumor nets, financial market reactions for Greel-linked assets, and one private thread Ines had described as "where expensive cowards panic honestly."

The case had woken.

It also had teeth in places Roan had not expected.

A medical ethics board requested clarification on Kael's undisclosed command oath. An independent practitioner association offered legal support, then immediately argued internally about whether Roan counted as independent now that Black Spire protected him. A Greycross local thread posted an old photo of Mira's clinic charity ledger and speculated wildly about whether Aldric had hidden House money in slum accounts. Roan reported that thread from three different burner accounts until it was locked.

Family stayed off the board.

That wasn't negotiable. Roan had no objection to becoming evidence. He had serious objections to letting strangers turn Mira's kitchen, Lyra's birthday gifts, or Greycross rent records into entertainment for people who confused curiosity with entitlement. He marked three posts for removal and sent them to Ines without comment. She removed five and sent back one word: *Amateur*. Roan accepted the criticism as technically fair and operationally prompt. It was irritating because it was accurate. He let it go.

Not neatly. Cases never woke neatly. They rose like disturbed ground, bringing worms with the bones.

Panel one: ROAN VANCE CLAIMS SIX MEN KILLED ALDRIC VANCE.

Panel two: GREEL HEIR CONFESSION LINKED TO OLD BLACK SQUAD DEATH.

Panel three: WHO WAS ALDRIC VANCE?

Panel six: NORTHERN COMPACT REVIEW BOARD CALLS EMERGENCY SESSION.

Resource ledger had changed faster than expected.

Roan had gained public attention, dormant witness movement, pressure on sealed records, and at least three new hostile narratives. He had lost remaining obscurity, several future safe routes, and the option to pretend this was still personal.

Enemy ledger expanded. Greel stayed primary. Unknown Black Squad survivor entered field. One of them listens. Potential leak among six, or among allies, or inside Codex-adjacent channels. The warning itself could be trap, truth, or both.

Rule ledger: public attention created protection only if continuously fed. Let silence stretch, and enemies would fill it. Speak too often, and precision degraded into noise.

Next debt: identify who posted from the archive before Vorath arrived.

Ines sat cross-legged on the conference table with three slates around her. Cole had told her twice to get down. She had obeyed both times in spirit by moving one inch lower and then returning when he looked away.

Iris stood at the market panel, watching Greel-linked asset confidence dip by four percent, recover two, then dip again when a legal commentator replayed Kael's confession alongside Roan's statement.

Selene read House reaction summaries from her own network.

Drogan stood at the far wall, eyes fixed on the Black Squad archive post.

He hadn't moved much since it appeared.

Roan gave him twenty minutes.

Then he said, "Name."

Drogan didn't pretend to misunderstand. "No."

"Bad answer."

"Only one you're getting."

Roan turned away from the display. "A dormant account controlled by surviving Black Squad personnel posted confirmation within minutes of my statement. That person either supports reopening the case, is baiting me, or is already compromised. Name."

Drogan's eyes remained on the wall. "You think knowing it helps?"

Roan held the look.

"You're wrong."

"Prove it."

Drogan finally looked at him.

The old instructor looked older than he had in the Rift, older than after Brendt's surgery, older than when Aldric's projection appeared and left silence behind. Some ages didn't come from years. They came from doors reopening.

"The third survivor," Drogan said, "is Lady the western signatory."

Selene's head lifted sharply.

Iris turned from the market panel.

Ines stopped typing.

Roan searched memory.

Name: the western signatory. The title carried old weight. Not Founding Seven. Not Greel. Associated with northern diplomacy, old battlefield medicine, intelligence networks, or all three depending on which rumor one believed. A woman with too many retired positions and no retirement.

"Full name," Roan said.

Drogan's mouth tightened. "the western signatory Vale."

Cyrus.

Of course.

The room rearranged itself around the implication.

Cyrus Ashford, humiliated rival, almost recruited by Kael, currently unstable variable, had a relative tied to Black Squad survival and Aldric's hidden past. Roan disliked coincidences almost as much as he disliked cheap coffee. They were usually unpaid labor by hidden hands.

"Relation?" Roan asked.

"Great-aunt. Maybe. Vale family records are deliberately messy."

Selene said, "Lady the western signatory was attached to northern theater humanitarian corridors during the same period as Aldric's final operation."

Iris frowned. "Humanitarian corridors with Black Squad access?"

"During the Rift surge, boundaries blurred."

Ines made a note. "That means smuggling."

Selene didn't deny it.

Roan looked back at Drogan. "Why not tell me?"

"Because the western signatory doesn't move unless something is already burning."

"It's burning."

"She also doesn't always move toward the fire."

That was a better answer.

Roan processed.

If the western signatory controlled the archive post, she had chosen to support the central claim without naming herself. That increased pressure while preserving deniability. If someone else used her access, then a Black Squad archive credential had been compromised. Both cases required contact.

"Can you reach her?" Roan asked.

"No."

Truth? Partial.

"You can send something she might see."

Drogan didn't answer.

Roan said, "Do it."

"I don't take orders from you."

"No. You take them from dead men and bad habits. This qualifies as both."

Drogan stared.

For one second, Selene looked like she might intervene. She chose not to. Good.

Drogan's jaw worked.

Then he took out his slate.

Progress.

The global martial boards turned the Aldric Vance case into a tournament within three hours.

That was predictable and depressing.

People who had never heard Aldric's name at breakfast had strong opinions by dinner. Some declared Roan courageous. Some called him reckless. Some accused him of exploiting a duel victory for attention, which required ignoring several injuries and the inconvenient fact that attention had already arrived with assassination attempts attached. Some dug through old footage and found Aldric dismantling a Crowned opponent in twelve seconds during an exhibition match. Others found financial records showing Aldric had refused House sponsorships three times.

That last thread became popular.

Independent practitioners loved dead independents. They functioned as proof the game had once been winnable.

House commentators responded by stressing Aldric's "controversial methods," "classified associations," and "unstable late-career decisions."

Roan saved those phrases.

Accusations revealed old talking points. Old talking points revealed old meetings. Old meetings had attendees.

Iris built a phrase-frequency map from hostile commentary and compared it against archived press language from six years ago.

Her results arrived at 21:12.

"Three phrases repeat," she said, projecting them onto the table. Failed containment judgment

unsanctioned operational deviation

refusal of lawful extraction

Roan read them.

"Extraction," he said.

Drogan's face changed.

There it was.

Roan looked at him.

Drogan said nothing.

Cole, who had returned after a closed call with the Board, looked between them. "Meaning?"

Roan answered because Drogan wouldn't. "The old narrative wasn't that Aldric died in a Rift failure. It was that he caused or worsened the failure by refusing extraction."

Selene scanned the phrases. "That frames him as insubordinate."

"And makes anyone who left him behind look procedural."

Iris's eyes hardened. "So if six men killed him, the cover story was that they tried to save him."

Drogan turned away.

Confirmation.

Roan wrote the three phrases in his notebook.

Under them, he added:

Who ordered extraction?

Who refused?

Who survived?

Ines's slate chimed. She looked down, then grinned slowly.

"I have something stupid."

"That isn't a definition."

She projected an old image onto the table. Low resolution. A still from a six-year-old emergency broadcast near the northern theater. Most of the image showed smoke, evacuation barriers, and officials moving behind ward tape. In the background stood a man with white hair and a long glaive case.

Vorath.

Not proof of murder. Not even proof of proximity to Aldric's death site unless the broadcast metadata held. But visual presence near the operation window.

The first formal institutional movement came from outside Black Spire.

At 20:31, the Inter-Spire Martial Records Board announced that it had received "multiple preservation requests" concerning the Aldric Vance operation file and would "review archival chain-of-custody status."

That language meant very little to most people.

Cole read it twice. "They aren't reopening."

"No," Selene said. "They're checking whether reopening would embarrass them."

Iris nodded. "And preserving the ability to claim they were already reviewing if proof leaks."

Cole looked at it.

"Don't bother."

Roan studied the board's phrasing. Chain-of-custody status. Not file contents. That meant someone suspected records had been altered, moved, or sealed improperly. Or they wanted to look like they suspected it.

Either way, a door that had been painted over now had a handle.

No outrage. No threats. No mention of Aldric by name. It expressed concern for student safety, criticized "reckless allegations made under medical duress," reaffirmed Lord Greel's commitment to lawful dialogue, and confirmed his arrival in Ironholm to "restore order through direct conversation."

Restore order.
