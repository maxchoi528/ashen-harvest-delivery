Chapter 83: The Account

Drogan was waiting in the shadow of the east gate when Roan walked back onto campus.

The Warden stood with his arms crossed, the Iron Hand catching the amber light of the perimeter lamps. Drogan's arms stayed crossed, but the grip on his bicep shifted. He had been standing there long enough for the metal to cool.

"You took your time," Drogan said.

"I stopped to check the blade. Greel-pattern. Serrated spine. Standard issue for their field operatives." Roan held up the knife. "I kept it."

Drogan's eyes moved from the blade to the fading cuts on Roan's forearm. "How many?"

"Forty Vitalis gained. Two cuts. One cracked rib." Roan felt the bone complain when he breathed. He kept his voice even. "He was peak Hardened. He'll report that I survived."

"He'll report that you barely fought back and still won." Drogan's voice was flat. "That's worse."

Roan considered the assessment. It was accurate. A target who won without visible effort was more concerning than one who fought hard and barely survived. The cleaner's report would reach Vorath Greel within hours, and the White-Bearded Man would update his model accordingly.

"Good," Roan said.

Drogan's jaw tightened. "You want him to know."

"I want him to wonder. Uncertainty is more expensive than certainty. Vorath Greel has been certain about the Vance line for six years. Let him wonder what changed."

The Warden was silent for a long moment. The only sound was the low hum of the perimeter lamps and the distant pulse of the Spire's barrier. Then he reached into his coat and pulled out a folded piece of paper. "This arrived for you an hour ago. Delivered by a courier I didn't recognize. Sent through channels I couldn't trace."

Roan took the paper. Unfolded it.

Three words, written in a hand he didn't recognize:

*Get to Suthmere.*

No signature. No explanation. Just a location, a date, and a time.

The damp of the paper against his fingers felt deliberate. Someone with resources and an intimate understanding of the hour's events.

"This is from someone who knew about the cleaner," Roan said. "Someone who knew I survived."

"Or someone who set it up."

Roan considered that. The timing was tight. A message arriving an hour after the attack meant the sender had been waiting for confirmation.

"You know who sent this."

Drogan's expression flickered. "I have a theory."

"Share it."

"Not yet." Drogan's voice hardened. "Because if I tell you what I suspect, you'll walk into Suthmere half-healed, chasing a ghost. Your father made that mistake. You won't."

Roan absorbed the rebuke. The Warden wasn't being cryptic for the sake of it. He was applying the same wall he had built around Aldric's full truth.

"Suthmere is a death trap," Drogan continued, turning to look north. "The peaks are patrolled by Greel's northern operatives where the Crown's grip frayed into contested ground. The weather kills unprepared climbers. The mountain itself kills experienced ones. Your father died pursuing whatever lies at the top of that range."

"My father died because he went alone." Roan folded the paper. The edges were sharp. "I won't make the same mistake."

The Warden studied him. The years sat heavy on his shoulders. "When do you leave?"

"Three days. I need to recover capacity and gather supplies."

"I'll have Brendt briefed. He'll cover for you."

Roan paused. "You're not going to stop me."

"I can't stop you." Drogan's voice was quiet. "I could lock you in a containment cell and you'd find a way out. I could put you on academic probation and you'd still find a way north. You're your father's son."

"Then why are you helping?"

Drogan met his eyes. "Because someone needs to survive what's at the top of that mountain. And I'm too old."

He turned and walked away, his footsteps steady on the stone path, his Iron Hand gleaming in the lamplight.

Roan stood alone at the gate. The night air carried the bite of residual cold from the Spire's foundations. The Suthmere address sat in his pocket like a second heartbeat. The mountain was a lead, not the end. The sender had known enough to reach him fast. That was worth the journey.

He tested his ribs with a breath. Sore. Healing.

Three days. Then the mountain.
