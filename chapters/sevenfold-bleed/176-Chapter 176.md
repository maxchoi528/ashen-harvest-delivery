Chapter 176: Three Days Awake

Roan didn't sleep for three days.

This wasn't dramatic. It wasn't healthy either, but health had been demoted below several more urgent categories. He used stimulant tea twice, meditative cycling every six hours, and one controlled pain response through the unhealed hole in his left hand to keep his mind from blurring at the edges. The Codex Shard objected after the second day with three increasingly direct warnings.

[Sleep debt impairing calculation.]

[Manual cognition degradation: 12%.]

[Recommendation: rest.]

Roan ignored it.

At hour fifty-one, the Shard tried a different tactic.

[Brendt Holloway survival probability improves if primary operator remains functional.]

That one made him pause.

For four minutes, he considered sleeping.

Then Selene arrived with the Greel import ledger and the pause ended.

The war room was technically a disused seminar office under the east library. Unofficially, it had become a crime scene with furniture. Roan had pinned maps across two walls: Ironholm corporate districts, Greel-linked warehouses, Vell scholarship channels, known student movement routes, market gate surveillance, municipal registry excerpts, and a rapidly growing chart of dead shell companies.

A third wall held names.

Not suspects.

Names.

Names became suspects when evidence attached. Until then they were handles.

Brendt Holloway's medical chart sat on the central table under a glass weight.

Roan didn't look at it often.

He knew every line.

Unconscious. Stable under healer supervision. Foreign poison residue reduced but not eliminated. Lung graft holding. Neurological response delayed. Prognosis guarded.

Guarded was a word doctors used when hope hadn't paid enough to own the sentence.

Selene placed the ledger beside three empty cups and a knife that had migrated there from Roan's belt at some point during hour thirty-eight.

"You need sleep," she said.

"No."

"You need it strategically."

"Already considered."

"And rejected because you are being rational or because you are angry?"

Roan looked up.

Selene didn't step back.

Good. Brave or exhausted. Both.

"I am angry," Roan said. "That doesn't make me wrong."

"No. It makes your correct conclusions more likely to choose expensive routes."

That was uncomfortably accurate.

Ines, sitting on the windowsill with a stolen administrative slate, pointed at Selene. "I like her version better than mine. Mine had more profanity."

Drogan stood near the door, arms folded, looking like he had aged another year in three days and blamed Roan personally. "Read the ledger."

Roan did.

The ledger showed cold-chain imports through three northern medical suppliers. On paper: cryo-preservation salts, surgical anesthetic, high-grade circulation stabilizers. In practice: enough restricted precursor material to manufacture black charm blades with barbed poison channels. The shipments entered Ironholm through a Greel-controlled freight office, passed through two legitimate clinics, and disappeared into a market vendor cooperative established eleven days before the assassination attempt.

Eleven days.

Kael had transferred before that.

Not proof he ordered it. Proof the operation prepared around his arrival.

Roan marked the supplier names in red.

The first business was Northglass Medical Preservation, a respectable cold-chain supplier with a lobby full of white furniture and a director who donated to three student wellness funds. Its invoices showed legitimate volume, but its waste disposal records were wrong. Too little spoiled material for the amount of unstable preservative it claimed to handle. Either Northglass had discovered miraculous storage efficiency or its failed batches were walking out through a side door.

The second was Oryn Freight Compliance, which owned no freight vehicles and employed sixteen auditors who all listed the same apartment building as residence. Their payroll moved through a Greel finance subsidiary on the seventeenth of each month. Their audit stamps appeared on the crate that had carried the black-blade alloy.

The third was a noodle stall cooperative. New. Poorly named. Registered by a woman who had died six years ago in a tram accident. The cooperative's vendor permits had been approved with suspicious speed by a municipal clerk whose gambling debt had been purchased by Vell auxiliary accounts two days earlier.

Roan wrote the clerk's name under *pressure points*, not *targets*. Clerks usually broke because someone above them had already applied pressure. Killing clerks was like burning receipts because one disliked the purchase.

At hour twenty-nine, Ines found a warehouse camera loop that repeated every thirteen minutes. At hour thirty-one, Selene matched the loop timestamp to an Ardent charity delivery that had never arrived. At hour thirty-three, Drogan returned from "walking" with blood on one cuff and the name of a retired ward installer who had modified the south gate camera blind spot. He refused to explain the blood. Roan didn't ask because the ward installer was alive and had provided a diagram.

At hour thirty-six, Cole sent a sealed administrative note containing restricted gate access logs and the sentence: *I am not asking how you will use this because I want to answer honestly later.*

Roan placed the note under useful adults. A small category.

At hour thirty-seven, they visited Northglass Medical Preservation without making an appointment.

Visit was the polite term. Drogan stood in the lobby and asked to speak with the director. Selene displayed a temporary audit injunction that looked more official than it was. Ines walked behind the reception desk while everyone looked at Drogan. Roan said nothing until the director, a smooth man with silver cuffs and a smile built for donor dinners, claimed Northglass had no relationship with Greel operations beyond standard shipping.

Roan placed the black charm alloy fragment on the man's glass desk.

The director's eyes flicked down for less than a quarter second.

Enough.

"Your waste numbers are impossible," Roan said. "Your refrigeration draw is inflated. Your third-floor cold room has two power feeds, one unregistered. You can tell us who signed for the overflow batches, or I can give this file to every competitor who wants your licenses."

The director looked at Selene. "Is this legal?"

Selene didn't blink. "That depends how quickly you answer."

He answered in nine minutes. Not fully. Men like that never did. But he gave them an initials code, S.G. And a delivery time pattern that matched the broker's booking windows. Roan left him alive, professionally damaged, and under the impression that cooperation had been his own idea.

Alive sources could be revisited. Ruined sources generated gossip. Dead sources generated paperwork and sometimes ghosts.

At hour forty, Mira called.

Roan almost didn't answer. Then he did, because not answering would make Lyra call, and Lyra extracted truth through tone with the persistence of a tax audit.

His mother's face appeared on the slate, older than she should have looked, eyes sharp despite the hospital fatigue she still tried to hide from him. "Are you hurt?"

"No."

Her eyes moved to his bandaged hand.

"Not significantly," he amended.

"Roan."

A world of warning in one name. Mothers had unfair compression ratios.

"Brendt is hurt," he said.

"I know. Lyra heard from a student forum before the school statement."

Of course she had. Black Spire information discipline remained theoretical.

"Alive," Roan said.

Mira closed her eyes once. When she opened them, fear had been folded away, not gone. "And you?"

"Working."

"That wasn't the question."

"It's the answer."

For a moment she looked so much like Lyra that Roan had to look at the wall instead of the screen.

"Your father worked after people were hurt," Mira said. "He called it necessary. Sometimes it was. Sometimes it was just easier than sitting beside a bed."

Roan didn't respond.

The sentence landed too close to places Drogan had been circling for months.

Mira's voice softened by one degree. "Don't confuse revenge with staying."

"I'm not."

She studied him. Didn't believe him. Didn't call him a liar. Both were worse.

"Tell Brendt's mother if she needs anything, she calls me," Mira said.

Roan blinked. "You don't know her."

"I know enough."

The call ended.

Roan stood motionless for twelve seconds, then wrote *Holloway family support* on a separate page and underlined it twice.

Brendt would complain if he knew.

Good.

Roan marked the supplier names in red again, darker this time, because the first marks hadn't cut through the paper enough.

"Three legal businesses," Selene said. "Two illegal processing sites. One likely courier broker. All connected to Greel holdings but buffered by intermediaries."

"Ownership documents?"

"In progress."

"Tax filings?"

Ines raised her slate. "Already ugly. One warehouse claims refrigeration expenses exceeding product volume by two hundred percent. Either they're hiding poison precursors or chilling very nervous furniture."

"Both possible."

Drogan pushed off the wall. "You have enough for Cole."

"No."

"You have enough for an inquiry."

"Inquiries are where evidence goes to become old."

"Roan."

Roan drew a line from the vendor cooperative to the missing body.

"Someone removed the vendor before security sealed the scene. That requires either Spire access, municipal security compromise, or a recovery team positioned nearby. Greel businesses supply the blade. Vell remnants provided noise. Kael built Cyrus as visible pressure. Unknown note warned he wasn't here for Cyrus. The assassination used Brendt as secondary target or acceptable collateral."

"Acceptable collateral," Selene repeated quietly.

Roan's pencil stopped.

The phrase sat badly.

He crossed it out and wrote: *Chosen pressure point.*

Better.

Brendt hadn't been collateral once the charm spike targeted him. The male assassin had thrown it at Brendt after the initial wound. That meant either the operation included a branch for killing Brendt if Roan adapted, or the assassin improvised under the assumption that harming Brendt destabilized Roan.

Correct assumption.

Professionals didn't always need orders to find the soft part.

Roan disliked that Brendt had become visible as one.

He disliked more that the dislike itself was visible now.

Ines slid down from the windowsill. "We found the courier broker."

Roan looked up.

She placed a photo on the table. Middle-aged man. Balding. Gray coat. Nervous eyes. Name: Tomas Rell. Licensed freight expediter. Prior ties to Vell scholarship auxiliary accounts. Recent debt purchase by a Greel finance subsidiary.

"Alive?" Roan asked.

"For now."

"Location?"

"Changing. He knows people are dead and his name is attached. He booked passage out through the east tram freight line tonight."

Drogan's expression darkened. "No."

Roan put on his coat.

Drogan stepped into the doorway.

"No," the old man repeated.

Roan stopped.

Drogan's voice was low. "You kill him, you kill yourself with him."

The sentence arrived exactly where Roan's calculations had reserved it—a script he'd already memorized. Drogan didn't mean legally, beyond the legal question. He meant shape. Pattern. The way revenge narrowed a person until every answer looked like a body. Drogan had seen Aldric travel that road, or thought he had. Maybe he had watched himself walk a parallel one and called it someone else's cautionary tale because that hurt less.

Roan looked at the names on the wall.

Brendt's medical chart reflected in the glass.

"I'm already dead," Roan said. "The rest is paperwork."

Selene's face went still.

Ines stopped smiling.

Drogan flinched as if struck.

Good.

No. Not good.

Accurate.

The distinction didn't help.

Drogan moved faster than most men his age had a right to move. His hand closed around Roan's coat front and slammed him back against the wall hard enough to rattle the maps.

Roan didn't reach for a knife.

Drogan's face was inches from his. "That sentence is poison."

"Yes."

"Spit it out."

"No."

Drogan's grip tightened. "Aldric said something like that once. Not the same words. Same rot. He thought survival after loss was clerical. He made himself into an instrument and called it necessity until he couldn't tell the difference between cost and identity."

Roan held his gaze.

"And did that make him wrong?"

Drogan's eyes went flat with old pain. "It made him alone."

Silence.

For the first time in three days, Roan's mind didn't move cleanly to the next line.

Alone.

Not dead. Not defeated. Alone.

The word found places logic had avoided.

Brendt in a medical bed because he had refused to let Roan stand alone inside a knife line. Selene bleeding on an archive ward. Ines disrupting locks without payment finalized because curiosity had become something inconveniently close to loyalty. Drogan standing in the doorway like an old barricade built from regret.

Roan didn't like emotional evidence. It lacked structure.

It was still evidence.

Drogan released him.

"You want the broker," Drogan said. "We take him alive."

Roan adjusted his coat. "Alive is less efficient."

"Alive talks."

"So do dead men's accounts."

"Alive talks faster."

Ines cleared her throat. "As the person who would prefer not to rob a corpse's entire financial history under active municipal alert, I support alive."

Selene said, "Alive lets us build legal venue."

Roan looked at her.

She held his gaze. "You wrote that answer in Tactical Ethics. Hybrid retaliation. Controlled evidentiary release. Force public violation."

Cole had told her. Or she had access. Either way, the phrase belonged to him and therefore annoyed him by being useful.

Roan looked back at the wall.

Kill broker: immediate satisfaction, limited information, legal risk, possible loss of proof chain.

Capture broker: operational risk, information yield, leverage against supply chain, path toward public trap.

Brendt would prefer alive.

That shouldn't matter.

It did.

"Fine," Roan said. "Alive unless inconvenient."

Drogan exhaled. "Acceptable."

"Define inconvenient," Ines said.

"No," Selene and Drogan said together.

- break -

Tomas Rell tried to leave Ironholm in a refrigerated cargo crate labeled *fermented fish culture samples*.

Roan almost respected the choice.

The east tram freight yard sprawled beneath a web of elevated tracks, lit by yellow lamps and patrolled by guards who cared more about smuggling fees than official security. Rain tapped on metal roofs. Cargo haulers moved between platforms. A line of refrigerated crates hummed near loading bay seven, each marked with agricultural seals and enough odor wards to make inspection unpleasant.

Ines had found Rell's booking through a bribed clerk, a scraped payment node, and what she called "administrative romance," which Roan chose not to investigate.

Drogan handled the guards by showing a Black Spire emergency credential and looking old enough to have killed their supervisors. Selene stayed in the control office to prevent logs from developing memory. Roan entered the crate row with Ines.

"Alive," Ines reminded him.

"Unless inconvenient."

"We are defining inconvenience narrowly tonight."

"Are we?"

"Yes. Selene made me promise."

"Bad habit."

"Promises?"

"Making them to Selene."

Ines smiled faintly and stopped beside crate seven. The refrigeration unit hummed louder than the others. Too loud. Masking breath or a second device.

Roan touched the latch.

Cold bled through metal.

Not ordinary refrigeration.

Cryo-Vitalis.

Greel technique residue.

He drew Sidewound.

Ines stepped back and unfolded a compact shock net.

Roan opened the crate.

Tomas Rell screamed before the door cleared six inches.

Not in fear.

In pain.

The broker sat strapped to the rear wall, frost crusting his lips, eyes wide and bloodshot. A cryo charm had been embedded under his collarbone. Not concealment. Execution delay. His fingers were black at the tips. Beside him, a small recorder crystal pulsed red.

A message.

Of course.

Roan stepped inside.

The temperature tried to bite through his coat. The Vance Pendant cooled but didn't alarm. Sidewound hummed once, picking out the charm's seam under Rell's skin.

Rell's eyes found him. "Vance."

"Who ordered the market strike?"

Rell laughed. It broke into a cough. "You think they told me names?"

"Yes."

The answer startled him.

Roan pressed Sidewound's tip beside the embedded charm. "You were important enough to silence. That means you knew enough to be useful or enough to be blamed. Either way, names."

Rell's breath hitched. "Greel subsidiary. Man called Soren. No family name. Northern accent. Paid through Vell auxiliary."

"Kael?"

"I never saw the boy."

Truth? Partial. Fear distorted voice but not enough to hide reflex. He hadn't seen Kael.

"Vorath?"

Rell's eyes rolled. "Don't say that name in a cold room."

Interesting.

The recorder crystal pulsed faster.

Ines, at the door, said, "Roan."

"I see it."

The crystal was counting down.

Not explosive. Broadcast.

Roan cut the embedded cryo charm with Sidewound.

[Seam disruption confirmed.]

Rell screamed again as frost inside his veins lost structure. He would live if treated quickly and lose several fingers if not. Acceptable.

Ines threw the shock net over the recorder crystal. It discharged and flickered, then still activated.

A voice filled the crate.

Young. Polite. Kael.

"Roan Vance. If you are hearing this, you found the broker. Good. That means you understand the first layer. I apologize for Holloway. He wasn't the intended kill."

Roan went still.

Ines stopped breathing.

Kael's recorded voice continued.

"But he was an intended lesson. You protect by proximity. That's inefficient. It gives enemies geometry."

Rell sobbed against the straps.

The crystal's red light brightened.

"I would prefer to settle this cleanly. Not in alleys. Not through vendors. Not with remnants embarrassing themselves in public. You know why I am here. Or you will soon. Ask yourself what your father would have done before he became a warning to better men."

The recording clicked off.

For three seconds, only the refrigeration unit hummed.

Ines looked at Roan carefully.

Roan cut Rell's straps.

The broker fell forward. Roan caught him by the coat and dragged him out of the crate.

Alive.

Drogan arrived at the bay entrance, saw Roan's face, and stopped.

"Broker talks," Roan said.

Drogan's jaw tightened. "And the message?"

"Kael wants clean ground."

Ines folded the damaged recorder crystal into a sealed pouch. "He also just admitted knowledge of the attack's structure."

"Not legal admission," Selene said through the comm from the control office. "But enough to bait him."

Roan looked up at the elevated tracks, where cold rain fell through industrial light.

Clean ground.

Kael wanted it because he thought he could win there, or because Vorath wanted Roan measured there, or because a legal duel would expose enough of Roan's abilities to serve as reconnaissance even if Kael lost.

Fine.

If the enemy wanted a stage, Roan would build one with better wiring.

"Selene," he said.

"Yes?"

"Pull campus duel law."

A pause.

Then, quietly, "Roan."

"Do it."

Drogan said, "You aren't thinking."

Roan looked at him.

"No," he said. "I am thinking with new data."

The new data didn't make him calmer. Calm wasn't the point. Calm was a presentation layer people overvalued because it looked similar to control from a distance. Roan had control over his hands, his voice, the next six tasks, and the angle at which Tomas Rell would be delivered to Cole's protected evidence room. He didn't have control over Brendt's breathing charm. He didn't have control over Kael's next move. He didn't have control over the fact that the phrase *intended lesson* had entered the room and wouldn't leave.

So he narrowed the scope.

"Ines, preserve the recorder and extract timing metadata. Selene, duel law plus public challenge precedents involving transfer students. Drogan, deliver Rell alive and keep him that way until formal deposition. I will map Kael's incentives."

Drogan's eyes narrowed. "You will sleep."

"After the map."

"No."

Roan stepped past him toward the freight office exit. Rain struck the roof overhead in hard industrial bursts. Somewhere beyond the yard, a tram horn sounded, long and indifferent.

"Four hours," Drogan said.

Roan stopped.

The old man's voice had changed. Not command. Bargain.

"You want to challenge a Greel heir on legal ground," Drogan said. "You want to turn a duel into evidence extraction. Fine. Do it with a brain that has slept enough to remember the difference between bait and dinner."

Ines looked at Roan. Selene said nothing through the comm.

Roan looked at Rell, shivering under Ines's emergency thermal sheet, alive and terrified and already revising his loyalties toward whoever would keep his fingers attached.

Four hours cost time.

It bought reduced error probability.

Brendt's voice in memory: *Your bedside manner is a crime.*

Roan exhaled. "Two."

"Four," Drogan said.

"Three."

Drogan looked like he wanted to hit him and tuck him into bed, which was an expression no man should have to carry. "Three and a half."

"Fine."

Selene's voice came through the comm, very dry. "Historic compromise."

Roan ignored her.

He slept three hours and a little over twenty minutes in a chair beside Brendt's medical room, woke before the alarm, and began writing the duel petition on the back of a hospital consent form.

Before the first line, he checked Brendt's monitor. Pulse weak but regular. Respiratory charm stable. Poison residue down by four percent. No consciousness response.

Four percent wasn't enough.

It was also not zero. In the arithmetic of survival, not zero was ugly and inadequate. That meant: worth weaponizing, if handled correctly enough.

Roan wrote the petition heading with a borrowed pen and decided that if Kael wanted clean ground, he would receive ground so clean every stain became visible.
