Chapter 96: The House That Notices

The morning of the mid-term assessment arrived with the weight of a ledger.

Roan stood in the Stone Hall, the underground training chamber where Drogan Hale conducted the evaluations that shaped Black Spire's dangerous talents. The air was cold and heavy, tinged with residual vitalis from the previous day's drills. No windows. One door. The space designed to hold pressure.

Drogan entered without greeting, a slate in his hand. His boots echoed against the stone.

"Standard procedure, Vance. The academy requires a formal measure of your theoretical and practical progress." He set the slate on a workbench and turned to face Roan directly. "You know how this works. I ask. You answer. If your answer is insufficient, I find the limit of your body."

Roan inclined his head.

"First question. You consumed a standard Ironblood Tincture last cycle. The manual records a high probability of rejection, vomiting, twelve-hour forced recovery. Your charts show none of that. Explain."

Roan had expected this question. The Ironblood was designed to tax the system—its whole purpose was to push the body past its normal limits, and the "rejection" was considered a necessary cost. He had inverted that cost on the first night.

But he couldn't say that.

"The manual assumes standard vitalis pathway architecture," Roan said. "I isolated the rejection vector from the binding agent. In vivo recombination prevented the hostile immune cascade before it triggered. The result is a clean absorption curve with no downtime."

"That's not in the curriculum."

"The evaluation was about results. Not methods."

Drogan's eyes held him for a long moment. The silence stretched.

"Accepted."

He picked up the slate, made a note.

"Second question. You have faced three formal sparring matches in the tournament prep bracket. In each, you were matched against an opponent with higher Vitalis density. You won all three. How?"

"Vitalis density is a measure of capacity, not application," Roan said. "I chose engagements where my opponent's density worked against them. The first overextended his output to prove dominance. The second exhausted himself trying to maintain a pressure zone. The third didn't expect me to close distance through his area denial."

"You let them exhaust themselves."

"I let them optimize their failure."

Drogan set the slate down and stepped into the center of the hall. The air shifted, heavy with expectation.

"Theory is clean," he said. "Let's test the body."

The practical assessment was a single continuous sequence, no breaks, no pauses. Drogan didn't warm up. He attacked from the first motion, his strikes carrying the weight of a man who had killed before and remembered the feeling precisely.

Roan parried the first three blows, sidestepped the fourth, absorbed the fifth on his forearm guard. The impact reverberated up through his shoulder, a dull ache that would slow a normal initiate.

Drogan was Hardened-tier. The gap in raw power was significant.

But the gap in control was narrower.

Drogan feinted high, dropped his center, and delivered a palm strike to Roan's shoulder. The technique was Seeping Frost—a surge of freezing vitalis that bloomed on impact, designed to lock the muscle fibers and collapse the target's mobility on that side.

Roan felt the cold invade. Harmful effect. Immediate threat to combat effectiveness.

His body responded before his mind finished the calculation.

The freezing vitalis reversed. Not cancelled—absorbed, converted, repurposed. The cold energy snapped into a sharp burst of kinetic acceleration. Roan's arm, rather than going numb, snapped into a faster counter, deflecting Drogan's follow-up strike and opening a window.

He tapped Drogan's ribs. Light contact. Enough to register.

Drogan halted.

The chamber was silent except for the rasp of breath.

Drogan's eyes were unreadable, but his voice held a new edge when he spoke. "Seeping Frost. I have crippled Forged-rank fighters with that technique in the Rifts. You turned it into a speed advantage. Explain."

Roan's face remained blank. "I modulated the energy cascade on entry. The cold attempts to slow the fibers by binding to the vitalis channels. I redirected the binding into a release cycle."

"You redirected a hostile technique in the middle of combat."

"I prepared for the possibility."

Drogan's mouth twitched. It was not a smile. It was the expression of a man who had just confirmed a suspicion he had been carrying for weeks.

"You are a liar, Vance," Drogan said. "But you are a useful liar. The chamber will record your evaluation as A+. Exceptional strategic adaptation and theoretical rigor."

"And the truth?"

Drogan picked up his slate. "The truth is you are not a standard variable in the academy's equation. That makes you valuable. It also makes you a target."

He walked toward the door, then paused.

"There are people watching you. Some from inside these walls. Some from much higher. Selene Ardent's house has been asking questions about exceptional initiates in the tournament preparation lists. They didn't name you specifically, but they noticed the pattern."

Roan said nothing.

"You earned your A+. Now earn the right to survive the attention it brings." Drogan left without another word.

Outside the Stone Hall, the corridor was empty. Roan stood in the half-light, letting the assessment settle.

Brendt found him ten minutes later, still in his training robe, a bruise yellowing on his jaw from his own evaluation.

"You look intact," Brendt said.

"A+."

"Bullshit. Drogan had me doing logistics drills for three hours. Said my 'strategic initiative is acceptable but my tactical patience needs work.' Whatever that means."

"It means you rush."

"I know what it means." Brendt fell into step beside him. "But you. A+. Everyone saw you take a full Seeping Frost to the shoulder. You should be limping."

"I accounted for the cold."

"You accounted for a technique you have never seen him use in training?"

"I accounted for the principle. Frost is vitalis slowed. I sped it up inside the channel."

Brendt shook his head. "That is not how it works."

"It is now."

They walked in silence for a few steps.

"You know what I like about you?" Brendt said. "You make the impossible sound like basic bookkeeping."

"That is because I treat it the same way. The books must balance."

"And do they?"

Roan considered the question. The assessment score was clean. His standing in the academy was secure. The Ironblood optimization was a confirmed success. Brendt was healthy, loyal, and sharp.

The variables were stable.

"For now," he said.

Back in his room, Roan closed the door and sat on the edge of the cot. He laid Sidewound on the table—still early in its development, a simple shard of potential waiting for the right forge.

The Codex Shard hummed quietly against his spine.

No messages from his father today. No cryptic phrases surfacing in his awareness. Just the steady presence, patient and waiting.

He reviewed the day's arithmetic.

The assessment was a clean victory. A block laid in the foundation. He had earned his A+ without exposing the Inversion's true depth, without burning a resource he could not replace, without making an enemy he could not afford.

Drogan's warning echoed in the back of his mind. Selene Ardent's house was watching. The Founding Seven were a distant threat for now, but distant threats had a habit of arriving precisely when you least needed them.

Tomorrow, he would collect the materials for the Sidewound upgrade. The day after, he would begin the next phase of his training.

The bottom line of the real account—the one written in blood, in silence, in the weight of a father's legacy—remained unchanged.

Six to go.

He closed his eyes and began the evening cycle, letting the world narrow to the quiet hum of preparation.
