Chapter 76: The Dorm Intruder

The third opponent hit the sand at seventeen minutes past the hour.

Roan stood over the body—alive, groaning, lucky—and let the crowd finish screaming. Three matches in one afternoon. The figures said that was excessive. The arithmetic also said the Greel envoy was watching from the Spectator tier, which made the math irrelevant.

"Roan." Brendt was at the rail, knuckles white on the iron. "That's enough."

Roan stepped over the body and walked toward the exit. The scoreboard updated behind him: Vance, R. W-W-W. Seven wins. Fifth on the pile.

The crowd's tone had shifted. A Tempered-tier competing at Hardened speeds. The public display showed his Vitalis at 4,800. The arithmetic was honest: he should be exhausted. Sevenfold Bleed ate muscle tissue. Three strikes in one afternoon meant he should be on the floor waiting for healers.

The Greel envoy leaned forward in the Spectator tier.

Brendt caught him in the prep corridor. Rough stone walls, no benches. The air was sweat and old iron.

"You saw the bracket."

"Saw it."

Shadow Fist had three fighters left. Matched against the lowest-seeded survivors in the next round. Rigged. House Greel paid for that preference. It meant nothing if Roan didn't survive to face them.

"Numbers say you'll hit one in the quarters if you win your next two," Brendt said. "Two more fights. You're burning out before you get there."

The corridor was empty. Good.

"I'm at sixty percent. The strikes aren't depleting me. They're working."

"Because you're Inverting them." Brendt lowered his voice. "You've been running this for weeks. The Shard can't be unlimited. Even your father—"

"Never pushed this hard?" Roan faced him. "You don't know what my father did. Neither do I."

Silence. Brendt's shoulders tightened.

"Someone's been watching the matches from the Guild," Brendt said. "Higher up than a local recruiter. They've been logging your technique signatures."

Roan's stomach dropped. The arithmetic recalculated.

"How high?"

"Higher than Ironholm. They asked for your combat records."

"What for?"

"Assessment. Possible recruitment." Brendt's eyes were steady. "Or containment."

Right. Containment was the polite word. The Guild's protocol for a problem before it became a public relations issue.

"Which means they haven't decided yet," Roan said. "If they had, I wouldn't see tomorrow's bracket."

"Maybe. Or maybe they're watching to see how much stronger you get before they move."

The prep corridor felt smaller.

"What are you suggesting?"

Brendt hesitated. "Slow down. The next two fights don't have to be Sevenfold Bleed. Use something else. Let them think you're plateauing."

"Plateau." The word was wrong. "You want me to sandbag."

"I want you to survive."

Roan looked at his hand. Steady fingers, dark veins. Sevenfold Bleed had rebuilt the tissue. His body ran on damage, metabolized pain into strength. It was exactly what he'd engineered.

The tally was changing.

Three fights. Three Inversions. The Shard held at three-sixths. Capacity wasn't stability. The Aldric Echo had been quieter. Less commentary. That could mean adaptation. Or it could mean a buildup the Echo couldn't see.

The next match was in two hours. A Hardened-tier from the outer districts. Independent. Hungry.

Roan was still Tempered.

On paper, his Vitalis was 4,800. Standard. But the strikes were changing something deeper. The Shard was rewriting capacity at a cellular level. The Guild was watching.

"You're not sandbagging," Brendt said. "Just adjusting your approach. There are other techniques—"

"There aren't. Hollowstrike takes twenty minutes of setup. Crimson Wake needs prep time I won't get against someone who's seen me fight. The only technique that guarantees a win in under five minutes is Sevenfold Bleed."

"You don't need to guarantee the win yet. You need to survive to the Shadow Fist fight. *Then* guarantee it."

"And if I wait too long? If the Guild decides I'm containment before I face the envoy?" Roan's voice was flat. "I spend five years in a holding facility while they extract the Shard."

Brendt's jaw tightened. He had no response. Neither did Roan. The calculation had no good answer, only different kinds of bad.

"Two hours," Roan said.

He walked past Brendt. The waiting chamber for the quarterfinals was smoother stone, air carrying a trace of cedar. The fighters here had the look of people who'd stopped pretending.

Roan sat in the corner and ran the tally.

The Greel envoy was watching. The Guild was watching. Brendt was worried. Capacity held at three-sixths. Two more fights to the semifinals. Two more chances to face a Shadow Fist fighter. Or two more chances to get captured before the real match.

His hand drifted to his side. The Shard sat warm along his spine. Embedded since the first Inversion in the Bloodscourge alley. It couldn't be removed without dropping him for a week. A liability. If the Guild wanted to study it, they'd cut it out.

He had maybe two weeks before that became a serious concern.

The figures said he needed to win this tournament. *Win*. The Greel envoy had to see him do it publicly, with techniques that should have destroyed him. The figures said the envoy would underestimate him. That underestimate would be Roan's only advantage.

Unless the envoy never gave him the chance.

Footsteps at the chamber entrance.

A girl, maybe sixteen, in grey practice leathers. She was staring with the expression of someone deciding between running and asking for an autograph.

"You're Vance. The one who does the thing with the veins."

"I do several things with veins."

"The strikes. You hit yourself and the other guy falls down." She stepped closer. "I'm fighting in the next bracket. But there's a man watching from the Spectator tier. Silver hair, scar on his jaw. He asked about your matches."

Roan's stomach dropped. The tally paused.

"What kind of questions?"

"How many times you've used the strikes. Whether you look tired. Whether you ever hesitate." She swallowed. "I didn't tell him anything."

Silver hair. Scar on the jaw.

He knew who that was.

Drogan Hale. The Warden. His father's old friend. Disappeared after the Vance family's fall.

Here. Now. Watching Roan specifically.

His chest tightened. The ledger categorized the sensation as 'unknown variable.' Drogan Hale's return. Threat assessment: opaque.

"Thanks," he said. "Tell him whatever you want."

She left, confused.

Roan ran the ledger again.

Drogan Hale. Two years absent. Abandoned the Vance family after Aldric died. Now here, asking about technique, endurance, hesitation.

Not a coincidence. Test or threat. Roan didn't have enough information to know which.

Capacity: three-sixths. Vitalis: 4,800. In ninety minutes, a Hardened-tier fighter with nothing to lose.

And in the Spectator tier, his father's oldest friend was taking notes. Assessing whether the son was worth saving or silencing.

The equation remained honest.

But the balance didn't tell him what Drogan Hale wanted.
