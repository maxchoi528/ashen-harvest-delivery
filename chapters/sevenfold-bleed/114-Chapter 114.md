Chapter 114: The Northern Pressure

Roan Vance won his second match with his hands in his pockets.

Not actually. The pockets part was a detail the crowd would remember because it looked arrogant, but the truth was simpler: his right hand had been free because the Eastreach fighter's guard collapsed earlier than expected, and his left had been carrying a minor strain from the first round that he didn't want the telemetry to flag. Hands in pockets read as style. The medical pane read as normal recovery.

He let the crowd think whatever it wanted.

The arena lights shifted to the next introduction. Roan walked the tunnel toward the prep room, rolling his shoulder. Behind him, the commentator was still explaining why Eastreach's superior reach hadn't mattered. The explanation would be wrong in three ways, but the audience wouldn't care. Victories wrote their own narratives.

Brendt met him at the door. "Clean?"

"Clean enough."

"Clean enough is your version of a massacre."

Roan didn't correct him. The match had been clean in the sense that nothing had leaked past his control. The shoulder strike, the two-finger placement, the pivot that put his spine outside the opponent's counter-range — it had all landed inside acceptable telemetry variance. No flare. No signature. No reason for anyone watching to reach for a red flag.

But the telemetry hadn't liked it.

He'd felt the shard's warmth during the final step. Brief. Contained. The kind of pulse that happened when the system decided a classification was below its attention threshold.

He'd ignored it during the match.

Now, alone in the prep room with the door locked, he pulled up the post-match data on his personal pane.

The numbers looked fine.

Vitalis output: 2,100 peak. Tempered boundary, consistent with his public profile.

Recovery rate: unremarkable.

Impact force: within expected range.

Everything read as a tournament athlete who had trained hard and gotten lucky on angle.

He expanded the raw telemetry.

There.

One frame — less than a tenth of a second — had been tagged with a Medical Audit flag.

Roan read the tag twice.

Medical Audit was not unusual. The tournament medical board reviewed flagged data for injury patterns, doping traces, and liability risks. A thousand athletes accumulated Medical Audit tags per round. The tag itself meant nothing.

What meant something was that the tag had been applied retroactively.

The original timestamps showed a gap of six hours between the match and the flag. Live telemetry didn't wait six hours. Live telemetry flagged within seconds of anomaly detection. A retrospective Medical Audit meant someone had accessed the record after the match, reviewed the unreviewed full-spectrum data, and decided to make it look ordinary.

He checked the authorizing ID.

Clinical desk. Third shift. Name he didn't recognize.

Could be a real clerk catching up on backlog.

Could be a ghost.

Roan closed the pane and sat on the bench.

The shard's warmth had faded, but the memory of it pressed against his lower spine like a question. The anomaly had been there. He'd felt it — a fraction of recoil, a refusal to be classified. And someone had seen it in the high-resolution spectral layer. Seen it, categorized it as medical, and buried it under a dull explanation.

That was the interesting part.

If they had wanted him caught, they would have flagged it as an artifact anomaly or unauthorized modification. Combat Audit would trigger a coach inquiry. Artifact Audit would bring the Sentinels. Medical Audit brought an overworked clerk and a queue.

Whoever had done this wanted the data hidden, not exposed.

Roan considered the implications.

Someone had access to Tournament Central's full-spectrum telemetry. Someone had enough authority to apply a retrospective tag. Someone had chosen to hide rather than strike.

That meant they weren't ready to move publicly.

That meant they were watching.

That meant he had a window.

He pulled up the public bracket for the next round. Aldermere's main fighter. Eamon Cole. Higher rank, longer record, better sponsorship. A match that would draw more attention. A match where the telemetry would be monitored by more than just a hidden tagger.

Roan smiled without warmth.

If someone wanted to watch, he would give them something worth watching.

But not yet.

First, he needed to test the shape of the cage.

He opened a secure channel to Ines Carrow's office relay — not a direct message, just a query routed through the administrative help desk. Generic request: *Requesting clarification on medical tag threshold for tournament telemetry. Preparing for upcoming high-contact match.*

Not a question that would trigger alarms. Just a toe placed on the edge of the water.

He sent it.

Then he leaned back and let the silence of the prep room settle around him.

Brendt knocked twice. "You coming to watch the next match? Cyrus is up."

"In a minute."

"You're brooding."

"I'm thinking."

"Same thing, with you."

Brendt's footsteps retreated.

Roan stayed on the bench and let his mind run the perimeter.

The Northern pressure was real. He'd felt it during the match — not as a physical presence, but as a weight in the air, as if the arena had been observed from an angle that didn't belong to any spectator seat. The shard had responded to it. The data had been sanitized because of it.

Someone in the North had seen what he was.

And instead of calling down the storm, they had chosen to wait.

That told him more than any profile could.

He stood, rolled his shoulder again, and walked toward the door.

By the time he reached the spectator alcove, Cyrus was already stepping into the arena for his own match.

Roan watched the younger fighter move — fast, precise, hungry. Cyrus was good. He would get better. But he moved like someone who believed victory was the only column in the ledger.

Roan had been taught to count other columns.

He settled into the shadow of the alcove and waited for the next round to begin.

Somewhere beyond the city, in the gray Ironholm dawn, a plane was descending.

He didn't know that.

But he felt the chessboard tilt.
