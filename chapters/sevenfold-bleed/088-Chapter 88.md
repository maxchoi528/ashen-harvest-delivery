Chapter 88: The Ashford Invitation

Chapter 88: The Greel Probe

The eastern training yard was quiet at four in the morning. That was why Roan preferred it.

The other students gravitated toward the central arenas where the morning drills drew crowds and the instructors offered visible approval. Roan had no interest in crowds. Crowds meant comparison. Comparison meant questions. Questions meant complications with the Inversion, and complications weren't worth the marginal gain in sparring partners.

He moved through the third sequence of the Hollowstrike form, each motion a controlled violence. The doctrine required you to absorb your own reflected damage, which sounded counterintuitive until you understood the principle: every strike you threw carried a shadow strike that hit you back. Most students abandoned it within weeks. The ones who persisted treated the reflected damage as a tax, something to be endured rather than leveraged.

Roan had different math.

He adjusted his stance, shifted his weight to the left heel, and let the next form-complete deliver its penalty. The reflected blow hit his ribs like a hammer wrapped in cloth, pain without injury, since the damage was technically self-inflicted. But self-inflicted meant registered. Registered meant convertible.

[Sidewound absorbed.]

[Conversion successful: +31 Vitalis.]

[Capacity: 2/4. — A.V.]

Not a lot. The Hollowstrike reflected damage was shallow, designed for repetitive drilling rather than intensive conversion. But every bit accumulated. And unlike the campus shops, where he had to explain why he wanted bottles of inferior catalysts, no one questioned why a student practiced a doctrine that made him hit himself.

The morning air smelled of dew and iron. Greycross's industrial sector was waking up three kilometers west, its furnace-smoke staining the sky a permanent grey. Roan had grown up breathing that smoke. This was better.

He completed the sequence again, felt the reflected damage convert, watched his capacity tick down. Four minutes of this and he'd be empty for the day, and that was before whatever training Drogan had planned.

Footsteps. Not stealthy. Deliberate.

Roan let the final form-complete carry him into a standing position, then turned. Brendt Holloway crossed the yard at a diagonal, his uniform jacket unbuttoned and his hair still wet from a shower. He looked like someone who'd woken up ten minutes ago and dressed in the same timeframe.

"You're here early," Brendt said. Not a question.

"You're here at all." Roan picked up his water canteen. "I thought you valued sleep."

"I value not failing the mid-term assessments. Which requires training. Which requires being awake." Brendt dropped his bag near the yard's edge and began unwrapping hand bindings. "The instructors released the practical exam schedule yesterday. Third-year students need to demonstrate competency in at least three combat forms, plus a sparring evaluation."

He set that aside for later. Third-year students. That meant Brendt was operating under pressure to advance, or at least to not fall behind his cohort. The Black Spire ranking system wasn't purely based on Vitalis readings. Practical assessments carried weight, and practical assessments required visible competence.

"You've been drilling Hollowstrike," Brendt observed. "Every morning for the past month, if the rumors are accurate."

Rumors. Good. Let them think he was obsessing over a difficult form. Let them think it was a weakness, a fixation, a sign of poor talent selection. Better that than the truth.

"It trains the core," Roan said. Noncommittal. "The instructors recommend it for students plateauing below the 300 mark."

"The instructors recommend it as a supplementary drill. Not a primary focus." Brendt's hands kept moving through the binding routine, but his eyes were sharp. "You've been treating it like a primary focus."

Roan considered the exchange. Brendt wasn't stupid, he was asking a question without asking it. The unspoken query was something like: *What are you doing out here?*

But there was no good answer to that question. Not one Brendt would accept, anyway.

"I plateau below 300," Roan said. "I'm following recommendations. It's not more complicated than that."

"Alright." Brendt accepted the deflection, but his expression suggested he wasn't convinced. "Want to spar? I've been working on my footwork patterns, and I need someone who won't go easy on me."

The offer created a decision point. Sparring with Brendt was useful, his style was aggressive and direct, which meant Roan could practice reading offensive patterns without the complication of defensive specialists. But it was also a commitment. An hour of active combat against someone Brendt's level would generate significant side effects, and Roan hadn't decided whether to spend his remaining capacity on drilling or on fighting.

Capacity was a resource. Resources required allocation. Allocation required strategy.

"Rules?" Roan asked.

"Light contact. Submission or ring-out wins. No technique restrictions." Brendt stepped onto the practice ground, already settling into his stance. "I want to see how the Hollowstrike work plays against someone who isn't a training dummy."

Wooden dummy. Brendt was indicating he wasn't a training target. He wanted a real exchange.

Roan set down the canteen and moved to the opposite side of the marked boundary. The space was maybe ten meters square, adequate for basic sparring, too small for extended mobility. It would force close-quarters engagement.

"Tournament rules," Roan said. "Three-point maximum. First to score twice wins."

"Fine."

They met at center. Brendt immediately pressed forward, closing distance with the intent to dominate the center and force Roan into a defensive shell. Standard aggressive strategy. Against a student at Roan's apparent level, it was effective, most people at the 200–250 range couldn't handle sustained pressure from someone with Brendt's natural aggression.

Roan wasn't most people.

He stepped backward, maintaining distance, letting Brendt commit to the advance. The first exchange was a probing strike, a fast jab designed to test reaction speed. Roan let it pass his guard, felt it brush his shoulder. Light contact. Brendt wasn't trying to hurt, only to score.

The second exchange was a combination: jab, cross, hook. Brendt's combinations were solid, technically sound, and completely predictable. He'd trained in the Ashford Academy style before transferring to Black Spire, which meant he favored linear power patterns. Everything came in straight lines.

Roan adjusted his guard, slipped the hook, and landed a counter-strike to Brendt's ribs. Point one.

"Good." Brendt reset, eyes brightening. "You're reading my patterns."

"I'm watching your hips." Roan didn't elaborate. The hips told you everything, where weight transferred, which direction the next strike came from, whether the momentum was committed or retractable. Most students watched shoulders or eyes. Those were feint indicators. Hips were intention indicators.

The second exchange started. Brendt changed his approach, using more lateral movement, trying to disrupt the rhythm. He wasn't just attacking now, he was setting traps, creating patterns that Roan would expect, then breaking them.

Interesting. Brendt was smarter than his reputation suggested.

But smart wasn't enough. Not against someone who had the Inversion running calculations in the background.

Roan let the third exchange develop, absorbed a clean hit to his forearm, felt the impact convert into a fraction of Vitalis. The damage was minimal, but it existed. Everything existed if you knew how to measure it.

He scored the second point with a leg sweep that Brendt should have seen coming.

"Three-nothing," the watching instructor called. "Holloway wins."

Roan reset his stance and studied Brendt. The sparring had lasted less than five minutes, but it had generated useful data: Brendt's pattern recognition was improving, his aggression was more controlled, and he'd been deliberately testing whether Roan could adapt to surprises.

He could adapt. But he hadn't shown it, because showing it wasn't the goal.

The goal was to win without revealing capability.

"The sweep," Brendt said, catching his breath. "That was clean."

"Beginner's luck."

"You're below three hundred and you swept someone with a forty-six centimeter reach advantage." Brendt shook his head. "Luck isn't that clean."

Roan said nothing. He retrieved his canteen and began the cooldown stretches, hoping Brendt would take the hint and find a different sparring partner. But Brendt lingered, untying his hand bindings with the deliberate patience of someone who had more to say.

"The assessments," Brendt started. "Third-years need three combat form demonstrations. First and second-years need two. That includes Hollowstrike, if anyone's keeping track."

Roan kept his expression neutral. "Noted."

"I'm saying you should register Hollowstrike as one of your forms. The mid-term practicals are in a couple of months. If you demonstrate competency then, you get the credit for the year."

More words. More implications. Brendt was suggesting Roan should make the Hollowstrike work visible, officially credited rather than hidden in morning practice sessions. That meant visibility. Visibility meant attention. Attention meant questions.

"I'm not a third-year," Roan said.

"No, but you're past the two-form minimum. Which means if you want to compete for ranking placement, you'll need to demonstrate three forms by the end of term." Brendt's tone was matter-of-fact. "Most students at your level pick forms that are easy to score well on. Hollowstrike isn't easy. It's also not flashy. But it's practical. And practical counts in the rankings."

Roan considered the angle. Brendt was giving him information about the assessment structure, which meant either Brendt was genuinely trying to help, or Brendt was probing for reasons why Roan spent so much time drilling a form he seemed too weak to master.

Neither option changed the fundamental calculus. The Inversion worked regardless of whether Roan's training was visible to the instructors. Making Hollowstrike official would require demonstrating competency, which meant performing the forms under observation, which meant potential questions about why the reflected damage didn't seem to affect him.

But it would also provide cover. A legitimate reason to practice the doctrine without hiding.

"I'll think about it," Roan said.

"Don't think too long. Registration closes in ten days."

Brendt moved off to find another partner, leaving Roan alone with the practice yard and the lingering data from their exchange. He'd scored two points and absorbed a minor Sidewound. Not the most productive session, but not a waste either.

Ten minutes later, the eastern yard was filling up with other early risers. The cluster meant complications. Roan gathered his equipment and headed toward the eastern gate, plotting his route to the secondary training grounds where the afternoon's scheduled sessions would take place.

The path crossed through a narrow corridor between the residential blocks. Morning traffic was light, the corridors were mostly empty except for a pair of second-year students walking in the opposite direction. They didn't look at Roan. He didn't look at them.

Halfway through the corridor, a door opened.

The Greel envoy stepped out.

The encounter wasn't random. The envoy was a fourth-year from House Greel, which meant he operated under a different set of expectations than the average student. House Greel had connections to the Founding Seven through Vorath Greel, the Northern Crown himself. That kind of lineage generated privilege, and privilege generated arrogance.

The envoy's expression made clear he wasn't interested in social pleasantries.

"Vance." The name came out like a curse. "You're the one who scored against Theon Mercer in last week's evaluation."

Mercer. Right. The second-year from House Ashford who'd been complaining about unfair judging in the sparring rankings. Apparently his loss to Roan had generated enough noise to reach other Houses.

"I scored a point," Roan said carefully. "Mercer scored two. The match wasn't close."

"Mercer is supposed to be the best in your cohort. He's supposed to receive sponsorship consideration by the end of term." The envoy stepped into the corridor, blocking the path. "Instead he loses to someone who can't break two-fifty on the Vitalis scale. Explain that."

The demand wasn't rational. Sparring outcomes depended on more than raw Vitalis readings, strategy, technique and psychology, plus simple matchup variance all played roles. But the envoy wasn't asking for explanation. The envoy was asserting dominance. He wanted Roan to either submit to questioning or to react in a way that justified escalation.

Option three: neither.

"Mercer relies on reach and linear striking," Roan said. Neutral. Factual. "I adjusted for his timing. That's not mysterious. It's just practice."

"Mercer practices six hours a day. Every day. You think you out-practice someone with better resources and better guidance?"

"I don't think anything. I observe. Mercer has excellent fundamentals and poor adaptation speed. That's a measurable pattern, not an insult to House Ashford."

The envoy's jaw tightened. The response had been technically deferential while still implying that Mercer's loss was his own fault. Diplomatic enough to avoid direct insult, disrespectful enough to register as dismissive.

The envoy wasn't interested in diplomatic.

"House Greel has been watching the rankings," the envoy said. "We've noticed some interesting discrepancies. Students at the bottom of the scale consistently outperforming students with better Vitalis readings. Students with questionable backgrounds rising faster than students with legitimate lineage." He paused. "The Conclave has protocols for identifying fraudulent claims. Vitalis readings can be verified. Combat form certifications can be audited. Training hour requirements can be confirmed."

The implication was clear. Someone was investigating him. House Greel was looking for reasons to challenge his advancement, and if they found any procedural irregularities, they could petition the Sentinel Bureau to audit his progress.

Roan ran the ledger. The Inversion gave him advantages that weren't visible on standard measurements, but it didn't violate any formal rules, unless someone decided that converting side effects into gains was fraud rather than technique. That was a gray area. A gray area that someone with enough influence could make very bright indeed.

"I appreciate the warning," Roan said. "If there's a formal inquiry, I'll cooperate fully."

"Cooperation is good." The envoy smiled. It didn't reach his eyes. "But cooperation won't help if the discrepancies are too large to explain. Some students rise too fast, then fall too far. Natural consequences."

The corridor felt smaller. The envoy's two companions flanked the path behind him, third-years from the looks of their uniforms, neither speaking but both watching. The configuration suggested the envoy had planned this encounter. Not a random challenge, but a structured intimidation.

The question was whether to engage or deflect.

Roan chose deflection. The envoy wasn't the real threat, he was a probe, sent by House Greel to measure response. Responding with aggression would confirm the threat was working. Responding with submission would confirm he was vulnerable. The correct move was to give nothing.

"I'll keep that in mind," Roan said. He stepped around the envoy, maintaining enough distance to avoid physical contact, and continued down the corridor. No hurry. No retreat. Just navigation.

The envoy didn't follow.

The corridor opened onto the main campus path, and Roan allowed himself a brief assessment. The envoy's threat was real but not immediate. House Greel was investigating discrepancies, which meant they had noticed something worth investigating. But investigation took time, and time meant opportunity. A couple of months until the mid-term assessments. Ten days until Hollowstrike registration closed.

The balance kept changing. New variables entered the equation. The question was whether those variables represented opportunity or constraint.

He reached the secondary training grounds and found his assigned session already in progress. Instructor Vale was demonstrating a modified version of the Sevenfold Bleed's second form, a version that incorporated more defensive footwork in exchange for reduced striking power. It was an odd choice for a combat institute. Defensive emphasis contradicted the Black Spire's offensive philosophy.

But it matched what Roan had observed in the actual Sevenfold Bleed texts. The second form wasn't just about attack, it was about position. You couldn't bleed an opponent if you were out of range. The strike only worked if you controlled the geometry.

He joined the formation and began the sequence.

The days blurred into a rhythm: morning drilling, afternoon sessions, evening inventory. Roan's capacity tracking became a second habit, every conversion logged, every expenditure weighed against potential gain. The Hollowstrike practice generated shallow returns but consistent ones. The sparring sessions generated more interesting data: Brendt's patterns, Mercer's adaptations, various opponents with various styles.

House Greel's investigation didn't surface publicly. But the absence of inquiry felt more ominous than inquiry itself. Silence meant planning. Planning meant eventually the planning would become action.

Nine days before registration closed, Brendt found him again.

"You registered Hollowstrike yet?"

Roan shook his head. "Still thinking about it."

"Thinking is expensive." Brendt set down his bag, then produced a folded document. "The instructors released the assessment schedule. Hollowstrike demonstrations are scheduled for week four. If you don't register by day ten, you can't demonstrate."

Day ten. One day from now.

The deadline crystallized something. Waiting had strategic value, delay reduced exposure, reduced exposure reduced risk. But delay also reduced options. If he didn't register now, he wouldn't have the opportunity to demonstrate the form for credit. And if House Greel was building a case against him, unverified capability was harder to defend than officially credited competence.

"Alright," Roan said. "I'll register today."

"Good." Brendt's expression shifted, something like approval, or maybe relief. "I wasn't sure you'd do it."

"Neither was I," Roan said honestly.

The registration office was on the second floor of the administrative building. Roan submitted the Hollowstrike declaration form, watched the clerk stamp it and file it, and left with a confirmation slip that meant nothing and everything simultaneously.

He had officially committed to demonstrating a combat form that was supposed to hurt its user.

Now he had to figure out how to do that without exposing the Inversion.

[Capacity restored: 4/4.]

[Planning phase engaged. — A.V.]

Two days later, Instructor Vale summoned him after the regular session.

"The registration came through," she said. "Hollowstrike. Interesting choice."

"The form has practical applications."

"It does. It also has practical penalties." Vale's expression was neutral, but her eyes were sharp. "The reflected damage mechanism generates cumulative stress. Most students who practice it seriously report fatigue, minor injuries, and reduced performance in other forms during intensive drilling periods."

Roan nodded. This was the opening. Vale was establishing the expected costs, which meant she would be watching for whether he displayed those costs.

"I understand the penalties," he said. "I'll manage them."

"Manage them carefully. The demonstration assessment is in sixteen days. If you can't perform at standard competency, you fail the form entirely. That counts against your overall ranking."

"I know."

Vale studied him. "You've been drilling every morning. Observed consistency. But you haven't complained about the penalties. You haven't requested modifications or alternative forms. You haven't shown the typical stress patterns."

More sharp observation. Vale was probing, not accusing, but the distinction was academic.

"I'm aware of my limits," Roan said.

"That's good. Awareness is important." Vale made a note on her roster. "Continue your preparation. I'll be observing the demonstration personally."

She dismissed him. Roan walked back to the training grounds, running calculations. Vale had noticed something. She hadn't identified it, nothing in her questions suggested knowledge of the Inversion, but she'd identified anomaly. A student practicing a high-cost form without displaying costs was a student worth watching.

That was fine. Being watched by one instructor was manageable. Being watched by House Greel's investigators wasn't.

He needed to engineer the demonstration to show enough competency to pass while generating enough visible cost to satisfy expectations. The arithmetic was complicated: too much performance and they'd question how he maintained it; too much cost and he'd have to sustain the damage before converting it.

The solution was timing. The damage had to be absorbed and converted in real time, but the conversion would eliminate the visible symptoms. If he could delay the conversion by half a second, just long enough to let the damage register as visible before it disappeared, then he'd show the expected penalties while maintaining the actual benefit.

He spent the next twelve days testing the timing.

The first attempt: conversion too fast, damage disappeared before anyone could see it. No good. Vale was watching for absence of symptoms.

The second attempt: conversion delayed too long, actual damage accumulated faster than he could manage. He ended up with genuine bruises and a concerned visit from the campus healer.

Third attempt: partial delay. Let the damage register for a fraction of a second, then convert. Visible bruising without actual injury. Minimal Vitalis yield but acceptable appearance.

By the twelfth day, he'd refined the timing to a pattern. Half a second of visible damage, then conversion. The damage would look like the expected penalties. The conversion would eliminate actual harm.

Vale observed his final practice session and made notes. She didn't say anything, but her expression suggested she'd noticed something she couldn't identify.

"Don't overthink it," she said at the end. "The demonstration is about showing what you've learned. Not about showing what you can hide."

The words landed like a warning.

Roan filed them away. He knew what he was doing. He knew the risks. He knew the consequences if the timing failed.

Sixteen days seemed like a long time. Sixteen days also seemed like nothing at all.

He went back to the eastern yard that evening and ran through the Hollowstrike forms one more time. The reflected damage hit his ribs, his arms, his legs, each impact registered, converted, accumulated. His Vitalis crept upward by fractions. His capacity ticked down from four to zero.

When he finished, he sat on the edge of the practice ground and watched the industrial smoke stain the sky.

House Greel was watching. House Ashford was watching. Vale was watching.

And somewhere in the math, there had to be a solution that let him pass the demonstration without revealing what he was.

The math was correct. He just hadn't isolated the timing variable yet. He would.
