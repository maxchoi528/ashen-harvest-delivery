Chapter 69: Waste Pill Economics

The Spire's waste management contract was a masterclass in institutional laziness.

Three thousand credits a ton. That was the figure the administration paid Korvan Sanitation to haul away the failed potion stock. Failed, in the Spire's definition, meant any batch that fell below ninety-four percent purity. The actual potency curve for most of the flagged material hovered just above ninety-one percent. Functional. Saleable. Profitable. The difference between "failed" and "expired combat-grade" was a matter of chemistry, accounting, and an audit trail that no one had bothered to follow in a decade.

Roan had spent the last three nights cross-referencing manifests. The volume was staggering. Twelve hundred pounds of degraded Bloodscourge per quarter. Another six hundred of mixed stimulants. A smaller fraction of failed Pyrebrand. All of it destined for a furnace in the lower districts, all of it logged as inert, and none of it accounted for after the incineration receipt was filed.

The arithmetic resolved cleanly.

If he could intercept the stream before it reached Korvan's team—reroute it through an internal Spire channel—he could turn a cost centre into a private supply line. The Spire saved on disposal fees. Korvan lost the contract. Roan gained a permanent engine for his conversion cycles.

It was exactly the kind of leverage he needed. Simple. Institutional. Invisible.

The sub-level corridor was colder than the main floors, the air thick with the smell of old concrete and chemical residue. Roan moved through the service door at a measured pace, no hesitation, his footsteps absorbed by decades of grime embedded in the stone. He carried a satchel and a set of sample cases. If anyone stopped him, he was a third-year assisting with an environmental audit. The paperwork for that cover was in his belt, stamped with Ines's procedural code.

No one stopped him.

Cargo hold B-7 sat at the end of the corridor, a reinforced chamber with a steel door and a mechanical lock that had been upgraded three years ago and never re-keyed. The rotation of the guards was predictable. The blind spot in the camera coverage was wide enough to drive a cart through.

Roan reached the door, slotting the key Ines had provided—a stamped metal token that opened exactly four doors in the entire Black Spire facility. She hadn't asked what he needed it for. Brendt had said she wouldn't. "She's not stupid. She knows you're running something. She also knows you're not running it for yourself."

The lock clicked. The door swung inward.

The hold was full of crates. Organized with a military precision that had eroded into chaos over years of underfunded maintenance. The disposal manifest said the flagged shipment was in the back row.

Roan walked directly to it. No scanning. No hesitation. He had already mapped the layout from the building schematics Ines had slipped him. The map was the plan. The execution was just geometry.

He opened the first crate. Twelve vials of Bloodscourge, dark red, slightly darker than the standard formulation. Degraded by humidity. Potency loss of roughly two-point-seven percent based on colour density. The Spire's threshold was conservative by eight percent.

Every single one of them was still good.

He loaded the samples into his case. Eleven more in the second crate. Ten in the third. Thirty-three doses total. Enough to run his Inversion cycle at current yields for the next six weeks if he rationed carefully.

But he wasn't going to ration them. He was going to weaponize them. Convert the side effects into sustained training output, turn the waste stream into a six-week sprint that would put him six months ahead of schedule.

The fourth crate contained something different.

Roan stopped, one vial in his hand, and studied the label. The handwriting was different from the others. Neater. More deliberate. The same disposal stencil, but the batch number beneath was marked with a note: *Lot 19. Pending Review.*

He had no context for that note. He filed it.

Then he heard the footsteps.

Not the guard rotation. The guard rotation wouldn't be due for another nine minutes. These were lighter, deliberate, coming down the corridor with the unhurried pace of someone who knew exactly where they were going.

Roan set the vial down. Moved behind the third row of crates. Pressed flat against the cold steel. Breathing through his mouth.

The door swung open.

A figure stepped through. Female. Athletic build. Movement precise, weight centred on the balls of her feet. She crossed directly to the third row—directly to the spot where Roan had been standing. She crouched, pulled a small notebook from her jacket, and started counting the vials on the shelf.

She was inventorying the stock.

*She works for Korvan.*

Roan adjusted his model. Korvan had a local presence. Someone who audited the waste before it was hauled. Ensuring the Skim ratio—taking the best of the "failed" stock for grey-market resale—was maximized before they passed the load off.

This woman was that presence.

He could wait her out. Let her finish her count, file her paperwork, leave. Then he could proceed with his samples. The risk was minimal.

But the arithmetic was incomplete.

If she was here, she would notice the crate he'd opened. She would flag it. The security review would tighten. His window would close.

*Better to turn the problem into leverage.*

Roan moved before she reached the door.

He came out of the shadow at an angle that put him between her and the exit, denying the escape route, forcing the confrontation into the enclosed space. Her reaction was fast. She pivoted, dropped her weight, both hands coming up in a guard position that was standard Spire curriculum adapted for tight quarters.

"You're not Korvan," she said.

"Neither are you."

"I work for the route operator. This is my territory."

Roan held his ground. "Your territory is built on a false economy. Korvan pays the Spire a flat fee for the entire waste stream. You skim the viable stock and sell it on the side. The margin is reasonable, but it's limited by the hauling contract."

She didn't answer. Her eyes tracked his stance, his hands, the exit behind him.

"The hauling contract is renewable annually," Roan continued. "Korvan's profit margin is tied to volume, not selectivity. But if the Spire's administration discovered that their waste management partner was running a secondary sales operation through the same channel..."

"You think you can threaten me out of my job?"

"I think you're smart enough to recognize a better offer."

The silence stretched. Her weight shifted, almost imperceptibly, toward the centre line.

"I'm not cutting out Korvan," Roan said. "This isn't a takeover. It's a refinement. I'll take specific potion classes—Bloodscourge, the degraded Pyrebrand, the compounds Korvan can't move on the open market without raising flags. I'll pay my share to the Spire as an internal recycling credit. Korvan keeps the rest of the waste stream, keeps the contract, keeps the plausible deniability. You keep your position and add a quiet percentage from my route."

She studied him. The calculation running behind her eyes was the same kind of arithmetic he would have performed in her place. Threat assessment. Asset evaluation. Feasibility.

"You've done the math," she said.

"I always do the math."

"And if I refuse?"

"Then I file a procedural complaint with the Spire's procurement office and let the whole contract collapse. Korvan loses the account. You lose the income. I find another way to access the stock." He held her gaze. "But that's worse for everyone. Including me."

Another pause. Then her shoulders dropped half a degree. Not surrender. Acceptance of a new equilibrium.

"You run this through a legitimate Spire channel, and I get a finder's fee."

"Ten percent."

"Fifteen."

"Twelve, and I cover the documentation costs."

She extended her hand. Roan took it. The grip was firm, professional.

"What's your name?" she asked.

"File it under the Internal Waste Recycling Initiative. The paperwork will be processed by the end of the week."

She smiled. It didn't touch her eyes. But the threat had been resolved into a transaction.

"I'll be watching the balance," she said. "Don't make me regret the math."

She left. The door clicked shut behind her.

Roan stood in the dark for thirty seconds, letting the adrenaline settle. The Bloodscourge samples were secure in his case. The encounter had consumed four minutes. He still had time to complete the circuit.

He took the samples. Left the rest of the stock for her inventory.

The corridor outside was silent. The guard rotation was still two minutes out.

Roan climbed back through the service stairs, counting each step as a beat toward the upper campus. The weight of the samples was nothing compared to the weight of the commitment he had just made. He was no longer a scavenger operating at the edges of the system. He had become a node inside it. A node that processed waste into fuel, fuel into power.

The engine was built.

Now he just had to keep it running.

---

Ines was waiting in the administrative alcove, a stack of forms already prepared. She didn't ask about the encounter. She handed him the documents and watched him sign.

"Internal Waste Recycling Initiative," she read aloud. "It sounds like a student project."

"It is a student project. Funded through the alchemy department's waste diversion budget."

"There is no waste diversion budget."

"There is now."

She slid the forms into her folder, shaking her head. "You're going to get us both audited."

"Not if the numbers hold." Roan stood, pocketing the copy of the approval. "The Spire pays three thousand a ton for disposal. The Internal Initiative charges them two thousand a ton for selective recycling. They save a thousand. Korvan loses the contract on the high-value fractions. Everyone who needs to win, wins."

Ines's lips pressed together. She didn't agree with him—he could see the calculation in her eyes—but she didn't argue.

"The approval takes effect at the start of next week," she said. "Your route starts Monday."

Roan nodded. It was done.

---

The basement of the Vance apartment smelled like concrete and the lingering trace of his Bloodscourge conversion. Roan sat on the floor, the sample case open beside him, the first vial in his hand.

Twelve vials. Thirty-three doses. A starting engine.

He swallowed the first dose.

The burn hit immediately, the familiar heat of Bloodscourge flooding his Vitalis pathways, the side effect pressing against his nervous system like a vice. But he didn't resist it. He let it come. Let it build.

Then he reached for the Inversion.

The reversal was instantaneous. The pressure rerouted into a sustained acceleration of his condensation rate. The copper taste dissolved into clarity. The Vitalis in his core thickened, compacted, folding into itself with the efficiency of a well-tuned machine.

The arithmetic settled.

The engine was running. It would need fuel. It would need protection from the attention it would inevitably draw. But it ran.

*Six to go.*

Roan looked at the remaining vials. Each one represented a step. A resource. A narrowing of the distance between him and the first name on the list.

The Codex Shard pulsed once against his spine. Warm. Approving. He felt Aldric's echo in the vibration, not in words, but in a feeling of alignment. The same strategy Aldric would have used. Turn the waste into the weapon.

Roan set the empty vial down.

Monday was three days away. He had fuel to process, a route to secure, and a world of institutional inertia to exploit.

He began the count for the second dose.
