Chapter 12: Lyra's Doubt

The morning training yard smelled of sweat and wet iron. Third-bell drills. Seventeen initiates running formation while Warden Drogan walked the perimeter with his iron hand clasped behind his back, eyes tracking every stumble.

Roan wasn't running formation.

He stood at the edge of the yard, shoulder blades against the cold stone wall, watching the same drill for the third time this week. The tally was plain. Formation running built discipline. Discipline built compliance. Compliance built predictability. Predictability built victims.

He'd done the balance six years ago in a police station hallway. He was doing it again now.

"Running again?"

Brendt appeared at his elbow carrying two canteens. He handed one over without asking. The metal was dented and sweating. Someone had carved *HOLLOWAY WAS HERE* into the side with what looked like a fork.

"You know the rules," Brendt continued, settling against the wall beside him. "Second week initiates run formation. No exceptions. If Drogan sees you standing—"

"Drogan sees me."

Brendt followed his gaze. The Warden had indeed turned. Had indeed made eye contact. Had then turned away and continued his circuit without a word.

"—and he doesn't care," Brendt finished, slightly deflated. "Right. Because you're special."

"I'm not special." Roan uncapped the canteen. Drank. Water, faintly alkaline, trace minerals. "I'm not running because my body doesn't need the discipline. It needs recovery."

Recovery. That was the word he used outwardly. Inwardly, the readout was different.

The Codex Shard had been quiet for thirty-one hours. Since the night he'd woken on the floor of his quarters with blood on his lips and the taste of copper on his tongue, the system had displayed nothing. No notifications. No Aldric quips. No capacity warnings.

The Shard was rebuilding. Processing the seven hours of continuous inversion from the night before, the sustained drainage of his Vitalis reserves, the reabsorption of the Bloodscourge's burning, the involuntary conversion of exhaustion into something that felt almost like fuel.

He'd consumed himself. The Codex Shard had converted that self-consumption into growth.

[Inversion depth: expanding.]
[Recovery status: rebuilding.]

The system notification appeared in his peripheral vision, faint as a half-remembered dream. He blinked and it was gone.

"Roan."

He realized Brendt had been talking. Brendt's jaw was tight. Not angry. Brendt didn't get angry at him, which was its own small miracle. Annoyed, then. Or concerned. The distinction was difficult to parse.

"Sorry," Roan said. Not sorry. He did it automatically now, the social calibration he'd been practicing since eleven years old in a police station where he'd learned that silence was a cage and words were keys. "What were you saying?"

"I was saying that Selene Ardent asked about you. Three times yesterday. That's unusual."

"Unusual how?"

"Unusual for Selene." Brendt made a face. "She doesn't ask about people. She observes them and files them into categories. *Useful. Useless. Threat.* You've been filed."

"Under which?"

"That's the problem. She didn't tell me. She just asked if you were keeping pace with the cohort's physical benchmarks. I said yes." Brendt paused. "I also said you were unremarkable. She smiled and walked away."

"She's testing you."

"She's testing you." Brendt's voice dropped. "I told her you were unremarkable because that's what I always tell people about you. It's our system. You remember. She looked at me like I was lying. She's smart, Roan. She's the second-most dangerous person in our year."

"Who's the first?"

Brendt gestured with his chin toward the far end of the yard.

Cyrus Ashford was running the obstacle course. Not formation drills, the obstacle course, which was reserved for students past their first month. He'd convinced someone to unlock the gate. He was moving through the timed sequence with mechanical precision: vault, climb, crawl, balance, sprint. His Vitalis reading was barely elevated. He was performing at maybe sixty percent capacity.

He was showing off.

More specifically, he was showing the proctors that the obstacle course was easy. That anyone who couldn't clear it was beneath notice. That he wasn't anyone.

"He hit Forged threshold last month," Brendt said quietly. "He's been sitting on it. Waiting for the right moment to break through publicly. He wants the Headmaster's attention before the junior rankings next term."

Roan watched. The arithmetic was quick.

Cyrus finished the course in four minutes and twelve seconds. The qualifying time was six minutes. He'd beaten it by nearly two minutes and hadn't broken a sweat. On his last obstacle, the hanging rope descent, he let go early, dropped the last four feet, and landed with a controlled roll that he held for exactly one second longer than necessary. Deliberate. Showmanship without the appearance of showmanship.

"He'll break through publicly within the week," Roan said.

"How do you—"

"His Vitalis is too stable. He's modulating his output to look like he's working hard. But his breathing is even and his hands aren't shaking. He's holding back at least forty percent of his actual output. That means he's got reserves he's not touching. Why hold reserves? Because he's preparing for something that requires a full tank."

Brendt considered this. "The junior rankings are the obvious answer."

"Too obvious. He'll break through before the rankings. Within three days. He wants the proctors distracted by his breakthrough announcement when the rankings come out, so they're predisposed to score him generously. It's a psychological anchor."

"You think he'll do it at the morning assembly?"

"Or the sparring exhibition tomorrow. Somewhere public. Somewhere with witnesses." Roan capped his canteen. "It doesn't matter. He's making a move. Everyone who's paying attention will notice. The ones who aren't paying attention will still feel the shift."

"And you?"

Roan didn't answer immediately. He was watching Cyrus walk off the course, wiping his hands on a cloth, exchanging a few words with one of the proctors. Casual. Friendly. The proctor laughed.

The question Brendt was asking was: *what are you going to do about it?*

Roan had three options. He could let Cyrus execute his plan and stay beneath notice. Safe. Preferred. The figures said the less attention he drew before his Inversion capacity stabilized, the better. He'd already used three of his three daily inversions in the last thirty-one hours. If he ran into a situation that required rapid conversion, he was operating on fumes.

He could also sabotage Cyrus's announcement. Leak a rumor, tip off a proctor, manufacture some minor scandal. But that required effort and created risk. Cyrus was connected and vindictive, then would trace the source eventually. The cost-benefit analysis was unfavorable.

Option three was simpler.

He could do nothing to Cyrus, and instead do something for himself. The blood he'd coughed up the night before had tasted like iron and ozone. The Codex Shard had been rebuilding ever since. The recalibration had not yet completed. That recalibration was the variable.

His father's notes, the ones Brendt had retrieved from the Hollow Library, contained a passage Roan had read eleven times:

*The Inversion doesn't create power. It redirects suffering. The Codex Shard is a vessel, not a source. Fill it too quickly and the vessel cracks. Fill it correctly and it expands.*

Fill it correctly. What did that mean, exactly?

The Codex Shard had been expanding since the night of the Bloodscourge. The daily capacity had ticked upward once: from three inversions to four. The conversion rate, based on the yield from his last three successful inversions, was holding steady at one hundred and ten percent. For every point of Sidewound absorbed, he was getting back one point one of Vitalis, plus a fractional bonus that was accumulating in the Shard's depth layer like interest in an account no one else knew existed.

If he could trigger a recalibration event, something that pushed the Shard to reassess its capacity, the daily limit might increase again. Four to five inversions per day. Five was significantly better than four. Five was the difference between having enough capacity to handle an unexpected threat and being caught flat-footed.

But recalibration events weren't triggered by will. They were triggered by stress. By pushing the system to the edge of its tolerances. By suffering damage that the Shard had to work to absorb.

The obstacle course wouldn't do that. Running formation wouldn't do that. Sitting in lectures and nodding at Drogan's explanations of Vitalis circulation wouldn't do that.

He needed something painful. Something with enough side effect to register on the Shard's tolerance threshold.

"You're doing that thing," Brendt said.

"What thing."

"The thing where you go quiet and your eyes do that thing like you're reading invisible math off the inside of your eyelids."

"I don't have a thing."

"You absolutely have a thing. It's terrifying. Even Selene noticed and she doesn't notice anything that isn't directly related to her advancement."

Roan handed back the canteen. "I need to borrow Spire Credits."

"How many?"

"Three hundred."

Brendt whistled. That was a significant amount for an initiate. Enough to buy a mid-tier catalyst off the authorized market. Or enough to bribe a junior proctor for access to the restricted training rooms, the ones with the environmental simulation equipment.

"For what?"

"Testing something."

Brendt studied him. His brown eyes were steady. Brendt wasn't stupid. He was the opposite of stupid. He was someone who absorbed information quietly and cross-referenced it against everything else he knew before drawing conclusions.

Roan respected that, even when it was inconvenient.

"I'm not going to tell you," Roan said.

"I know."

"You'll lend them anyway."

"Probably." Brendt pushed off the wall. "I'm transferring them now. Don't do anything stupid."

"I don't do stupid things. I do things that look stupid from a position of insufficient information."

"Copy that." Brendt started walking, then stopped. "The sparring exhibition. Are you going to be in it?"

Roan considered. The sparring exhibition was technically voluntary. Most initiates avoided it during their first month, it was a chance for the older students to haze the new ones under the guise of training. Cyrus would almost certainly be there. So would Selene, watching from the observation tier.

"I don't know yet," Roan said.

Brendt nodded and left.

- break -

By afternoon, the credits had cleared.

Roan was sitting in the campus pharmacy with a small amber bottle on the counter in front of him. The pharmacist, a pinched woman with gray hair and the perpetual expression of someone who had heard every excuse in the world, looked at him, looked at the bottle, looked back at him.

"Sablefang extract," she said. "You understand the risks."

"Yes."

"You understand this is a Tier One catalyst. Forged-tier students use this. You are—"

"Initiate peak. I know."

"Close to threshold, then. Still." She leaned forward. Her breath smelled like mint and something medicinal. "Sablefang venom acts on the nervous system. The intended effect is a rapid spike in Vitalis output, thirty percent increase for approximately four minutes. The side effects—"

"Include a fifty percent chance of cardiac arrest in individuals below Hardened tier," Roan finished. "I read the documentation."

"Then you also read that the documentation recommends a minimum two-week recovery period between doses."

"Forty-eight hours is the minimum medical standard. Two weeks is the recommended interval for optimal Vitalis integration." He slid the credits across the counter. "I'm a fast integrator."

The pharmacist held his gaze. He watched her do the math: initiate, no visible advancement signs, asking for a Tier One catalyst with known mortality risk, offering credits without negotiation.

She took the credits.

"Sign here. And here. And initial that you understand that Black Spire Combat Institute accepts no liability for—" She pushed a form across the counter. He signed where indicated. She bagged the bottle. "Take it to the supervised consumption room. Second floor, west wing."

"I will."

She didn't believe him. He didn't care.

He walked out of the pharmacy with the bottle in his jacket pocket, feeling the faint warmth of the glass through the fabric. Sablefang extract was unstable. Unpredictable. It was a catalyst that sensible people avoided and desperate people abused.

Roan was neither. He was calculating.

The Codex Shard handled poison the same way it handled burns, blood loss, and neural overload—as a Sidewound. The Sablefang venom would hit his system, create a cascade of damage, and the Shard would attempt to convert that damage into Vitalis. At his current conversion rate of one hundred and ten percent, a successful conversion would yield a solid net gain. Even an incomplete conversion—if his heart survived the attempt—would stress the Shard enough to measure its limits. The fifty percent chance of cardiac arrest was the variable. If the Shard could absorb and invert the venom's effects quickly enough, his heart would hold. If it couldn't, if the processing lag or the recovery strain prevented full conversion, then the venom would do what venom did.

Either answer was useful. Mapped limits were worth more than safe guesses.

His hand went to the back of his neck, where the Shard had embedded itself during the Bloodscourge ceremony. The spot was warm. Always warm, now. Like a second pulse.

The Shard was still rebuilding. If he took the Sablefang now, while the system was still processing the previous strain, the probability of a successful conversion was lower than it would be after the recalibration completed.

That was the point.

He needed to know what happened when the system was stressed beyond its recovery capacity. He needed to know the breaking point. He'd rather learn it here, in a controlled environment, than discover it during a real fight.

He needed to push the vessel until it cracked.

Or didn't crack. Either answer was useful. Cracking meant the Shard had limits that needed to be mapped. Not cracking meant the Shard was more resilient than the documentation suggested, which opened different options.

"Roan."

He stopped. Selene Ardent was leaning against the corridor wall twenty feet ahead, arms crossed, watching him with the stillness that meant she had been waiting.

Not good.

He'd been so focused on the Sablefang plan that he'd walked the most direct route back to his quarters. The corridor connected the pharmacy wing to the residential halls. It was also the route that passed closest to the observation tier where Selene's dormitory windows faced.

She'd seen him come out. She'd had time to intercept.

He adjusted his trajectory by half a step, ostensibly to avoid a maintenance cart, to put a bench between them, and kept walking. The Sablefang bottle was in his left pocket. His hand rested near it, casual, ready.

"I was hoping to catch you," Selene said, falling into step beside him without apparent effort. She was three inches taller than him and moved like someone who'd been trained to fill space without threatening it. A useful skill in politics. A dangerous one in combat. "You've been absent from the afternoon drills for two weeks. The proctors are starting to talk."

"The proctors can afford to talk."

"True. Drogan covered for you. Said you were on supplementary conditioning." She tilted her head. "Is that accurate?"

"Yes."

Lie or truth. The mix-and-match approach. Give them enough real information that they'd stop digging for the parts that weren't. Drogan had indeed authorized supplementary conditioning, it just wasn't the real reason for his absences.

"And the pharmacy visit?"

She'd seen that too. Of course she had.

"Sablefang extract," he said, because lying to Selene about something she could verify in thirty seconds was a waste of everyone's time. "Tier One catalyst. For the supplementary conditioning."

Her eyebrows rose by a millimeter. Selene Ardent, for whom surprise was apparently a quantifiable reaction. "You're self-administering Sablefang venom. Without supervision. At Initiate tier."

"Yes."

"Do you have a death wish?"

"No. I have a tolerance for risk that exceeds conventional medical guidelines."

"That's not comforting."

"It wasn't meant to be comforting."

She was quiet for a few steps. He counted: one, two, three, four. She was processing. Selene processed everything. She was already running scenarios, calculating the probability that he'd survive, the probability that he'd die, the probability that his survival or death would affect her plans in some way.

"Brendt vouched for you," she said finally. "He said you were unremarkable. He was wrong."

"I'm aware."

"But he vouched for you anyway. That means something." She stopped walking. He stopped because she was blocking the corridor. "Whatever you're doing, the Sablefang, the supplementary conditioning, whatever is making you absent from drills, make sure you can justify it if someone with authority asks."

"I'm not planning to be asked."

"People always plan not to be asked. Then they get asked." Her gray eyes held his. "I'm not offering this as a favor. I'm telling you because Brendt trusts you, and Brendt is the only person in this cohort who has been useful to me in a measurable way. I protect my investments."

"Noted."

She stepped aside. He walked past. He was ten steps past her when she spoke again.

"The sparring exhibition. Tomorrow. You should attend."

He didn't turn around. "Why?"

"Because Cyrus Ashford is going to make a scene. Something will go wrong. Someone will get hurt who shouldn't." A pause. "It would be useful to have someone in the room who is watching and not participating. Someone who notices things."

She was recruiting him. Or testing whether he could be recruited. The Selene Ardent information-gathering network had a gap, something she couldn't see from the observation tier, something that happened in the room itself. She wanted an asset inside.

The tally: she was offering information about tomorrow's exhibition in exchange for his presence. The information was useful. The assignment was low-risk. Attending the exhibition as a spectator was well within his cover.

But Selene's *favors* were never free. If he attended on her behalf, he'd be in her ledger. That was a cost he couldn't fully calculate yet.

"I'll be there," he said, and kept walking.

Behind him, he heard her footsteps recede in the opposite direction.

- break -

Evening. His quarters. Door locked, window shuttered, the single desk lamp casting a circle of yellow light over the bottle.

The Sablefang extract caught the light and glowed like trapped amber. Inside, something moved, not liquid surface tension, but something alive. The venom was harvested from the hollow fangs of a Sablefang viper, preserved in a stabilizing solution. It was still technically alive. It wanted to be inside him.

He sat at the desk with the bottle in front of him and the Codex Shard warm against the back of his neck.

The ledger was done. He was here to execute.

The recalibration was still running. The Shard was rebuilding. The probability of successful conversion was approximately forty percent. The probability of cardiac arrest was approximately twelve percent if he took the full dose, or approximately four percent if he took a measured half-dose.

The measured half-dose would still trigger a recalibration event. A smaller dose meant less stress on the system, which meant a smaller signal. But the Codex Shard had demonstrated sensitivity to any absorbed Sidewound above a certain threshold. The question was whether half a Sablefang dose crossed that threshold.

He didn't know. That was the point of the test.

He uncapped the bottle.

The smell hit him immediately, something between copper and ozone, with an undertone of something sweet that his hindbrain identified as *danger*. His hands were steady. His breathing was even. The Codex Shard pulsed faintly against his spine, sensing the incoming threat the way a dog senses a storm before it arrives.

He poured exactly half the contents into a secondary container and set it aside.

Half the dose. Four percent cardiac arrest risk instead of twelve. Fifty-fifty conversion success instead of forty percent. The balance was slightly better.

He raised the bottle to his lips.

He drank.

The venom hit his stomach and didn't wait for digestion. It went through the lining of his gut like fire through paper, spreading through his bloodstream in seconds. His vision went sharp, the Sablefang's first effect—neural sensitization—every sensory input amplified to razor clarity.

His heart responded to the intrusion.

[Warning: Unknown toxin detected.]
[Sidewound magnitude: significant.]
[Inversion processing: 12%.]

His ribs felt like someone had wrapped them in wire and was slowly pulling it tight. His pulse doubled. His fingertips went cold. The pain was extraordinary—not sharp, but dense, pressing, the sensation of being compressed from the inside.

He put down the bottle.

He sat very still.

The room was too bright. The lamp was too loud. He could hear his own blood moving through his veins, a sound no person should hear, and beneath it the faint hum of the Codex Shard as it struggled to process the incoming data.

[Inversion processing: 18%.]

Eighteen percent. The system was fighting. The venom was spreading. The Shard was absorbing it in fragments, converting what it could, rejecting what it couldn't, and the interaction between the two was manifesting as a pressure in his chest that wasn't pain and wasn't pressure but something in between.

His left hand cramped. He unfolded it deliberately.

[Sidewound type recognized: Sablefang venom, Tier One.]
[Conversion rate: 112%.]
[Inversion processing: 24%.]

The numbers were low. The processing was slow. Twenty-four percent after thirty seconds meant the Shard was absorbing the venom but at a rate that couldn't outpace the damage being done. His heart was compensating for the venom's interference, working harder, burning through its own reserves.

He was running out of time.

He could feel it, the margin between what the Shard was absorbing and what the venom was delivering, growing incrementally wider. In another thirty seconds, the gap would be too wide. The venom would reach critical mass in his cardiac tissue and the forty percent conversion failure would become a hundred percent failure.

His vision narrowed.

He focused. The way he'd learned in the police station, in the foster homes, in the first weeks at Black Spire when he'd had to learn to function on four hours of sleep and a body that was still adjusting to the Codex Shard's demands. He focused inward. Found the Shard. Felt its warmth, its hum, its stubborn refusal to let him die.

*Push,* he told it. Not with words—words weren't necessary. The Shard was his father's work, and somewhere in its architecture was a mechanism that responded to will. He'd discovered this during the Bloodscourge ceremony: the Shard didn't just passively absorb. It could be directed.

*Push through. Absorb faster.*

[Inversion processing: 31%.]

Not enough. Not nearly enough. The venom was ahead by miles.

But the Shard was listening.

[Inversion processing: 38%.]
[Capacity threshold approaching.]

His heart stuttered. He felt it, a momentary pause, a skipped beat, and then a resumption that was harder than it should've been. The four percent cardiac arrest risk was materializing—not as a full failure but as a warning shot. His heart was straining.

*Faster.*

[Inversion processing: 46%.]
[Conversion rate adjusting: 112% → 118%.]

The Shard was adapting. Just like his father's notes said it would. The vessel wasn't cracking, it was expanding, adjusting its absorption rate upward in response to the increased pressure. The venom was still winning, but the gap was narrowing.

His vision grayed at the edges. He blinked it back.

[Inversion processing: 54%.]
[Unknown variable: user will factor. Calculating.]

The Shard was incorporating his will into its equation. He wasn't a passive recipient of the Inversion—he was a participant. The system was designed to work with the host, not for the host. That was the insight his father's notes had hinted at and that Roan had just confirmed through trial and nearly fatal error.

He could influence the conversion rate. Not arbitrarily—the Shard set a baseline—but within a range. He'd pushed it from 112 to 118 percent through sheer intent. If he could do that under stress, during a real fight, with his life on the line—

[Inversion processing: 67%.]

The venom's lead was gone. The Shard was ahead now, absorbing faster than the toxin could deliver new damage. The pressure in his chest began to ease. His vision stabilized. His heartbeat, which had been racing, began to slow.

[Inversion processing: 84%.]
[Inversion processing: 91%.]
[Inversion processing: complete.]

[Conversion successful: +94 Vitalis.]
[Sidewound eliminated: Sablefang venom, Tier One.]
[Codex Shard recalibrating.]

He exhaled.

The room resolved around him. Desk, lamp, bottle, window. His hands were still steady. His heart was beating normally. The cramping in his left hand had faded to a faint tingling that felt almost pleasant—the aftereffect of blood returning to constricted capillaries.

[Codex Shard recalibration complete.]
[Daily capacity: 4/4 → 5/5.]
[User will factor logged. Maximum observed influence: +6% conversion rate variance.]
[Your father would have called that reckless. He also would have been impressed. — A.V.]

He allowed himself one moment of something that wasn't a smile.

Five daily inversions. One confirmed method to increase conversion rate under stress. A four percent survival margin that he'd chosen and executed correctly. These were the figures. The columns didn't lie, and the figures said he was better equipped than he'd been this morning.

He put the remaining half-dose of Sablefang extract back in its bottle, recapped it, and stored it in his desk drawer. He'd use it after the sparring exhibition. After he'd seen what Cyrus was going to do and assessed the new threat map.

Selene had told him something would go wrong. She was almost certainly right. The question was whether the something was a problem for him or an opportunity.

He thought about Cyrus on the obstacle course, running at sixty percent capacity, holding reserves. He thought about Selene in the corridor, gray eyes calculating, offering information for future leverage. He thought about Brendt, who'd lent him three hundred credits without asking a single question.

He thought about the seven targets. The six names. The blank space where the seventh should be.

He stood. He unlocked the door. He went to bed.

Tomorrow, the exhibition. Tomorrow, Cyrus made his move. Tomorrow, something would go wrong.

He was counting on it.
