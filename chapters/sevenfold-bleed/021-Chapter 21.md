Chapter 21: Mira's Job

Greycross at dawn was a study in exhaustion.

The Chemical Health Processing Plant let its night shift out at six-thirty, and the workers who emerged had the hollow look of people who had traded their sleep for a wage that no longer covered what they'd sacrificed for it. Mira Vance pulled her shawl tight against the morning damp and walked home without stopping at the cairn. She had stopped the day before. She had another shift in a few hours, and stopping only made the cold settle deeper. Her hands ached. They always ached. The doctor called it repetitive stress calcification. Mira called it the price of tuition.

She thought about Roan's letters. Short. Safe. He never asked for money, but she knew what it cost to be at Black Spire. She knew what it cost to be a Vance.

She didn't ask what he was doing. She didn't want to know the cost of that, either.

---

The training yard smelled of sweat and old iron. It might as well have been a different world.

Roan crouched at the edge of the sparring circle, watching Brendt work through his cooldown stretches. The ache of the Ash-Veined Lotus was a familiar companion by now, a low-grade fire that lived just under his skin. His Vitalis had climbed to nearly 1,740 in three days. The cost, in credit and in favor, had climbed with it.

The arithmetic resolved cleanly. Twelve doses. Forty-eight hours of accelerated advancement compressed into less than a third of the normal time. Mira's double shifts. The loan from Drogan. The ledger balanced on paper. It never balanced in the body.

"You've got that look again."

Roan glanced up. Brendt had finished stretching and was walking over, a towel around his neck, his expression caught between concern and reluctant amusement. Brendt's Vitalis had stabilized around the 900 mark after their last sparring session. His body was still adjusting.

"What look."

"The one that says you're calculating the exact angle of attack for the next person who looks at you wrong." Brendt dropped onto the bench beside him. "Also the one that usually precedes you doing something stupid."

"I'm not—"

"You bought a week's worth of Ash-Veined Lotus from the supply depot on credit. I saw the requisition."

Right.

Roan had. He'd maxed out his monthly credit allocation and taken a small loan from Drogan, something that would take him until next term to repay. The Lotus was theoretically restricted to students past the first-year mark. The supply depot had let it slide because Drogan had put in a word, and because the depot clerk owed Brendt a favor. Every resource channel had a human cost attached.

"It was a calculated risk."

"That you keep taking."

"That keeps paying off."

Brendt shook his head but didn't push it. They'd had this conversation before, and Brendt had learned that Roan treated financial risk the same way he treated combat: as a problem to be solved, not a debt to be feared.

"So what's the plan for today? Drogan's still gone, and last I checked, you can't exactly practice Sevenfold Bleed without supervision."

Roan stood. His legs ached. The Lotus had accelerated his muscle development as a side effect, and his body was still catching up to the changes. "I'm not going to practice Sevenfold Bleed."

Brendt blinked. "Then what—"

"I need to spar. Full contact. Not the half-speed stuff they do in group drills."

"You want me to hit you."

"I want you to try to hit me. There's a difference."

Brendt's brow furrowed. "Roan, the Sevenfold Bleed techniques require you to injure yourself to generate—"

"I know what they require."

The first strike had nearly killed him. Seven Vitalis channels simultaneously on the verge of rupture, his body processing damage faster than his nervous system could keep up with. He'd spent two days in the medical wing recovering, officially for exhaustion, unofficially because Drogan had told the healers to keep their mouths shut.

The second strike wouldn't be easier. The third would be harder. By the time he reached the fourth, fifth, sixth, he was looking at damage that even the Inversion might not be able to process cleanly.

But he didn't have a choice.

The Greel envoy had reached Hardened three days ago. The news had filtered through the student body like wildfire, and Roan had watched the social dynamics shift in real time. People who'd been neutral toward him became slightly more distant. People who'd been hostile became slightly more smug. The tally hadn't changed. The envoy was still a bully with more money than sense. But the perception had.

A Tempered fighting a Hardened was supposed to be a joke.

Roan refused to be the punchline.

"What are you trying to do?" Brendt asked.

"I'm trying to find the edge."

"The edge of what?"

He turned to face Brendt fully. "The edge where damage stops being damage and starts being something else. I need to know what the Inversion's saturation curve looks like before I'm in a situation where I have to find out in real time."

Brendt was quiet for a moment. Then he nodded slowly.

"Okay. But I'm not pulling my hits."

"I'd be disappointed if you did."

---

They found a quiet corner of the training yard, one of the sections reserved for upper-year students who needed space for heavier techniques. The ground here was reinforced concrete instead of the standard packed earth, scored with burn marks and shallow gouges from decades of use.

Brendt settled into a fighting stance. His Vitalis was lower than Roan's, but his technique was cleaner, his instincts sharper. He'd been training for three years before Roan arrived, and he'd spent that time building fundamentals that couldn't be rushed.

Roan matched his stance. Sidewound wasn't with him. He'd left it in his quarters. This was about finding limits, not demonstrating capabilities.

"On three?"

"Go."

Brendt moved first.

He came in low, aiming for Roan's left knee, a classic destabilization technique designed to off-balance without causing serious damage. Roan shifted his weight, absorbed the impact, and threw an elbow toward Brendt's temple.

Brendt blocked and pivoted, delivering a short kick to Roan's thigh.

Roan felt it. The impact registering in his nervous system, the faint bloom of pain starting to build. And then nothing.

The Inversion processed the damage automatically, converting the kinetic impact into a small pulse of energy that cycled through his Vitalis channels. By the time Brendt disengaged, Roan had already recovered.

That was the theory, anyway.

In practice, it was messier.

"You're holding back," Brendt said, circling.

"I'm adjusting."

"You always do. That's the problem." Brendt threw a jab, testing Roan's guard. "You're treating this like an equation. But combat isn't math."

"Everything is math. You just have to find the right variables."

"Then find them."

He came in harder this time. A combination: jab, cross, hook, each strike landing with increasing force. Roan's body processed each impact, but the processing took a fraction of a second, and that fraction was enough to accumulate into a growing pressure behind his eyes.

Too much. Too fast.

He backpedaled, creating space.

"What's wrong?"

"Nothing." Roan rolled his shoulders. "Just recalibrating."

"You're not processing fast enough."

Not a question. Brendt had figured it out, the same way he figured out most things: by watching, not by asking.

"The Inversion requires a minimum threshold of damage before it triggers," Roan said. "Too light and the conversion doesn't activate. Too heavy and the processing queue backs up before the conversion takes hold."

"So there's a sweet spot in the curve."

"There's always a sweet spot."

Brendt nodded slowly. "Let me try something."

He reset his stance, then threw a single strike, not at Roan's body, but at his forearm. The impact was precise, calibrated, designed to exceed the threshold without crossing into the range that would cause the queue to stutter.

Roan felt it register.

`[Sidewound absorbed. Conversion: +12 Vitalis. Processing load: moderate.]`

There. That was the mapping he needed.

The problem with Sevenfold Bleed wasn't the damage. It was the synchronization. The technique required him to channel energy through seven Vitalis channels simultaneously, which meant the damage had to hit all seven at once. One at a time wouldn't work. The conversion had to be parallel.

The first strike had worked because Drogan had positioned his hands on Roan's back and delivered the force as a single coherent pulse. Seven Vitalis channels inflamed at once. Instant activation.

But in actual combat, no one was going to deliver a synchronized strike to his entire body. He had to create the conditions himself. That meant understanding the saturation curve, the relationship between input volume and processing delay.

"How do you feel right now?" Brendt asked.

"Like someone hit me with a hammer."

"That's not what I mean." Brendt stepped closer. "When the Inversion kicks in, what's the sensation?"

Roan considered the question. It wasn't something he'd really thought about before. The conversions happened so quickly that he'd never bothered to analyze the experience.

"Like pressure releasing. Like the pain opens a door and something walks through."

"A door to where?"

"I don't know yet."

Brendt was quiet for a moment. Then he said: "Try something. Don't defend."

"What?"

"Don't defend. Let me hit you a few times in a row, fast, low-intensity, below threshold. I want to see if you can force the system to adapt by overloading the queue."

Roan's instincts screamed against it. Defensive positions existed for a reason. Letting someone wail on you was how you got hurt.

But he also recognized what Brendt was offering. A controlled environment to test limits. To find the edge.

"Okay."

Brendt moved in.

The first hit landed on Roan's shoulder. Light. Below threshold. His body registered the impact but nothing happened.

The second hit landed on his ribs. Same result.

The third hit. Pain bloomed through his chest as the accumulated micro-damage finally exceeded the threshold. The Inversion activated, converting the energy into a rush of warmth that flooded his Vitalis channels.

Brendt's eyes widened. "There. I saw that."

Roan felt it too. The fourth and fifth strikes had been processed before the feedback reached his conscious mind. His body had adapted mid-sequence.

"How much can you take before it chokes?"

"I don't know." He was breathing harder now. "The system has a processing limit. There's a queue. Too much simultaneous data and the conversion stutters."

"You think."

"I'm working with incomplete data."

Brendt laughed, a short, sharp sound. "You're going to get yourself killed trying to map out your own body."

"Probably. But not today."

He reset his stance. "Again."

They went for another twenty minutes. Brendt varied his attacks, testing different angles, different intensities, different rhythms. Roan took each hit, processed what he could, and absorbed the rest.

By the time they stopped, his entire body was one dull throb of pain. He'd taken forty-seven impacts. The Inversion had converted thirty-two of them. The remaining fifteen had landed as actual damage: bruises, contusions, injury that would take days to heal.

His Vitalis had climbed another forty points. Marginal, but measurable.

"Next time you want to do this," Brendt said, grabbing his towel again, "give me at least a day's warning. My arms are going to be dead tomorrow."

"I'll cover your sparring assignments for a week."

"You'd better."

They walked back toward the main yard in silence. The sun was beginning its descent toward the western wall, casting long shadows across the packed earth. Other students were wrapping up their sessions, heading toward the dormitories or the dining hall.

Roan was calculating the odds.

The Greel envoy had reached Hardened. The standard progression curve suggested he'd need at least six months to consolidate his position before attempting further advancement. Six months was enough time for Roan to catch up, if he could maintain his current rate of advancement, if the Inversion continued to perform as designed, if no one else accelerated past him.

The ledger worked. Barely.

But math always worked. Execution was where things fell apart.

"Hey."

Roan looked up. Brendt had stopped walking, his expression strange.

"What?"

"There's something I should tell you. About the envoy."

"What about him?"

Brendt hesitated. Then he said: "He was seen leaving the Black Rite annex this morning."

The Black Rite annex. A subsection of the library that most students didn't know existed, or pretended not to know. It housed the older, more esoteric texts. The ones that fell into legal gray areas. The ones that pushed against the boundaries of what the Discipline considered acceptable.

"He was reading," Brendt continued. "Not browsing. Reading. For two hours."

Roan processed this. The Greel envoy wasn't a reader. The Greel envoy was a wrestler, a brawler, someone who solved problems with his fists and his family's name. Seeing him spend two hours with a text was like watching a shark learn to play chess.

"What was he reading?"

"I don't know. Someone mentioned it in passing, didn't seem like a big deal. But when I heard the name of the annex..."

Roan nodded slowly.

The Black Rite of Veyrn. One of the texts stored in that annex. The same text Drogan had warned him about, the one that studied the boundary between strength and madness, between power and self-destruction.

If the envoy was reading that, he wasn't just building muscle. He was building something else.

"He's not as stupid as everyone thinks," Brendt said. "His family has resources. Connections. If they pushed him toward the Black Rite—"

"Then he's making a trade," Roan finished. "Power now. Cost later."

"That's what worries me. The Black Rite isn't supposed to be accessible to anyone below Forged. It's dangerous even for people who know what they're doing."

"Since when has danger stopped anyone from wanting to get stronger?"

Brendt didn't have an answer for that.

Roan looked back toward the training yard. The sun was nearly touching the wall now, painting the sky in shades of orange and gold. Somewhere in this building, the Greel envoy was running drills that would leave permanent marks on his nervous system. Somewhere, he was building toward a goal that had nothing to do with academic achievement.

The slope of the problem had tilted.

It wasn't just about catching up anymore. It was about figuring out what the envoy was building toward, and whether Roan had enough time to match it.

"We should go," Brendt said. "Dinner's in an hour, and you look like you need a shower."

"I'm fine."

"You have a bruise the size of my fist on your ribs."

"I'll ice it later."

Brendt shook his head but didn't argue. He'd learned that arguing with Roan about self-care was like arguing with a wall about gravity: technically possible, but pointless.

They walked toward the dormitories in silence.

As they passed the administrative building, Roan caught a glimpse of Drogan through one of the upper windows. The warden was standing with two other figures, both older, both wearing the formal robes of senior faculty.

They were discussing something. Their body language suggested tension, but not hostility. More like the tension of people who were on the same side but disagreed about method.

Drogan glanced down and caught Roan's eye. For a moment, something passed between them: acknowledgment, perhaps, or warning.

Then Drogan looked away, and Roan kept walking.

---

The shower was cold. Deliberately so. The ice water would reduce the swelling in his bruises and force his body to accelerate its natural recovery processes. It wasn't pleasant, but nothing about the path he'd chosen was supposed to be pleasant.

He stood under the spray for fifteen minutes, letting the cold water do its work. His muscles ached. His skin was tender where the Lotus had burned through. His ribs throbbed with each breath.

None of it mattered.

What mattered was that he'd established a baseline. He knew, roughly, how fast the Inversion could process damage at his current level. He knew the threshold for activation, the delay before conversion, the saturation limits he had to work within.

The second strike of Sevenfold Bleed was still theoretical. He couldn't attempt it without Drogan present—the risk of permanent damage was too high. But he'd identified the parameters. He'd mapped the territory.

The next step was execution.

He turned off the water and grabbed a towel. His reflection in the mirror was unremarkable: same face, same dark hair, same eyes that gave nothing away. The bruises on his arms were starting to purple. The crystalline residue from the Lotus was still visible along his forearms.

He looked like someone who'd been through a war and wasn't done fighting.

Good.

He dressed quickly and headed back to his quarters. The evening was his—time that most students spent socializing, studying, or resting. Roan would spend it the way he spent every evening: reading, calculating, planning.

The documents from Ironholm's archives were waiting on his desk. He'd obtained them through channels he preferred not to think about: favors called in, debts accumulated, secrets traded. They contained historical records of the Black Rite's effects on previous practitioners.

The pattern was consistent. Every student who had attempted the Black Rite before reaching Forged had experienced some form of degradation. Memory loss. Emotional instability. In extreme cases, complete personality collapse.

The Greel envoy was playing with fire. Whether he understood the risk or not, whether his family had prepared him for it or not, the Black Rite didn't care about intentions. It only cared about balance.

And the envoy was nowhere near balanced enough.

Roan sat down. On a separate sheet of paper, he wrote a short letter to Lyra.

*Lyra,*

*Tell Mother to drop the afternoon shift. I've secured a grant from the Spire. The budget will hold. I'll visit when I can.*

*Train hard.*

*—Roan*

He sealed it. The grant didn't exist yet. He would make it exist. He had assets he could liquidate, favors he could call in. The letter was a commitment—a binding promise to his own ledger.

Outside, the Black Spire rose against the sky. In Greycross, his mother was probably sleeping through the noise of the factory whistles, or not sleeping, lying awake with her aching hands.

The arithmetic had to hold. For all of them.

The variables pointed in one direction. The envoy was ahead, but the envoy was burning through his own foundation. Roan was burning through borrowed time and borrowed capital. The question was whose reserves collapsed first.

He pulled the first document toward him and began to read.

The window behind him darkened as the sun finally set below the wall. He didn't look at it.

He had work to do.
