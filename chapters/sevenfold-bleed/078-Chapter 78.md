Chapter 78: The Murdered Father

The message arrived folded into a training schedule that Roan found tucked under his door at 2:17 AM.

Four words. Drogan's handwriting.

*Storage Four. Come alone.*

Roan read it twice. The paper was standard issue, the kind used for bulletin notices, nothing traceable. But Drogan had chosen this method instead of a formal summons, instead of a message through official channels. That meant the Warden wanted no record of this meeting.

He dressed in silence, left his quarters without waking the corridor, and navigated the lower levels by memory. Storage Four was a disused equipment locker on sub-level two, door wedged open, interior lit by a single bulb that flickered at the edge of its lifespan.

Drogan was already there.

The Warden sat on an overturned crate, hands resting on his knees, his Iron Hand catching the yellow light. He wasn't in uniform. No insignia, no rank markers, just a civilian coat that looked like it had been worn through several decades and several climates. A bottle stood on the floor beside him, open, half-empty. Whiskey, from the smell.

Roan closed the door behind him.

"You don't drink," he said.

"I don't drink on duty." Drogan's voice was rougher than usual. "I'm not on duty."

Roan waited.

Drogan looked at him for a long moment. The kind of look that preceded something irreversible. Then he reached into his coat and pulled out a folder. Worn. Stained. Bound with string that had been tied and retied so many times the fibers were grey.

"Sit down."

Roan sat on a second crate, across from Drogan. The folder lay on the floor between them.

"Your father," Drogan said, "did not die in a Rift."

The words hung in the air. Roan had known this. Suspected it, assembled evidence, drawn conclusions. But knowing and hearing were different. Hearing made it real in a way that knowing never could.

"I know," he said.

Drogan's eyes sharpened. "You know, or you suspected?"

"I found the records. The gaps in the official timeline. The inconsistencies in the Rift report." Roan kept his voice level. "I also found his journal. The last entry mentioned a meeting. Six signatures."

Drogan's breath caught. A sound so small that Roan almost missed it.

"Six signatures," Drogan repeated. "You read the names."

"Yes."

"All of them."

"Yes."

Drogan picked up the bottle. Drank. Set it down. His hand stayed on the neck, knuckles white.

"I was supposed to be there that night," he said. "Aldric and I had been tracking the same thread for months—the Inversion suppression, the Conclave's hidden archive—the shadow council of old houses that had been controlling advancement thresholds for generations. He found the evidence first. Called me. Said he needed backup for a meeting."

He stopped. The silence stretched.

"I was late. Fifteen minutes. I'd stopped to check on a junior officer who'd been injured during a training exercise, and by the time I arrived at the rendezvous point, Aldric was already—" Drogan's voice broke. He recovered it. "He was on the ground. They had already left. Six chairs around a table, six glasses, six signatures on a document that declared Aldric Vance a threat to Conclave stability and authorized immediate termination."

Roan's hands were still. His breathing was steady. His face showed nothing.

But behind his eyes, the names were assembling themselves.

Vorath Greel. The white-bearded patriarch of the Northern Crown.

The second name. He kept patience while others acted.

Maddox Vell. Bought maps, sold children. Iris Vell's grandfather.

The fourth name. The western signatory who regretted everything and signed anyway.

The fifth name. The blade. Clean kills, no speeches.

Lady Sirenne. Believed ownership was mercy.

Six names. Six murderers. Six people who had sat around a table and decided that Aldric Vance deserved to die.

"You have the document," Roan said.

"I have a copy." Drogan's voice was flat now. Controlled. The voice of a man who had spent years learning to deliver this information without breaking. "The original is in the Conclave's sealed archives, buried under enough classification layers that accessing it would trigger alerts across three jurisdictions. But I made a copy before I left the Black Squad. I've kept it for six years, waiting for someone who had the right to see it." He paused. "Someone who could carry it without breaking. After what you did today, I knew you were ready."

He untied the string on the folder. Pulled out a single page, yellowed, creased from folding and refolding.

He held it out.

Roan took it.

The page was a copy of a copy. The text was faded, but legible. A formal declaration, written in the precise language of Conclave legal documentation. It cited Aldric Vance by name, listed his alleged crimes, and concluded with a sentence of termination. At the bottom, six signatures. No names beneath them, but the handwriting was distinct, each one different, each one belonging to a person who had sat in a room and voted to kill his father.

Roan read it once.

Then he read it again.

The room was very quiet. The bulb flickered. Drogan's breathing was slow and deliberate.

Roan set the page on the crate.

"Six," he said.

"Six."

"And you know who each signature belongs to."

"I do." Drogan's voice was barely above a whisper. "I've spent six years learning every detail of that night. The meeting was held in a sub-level chamber beneath the Conclave's northern headquarters. The document was drafted by House Greel's legal attaché. The execution was carried out by a contracted specialist whose identity was redacted from the official record."

"But you know."

"I know." Drogan met his eyes. "I know who killed your father, Roan. I know where they were sitting. I know what they said. I know which of them argued for a slower method and which of them wanted it done quickly. I know which one hesitated."

"And which one didn't."

"None of them hesitated enough." Drogan's jaw tightened. "Lady Sirenne signed first. She said a clean death was cleaner for the record. Vorath didn't speak. He just put his name down after the others, like he was signing a supply order."

Roan absorbed that. Let it settle into the space behind his ribs where the Codex Shard had its anchor point. The Shard was warm. Not alert-warm. Acknowledgment-warm. As though the system understood that the information it had been carrying for months had finally found its match.

He stood.

The crate scraped against the concrete floor. Drogan looked up at him, and for a moment the Warden's face was not the face of a Black Spire disciplinarian. It was the face of a man who had carried a secret for six years and had finally set it down.

"Roan—"

"I need a minute."

He walked to the far wall of the storage room. Placed his palms flat on the concrete. The surface was cold, rough, indifferent to the weight of what he had just learned.

He stayed there, breathing, while the names ran through his head in sequence.

Vorath Greel. White beard. Northern Crown. The man who had sent a nephew to watch Roan's progress.

The second. No face yet. Patient patience, the fragments said.

Maddox Vell. The Vell patriarch. Iris Vell's grandfather. Bought maps, sold children.

The fourth. The western signatory. Regrets beautifully, still signed.

The fifth. The blade. Clean kills, no speeches.

Lady Sirenne. Believes ownership is mercy.

Six people. Six signatures. One father.

Roan stood at the wall for three minutes. Then he turned, walked back to the crate, and picked up the folder.

"I'm taking this."

"It's a copy."

"I'm taking it anyway." He tucked the folder inside his jacket. "The original."

Drogan's eyes narrowed. "The Conclave's sealed archive is protected by—"

"I didn't ask how to access it. I asked where it is."

Drogan studied him. The assessment was visible in his expression, the calculation of whether this information would help or destroy the person receiving it.

"The northern headquarters. Sub-level seven. Restricted archives, containment tier gold." He paused. "The same building where the meeting was held."

Roan nodded once.

He walked to the door. His hand was on the handle when Drogan's voice stopped him.

"Aldric was my brother," Drogan said. "Not by blood. By choice. The day he died, I forgot how to have brothers. I'm trying to remember."

Roan didn't turn around.

"Keep trying," he said.

He opened the door and stepped into the corridor.

The lights were dim at this hour. The stone floor absorbed his footsteps. He walked without direction, letting his body choose the path while his mind processed. The folder was a weight against his ribs. The six names were a weight behind his eyes.

He stopped in an empty training hall. The mats were clean, recently mopped. The equipment racks were organized. A place where students came to learn how to fight, how to survive, how to become something more than what they were born as.

Roan walked to the nearest sparring platform. He placed the folder on the floor. He set his feet.

Then he punched the heavy bag at the center of the platform.

Not a training punch. A full-power strike, Vitalis cycling through his arm, the Codex Shard feeding energy into the motion. The bag tore. Sand spilled across the mats. The chain holding it snapped, and the bag collapsed in a heap of ripped canvas and pouring grit.

Roan stood in the middle of the wreckage.

His hands were steady. His breathing was controlled. His face was empty.

He picked up the folder and walked out.

Six names. One file. The original was in sub-level seven. He needed a way in.

The training hall lights dimmed behind him.
