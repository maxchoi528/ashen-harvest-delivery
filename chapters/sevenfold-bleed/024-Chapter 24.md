Chapter 24: The Crack Spreads

The morning session began with Brendt's fist connecting with the practice dummy's chest, sending it swinging on its chains. The hollow thunk of padded knuckles against leather-covered wood filled the training hall, steady as a metronome.

Roan counted the strikes. Forty-seven. Forty-eight. The Wraithmilk runs were self-funding now. The bottleneck wasn't supply anymore—it was time. And time was the one resource he couldn't manufacture.

"You're distracted," Brendt said, not breaking rhythm.

"I'm assessing."

"Assessing what? It's a dummy."

Roan's jaw tightened. The dummy's pivot point had been oiled recently, the resistance was lower than last week. Brendt's strike count was off because he wasn't compensating. Either Brendt hadn't noticed, or he had and was testing whether Roan would mention it. The smart money said the latter. Brendt had been doing that more often lately, testing thresholds. Seeing what Roan would and wouldn't say.

Brendt landed forty-nine. The dummy swung wider. His form was still textbook, he'd drilled the basics until they were reflexive, but his weight distribution was forward-heavy. If this were a real fight, Roan would already be calculating the hip pivot needed to dump him. As it was, the dummy didn't care about weight distribution.

"Sparring in ten," Brendt said, landing fifty. "Drogan's watching the juniors this morning. Just us."

Roan nodded and moved to the weapons rack. The practice blades were balanced, deliberately dulled to prevent serious injury. He'd argued once that the dulling was excessive. Initiates were supposed to learn where blades could go wrong, but the instructors had shut that down with the standard liability speech. One severed finger in training was one too many.

He selected a blade, tested the grip, ran through the basic guard positions. His hands knew the motions. His Codex Shard was silent, no Inversion prompts, nothing but the weight of steel and leather.

Three days since the Pyrebrand cycling. Three days of rebuilding capacity.

[Inversion Capacity: 2/4. — A.V.]

Not enough. Not nearly enough. He'd burned through the Tempered baseline in a single night, drinking the Bloodscourge and cycling the Pyrebrand and then doing it again when the columns didn't move fast enough. The Codex Shard had logged each conversion, and he'd watched his Vitalis climb from the low hundreds toward something that almost felt like power.

Two hundred and forty. Two-sixty. Three hundred.

The system capped at four for a Tempered. He'd used one on the Bloodscourge, one on the first Pyrebrand cycle, one on the second. That left one for emergencies. And he'd burned that one on the Crimson Wake first form two days ago, feeling the blade slide through the practice dummy's padding and into the straw core, and then the Inversion had kicked in and he'd gotten the notification that his body had "converted the kinetic feedback into improved tissue density."

Brendt had asked what happened. Roan had said the dummy was defective.

The dummy had been fine. Roan had looked it over afterward, leather cracked at the shoulder, straw leaking, but nothing structurally wrong. His blade had just... gone deeper. As if the resistance had inverted.

That was what the Crimson Wake did, he understood now. It traded blood for cutting power. The more you gave, the more you got. Normal practitioners would bleed out over sustained use. Roan bled in, his body reversing the flow.

"Daydreaming?" Brendt asked. He'd finished his set and was rolling his shoulders, working out the stiffness from fifty straight strikes. "We're supposed to be sparring."

"We're supposed to be learning," Roan corrected. "There's a difference."

"The instructors call it sparring."

"The instructors call it supervised contact with learning objectives."

Brendt's mouth twitched. That was as close to a laugh as he ever got during training. Brendt was focused in a way that Roan found almost pathological. The man had ranked third in their cohort before Roan had started manipulating the display rankings, and even now that Roan was playing lower, Brendt still moved like someone who believed every session was being evaluated.

They stepped onto the practice floor. Standard sparring distance: six feet without contact until the signal. "Rules?" Brendt asked.

"Clean hits only. No grappling below the waist. Stop when I tell you to stop."

"Always with the stopping conditions."

"Because you have a habit of not stopping when you should."

Brendt's jaw tightened. There. Roan had touched something. Brendt was aggressive in a way that was useful in small doses and dangerous in larger ones. He'd pushed through pain barriers during the Bloodboar trials, taken hits that should have dropped him, and kept going on some internal fuel that Roan couldn't map.

The signal came. Brendt moved first, as usual.

His opening was a feint, left shoulder dip, weight shift, blade angling for Roan's outside guard. Standard textbook. Roan read it, blocked, countered with a tap to Brendt's exposed forearm. Clean contact. Point scored.

Brendt adjusted. That was the thing about him, he learned. The second feint came from the opposite direction, and his actual strike was a low cut that Roan had to duck under. Close. If this were a real fight, that cut would have opened Roan's thigh.

Roan swept his blade up, caught Brendt's descending edge, twisted. The practice blades held. Brendt's feet scrambled for purchase. Roan released the bind, stepped back, reset.

"Four-one," Roan said.

Brendt's eyes narrowed. He wasn't counting the same way Roan was, he was tracking the exchanges that mattered, not the minor touches. Four points where Roan had scored, one where Brendt had nearly broken through.

But one of those exchanges nagged at Roan. The low cut. He'd seen the setup, read the sequence, but his body had been a half-beat slow responding. Not from lack of skill. From something else.

The Codex Shard pulsed, faint. A warning or a data point, he couldn't tell.

"Same again?" Brendt asked.

"Same again."

Brendt advanced. This time his feint came from a different angle, shoulder, hip, blade line all shifting together in a combination Roan hadn't seen before. New drill. He'd been working on something, refining his approach.

Roan tracked the sequence, found the opening at step three when Brendt's weight transferred onto his lead foot. He struck, not a tap this time, but a firm push that knocked Brendt's blade aside and left his chest open. Roan's practice edge stopped an inch from contact.

Brendt froze. His Vitalis was elevated; Roan could see it in the flush of his skin, the quickened breathing. But his eyes were tracking Roan's blade, not Roan's face. He was recalculating.

"You moved differently that time," Brendt said.

"I adapted."

"You read the combination."

"I read you. The combination was secondary."

Brendt reset, but something had shifted in his posture. He was being more careful now, more conservative. That was good. It meant he was learning that Roan wasn't someone to rush.

But it also meant the session was becoming less useful.

Roan shifted his guard. "Show me the new drill. The sequence you used at the start."

Brendt hesitated. Training halls had a culture of non-disclosure, the better students didn't share techniques, at least not openly. It was a form of currency. But Brendt had already used the move; he'd already shown the gap in his own defense.

"Your shoulder rotation," Roan said. "Third step. You were compensating for something."

"My elbow was tight."

"So you adjusted mid-sequence. Improvised." Roan filed that. Brendt was more adaptive than his reputation suggested. "Show me the adjustment. I want to see the full range."

Brendt's expression flickered. Suspicion, or maybe gratitude. Hard to tell with him.

He ran through the sequence again. Shoulder dip, weight shift, low cut, but this time Roan watched for the elbow, saw the slight hitch in the motion where Brendt's body had automatically compensated for the imagined restriction. The actual move had been better than the version Roan had seen. Brendt had been holding back.

"Why didn't you use that version in the spar?" Roan asked.

"You read the first one. I didn't want you reading this one."

Roan tilted his head. That was smart. Not as smart as varying his feints randomly, but smart enough. "You're learning to manipulate information."

"You're the one who taught me that was possible."

The words hung in the air. Brendt's Vitalis was still elevated, but his breathing had steadied. He was holding something in reserve, not physical, something else. An assessment, maybe. A recalculation of his own.

"Same sequence," Roan said. "Full version. I'm going to try to read it."

Brendt ran the combination. Roan watched, tracking the shifts, and when the low cut came, he moved differently, not stepping back, but inside, letting the blade pass his hip while he brought his practice edge up to Brendt's exposed throat.

Clean contact. Deep enough that if the blade had been sharp. Brendt's eyes went wide. He wasn't breathing now. He was staring at the point of contact, at the controlled precision that had placed Roan's blade exactly where it needed to be without any of the wild excess that usually accompanied close-range combat.

"How," Brendt said.

"I adapted."

"You were inside the sequence. Before it finished."

"Yes."

"That's not possible at this range. The timing—"

"The timing was there. You were the one who established the rhythm. I just used it."

Brendt's jaw worked. He was processing, Roan could see it. Trying to map the technique, to reverse-engineer the logic. That was Brendt's mind, always looking for the underlying structure, the rules that governed the visible world.

"Again," Brendt said.

"Same rules?"

"Same sequence. You try to read it, I'll try to complete it."

Roan nodded. This was more useful. Teaching each other in real time, pressure-testing each other's capabilities. Brendt would get better at hiding his tells. Roan would get better at reading them.

The signal came. Brendt started the combination.

Roan moved.

- break -

The morning session ended with Brendt bent over the weapons rack, returning his practice blade with hands that trembled slightly from exhaustion. Roan was breathing harder than he wanted to admit, the last exchange had required a full commitment that had left his shoulders burning.

"Twenty-three to nineteen," Brendt said, slotting the blade into its berth.

"You're counting differently than I am."

"I'm counting exchanges where neither of us scored. It makes the margins clearer."

Roan considered that. It was a valid methodology. Most students tracked gross points because it was simpler. Brendt tracked net exchanges because he wanted to understand the dynamics. The distinction mattered when the scores were close.

"Your elbow bothering you?" Roan asked.

Brendt paused. "How did you—"

"Third sequence. You aborted three times. I assumed it was pain management."

"It's not pain. It's a mobility issue. Old injury, healed wrong."

Roan filed that. Brendt had been carrying physical limitations into combat without mentioning them. That was a gap in his self-assessment, either he didn't know his own body well enough to account for the restriction, or he knew and was ignoring it.

"Get it looked at," Roan said. "The training hall has a physio."

"The waiting list is three weeks."

"Or Drogan knows someone. He's got connections."

Brendt's expression went careful. Mentioning Drogan was a delicate matter, the Warden was officially their mentor, but in practice he was something more complicated. A teacher with resources. A guardian with secrets. Figure whose name came with weight.

"I'll think about it," Brendt said.

"You'll do more than think about it. Your elbow is a liability."

"My elbow is my problem."

"Until it becomes mine. When you're covering my flank and your arm gives out—"

"I'm not covering your flank."

Roan let that sit. Brendt was resistant, which meant the issue was sensitive. Not the elbow specifically, the elbow was just the symptom. Something about the injury carried meaning. Something about asking for help cost more than Brendt was willing to pay.

The training hall doors opened. A junior student entered, saw them, hesitated.

Roan recognized her. Third cohort, maybe. Thin, dark hair pulled back severely. He didn't know her name.

"Sorry," she said. "The Warden said to tell you, he'll be in the main hall at midday. Both of you."

Brendt straightened. "Did he say why?"

"No. Just said both of you."

She left, closing the doors behind her.

Roan and Brendt exchanged a look. Drogan summoning both of them at the same time meant something. The Warden ran his students on separate tracks, mostly, individual training sessions, tailored feedback, minimal overlap. Bringing them together meant a group exercise, or a conversation that required witnesses.

"Question," Brendt said slowly. "Why would he summon us together?"

"He wouldn't."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only answer that makes sense. He wouldn't summon us together unless the situation required it."

"What situation?"

Roan shook his head. He didn't know. That was the problem. Drogan had been careful about information, parceling out details in fragments that Roan had spent weeks assembling into a picture he wasn't sure was accurate. The Greycross connection. The Vance name. The things that had happened to his father that the Academy records didn't mention.

Brendt was still watching him. "You're hiding something."

"Everyone's hiding something."

"Not like you. You've been different since the Bloodboar trials."

Roan said nothing. Brendt was perceptive in his own way, not the calculating perceptiveness that Roan relied on, but something more instinctual. He felt the shape of things, if not their edges.

"Drogan knows something," Brendt said. "And you're afraid he's going to tell you."

Roan's jaw tightened. Not fear. Anticipation. There was a difference, and Brendt was wrong about which it was.

"Drogan knows many things," Roan said. "The question is whether he decides we need to know them too."

The training hall was silent except for the distant clang of equipment from the far end of the building. The juniors were finishing their morning session. Soon the lunch rush would start, and the halls would fill with noise and bodies and all the small competitions that characterized Academy life.

Roan returned his practice blade. He had two hours before midday. Time enough to eat, to review his notes on the Inversion patterns, to prepare a list of questions for Drogan that he would not ask directly.

"You're going to the library," Brendt said. It wasn't a question.

"Research."

"The library isn't open until afternoon on Thursdays."

Roan paused. That was a detail he should have tracked. The library schedule was basic administrative information. He'd been sloppy.

"Roan."

"What."

"You don't have to carry everything yourself. I know we're not, we're not friends exactly. But I'm not going to report you to the instructors for whatever you're doing."

Roan turned. Brendt's expression was serious, his Vitalis had settled back to baseline, his posture loose in a way that suggested openness. An invitation. Or an offer.

"I know," Roan said. "That's why I haven't pushed harder on the elbow thing."

Brendt blinked. "That's... Not the same."

"It's exactly the same. You're carrying something you won't let anyone help with. So am I."

Silence settled between them. The distant clang of equipment. The low hum of the building's ventilation system. The weight of things unsaid.

"Library at one," Brendt said finally. "If you want company."

Roan considered. The library was a resource, but it was also a trap, information in the Academy was curated, filtered through the lens of what the instructors wanted students to know. The real information, the dangerous information, wasn't in the stacks.

But Brendt had connections. Third cohort meant he'd been at the Academy for two years already, long enough to learn the hidden geography of the institution. The back channels. The whispers.

"One o'clock," Roan said. "But we're not researching what they want us to research."

Brendt's mouth curved slightly. That was as close to a smile as he ever got. "Finally. I was starting to think you were just going to watch me struggle alone forever."

The training hall doors opened again. This time it was Theon Mercer, leaning against the frame with the posture of someone who wanted to be seen wanting to be seen.

"Vance. Holloway. Still practicing? Some of us have lives beyond the ring."

Brendt's shoulders tensed. Roan's hand drifted toward the weapons rack before he caught himself. Theon was baiting them, that was his function, as far as Roan could tell. He existed to create friction, to test reactions, to identify weaknesses that could be exploited later.

"Catch you later," Theon said to Roan specifically, his eyes lingering. "I've got some information you might want."

He left. The doors swung shut.

Brendt exhaled. "What was that about?"

"Provocation."

"I know that. What's he got on you?"

Roan shook his head. He didn't know. That was the problem. Theon had been circling him for weeks now, dropping hints about conversations Roan hadn't been part of, implying access to information Roan hadn't seen. It was a standard harassment tactic, make the target paranoid, watch for reactions, build leverage.

But Theon was too obvious about it. That suggested the information he had was either very small or very dangerous.

"Library at one," Roan said again. "We can talk there."

Brendt nodded. They left the training hall separately, a small precaution, but necessary. Theon's presence meant they were being watched, or soon would be. The Academy had a culture of observation, of students cataloging each other's behaviors and passing notes to instructors who pretended not to notice.

The corridors were filling with the lunch crowd. Roan moved against the flow, heading toward the dormitories. He needed to eat, to rest, to review the Inversion patterns he'd been tracking. Two hours until the meeting with Drogan. Two hours until whatever came next.

His Codex Shard pulsed.

[New Sidewound pattern detected. Origin: external Vitalis signature within 50 meters. — A.V.]

Roan stopped walking.

The corridor was full of students, noise and motion and all the chaos of midday transition. Somewhere in that mass was someone with a Vitalis pattern the Codex Shard recognized. Not an enemy, he would have felt that. Not a friend in the usual sense. Something else.

[Pattern match: 73% correlation with House Greel signature. Source moving toward the main hall. — A.V.]

Roan processed the readout. Aldric had pre-loaded the Shard with House Greel lineage markers. The database was thorough, a dead man's silent accounting of enemies. Roan had assumed it was a theoretical precaution—a contingency for a war already won or lost. The Shard was proving him wrong.

Roan calculated the implications. House Greel was the white-bearded man's family. Theon Mercer had just passed through the training hall. Theon Mercer had family connections to the northern Conclave, connections that were an open secret at the Academy, spoken about in whispers that Roan had been carefully not listening to. Theon's information wasn't small.

Theon had information about House Greel, and he'd just told Roan he wanted to talk.

Roan moved. Not toward the dormitories. Toward the main hall, angling through the crowd with a speed that was almost but not running. He needed to see. Needed to confirm. Needed to know if Drogan's summons and Theon's offer and the Codex Shard's warning were all parts of the same sequence or separate variables he hadn't accounted for.

The main hall doors loomed ahead. Students flowing in and out, the noise of eating and conversation and the low throb of Vitalis from a hundred different sources. Inside that noise was a pattern that shouldn't exist, a signature that belonged in the northern territories, not Ironholm, not the Black Spire.

Roan stopped at the doors. Looked.

The main hall was full. Long tables, packed with students. The lunch rush in full flow. At the head table, where the senior instructors sometimes ate, Drogan was sitting with a woman Roan didn't recognize. Dark hair, sharp features, posture that suggested authority, worn like armor and stitched with war. She was speaking to Drogan in low tones, and Drogan was listening with an expression Roan had never seen before.

Respect. And beneath it, something that looked almost like fear.

[Pattern match upgraded: 94% correlation. Source identified as Kara Greel, first cousin of the white-bearded man. Rank: Hardened. — A.V.]

Hardened. Second tier. A woman who could walk into the Black Spire and sit with a Warden like an equal.

The system was still learning. The accuracy was sharpening in real time. This wasn't passive inheritance recognition—the Shard was actively scanning, cross-referencing, building a profile from a single signature. Aldric hadn't just left a list of names. He'd left a hunting protocol.

And then she turned her head, and she looked directly at Roan standing in the doorway.

Her eyes were cold. Grey, like winter iron. They found him, held him, and for a moment Roan felt the full weight of a Vitalis signature that was orders of magnitude beyond his own. The Codex Shard screamed warnings he couldn't process.

[Warning: Vitalis differential exceeds safe interaction parameters. Recommend retreat. — A.V.]

Roan didn't move.

She smiled. It wasn't a warm expression.

"You must be Roan Vance," she called across the hall. Her voice cut through the noise like a blade through silk. "I've heard so much about you."

The hall went quiet. A hundred faces turned toward Roan. A hundred Vitalis signatures shifted in his direction, tracking, assessing, waiting to see what he would do.

Brendt appeared at his elbow, breathing hard from following. "What is she doing here?"

Roan didn't answer. He was calculating. A Hardened-tier Greel, here at the Black Spire. Drogan's summons at midday. Theon's offer of information. The Codex Shard's warning.

The figures said this wasn't coincidence. The figures said everything was connected.

The question was whether he had the capacity to find out before the situation found him first.

[Inversion Capacity: 2/4.]

Two uses. He had two uses until the daily reset, and a Hardened-tier Conclave member was already inside the building, already talking to his mentor, already looking at him like he was something she wanted to take apart.

Kara Greel tilted her head. Waiting.

Roan stepped into the hall. The doors swung shut behind him.
