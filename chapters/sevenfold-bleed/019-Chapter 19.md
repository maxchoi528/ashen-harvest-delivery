Chapter 19: Wraithmilk Deep

The sparring circle had been drawn in white chalk on the training hall floor, twelve feet across, no boundaries enforced, instructor oversight nominal at best. Rook Aldwin stood opposite Roan with the stillness that meant he was either very confident or very afraid. Both looked identical on the surface.

"Half-strength," Instructor Vance had announced before stepping back to the observation rail. "Until yield or disqualification. No permanent damage."

The unspoken caveat was obvious. *Permanent* was doing a lot of work in that sentence.

Roan watched Rook's breathing pattern. Four-second inhale, two-second hold, six-second exhale. Combat-ready rhythm, but the cadence had a hitch at the top of each cycle, the sign of someone who'd been training hard recently and hadn't fully integrated the strain into baseline. Rook had been pushing his limits. Trying to close the gap with the top quartile before the term's first formal evaluation.

The tally was straightforward. Rook was Tempered, mid-range for the cohort. Vitalis reading somewhere around six hundred, give or take eighty depending on the hour. His standing in the class hovered around fifteenth. He'd been eighth two months ago.

And now he was picking a fight with the kid everyone called "the Vance ghost."

Roan held the observation and waited.

Rook moved first, feinted left, tested the line, pulled back. Standard probing technique. He wasn't incompetent. The sequence took just under two seconds, which meant his reaction time was solid. He could track a moving target, initiate contact, reset stance. Basic competency across the board.

What he couldn't do was adapt mid-sequence. The feint-then-strike pattern worked if the opponent bit on the feint. If they didn't, the entire sequence became a telegraphed mess that left him two moves behind his own rhythm.

Roan sidestepped. Not because he had to. Rook's opening strike would've connected if he'd committed fully, but because the figures said the cleaner response was to demonstrate what he wasn't going to do. Let Rook recalculate. Let him sweat.

The fist whistled past his shoulder. Close enough to feel the displaced air.

Better.

Rook reset, recalibrated, came again. This time he committed. Straight punch, maximum power, leading with the shoulder to disguise the trajectory. Faster than the feint-strike combination. Less elegant, more dangerous.

Roan stepped inside the arc.

Not flashy. Not a counter-kick or a spinning elbow or any of the techniques the more theatrical students favored. Just a simple step that put him inside the power band where Rook's extended arm became a liability rather than a weapon. His palm touched Rook's chest, just barely, just enough.

He pushed.

Rook staggered back three steps, caught his balance, came forward again with more anger than sense. The second exchange was faster, messier. Rook threw a hook that would've cracked ribs if it landed. Roan ducked under, let the blow pass overhead, and tapped Rook's kidney with two fingers. Not hard enough to damage, hard enough to remind the body that it had a vulnerability there.

Rook flinched. Instinct overrode intention. He dropped his guard two inches to protect the kidney, and Roan was already past it.

Three taps. Solar plexus. Floating rib. Left knee.

He stepped back.

Rook stood in the center of the circle, breathing hard, fists up, eyes searching for an opponent who wasn't there anymore. He couldn't find Roan, the window for that had closed three seconds ago. By the time he'd realized the danger had passed, Roan was already back at the starting position, hands loose at his sides.

"What—" Rook's voice cracked. "What the hell—"

"You're telegraphing your reset," Roan said. "Every time you commit to an attack, you pull your right foot back six inches before you step forward. I can see it before you decide which attack you're using."

He could also see that Rook's instructor hadn't been watching his footwork. That his previous training had stressed upper-body mechanics at the expense of foundation work. That he'd been taught to prioritize power over adaptability, and that the habit was so deeply embedded he couldn't recognize the pattern as a problem.

None of that was Rook's fault. His instructor had failed him, and no one had corrected it because no one had bothered to watch closely enough.

But Rook had picked this fight. He'd studied Roan for two weeks, identified what he thought was a weakness, a small frame, a quiet demeanor, a history of avoiding direct confrontation, and decided to test it publicly. He'd assumed the Vance ghost was a reputation built on something other than ability.

The figures said otherwise.

"Again," Rook said.

Roan inclined his head.

The second exchange lasted four seconds longer. Rook had adjusted his footwork, dropped the six-inch retreat, tried to mask the pattern with a hip rotation that made him slower but harder to read. It was an amateur correction, the kind that fixed one symptom while creating three new problems.

The hip rotation telegraphed his weight distribution. Roan could see which direction Rook was planning to move before Rook decided to move there. The fix had made the pattern more readable, not less.

He let the exchange continue longer than necessary. Let Rook commit to a combination that would've mattered if Roan had been any of the other students in the room. Let Rook build a little confidence, get his heartbeat up, start to believe the adjustment was working.

Then he broke the sequence.

Three strikes. Center mass, throat, right wrist. The wrist was the key. Roan caught it during Rook's follow-through, used Rook's own momentum to lever the arm into an awkward angle, and stepped behind him in a single motion.

Rook found himself on his knees, arm twisted, Roan standing at his back with one hand controlling the wrist and the other resting lightly on his shoulder.

Yield or continue. Instructor Vance had been specific about the options.

Rook tried to pull free. His Vitalis spiked, he was pouring everything into the escape attempt, burning energy he couldn't afford to waste. The arm didn't move. Roan's position was geometry, not strength, and geometry couldn't be outmuscled.

A minute passed.

Rook's breathing dropped from combat rhythm to something slower, more controlled. The spike faded. His body recognized what his pride hadn't: this wasn't a stalemate he could break through. He was stuck, and the only variable he could change was his own willingness to continue.

"Yield," he said.

Roan released the wrist, stepped back, returned to center.

Rook rose, rolled his shoulder, rolled his neck. He'd be sore tomorrow, the position Roan had held had compressed something in the shoulder joint, but no permanent damage. That was what the instructor had specified.

The watching students were quiet. Some were taking notes in their heads. Several were recalculating their assessment of the ghost in the corner.

That was fine. Let them calculate.

Roan took his position again, waiting for the next challenge. Two more students stepped into the circle before the hour ended. Neither lasted longer than Rook. Neither required more than three exchanges.

The first was a Tempered with decent fundamentals but a tell in her elbow, she led with the joint half a second before her fist arrived, which meant Roan saw every punch coming from the moment she decided to throw it. He deflected the first three and used the fourth to walk her into a leg sweep. Clean. Efficient.

The second was a Hardened who'd apparently decided the Vance ghost needed to be reminded of his place. He came in heavy, trusting his rank to carry him through the gaps in his technique. The ledger resolved cleanly: drag it out and invite a dozen more tests before supper, or collapse him fast and let the rumor do the work.

Roan let him close the distance, let him think the grapple was working, let him commit to a double-handed press that would've pinned a smaller opponent to the floor. Then he twisted his torso eighteen degrees, redirected the force sideways, and used the Hardened's own weight to dump him on his back.

The Hardened rose with murder in his eyes. The third exchange was genuine, he wasn't holding back, wasn't testing, was trying to hurt Roan with every strike. It lasted nine seconds.

Nine seconds was enough.

Roan took a strike to the ribs to establish position, absorbed a knee to the thigh to control the angle, and used a headbutt that he'd deliberately positioned to look accidental but wasn't. The Hardened's nose started bleeding. His vision blurred from the impact point, and by the time he cleared it, Roan had already stepped back to the starting position and was standing calm.

No broken bones. No permanent damage. But the Hardened would be seeing stars for the rest of the afternoon.

Instructor Vance finally intervened after that one. Not because the exchange had crossed any lines, the headbutt was borderline, but the Hardened had been the aggressor throughout, but because the watching students were starting to look nervous rather than impressed.

"Enough," Vance said. "Vance, with me."

The instructor's office was small, functional, cluttered with training manuals and assessment forms. Vance closed the door behind them and gestured to the single chair.

"Sit."

Roan sat.

Vance leaned against his desk, arms crossed, studying Roan with the expression of a man trying to solve an equation that kept coming up with too many unknowns.

"You're sandbagging," he said.

Roan didn't answer. The statement didn't require one.

"The first three exchanges with each opponent, you're showing them exactly enough to make them think they have a chance. Then you close it out faster than they can react. That's not improvisation. That's a system."

"Observation-based adaptation isn't a system. It's a skill."

"It's a system you developed. Deliberately." Vance's eyes narrowed. "You're performing below your actual capability by roughly forty percent in these sessions. I've been watching the whole cohort's improvement curves. Yours should be flatlined, you're not getting better because you're not trying. But every three weeks, you demonstrate something new. Something you couldn't have learned in the interim without either working harder than anyone else in the cohort or already knowing it beforehand."

Roan kept his face neutral. Vance was good at his job, better than the students realized. He was tracking data that most instructors would've ignored because the patterns only showed up in aggregate. Single data points meant nothing. Trend lines meant everything.

"The Vance family has a certain reputation," Vance continued. "Your father was brilliant. Your uncle was better. Both of them had a habit of demonstrating capability well beyond what their visible training would support." He paused. "You're the same age your father was when he first drew public attention. Same circumstances, a quiet student in a large cohort, no visible indicators of exceptional work rate, but outputs that don't match the inputs."

"Coincidence," Roan said.

"Maybe. But the pattern's the pattern." Vance straightened. "I don't know what you're doing outside of official sessions. I don't know what resources you're drawing on. But I know you're not showing me what you can do. And that means one of two things."

Roan waited.

"Either you're protecting yourself from something, in which case I respect the caution and I'll leave you alone. Or you're planning something that requires you to be underestimated, in which case I want to know what it is so I can decide whether I need to be worried."

The ledger had never accounted for an instructor this perceptive.

"My father left notes," Roan said. It wasn't the whole truth, but it was close enough. "Private training journals. I've been working through them independently."

Vance absorbed this. "Your father was a Forged-class before he died. If he left journals, they'd be appropriate for Tempered-level training at best. You shouldn't be able to learn anything useful from them at your current tier."

"His techniques don't require high Vitalis to demonstrate. They require understanding of underlying mechanics. The principles scale down."

"And the forty percent gap?"

"I'm not sandbagging. I'm managing my training efficiency. If I demonstrated my actual capability, I'd draw attention I don't want. The journals include specific guidance about avoiding premature exposure."

It was plausible. Vance was calculating the odds. Roan could see the process in his instructor's micro-expressions, the way his jaw shifted slightly with each variable. Plausible was enough. Plausible didn't demand verification.

Vance exhaled slowly. "Alright. I'll stop pushing. But if you're drawing on resources your father left behind, I need to know they're being applied safely. The Vance family's history includes some unconventional methods. If you're following any of those paths, I need to make sure you're not going to hurt yourself or others."

"My methods are entirely conventional. Just applied with better understanding than the standard curriculum provides."

"Uh-huh." Vance didn't sound convinced, but he didn't push further. "Go back to the floor. We're done for today."

Roan rose, crossed to the door, paused.

"Instructor. Your assessment of my footwork patterns is correct. I am showing less than I'm capable of. But I'm not showing nothing. The gaps you're identifying are real gaps. I'm just controlling how fast I close them."

He opened the door.

"By the end of the term, I won't have those gaps anymore. I'll be better than I'm showing. And when that happens, you'll know."

He stepped out before Vance could respond.

The corridor outside the training hall was empty. Late afternoon, most students in the dorms or the dining hall. Roan walked without hurrying, his mind running calculations on the conversation he'd just finished.

Vance was a threat, but not an immediate one. He was suspicious, not hostile. He'd backed off when Roan gave him a plausible explanation, which meant he was willing to accept uncertainty if it kept the situation stable. That was useful. An instructor who pushed harder would be more dangerous than one who backed away.

But Vance had also made it clear that he was watching. That meant the forty-percent gap couldn't stay at forty percent forever. At some point, the visible improvement would exceed what the "journals" explanation could support, and Vance would start looking for alternative sources.

Roan needed to either give Vance something real, enough to satisfy his curiosity without exposing the actual mechanism, or find a way to close the gap faster than Vance's observation period could track.

Neither option was simple. Both had costs.

He turned the corner toward the dormitory wing and nearly walked into Brendt.

"Finally," Brendt said, falling into step beside him. "I've been waiting twenty minutes. Where'd Vance drag you?"

"Office. He figured out I'm sandbagging."

Brendt whistled low. "Damn. That's not great."

"It's manageable. He thinks I'm working from my father's journals. I didn't correct him."

"Will that hold?"

"For now. Eventually I'll need to give him something real, or he'll start digging. But I have time. A few weeks at least."

They walked in silence for a moment. Brendt was watching the hallway, monitoring for listeners the way Roan had taught him to, subtle, automatic, part of the background noise rather than explicit scan. Good habit. He'd picked it up faster than expected.

"Rook's people are asking about the Wraithmilk," Brendt said quietly. "They approached me after your session. Wanted to know whether there was something in it. Whether someone's been supplying you."

Roan slowed his pace. The calculation updated instantly. Rook's circle connecting the performance to the Wraithmilk supply chain was a risk vector he'd accounted for in the abstract but hadn't expected to materialize this quickly.

"Who specifically?"

"Three of them. Rook's old inner circle. They're not angry, they're curious. But curious turns into questions. Questions turn into people watching your movements."

"What did you tell them?"

"I said you were just good. That you'd been training hard and everyone had underestimated you." Brendt paused. "I may have implied that you'd been working with Drogan outside of official hours."

That was clever. Drogan was respected enough that crediting him with Roan's development would make the improvement seem plausible rather than suspicious. And it wasn't entirely a lie. Drogan had been helping, in his own unorthodox way.

"They buy it?"

"Some of them. Rook's still angry. He thinks you humiliated him on purpose."

"He was looking for humiliation. He just didn't know it."

Brendt laughed, a short, sharp sound. "Yeah, well. He found it." The laughter faded. "You should watch yourself. Anger makes people stupid, and stupid people do things they can't take back."

Roan nodded. He was already calculating the probabilities—the likelihood of a direct confrontation, of coordinated interference, of someone bringing in an outside authority. The tally said the immediate risk was low. Rook was embarrassed, not motivated. Embarrassment faded. Motivation persisted.

But Brendt was right to flag it. The social dimension was part of the balance, and the columns didn't lie.

"I need to talk to Drogan tonight," Roan said. "After evening meal. You have anything scheduled?"

"Free until tenth bell." Brendt hesitated. "Want me there?"

"No. This one's about me. But I might need you to run interference afterward, depending on how it goes."

"Interference like what?"

"I need to ask him some questions about my father's history. Some of the details aren't in the journals. And I need to know how much the instructors here know about what the Vance family was doing before my father died."

Brendt's expression shifted slightly. "That's not standard training talk."

"No. It's not."

They reached the corridor junction where their paths split. Brendt's room to the left, Roan's to the right. Brendt lingered for a moment, studying Roan's face with the intensity that meant he was trying to read something beyond the surface.

"Your father," he said quietly. "Was he doing something that got him killed?"

Roan didn't answer immediately. The question was bigger than Brendt knew, bigger than the official story about a Rift expedition gone wrong. But Brendt had earned some degree of trust, and trust required at least partial honesty.

"I don't know yet," Roan said. "But I'm going to find out."

Brendt nodded slowly. "Okay. I'll be around. Tenth bell, you need me, I'm there."

He turned left. Roan watched him go, then turned right toward his room.

The cell was small, spartan, exactly what the Black Spire provided to students below the rank of Hardened. Single bed, desk, storage chest, barred window overlooking the inner courtyard. Roan's few possessions were neatly arranged—training clothes in the chest, books on the desk, Aldric's old notebook hidden in a compartment beneath the floorboards.

He sat on the bed, back against the wall, eyes closed.

The conversation with Vance replayed in his mind. The questions, the assessments, the subtle probing that had almost broken his cover. Vance was smart. Better than the average instructor. And he was paying attention in a way that would eventually force Roan's hand.

The same calculation applied to the larger situation. Someone was watching. Someone had been watching since before he arrived at the Black Spire. The attempt on his life at the Greycross border had been too coordinated, too well-timed, to be coincidence. Someone knew about the Codex Shard, or suspected its existence, and was trying to locate it before Roan could use it effectively.

The Vance ghost. A reputation built on threat assessment and strategic ambiguity. But reputations could be wrong. Reputations could be investigated.

He opened his eyes, reached beneath the floorboards, withdrew the Codex Shard.

It was warm in his palm, always warm, a constant low-level heat that the doctors who'd examined him after the Greycross incident had been unable to explain. The surface was unmarked, featureless, unremarkable to anyone who didn't know what it was. But for a moment, as he turned it over, he felt the faint vibration shift beneath his thumb. Not a warning. Not a signal. Just a texture difference, a reminder that the thing was alive in ways he didn't fully understand.

Sidewound. The term his father had used. A wound that existed to be inverted.

Roan ran his thumb across the surface, feeling the subtle change in frequency. Every time he used the Inversion, it logged the data. Every time he absorbed a side effect and converted it to Vitalis, the pattern was stored. The system was learning from him, adapting, building a database of everything he could do.

What it didn't know was why.

He closed his fingers around the Shard, felt its weight settle against his palm. Four hundred and twenty grams. Heavier than it looked, heavier than it should be. His father had designed it to be concealed, to look like a cheap trinket rather than a weapon. To look like something a student might pick up at a market stall and forget about.

But it wasn't forgotten. It was waiting.

The Inversion had limits—daily capacity, recovery time, the ever-present risk of backlash if he pushed too hard. But within those limits, it was exactly what his father had promised: a method of converting suffering into strength, of turning the weapons his enemies tried to use against him into tools he could wield.

All he had to do was survive long enough to unlock its full potential.

He replaced the Shard in its compartment, replaced the floorboard, sat back on the bed.

Drogan would have answers. Or at least more questions. Either was useful.

The evening meal bell rang somewhere in the distance, a deep, resonant tone that echoed through the stone corridors. Forty minutes until the dining hall opened. Forty minutes to prepare.

Roan stood, crossed to the desk, opened the bottom drawer, and withdrew a leather journal filled with his father's handwriting.

He had questions that needed answers. Drogan would have at least some of them.
