Chapter 94: The Second Bet

The corridor on the third sublevel smelled of machine oil and old copper. Roan kept his breathing measured, one hand trailing the wall, cold iron, riveted seams, the faint vibration of the ventilation system three floors above. The mapping was off. Or rather, the mapping was correct, but someone had moved the contents.

Eckhart's workshop had been here six months ago. Now there was only a sealed door, a new lock, and a sign that read AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY in bureaucratic font.

Roan crouched, examining the lock. Electronic keypad, six-digit code, tamper-resistant housing. Standard Black Spire security architecture. It would take four hours to bypass without the override. With the override, it would take four seconds.

He had the override. Drogan had pressed it into his hand two weeks ago, after the thing with the Greel envoy, when the Warden had started treating him like someone who might need doors opened. The problem wasn't access. The problem was what waited behind the door.

Brendt was inside. Brendt had been inside for six hours. An anonymous note, hand-delivered, requesting his presence regarding "the matters discussed at Aldermere."

That phrase meant exactly one thing. Someone's life had been measured against information and found wanting.

Roan punched in the code. The lock disengaged with a soft hiss.

The workshop beyond looked nothing like a workshop. Someone had stripped out the tools and replaced them with soft lighting, neutral walls, a table with two chairs. On the table sat a tea service. Still steaming. Two glasses. One empty. One untouched.

Brendt sat in the left-hand chair, wrists bound to the armrests, a bruise darkening along his jaw.

Across from him, Selene Ardent poured herself tea with the calm of someone who had all the time in the world.

"Roan." She didn't look up. "I was wondering when you'd arrive."

The arithmetic resolved cleanly. Selene Ardent, daughter of House Ardent, top-ranked student, occasional ally, permanent wildcard, had taken Brendt hostage in the middle of Black Spire's secured sublevel. Either she was stalling for something, or she was confident she could survive the consequences, or she was operating under someone's authority that overrode standard protocol. All three.

Roan stepped fully into the room. The door sealed behind him. He kept his hands visible, his posture neutral.

"You have him," he said. Not a question.

"I have him." Selene finally looked up. Her eyes were the same cool grey they always were, but there was something tighter in the way she held herself. A wire pulled to the edge of snapping. "He's unharmed. I want to be very clear about that."

"Roan, she's not wrong." Brendt's voice was hoarse but steady. He met Roan's eyes with a look that said, well, this is embarrassing. "They haven't done anything to me. The bruise is from the grab. Non-lethal."

"They," Roan repeated.

Selene gestured with her teacup. "My associates. Non-lethal containment protocols unless provoked." She paused. "I wasn't sure if you'd come alone."

"You wanted me here." Roan moved to the table. Slowly. He didn't take the chair. He stood at the head of the table, looking down at her. "You wanted me to see Brendt in the chair. You wanted me to feel the pressure before you opened your hand. So open it."

Selene's composure flickered. Just for a moment. The rehearsed script was already bending under the weight of his refusal to sit.

"The assassination attempt on your mother," she said. "Two months ago. It wasn't the Greel envoy's people."

"Go on."

"It was ours. House Ardent authorized the operation."

The silence lasted three full seconds. Roan used each one to read her. The tight neck, the stillness of her hands, the way she didn't blink.

"You're telling me this now," he said. "In a locked room. With a hostage. You've exhausted your other options." He leaned forward slightly. "Who's pressuring you, Ardent?"

Selene's jaw tightened. Almost imperceptible. But Roan had been watching people lie for seventeen years.

"My family needed what your father left behind. The Veyrn Sanctum." She set her cup down with exact precision. "Your mother gave us partial data under duress. Enough to confirm it exists. Not enough to find it."

"The Veyrn Sanctum." Roan said the words like he was testing the weight of them. "My father was a cemetery caretaker who married above his station. You expect me to believe he left a buried vault of forbidden knowledge?"

"I expect you to believe what you've already seen." Selene's gaze was steady. "Your father walked out of the Hollow Reaches carrying the first Inversion core in seventy years. The Codex Shard on your chest is his work. The path to the Sanctum is locked behind it. That's what we need."

Roan let the silence stretch. Let her sit in the gap she'd created.

"You came at my mother," he said quietly. "You put hands on my family. And now you want to talk about partnerships."

"I came because we're out of time." Selene's voice was clinical. "The assassination attempt was a mistake. I told them it was a mistake. But the information she gave us opened a door, and now other people know it exists. The Sentinel Bureau is already moving. We have a contact inside. They're the ones who told us about you. About the Codex Shard."

Brendt stirred in his chair. "They're called Wraith. That's the codename. The Bureau handler who coordinates House Ardent's operations in this region."

Roan turned to him. Brendt's expression was carefully blank, but there was an edge to his voice. He'd been holding this piece, waiting for the right moment.

"You knew this when you sat down," Roan said.

"Found out while I was waiting. Selene's been very talkative, once she realized I wasn't going to break."

Roan turned back to Selene. "You're not running this operation. Wraith is. House Ardent is the front wall, but the Bureau owns the building."

Selene's silence was confirmation enough.

"So here's your real problem," Roan said. "You can't access the Sanctum without the Codex. The Bureau knows about the Codex. Which means the Bureau will move to take it, and when they do, they'll burn through your family's assets to get it. You're not here to offer a partnership. You're here to beg for a head start."

"I'm here to offer a door." Selene's composure was returning. She reached into her jacket and produced a small data slate. Thin, black, the kind that erased itself after twenty-four hours. She set it on the table between them. "The Veyrn Sanctum is real. It exists inside the Hollow Reaches. Everything my family has gathered in the last two months is on this slate. The partial data from your mother. The expedition logs. The theories about what's inside."

"And in exchange?"

"Access. When you find the way in, we're part of the team that walks through."

Roan looked at the slate. Then at Brendt. Then at Selene.

"You made a bet when you sent people after my mother," he said. "You lost that one. This is your second bet. You're betting I'll trust you. You're betting I'll see the math the same way you do."

"I'm betting you're pragmatic enough to know a good deal when you see one."

"Here's my counter-offer." Roan's voice was flat. "First: Brendt walks. Now. Second: you give me a way to reach Wraith directly. Third: we table the Veyrn Sanctum until I've verified what's on that slate. If it holds up, I'll contact you. But we do it on my timeline, not yours, and not the Bureau's."

Selene's eyes narrowed. "That's not an offer. That's a leash for me to wear while you run in the opposite direction."

"It's the only bet you're going to get." Roan reached down and picked up the slate. "You're betting I'll come around once I see it's real. I'm betting I'll find what my father left behind before you find a way to make the key work without me." He met her eyes. "Let's see whose arithmetic is better."

Selene held his gaze for a long moment. Then, slowly, she reached into her jacket and produced a small communication device. She set it on the table.

"I've used it three times. It reaches Wraith directly."

Roan picked it up. Small, nondescript, the kind that could be hidden in a pocket without anyone noticing.

"Brendt," Selene said.

She produced a key from her belt and unlocked the restraints. Brendt rubbed his wrists, wincing slightly, but his expression was alert. Watching.

"You've verified my good faith," Selene said. "Now what?"

Roan stepped back from the table. The slate was in one pocket. The communicator was in the other.

"Now we wait and see whose math is better." He turned toward the door. "Don't touch him again. Don't touch anyone in my circle again. If I find out Wraith has moved on my family while I'm making up my mind, I'll stop looking for the Sanctum and start looking for you."

He punched in the override. The lock disengaged. He stepped through, Brendt close behind.

The corridor outside was quiet. The associates Selene had mentioned were gone, retreated once they'd seen the situation resolve. Roan walked at a measured pace, his mind working through the implications.

Selene was desperate. The Bureau was involved. The Veyrn Sanctum was real enough to kill for.

And his mother had known.

Brendt fell into step beside him. "You're not going to trust her."

"I'm not going to trust anyone who puts a knife to my friend's throat and calls it a negotiation."

"Then what are you going to do?"

Roan reached the elevator bank. Punched the button.

"Find out who Wraith is. Find out what my mother actually told them. And then find the Veyrn Sanctum."

"That could take months."

"If I'm lucky." The elevator doors opened. Roan stepped inside. "Selene doesn't know what she started when she sent them after my mother. Neither does Wraith."

Brendt stepped in beside him. The doors closed.

"That sounds like a threat."

"No." Roan watched the floor numbers climb. "That's a promise."
