Chapter 172: Cyrus and the Transfer

Cyrus Ashford didn't meet Kael Greel in a dark alley.

That would've been too honest.

He met him in a sanctioned peer review room on the fourth floor of the academic wing, under faculty scheduling authority, with a medical support notice attached and a pot of institutional tea cooling on the table between them.

Roan had to respect the structure.

Not the ethics. The structure.

Three days after Kael's arrival, Lysa Renn filed a request to resume peer support contact with Cyrus under recovery guidelines for post-tournament stress adjustment. Cole approved it. The system assigned a room. Cyrus accepted after seventeen minutes, which suggested hesitation rather than refusal. Good.

Then Kael North, still using the alias in student-facing systems, registered for the same open academic review block under Professor Ilmere's coursework remediation.

Not the same meeting. Same corridor. Same time.

Coincidence, if one were determined to be murdered politely.

Roan watched the corridor feed from Ines's borrowed maintenance terminal in an unused storage room one floor above.

Borrowed wasn't the word the maintenance department would use. Ines had described the access as "temporarily liberated from underemployment." Selene had objected for thirty seconds, then helped mask the login trail because apparently limited trust had begun eating everyone's procedural boundaries.

Brendt listened remotely again, under protest that he couldn't see the feed.

"You are describing facial expressions badly," Brendt said through the audio bead.

Roan didn't look away from the screen. "Kael is standing."

"That isn't a facial expression."

"He's doing it suspiciously."

"You see? This is why I need video."

"No."

Selene, seated beside Roan with a tablet open, said, "He means Kael positioned himself where Cyrus will see him when leaving but Lysa won't have to pass him."

"Thank you," Brendt said. "Selene understands narration."

"I understand surveillance," Selene said.

Ines leaned against the shelves, eating something from a paper packet. "Same art, worse lighting."

On the feed, Lysa Renn arrived first.

Roan had reviewed her file. Brendt's note at the bottom read: "She pulled me out of a collapsed ward in Ironholm. Trust her." The formal record listed second-year medical support track. Tempered peak with strong stabilizing Vitalis control but limited combat ambition. Former support member in Cyrus's tournament preparation circle. Left after his post-final conduct became abusive toward staff, though the report had phrased it as "irreconcilable methodology concerns." Sensible. She had short brown hair, tired eyes, and the expression of someone who had learned to triage other people's stupidity without accepting ownership.

Good choice by Brendt.

Annoying.

Lysa entered the room.

Cyrus arrived six minutes late.

Not enough to insult. Enough to signal reluctance.

His posture was defensive before the door closed. Arms tight, shoulders back, chin lifted. A man preparing to reject pity.

Kael appeared two minutes later at the far end of the corridor, carrying three books and looking mildly lost.

"Ridiculous," Ines said.

Selene's mouth tightened. "He's good."

Kael checked a room number and frowned, then stopped outside the wrong door.

The door beside Cyrus's meeting room.

He opened a book and waited.

Inside the peer review room, Lysa and Cyrus spoke. The camera had no audio; Cole had refused to authorize audio surveillance of a medical-adjacent meeting without cause strong enough to survive audit. Roan had considered listing House Greel assassination grooming and been told, by Cole's silence, that this still didn't solve admissibility.

Paper cages everywhere.

So they watched body language.

Lysa sat angled rather than square, non-threatening. Cyrus stayed near the door at first. She gestured toward the chair. He refused. She said something. He snapped back. She didn't react. Good.

After four minutes, Cyrus sat.

Brendt asked, "Did he sit?"

"Yes."

"Good."

"You sound relieved."

"I am a complex person."

"You are a person who selected well."

Silence from the audio bead.

Then Brendt said, too lightly, "Careful. Praise may affect my recovery."

Roan ignored the deflection.

On the feed, Lysa slid a paper across the table. Cyrus didn't take it. She pulled it back and wrote something on it instead.

Selene watched closely. "She isn't trying to comfort him."

"No," Roan said. "She's making him argue with something external."

"Which helps?"

"Maybe."

Ines chewed thoughtfully. "I prefer knives."

"We know," Selene said.

Cyrus leaned forward. Anger, but engaged. Lysa held up one hand. Boundary. He stopped.

Good.

For twenty minutes, the meeting looked salvageable.

Then Cyrus stood too quickly.

Lysa didn't.

He said something sharp enough that even without audio, the shape of it cut. Lysa's face changed, not wounded, exactly. Disappointed in a way that seemed practiced.

Cyrus turned and left.

Kael, outside, looked up from his book at exactly the right time.

"Here," Roan said.

Cyrus almost walked past.

Almost.

Kael shifted half a step, not blocking him. Available.

Cyrus stopped.

The corridor camera caught both in profile. Kael spoke first, likely apology or room question. Cyrus responded shortly. Kael smiled, shook his head, and said something that made Cyrus glance back at Lysa's closed door.

Brendt's voice came tight. "What is happening?"

"Contact."

"Bad?"

"Professional."

"That's worse."

Yes.

Kael didn't linger. He spoke for less than thirty seconds, then stepped away as if embarrassed by the interruption. He left the corridor before Cyrus could decide whether to continue.

That was the move.

Not recruitment. Not yet.

Permission.

A first contact so small Cyrus could pretend it hadn't mattered while thinking about it all evening.

Roan memorized every visible word shape he could. The feed quality limited certainty, but one phrase looked clear.

*They hate needing you.*

Maybe.

If accurate, that was exceptional poison.

Not "they hate you." Too crude. Not "you deserve revenge." Too direct. "They hate needing you" implied Cyrus still had value, that the institution's pity was fear in disguise, that rejection could be reinterpreted as leverage.

Lysa emerged three minutes after Cyrus left.

She didn't look toward Kael's retreating back. That was good discipline, or very good anger. She walked to the end of the corridor, took the stairwell instead of the lift, and paused on the landing beneath Selene's position.

Selene's voice came through the bead. "She knows I am here."

Roan watched the empty corridor feed. "Did you reveal?"

"No."

"Then she's competent."

"I am aware."

A moment later, Lysa's voice entered the channel through Selene's bead, quiet and controlled. "Tell Holloway his favor is now expensive."

Brendt inhaled over the audio. "Lysa?"

"You sound awful."

"I am recovering heroically."

"You are recovering loudly, then. Same thing for you."

Roan said, "Report."

Lysa paused. "Vance?"

"Yes."

"I dislike that I am unsurprised."

"Report."

"She's doing you a favor," Brendt said.

"And reporting determines whether it remains useful."

Lysa made a small sound. "He isn't ready to be helped by anyone he thinks is above him. He hears concern as confirmation that he fell. He hears discipline better than sympathy, but only if it comes from someone who doesn't seem to benefit from his recovery."

Roan looked at Brendt's speaker. Brendt said nothing.

Lysa continued, "He asked whether official records can be corrected after a public loss. Not whether people forgive. Not whether he can move on. Records."

Selene whispered, "Status wound."

"Yes," Lysa said. "And he's angry enough to accept almost any path that calls itself correction instead of comfort."

Roan's gaze returned to the screen, where Kael had vanished from view. "Did he mention Kael?"

"No. But when he left, he wasn't finished being angry at me. Someone intercepted that anger in the corridor."

"You saw?"

"I heard a voice. Northern accent, softened. Polite."

Kael had wanted Lysa to hear enough to worry, not enough to accuse.

Professional.

Lysa said, "Holloway, you didn't tell me this was House-level."

"I told you predatory mentorship," Brendt said weakly.

"You always did understate catastrophes when trying to be considerate."

"I have many virtues."

"Name two later. I am busy."

Despite the situation, Ines smiled.

Lysa's voice hardened. "I can keep meeting him if he permits it. But if an alternative gives him measurable improvement, I lose him."

Roan said, "Then we provide measurable improvement first."

"Through whom?"

"Working."

"Work faster."

The channel clicked off from Selene's side.

Brendt was quiet for several seconds.

Then he said, "She sounded tired."

"Yes."

"She always sounds tired when people are making themselves harder to save."

Roan didn't answer.

He had known people like that in Greycross. Not healers, exactly. People who patched wounds in back rooms for cash and cursed at anyone bleeding on the clean towels. They all sounded tired. The world produced more damage than competent hands could reasonably repair, and the damaged often bit.

Cyrus was biting.

Kael was offering him sharper teeth.

Roan leaned back.

"Kael wins first contact," he said.

Brendt swore softly.

Selene closed her eyes for a second. "Can Lysa recover it?"

"Maybe. If we don't contaminate."

"Meaning?"

"No Roan contact. No official warning. No visible intervention."

Ines brushed crumbs from her fingers. "So we watch him eat poison slowly."

"We identify antidote before dosage becomes lethal."

"That sounded like yes."

"It was yes."

Roan disliked it.

That didn't make it wrong.

- break -

The antidote, if it existed, wasn't kindness.

Kindness would insult Cyrus. Pity would enrage him. Threats would validate Kael. Isolation had already made him vulnerable. Public inclusion would look staged. Private outreach from Roan would fail catastrophically, unless the goal was to drive Cyrus directly into Greel arms and hand him a decorative knife.

Roan built the decision tree on his board while Selene reviewed House Ardent's next archive access path and Ines prepared a list of Kael's observed contacts.

Cyrus needs:

1. Status not tied to defeating Roan.

2. Competence witness not fit Roan.

3. Rivalry reframed as recoverable loss, not identity death.

4. Physical training path with visible progress.

5. Someone to dislike who isn't Roan? Dangerous but possible.

Brendt, listening through audio, said, "That fifth point is ominous."

"You aren't supposed to be reading the board."

"I can hear the marker."

"That isn't how reading works."

"I know you."

Roan looked at the audio bead.

Selene did too, with faint interest.

Brendt continued, "Don't give Cyrus a new enemy to stabilize him."

"I wrote dangerous."

"You wrote possible after it."

Roan capped the marker. "Medical patients shouldn't psychoanalyze strategy."

"Friends should prevent murder boards from becoming too murdery."

Ines looked up. "Murdery isn't a word."

"It should be, around Roan."

Selene said, "Unfortunately, I understand the meaning."

Roan erased point five.

Not because Brendt had ordered it. Because the objection was correct.

Cyrus didn't need a new enemy. He needed an arena where loss could become work instead of rot.

"Placement drill remediation," Roan said.

Selene looked up. "What?"

"Cyrus appealed reputational harm. That means he cares about record correction. Offer a legitimate path to recover official standing through technical evaluation. Not combat against me. Not public duel. Structured performance."

Ines nodded slowly. "Give him a ladder."

"Give him a ladder someone else holds."

"Lysa?" Selene asked.

"No. She handles medical-social stabilization. The ladder needs authority he respects and doesn't read as pity."

"Professor Ilmere?"

"Wrong domain."

"Drogan?"

"Too close to me."

Ines smiled. "Cyrus respects power. Give him an instructor who dislikes you."

Roan looked at her.

She smiled wider.

"No," Selene said immediately.

Ines said, "You don't know who I mean."

"I know your tone."

Roan said, "Master Vale."

Silence.

Selene stared at him. "His father?"

"No. Uncle. House Vale technical examiner. He publicly criticized Drogan's tournament methods after the final."

Brendt groaned over the audio. "Oh, I hate that this is plausible."

Master Orien Vale was a dueling traditionalist with enough technical knowledge to be irritating and enough pride to hate Roan on principle. He had called Roan's final strategy "unworthy of formal combat culture," which had improved Roan's opinion of his own final strategy. But Orien Vale also valued discipline and record, plus House face. If Cyrus continued to decay under Greel influence, House Vale lost more than pride. They lost a useful heir.

A formal remediation evaluation under Orien's supervision could offer Cyrus status repair without requiring him to trust anyone fit Roan.

It could also give House Vale reason to monitor Kael.

Useful.

Risk: House Vale might use the process to sharpen Cyrus against Roan later.

Acceptable. Later enemies could be handled later. Current poison needed dilution.

Selene said, "Can Cole arrange it?"

"Not without seeming to intervene," Roan said.

Ines pointed at herself with a crumb-dusted finger. "I know someone in the Dueling Council office who owes me money."

"Of course you do," Selene said.

"Technically he owes someone else money, but I own the debt now."

Roan considered. "Send anonymous recommendation citing reputational exposure and talent waste."

"House Vale loves both phrases."

"Good."

Brendt said, "Add that Cyrus is being approached by northern transfer influence."

"No," Roan said.

"Too obvious?"

"Too useful if intercepted."

"Right."

Brendt sounded tired now. Roan checked the time.

"You are past your rest window."

"I am contributing."

"You contributed. Sleep."

"Don't hang up on—"

Roan ended the channel.

Selene looked at him.

"What?"

"That was abrupt."

"He would continue."

"He cares."

"Yes. That's why he would continue."

She accepted that, though not comfortably.

Ines stood. "I'll send the recommendation. Also, Kael purchased two practice-room reservations under his alias. One solo, one open slot."

"For Cyrus?" Selene asked.

"Not named. Open slots let someone join without record until check-in."

Roan looked at the board.

Kael was moving faster than expected.

Not rashly. Efficiently.

"Time?"

"Tomorrow evening."

"Location?"

"East annex practice room six."

Roan knew the room. Small, reinforced, poor observation angles, old cameras with blind spots near the equipment racks.

Naturally.

"We watch," he said.

Selene's eyes narrowed. "If Cyrus enters?"

"Then we let the conversation start."

"And if Kael gives him the knife?"

Roan touched Sidewound's hilt through his coat.

"Then we decide whether the handle or the blade is cheaper to break."

- break -

Cyrus entered practice room six at eighteen-oh-three the next day.

Kael was already inside.

Roan watched from the maintenance crawlspace above the room, lying flat on cold metal beside Ines while Selene monitored the corridor below. The crawlspace smelled of dust and oil, plus old insulation. It offered a narrow view through a vent grille and no comfortable position for knees. Ines had claimed the better angle by arriving early, which Roan respected and intended to repay by not moving when her elbow inevitably went numb.

The room below held two practice dummies, a weapon rack, a padded floor, and Kael Greel wearing plain training clothes.

Cyrus stopped at the door. "You booked this?"

Kael looked up. "Open slot. I can leave."

He began to gather his towel.

Good move.

Offer retreat. Make Cyrus choose.

Cyrus said, "Don't."

Kael paused. "All right."

Cyrus stepped inside. The door closed.

Selene's voice whispered through the bead. "Corridor clear."

Roan didn't answer.

Below, Kael picked up a wooden practice blade and tossed it lightly to Cyrus, hilt first.

Cyrus caught it.

There was the knife.

Not literal. Worse.

A tool shaped like permission.

"You want a bout?" Cyrus asked.

"No," Kael said. "I want to understand what happened in the final."

Cyrus's face shut down. "Ask the forums."

"I did. They're stupid."

Cyrus almost smiled.

Kael continued, "I watched the footage. Vance didn't beat your strength. He bought your assumptions and sold them back to you with interest."

Roan considered objecting to the phrasing.

Unfortunately, it was accurate.

Cyrus gripped the wooden blade. "You came here to analyze my humiliation?"

"I came here because everyone else is pretending it was either tragic or deserved. Both are useless."

Cyrus said nothing for a long moment. Then: "Why should I trust your analysis?" Kael met his gaze without flinching. "You shouldn't. But you can verify it yourself." Cyrus didn't leave.

Kael stepped toward the dummy and tapped its shoulder with his blade. "You had superior reach, cleaner baseline, better public technique, and institutional support. He had timing, secrecy, and a willingness to make the fight ugly before you understood the rules changed."

Cyrus's jaw worked.

"He cheated," Cyrus said.

Kael tilted his head. "Did he break rules?"

"No."

"Then he didn't cheat."

That was unexpected.

Roan went still.

Cyrus looked angry. "Whose side are you on?"

"Yours, if you want to stop being easy to beat."

Silence.

Excellent poison.

Not flattery. Not revenge. Improvement.

Kael had understood Cyrus more accurately than the others. Cyrus didn't only want Roan hurt. He wanted the final to become something other than proof of his own inadequacy. Kael offered a path: the loss was a technical failure, not an identity death. Correct the failure, and the self survives.

Dangerous because it was partly true.

Cyrus lifted the practice blade. "Show me."

Kael smiled.

Not the polite smile from the reception hall.

Smaller.

Sharper.

He stepped in.

For ten minutes, he dismantled Cyrus without appearing superior.

He corrected foot angle. Timing. Recovery after feint. Overcommitment when insulted. He let Cyrus land two strikes that Roan was almost certain he could have avoided. He named Roan's public habits without malice and showed counters that would work against the version of Roan visible on tournament footage.

Visible version.

Not Sidewound. Not Third Refusal. Not Crimson Wake refinement. Not the temple. Not the Black Rite. Not the real peak.

Still, the lesson had value.

Cyrus listened.

That was the problem.

At the end, Kael stepped back. "You don't need revenge first."

Cyrus breathed hard, sweat on his face. "First?"

"You need competence so revenge doesn't embarrass you twice."

There it was.

The hook under the medicine.

Cyrus looked down at the wooden blade.

Roan waited.

The correct answer, from a healthy person, would be to reject the premise. To say he wanted to recover, not revenge. To say he would train for himself.

Cyrus wasn't healthy.

He said, "Again."

Kael nodded.

From the corridor, Selene whispered, "Do we intervene?"

Roan watched Kael reset his stance, watched Cyrus mirror him, watched the first line of Greel influence settle into muscle memory.

They didn't leave the crawlspace immediately.

Cyrus and Kael continued for another seventeen minutes, and those seventeen minutes mattered more than the first ten. The first lesson had been recruitment. The next was investment. Kael made Cyrus repeat one correction until frustration became concentration, then stopped just before fatigue made the work ugly. He never praised too much. He never mocked. He gave small, precise acknowledgments that landed harder than compliments because Cyrus couldn't easily dismiss them as pity.

"Again," Kael said after one exchange.

Cyrus repeated.

"Better."

One word. Clean dose.

Roan felt Ines shift beside him. "He isn't making Cyrus dependent on anger alone."

"No."

"That's worse."

"Yes."

Selene whispered from below, "Why?"

Roan watched Kael adjust distance so Cyrus solved the next exchange himself. "Because anger burns fast. Competence compounds."

Kael wanted Cyrus improved. Anger alone was not enough. An enraged tool broke after one swing. An improved one could be pointed repeatedly and might even believe the hand guiding it had given him back dignity.

This was why Roan had not intervened.

Also why not intervening tasted like ash.

Cyrus landed a clean strike on Kael's shoulder. Kael allowed the contact to count, stepped back, and inclined his head.

Cyrus stared at the mark his wooden blade had left on Kael's training shirt.

For the first time since the tournament, his face looked less dead.

Not happy. Not healed.

Worse. Hopeful in the direction of a knife.

Roan had seen that expression in Greycross boys after gang recruiters taught them how to throw a punch properly. The first lesson was always free. Not because the recruiter was generous. Because the first moment a boy felt his weakness become technique, he mistook the hand on his shoulder for salvation.

Cyrus lowered the practice blade. "Why help me?"

There it was.

Kael wiped sweat from his jaw with a towel. "Because wasted talent irritates me."

Good answer. Too good.

No loyalty claimed. No revenge offered. Just judgment and utility. Exactly answer Cyrus could accept without feeling bought.

"And Vance?" Cyrus asked.

Kael smiled faintly. "What about him?"

Cyrus's grip tightened.

Kael shook his head. "Train to beat the mistake first. The person comes later."

Roan closed his eyes for one second.

Not from anger. To remove every irrelevant reaction and leave the sentence clean.

Train to beat the mistake first. The person comes later.

Kael was building a doctrine.

A doctrine could survive setbacks. That made it far more dangerous than impulse.

When the session ended, Kael left first. Cyrus remained in the room alone, staring at the practice blade in his hand.

He didn't throw it aside.

He placed it back on the rack carefully.

That was the worst part.

"No," he said.

Ines turned her head slightly in the dark crawlspace. "You sure?"

No.

But certainty was a luxury for people not standing between poison and knives.

"If we stop it now," Roan said, "Cyrus chooses him fully."

"And if we don't?" Selene asked.

"Then we build a better counterlesson."

Below, Kael corrected Cyrus's grip with two fingers and no visible aggression.

Cyrus accepted the correction.

Roan's hand rested on Sidewound's hilt, motionless.

Some cuts had to wait until the seam appeared.

Kael Greel had chosen Cyrus Ashford as a handle.

Now Roan needed to decide whether to blunt the blade, break the hand, or let the weapon swing once and catch the person behind it.

He gave himself until the next session. If the seam didn't appear by then, he'd force Vale's hand.

None of the options were clean.

That didn't make them unfamiliar.
