Chapter 55: Bait Accepted

The liquid hit his palm like cold fire.

Roan didn't flinch. Flinching was for people who hadn't calculated the odds. He held his hand up, watching the amber substance spread across his skin, and ran the math.

Cyrus had miscalculated. Not about the vial—he'd calculated that correctly. About what Roan would do with it.

"You absolute idiot," Roan said. Not to Cyrus. To the liquid currently seeping into his pores. "Do you have any idea what you've given me?"

Cyrus stood frozen at the table's edge, face cycling through confusion and fury, plus dawning horror. Behind him, Selene had gone very still.

Roan closed his fist. The liquid couldn't evaporate—it was too viscous, designed for topical absorption. That meant it would have to go somewhere. Into his skin. Into his bloodstream.

Which was exactly what the Codex Shard was waiting for.

*Sidewound detected. Class: Alchemical poison. Initiating Inversion protocol.*

Roan felt the cold spread through his palm as the Shard began its work. The poison's effects started to manifest—a burning sensation along his meridians, designed to corrupt Vitalis flow—but the Shard caught them at a heartbeat and inverted.

*Conversion successful: +85 Vitalis.*

*Sidewound purged. Toxin neutralized.*

*Adverse pattern inverted.*

The burning stopped. His hand was clean. No cost. No ledger. The Inversion didn't charge for its function; it simply resolved the contradiction.

He opened his fist and showed Cyrus. "Thanks for the donation."

Silence.

Cyrus's mouth worked soundlessly. Then: "That—that should have—"

"Killed me? Crippled me? Given you an excuse to be disappointed in your own incompetence?" Roan picked up a napkin and wiped his palm with methodical calm. "Let me explain what you got wrong, Ashford. The vial was good. The delivery was excellent. The poison itself was worth more than your entire family heirloom collection."

He dropped the napkin in the trash.

"What you failed to account for was that I've been poisoning myself deliberately for six months. My body's adapted to reject toxins. And my father developed a countermeasure."

Selene let out a breath that might have been a laugh.

Cyrus looked like he'd been slapped. "You can't—the academy's best alchemists designed that compound—"

"And it would have worked on anyone else." Roan pulled out his credit chip, examined it, and set it on the table next to Cyrus's abandoned poison vial. "Here's what's going to happen now. You're going to leave. You're going to tell anyone who asks that this meeting was a business negotiation that didn't pan out. And you're going to remember that the next time you consider wasting my resources."

He didn't raise his voice. He didn't need to.

"And if I find out who you bought that compound from?" Cyrus's voice had gone cold, the bluster replaced by something more dangerous. "You're going to tell me. Because that was Ashford property you just destroyed."

"Alchemical property," Roan corrected. "There's a difference. And no, I won't tell you. Because whatever poor excuse for an alchemist sold you something that worthless doesn't deserve to be harassed by an Ashford tantrum."

He turned to Selene. "Walk with me?"

Cyrus said nothing as they left. Roan let the immunity story settle in the air behind him. It was a patch, not a shield. It would hold against Cyrus's bruised pride for a day, maybe two. Long enough for his immediate turmoil to cool. By the time someone sharper revisited the details, the trail would be cold.

---

The corridor outside the study was cold stone and morning light. Selene walked beside him in silence for thirty paces before speaking.

"The compound he used. That wasn't standard grade."

"I know."

"It was military-grade nerve corrosive. One drop on skin, forty seconds to systemic absorption. Untreated, permanent damage to meridian pathways within three minutes." She glanced at him. "You should be screaming right now."

"I'm not."

"No. You're not." Her eyes were sharp, calculating. "Which means either you're hiding pain better than any student I've ever trained, or—"

"Or?"

"Or you did something I don't understand."

Roan allowed himself a small pause. The tally was complicated here. Selene had just watched him shrug off something that should have been fatal. She'd drawn her own conclusions—wrong ones—but conclusions nonetheless.

He could lie. Maintain the status quo.

Or he could give her a piece of the truth and see what she did with it.

"My father," he said carefully, "left me something. A defensive mechanism. It activates when I'm exposed to harmful substances and converts the damage into something useful."

Selene stopped walking. She just looked at him. Not with suspicion. With something closer to recognition.

"Your father," she repeated. "Aldric Vance."

"Yes."

"And this mechanism. It lets you absorb poison?"

"Essentially."

"It should be impossible."

"Most things about me are." He started walking again. She fell into step beside him, still silent, processing.

"The Ashfords won't let this go," she finally said. "Cyrus will tell his father. They'll investigate what compound you used to counter the nerve corrosive. They'll trace it back to you eventually, if they haven't already."

"Let them investigate."

"You're not concerned?"

"I'm concerned about the right things." He thought about it. "The Ashfords will spin this as me being reckless, dangerous, unstable. Fine. Let them. It's better than the alternative."

"Which is?"

"They know what I really am."

Selene's expression flickered. "And what is that?"

"Someone who doesn't play by the rules they think apply." He reached a junction in the corridor and paused. "You should go. I have training."

"With Warden Hale?"

"Yes."

She nodded slowly. "He knows. About your father's mechanism."

"Everyone who matters knows."

"And Brendt?"

Roan hesitated. "Brendt knows that I'm different. He doesn't know the specifics. I'd like to keep it that way."

"Even though he's proven himself trustworthy?"

"Especially because of that. The less people know, the less they can accidentally reveal." He met her eyes. "You understand. You have secrets of your own."

She didn't deny it. "Be careful with Cyrus. He's not as weak as he pretends to be."

"I'm never careless."

He turned and walked away.

---

Drogan's training hall was underground, accessed through a concealed door behind the Black Spire's eastern armory. The space was cavernous—high ceilings, reinforced walls, and a floor that looked like old bloodstained stone but was synthetic material designed to absorb impact.

Roan arrived to find the old man already waiting, standing in the center of the room with his arms crossed.

"The Ashford boy tried to poison you this morning."

Roan hadn't heard about Drogan's information network. He kept that to himself and responded. "Yes."

"And you countered it with your father's technique."

"Yes."

"How much did you gain?"

He considered lying. Decided against it. "Eighty-five Vitalis. No tax. The toxin was classified as a Sidewound type the Shard recognized. It inverted cleanly."

Drogan nodded slowly. "That's efficient."

"Cyrus brought military-grade compound. The alchemist who sold it to him will be disappointed when they hear it didn't work."

"Military-grade." Drogan's eyes narrowed. "The Ashfords don't have military contacts. Not officially."

"They have something."

"Or someone gave them access." The old man uncrossed his arms. "This changes the readout."

Roan felt the shift in the room's atmosphere. Drogan was moving from mentor to strategist. "You think the poisoning was a test."

"I think the poisoning was the opening move in a larger game. Cyrus is ambitious, but he's not stupid enough to try assassination in the first week of the new semester. Not unless someone convinced him the risk was worth it."

"Who?"

"That's what we need to find out." Drogan gestured toward the training circle. "But first, you need to understand something. The poison didn't just fail to kill you. It announced you. Every day you walk the Black Spire's corridors wearing that pendant is a day someone is testing it, measuring it, trying to figure out what it can and can't do."

The Codex Shard pulsed from his spine. He resisted the urge to touch it.

"The Inversion is a gift," Drogan continued. "But it's also a beacon. And eventually, someone will come along who knows what that beacon means."

"My father."

"Your father is dead." Drogan's voice was flat. "But his enemies aren't. The Inversion archive was destroyed for a reason, Roan. And the people who destroyed it are still alive."

The words hung in the air. Roan had known this—in the abstract, in the back part of his mind that processed threat assessments. But hearing Drogan say it aloud made it real.

"You're saying the Founding Seven know about the Codex Shard."

"I'm saying they might. Or someone who serves them might." Drogan moved to the center of the training circle. "Which means we have a limited window. The longer you stay visible, the more likely someone puts the pieces together."

"How long?"

"Depends on how sloppy your enemies are." The old man's stance shifted into a combat-ready position. "Now. Show me what you learned from the poison."

---

The training was brutal.

Drogan didn't hold back—not physically, but in information. He attacked with questions while Roan blocked strikes, demanding tactical analysis mid-combat, forcing him to think while his body was under pressure.

"Cyrus is a pawn. If he's being used, who's controlling him?"

"Ashford family leadership. His father."

"Wrong. Cyrus has been acting independently for months. His father thinks he's focused on academics. This poisoning attempt was personal, not familial."

Roan adjusted his blocking angle. "Then he's being influenced. Not ordered."

"Better. How?"

The old man's fist caught him in the ribs. Roan staggered, recalculated, struck back with a counter that Drogan deflected easily.

"I don't have enough data."

"Wrong again. You have plenty of data. You're not using it." Drogan stepped back, letting Roan recover. "Think. Cyrus's behavior patterns. Who benefits from his independence? Who has access to him?"

The answer surfaced slowly. "Someone inside the family. Someone who wants Cyrus to make moves his father wouldn't authorize."

"Go on."

"The Ashford patriarch is traditional. Conservative. He wouldn't risk direct action against a Vance—not publicly." Roan straightened, feeling the pieces click into place. He had seen the name before. Lineage records, alumni archives, marginal notes on a failed succession vote. The pieces had been in front of him for weeks. He just hadn't assembled them. "But someone younger—more ambitious, with their own agenda—"

"Would see a Vance revival as a threat to the entire power structure."

"And would use a pawn like Cyrus to test my defenses."

Drogan nodded. "Now. Who's the most likely candidate?"

Roan considered the Ashford family tree. "His uncle. Edric Ashford. He's been positioned for a leadership role but keeps getting passed over."

"Good." The old man's voice carried grudging approval. "Now. How do you use this?"

"I don't."

Drogan paused. "Explain."

"I use them using me. Right now, they think they've learned something about my defenses. They think the poisoning revealed a weakness they can exploit. Let them think that." Roan rolled his shoulder, working out the stiffness. "The information asymmetry is my advantage. I know they're watching. They don't know I know."

"And when they make their next move?"

"Then I'll know who to target." He met Drogan's eyes. "But right now, I need to get stronger. Faster. The people you're talking about—they're not Tempered or Hardened. They're Forged at minimum. Maybe Crowned."

"Sovereign-level isn't out of the question."

Roan absorbed this. Sovereign. The eighth tier. The rank his father had allegedly reached before dying.

"Then I need to break through to Hardened before the end of this semester. And Forged within the year."

"Ambitious."

"Numbers are harder to bribe than people. If I'm going to survive what's coming, I can't stay at Tempered forever."

Drogan studied him. Then, unexpectedly, the old man's face creased into something like a smile.

"Your father said the same thing once. Right before he made a decision that got a lot of people killed."

The words landed like a punch. Roan didn't let his expression change.

"I'm not my father."

"No. You're more careful." Drogan stepped out of the training circle. "That's good. The Discipline doesn't need martyrs. It needs survivors."

---

Later, after the training was done and Drogan had dismissed him with instructions to review his Hollowstrike footwork, Roan sat alone in the underground chamber.

The Codex Shard pulsed from his spine.

He closed his eyes and checked his status.

*Vitalis: 1,412.*

*Rank: Tempered (late sub-stage).*

*Estimated time to Hardened breakthrough: 4–6 weeks at current rate.*

The figures were good. Better than good. His recovery from the poisoning had pushed him past the 1,400 mark—a threshold he'd been approaching for weeks.

But Drogan's words kept echoing.

*Your father is dead. But his enemies aren't.*

Roan opened his eyes and stared at the wall.

His father had been Sovereign-class. Crowned-level, maybe. The Founding Seven had destroyed an entire civilization to eliminate the Inversion method. And now, some fragment of that method was sitting in Roan's chest, pulsing with every heartbeat.

How long before someone noticed?

How long before the same people who'd killed Aldric Vance came looking for his son?

He didn't have answers. He had math. And the figures said he needed to get stronger, faster, before the window Drogan mentioned closed entirely.

Roan stood. There was a market district three blocks from the Black Spire that sold alchemical supplies to students with more curiosity than caution. He needed to restock his poisons. If he was going to keep the Inversion ready for the next threat, he needed a constant supply of low-grade toxins to trigger the reversal on demand.

Not for strength. For readiness.

The Codex Shard pulsed again.

*You're thinking like a soldier,* the ghost of his father's voice said. *Good. But don't forget to think like a Vance.*

*Math doesn't lie,* Roan thought back. *And Vances don't lose.*

He walked toward the exit, already calculating his next move.

The game was bigger than he'd thought. But he was used to playing games with uneven odds.
