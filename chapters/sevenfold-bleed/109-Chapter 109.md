Chapter 109: Tournament Ledger

The compression array had seven flaws. Roan had found five.

The sixth was the floor grout, feldspar and lime, both moderate Vitalis conductors, bleeding ambient Rift energy back up through the stone in a loop that would cost him six percent efficiency. He patched it with a strip of vulcanized rubber from his pack. The seventh flaw was himself. Seventeen years old. Growth plates still open in his knees, his shoulders, the base of his skull. The Veyrn Sanctum's architecture had been calibrated for Aldric Vance at twenty-four, fully fused, fully grown.

Roan had to compensate for the difference or the marrow pulse would misalign and shatter his tibia instead of hardening it.

He recalculated the angle of his splayed legs, shifting his left foot three centimeters outward. The array's channels, etched into the floor by a hand he only knew from echo and reputation, lit in sequence. Amber. Rust. Dull arterial red. The drained Tier 2 Vitalis Core seated in the central well sputtered, unstable, exactly as advertised. He'd bought it from a Rift salvager for forty credits because its bleed-rate made it useless for standard training.

For Roan, useless was the point.

The tournament registration closed in nine hours. He was still Tempered. Publicly, barely that.

The figures said the Core's decay would produce twelve sequential Sidewounds across his major bone nodes if he held the channel open for nine seconds. His current daily capacity sat at three of five. The first three pulses would convert cleanly. The remaining nine would have to ride on whatever residue remained, or on raw endurance.

Three converted. Six endured raw. The equation was simple.

The question was whether his nervous system would stay coherent long enough to care.

Roan seated himself in the array's center, spine aligned to the channel hub, palms down on the cold stone. The Sanctum was silent except for the low thrum of Ironholm's municipal Vitalis grid filtering through bedrock three stories up. He activated the well.

The Core screamed.

Not sound. Vibration. A subsonic shudder that entered through his coccyx and climbed.

His left femur went first. The marrow didn't burn. It crystallized, a lattice of expanding frost racing outward from the hollow, converting blood to slush faster than his heart could push fresh heat. The pain had a topology. Roan mapped it. Distal end first, proximal lag by two-tenths of a second. The Shard registered the insult.

[Sidewound absorbed. Conversion successful: +42 Vitalis. Daily capacity: 2/5.]

The femur reset. Denser. He felt the weight of it immediately, a leaden tug inside his thigh that his muscles hadn't been recruited to manage. He adjusted his Vitalis flow to compensate, redirecting channel pressure to the right femur before his gait could destabilize.

Second pulse. Right femur. Same topology, mirrored.

[Sidewound absorbed. Conversion successful: +39 Vitalis. Daily capacity: 1/5.]

Two-tenths faster than the left. His symmetry was off. Growth plate variance. He kept the discrepancy, left distal epiphysis still unfused, buffering the pulse, and leaned forward, shifting torso mass to preload the lumbar channel.

Third pulse. Pelvis.

[Sidewound absorbed. Conversion successful: +41 Vitalis. Daily capacity: 0/5.]

Capacity depleted.

The pelvis locked, a ring of stone seating itself inside his hips. Roan exhaled through his teeth. Three down. Nine to go. The Core had eight seconds of decay remaining. Without conversion, the Sidewound would persist as pure trauma. The registration-window window was irrelevant now. The Shard had nothing left to give.

Fourth pulse. Ribs four and five on the left side.

The crystallization skipped the marrow entirely and sheeted across the bone surface. A plating of frost under the skin. Roan's left arm spasmed. He pinned it with his right knee, holding the channel posture. The ribs groaned. Not broken. Bending. The stress load traveled into his sternum, seeking the sternal angle, the weakest junction. He flooded the joint with raw Vitalis, unconverted, unrefined, wasteful, to keep it from shearing.

Five seconds left.

Fifth pulse. Scapulae.

Wrong.

The array had been designed for broader shoulders. Aldric's shoulders. The pulse caught his left scapula on the lateral border and ricocheted toward the glenoid cavity instead of driving into the blade's core. Bone-dense ice formed in the joint. His arm went dead from the elbow down, a cold so absolute it read as numbness.

Roan looked at the hanging limb. Evaluated it. The arm was a non-essential variable for the next four seconds. He locked his cervical spine and diverted all remaining Vitalis to his trunk.

Sixth pulse. Skull base.

The world went white.

Not metaphor. The occipital plate took the impact and his visual cortex misfired, flooding his field with blank light. He was blind and freezing, plus three pulses remained. His heart rate, he counted it by the thud against his frozen ribs, spiked to one-seventy. The Core's thrum was a drill inside his mastoid process.

Seventh pulse. Mandible and teeth.

A molar cracked. He swallowed the fragment. Blood, hot and copper, cut a channel through the ice in his throat. The warmth was almost worse than the cold. It reminded his nervous system what temperature it was supposed to prefer.

Eighth pulse. Tibiae.

Both shins lit up like struck flint. The growth plates screamed. The epiphyseal gaps, those thin cartilaginous seams he'd miscalculated, took the pulse as invitation to fuse prematurely, and wrong. Roan felt his left leg shorten by a millimeter. Then two. The bone was compressing unevenly, the distal end fusing faster than the proximal.

Not good.

He had one pulse left. One second. If the tibia set in this configuration, he'd limp for the rest of his life. No Inversion would fix a malunion. The ledger was catastrophic. A two-millimeter discrepancy in tibial length altered gait torque by twelve percent. Gait torque altered spinal alignment. Spinal alignment. Ninth pulse. Spine. Lumbar three through five.

It hit like a hammer down a channel of frozen oil. The vertebrae didn't crystallize. They ignited. Not fire. Vitalis density so extreme it overwhelmed his perception and parsed as heat. The ice in his ribs, his femurs, his scapula, it all linked through the spinal column, a circuit completing itself. The array's channels blazed white. The Core detonated silently, spent.

Roan didn't feel himself hit the stone.

- break -

Consciousness returned as pressure.

Not pain. Pain would have implied nerves still protesting. This was pressure, a sustained, depthless compression, as if the atmosphere of the room had thickened to the consistency of wet cement. Roan opened his eyes. The Sanctum's ceiling was where he'd left it. That was the first good news.

He tried to sit up.

His elbows locked. Not from injury. From weight. His bones had become anchors and his muscles were still calculating how to lift them. He rolled instead, using momentum, and got his knees under him. The stone floor groaned. A hairline fracture appeared beneath his left knee. He stared at it.

Two days ago, he'd punched that same floor. It had held.

Roan stood. The motion took four seconds. Every tendon in his legs had to retension to account for the new density. He raised his right hand, turned it, made a fist. The air in the chamber didn't warp, he wasn't emitting raw Vitalis, but the movement displaced it with a heaviness he could feel against his fingertips. His own mass had become a weapon.

The Shard stirred.

[Tier breakthrough detected. Hardened architecture initialized. Daily capacity: 5/5.]

He'd expected relief. He got a new dataset.

The Vitalis pool inside him had restructured. Where Tempered had felt like water moving through wax, Hardened felt like mercury through oil. Slower. Denser. More deliberate. His baseline reading, he didn't need the Shard to tell him, had compressed and then expanded past his Tempered peak. He was no longer measuring in the same frame.

Then the second message.

[Anomaly: Vance-pattern marrow resonance detected. Emission traceable. Hide, or don't. I never did. — A.V.]

Roan went still.

The harmonic. Aldric's unique signature, encoded into the bone architecture, now encoded into him. The Black Spire trained Hardened cadets by the dozen. Their marrow emitted standard frequencies, nothing a Conclave scanner would flag. A Vance-pattern frequency was a signature in a dead language. It wouldn't just say *Hardened*. It would say *Aldric's line*.

The Founding Seven had killed Aldric.

Roan looked at the Sanctum's sealed door. Ironholm lay beyond it, noisy with Vitalis traffic. But noise only masked signals if the signal was generic. A Vance-pattern harmonic in a city full of Black Spire students was a single off-key note in a symphony. Audible to anyone listening for it.

At Tempered, the resonance would have been too faint to register beyond direct contact. Hardened had turned it into a beacon.

He had to mask it.

Roan closed his eyes and reached inward, pressing his Vitalis flow into a configuration he'd never attempted. Suppression. Aldric had written nothing about it. Aldric, per the Echo, had never bothered to hide. But Roan wasn't Aldric. He folded his new Hardened density inward, layer by layer, forcing the marrow resonance into a damped oscillation. It was like trying to breathe while submerged. The pressure mounted behind his eyes.

The harmonic dropped. Not gone. Dimmer.

He held the configuration for twenty minutes. When he released it, the resonance returned, but fainter. A whisper instead of a shout. It would decay naturally in. He calculated.

Four hours. Maybe five, if he stayed away from other Hardened who might create interference patterns that amplified the anomaly.

He had nine hours until tournament registration closed. The balance was tight.

Roan moved to the Sanctum's exit, stepping over the spent Core and the hairline crack in the floor. His pack went over his shoulders. The strap, rated for fifty kilos, creaked. He paused, adjusted the distribution, and thumbed the release on the hidden door.

- break -

The undercroft beneath the Spire's east wing wasn't on any student map. It housed redundant steam pipes and the skeletons of Vitalis conduits from before the Founding Seven rebuilt the city grid. Dust on the floor. Uniform. Undisturbed for months.

Except for the footprints.

Roan stopped three meters from the Sanctum's hidden threshold. The prints were fresh. Small. Aldermere-issue tactical soles, the kind with the hexagonal grip pattern the western academies favored. They led toward the corridor's single working light, then stopped.

They hadn't come from the Sanctum. They'd come from the stairwell, walked to this exact junction, and waited.

Roan's jaw tightened.

He followed the prints anyway. There was no other exit. His new weight made silence impossible, each step compressed the concrete microscopically, a gritting sound too low for most ears but present. He would need to relearn his gait. Eight percent mass increase. He'd have to redistribute impact across the metatarsals differently, or every step he took would leave forensic evidence of his density.

The figure was leaning against a corroded support beam beneath the flickering light.

Selene Ardent.

Her uniform was Aldermere gray, trimmed in white. The Twin Embers rode her hips, dormant, but their hilts were loose in their sheaths. Ready. Her hair was pulled back with a metal clasp that caught the light. She didn't look at him immediately. She looked at the floor behind him. Then at his hands. Then at his shadow.

She could see it. The density. The way the light bent wrong around his shoulders.

Interesting.

"Vance," she said.

Roan said nothing. He calculated distances. Seven meters to the stairwell. Four meters to her. The pipes were cast iron, too thick to punch through without making noise. The light above them was old fluorescent, cycling at sixty hertz. If he killed it, he'd have a two-second window of darkness. In that window, he could close the gap. Her Embers were fast, but she wasn't expecting him to move at Hardened mass with Tempered acceleration profiles.

Kill her?

No. Wrong math. Aldermere exchange student. Tournament entrant. Daughter of House Ardent, gray faction, not openly hostile. Her death would trigger an inquiry. An inquiry would route through Sentinel Bureau channels. Channels that would scan for anomalies.

Her survival, however, posed its own problems.

"You're bleeding," Selene said.

Roan glanced down. His left boot had a dark spot on the outer leather. Nosebleed residue. He'd wiped his face before leaving the Sanctum. Missed the boot. A miscalculation. He'd been too focused on the harmonic, the gait, the density.

Stupid.

He looked back at her. Her right hand rested on her hip, not on her blade. A posture of observation, not aggression. But her pupils were dilated. She was reading his Vitalis pressure. And because he'd just spent twenty minutes suppressing his harmonic instead of his aura, she was reading it *low*. Tempered-low. Which was impossible for someone who'd just walked out of a sealed undercroft leaving cracks in the floor.

She knew he was masking.

"Not on your file," Selene said. "The boot. You're supposed to be tidy."

Roan considered his response options. Denial would insult her intelligence. Threat would invite escalation. Silence would confirm everything.

He chose silence anyway.

Selene pushed off the beam. She took two steps toward him. Not close enough to touch. Close enough that his newly dense presence would press against her own aura. She felt it. He saw it in the slight flare of her nostrils, the minute adjustment of her stance as her body recalibrated to the proximity of a Hardened skeleton.

She didn't flinch. But she didn't step closer, either.

"This corridor," she said, "registers as disused on every Black Spire maintenance log since the ninety-eight refit. No Vitalis lines. No cameras. No reason to be here." She tilted her head. "So imagine my surprise when my sensor lit up with a Veyrn-frequency harmonic. Three minutes ago. Right here."

Veyrn-frequency.

Not Hardened. Not generic. Veyrn.

She hadn't come to the Black Spire for the tournament early. She'd come looking for Veyrn echoes. And she'd brought a sensor calibrated to find them.

Roan's mind raced. House Ardent was gray, neither allied with House Greel nor openly friendly to the Vance name. But gray didn't mean ignorant. If Selene had a Veyrn-tuned sensor, she had information. If she had information, she had a source. And if she had a source, someone had talked about Aldric's work.

Selene reached into her pocket. Not for a weapon. For a device. Small. Handheld. She held it up between two fingers, screen facing him. The display showed a waveform, a marrow resonance graph. The peak was labeled in Aldermere script, but the shape was unmistakable. It matched the anomaly the Shard had warned him about.

"This pinged," she said. "Once. Then it went quiet. Like someone stuffed a pillow over it." She pocketed the device. "You're not a ghost, Vance. Ghosts don't learn."

She walked past him. Close. Her shoulder passed within a hand's breadth of his chest. He felt the heat of her aura, disciplined and sharp, sliding against his suppressed density like a blade on whetstone. She didn't look back.

"Fix your harmonic before the tournament," she said, her voice carrying down the pipework corridor. "Or don't. But if the Conclave arrays in the arena pick up a Veyrn signature during my bout schedule, I'll be forced to explain why I was standing near you. I dislike explaining."

Her footsteps receded. Hexagonal soles. Precise. Unhurried.

Roan stood alone in the undercroft. He looked at his boot. The blood. Then at the corridor where she'd disappeared.

The arithmetic had said his greatest risk was the breakthrough itself.

Too many variables were still missing.

Selene Ardent hadn't detected a hidden Hardened student. She'd come equipped to hunt a frequency that should have died with Aldric. Which meant someone had told House Ardent to keep watching. Someone who knew the Veyrn Sanctum existed. Someone who expected a second Vance to eventually show up in the noise.

Roan sorted the possibilities. Drogan wouldn't sell him—too much history, too much at stake. Old Pell didn't have the reach to feed intelligence to a gray house. That left a leak inside the Spire faculty, or a record from the Sentinel Bureau's sealed archives that someone had copied before the purge. Or Aldric himself, dead seven years, had left enough of a trail that Ardent had been following it independently.

None of the options were clean. All of them meant the field was wider than he'd mapped.

He reached down and scraped the blood from his boot with a thumbnail. He straightened. The fluorescent light above him flickered and died, then reignited.

He had four hours to mask a harmonic he hadn't known he would emit. And now he had a witness who knew exactly what to listen for.

The tournament registration could wait. This couldn't.

He took a step toward the stairwell. The concrete beneath his heel split. He looked at the fresh crack, catalogued the force required to produce it, and adjusted his stride.

Relearning would take time.

Time he wasn't sure he had.
