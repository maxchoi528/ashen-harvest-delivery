Chapter 52: Cyrus Ashford

The classroom smelled of old chalk and assumptions that had calcified into institutional furniture.

Roan Vance sat three rows from the back, a position calculated for maximum visibility control with minimum attention draw. From here he could see every face, gauge every Vitalis reading flickering across the instructor's monitoring panel, and, most importantly, avoid the front row where instructors called first. The seat had taken three days to claim, requiring careful observation of the previous occupant's schedule, a strategically timed conversation about sparring opportunities, and one surreptitious relocation of a water bottle that made the original claimant choose a different chair permanently.

The ledger of seating. Small optimizations accumulated.

He reviewed the room's state as he did every morning. Seventeen students from the six major Houses, their affiliation marked by pins, rings, or the subtle confidence of people who had never worried about tuition. Twelve scholarship admits, identifiable by their careful uniforms and the way they sat at the edges of conversations rather than the center. Eight unknowns, transfer students, foreign entrants, or private individuals who kept their backgrounds obscured. And Roan, who fell into no category comfortably by design.

Instructor Helene Voss stood at the lectern, her silver hair pinned in a knot that looked severe because it was. She was Tempered, barely, according to the public registry, and had spent thirty years teaching Ethical Frameworks of Combat to students who would outrank her before graduation. That kind of career produced either bitterness or brilliance. Voss had chosen efficiency. She didn't care if students listened, only that they performed.

“Today’s debate topic,” she announced, tapping the display board with her stylus. The words materialized in crisp blue light against the dark composite surface. “ ‘Is mercy a strength or a weakness?’ ”

A murmur rippled through the room, the sound of thirty-seven people simultaneously deciding whether this topic mattered to their future careers. Roan didn’t join the murmur. He was calculating.

Mercy. The word had specific weight in combat philosophy. At the Initiate level, the Academy taught that mercy demonstrated control, that a warrior who could kill but chose not to exhibited mastery over their own Vitalis. At the Tempered level, instructors started asking harder questions: what if mercy leaves an enemy capable of retaliation? What if the resources spent on mercy exceed the resources saved? By the Hardened tier, the word practically vanished from the curriculum, replaced by terms like “tactical restraint” and “post-combat resource allocation.”

Roan had studied the progression. He knew where mercy stopped being a virtue and started being a liability.

“The rules are standard.” Voss’s voice carried the flat precision of someone who had delivered this lecture four hundred times. “One speaker presents the affirmative. One presents the negative. Rebuttal follows. The class votes. No Vitalis demonstrations, this is rhetoric, not combat.”

Roan noted the parameters. Rhetoric meant reading the room. Combat meant reading the opponent. Both were calculations, but the variables differed. In combat, you factored Vitalis output, reaction speed and terrain, then the psychological profile of your enemy. In rhetoric, you factored ego, social standing, the desperate human need to be perceived as intelligent, and, most critically, who in the room wanted whom to fail.

Cyrus Ashford’s hand rose before Voss finished speaking.

Of course it did. Roan had watched Cyrus for three weeks now, since the first day of term when the Ashford heir had arrived with a retinue of four fellow House members, a custom-fitted training uniform, and a Vitalis reading of 287 that he’d announced loudly enough for the entire dormitory wing to hear. The number was solid. Roan’s own display read 312, but he’d calibrated his public output to hover around 180, a figure that placed him safely in the “above average but boring” range. Boring meant invisible. Invisible meant safe.

“Instructor Voss.” Cyrus stood without waiting for acknowledgment, a liberty he took because his family had endowed the eastern lecture hall and the climate control system that kept it habitable. “I will take the affirmative. Mercy is the mark of civilization. The strong who show mercy demonstrate control over their power, they choose restraint where others would choose destruction.”

He smiled. It was a smile that had been taught by tutors, practiced in mirrors, and deployed in social situations like a calibrated weapon. Roan recognized the type. He’d used similar expressions himself, though his were generally designed to make people underestimate him rather than admire him.

“Consider the Founding Seven,” Cyrus continued, pacing to the center of the room with the confidence of someone who had never been told to sit down. “When House Greel unified the Northern Reaches, he didn’t exterminate the border clans. He showed mercy. He incorporated them. That mercy bought him loyalty that no amount of Vitalis could purchase. The records from the Conclave Archives—”

“Primary sources only in this classroom, Mr. Ashford.” Voss didn’t look up from her tablet. “The Conclave Archives are secondary compilations written three centuries after the events they describe.”

Cyrus’s smile flickered. Not enough for most observers to notice, but Roan tracked the micro-expression: a barely perceptible tightening of the jaw, the rapid suppression of a blink, a subtle stiffening of the shoulders—textbook compensations for a direct hit to the ego. Frustration, poorly suppressed. Interesting. Cyrus had prepared a speech built around archival authority, and Voss had just removed his foundation with the casual precision of someone who had made this correction hundreds of times before.

“Of course.” Cyrus recovered smoothly, turning toward the class rather than the instructor. “Then consider the Codex of Aldermere, Section Seven, Paragraph Twelve: ‘The warrior who strikes without necessity isn’t powerful. He’s merely uncontrolled. True strength lies in the capacity to harm and the wisdom to withhold.’ ”

Roan watched the room respond. Several students nodded. House Aldermere affiliates, predictable allegiance. Others took notes with the desperate energy of people who hadn’t done the reading and were hoping to transcribe their way to understanding. Theon Mercer, Roan’s roommate, sat two seats away, his expression suggesting he was calculating something entirely unrelated to philosophy. The molecular structure of the sandwich he’d abandoned at breakfast. Theon had a gift for appearing present while being completely absent.

“The Aldermere monks wrote during the Hollow Purge,” Cyrus continued, warming to his revised material. “They watched Tempered warriors massacre civilian populations because those civilians might’ve been Hollowkin sympathizers. The monks argued, correctly, that mercy was the only thing separating civilization from the Rifts. Without mercy, we become what we fight. Without restraint, strength becomes indistinguishable from brutality.”

He turned, sweeping his gaze across the room with the theatrical timing of someone who had rehearsed this gesture. “My own great-grandfather, Castor Ashford, demonstrated this principle during the Ironholm Rebellion. He had captured the rebel leader, a man who had killed seventeen Sentinels and burned two supply depots. My great-grandfather had every right to execute him. Instead, he offered terms. The rebel became a scout, then a trainer, then a commander who saved more lives than he had taken. Mercy didn’t weaken House Ashford. It gave us an asset that no amount of Vitalis could have purchased.”

Roan filed the story. It was well-chosen, personal, verifiable, emotionally resonant. An anecdote that made abstract philosophy concrete. Cyrus had either excellent speechwriters or a genuinely capable mind beneath the polished presentation.

“Mercy is strength,” Cyrus concluded, “because it proves the strong don’t need to prove their strength. Only the insecure kill unnecessarily. Only the frightened eliminate every threat. True power is comfortable with restraint. True power chooses mercy not because it must, but because it can afford the moral superiority.”

He sat down to scattered applause, mostly from the front rows. Eleven minutes. Roan had timed it.

“Thank you, Mr. Ashford.” Voss made a note on her tablet. “The negative?”

Silence.

The class turned. Roan felt the collective gaze like a pressure change, thirty-seven pairs of eyes suddenly focused on the back rows where the unimportant students sat. He’d planned for this possibility, the instructor calling on someone random to balance the debate, but he’d calculated the probability at roughly twelve percent based on Voss’s established patterns. She usually let volunteers step forward. She usually didn’t force participation.

Twelve percent wasn’t zero. He’d failed to account for something. Perhaps Voss had noticed him calculating seating arrangements during the first week. Perhaps she enjoyed disrupting patterns. Perhaps she’d observed that no one else would step forward to oppose Cyrus, and she found that dynamic academically dishonest. Either way, the variables had shifted, and Roan needed to recalculate his exposure.

He stood.

“Vance,” Voss said, not a question. “You have a position on mercy?”

He walked to the front of the room at a pace that projected confidence without aggression. The calculation was precise: too fast looked eager, too slow looked uncertain. Three seconds to reach the center. Two seconds to turn. One second of silence before speaking. The timing mattered. Audiences remembered openings more than conclusions.

“Mercy is a tax the strong pay to feel human.” Roan’s voice carried to the back row without effort. “I’d rather not.”

The silence that followed was different from the attentive quiet of Cyrus’s speech. This was the silence of a dropped object in a room full of people who had forgotten gravity existed.

Cyrus’s smile vanished completely. “That’s not an argument. That’s a sound bite.”

“Sound bites endure because they’re true.” Roan didn’t look at Cyrus. He looked at the class, reading faces. Shock. Confusion. One student in the third row, Ines Carrow, House Carrow, neutral territory, showing something that might’ve been evaluation rather than reaction. “You spoke for eleven minutes about civilization and control. You never answered the actual question: why does the strong need mercy at all?”

“Mercy builds alliances,” Cyrus said, recovering his rhetorical footing. “It creates social capital that—”

“Social capital is a ledger. Mercy is an expense.” Roan interrupted without raising his voice, which made the interruption cut deeper. “You pay it when the cost of killing exceeds the cost of letting someone live. That’s not morality. That’s accounting dressed in nicer clothing.”

Voss’s stylus had stopped moving. She was watching now, watching, the way an engineer watched a bridge under unexpected stress.

“The Codex of Aldermere,” Roan continued, “which you quoted so authoritatively, was written by monks who had never killed anyone. Their perspective on mercy was theoretical. The Founding Seven’s mercy was strategic—Greel incorporated the border clans because extermination would have cost him thirty percent of his standing forces and destabilized the region for a generation. He didn’t choose mercy. He chose the option with better long term yield.”

“You’re reducing human connection to economic calculation.” Cyrus’s voice had sharpened, the polished edges showing stress fractures.

“I’m describing reality. You’re describing aspiration.” Roan turned to face him directly. “You want to know if mercy is strength or weakness? Watch what happens when someone shows mercy to an enemy who didn’t ask for it. The enemy doesn’t become grateful. He becomes emboldened. He interprets restraint as inability. Then you have to kill him anyway, except now you’ve wasted resources on the pretense of mercy, and you’ve taught every other observer that your threats are negotiable.”

A student in the front row, House Greel affiliate based on the emerald pin, was staring with an expression Roan couldn’t fully parse. Something between horror and recognition.

“That’s cynical,” Cyrus said.

“That’s efficient.” Roan shrugged, a gesture he’d practiced to look careless. “The strong don’t need mercy. The weak need it from the strong. Framing it as strength is a comfort mechanism for people who can’t afford to be seen as ruthless.”

“And you’d rather be seen as ruthless?”

“I’d rather be correctly assessed.” Roan turned back to the class. “Mercy isn’t weakness. It’s not strength either. It’s a luxury purchase made by people who have already won. The question isn’t whether to show mercy. The question is whether you can afford the tax. Most can’t. They just pretend they can.”

He walked back to his seat.

The silence held for four seconds. Roan counted, before Voss finally moved, tapping her tablet with a deliberate click.

“Vote,” she said.

The results appeared on the display board: affirmative seventeen, negative twenty. Cyrus had lost his own debate by three votes.

“Class dismissed.” Voss stood, gathering her materials with the efficient movements of someone who had already begun processing the next lesson. “Vance. Ashford. Remain.”

The room emptied with the controlled chaos of students eager to escape academic tension. Roan stayed seated, calculating the variables of this detention. Voss wasn’t an instructor who wasted time on punishment. If she wanted to speak with them, there was a specific reason.

Cyrus remained standing, his composure reassembling itself like armor after a skirmish. “Instructor, I should note that Vance’s argument was logically fallacious. He shifted from defining mercy to attacking the motives of those who practice it. That’s an ad hominem—”

“I know what it is, Mr. Ashford.” Voss didn’t look up from her tablet. “I also know that he convinced twenty of your classmates in under two minutes, while you needed eleven to hold seventeen. Efficiency matters in combat. It matters in rhetoric. It matters in life.”

She looked up then, her gray eyes moving from Cyrus to Roan with the sharp assessment of someone who had spent decades distinguishing between genuine capability and polished presentation.

“Mr. Ashford, you have resources. Connections. Training. Your Vitalis reading is solid, your technique is clean, and your family has ensured you lack for nothing.” She paused. “Mr. Vance has none of those advantages. Yet he just demonstrated something you haven’t learned: how to read a room faster than your opponent can read his notes.”

Cyrus’s jaw tightened. Not a subtle millimeter this time—a full centimeter of suppressed fury.

“This wasn’t a graded assessment,” Voss continued. “But if it were, Vance would have received full marks for tactical adaptation. You, Mr. Ashford, would have received full marks for preparation, and a penalty for failing to adjust when your prepared material became irrelevant.”

She turned to Roan. “Your argument was amoral, structurally incomplete, and deliberately provocative. It was also the most honest assessment of mercy I’ve heard in fifteen years of teaching this course. Don’t make a habit of honesty. It draws attention.”

Roan nodded, filing the warning with the same precision he filed everything else. Attention was dangerous. But Voss’s reaction suggested something else: she’d recognized the ledger behind his delivery, more than the content. That made her more perceptive than most. Perceptive instructors required adjusted behavioral parameters.

“You’re both dismissed.”

Cyrus left first, his footsteps sharp against the floor. Roan waited four seconds, then followed at a pace that suggested he had nowhere urgent to be.

In the corridor, Cyrus was waiting.

“That was clever.” The polished smile was gone, replaced by something harder and more genuine. Roan found it more interesting than the mask. “The ad hominem trick. Redirecting from the topic to the speaker’s credibility.”

“It wasn’t a trick.” Roan kept walking. “It was observation. You believe your own argument. That makes you predictable.”

“And you don’t believe yours?”

Roan stopped. They were alone in the corridor, midway between the lecture hall and the dormitory wing. The afternoon light through the windows cast long shadows that made the space feel like a stage set for something inevitable.

“I believe the balance.” Roan met Cyrus’s eyes. “Mercy is expensive. The question is whether you can pay. You can. That makes your position affordable. Don’t mistake affordability for virtue.”

“And you can’t afford it?”

“I don’t buy things I can’t explain on a ledger.” Roan resumed walking. “You should return to your friends, Ashford. They’re analyzing your defeat already.”

“It wasn’t a defeat. It was a classroom debate.”

“Yes.” Roan didn’t look back. “Imagine how you’ll feel when it matters.”

He turned the corner and was gone.

The dormitory was quiet when Roan reached his room. Theon sat at his desk, surrounded by scattered papers that seemed to organize themselves according to principles Roan couldn’t identify.

“Heard you destroyed Ashford in Voss’s class.” Theon didn’t look up from his notes. “Campus net is already circulating a transcript. Someone recorded the whole thing.”

Roan paused, his hand on the doorframe. “That wasn’t the plan.”

“Plans change.” Theon finally looked up, his pale eyes showing something that might’ve been amusement. “You know what the post title is? ‘Who is Roan Vance?’ Twenty-three comments in the last ten minutes. Someone found your entrance exam scores. Someone else claims you’re from Greycross. House Vance, apparently.”

Roan’s fingers tightened on the frame. Not enough to show stress, just enough to register the variable change. Greycross. House Vance. Two data points that shouldn’t connect, connecting.

“I didn’t authorize that information.”

“Information doesn’t need authorization. It just needs curiosity.” Theon returned to his notes. “You’re playing low, Vance. But low players who win public arguments don’t stay low for long. The arithmetic of attention. Small losses accumulate.”

Roan sat on his bunk, reaching for the Codex Shard’s presence against his spine. The hum was there, steady and indifferent.

[Observation: Social visibility increased 340%. Recommend: recalibrate public profile. — A.V.]

His father’s ghost, offering commentary from beyond death. Roan kept the advice without acknowledging it.

He thought of Cyrus. The stillness in his eyes before the confrontation—that hadn’t been mere defeat. It was recalibration. Talent that could learn from humiliation was more dangerous than talent that never faced it. Roan filed the assessment.

The math of attention. Theon was right. Small losses accumulated, and so did small victories. Today he’d won a debate, lost anonymity, and gained an enemy with resources that dwarfed his own.

The ledger wasn’t balanced. But it never was.

He lay back on his bunk and stared at the ceiling, calculating the next three moves before Cyrus Ashford could organize his response. The answer took eleven minutes. The result was imperfect but acceptable.

Mercy might be a tax. But attention was a debt that compounded faster than any interest rate Roan had ever encountered.

He sat up and reached for the campus net terminal. A single change to the training hall reservation system—barely noticeable, but it would shift one encounter in his favor. A quiet ember.

He lay back down, closed his eyes, and waited for the next variable to shift. This time, he had chosen the ground.
