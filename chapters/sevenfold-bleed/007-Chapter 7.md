Chapter 7: If You're Not Afraid

Roan sat at the desk in his room, the Garrick contract spread flat under the lamp. Three pages of fine print, two signatures, one seal. His name at the bottom, dated three days ago. Two hundred credits in cash sat in a locked drawer—his mother's savings, leant without question when he told her it was for academy supplies. She hadn't asked which supplies. She never did.

He had a contract with a man who sold failed pharmaceuticals at bulk discount, and two hundred credits to turn into something larger.

The arithmetic resolved cleanly.

Garrick operated on volume. He bought expired and rejected stock from the Spire's medical surplus, stored it in a damp basement, and moved it through back-channel buyers who didn't ask about origin. The contract gave Roan access to that inventory at cost-plus-five-percent, with no minimum purchase and no return policy. Garrick had assumed he was buying for personal use—a desperate student looking for cheap enhancers.

Roan was not buying for personal use.

He pulled out a second sheet of paper, blank, and began writing.

First, the available products. He had walked through Garrick's inventory list yesterday: forty-three line items, mostly failed healing salves, mislabeled stimulants, and batches of low-grade Vitalis tonics that had destabilized during storage. None of it was sellable through legitimate channels. That was why Garrick sold cheap.

But cheap didn't mean useless. It meant the intended effect had degraded or the side-effect profile had widened. Normal buyers avoided those because the risk outweighed the benefit. Normal buyers didn't have Inversion.

Roan circled three items.

The first was a batch of Marrow-Depletion Serums—originally designed to calm overactive Vitalis channels in patients with cultivation sickness. The serum worked by introducing a controlled foreign energy that the body's channels would reject, forcing the system to exhaust itself flushing the invader out. The side effect was temporary channel fragility and pain. Garrick's batch had expired eighteen months ago and the rejection effect was now unpredictable—some patients would purge it fast, others would suffer violent backlash.

Roan wrote: *Target buyer: Rift scavengers who need short-term Vitalis suppression to avoid detection by Hollowkin. Unpredictability is a feature if you time the dose right. Price markup: 300%.*

The second item was a lot of Ghostroot Extract, originally used to numb pain before battlefield surgery. The active compound degraded into a mild hallucinogen after six months. Garrick had marked it as loss. Roan recalculated. A hallucinogen, properly diluted, could be sold as a stress-relief tincture to students who couldn't afford the academy-sanctioned version. The numbing property remained strong enough to make the product feel effective.

*Target buyer: Spire students under exam pressure. Low detection risk. Markup: 200%.*

The third was the most interesting. A crate of Stabilized Hollowshale Dust, rejected because the stabilizing agent had crystallized into solid chunks. The dust itself was still pure; it just required crushing and sieving. Hollowshale was used in defensive enchantments and temporary barrier arrays. Any craftsman with a mortar could restore it to usable form.

*Target buyer: Independent enchanters and small workshops. Markup: 500% after processing.*

He set the pen down.

Three products, two hundred credits seed capital, a contract that let him buy at cost. The plan was simple: purchase the entire lot of each item, reprocess or repackage them in his basement workshop, and sell them through channels that didn't intersect with Garrick's. The profit from the first sale would fund the second round. The second would fund the third. By the end of the month, he would have enough to buy the Bloodscourge without touching his mother's savings again.

He looked at the window. Greycross's night lights flickered through the grime. Somewhere out there, Old Pell would be nursing a glass at The Last Cycle. The old soldier had information Roan needed—about Bloodscourge, about the white-bearded man, about the road his father had walked. But that was tomorrow's question.

First he needed the capital.

He folded the contract, placed it in the drawer next to the cash, and locked both. Then he lay back on the bed and stared at the ceiling.

Three variables, one intersection. The arithmetic had to hold.

He slept.

Morning came grey and damp. Roan ate a quick breakfast—bread and tea, his mother already gone to her shift at the textile mill—and headed out. Garrick's shop opened at eight. He arrived at seven-fifty, stood in the drizzle until the bolt slid back.

Garrick was a thin man with a perpetual squint, as if the world was always slightly too bright. He blinked when Roan handed him the list.

"All of it?"

"All three lots."

"That's—" Garrick counted in his head. "A hundred and seventy credits, even taking the bulk write-off price. You got that kind of money?"

Roan set the stack on the counter. One hundred and seventy, counted twice, wrapped in paper. Garrick's fingers twitched toward it, stopped, then picked it up.

"You're buying expired shit, kid. I ain't gonna ask what you're planning, but I'm gonna warn you: half that Ghostroot is probably turned into sugar water by now."

"I'll take the risk."

"Your funeral." Garrick tucked the money into his apron and turned to the back room. "Give me ten minutes to pull the crates. You got a cart?"

"No."

"Then you'll be making trips." Garrick disappeared into the cluttered aisles.

Roan waited. Ten minutes turned into twenty, but he didn't complain. When Garrick emerged, the crates were stacked on a rusted hand truck—three boxes, sealed, heavy. The total weight was manageable if he split them into two loads.

He made the first trip alone, hauling one crate through Greycross's morning streets. The drizzle stopped by the time he reached the basement entrance of his building. He descended the narrow stairs, unlocked the reinforced door his father had installed years ago, and slid the crate inside.

The basement workshop was small. A workbench, a sink, a lockbox, shelves lined with glass jars and brass scales. Aldric had used this space for something Roan didn't fully understand yet—the notes were still being deciphered—but the tools were precise and well-maintained. They would serve a different purpose now.

He opened the crate. Ghostroot extract, forty vials packed in straw. He lifted one, held it to the light. The liquid was amber, slightly hazy. He unstoppered it, sniffed. Sharp, herbal, with an undertone of something sweet.

The stale tincture could still numb pain. The degraded hallucinogenic property was mild enough that ordinary users wouldn't notice—they'd feel relaxed, maybe a little lightheaded. Dilute it to three-quarters strength and it would sell as "stress-relief tincture" without drawing attention.

He set to work.

By noon, he had processed twelve vials into twenty-four diluted doses, each in a clean glass bottle with a handwritten label that read *Calmflow Tincture—Take one teaspoon under tongue as needed. Not for continuous use.* No false claims, no hidden ingredients, just a description of the effect. If anyone tested it, the active compound would match the label. The degraded hallucinogen was an unintended side effect, not an added drug—legally, that was a grey area he could exploit.

By evening, all forty vials were bottled and labelled. He had seventy-two units of Calmflow Tincture ready to sell at five credits apiece. That was three hundred and sixty credits revenue if he moved the full stock.

One hundred and seventy invested. Three hundred and sixty projected return. A surplus of one hundred and ninety credits. Enough to buy the next batch and start the cycle again.

He sat on the workbench, wiped the sweat from his forehead, and let the numbers settle.

The seventh chapter of the plan was this: spend nothing, hoard every credit, and let the arithmetic compound until he could afford not just the Bloodscourge, but the security to survive it. The cheap version, the black-market variant that killed more fools than it gifted—he would buy it only when he had enough left over for antidotes, emergency care, and a safe place to endure the transformation.

That was the practical shape of the contract. Not a grand strategy. A ledger.

Tomorrow he would visit Old Pell.

Tonight he counted the bottles.
