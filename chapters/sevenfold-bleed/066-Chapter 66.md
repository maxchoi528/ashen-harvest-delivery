Chapter 66: Precise Violence

The first cut tracked across Roan's shoulder blade. Clean. Measurable. He didn't flinch.

He calculated the angle in the same instant the blade carved through his uniform. Twelve degrees off-center. Low-angle upward strike. Attack designed to follow a feint, except the feint hadn't been a feint. It had been the attack itself, and the second blade was already moving.

Two blades. Two attackers. A corridor with a single exit behind him.

The math resolved before he finished breathing out.

He let the first wound settle. Let the pain register, sharp, clean, the kind that reminded you your body was still attached to your nerves. The second blade swept toward his ribs, and he was already pivoting, already moving, Sidewound clearing the sheath in a motion that looked like surrender.

It wasn't surrender. It was positioning.

The corridor narrowed ahead. Twenty feet of open space before the next junction. Behind him, the exit. In front of him, the two Ashford retainers who had apparently decided that the Black Spire's internal corridors were an appropriate place for a private execution.

Roan evaluated. Retainer one: hard right, blade still extended from the first strike, recovering. Retainer two: left side, second blade mid-swing, committed. Both Tempered. Both competent. Neither expecting him to stand still when every instinct told him to run.

He didn't run.

He stepped forward.

The move was counterintuitive, closing the distance instead of creating space. Retainer one adjusted, blade angling for a thrust. Retainer two completed the swing that would have opened Roan's flank.

Neither adjustment accounted for what happened next.

Roan hit the gap between them at a forty-five-degree angle, Sidewound moving in a horizontal cut that wasn't powerful enough to kill but was precise enough to be unavoidable. The flat of the blade caught the first retainer's wrist—not the edge, the flat—sending a numbing shock through the joint. The retainer's grip failed, his blade scraping across the stone floor.

The thrust went wide.

Retainer two's follow-through struck empty air where Roan's ribs had been a quarter-second before.

The tally worked. Retainer one was now holding a blade with a hand that couldn't control it. Retainer two was committed to a motion that left her torso exposed.

Roan pressed the advantage. Two strikes. Not to kill, to disable.

First strike: the pommel of Sidewound driving into the nerve bundle beneath the first retainer's clavicle. A short, precise jab. The retainer's arm went dead, his blade clattering to the floor as his legs buckled. He had four seconds before the feeling returned.

Second strike: a kick to the second retainer's forward knee. Not a break, a hyperextension. She dropped, ankle buckling, and Roan stepped past her before she hit the ground.

He was six feet from the corridor's end when the sound behind him resolved into something more threatening than groaning men.

"The Vance boy knows how to move."

The voice came from the junction ahead. Male. Older. The tone was the calm that came from absolute confidence.

Roan didn't slow down. He didn't accelerate. He maintained his pace, letting his eyes scan the junction as he approached.

The man waiting there was built like a siege weapon. Shoulders broad enough to block the corridor. Hands that looked like they'd been designed for breaking things larger than blades. He wore the simple grey of a senior retainer, which meant either House Ashford's elite or someone paid enough to not care about the difference.

The badge on his collar had a single silver stripe. Second-tier senior. Forged rank.

Forged. A realm far above his own.

The man smiled. "They said you were clever. Didn't mention you were stupid." He stepped into the corridor, and the space seemed to compress around him. "Running toward me isn't clever, boy. It's just moving the inevitable closer."

Roan stopped at eight feet. Close enough to talk. Far enough to react.

"You stopped your retainers from using lethal force," he said. His voice was level. Observational. "That means someone wants me alive. To question. Which means you can't just crush my skull." He tilted his head slightly. "What exactly did Ashford promise you for a job that requires this much restraint?"

The man's smile thinned. A flicker of recalculation passed through his eyes before locking back into place.

"Smart enough to be annoying." He took a step forward. "You're right. I can't kill you. But I can break things that grow back. Fingers. Kneecaps. Damage that says *next time* without having to use words." Another step. "You understand. You do it yourself."

Roan considered the man's stance. Weight forward, right side dominant, hands ready but not raised. He wasn't planning to grapple, he was planning to corner and dismantle. The approach was methodical.

Against someone with a Forged's speed and strength, Roan's options were limited.

Except he wasn't here to fight. He was here to get somewhere.

The corridor behind the senior retainer split into two paths. Left led to the training halls. Right led to the administrative wing, the fastest route to the registrar's office where Brendt's appeal was being processed.

The senior retainer was standing in the path of least resistance. Deliberately. He knew exactly what Roan wanted.

"Here's how this works," the man said. "You come quietly. We have a conversation. If you're useful, you walk away with nothing worse than a headache and a reminder that Black Spire corridors are monitored." His expression hardened. "If you're not useful, you become a cautionary tale. Either way, you're not making that appointment."

The ledger here was different.

Against two Tempered retainers, Roan's combat calculation had been sound. Speed differential: manageable. Sidewound advantages: favorable. Terrain: exploitable.

Against a Forged with a three-tier advantage, those calculations collapsed. Even with Inversion, he couldn't generate enough Sidewound to close the gap. The man's Vitalis output would be five times his own at minimum. Maybe seven.

He couldn't win. He also couldn't walk away, not if Brendt's appeal was being processed right now.

What he could do was change the parameters.

"You think I came alone," Roan said.

The senior retainer's eyes narrowed. Not fear, calculation. He scanned the corridor behind Roan, checked the side passages, evaluated the shadows.

The pause lasted a visible wind-up.

It was enough.

Roan moved left, not toward the training halls, but toward the wall. The corridor was old, built in an era when ventilation was handled through grates rather than integrated systems. The grate to his left was rusted and loose, just large enough for a person to fit through if they didn't care about bleeding.

He didn't care about bleeding.

His shoulder hit the grate. The rust gave way with a screech of corroded metal. He rolled through the opening, metal scoring a line across his back that registered as white-hot feedback.

He hit the floor of an adjacent corridor, a maintenance passage, narrow and dark, and he was already moving before the senior retainer's curse finished echoing behind him.

The Forged would go through the grate. That would take time. The maintenance corridor wasn't designed for speed, it was designed for access to the old heating systems. Pipes, narrow turns, low ceilings.

Roan ran.

The corridor branched three times in the next thirty seconds. He chose paths based on the simplest heuristic: which direction felt most like *down*. The administrative wing was in the lower levels. Gravity was his friend.

Behind him, the sound of a massive frame forcing its way through a too-small opening told him the senior retainer had committed. That meant this wasn't about capture anymore. The man was too angry to follow orders.

That changed the balance again.

Roan ran faster.

He emerged from the maintenance corridor into a service stairwell, took the steps two at a time, and burst through the door marked *Administrative Wing. Authorization Required*.

The hallway beyond was polished stone and warm lighting. Students with authorization badges moved between offices. A security drone hummed near the far end.

Roan pulled his own badge from his uniform, showed it to the drone, and kept walking. His back was bleeding through his shirt. His shoulder was stiff from the first cut. He looked like he'd been through something unpleasant.

He looked like someone who had somewhere urgent to be.

The registrar's office was at the end of the hall. The door was closed. Through the window, he could see a clerk behind the desk, filing something, and beyond that, Brendt.

Brendt was sitting in a chair, hands clasped, face carefully neutral. Neutral that meant he was trying not to hope. Neutral that meant he was afraid to find out.

The door had a buzzer. Roan buzzed.

The clerk looked up, saw him, frowned at whatever was visible on his uniform. Blood. Damage. Appearance that made bureaucrats nervous.

"This is a restricted area," the clerk said. "You need to—"

"Appeal status," Roan said. "Now. Before it gets filed."

The clerk's frown deepened. "Sir, I can't just—"

He didn't wait. He pushed the door open and stepped inside, letting the blood and the breathing and the absolute refusal to leave communicate what his words weren't bothering to.

Brendt's head snapped up. His eyes went wide, then narrow, then calculating in that order. He read the situation in about two seconds, which was faster than the clerk.

"What happened to you?" Brendt asked.

"Numbers," Roan said. "Ashford's people are in the building. Senior retainer. Forged tier." He turned to the clerk. "My friend's appeal. What is the current status?"

The clerk was standing now, hand moving toward an alarm panel. "I need to call security—"

"Do that," Roan said. "And when security arrives, they'll find out that House Ashford sent a Forged into a monitored zone to intercept a student appeal. Want to explain that to Headmaster Cole? Because I don't think Ashford wants that conversation either."

The clerk's hand stopped.

Roan could see him running the calculations. Ashford had influence, but not absolute influence. Black Spire's administrative autonomy was a point of pride. Any hint of noble houses manipulating internal processes would create problems for everyone involved, including the clerk who signed off on whatever Ashford wanted signed off on.

The clerk looked at his screen. Looked at Brendt. Looked at the blood still seeping through Roan's uniform.

"The appeal was filed forty minutes ago," he said slowly. "Standard processing takes seventy-two hours. But if there are extenuating circumstances, interference, intimidation, documented threats, the review can be expedited." He paused. "This corridor has cameras, doesn't it?"

Roan didn't smile. "Every corridor has cameras."

"Right." The clerk looked at his screen again. "Then I'm going to note here that there's been a reported incident in the residential wing involving unauthorized personnel. And that the student filing this appeal has requested immediate review based on safety concerns." He tapped something. "That should move things along."

Brendt stood up. He crossed to Roan, and for a moment his jaw worked. He looked like he was going to argue, then thank him, then argue again. He didn't.

"You didn't have to—"

"I know," Roan said. "I also know that Ashford just spent political capital they didn't have to spare. And I know that Forged retainer is breaking something right now because he lost his target." He looked at the clerk. "How long until the expedited review?"

"Twenty-four to forty-eight hours," the clerk said. "Longer if Ashford protests. Shorter if—" He gestured vaguely. "If something makes it urgent."

"Make it urgent," Roan said.

The clerk stared at him. Then he turned to his screen and started typing with considerably more energy than before.

Brendt was still standing there. Still doing that complicated thing with his face.

"Twenty-four hours," Roan said. "Maybe less. That's the window. We need to not be found until then."

"We?"

"I'm apparently a variable in their calculations now. Might as well be useful."

He turned and walked toward the door. Behind him, Brendt followed.

The corridor outside was the same polished stone and warm lighting as before. Students moved between offices. Security drones hummed. Everything looked exactly like it had five minutes ago.

Nothing was the same.

Ashford had escalated. That meant the timeline had compressed. Whatever they were afraid of, whatever had made Cyrus push for this kind of overt operation, it was time-sensitive.

They had twenty-four hours. Maybe less.

Roan calculated the variables and found them insufficient. Too many unknowns. Too many actors with motivations he couldn't fully model.

One thing was certain, though. The Ashford people would be looking for him now. Not as a point of leverage, but as a loose end.

Arithmetic had no incentive to flatter him. But the arithmetic was incomplete.

He needed more information. Fast. One name surfaced: Selene Ardent. She monitored the noble channels. A move this clumsy from Ashford was exactly the kind of overreach she collected. Whether she wanted a conversation or not, she was the only variable left that could balance the equation.

Roan walked toward the training halls, Brendt at his side, the weight of incomplete calculations pressing against the back of his skull. Behind him, somewhere in the building's depths, a Forged-tier retainer was looking for blood.

The cameras recorded everything.

That was either a problem or a solution, depending on who got to the footage first.
