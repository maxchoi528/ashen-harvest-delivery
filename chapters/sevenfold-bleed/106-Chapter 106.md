Chapter 106: Inter-Spire Opening

The door to the Veyrn Sanctum groaned shut behind him, and Roan counted to thirty before he allowed himself to exhale.

Thirty-one. Thirty-two.

The corridor stretched ahead, lit by the same sickly green luminescence that seemed to seep from the stone itself. He'd memorized the route during his last visit. Third left, down two flights, through the maintenance hatch that Drogan pretended not to know about. Twelve minutes from the Sanctum to his dormitory if he moved quickly.

Thirteen if he wanted to check whether anyone had followed.

The Codex Shard hummed against his spine, warm, patient, wrong in ways he still couldn't articulate. Months since he'd activated it. Months of watching his Vitalis climb past numbers that should've been impossible for someone of his apparent rank. Months of pretending to be the unremarkable student everyone expected him to be.

The arithmetic resolved cleanly. The execution wasn't.

He paused at the first junction, pressing his back against the cold wall. Footsteps. Two sets, maybe thirty meters back. The cadence was wrong for casual students, the measured tread of people who knew exactly where they were going.

Right.

Roan slipped into the maintenance shaft before they rounded the corner.

The crawlspace smelled of rust and old moisture. He moved through it by memory and touch, his breathing shallow, his mind already running calculations. Months. In months, he'd gone from Initiate to Tempered, actual Tempered, not the paper rank they'd assigned him. His Vitalis had climbed from the mid-hundreds to nearly three thousand. At his current rate, he'd hit Hardened before the semester ended.

The problem wasn't the power. The problem was the visibility.

Someone had noticed. Someone with enough authority to dispatch trackers through the Sanctum's secondary corridors. The footsteps had been too confident, too unhurried—they weren't security looking for trespassers. They were hunters who'd been told exactly where to look.

Roan emerged in the basement level beneath the eastern dormitories, pulling himself up through a floor grate that took him directly into the janitorial supply closet. He checked his reflection in a scratched metal shelf. Dirt on his collar. Hair slightly disheveled. Nothing that would stand out in a crowd of two hundred students.

The question was who. And why they'd waited months to start looking.

The Codex Shard pulsed again, warmer now, almost eager. The sensation still made his skin crawl. Whatever lived in that shard wasn't dormant. It wasn't passive. It was watching through him, learning through him, waiting for something he couldn't see.

*You're paranoid,* he told himself.

The shard offered no response. It never did when he addressed it directly.

He stepped out of the closet and into the dormitory corridor, falling into the same shuffling gait he used when he was tired after training. Months of practice. Months of becoming the person everyone expected him to be.

The problem was that the person everyone expected was a mediocrity with mediocre grades and a mediocre future. And months of Inversion had made that disguise increasingly difficult to maintain.

Drogan found him in the training yard an hour later, running forms with a practice blade that felt heavier than it should.

The old man watched for a full minute before speaking. "You're holding it wrong."

Roan adjusted his grip. "I'm holding it exactly how I've been holding it for months. You never mentioned it before."

"Before, you were coasting. Now you're fighting yourself." Drogan's metal hand caught the blade mid-swing, stopping it with a sound like a bell. "Your form is perfect. Your intent is fractured. Something's changed."

*Everything's changed.*

"Nothing's changed," Roan said. "I'm just tired."

"You're lying." No accusation in the words, just the flat observation of someone who'd spent decades reading people who wanted to stay unread. "And you're doing it less well than you did a month ago. Something's eating at you. Either tell me what it is, or let it eat at you quietly and hope it doesn't affect your performance in the tournament."

The tournament. Right. The Inter-Spire Opening in Ironholm. The ceremony was tomorrow.

"The tournament's handled," he said. "I've run the simulations. I know what my numbers need to look like."

"Your numbers." Drogan released the blade, letting Roan resume his forms. "You mean the figures you want people to see, or the tally you're producing?"

The blade stopped halfway through the arc.

Roan didn't turn around. "What numbers do you think I'm producing?"

"I think you've been hiding something since the day you walked into my office with a Codex Shard you shouldn't have had and a Vitalis reading that didn't match your file." Drogan's voice was calm, almost conversational. "I think whatever you're hiding is getting harder to maintain. And I think someone else has noticed, which means you have approximately two weeks before this situation stops being your problem and becomes mine."

The shard hummed against Roan's spine, warmer, faster. A sensation he was beginning to recognize as warning.

"Someone followed me today," he said. "Through the Sanctum. Two of them, maybe three. They knew the routes."

"Not security?"

"Too confident. They weren't guessing."

Drogan was quiet. When he spoke again, there was something in his voice that Roan hadn't heard before, something careful, something measured. "The House Ashford delegation arrived this morning. Cyrus Ashford is with them, along with two escorts. They're here to observe the tournament."

The name hit like cold water.

Cyrus Ashford. The prodigy from Aldermere. The man who'd publicly humiliated Roan's roommate during last year's exchange program, mocking Theon's technique, his heritage, his family's modest means. The man who'd laughed when Theon had tried to respond and told him that some people were born to be mediocre.

The man who'd looked at Roan during that same exchange and dismissed him with a single glance before walking away.

"Cyrus isn't here for the tournament," Roan said. His voice sounded strange in his own ears, too flat, too controlled. "He's here to scout. Which means someone's told him there's something worth scouting."

"Or someone told him there's someone worth watching." Drogan moved around to face Roan, his metal hand catching the light. "You've been climbing fast. Faster than anyone with your apparent background should. Faster than anyone with your file should be able to without raising flags."

"I know what my file says."

"Do you know what your file said three months ago?"

Roan went still.

Drogan continued, his voice dropping. "Three months ago, your admission file had you listed as a low-Tempered with a projected ceiling of Hardened. Standard intake for someone with your family's background. A minor branch of a minor house. Resources limited. No particular talent to speak of. Mediocre everything."

"And now?"

"Now your file says you've exceeded your projected ceiling by a factor of three, and the projection itself has been flagged for review by someone with authority to flag projections." The old man's eyes were steady, unreadable. "Someone updated your file—legitimately. Which means someone with access to the administrative system has decided to make your progress visible."

Roan's hand tightened on the practice blade. The columns were cascading through his mind, the probability matrices, the resource calculations, the hundred possible explanations and their likely costs. None of them ended well.

"Who has that kind of access?"

"Senior faculty. Administrative council. Heads of major houses." Drogan paused. "The Ashford family has significant influence in the administrative council."

Cyrus. This was Cyrus. The tournament was a trap—not for everyone, but for him specifically. Someone had updated his file to make his progress visible, and Cyrus Ashford had been sent to Ironholm to see it for himself. To verify. To confirm.

The question was: confirm what?

"You're thinking this is about you," Drogan said. "It's not. Not entirely."

Roan blinked. "Explain."

"The Ashfords have been digging into the Spire's administration for a decade." Drogan's metal hand flexed, gears grinding. "Placement. Curriculum access. Resource allocation." He let the list hang in the air. "The Headmaster has blocked them at every turn—until now. This tournament? First time in years they've gotten a real pass inside. You think that's coincidence?"

"You think they're using me to make a point."

"I think you're convenient. A minor student from a minor house who happens to be climbing faster than expected. If they can demonstrate that the Black Spire's admission system is flawed—that students like you are being elevated beyond their station—it gives them ammunition for administrative reform. And if they can break you publicly during the tournament, so much the better."

Roan let out a breath. The ledger was clarifying, pieces sliding into place with a kind of horrible elegance. "They want to use me as evidence that the Spire is broken."

"They want to use your destruction as evidence. A cautionary tale. *See what happens when the system fails to properly evaluate students without sufficient pedigree.*"

*They're going to try to break me in front of everyone.*

The thought should've been terrifying. Instead, it was almost clarifying. A problem with defined parameters. Enemies with clear motivations. A tournament that was being positioned as a stage for his public unmaking.

He could work with that.

"There's a problem with their logic," Roan said.

"Oh?"

"They're assuming I'm going to lose."

Drogan's expression shifted, something that might've been approval, or might've been concern. "You sound confident."

"I sound like someone who's done the balance." The practice blade felt right in his hand now—heavier than it should be, but balanced in a way that made the weight useful. "They've built an entire strategy around what my file says I can do. They've planned for a low-Tempered with limited resources and no particular advantages."

"And?"

"And months ago, I stopped being that person." The shard pulsed against his spine, warm and eager, and for the first time, Roan let himself acknowledge what lived inside it. "I have no idea what I'm going to do in that arena. But I know exactly what I'm not going to do."

"Which is?"

"Lose."

The next three hours were the most focused training he'd done since activating the Codex Shard. Drogan pushed him through forms until his muscles screamed, until the boundary between his body and the blade blurred into a single weapon. No Inversion. No shortcuts. Just the brutal mathematics of repetition—strike, recover, strike again.

The shard watched through him, learning, always learning.

By the time the sun touched the horizon, Roan was drenched in sweat and running on empty. His Vitalis had dropped to the low thousands, not from combat, but from the sustained exertion of forms training without any Inversion support.

Drogan called a halt. "You need to eat. Rest. Clear your head before tomorrow."

"What happens tomorrow?"

"Cyrus Ashford has requested a formal sparring exhibition with one of our Tempered-tier students. The Headmaster approved it this afternoon." The old man's voice was carefully neutral. "The student he requested was you."

Of course it was.

"When?"

"Tomorrow evening. Before the opening ceremony."

That gave him approximately eighteen hours to prepare for a fight against someone who'd been training since before Roan was born. A prodigy from a house that had produced warriors for twelve generations. A man with resources and connections, plus every reason to want Roan broken in public.

Roan looked down at the practice blade in his hand. The metal was worn smooth from years of use, the edge consistent, the weight familiar. It wasn't Sidewound—it wasn't designed to channel whatever lived in his body. But it was still a blade. And mathematics didn't care about pedigree.

"Tell them yes," he said.

Drogan raised an eyebrow. "You don't want to request a delay? More time to prepare?"

"I don't need more time." He set the practice blade in its rack, aligning it perfectly with the others. "I've had months of time. I've had months of watching everything I do being monitored by people who think they're clever. I've had months of pretending to be something I'm not."

He turned to face the old man fully. "They want to see what I can do? Fine. Give them a show."

The shard pulsed, warmer, faster, more present than it had been in days. Not warning now. Something else. Something that felt almost like anticipation.

The sensation pressed against his ribs, a wordless pressure. It wasn't a voice. It was a feeling. *Readiness.*

---

The dormitory was dark by the time Roan returned, but he didn't sleep. Instead, he sat on the edge of his bed with his eyes closed and his Vitalis cycling through the forms Drogan had drilled into him, each repetition burning the patterns deeper into his muscle memory.

Across the room, Theon muttered something in his sleep—a name Roan recognized—then went still. The sheets were twisted into a knot around his legs. The pattern was familiar by now.

Roan watched his roommate's breathing even out and felt something cold settle in his chest.

Cyrus Ashford had done that. Not through violence—through something worse. Through words. Through dismissal. Through the precise application of contempt so complete that it became a kind of cage.

And tomorrow, Cyrus was going to do it again.

Or he was going to try.

The Codex Shard hummed against his spine, and for the first time, Roan let himself acknowledge what he was planning. Not survival. Not a respectable showing. Not even victory.

Something more permanent.

Cyrus had built his entire identity around being better than people like Roan. Better than people like Theon. Better than anyone without the right bloodline, the right resources, the right connections. If Roan could break that identity—publicly, definitively, in front of everyone who mattered—it wouldn't just end Cyrus Ashford's career.

It would expose the lie that sustained every house like the Ashfords.

The arithmetic was elegant. The execution would be brutal.

He reached for the Vitalis cycling through his channels, pulling it tighter, faster, letting it burn away the last of his hesitation. The shard responded, its warmth spreading through his spine, his ribs, his chest.

There was no counter. No tally of remaining uses. The Inversion didn't work like that. It waited, hungry, for the next wound—and it didn't care how many times Cyrus hit him. It only cared whether Roan could survive long enough to make one of those hits count. He tested the weight of his own channels, the resilience of his marrow. Hardened thresholds. Tempered sinews. Against a prodigy like Cyrus, the margin was slim.

It had to be enough.

Outside the window, Ironholm's lights painted the sky orange and grey. Somewhere in that city, Cyrus Ashford was sleeping in quarters that cost more than Roan's entire family's net worth. Somewhere in that city, the heads of major houses were preparing to watch a minor student from a minor family get destroyed in public.

They were all wrong about what was going to happen tomorrow.

Roan closed his eyes. The anticipation was a living thing now, coiled in his chest.

Outside the window, the first grey light of dawn touched the spires of Ironholm. The opening ceremony was hours away.

Let them come.
