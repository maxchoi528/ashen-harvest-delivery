Chapter 132: A Knife in the Rules

The warehouse smelled like rust and old blood. Two things Roan had learned to distinguish within the same breath—one told him the structural integrity was compromised in the eastern quadrant, the other told him he'd arrived on time.

He crouched behind a stack of shipping containers, five meters from the door. His Vitalis reading sat at 7,500. Comfortable. Not the 10,000 he'd touched in the Black Spire's arena three weeks ago, but that had been a controlled environment with a controlled opponent. This was neither.

Cyrus Ashford had sent the message through Brendt, which meant it wasn't a trap—or at least, not one Cyrus intended to spring himself. The Ashford heir wanted something badly enough to open a back channel, and the fact that he'd used Brendt as the intermediary meant he'd calculated the cost of that favor.

The balance wasn't complicated. Brendt owed Cyrus nothing. Which meant Brendt had extracted something in exchange. Brendt was many things—loud, reckless with his loyalty, incapable of strategic deception—but he wasn't stupid when it came to protecting people he'd chosen to protect.

Roan had been chosen. That had a value he hadn't fully assigned until now.

The door groaned open. A single light source, low and orange, casting long shadows. A figure entered. Tall, broad shoulders, moving with the deliberate economy of someone who'd learned to fight before he'd learned to read.

One guard. No, two. The second stayed at the threshold, hand resting on a sidearm that cost more than Greycross's entire district precinct. Private security. Ashford family insignia on the collar.

The first figure stopped in the center of the warehouse, hands visible, posture deliberately non-threatening. Not Cyrus, then. Too early in the evening for the Ashford heir to show his cards in person.

"You came alone." The voice was female, measured, professional. "We appreciate that."

Roan didn't move. His eyes tracked the second guard's position, the angle of the single light, the gaps between containers where a second shooter could be stationed. Six seconds of analysis told him the layout was suboptimal. Either this was genuinely a negotiation, or someone was relying on his arrogance to accept the obvious setup.

"I'm here." Roan's voice carried across the space, flat, neutral. "Talk."

The woman tilted her head, a curious gesture, almost amused. "Direct. I was told you'd be direct." She took a step forward, and the guard at the door shifted but didn't raise his weapon. "My name is Maren. I work for House Ashford in an advisory capacity. Specifically, I advise on matters that require discretion."

"Matters."

"Mr. Ashford requires your expertise. Not your services—your expertise. There's a situation developing in the Hollow Reaches, and Mr. Ashford believes your knowledge of that territory exceeds what can be obtained through conventional channels."

Roan's jaw tightened. He didn't step out from behind the containers. "I don't consult."

"Forty thousand Spire Credits. Initial consultation fee. Additional compensation upon successful resolution of the situation." Maren's voice didn't waver. "Mr. Ashford is aware you've declined previous offers. He's aware of your reputation for selective engagement. This offer is structured to reflect that selectivity."

Forty thousand. More than a semester's tuition. More than most initiate-level hunters made in a year. Precisely calibrated to be dismissible.

It was the opening move in a negotiation designed to fail gracefully.

"You came a long way to deliver a message." Roan stepped out. Not toward Maren, but to the side, putting a stack of containers between him and the second guard. "Tell Cyrus I appreciate the offer. Tell him the Hollow Reaches isn't a place I consult on."

Maren studied him for three full seconds. "Mr. Ashford anticipated this response." She reached into her coat slowly, hands visible, and produced a slim envelope. "He asked me to provide context, in case you'd reconsider. He understands you have… history in the region. He knows about the Vance cairn."

Roan's hand found the edge of a container. His fingers pressed against cold metal, grounding himself in the physical. The Vance cairn. The hidden sanctuary. Known only to Drogan, Mira, and himself.

Someone had talked. Or someone had guessed correctly.

"The cairn is private." His voice hadn't changed pitch, but something in the delivery had shifted. Maren's eyes narrowed almost imperceptibly. "Whatever Cyrus thinks he knows, he's wrong."

"Mr. Ashford doesn't *think* anything." Maren set the envelope on the floor between them, then stepped back. "He knows the following: that you entered the Hollow Reaches forty-seven days ago without logging a mission with the Rift Hunters Guild. That you were gone for nine days. That when you returned, your Vitalis reading had increased by approximately sixteen percent—a gain inconsistent with standard training protocols at your tier. And that the Hollow Reaches is currently experiencing an unusual cluster of Hollowed Sovereign activity, concentrated in the quadrant nearest to Mount Veyrn."

She paused. "Mr. Ashford finds coincidences personally offensive. So do I."

Right.

Roan looked at the envelope, then at Maren, then at the guard whose hand hadn't left his sidearm. The arithmetic was reassembling itself. Forty thousand wasn't the real number—it was the wedge. The real number was whatever Cyrus was willing to spend to understand why a seventeen-year-old Tempered had walked into a region that had killed three Crowned-tier hunters in the same timeframe and walked out stronger.

The Vance cairn was the pressure point. Not because Cyrus knew its location—he couldn't, or he wouldn't have bothered with a consulting fee—but because he knew Roan had been there. Which meant he'd been watching. Which meant the question wasn't whether House Ashford was a threat, but whether they were the only ones watching.

"I don't consult." Roan repeated the words, but the inflection had shifted. Not a refusal. A starting position. "But I'm not unreasonable."

Maren's posture didn't change, but something behind her eyes did. Professional satisfaction. "Mr. Ashford hoped you'd say that."

"How much is the full number?"

"That depends on the quality of information. Mr. Ashford is prepared to offer up to two hundred thousand Spire Credits, plus incidental expenses, for actionable intelligence regarding the Hollow Reaches anomaly." She paused. "And he's prepared to guarantee your family's safety in Greycross as part of the arrangement. Non-aggression clause. Binding on House Ashford and affiliated parties."

The offer landed like a physical weight. House Ashford guaranteeing Mira and Lyra's safety—not as a gift, but as a contract term. That meant Cyrus had already calculated the probability that Roan would refuse a direct request. This way, refusal cost him the guarantee. Acceptance bought it.

The Ashford heir was learning.

"Three conditions." Roan stepped forward, closed the distance between him and the envelope, but didn't pick it up. "First: I don't meet with Cyrus. You coordinate, I deliver, payment transfers. No face-to-face until I say otherwise. Second: the non-aggression clause extends to Drogan Hale and any associate I've publicly identified. Third: no records. This conversation didn't happen. This arrangement didn't exist."

Maren considered this for four full seconds. "First condition: accepted. Second condition: accepted, with the notation that Warden Hale is already covered under separate arrangement with House Ashford. Third condition…" She smiled, and it didn't reach her eyes. "Mr. Ashford anticipated you'd ask for that. He finds it… refreshing. You're not what the Black Spire rankings suggest you are."

"No one is what the rankings suggest they are." Roan picked up the envelope. "That's the point."

He turned and walked back the way he'd come—past the containers, through the gap in the eastern wall where structural compromise made for an easy exit. Behind him, he heard Maren speak to her guard in a low voice, but the words were indistinct and irrelevant.

Four hundred meters from the warehouse, he opened the envelope.

Inside: a data chip, a handwritten list of coordinates, and a single sheet of paper with a name.

*Kaelen the Unbroken.*

Below it, in smaller text: *The Unbroken Peak. Suthmere. He knows you.*

Roan stopped walking.

The name was in his father's notes—referenced twice in the Codex Shard fragments Aldric had left behind. Not as a threat. As a place marker. A debt.

*Kaelen the Unbroken. Find him if the world turns against you. He owes me a life. He will owe you one too.*

The words had been cryptic at the time. Now, after the Second Door, after the conversation in the bread shop basement, the meaning was clear. Kaelen wasn't an enemy. He was a contingency. A Throne-tier specter standing at the edge of the board, watching to see what Roan would become.

And Cyrus Ashford had just handed Roan a direct line to him.

The match.

Roan pocketed the crumpled paper and the data chip. The coordinates could wait. The name couldn't.

He calculated the distance to Suthmere: fourteen hundred kilometers. Travel time by commercial transit: eighteen hours. By private charter: six. By Tempered-tier sustained movement across back channels: he could make it in thirty-six hours if he pushed and didn't sleep.

The figures said nothing about what he'd find when he arrived. The figures said nothing about whether Kaelen would be waiting, or whether this was another test, or whether the Hollowed Sovereign activity in the Hollow Reaches was related to everything else and Roan was finally assembling the board correctly.

The figures said nothing.

And that was the point.

Roan kept walking.
