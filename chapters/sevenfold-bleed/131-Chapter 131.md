Chapter 131: The Counter-Offer

Roan discovered that surviving a Deep Rift was less expensive than preparing to survive one, which felt like poor design.

The Black Spire Expeditionary Procurement Hall occupied four underground floors beneath the western training wing and had the atmosphere of a bank, an armory, and a predatory aquarium stitched together by committees. Glass-fronted counters lined the first level in concentric rings. Behind them, equipment hung in clean, well-lit arrangements designed to make students believe safety could be purchased if their families loved them enough. Armor plates. Filter masks. Ward spikes. Collapsible shelters. Medical sealant. Route markers. Sample containers. Emergency stimulants. Water purifiers. Rope in fifteen grades, three of which were apparently illegal to export. Boots advertised as Rift-rated by people who had never seen a Rift eat leather.

Every item had a price.

Most had lies.

Roan stood before a display of respiratory filters and watched three second-years argue themselves into buying the expensive model because the salesman used the phrase "military adjacent."

Adjacent did a lot of work in commerce.

Brendt stood beside him with a procurement slate, looking increasingly ill. "We have spent twelve thousand credits and you haven't bought armor."

"I bought armor."

"You bought a repair kit for armor."

"Armor that can't be repaired is a coffin with straps."

"That sounds wise, but I notice I still don't have armor."

"You have shield frames."

"I have rented shield frames."

"Then don't get attached."

Brendt looked at the ceiling. "I miss when our biggest problem was tuition."

"That was never our biggest problem."

"It was emotionally manageable."

Roan took the slate from him and removed the premium filter set from their cart.

Brendt stared. "Why?"

"Overpriced."

"It says Tier Three rated."

"It says Tier Three compliant. Different word."

The salesman behind the counter stiffened in the way people did when a customer stepped on the correct nerve.

Roan turned the package and pointed to the certification line. "Compliant means it passed baseline particulate exposure under stable conditions. Rated would mean independent stress testing under active Rift distortion. This model fails after prolonged humidity. The sealant layer absorbs gray mist residue and swells."

The salesman smiled with too many teeth. "That issue was corrected in the current batch."

"No. The current batch changed packaging."

Brendt looked from Roan to the salesman. "Is that true?"

"No," the salesman said.

Roan opened a public recall database on the slate and placed it on the counter.

The salesman looked at it.

Then at Roan.

Then at Brendt, perhaps hoping for a softer target.

Brendt raised both hands. "I'm just here to carry things and develop anxiety."

Roan selected four mid-grade filter masks from the lower shelf. "These."

The salesman's smile weakened. "Those lack advanced odor neutralization."

"If we are close enough for odor to be the deciding factor, we have already mishandled several earlier decisions."

Brendt took the masks. "I hate that this is reassuring."

The arithmetic resolved cleanly. Eighteen hundred credits bled from the filter line and flowed into the medical column. That column was growing faster than he liked.

Deep Rift preparation had rules. Real rules, not brochure rules.

First: assume maps lie.

Second: assume equipment fails in the order that causes the most narrative inconvenience.

Third: assume every specialized item has been priced by someone who knows frightened students don't negotiate well.

Fourth: if Drogan says "you'll need it," buy two, because Drogan considered explanation optional and danger mandatory.

Fifth: never buy anything whose primary value depends on the user remaining calm. Calm was a resource, not a guarantee.

Roan had built the procurement ledger across three nights after Drogan finally agreed to take him into a Tier Three Rift. The agreement itself had been brief.

Roan had presented the survey anomaly, the tournament winnings, the Codex warnings after House Greel's recognition, and the fact that waiting allowed enemies to choose terrain.

Drogan had said no.

Roan had said, "Then I go with someone less competent."

Drogan had stared at him for thirteen seconds.

Then he had said, "You are a rotten little extortionist."

Roan had said, "Yes."

Permission followed.

Now permission had become invoices.

Ines arrived twenty minutes late carrying coffee and wearing the expression of someone about to enjoy another person's financial pain.

"You started without me," she said.

"You were late."

"I was gathering market intelligence."

"You were buying coffee."

"Coffee is how civilized people gather intelligence."

Brendt pointed at her cup. "Did you bring us any?"

"No."

"Civilization has failed."

Ines ignored him and leaned over the slate. Her eyebrows rose. "You are spending all your tournament credit."

"Yes."

"All of it?"

"Yes."

"Interesting."

Brendt looked alarmed. "Interesting bad?"

"In Roan's case, interesting usually means illegal, profitable, or medically inadvisable. Sometimes all three."

"I meant strategically," Roan said.

"So did I."

She scrolled through the list with quick, precise finger movements. "Too much on medical sealant."

"No."

"Too little on survey tags."

"No."

"Your rope selection is embarrassing."

Roan took the slate back. "Rope is rope."

Ines looked genuinely offended. "That's a sentence that kills promising criminals."

Brendt nodded solemnly. "I don't know enough about rope to disagree, but her tone was persuasive."

Ines dragged them to a counter on the second ring.

The rope specialist was a narrow woman with gray hair, one mechanical hand, and the dead-eyed patience of someone who had watched students buy bright gear and return as paperwork. Roan liked her immediately, so much more than the filter salesman.

"Tier Three expedition," Ines said. "Small team. Mixed combat and survey. Expected humidity, ash, possible vertical instability."

The specialist looked at Roan. "Budget?"

"Low."

"Honest low or student low?"

"Painful low."

The woman nodded once and pulled three coils from beneath the counter instead of the display wall.

Good. The display wall was for parents.

She laid them out: black braided cord, gray flatline, dull red cable with tiny embedded rings.

"Black for hauling. Gray for people. Red for things that might stop being places while you're standing on them."

Brendt blinked. "That last sentence seems important."

"It is," the woman said.

Roan tested the gray flatline between his fingers. Smooth, too light, slight Vitalis response under pressure. "Failure mode?"

"Cuts clean under shear beyond rating. No elastic snap. If it fails, you fall instead of losing a hand first."

"Acceptable."

Brendt made a noise. "Our standards have changed."

Roan bought all three coils.

The final purchase before Drogan arrived was insurance.

Not the official policy. The official policy excluded Rift exposure, monster attack, unauthorized deviation, equipment misuse, environmental distortion, and "other anomalous causation," which left coverage for falling down normal stairs on the way to the expedition. Roan bought that policy because institutions relaxed when forms existed, then purchased a private extraction bond through an ugly little vendor two levels down whose office smelled like ink and desperation.

The vendor slid the contract across a scratched counter. "Bond pays if your remains, confirmed death token, or legally sufficient absence marker returns."

Brendt stared. "Legally sufficient absence marker?"

"Piece of gear with blood and no body after seventy-two hours," the vendor said.

"That's horrifyingly specific."

"Business likes specificity."

Roan read every clause.

The payout was small. The real value lay in the notification chain: if triggered, Mira and Lyra received funds before Black Spire could bury the event under review procedures.

He signed.

Brendt didn't joke for almost a minute after that.

Roan preferred the jokes. Silence made the contract feel heavier than it was.

It wasn't sentiment.

It was contingency planning.

The distinction was operationally important and emotionally useless.

Twenty-seven hundred credits gone. The survival probability bump was measurable. Brendt was staring at the receipt. That was the emotional cost. Worth it.

At midday, a Guild procurement officer attempted to upsell them on a certified guide.

The officer was square-faced and pleasant, then professionally predatory. He wore a blue badge that meant he could approve student discounts and a gold pin that meant he was rewarded for preventing them.

"A Tier Three environment isn't recommended for first independent exposure," he said. "For a modest additional fee, the Guild can assign an experienced pathfinder to your party."

Roan checked the fee. "That isn't modest."

"It reflects expertise."

"It reflects hazard markup and the assumption that frightened students can't divide by four."

The officer's pleasantness thinned. "Pathfinder casualty rates aren't trivial."

"Then why is your cheapest assigned guide rated only for Tier Two transit?"

Brendt leaned over. "Is he?"

Roan turned the slate. "His certification excludes unstable architecture, active predation, and deep signal loss."

Brendt looked at the officer. "What exactly would he guide?"

The officer coughed. "Surface approach and emergency extraction coordination."

"In other words," Roan said, "he would walk us to the dangerous part and then become expensive luggage."

Ines, who had arrived just in time to hear this, said, "I charge less for luggage work."

The officer looked at her badge and decided the conversation had become less profitable.

They declined the guide.

He saved four thousand five hundred credits and acquired a mildly offended procurement officer. False confidence was not a resource. The risk column didn't move.

They also bought food, which turned into the most insulting negotiation of the day.

Rift rations came in three categories: edible and efficient, plus decorative. Edible rations tasted like food and cost like jewelry. Efficient rations tasted like compressed municipal regret but kept a combatant moving. Decorative rations came in sleek packaging and were designed for social media posts by students who would turn back before the air got strange.

Brendt reached for edible.

Roan selected efficient.

Brendt looked betrayed. "We may die down there."

"Then taste becomes less important."

"If we don't die, morale matters."

"Morale can be purchased with survival."

"That's the worst slogan I have ever heard."

Ines picked up one edible pack and read the ingredients. "This has powdered cream, dried fruit, and stimulant chocolate."

Brendt pointed at her. "See?"

"It also spoils after seal breach and attracts burrow vermin."

Brendt lowered his hand. "Betrayal everywhere."

They compromised by buying efficient rations and one sealed packet of bitter stimulant chocolate for emergency morale, which Roan classified as medical support to avoid admitting Brendt had made a point.

The ledger didn't judge him.

They moved through the hall ring by ring.

Roan rejected decorative armor with House crests, stimulant packs with addiction clauses hidden behind subscription pricing, a field stove that required clean air to function, and a "Rift scent masking charm" whose active ingredient was peppermint oil and fraud. He bought alcohol capsules, compression bandages, three kinds of sealant, thermal sheets, collapsible lamps, chalk, backup chalk because chalk was cheap and maps were liars, two moisture collectors, ward wire, battery cells, sample tubes for Ines after she threatened to use ration containers, and boot liners rated for acid exposure.

Brendt got armor eventually.

Not expensive armor.

Good armor.

There was a difference, and explaining it took twelve minutes because Brendt kept touching the shiny plated set like it had personally promised to save him.

"The bright one has higher impact rating," Brendt said.

"Single impact," Roan said.

"That seems relevant when monsters hit you."

"The second impact kills you."

"This one?" Brendt tapped the matte composite vest Roan had selected.

"Lower peak rating. Better spread. Repairable. No proprietary sealant."

"You hate proprietary sealant."

"I hate dying because a company wanted recurring revenue."

Ines lifted her coffee. "A morally defensible position."

Brendt sighed and chose the matte armor.

Roan chose less for himself.

Not because he intended to be reckless. Because armor changed movement, movement changed timing, and his techniques already ran on narrow margins. Too much plating would slow Sevenfold transitions and interfere with Pyrebrand heat bleed. He bought reinforced underlayers, bracers, a throat guard, and a plain coat with hidden attachment points.

Ines also found the one thing Roan had missed.

"Camp anchors," she said, stopping at a narrow shelf near the back of a ward-supply alcove.

"I have ward wire."

"Ward wire makes a line. Camp anchors make the line remember it's supposed to remain a line when the ground decides geometry is optional."

Brendt pointed at her. "That sounded important."

"It was."

Roan examined the anchors. Heavy. Ugly. Expensive in a way that suggested usefulness rather than branding. Each spike contained a dull core of stabilized alloy and a replaceable crystal head.

Failure mode: crystal burnout, spike remains physical.

Good.

He added six.

The budget made a small wounded sound. Ines then pulled a sealed case from behind a stack of obsolete ward cores. "Emergency navigation kit. Acoustic mapping. Last year's model. Procurement flagged it as surplus because the casing is the wrong shade of gray. The functionality is identical."

Roan ran a quick diagnostic on the slate. The readout matched factory spec. "That is theft."

"Administrative reclassification," Ines said. "I do this for a living."

"Hundred credits," the counter clerk said, eager to be rid of it.

Roan added it to the cart.

Brendt looked at the total. "Was that the last of it?"

"Yes."

"Completely?"

Roan checked all accounts. Tournament credit, private market fund, salvage reserve, emergency cash.

"Completely enough."

"That isn't a comforting modifier."

"It's an accurate one."

Roan left the complaint hanging. It was true and unactionable, the worst category of truth.

Drogan arrived while Roan was comparing utility knives.

He appeared at the edge of the counter with no announcement, which was typical. His gaze swept the cart, the slate, Brendt's armor, Ines's sample tube pouch, and Roan's selected blades.

"No," Drogan said.

Roan looked up. "To which item?"

"All knives on that counter."

The counter clerk, who had been preparing to praise the selection, shut his mouth.

Roan glanced at the blades. "They meet requirements."

"For cutting rope and making yourself feel prepared." Drogan picked up one of the knives, flexed it against his thumb, and put it back with contempt. "Not for things in a Deep Rift."

"They're backup tools."

"They're future excuses."

Roan folded his arms. "Then specify."

Drogan looked around the hall, then toward the lower service stairs at the far wall. The air changed. Less filtered. Older. Brendt's mouth opened, then closed. Even he felt it.

"No."

Roan followed his gaze.

A sign above the stairs read: AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY.

Naturally.

Drogan started walking.

The counter clerk said, "Sir, that section requires—"

Drogan looked at him.

The clerk discovered an urgent need to reorganize receipts.

They followed.

The lower service level had none of the first floor's polished confidence. Narrow corridors. Concrete walls. Exposed ward conduits. Storage cages. Repair benches. The smell of oil and metal, plus old dust. Better. Places became more honest when customers weren't meant to see them.

Drogan led them past two locked doors, down another half flight, and into a cramped workshop where broken gear hung from hooks like defeated animals.

An old man sat behind a bench filing the edge of a spearhead.

He looked up once.

"No," he said.

Drogan stopped. "I didn't ask."

"You brought students. That's a question with legs."

"This one is going into a Tier Three."

The old man looked at Roan properly.

Then his eyes narrowed.

Not at Roan's face.

At his spine.

The Codex Shard went very quiet. A cold pulse settled in Roan's marrow.

`[External debt vector detected.]`

Roan's right hand lowered by half an inch, toward where a knife would have been if he hadn't slaughtered the budget on anchors and acoustic maps.

The old man noticed and smiled without warmth. "At least he knows when a room goes bad."

Drogan's voice hardened. "Eckhart."

So this was Eckhart.

The weaponsmith wore a stained apron, thick gloves, and the expression of a man who considered every living customer an argument for retirement. His hair was iron gray and burned short on one side. The left half of his jaw carried old scar tissue. Behind him, a forge glowed deep red despite the room's poor ventilation, and three unfinished blades lay under black cloth.

Eckhart set down the spearhead.

"What is your name, boy?"

Roan considered lying.

Drogan had brought him here, which meant the name would matter and lying badly would waste time. Lying well would create debt. Debt around old weaponsmiths seemed expensive.

"Roan Vance."

The room changed.

No ward flared. No weapon moved. Brendt didn't notice immediately. Ines did. Drogan's shoulders tightened, which told Roan the reaction had been expected and unwelcome.

Eckhart stared at him.

For two seconds, the old man looked not old, but struck.

Then it vanished behind irritation.

"Of course," he said.

Roan waited.

Eckhart looked at Drogan. "You're an idiot."

"Yes," Drogan said.

"Does he know?"

"No."

"Typical."

Roan's patience shortened. "Does he know what?"

"Enough to be irritating," Eckhart said.

"That isn't an answer."

"No. It's a diagnostic."

Roan let the silence sit. Eckhart's eyes went again to his spine, then to his hands, then to the procurement slate.

"You bought garbage knives."

"I didn't buy them."

"Good. You're less stupid than the average student."

"Low bar."

"Correct."

Drogan stepped aside. "He needs a blade that won't betray him in a Tier Three."

"Everyone needs that. Most deserve what they can afford."

Roan held up the slate. "Budget is remaining credit plus private funds."

Eckhart took the slate, looked at the number, and snorted. "That buys a handle."

"I can supply materials."

"What materials?"

Roan opened his pack and removed three sealed items: a sliver of Rift-black alloy won through tournament salvage rights, two shards of hardened Husk claw from earlier sanctioned missions, and a narrow strip of grave-iron he had acquired through Ines's market contact at a price that made Brendt refuse to speak for eleven minutes.

Eckhart's expression changed by degrees.

At the grave-iron, he stopped pretending not to be interested.

"Where did you get that?"

"Market."

"Which market?"

"The kind that doesn't issue receipts."

Eckhart looked at Ines.

She smiled politely.

"Hm," he said. "Thieves with taste. Rare."

Roan placed the materials on the bench. "Can you make something?"

"Yes."

"Will you?"

Eckhart looked at his spine again.

The Codex Shard remained silent in the way a knife remained silent before being drawn.

"Cost," the old man said.

Roan waited for the number.

Eckhart named one so low Brendt audibly inhaled.

Ines stopped smiling.

Drogan closed his eyes for half a second.

Roan looked at the old man. The number was an insult to the market. Two thousand credits for grave-iron and Husk claw work wasn't a price. It was a confession. Roan had come prepared to argue against a number ten times higher. Now Eckhart had countered an argument Roan hadn't even made.

"That's material cost," Roan said.

"Yes."

"Labor?"

"Waived."

"Why?"

"Because I'm old and sentimental."

"No."

Eckhart's eyes sharpened. "No?"

"You aren't sentimental. Try again."

The old man leaned back. The forge light caught his scarred jaw. "Your father had the same mouth."

There it was.

The room didn't become quiet. It had already been quiet. It became narrower.

Roan didn't step forward. Stepping forward would concede impact.

"You knew Aldric."

"I knew a man who used that name."

Debt again.

Drogan looked away.

Useful.

Painful for someone. Not Roan, yet.

"What did you owe him?" Roan asked.

"Not today."

"That sounds negotiable."

"It isn't." Eckhart's voice was flat. Final.

Roan watched his posture. He could push. Pros: immediate information, possible Aldric connection, leverage over Eckhart, clarification of Drogan's silence. Cons: losing access to a skilled weaponsmith before a Deep Rift, forcing Drogan into old trauma in a public-adjacent workshop, revealing how much he didn't know, triggering whatever ward had just warmed under the floor.

The ward was subtle.

Not subtle enough.

Roan looked down.

Eckhart smiled. "Good eyes."

"Bad floor."

"Expensive floor."

Roan met his gaze. "That isn't your price."

"You don't know my price."

"I know you just countered a negotiation I hadn't started. That makes you someone who is afraid of being asked for too much, or afraid of being paid too little."

Eckhart's smile thinned. "The number stands."

"It isn't fair."

"No. But I'm old, not sentimental. Take it or leave it."

Roan held his stare for three long breaths.

"Make the blade."

"Specifications?"

"Short enough for close quarters. Long enough to reach core seams. No House markings. No tracking. No decorative idiocy. Grip must tolerate blood and ash, then one-handed use. Balance for precision, not chopping."

Eckhart listened without writing anything down.

"Vitalis channel?"

"Minimal."

Eckhart's eyebrows rose. "Most boys ask for more."

"Most boys are trying to compensate."

Brendt whispered, "He says in a basement full of weapons."

Ines whispered back, "Let him have the line."

Eckhart looked like he might laugh again. Instead he gathered the materials. "Come back tomorrow evening."

"That fast?" Brendt asked.

"No," Eckhart said. "But I dislike anticipation."

Roan transferred the agreed amount.

His slate displayed the remaining balance.

Almost nothing.

As they left the workshop, Brendt looked at the receipt summary again and winced. "We are broke."

"No," Roan said. "We are equipped."

"That sounds like broke with boots."

"Good boots."

Ines took another sip of coffee. "I admit, the boots are good."

Drogan walked ahead of them, silent.

Roan watched his back. The old man had known Eckhart would react. Had known enough to be afraid of the reaction, or ashamed of it. Another piece of Aldric's life had surfaced from beneath institutional lies and refused to explain itself.

He had lost liquidity. He had gained preparation. Procurement logs would show visible seriousness. The new unknown was Eckhart's connection to his father, a debt vector he hadn't expected to find in a basement forge. The next debt was tomorrow's blade.

Fine.

Roan had learned patience from poverty and poison, plus bureaucracy.

He could wait one day for a blade.

He could wait longer for answers.

But not forever.
