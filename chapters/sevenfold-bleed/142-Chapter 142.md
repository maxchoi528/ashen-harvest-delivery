Chapter 142: The Rift Economy

No one climbed the temple steps immediately.

That was the first sensible decision the group had made all morning, and Roan appreciated it more because nobody announced it as sensible. Announced caution tended to become performance. Silent caution remained useful.

The temple stood ahead, clean and black and patient. Its seven ribs curved around the dome like fingers protecting a wound.

They established a perimeter before studying anything.

That should've been obvious. It hadn't been obvious to the missing the western signatory team, if the black marker meant what it appeared to mean. Or perhaps it had been obvious and still not enough. Roan didn't find that comforting, but he found it educational.

Drogan took the left approach lane, where the avenue curved between two ribbed walls. Ines placed three passive alarms behind broken stone and one decoy marker in plain sight. Brendt anchored himself near the lower right step with his shield braced against a fallen pillar, giving him a stable line despite the ankle. Roan checked the rooflines, the temple ribs, the base of the stele, and the ground beneath Brendt's boots twice.

The Husk pack remained outside the avenue.

Watching.

At least twelve shapes were visible between pillars. The rest kept to mist. None crossed the invisible boundary where ash ended and clean stone began.

"They fear it," Brendt said.

"Or they guard it by refusing to contaminate the approach," Ines said.

"I preferred fear."

"Fear and guard can share a uniform," Roan said.

"That didn't improve it."

Roan looked at the nearest Husk silhouette. Its head was angled toward the temple, not toward them. Reverence? Hunger? Recognition? Hard to know without dissection, and the temple perimeter seemed a poor laboratory.

He drew a line in ash with the tip of his standard knife, just outside the clean stone. The nearest Husk clicked once.

Roan drew the line one inch closer.

Three more Husks clicked.

Not attack. Warning.

"Boundary response confirmed," he said.

Drogan looked irritated. "You could confirm less creatively."

"I used ash, not blood."

"That isn't the standard I want you using."

"It's the standard available."

Brendt leaned on his shield. "For what it's worth, I object to all future tests involving blood, bones, names, voices, doors, water, symbols, or Roan's definition of available."

"Denied in advance," Roan said.

"Efficient tyranny."

The line in the ash remained. No creature crossed it. After ten seconds, the clicking stopped.

Boundary didn't respond to inert matter. It responded to intent, blood, or living crossing. Useful.

The stone door at the top of the steps didn't open, but it had changed after Roan spoke. A seam glowed faintly along the central mark, not with light exactly, but with contrast. The air around it looked a degree sharper than the air elsewhere.

Behind them, the Hollowed City waited.

The Husk pack didn't enter the avenue.

Roan had expected as much after the fountain. Expected didn't mean understood.

Predators avoided the temple perimeter. The marker constructs had died near the approach square. The blood-script had addressed Drogan's debt. The water had imitated voices. The clean objects had lured or watched. Each phenomenon had a different mechanism but the same directional pressure: bring them here, but don't cross for them.

That meant the temple either wanted voluntary entry or required it.

Roan disliked consent-based traps. They made legal arguments afterward.

Drogan stood beside him at the base of the steps, blade still sheathed. His eyes hadn't left the door.

"You said the tower collapsed inward," Roan said.

"It did."

"You saw it?"

"I was running from it."

"That weakens observational reliability."

"I was carrying your father at the time."

"That explains the bias."

Brendt made a small choking sound. "Roan."

Drogan looked at him.

For a moment, the old man's expression suggested violence had applied for consideration and been narrowly rejected.

Good. He was still present.

"The tower fell," Drogan said. "Stone folded inward. Stairs, walls, upper bell frame. Everything. When we reached extraction, there was a crater behind us."

"Could the temple have risen afterward?" Ines asked.

"Buildings don't rise," Brendt said.

Ines glanced at the gray water flowing uphill in the canal far behind them.

"Fine," Brendt said. "They shouldn't."

Roan crouched and examined the first step.

Clean stone, matte black, faintly warm. No ash. No visible carvings on the horizontal surface. Along the side edge, however, small symbols ran in a tight line, each etched so shallowly that only the angled Rift light revealed them.

He didn't touch.

The Codex Shard offered no translation.

Roan performed three non-contact tests before allowing himself to approach the stele again.

First, he tossed a pebble onto the lowest step.

Nothing happened. The pebble landed, bounced once, and rested against the step inscription. No light. No sound. No sudden temple appetite. Brendt looked disappointed, which was either relief misfiled or the beginning of dangerous expectations.

Second, Roan extended a thread of neutral Vitalis toward the same step and stopped it one inch above the surface.

The step inscriptions darkened.

Not much. Enough.

[Contact threshold detected.]

[Pattern family: Veyrn.]

[Access state: dormant.]

He withdrew the thread.

The inscriptions faded.

Third, he drew Sidewound one inch from its sheath.

The central mark on the door brightened.

Drogan swore under his breath.

Roan sheathed the blade immediately.

The mark dimmed, but not all the way.

"The weapon is a key," Ines said.

"Or a bell," Roan said.

"Those are very different."

"Not to whatever answers doors."

Brendt raised one hand. "Can we avoid ringing the murder bell?"

"For now."

"That phrase again."

Drogan stepped closer to Roan, lowering his voice. "Eckhart made that blade from what you brought him."

"Yes."

"Where did the material come from?"

Roan looked at the door. "Tournament salvage, old Aldric scrap, and one piece I didn't recognize from his kit."

Drogan closed his eyes briefly. "The black case."

"Probably."

"You forged a temple key without knowing it."

"Eckhart forged it. I merely funded the mistake."

"Roan."

"Yes. I know."

He did. That was the problem. Sidewound wasn't compatible with the Codex Shard. It was compatible with this place. Eckhart had recognized enough to make it properly, or Aldric had left enough behind that any competent old debt could assemble the next step.

Inheritance, again.

Roan preferred money. Money at least admitted it wanted to be spent.

The Codex Shard was interesting.

It had translated the funerary challenge at the fountain. It recognized the seven-line marker. But these step inscriptions remained unreadable or deliberately withheld.

"Ines," Roan said.

She was already taking out a lens crystal. "I know. No contact. Visual record only."

"And no flash."

She paused. "I also knew that."

"Now you know it twice."

"Your concern is moving."

"Move less while recording."

Brendt lowered himself onto a broken stone block and extended his injured leg with visible relief. He kept his shield across his lap. Sensible. Pain hadn't made him stupid. That was one of Brendt's better traits and one of the reasons Roan had not objected harder to his presence on the expedition.

Drogan noticed Roan noticing.

"He shouldn't climb unless necessary," Drogan said quietly.

"Agreed."

"That was too fast."

"His ankle is compromised, the steps may test blood or Vitalis, and the temple appears keyed to my lineage. Bringing him to the door adds risk without function."

Drogan studied him. "You can say you don't want your friend hurt."

"I could. It would add nothing."

"It would add honesty."

Roan looked at him. "No. It would add performance. The decision is already made."

The old man's mouth tightened, but he didn't argue.

Good.

Brendt, who had absolutely heard them, stared very hard at the opposite wall and said nothing. That was also good. Growth. Shock.

Ines finished recording the first inscription line and shifted to the second step. "These aren't Hollowkin script. At least not any variant in public archives."

"Public archives," Roan said.

"I am offended by your tone and not by your implication. Houses the western signatory may have more."

Drogan's head turned slightly at the western signatory.

Roan kept the reaction. Again.

"Do they repeat?" he asked.

Ines moved the lens across the edge. "Not exactly. Seven clusters per step. Each cluster has a central slash and six peripheral marks. Variations every step. Could be numeric. Could be prayer. Could be lock-state notation. Could be a very old shopping list."

Brendt raised a hand weakly. "I vote shopping list."

"Denied," Roan said.

"You deny too many things."

"People offer poor submissions."

Roan moved his gaze up the steps.

There were forty-nine.

Of course there were.

Seven lines. Seven ribs. Seven clusters per step. Forty-nine steps. The architecture had the subtlety of a creditor with a hammer.

"Seven by seven," he said.

The Codex Shard pulsed.

[Structural numeracy confirmed.]

[Pattern family: Veyrn.]

Veyrn.

The name didn't arrive with definition. It arrived with pressure, like a door in memory opening onto a room he had never entered.

Roan's vision flickered.

For half a second, he saw the temple whole, not ruined, not hidden, not black against gray mist, but standing beneath a red sky while thousands of people knelt on the avenue below. Not worshipping. Bleeding. Each person had one palm cut open and pressed against the stone. Seven lines of blood ran from the top step to the street.

Then the vision vanished.

Roan didn't sway.

He had learned early that bodies liked dramatizing inconvenient data. He refused to reward them.

Drogan was watching him. "What did you see?"

"A crowd bleeding on the steps. Red sky. Temple intact."

Brendt exhaled. "That's one of those things I wish you would phrase less calmly."

"Panic doesn't improve accuracy."

"It improves relatability."

Ines looked up from her lens. "Veyrn?"

Roan looked at her.

"You said it under your breath," she said.

Had he?

Annoying.

"The Shard identified the pattern family as Veyrn," Roan said.

Drogan's face changed again.

"You know it," Roan said.

The old man shook his head slowly. "Aldric said that word after he came out of the chamber. Once. I thought it was a name."

"It may be."

"He said, 'Veyrn still remembers.' Then he collapsed."

The Codex Shard went cold.

Not pulsing. Cold.

Roan looked at the temple door.

Veyrn still remembers.

Buildings didn't remember. Civilizations did. Systems did. Weapons sometimes did, if fools built them sentimental enough. Temples remembered only when someone made them into machines.

This place was a machine. A tomb. Both.

"We need the stele," Roan said.

Ines pointed to the left of the staircase. "There."

He had missed it because the door wanted attention.

A stone slab stood half-recessed into the wall beside the lower steps, tall and narrow, its surface angled away from the avenue. Unlike the step inscriptions, the stele's markings were deep and clear, then arranged around the same seven-line circle that marked the door. Ash had gathered at its base but not on its face.

Roan approached slowly.

Drogan moved with him.

"No touching," Drogan said.

"You already said."

"I'm saying it again because you heard it creatively."

"Accurate."

They stopped one arm's length from the stele.

The central symbol matched the Codex Shard's earliest prompt icon, the one Roan had seen only in internal flashes when Inversion came online. Seven narrow cuts converged around a hollow center.

The stele didn't merely contain text. It contained priority.

Roan noticed it on the third pass. His eyes wanted the central wound-symbol first, then the inner ring, then the outer ring. That sounded ordinary until he forced himself to look at the lower left corner and felt his attention slide away as if the stone had been greased. Not mind control. Directional emphasis. A reading order enforced through pressure.

He disliked that enough to read it wrong on purpose.

Starting at the bottom, he moved counterclockwise along the outer ring, skipping every seventh mark. The Codex Shard pulsed twice, then gave up translating. Fine. He didn't need full translation yet. He needed response.

The central symbol dimmed by a fraction.

"It noticed," he said.

Ines looked delighted despite the setting. "Defensive typography."

"Hostile typography," Brendt said.

"All typography is hostile if you bill by the page," Ines said.

Roan continued misreading.

On the second circuit, a thin red line appeared across the stele's lower edge. It didn't drip. It wrote a single mark, then stopped.

Drogan leaned closer despite himself. "What does it mean?"

The Shard translated after a reluctant pause.

[Correction.]

Roan almost smiled. "It's annoyed."

Brendt looked personally offended. "The rock is annoyed."

"Yes."

"With your reading comprehension."

"Apparently."

"I hate that I understand why."

The test established three things. The stele could monitor interpretation. It preferred a specific reading order. It corrected rather than punished first deviation. That suggested an instructional function, more than a lock. Machines meant to kill didn't usually care whether the victim read properly before dying. Teachers did, and teachers were often worse.

Roan stopped misreading before the correction escalated.

The red mark dried into black and remained.

A recorded error. Wonderful.

"You vandalized the ancient murder tablet," Brendt said.

"It vandalized itself."

"Because of you."

"Agency matters."

Ines recorded the correction mark with visible hunger. "That may be the first responsive Veyrn inscription sample in any modern archive."

"Public archive," Roan said.

She didn't deny it.

Drogan's gaze stayed on the mark. "Aldric argued with walls."

Roan looked at him.

"In the tower," Drogan said. "Not like this. He would read something, say no, and the wall would change. Mercer thought he was unlocking it. Aldric said he was correcting bad grammar."

That sounded like his father.

Not because Roan had known him well enough to judge. He hadn't. The father of Roan's childhood had been a tired man with careful hands and too many locked drawers. But the Shard's notes carried a dry impatience that made the image plausible: Aldric Vance, bleeding in an alien tower, arguing grammar with a wall while soldiers died upstairs.

Roan wrote: Veyrn systems respond to interpretive challenge.

Then, after a pause: possible inherited irritation.

Brendt leaned just enough to read it and snorted. Pain made the sound short.

"Don't laugh," Roan said. "It strains the ankle."

"That note deserved injury."

The focus returned to the stele.

Seven lines.

Not a sun. Not a wheel. A wound with structure.

Around it ran three rings of text.

The outer ring: Hollowkin funerary script, or close enough that the Shard translated fragments.

[...blood that turns the blade...]

[...debt carried through exile...]

[...gate remains shut to conquered names...]

The middle ring didn't translate.

The inner ring didn't need to. Roan could read it.

That was the problem.

He didn't know the language. He didn't know the alphabet. His eyes moved over the marks and meaning arrived anyway, not as words but as positions in thought.

Seven harms.

Seven returns.

A wound isn't weakness unless it closes wrongly.

Name the blood.

Name the debt.

Name the exile.

Then enter.

The three declarations created a problem of sequence.

Name the blood. Name the debt. Name the exile. Then enter.

Blood was easiest and therefore bait. Roan could say Vance, but Vance was an adopted surface name if Drogan's history meant anything. He could say Aldric, but the temple wanted the entrant's blood, not the father's. He could say whatever name the Shard hid, assuming the Shard would provide it, which it hadn't. That left Roan with a set of names arranged from safest to truest and no guarantee that the safest would count.

Debt was worse. Eckhart's debt. Drogan's debt. Aldric's debt. Roan's revenge. The fountain had already declared debt where blood fell, tying Drogan to Lysa or Aldric or both. The temple might not distinguish moral debt from ritual obligation. Old systems often lacked social nuance, which made them resemble tax offices.

Exile was worst.

Roan wasn't exiled. Not technically. He had been born in Greycross, registered in Ironholm, admitted to Black Spire, and threatened by enough institutions to prove citizenship. Aldric had been the exile. The Shard might consider Roan an extension of that exile through blood. Or it might consider him a local contamination carrying a stolen key.

That distinction seemed important.

"You are making the list face again," Brendt said.

"There are at least three required declarations and every safe answer is incomplete."

"I withdraw the complaint. Continue the face."

Ines folded her arms. "Can we give partial declarations?"

"Probably."

"Will that partially open the door?"

"That isn't a phrase I enjoy."

"Neither do I, but it may matter."

Drogan said, "Aldric used his full name once in the tower."

Roan turned sharply. "What name?"

The old man's face closed.

"I couldn't hear all of it."

"What did you hear?"

Drogan looked at the temple door as if it might answer for him. It didn't.

"Aldric Veyrn," he said at last. "Or Aldric of Veyrn. The rest was drowned by the bells."

The Codex Shard pulsed once, deep and slow.

[Name-fragment acknowledged.]

[Access relevance: high.]

Roan's mouth dried for a single breath.

Not because the name meant much yet. Because the Shard had acknowledged it like a lock hearing the first correct tooth of a key.

Aldric Vance hadn't merely come from Veyrn.

He had belonged to it. Been named by it. Betrayed it, saved it, carried it, or failed it badly enough that temples still waited for his bloodline to explain itself.

Roan looked at the seven-line wound and adjusted the order of future questions.

House Greel could wait.

The western signatory could wait.

For the next five minutes, the dead civilization got priority.

Roan stepped back.

Brendt shifted on the stone block and immediately regretted it.

Roan glanced back. The charcoal rings around the punctures remained intact. No gray spread. Sweat on Brendt's neck had increased, but that could be exertion, fear, pain, or the normal response to sitting beside a temple that corrected grammar with blood.

"Status," Roan said.

"Ankle hurts. Pride worse. Still useful."

"Shield arm?"

"Fine."

"If the door opens?"

Brendt lifted the shield an inch. "I block anything unfriendly and complain afterward."

"Good."

"That was almost normal teamwork."

"Don't get used to it."

Drogan's hand caught his shoulder before anyone else noticed the movement.

"What?"

Roan looked at the old man's hand, then at his face.

Drogan removed it.

"It wants names," Roan said.

Brendt, from the stone block, said, "I vote we give it fake ones."

"It will know."

"I vote we leave."

"Noted."

"You say noted when you mean ignored."

"Efficient notation."

Ines came closer but stopped outside arm's reach. "Names as in personal names, lineage names, or ritual titles?"

"Yes."

"Deeply unhelpful."

Roan studied the stele again, careful not to fall into the inner ring's meaning too quickly. "Blood, debt, exile. Three declarations. The door likely requires one from a keyed entrant."

"You," Drogan said.

"Likely."

"No."

"That wasn't a question."

"Still no."

Roan looked at him. "This is why I don't ask permission."

Drogan stepped closer. "You think this place is your inheritance because it recognizes the thing in your spine. You don't know what it recognizes. You don't know whether it wants Roan Vance, Aldric's son, the Shard carrier, or whatever name Aldric buried before he came to us."

"Correct."

"That should slow you down."

"It has."

"Not enough."

Roan didn't answer immediately.

The efficient move was to test safely. Blood sample on loose stone. Observe response. Then perhaps Brendt's blood as control? No. Brendt was injured and contamination trace remained. Ines? Contractually complicated and ethically noisy. Drogan? The fountain had already marked him as debt-bearing. Using him as a control near a blood-locked temple was scientifically impolite.

Roan could use his own blood.

That was the point.

The trap, if trap, had been built for him or someone like him. Refusing to interact would preserve safety and lose the first clean link to Aldric's origin. Interaction risked activation, identity exposure, or worse: the temple accepting him.

He disliked doors that opened.

Closed doors were simpler.

Ines's lens clicked softly as she recorded the stele. "There's a lower inscription. Almost hidden."

Roan shifted his angle.

At the base of the slab, beneath the ash line, seven small marks had been carved into the stone. He brushed ash away with the tip of his standard knife, not his hand.

The marks emerged.

This time the Shard translated immediately.

[Await the remnant that walks backward through harm.]

No one spoke.

The Vance Pendant became so cold it burned against Roan's sternum.

[Lineage challenge active.]

[Recognition risk: severe.]

[Recommendation: withdraw or proceed decisively.]

Withdraw or proceed decisively.

That wasn't guidance. That was philosophy with brackets.

A sound came from the top of the steps.

Stone grinding on stone.

The sealed door didn't open. A vertical slit appeared beside it, too narrow for a person, wide enough for a voice.

Something breathed behind the door.

Not a Husk. Not human.

The voice that followed was rough and layered, then shaped around consonants English didn't want.

Roan understood it anyway.

"Blood-mark," it said. "Prove."
