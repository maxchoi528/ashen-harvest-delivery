Chapter 28: Stabilization

The training grounds behind the secondary barracks had been abandoned for three months. Nobody questioned why.

Roan found the collapsed section of fence, slipped through, and walked the overgrown path to the old sparring circle. The ground here was packed hard by decades of students who had graduated, left, or died in Rifts. The weeds didn't grow as thick because of it, some old habit in the soil.

He placed his hand against the stone pillar at the circle's edge. Solid. Good.

Drogan would be at the main hall for morning inspection for another forty minutes. Brendt had class until noon. The groundskeeper started his rounds at the east dormitories and never circled back until the afternoon bell.

Forty minutes was enough.

He shed his jacket, folded it against the pillar. The morning was cold enough that his breath came in thin clouds, but the chill was clean. Focused. He preferred it to the artificial warmth of the dormitories.

The black iron anchor sat heavy against his chest, a warmth that had become ordinary over the past weeks. He didn't need it to be comfortable. He needed it to work.

The equation left little room for comfort. He'd been Tempered for nineteen days. The Bloodscourge aftereffects had finished cycling through his system by day three, and since then his Vitalis had climbed at a steady forty units per day, the Inversion's background efficiency converting the residual strain of the breakthrough into usable energy. No dramatic surges. Just a slow, compounding tide.

Current count: approximately 760 Vitalis.

Standard for a Tempered student at the twenty-day mark: 250.

The gap was substantial. It was also invisible, because he controlled how much the Codex Shard reported, and the Shard was careful about what it showed the world.

But that gap was a number. Numbers could be closed.

The problem wasn't potential. The problem was activation speed.

He closed his eyes. Drew a breath. Let his Vitalis gather in his core, not the dramatic full-body eruption that Cyrus Ashford performed in the training halls, but a precise, compact sphere held two inches behind his navel. Efficient. Minimal heat signature. Minimal waste.

Then he released it all at once, channeling through his meridians in the pattern Drogan had drilled into him until his fingers ached.

*First strike.*

The Sevenfold Bleed formula activated. Not the full seven paths—he wasn't ready for that, and the cost would be ruinous at his current capacity. Just one path. The first. A single channel of self-inflicted damage, designed to push blood and Vitalis through the body's fastest conduit.

The pain was immediate. Bright and sharp, a line of fire threading from his left shoulder down to his fingertips. His body registered the wound as real. His nerves fired signals of distress.

The Codex Shard caught the Sidewound. The reversal was instant, a net gain of forty-one Vitalis settling into his core.

[Your father broke his collarbone mastering this. You broke a sweat. Progress. — A.V.]

Roan opened his eyes. Forty-one Vitalis for one strike. Against an average gain of forty per day, that was one day's growth in a single activation. The yield was good, better than good, given his current Conversion Rate.

But the arithmetic exposed the limitation immediately.

One activation cost roughly thirty seconds of recovery before he could safely activate again. The gross number was impressive. The net number was disappointing when he divided it against the time and pain required.

He hit the pillar with his fist. Not hard enough to break anything, but hard enough to sting.

Right.

Over a full week of training, four activations per day, that was sixteen extra Vitalis per day of actual gain, on top of the passive forty. Fifty-six per day instead of forty. Over a month: 1,680 instead of 1,200.

The number was large enough to matter, but it wasn't the number that concerned him.

The concern was that someone else might be gaining faster.

He reset. Drew breath. Activated again. The pain was becoming familiar. That was dangerous. Familiarity bred complacency, and complacency in the discipline killed people.

The second strike landed. Forty Vitalis.

The third activation was faster this time, the meridians remembering the path. Thirty-nine Vitalis. The yield was dropping. His body was adapting to the Sidewound pattern, which meant each successive activation was producing marginally less damage as his system buffered the effect faster. Adaptive resistance. Expected, but annoying.

He had one more in him before the physical risk outweighed the gain. He reached for a fourth. A hairline crack of strain whispered through the lung meridian. The message from his own body was clear: *push again, and the crack widens.*

He let the channel cool. He couldn't afford to be dull tomorrow. Drogan noticed dull. Drogan noticed everything.

Roan let the meridian channels cool, watching his breath even out in the cold air. He rolled his shoulders, testing the residual strain. Manageable. The Sevenfold Bleed wasn't tearing through him the way the first trial had; his body was learning to absorb the self-damage without cascading failure.

Small progress. The best kind.

He was about to restart when footsteps crunched on gravel behind him.

He didn't spin. Didn't tense. He turned unhurriedly and found Ines Carrow standing at the edge of the sparring circle, arms crossed, dark hair pulled back tight.

"Roan," she said.

"Ines."

"You're up early."

"Couldn't sleep."

She studied him with those cool grey eyes that assessed everything and committed to nothing. Ines Carrow was Thornwood's second-year, ranked eleventh in their cohort, and the only student at Black Spire who Roan actively respected. Not because she was strong, though she was, but because she was smart enough to stay quiet about it.

She didn't ask about the destroyed section of fence. She didn't ask why he was at an abandoned training ground at dawn. She looked at the pillar he'd been using, noted the faint cracks forming in the stone where his strikes landed, and nodded once.

"Spar?"

The offer caught him off guard. Ines didn't offer to spar. She trained alone, or with Selene, or with the senior students who outranked her. She didn't waste rounds on first-years who couldn't challenge her.

"Why?" he asked.

"You hit that pillar forty-seven times in the last twenty minutes. Your form is wrong." She stepped into the circle, movements precise. "Drogan teaches Crimson Wake channeling through the core meridian first, but you're cycling through the lung meridian. You're faster, but you're burning your lung capacity. You won't be able to hold extended exchanges."

He looked at his hands. The Sevenfold Bleed activation pulled energy through whichever channel offered the least resistance, and his lung meridian was slightly wider from the Bloodscourge aftereffects.

Ines had caught that in twenty minutes of observation. From outside the circle.

"You're Tempered," she said. It wasn't a question.

"Sixteen days ago."

"Sixteen days and you're activating Crimson-class channeling without a catalyst. That's not standard."

Nothing about him was standard. That was the point.

"Spar," she said again. "Show me."

Roan considered. Ines was ranked eleventh in their year with a Vitalis reading somewhere north of three hundred. She was faster, more experienced, and her technique was polished to a razor's edge. A fair fight would put him at a significant disadvantage.

He didn't fight fair. That was why he was still alive.

He stepped into the circle's center.

Ines was already moving. She didn't wait for him to settle, flowing forward with a low sweeping kick aimed at his left leg, testing his base. He shifted his weight, let the kick graze his calf, and answered with a palm strike aimed at her floating ribs. She deflected with her forearm, redirected his momentum, and spun into an elbow strike that he caught with an open palm.

The impact rattled through his wrist.

Fast. She was genuinely fast.

He disengaged, reset his stance, and watched her settle into a defensive posture. Waiting. Assessing. Exactly what he would do.

So he did something she wouldn't expect.

He activated the Sevenfold Bleed. One path. Small channel. Minimal damage.

The strike came from an angle his body couldn't support, deliberately so. The pain flared, the meridian screamed, and his Vitalis spiked as the Codex Shard caught the Sidewound and inverted it.

The strike that connected with her guard was faster than it should've been by forty percent.

Ines's eyes widened. Just slightly. Then she grinned, a real one, sharp and appreciative.

"There it is," she said softly.

She didn't ask what it was. She didn't demand to know how he'd accelerated his strike speed by that margin in a single heartbeat. She adjusted. Her next attack came from a shallower angle, cutting off the high line he'd used. He blocked, redirected, felt the force of her blow travel up his arm.

She was stronger. But she was also fighting him like he was worth fighting, and that changed the math.

He gave ground. Deliberately. Let her push him back across the circle, absorbing the technique she was demonstrating with each exchange. She was showing him something with every strike, her style, and Drogan's underlying logic. The way Crimson Wake wanted to be used.

It clicked.

The lung meridian cycling was wrong because it prioritized speed over stamina. Drogan's system wanted the core meridian first, which was slower but sustainable. The Sevenfold Bleed was a burst technique; it didn't need to be sustained. The lung meridian was the correct choice for his purposes.

He just needed to control the afterburn.

He planted his feet. Stopped giving ground. Caught her next combination on his forearms, absorbed the impact through his legs, and countered with a strike that threaded through her guard at the last possible moment.

It landed on her shoulder. Barely. But it landed.

Ines stopped. Tilted her head. Looked at his hand like it had grown a second finger.

"You hit me," she said.

"Sometimes."

"That wasn't a complaint. That was acknowledgment." She stepped back, rolled her shoulder where his strike had landed. "Drogan's been watching you. You know that."

"I know."

"He thinks you're hiding something."

"I'm hiding everything. That's not news."

She studied him. The morning light had shifted, warming to pale gold. Somewhere behind them, the main hall bell rang for first period.

"You want to know a secret?" she said.

Roan said nothing. Ines took that as permission.

"Thornwood has a cache. Equipment, catalysts, nothing illegal, just a supply depot the senior students maintain for field rotations. It's on the fourth sublevel of the east barracks. Combination lock, seven digits, the founding year of the Conclave." She paused. "I'm giving you the location. You owe me a proper spar when you've closed the gap."

She turned and walked toward the fence.

"Don't get caught," she said over her shoulder. "And don't tell anyone I told you. Selene would kill me."

She was gone.

Roan stood in the sparring circle, pulse settling, the morning cold settling back over his skin. He didn't react to the information immediately. He filed it under actionable intelligence and let the immediate picture clear before he tested its edges.

Ines Carrow had just handed him a resource advantage with a counter-signature attached. The logic held: she saw potential and wanted a return on her observation. He could respect that.

He gathered his jacket from the pillar, shrugged into it, and walked back through the collapsed fence.

Drogan stood at the courtyard entrance, arms folded, watching the stream of students file toward the academic wing. His eyes found Roan immediately.

Roan approached. Slow. Composed. A first-year with no reason to be elsewhere.

"Warden."

"Roan." Drogan's voice was flat. "You're up early."

"Training grounds were occupied in the main hall. I found somewhere quiet."

"I noticed. The abandoned circle. Good choice." Drogan tilted his head slightly, eyes tracking something in Roan's posture. "You're holding your right shoulder differently. Lung meridian strain."

"Slight. I'll correct it."

"You'll correct it by letting it rest for two days. I want clean form in tomorrow's group drill, not compensating patterns."

Roan inclined his head. Acknowledged. Said nothing.

Drogan held the silence for three seconds longer than comfortable.

"Carrow was out there with you," he said.

The statement hung.

Roan kept his expression neutral. "We crossed paths. She observed my training. Offered corrections."

"Corrections."

"Form adjustments. She has good eyes."

Drogan's jaw worked slightly. "Carrow doesn't offer corrections to students she doesn't respect. And she doesn't cross paths with first-years before breakfast."

"Perhaps she needed the exercise."

Drogan stared at him. Roan returned the stare with practiced blankness.

After a moment, the Warden snorted. "Get to class, Vance."

Roan walked past him, through the courtyard, into the academic wing. The hallway was crowded with students and thick with the smell of cheap coffee from the vending alcoves. He took his normal route, slipped into the lecture hall, found his seat in the third row where the sightlines were best and the exits were nearest.

Brendt looked up as he sat down. "You're late."

"Not late. Precisely on time."

"You're late relative to your usual arrival. Which means something happened."

"Everything is information," Roan said, pulling out his notepad. "Not all information is relevant."

"That's not an answer."

"No. It isn't."

Brendt stared at him, decided he wasn't getting anything else, and turned back to his notes.

Roan opened his notebook to a fresh page. Drew a line down the center. On one side, he listed what he had: a new understanding of Crimson Wake channeling, a lead on the Thornwood cache, and a sparring debt to Ines Carrow.

On the other side, he listed what he didn't have: certainty about her full motives, visibility into Drogan's knowledge base, and enough information to determine whether the cache was worth the risk of infiltration.

The tally was incomplete.

It usually was.

He set the pen down and listened to the lecturer drone about Rift classification taxonomy, but his mind was already three steps ahead, running scenarios on the east barracks sublevel, the combination lock, the probability of patrol schedules.

A tap on his desk. Brendt had slid a folded paper across.

Roan unfolded it under the desk, out of the lecturer's sight line.

*You smell like the old training ground. What were you doing out there?*

Roan wrote beneath it: *Training.*

He slid it back.

Brendt read it, wrote: *Training doesn't make you smile.*

Roan looked at the paper for a moment. He hadn't realized he'd been smiling.

He turned the page over and didn't respond.
