Chapter 22: The Greel Net

The morning air tasted like iron. Roan noted that and moved on.

Drogan's summons had arrived before dawn, a single slip of paper slipped under his door, two words in the Warden's clipped handwriting: *Training hall. Now.*

Not a request.

Brendt was still asleep in the adjacent bunk, which suited Roan just fine. He dressed in training clothes, left the Codex Shard hanging against his chest where it belonged, and walked the empty corridors of the Black Spire's eastern wing without hurry.

The training hall occupied the basement level. Three stories of reinforced stone between it and the sky. The air down here never smelled clean, sweat, old blood, the mineral tang of weapon oil. Roan had catalogued a hundred sensory details about this place during his first week. He filed none of them now. The answer came fast: Drogan didn't summon students before breakfast without reason.

The hall doors were open.

That was the first anomaly. The second was the circle of lanterns illuminating the center of the sparring floor.

And the third,

"Warden." Roan stopped at the threshold. "I didn't realize private sessions were scheduled."

Drogan stood at the circle's edge, arms crossed over his chest. His iron hand caught the lantern light, dull and heavy. Behind him, a figure Roan had never seen before: lean, grey-haired, wearing the dark blue coat of a Sentinel Bureau official.

"Roan Vance." The grey-haired man spoke first. His voice was sandpaper on glass. "I've heard interesting things about you."

Roan waited. Silence was a tool. People filled it when they were uncomfortable, and uncomfortable people made mistakes.

The man smiled without warmth. "I am Investigator Halric. The Sentinel Bureau has taken an interest in recent events at this academy. Specifically, certain rumors about unusual Vitalis readings during the entrance trials."

No reaction. The balance processed: someone had noticed the discrepancy between his public test scores and his actual performance. The Bureau was here to investigate.

"Rumors travel," Roan said. "I'm not sure what that has to do with me."

"It has everything to do with you, Mr. Vance." Investigator Halric stepped closer to the lantern circle. "You scored at the forty-third percentile during the entrance trials. Unremarkable. And yet, three weeks into term, you bested Caelum Marsh in hand-to-hand combat. A young man who scored in the ninety-first percentile."

Caelum Marsh. The name surfaced from Roan's memory, one of the upperclassmen he'd crossed paths with during that early skirmish. The one who'd underestimated a "bottom-percentile" newcomer.

"Fights aren't won by test scores," Roan said.

"No. They're not." Investigator Halric's eyes narrowed. "Which is why I'm here. The Bureau has protocols for cases involving potential falsified records, Vitalis-suppression artifacts, or prohibited training methods. You've been flagged."

Roan's jaw tightened by exactly one millimeter. A calculated response. Not the flinch of guilt, but not the blankness of someone with nothing to hide either. He needed them guessing, not certain.

"I train hard," he said. "I study the Discipline. The rest is none of your concern."

"It's very much my concern when public Vitalis records don't match observable reality." Investigator Halric produced a small crystal device from his coat, a certified truth-sphere, by the look of it. "This will determine whether you're concealing something. A simple blood test. You have nothing to worry about if your records are accurate."

Drogan hadn't moved. His expression was unreadable, but Roan caught the subtle tension in his shoulders. The Warden hadn't warned him about this. Which meant Drogan hadn't known.

The truth-sphere could detect Vitalis fluctuations, suppressed readings, foreign artifacts. It couldn't directly read the Codex Shard, but it could measure the *effects* of the Codex Shard if Roan was actively Inverting or had recently absorbed side effects.

And worse, if it detected the Codex Shard's presence against his chest, questions would escalate. The Shard looked like a cheap pendant now, but anyone with real knowledge would recognize its density, its wrongness.

"You're questioning a student's integrity based on rumors." Roan's voice stayed flat. "That's not protocol. That's harassment."

"It's investigation." Investigator Halric raised an eyebrow. "Refusing a Bureau inquiry is grounds for immediate expulsion from any academy in the Conclave."

There it was. The trap. Comply with the truth-sphere test, and the Codex Shard's presence might raise flags. Refuse, and he was out of Black Spire before he'd learned enough to matter.

Drogan finally spoke. "Investigator. The boy's a student, not a criminal."

"Which is why I'm offering him a chance to clear this up quietly." Halric's smile didn't reach his eyes. "Unless, of course, there's something he doesn't want cleared up."

The hall fell silent. Somewhere above them, the morning bells would ring soon. Students would wake, eat breakfast, head to morning drills. Roan had exactly as long as it took for someone to notice the Warden wasn't at breakfast.

He ran the calculations. Three options.

Option one: comply. Submit to the truth-sphere. If it detected the Codex Shard, escalate. If it only detected elevated Vitalis, he'd have to explain that too. Best case, they called him a late bloomer and let it go. Worst case, they confiscated the Shard and interrogated him about where he'd gotten it.

Option two: refuse and accept expulsion. Walk away clean, try to re-enroll somewhere else. But the Bureau would remember his face. His name would be flagged. Finding another academy would be harder.

Option three: buy time. Create a distraction. Disrupt the test without disrupting it directly.

His fingers twitched toward the pendant.

[Sidewound detected: none.]

The Codex Shard didn't speak in words. It spoke in a cold awareness at the base of his skull. A reminder that Inversion required actual damage. He couldn't fake a side effect to absorb.

But he could do something else.

"Before I submit to anything," Roan said slowly, "I want to understand the Bureau's actual authority here. You're investigating me based on a rumor from a fight I won. That's not probable cause. That's fishing."

Investigator Halric's smile faltered slightly. "I have authority to investigate any Vitalis-related anomaly at Conclave institutions."

"Authority to investigate. Not authority to test without consent." Roan held the man's gaze. "Article Seven of the Sentinel Protocol states that any invasive examination of a student's body or personal effects requires either signed consent or a formal tribunal ruling. Do you have a tribunal ruling, Investigator?"

He didn't.

Roan had read the Protocol from cover to cover during his first week at Black Spire. The Warden had given him a list of essential texts. He'd read them all. Most students hadn't bothered.

Investigator Halric's smile thinned into something sharper. A man recalculating. "Smart boy. You know the text. That makes this more interesting."

"This isn't over," Halric said finally.

"No," Roan agreed. "It isn't."

The Investigator turned on his heel and walked out. His footsteps echoed in the empty hall, sharp and clipped.

The lanterns swayed. Drogan exhaled slowly.

"Roan."

"Warden."

"You just made an enemy of the Sentinel Bureau." Drogan's voice was carefully neutral. "Did you know that?"

"I know." Roan didn't look away. "I also know that truth-sphere would have found things they weren't looking for. Better they leave confused than certain."

Drogan studied him. The iron hand flexed once, a mechanical grinding of gears.

"Your father would have done the same thing," the Warden said. "Probably. Harder to tell with Aldric. He liked to let people think they were winning until the knife was already in."

Roan didn't react to that either. Processing Aldric references was a skill he'd developed early. You couldn't flinch every time someone mentioned a ghost.

"The Bureau will be watching you now," Drogan continued. "More closely than before. Whatever you're hiding, and you're hiding something, boy, don't insult me by denying it, they're going to find it eventually."

"Maybe." Roan allowed himself one small shrug. "But eventually isn't today."

"And what happens when eventually comes?"

"Then I'll deal with it."

Drogan's lips pressed into a thin line. He looked, for a moment, like he wanted to say more. Instead, he gestured toward the sparring circle.

"We still have training. Since you're here." The Warden's voice shifted, dropping the weight of the Bureau, the politics, the danger. "Show me your Sevenfold Bleed. First stance."

- break -

The morning drills lasted two hours.

Sevenfold Bleed was a body-breaking technique. That was the honest description, the one the texts didn't print: every strike you landed cost you something. Every blow you took cost you more. The art assumed you had more to give than your opponents did, that the arithmetic would eventually favor endurance over damage.

Most practitioners burned out by Tempered rank. The Vitalis channels couldn't sustain the repeated strain.

Roan wasn't most practitioners.

He cycled through the first stance, *Earthbound Wound*, and felt the burn trace his left side. The technique wanted to crack his ribs outward. The Codex Shard hummed against his chest, waiting.

[Sidewound detected. Conversion successful: +14 Vitalis.]

Fourteen points. Small. Consistent. The math was good.

Drogan watched from the circle's edge, arms still crossed. The Warden's own Vitalis was a wall of pressure that Roan could feel without seeing, something older and heavier than anything he'd encountered at the academy. Forged, maybe higher. Rank that didn't announce itself.

"Again," Drogan said.

Roan cycled the stance. Again. Again. Again.

By the end, his left side was a map of bruises under his training shirt. The Codex Shard had absorbed eleven side effects in two hours. He'd gained roughly one hundred and fifty Vitalis from the session, bringing his baseline total to around two hundred and twenty. The burst during his father's cairn ritual had spiked higher momentarily, but that was a singular event, not his working threshold. Not enough to break through to Tempered, but close. Another week of sessions like this, and the breakthrough would happen naturally.

The thought was almost comforting.

"Stop." Drogan raised a hand. "You're pushing too hard. Your form is degrading."

Roan let the stance fall. His breathing was steady, but he could feel the strain in his shoulders, the tightness in his jaw. The Warden was right. He was getting sloppy.

"You're hiding something," Drogan said again. Not a question this time. "I won't ask what. But I need to know it's not going to kill you."

"It's not going to kill me."

"That's not the same as saying it's safe."

"No." Roan wiped sweat from his forehead. "It's not."

Drogan nodded slowly. The iron hand hung at his side, catching the light. "Your father used to say that safety was a luxury for people who could afford it. Everyone else made do with calculated risks."

"Did he." Flat. Not a question.

"He did." The Warden's voice softened slightly. "He also used to say that the people who cared about you deserve to know when you're taking risks on their behalf. Not asking you to stop. Just asking you to let them watch your back."

The words landed somewhere in Roan's chest. He acknowledged them without processing them fully. Later. There would be time for processing later.

"I'll keep that in mind," he said.

Drogan studied him for another moment, then turned toward the exit. "Breakfast is in twenty minutes. I suggest you eat. The morning drills are public today, and I want you there. Visible. If the Bureau is watching, let them see you training openly."

"You think that's wise?"

"I think hiding is what guilty people do." Drogan's voice carried a dry edge. "And you're many things, Roan Vance. But I've watched you for three weeks. You don't move like someone with something to hide. You move like someone who knows exactly where every threat in the room is and exactly how to handle it."

Roan said nothing. The room went quiet. "Breakfast," Drogan repeated. "Don't be late."

He left.

Roan stood alone in the training hall, surrounded by the lingering scent of sweat and lantern oil. His ribs ached. His muscles burned. The Codex Shard pulsed faintly against his chest, warm in a way that had nothing to do with Vitalis.

The tally, as always, was incomplete. The Bureau would return. The Codex Shard would eventually be noticed. Aldric's legacy would draw attention he couldn't yet afford.

But not today.

Today, he had a number: two hundred and twenty Vitalis, and climbing. He had a technique that turned damage into strength. He had a Warden who knew something was wrong but hadn't asked the right questions yet.

And he had time.

That was the variable that mattered most. Time to grow strong enough that when the Bureau came back, when the questions got harder, when the Codex Shard's presence became impossible to ignore, he would be ready.

Or he would be dead.

The columns didn't lie. But sometimes the math was incomplete.

Roan walked toward the exit, his footsteps echoing in the empty hall.

- break -

The breakfast hall was a cathedral of noise.

Three hundred students sat at long wooden tables, their voices rising and falling in waves. The smell of porridge, eggs, cured meat filled the air. Roan collected his tray and found a seat near the far wall, back to the corner, eyes on the entrances.

Brendt was already there. Of course he was. The boy had an uncanny sense for finding Roan in a crowd.

"You missed the morning briefing." Brendt slid into the seat across from him, grinning. "Cyrus Ashford challenged someone to a duel. Publicly. Over a comment about his Vitalis scores."

"Score?"

"Second year, but ambitious." Brendt leaned in, voice dropping. "The kid said Ashford's family bought their way to high rankings. Cyrus didn't take it well."

Roan chewed slowly. The information processed: Cyrus Ashford was volatile and image-conscious, then connected to House Ashford. Challenging people publicly was a pattern, not an anomaly. He'd need to watch for that.

"Anyone accept?"

"Not yet. The deadline is this afternoon." Brendt's grin faded slightly. "There's something else. I heard a rumor about the Sentinel Bureau being on campus. Investigating something."

"Investigating what?"

"That's the thing. Nobody knows exactly. But someone said they were asking questions about entrance trial scores. Comparing public records to... Something else."

Roan's expression didn't change. But internally, the calculations accelerated. The Bureau wasn't just investigating him. They were casting a wider net. Multiple anomalies. But that didn't explain how the Bureau had singled him out for a dawn interrogation. His fight with Marsh was a footnote. His entrance scores were buried in the middle percentile. To flag him this quickly, someone inside the Spire had to have leaked his name. A staff member with access to sealed records, or a student who had seen too much and talked to the wrong people. The Bureau wasn't just fishing. They had a spotter inside the walls.

"Who else?" he asked quietly.

"What?"

"What else did they ask about? Anyone else flagged?"

Brendt blinked, not expecting the question. He thought for a moment, brow furrowing.

"I... Don't know. I heard the Bureau was interested in 'anomalous readings,' plural. But no names were mentioned."

The words settled in Roan's chest. Multiple anomalies. A spotter. The Bureau's architecture was more thorough than he'd assumed.

"Roan." Brendt's voice dropped lower. "Are you in trouble?"

"No."

"You hesitated."

"I was thinking." Roan met his friend's eyes. "There's a difference."

Brendt didn't look convinced. But he also didn't push. That was the thing about Brendt Holloway, he knew when to stop asking questions. It was one of the reasons Roan tolerated his presence.

The breakfast hall continued its morning noise. Students laughed, argued, complained about instructors. Normal life, continuing as if the Bureau's shadow hadn't fallen over the Black Spire.

Roan ate slowly, methodically, his eyes tracking every entrance, every figure who lingered too long near the walls, every whisper that carried in the close air.

The Bureau would return. The duel deadline would pass. Someone inside the Spire was feeding information outward, and Roan had no idea who.

The meal ended. Morning drills approached.

He had a choice: identify the informant, or focus on his own concealment. Resources were limited. Attention was a resource.

He ran the balance one more time.

*Later*, he decided. *I'll deal with it later.*

For now, he needed to be visible. Normal. Unremarkable.

The hardest thing to fake, it turned out, was being exactly what everyone expected.

- break -

The morning drills were held in the outer courtyard, a wide expanse of packed earth flanked by weapon racks and training dummies. Two hundred students arranged themselves in rows, overseen by a half-dozen instructors.

Roan took his position in the back row, second from the left. Natural instinct: keep the exits visible, minimize the angles of attack, maintain peripheral awareness of the crowd.

Instructor Marsh, no relation to Caelum, he'd checked, called out the first drill: *Foundation Stance, forward lunge, repeat*.

Basic. Fundamental. Movement that looked simple but built the muscle memory for everything else. Roan executed it precisely, without flourish, without showing the extra strength coiled in his frame.

Beside him, a girl with red hair stumbled on her lunge. She recovered quickly, but Instructor Marsh's eyes were already on her.

"Better, Miss Vell," the instructor said, voice carrying across the courtyard. "But your balance is shifting. Again."

Vell. The name registered. House Vell, one of the old house lineages. Not a major branch, but connected to the power structure that governed the Conclave.

Roan filed the girl's name and face. Later, he'd need to know more about her. For now, she was just another variable in an increasingly complex equation.

The drills continued. Lunge, block, strike, reset. Over and over, until the movements became automatic, until the body stopped thinking and started reacting.

Roan's mind didn't stop. It never stopped. Every face in the crowd was catalogued. Every whisper was parsed for meaning. The Bureau's presence hung over the courtyard like a storm front, not here yet, but coming.

By midday, the drills ended. Students dispersed to lunch, to private training, to the library's quiet corners. Roan lingered near the weapon racks, inspecting a practice blade he had no intention of buying.

The second choice loomed.

The red-haired girl. Iris Vell, he confirmed, was walking toward the eastern dormitories. Alone. Her movements were tired, slightly off-balance. She'd been working harder than her body could sustain. Exhaustion that came from pushing toward breakthrough without the resources to support it.

*Interesting.*

Roan set down the practice blade and followed at a safe distance. Not stalking, just observing. If she was the second anomaly the Bureau was investigating, he'd need to know why. And if she wasn't, he'd wasted twenty minutes for nothing.

The arithmetic of attention. Every minute spent on her was a minute not spent on his own advancement.

But information had value too. And right now, information about the Bureau's investigation was worth more than a marginal improvement in his training.

Iris Vell entered a dormitory building. Roan stopped outside, leaning against a wall, waiting.

Five minutes later, she emerged from a side entrance. Different clothes now, casual wear, not training gear. She was heading toward the academy's eastern gate.

Roan followed.

She didn't go far. Halfway across the courtyard, she stopped. Turned. Looked directly at him.

"You're following me." Her voice was flat, tired. "Why?"

No point in denying it. She already knew.

"Curiosity," Roan said. "Yours or mine?"

Iris Vell studied him with eyes that were older than her face suggested. Dark eyes, sharp with intelligence and exhaustion. She was shorter than him by an inch, thinner than she should be, with a warrior's calluses on her palms that didn't match her aristocratic bearing.

"You're Vance," she said. "The one who beat Caelum Marsh."

"I won a fight. He lost a fight. That's all."

"That's not what people say." She tilted her head slightly. "They say you shouldn't have won. They say your scores don't match your performance. They say the Bureau is investigating you."

"People say a lot of things."

"I investigate things too." Iris Vell's voice didn't change. "I've been watching you since the entrance trials. Your Vitalis readings were suppressed. Deliberately, I think. You let yourself score low enough to avoid attention, but not so low that it seemed fake."

The words landed like stones. Roan felt the Codex Shard pulse against his chest, a cold warning. Theatre. The confession was too clean. Either she was desperate, or she was playing a game deep enough that she wouldn't blink when it cost him. House Vell had its own accounts to settle.

"And?" he said.

"And I want to know how." Her eyes didn't waver. "Because I'm doing the same thing. And I want to know if we're both going to get caught."

The courtyard was empty. Midday sun, warm on the stone. Two teenagers who had more secrets than they could carry, standing at the edge of discovery.

Roan considered the options. Iris Vell might be an ally. She might be a trap. She might be exactly what she seemed: someone else hiding the same way he was, looking for answers.

The math didn't give certainty. Only probability.

He made his choice.

"Not here," he said. "Tonight. The library's west archive, after curfew. Come alone."

"Why should I trust you?"

"You shouldn't." Roan turned away, walking toward the dormitories. "But you're going to anyway. Because you need answers more than you need safety."

Behind him, he heard her exhale. Not agreement. Not acceptance. Just the sound of someone deciding to take a risk they couldn't afford.

The footsteps faded as he walked.

Another variable entered the equation. Another unknown to solve.

The tally was getting complicated.

But complicated meant interesting. And interesting meant the Bureau wasn't the only game in play anymore.

The Bureau's net was too precise, their timing too tight. A net this finely cut wasn't cast without a hand on the other end of the line. Someone was pulling from the north.

Tonight would tell if that was a mistake.
