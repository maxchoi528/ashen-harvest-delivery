Chapter 162: Roan Shows Brendt the Letter

Roan didn't hand Brendt the letter.

Brendt's hands weren't steady, and the document mattered more than politeness. Instead Roan placed the copied pages on a rigid tray, angled them so Brendt could read without moving his left shoulder, and activated a privacy ward from the small black disk Cole had provided.

He didn't trust Cole's disk.

Naturally.

So he placed Ines's countermeasure charm beside it, then overlaid a crude signal snare of his own using wire from a broken medical tag, two coins, and a strip of sealant foil.

Brendt watched this process in silence for almost thirty seconds.

"Is that necessary?" he asked.

"No."

"Then why—"

"It's insufficient."

"Right. Forgot who I was asking."

The ward field settled around the bed in a faint shimmer. The room's sounds softened: rain, corridor footsteps, distant voices, the soft rotation of medical arrays. Brendt's monitors continued to feed through a hardline cable Roan had inspected. Dr. Seth would yell about unauthorized ward layering if she came in.

Roan had timed the visit after her third coffee, when her yelling efficiency statistically dipped.

He tapped the first page. "Read slowly. Stop if your pulse spikes."

"Can I make jokes if my pulse spikes?"

"No."

"Can I make jokes if yours does?"

"Mine won't."

"Coward."

Roan almost told him to read.

He didn't need to. Brendt began.

The letter wasn't long. Aldric had written like a man who expected every word to be found by enemies and judged by a son. There were no poetic apologies. No dramatic self-defense. No complete technical diagrams, which Roan still resented. The first page established identity. The second established history. The third named the Founding Seven as destroyers and thieves, plus beneficiaries of a system built on the broken bones of Veyrn.

Brendt read the line about Earth twice.

Then again.

His eyes lifted slowly.

"Your father wasn't from Earth."

"No."

"Not metaphorically."

"No."

"Not like Greycross people saying Ironholm rich kids live on another planet."

"No."

"Not Earth."

"Yes."

Brendt looked back at the page.

The monitor above his bed ticked up three beats per minute.

Roan watched it.

Brendt noticed. "I am allowed a small cardiovascular reaction to extraterrestrial paternity."

"Two beats would've been sufficient."

"I'll aim lower next revelation."

He continued reading.

At the paragraph describing the fall of Veyrn, Brendt stopped joking.

That was expected. Brendt used humor the way some people used shields: not to deny danger, but to stay standing while it arrived. The letter gave him too much danger in categories he had no training for. A city burned through inverted laws. Temples split open. Children with lineage marks hunted because their blood carried system keys. Founding envoys arriving as allies and leaving as executioners. Aldric running through a gate that hadn't been meant to lead to Earth, carrying a damaged Codex Shard and enough guilt to poison thirty years.

Brendt read with his jaw tight.

Roan watched his hands, his breathing, the pulse monitor.

Not his face.

Faces invited responses. Data didn't.

When Brendt reached the line *They didn't defeat us because they were stronger. They defeated us because we taught them where to stand*, he stopped for almost a minute.

"That's horrible," he said.

"Yes."

"No qualifier?"

"None."

"Good."

He read on.

The third page hurt more.

Not visibly. Aldric had not written it to hurt. That made it worse. The page explained why he had hidden the Shard, why he had married Mira without telling her the full truth, why he had built systems inside cheap metal and basement exercises and things that looked like bad inheritance because good inheritance drew predators. It explained that love had made him careless and fear had made him cruel in quieter ways. It warned Roan that revenge would be natural and understandable, plus strategically incomplete.

The last redacted line before Aldric's signature remained visible:

*If he comes for you, don't fight him as my son. Fight him as the wound he failed to close.*

Brendt stared at that line.

"He," Brendt said.

"House Greel. Most likely."

"The white-bearded man."

Roan nodded.

"One of the six."

"Yes."

"And maybe not the worst."

"Likely not."

Brendt closed his eyes.

The monitor ticked up again.

Roan waited.

If Brendt panicked, the correct response would depend on form. Verbal panic could be contained. Physical panic might require Dr. Seth. Moral panic, the kind where someone decided the situation was too large and their role too small, would require a different approach: either release him from involvement or bind him with limited information and concrete tasks.

Roan had prepared three versions.

Version one: Brendt withdraws. Roan agrees, arranges protection, limits exposure, lies where necessary. Loss: trusted ally. Gain: lower risk to Brendt. Emotional consequence: irrelevant except it wasn't, which made it tactically irritating.

Version two: Brendt stays but demands full truth. Roan refuses full technical disclosure but grants strategic overview. Risk: resentment. Manageable.

Version three: Brendt stays without understanding the scale. Unacceptable. Brave ignorance was just stupidity with better posture.

Brendt opened his eyes.

"Who else knows?"

"Drogan knows parts. Cole knows older parts. Ines knows enough to be dangerous and not enough to be satisfied. Iris suspects fragments. Greel knows the Shard exists. The Vell patriarch likely knows less than he thinks."

"Your mother?"

"No."

Brendt didn't speak for a while after that.

He reread the first page, then the second, then the sentence about Veyrn teaching its killers where to stand. His eyes moved slower each time, not from confusion but from the unpleasant instinct to look for a place where the words became less true. Roan understood the instinct without sharing it. He had tried it himself in the temple, with Aldric's untouched tea on the table and six photographs on the wall. The facts had remained impolite.

The privacy ward hummed.

Outside it, the medical wing continued its ordinary work. A cart rolled past. Someone laughed down the corridor, too young and too relieved. A healer gave instructions in a brisk voice. Normal life, separated from genocide by one door and several layers of ignorance.

Brendt finally said, "The Founding Seven built the current order after stealing from Veyrn."

"Parts of it."

"The Spires. The power classifications. The public theory."

"Some of the public theory. Not all. Systems accrete. Theft becomes curriculum after enough committees."

"Don't make committees sound scarier than murder cults."

"They have better survival rates."

Brendt stared.

"Sorry," Roan said.

"Was that an apology?"

"A tactical concession."

"I'll take it."

He looked back at the pages. "Your father's letter says the Seven didn't all agree on what to do with survivors."

"Yes."

"Which means one of them may not be actively hunting you."

"May."

"And one of them may be worse than House Greel."

"Likely."

"And the seventh writes himself." Brendt's voice lowered around the phrase. "What does that mean?"

Roan had asked the Shard the same question.

The Shard had answered with silence, then a prompt that read: [Insufficient authorship.] That had been exactly as useful as asking a locked door for architectural philosophy.

"Unknown," Roan said. "Possibilities: the seventh founder erased records of themselves; the seventh seat was never occupied; the seventh is a role created by actions rather than identity; or Aldric was warning me not to assume the list is fixed."

"Which do you think?"

"All of them are dangerous enough to plan around."

"That isn't an answer."

"It's the answer I have."

Brendt nodded once. He was learning to accept the difference between secrecy and uncertainty. Good. The two required different responses. Secrecy could be pressured. Uncertainty had to be mapped.

"And the Codex Shard," Brendt said. "It is more than a system."

"No."

"It's a key."

"At minimum."

"A weapon?"

"Sometimes."

"A person?"

Roan didn't answer immediately.

The Shard had produced Aldric's projection. It carried tone, memory fragments, warnings, classifications. It reacted to names. It withheld. It advised. It wasn't Aldric, and Roan distrusted any grief that tried to make a tool into a father because the alternative was absence.

But not a person wasn't the same as only a tool.

"Not in the way you mean," he said.

Brendt noticed the caveat. Of course he did. "That's a terrible sentence."

"Yes."

"We should put it on the list."

"There's already a list."

"Of terrible sentences?"

"Of things that might kill us."

"Same document eventually."

That wasn't wrong.

"Lyra?"

"No."

"Good."

Roan looked at him.

Brendt looked back. "What? That was the correct answer. Your sister is fourteen. Your mother has already survived enough of your family's secrets to deserve a break."

"You are accepting that quickly."

"I was nearly murdered by antler monsters, Greel contractors, and a building. My standard for quickly has changed."

"Buildings usually count as terrain."

"That building had intent."

"Fair."

Brendt shifted and winced, then stopped. "What do you need?"

There it was.

Not *Do you want revenge?* Not *Are you all right?* Not some heroic promise about standing together until the end. Brendt had skipped the theater and gone to logistics.

Exposure developed resistance indeed.

Roan sat back. "You should ask what this costs you before offering."

"I know what it costs."

"No, you don't."

"Fine. I know enough to start. More than I knew before I followed you into a Tier Three Rift, less than I need, exactly as usual."

"Brendt."

"Roan."

A nurse passed outside the door. Her shadow crossed the frosted glass and moved on.

Brendt lowered his voice. "You spent your last inversion on me. Don't make that decision stupid by pretending I'm going back to being the guy who carries packs and complains about insurance exclusions."

"You are very good at carrying packs."

"I am amazing at it. Legendary. But not the point."

"Your combat role will be restricted for weeks."

"Then use my other talents."

"You have other talents?"

"I am wounded and emotionally processing cosmic genocide. Be nicer."

"No."

"Worth asking."

Roan looked at the letter.

Tasks. Concrete tasks kept fear from becoming fog. Brendt needed tasks. Roan needed capacity. Their needs overlapped.

"You can audit equipment procurement," Roan said. "Not weapons. Infrastructure. Safehouses, transport, medical reserves, redundant communications. We have been operating like students with expensive knives. That's no longer sufficient."

Brendt's expression sharpened by degrees. "Budget?"

"Tournament winnings, salvage payout if Cole doesn't steal it administratively, recovered mercenary assets, and whatever Ines can launder without making me regret knowing her."

"So a lot and not enough."

"Correct."

"Legal exposure?"

"High."

"Moral exposure?"

"Define moral."

"Never mind. Operational exposure?"

"Extreme."

Brendt nodded slowly. "We need a ledger separate from school accounts. Supplies stored outside campus. A legitimate front—Rift salvage consultation after your public expedition identity becomes harder to hide. Medical access that doesn't depend on Dr. Seth liking your terrible face. Emergency exit from Ironholm. Two. One legal. One not."

Roan watched him.

Brendt continued, gaining speed despite the exhaustion. "Lyra and Mira need distance without knowing why. Not panic distance. Opportunity distance. Something that looks like school advancement or health retreat. If they resist, use money. If they ask questions, don't answer, which you are already tragically good at. Drogan needs a secure channel. Ines needs contract limits written by someone less emotionally dead than you but more paranoid than normal people, which is unfortunately me now."

The pulse monitor ticked up.

Roan let it. He was mapping the sudden architecture of Brendt's reorganization. The pack business was just the visible layer; Brendt had always organized more than he showed. Adrenaline didn't invent competence. It just burned away the habit of hiding it.

"And Iris?" he asked.

Brendt's face changed.

"Iris needs a box."

"Explain."

"Not prison. Options. Three choices, all bad for her patrons, one survivable for her. If she has only one way out, she'll betray you because people hate being cornered. If she has too many, she'll optimize for herself and accidentally ruin us. Give her three. She'll think choosing one means she won."

Roan stared at him.

"That," he said, "is almost competent."

Brendt closed his eyes. "I'm too injured to enjoy the compliment. Please store it for later."

"No."

"Cruel."

"Efficient."

For the first time since returning from the Rift, something in Roan's chest loosened by a measurable fraction. Not relief. Relief implied the problem had decreased. It hadn't. If anything, showing Brendt the letter had expanded the problem to include another life with full knowledge attached.

But it had also converted Brendt from protected liability to informed participant.

Dangerous.

Useful.

Chosen.

That last word tried to become sentimental. Roan filed it under *later* and locked the drawer.

Brendt opened his eyes again. "Are you going to tell me the parts you redacted?"

"No."

"Good."

Roan paused. "Good?"

"If you told me everything immediately, it would mean either you were panicking or you had lost all standards. Both would scare me more than ancient alien revenge families."

"Veyrn wasn't alien in its own frame of reference."

"Roan."

"What?"

"Don't terminology-correct my trauma."

"Noted."

"Thank you."

Brendt's planning didn't stop there.

That was the problem with giving a practical person a terrifying truth. After the first shock, he started building shelves for it.

"We also need social cover," he said, voice thinner now but still moving. "You are famous after the tournament. Famous people can't disappear without people making stories. So we give them stories first. Publicly, you become what they already half-think you are: the poor genius who got addicted to Rift salvage because he wants better gear and hates sponsors. That's believable. It explains money, injuries, weird materials and Ines, plus Drogan yelling."

"Cole suggested similar rumor noise."

"Cole is competent?"

"Unfortunately."

"Good. Use him, but don't let him own the story. Administrators love owning stories. Then they start editing people."

Roan looked at him again.

"What?" Brendt said.

"You are more useful under medication than several conscious adults."

"Store that compliment too."

"No."

"Monster."

His eyelids dipped, then forced themselves open. "Second, we need a normal reason for me to stay near you after recovery. If I'm just your wounded friend, enemies use me as pressure. If I'm your quartermaster, attacking me looks like attacking your operations and maybe your money. People respect money more than friendship."

"Often."

"Third, we make my weakness expensive to exploit. Visitors logged. Messages screened. Public schedule false. Physical therapy in rotating rooms. I want a shield in here when Seth allows it. Not a training shield. Real one."

"You can barely lift your hand."

"Then it can sit beside the bed and make me feel less ornamental."

That was a reason Roan understood less, but didn't dismiss. Morale wasn't sentiment. Morale was the part of a person that decided whether pain was a fact or an instruction.

"Done," Roan said.

"Fourth—"

"Sleep."

"I have more."

"Write it later."

"My writing hand is attached to the side currently reinvented by medicine."

"Dictate later."

"You will edit out the human parts."

"Yes."

"Then get someone else."

"Ines will add crimes."

"Drogan will add knives."

"Cole will add committees."

"Seth?"

"Seth will add medical compliance."

Brendt looked horrified. "You. Fine. But no deleting jokes."

"One joke per page."

"Three."

"One."

"Two and one sarcastic footnote."

"Done."

He seemed satisfied by that, which proved the medication was working.

The letter pages remained between them, heavy despite being copies. Roan gathered them carefully, one at a time. Brendt watched the motion with clearer eyes than he should have had.

"You're afraid I'll look at you differently," Brendt said.

Roan's hand paused for less than a second.

"No."

"Liar."

"Careful."

"I am in a hospital bed. You can't threaten me until Seth leaves."

"Seth isn't my only option."

"There. Normal."

Roan placed the final page into the case. "How you look at me has limited operational impact."

"That's both true and completely false."

"Pick one."

"No. Humans contain contradictions. Even you, despite your best efforts."

Roan sealed the case.

Brendt's voice softened, losing the joke edge. "I don't know what you are now. Veyrn, Earth, Shard, whatever. I know what you did when I was dying. I know what you didn't do when Iris walked away. I know you are an absolute bastard about bedside manner. That's enough data for today."

Roan didn't answer.

There were responses available. Most were bad. Acknowledgment risked sentiment. Deflection would be noticed. Insult would be comfortable but cheap.

He chose silence.

Brendt accepted it, which was why he had been given the letter in the first place.

Roan stayed seated until the breathing pattern stabilized.

The medical arrays dimmed one by one into night mode. Outside the window, Black Spire's wardlights moved across the rain like slow search beams. Roan counted the sweeps automatically. Seven seconds between western pulses. Eleven between the higher towers. A blind patch near the old library roof lasting three seconds if one crossed from the maintenance gantry.

He wrote that down, then crossed it out.

This wasn't the time to plan rooftop movement.

He also stayed because leaving immediately after disclosure would teach the wrong lesson.

Brendt would never phrase it that way. He would make some joke about abandonment, involving Roan invoicing emotional labor at criminal rates. But jokes didn't erase the underlying arithmetic. If Roan showed him a dead civilization, a hunted bloodline, and a role in the machinery of survival, then walked out the moment Brendt became inconveniently unconscious, the message would be clear: information shared, burden transferred, friendship optional.

That wasn't the message Roan wanted.

*Wanted* was a suspicious word.

He let it stand.

A knock came at the door.

Brendt's eyes flicked toward the case one last time.

"Hide it well," he murmured.

"Obviously."

"No. Not obviously. People hide precious things where fear tells them to. Hide it where boredom tells them not to look."

Roan paused.

"That," he said, "was useful."

"Store it with the compliments."

"Your storage instructions are becoming demanding."

"Near-death privilege."

"Temporary."

"Then I will abuse it efficiently."

That sounded like something Roan would have said, which was concerning on several levels. He decided recovery would need to include social quarantine from himself. Brendt had enough injuries, and personality contamination would be harder to bill to school insurance.

Roan collected the pages before answering. "Who?"

"Seth," said the chief healer from outside. "Open before I override your amateur ward stack and make fun of the wiring."

Brendt whispered, "She noticed."

"Obviously."

Roan sealed the letter copy back into the case, collapsed the wire snare, pocketed Ines's charm, and deactivated Cole's disk. Then he opened the door.

Dr. Seth stood outside with a tray, two assistants, and the expression of a woman who had found incompetence in her house and intended to bill it rent.

Her eyes went to Brendt first, then the monitors, then Roan's pockets.

"Secrets later," she said. "Medicine now."

"He's stable," Roan said.

"He's stable because I bullied his organs into cooperation. Don't take credit."

Brendt, traitor that he was, smiled faintly.

Seth entered and replaced two infusions while her assistants checked the graft film. Roan moved aside but didn't leave. Seth allowed this, which meant either she approved of his presence or had decided removing him would waste energy better spent preventing death.

When she finished, she looked at Roan. "You. Sit in that chair or get out. Standing like a corpse with opinions is bad for my patients."

Roan sat.

Brendt's eyes were already drifting under the medication.

Before sleep took him, he turned his head slightly.

"Roan."

"Yes."

"I always knew you weren't normal."

Roan waited.

Brendt's mouth moved into a crooked, exhausted smile.

"I just didn't know how cool not-normal could be."

The medicine pulled Brendt under slowly rather than all at once.

Before it did, he insisted on one more practical matter because apparently near-death had improved his sense of timing but not his sense of restraint.

"The letter," he murmured. "If something happens to you."

"It won't."

"Roan."

"There are copies."

"Where?"

"You don't need to know yet."

"Good. Tell me one thing I do need to know."

Roan considered.

"If I disappear for more than forty-eight hours without a coded message, you send the sealed packet marked *Orchard* to Lyra's school counselor, not Lyra. Counselor first. She will know it concerns scholarship transfer."

Brendt's eyes opened a little wider despite the sedative. "You planned evacuation through scholarships."

"Opportunity distance. Your term."

"I said that five minutes ago."

"It was useful."

"You are stealing from the injured."

"Efficiently."

"Good. What code?"

"If the message says the weather in Greycross is better than Ironholm, it's mine and safe. If it says anything sentimental, assume coercion."

Brendt smiled faintly. "That's the most secure code phrase I have ever heard."

"Because sentiment would be suspicious?"

"Because everyone who knows you would believe it."

Then Seth's medicine finally won, and Brendt's eyes closed.

Roan stayed seated until the breathing pattern stabilized.

Then he fell asleep.

In the quiet between shifts, the signal snare in his pocket registered a soft, patterned pulse. Cole's disk. A query. Someone testing the connection. He filed the anomaly under *tomorrow*.

Dr. Seth looked at Roan. "Don't smile. You'll reopen something."

Roan didn't smile.

Not where anyone could use it.
