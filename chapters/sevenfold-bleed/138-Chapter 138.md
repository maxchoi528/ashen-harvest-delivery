Chapter 138: The Descent

Crimson Wake wasn't a technique designed for confined spaces.

Roan knew that before he used it. Knowing didn't improve the situation. The pump house had become a box full of bad decisions: one breached door, one false exit leading into a culvert that sounded crowded, two roof openings, three compromised floor sections, a damaged shield frame, one missing rifle, and enough coordinated Husks outside to make retreat a negotiation rather than a movement.

A normal practitioner would have widened the room with brute force, blasted through a wall, or trusted Drogan to solve the problem by being Drogan. Roan considered all three options in the time it took the first Husk to cross the threshold.

Blasting through a wall risked opening into more Husks or collapsing the building. Trusting Drogan meant spending the old man's strength before they reached the real objective. Fighting conventionally gave the pack more time to herd them toward the culvert.

So Roan used blood.

The cut across his left palm was shallow by medical standards and sufficient by ritual ones. Crimson Wake didn't require much blood to begin with. That was the problem. Techniques that asked for a little blood usually wanted the rest later.

The Husk at the doorway came low, all elbows and mouth, using the corpse of the door as partial cover. Brendt shifted to meet it, shield angled right. Good instinct. Wrong target.

A second Husk unfolded above the frame, claws hooked into the broken lintel, waiting for Brendt's shield to commit.

Roan stepped past him.

"Down," he said.

Brendt dropped without arguing.

That saved his face.

Roan's bloody hand closed over Sidewound's grip. Pain climbed through his palm as the blade's channel drank the shape of the wound, not the blood itself. It tasted the injury, recognized the structural failure, and tried to make a conversation out of it.

The Codex Shard opened.

[Crimson Wake vector confirmed.]
[Self-inflicted vascular stress accepted.]
[Invert?]

Yes.

For a heartbeat, the cut in his palm became everything.

Not larger. More exact. Roan felt each separated capillary, each torn line of skin, the little pressure loss inside his hand, the body's automatic panic sealing the wound before it became costly. The Shard seized that reaction and turned it inside out. Blood didn't leave him. It remembered leaving, and that memory sharpened into a red line along Sidewound's edge.

[Daily Capacity: 5/5 → 4/5]
[Crimson Wake initialized.]
[Environmental load detected. Repeated vascular response may accelerate fine motor fatigue.]

The first cut crossed the doorway.

It didn't look dramatic.

One of the things Roan liked about it.

No wave of red light burst across the room. No heroic arc split the air. Sidewound moved from low left to high right, and every Husk whose movement had placed stress through an open seam discovered, briefly and terminally, that their bodies had been audited by someone with no interest in extensions.

The one at the threshold came apart through shoulder and throat, plus core line. The one above the lintel lost three limbs and most of its spine. Two more outside, half-visible through the doorway, split at the joints as their own charge carried them through the Wake's edge.

For one breath, the entrance cleared.

Then the room punished him for using a line technique in a box.

The Wake rebounded.

Not physically. The cut didn't bounce off walls. The problem was worse. Crimson Wake followed blood-stress and movement vectors. In the pump house, every ally had a pulse, an injury, and a motion line. Brendt's shoulder abrasion. Ines's shallow scrape from the roof debris. Drogan's cut forearm. Roan's own ribs, still complaining from the earlier claw strike.

The technique looked for all of them.

Roan had anticipated that.

Mostly.

He twisted his wrist half a degree and drove Sidewound point-first into the floor.

The blade bit through dirty stone and found the seam beneath, the same seam the Husks had used to break upward. Roan fed the Wake downward instead of outward, giving it a larger wound to follow. The floor cracked from door to drainage channel in a jagged red-veined line.

The crowded wet sound from the culvert became a collective scream.

Good.

Roan pulled the blade free.

His left palm had sealed too fast. The skin was pale around the cut, bloodless and numb.

Less good.

He flexed his fingers. The smallest finger lagged by a fraction.

Annoying, not disabling.

The air in the pump house tasted wrong—old and loaded with something that clung to the lung. The Shard had already logged it.

Drogan killed the next Husk before it reached the doorway, not with a flourish but with a short thrust through the skull hinge. His eyes flicked once to Roan's hand.

"Functional?" he asked.

"Four fingers are enthusiastic. One is negotiating."

"Bad time for jokes."

"Then stop asking bad questions."

Brendt rose behind his shield, saw the bodies outside, and made a sound that wasn't a laugh. "You cleared the door."

"Temporarily."

"Of course. Temporarily. Wouldn't want hope getting comfortable."

Ines slid under the pump casing and retrieved her rifle case with one hand. "Below is still active."

"Yes," Roan said.

"Also angry."

"Expected."

The floor split again near the drainage channel.

Roan threw his spare flare stone into the crack before anything emerged. Light burst below, revealing the culvert through broken stone. Pale bodies writhed underneath, stacked in wet layers. Not dozens. More than dozens. Husks packed shoulder to shoulder in the black maintenance tunnel, waiting for the team to drop into them.

Brendt looked down and went very still.

"I would like to formally withdraw my previous vote," he said.

"Already recorded," Ines said.

The Husks below began climbing.

Drogan moved to the door. "We leave now. Not through the culvert."

"Door route has active pack," Ines said.

"Less active than below."

Roan looked past Drogan into the street.

The first Crimson Wake had bought them a corridor, but only that. Husks still clung to the outer walls and adjacent roofs, their eyeless heads turning toward the pump house. Some were damaged. Others waited. Too coordinated. Too patient.

They hadn't come merely to feed.

They had wanted the team underground.

Which meant the street was safer than the route the enemy preferred. Not safe. Safer. The distinction was enough.

"Formation," Roan said. "Drogan front. Brendt behind him. Ines center. I take rear until the roofline shifts."

Drogan's mouth tightened. He didn't object.

Brendt shifted his shield. The frame whined. One hinge had bent inward during the door impact.

Roan noted it. "Your left brace is compromised. Don't catch overhead strikes on it."

"I will inform the Husks."

"Do it briefly."

Ines fired a shot through the roof breach. Something outside fell with a heavy slap. "Route marker line still pings east. North markers unreliable."

"East takes us back toward the entry," Drogan said.

"Good," Brendt said.

"Not out," Drogan added. "Forward camp."

"Less good."

The floor cracked wider.

Roan turned, drew Sidewound low, and cut the nearest emerging Husk across the fingers. The blade found the seam, removed its grip, and the creature slid back into the glowing mass below. Others climbed over it.

Not enough time.

He considered a second inversion. Crimson Wake again would solve the immediate breach and create unacceptable hand damage, especially with whatever hung in the air. Pyrebrand would increase output but risk organ strain in the same contaminated environment. Sevenfold overload was too broad for the room. Sidewound alone remained best.

Boring, but best.

"Move," Drogan said.

They moved.

They had made twenty meters before Roan understood the pack's second lesson.

The Husks didn't pursue like animals. Animals chased the nearest motion, broke formation around obstacles, and lost discipline once blood entered the air. These creatures reacted to injuries as a shared resource. A crippled Husk didn't drag itself after them. It crawled into the path of the next one and became cover. A blinded Husk didn't flail. It lowered its body across a narrow passage and forced Brendt to step over or spend time smashing it aside. The pack converted damage into terrain.

That was clever.

Roan hated clever enemies on principle. They wasted more time.

"Don't finish them unless they block movement," he said.

Brendt, panting behind the half-bent shield, glanced back. "That feels morally inverted."

"Dead bodies are furniture. Living bodies improvise."

"You can make any sentence worse by explaining it."

"Then move."

Drogan took them through a loading arch beside the old market instead of the main avenue. A hanging sign creaked overhead, its letters scraped away except for a single curved mark Roan didn't recognize. Ines noticed it too and looked as if she wanted to stop for a rubbing. Roan caught her sleeve and pulled her along before greed could vote.

"I saw it," she said.

"Yes."

"It may be relevant."

"So is breathing."

"You are becoming hostile to scholarship."

"Only scholarship with teeth behind it."

A Husk sprang from behind a stall and hit Drogan from the left. It had hidden under a sheet of petrified cloth, body compressed flat enough that Roan had mistaken it for debris. Drogan didn't turn toward it. He drove his elbow backward, caught the creature in the throat, and continued moving while its claws scraped harmlessly across his shoulder guard. Roan cut its trailing knee seam as he passed. The joint failed. The Husk collapsed into the path of two others.

No kill. Better delay.

Sidewound warmed in his hand, approving or feeding on exposed structure. Roan chose to interpret it as data, not mood. Objects with moods encouraged poor decisions.

The pack clicked again from three directions.

One. Three. One.

Then a lower sequence from the roofs.

Four. One. Four.

"They're relaying," Ines said.

"How many groups?" Drogan asked.

"At least three. Maybe four. Hard to separate echoes."

"Assume four."

"I was already assuming five," Roan said.

Drogan looked at him over his shoulder. "Why five?"

"Because four is what they want us to count."

"Paranoid."

"Recently rewarded."

They cut through a storage hall whose roof had fallen in sections, leaving bands of pale light across the floor. Roan avoided the bands after the first dead rat skeleton inside one cast a shadow in the wrong direction. He didn't know what would happen if one stepped into it. That was enough. Drogan saw the adjustment and shifted route without asking, which saved sixteen words and Brendt's other boot.

At the far exit, the floor sloped down under a collapsed balcony. A gap remained beneath, just large enough for a crouched person. Too small for Brendt's shield. Too narrow for Drogan's shoulders if he kept his pack.

Ines pointed right. "Alternate door."

Roan saw the door. Clean handle.

"No."

Drogan's answer overlapped his. "No."

Brendt looked from one to the other. "I appreciate the consensus and dislike the implications."

Roan slung his pack forward and dropped to one knee beside the gap. "Packs through first. Brendt's shield last. Ines covers roof."

"That leaves us compressed," Drogan said.

"For nine seconds. Door leaves us stupid forever."

No one argued.

The gap smelled of wet ash and old oil. Roan pushed Brendt's spare pack through, then Ines's rifle case. A claw scraped stone somewhere behind them. Ines fired once. A body hit the floor. Drogan shoved Brendt down next, not gently.

"Ankle," Brendt hissed.

"Keep it," Drogan said.

Brendt crawled.

The balcony above shifted.

Roan looked up and saw a Husk clinging to the underside of the broken slab, its limbs spread flat, its mouth open without sound. It had been waiting for the last person through.

For him.

Better.

Roan didn't draw back. He thrust Sidewound upward through the narrow space between balcony supports. Bad angle, shallow contact without room for power. The blade touched the creature's chest seam and slid off.

The Husk dropped.

Roan let it fall onto his left shoulder.

Pain flashed as its claws bit into his coat and scraped the Vance Pendant's hidden edge beneath his shirt. The pendant spread the pressure, not the puncture. One claw found skin above his collarbone.

[Minor trauma detected.]
[Invert?]

No.

He drove his head back into the creature's jaw, not to stun it, which would've been optimistic, but to force its throat line open. Sidewound cut across that line half a breath later. The Husk came apart enough to stop being a problem.

He shoved the corpse aside and slid through the gap as the balcony collapsed behind him.

Dust rolled over the passage.

Brendt stared at the blood on Roan's collar. "You are hit."

"Shallow."

"You say that about wounds that would make normal people reconsider careers."

"Normal people chose poor careers."

Drogan grabbed Roan's collar, checked the puncture with two fingers, and released him. "Clean enough. Move."

Clean enough wasn't clean. Roan added it to the list.

The first ten meters outside the pump house were almost clean. Almost was the important word. Roan counted six corpses from the Wake, three twitching remains, one Husk pretending to be a corpse badly, and two roofline silhouettes tracking their pace.

He killed the pretending corpse with a downward stab through the spine seam as they passed.

[Husk tissue destabilized.]
[Yield: negligible.]

The Shard was developing a tone.

They crossed the dry canal bridge at a jog. The bridge had no railing, because apparently the Hollowed City believed in architectural hostility. The canal below was a black slot filled with mist and the sound of claws. Brendt kept his shield angled toward the left wall, breathing hard but controlled. Ines dropped two decoy markers behind them. Drogan didn't look back.

The pack followed.

Clicking chased them across the stone.

One. Three. One.

Then two. Two. Four.

New pattern.

Roan didn't like new patterns.

"They changed signal," he said.

Drogan grunted. "They know we didn't take the culvert."

"So command intelligence confirmed."

"Yes."

"You could have opened with that."

"Run faster."

A Husk dropped from the right-hand building onto the bridge ahead of Brendt. It landed wrong, too far forward, overcommitted, likely driven by command pressure rather than instinct. Brendt saw it and did what he had learned after months of being hit by people better than him.

He didn't block.

He stepped in.

The damaged shield frame caught the Husk under the jaw and drove upward. The creature's head snapped back. Ines shot it through the exposed throat core before it recovered.

"Good," Roan said.

Brendt sounded genuinely offended. "Now?"

"Yes."

"This is when you choose positive reinforcement?"

"It was efficient."

"I hate that it worked."

The bridge shuddered.

Something below struck one of the supports.

Drogan accelerated.

The far side of the bridge opened into a narrow market street filled with stalls that had petrified in place. Cloth awnings hung stiff as metal sheets. Bowls and crates, plus old hand scales sat on tables under layers of ash. Roan saw a line of ceramic cups along one stall, all perfectly clean.

"Left," Drogan said immediately.

They turned left.

A Husk slammed into the space they would have entered if they had continued straight. It came through the awning, folded around a vertical pole, and snapped its mouth open silently.

Clean cups as lure markers.

Roan added it to the list.

The street narrowed. Roofs leaned closer. Bad for falling enemies. Good for limiting numbers. Drogan chose it anyway, which meant he valued the second quality more. The old man was still rationing strength. Sensible. Also irritating.

The pack tested them three times in the next hundred meters.

First from above: Ines spotted the shadow through a reflection in a broken metal tray and shot before it dropped.

Second from behind: Roan cut the lead Husk's knee seam, let its body block the ones behind it, and used no further effort.

Third from below: a claw punched through a drainage grate and caught Brendt's boot.

That one mattered.

Brendt reacted fast, chopping down with the edge of his shield. The claw didn't release. It tightened. The grate cracked. Another hand emerged, then another, pale fingers hooking around his ankle brace.

Roan was closest.

He could cut the claw. Easy.

He could also cut the boot strap and leave the claw holding leather. Faster.

He cut the strap.

Brendt stumbled free with one boot half-loose, and the Husk below dragged an empty heel guard into the grate with enough force to bend iron.

"My boot," Brendt said.

"Alive people can buy boots."

"I liked that boot."

"Then mourn while moving."

They moved.

The forward camp lay in an old signal station near the outer ring, according to Drogan. Reinforced, warded, used by licensed teams as a temporary rally point. Roan had doubts about all three adjectives.

The haze thickened as they pushed deeper, carrying a faint chemical bite that made the edges of thought feel worn. The exposure risk arrived three streets later. It didn't announce itself with pain. Pain would've been considerate. Instead, Roan lost the name of the street he had just crossed. Not important by itself, the street had no useful name in the first place, but the absence had edges. He remembered the leaning wall, the broken awning, the Husk corpse with its jaw split sideways. He remembered telling Brendt to step over a wire. He remembered Ines cursing at a marker that dissolved in her hand.

The sequence remained. The label vanished.

Minor load, then. Memory surface, not core.

He wrote one word on his glove with a charcoal nub while moving: *names*.

Brendt noticed because Brendt noticed the wrong things at useful times. "Did you just take notes while running from monsters?"

"Yes."

"About what?"

"Forgetting."

"That's a bad topic to forget."

"Hence the note."

Drogan's shoulders tightened ahead of them. He had heard. Good. Roan wanted him to hear without stopping for another conversation that would start with no and end with more secrets. Crimson Wake had cost more than blood—the exposure it demanded was measurable in seconds he couldn't spare. Not much this time. Enough to respect.

A Husk struck from the left through a second-floor window, using the frame as a spring. Roan could have killed it with Sidewound. Instead he grabbed a rusted hanging chain from a stall and pulled.

The chain released a collapsed awning support.

The Husk hit the falling support midair and tangled, then crashed into a display of fossilized baskets. It would recover in seconds. Seconds were enough.

"You didn't kill it," Ines said.

"Killing spends time."

"You are having a very educational breakdown of priorities."

"Invoice later."

They didn't take the next right because a clean lantern burned in the alley mouth. No flame source, no fuel line without heat shimmer. Roan pointed left, Drogan adjusted, and Brendt limped faster without asking why.

That was progress too.

The Hollowed City punished optimism. It punished pessimism too, but at least pessimism looked less surprised.

They reached the signal station as the gray dark thickened into something nearly black.

It stood on a raised platform overlooking a split rail yard, a squat building with a collapsed antenna tower leaning across its roof. Unlike the pump house, it bore modern additions: fresh ward stakes, steel shutters, a coded lock, emergency lamps, and three layers of warning tape.

Human maintenance.

Recent.

Roan liked it immediately less.

Drogan reached the door and pressed his hand to the lock.

The lock flashed red.

Ines swore quietly.

Drogan tried again.

Red.

Behind them, clicking gathered in the market street.

Brendt shifted his shield. His loose boot scraped stone.

Roan looked at the lock, then at Drogan. "Your access expired."

"Shouldn't have."

"Reality has been disappointing all day."

Ines moved beside the panel. "Give me ten seconds."

"You have four," Drogan said.

"Then give me better tools."

Roan stepped past her and drove Sidewound into the gap between door and frame.

The blade didn't cut the lock. It found the stress seam where the modern ward plate sat over older metal. Roan twisted, carefully. Too much force would trigger emergency sealing. Too little would do nothing.

The first Husk appeared at the far end of the street.

Then four more.

Ines watched his hand. "That isn't how locks work."

"It's today."

The seam opened.

The door clicked.

Drogan grabbed the handle and hauled it wide. "Inside."

Brendt went first this time, shield raised. Ines followed. Roan entered third. Drogan came last and slammed the door as the first Husk hit the platform.

The ward line activated.

Blue light crawled over the frame.

The Husk struck the door and stopped dead, smoke rising from its hands.

For three seconds, nobody spoke.

Then Brendt lowered himself onto the floor and said, very calmly, "I am going to pass out now. Please schedule any objections for later."

He didn't pass out.

That would've been too convenient.

Instead, he pulled off his damaged boot and revealed the wound.

Three thin punctures marked his ankle where the claw had pierced through the brace before Roan cut him free. The skin around them was already gray.

Drogan's face changed.

Not much.

Enough.

Roan crouched beside Brendt and opened the medical kit.

"That looks bad," Brendt said.

"Yes."

"You considered lying there."

"Briefly."

"Terrible friendship."

Roan cut away the sock.

The gray spread another millimeter.

The Codex Shard pulsed.

[Husk contamination confirmed.]
[Inversion capable.]
[Current conditions: suboptimal.]
[Projected outcome: uncertain—dependent on available resources.]

Roan looked at the wound.

Brendt watched his face.

Outside, the Husks began clicking again.

One. Three. One.

Inside the signal station, Roan did the arithmetic and didn't like the sum.
