Chapter 193: Three Years

The second door was under a bakery.

Roan decided, after seventeen seconds of staring at the cracked green awning and the hand-painted sign promising rye loaves, meat pies, and other forms of civilian optimism, that the old world had possessed a sense of humor. Not a good one. Not an intentional one. But humor all the same.

A city-scale hunt field had been anchored into Ironholm's northern ward lattice by a Throne-tier murderer with a white beard and an expensive sense of entitlement, and the nearest useful countermeasure apparently required them to break into a closed bakery at midnight.

That figured.

The street around them was narrow and old, and badly lit. Most of Ironholm's central districts hid their age behind black glass and ward-light, but the northern terraces had been built before the Spire expansion and had never forgiven progress for happening elsewhere. Brick buildings leaned close over the road. Snow gathered in window boxes. The air smelled of yeast and soot and cold metal.

Roan sat in the back of an unmarked medical transport while Drogan stood at the bakery door with one hand on the lock. Kaelen the Unbroken leaned against a streetlamp as if the last twenty minutes had involved minor inconvenience rather than a clash with Vorath Greel in a café and the discovery that the city itself had been converted into a partial listening device.

Brendt shouldn't have been here.

That was Roan's first conclusion.

His second was that sending Brendt away would have required explaining enough to make him more likely to follow later. Therefore, Brendt stood beside the transport with one arm in a sling, a shield frame folded against his good forearm, and the grim expression of someone who had been told to stay behind too often and had started taking it personally.

Ines wasn't present. Selene wasn't present. Cole's people were handling the café cleanup, witness control, and the legal fiction that no foreign grandmaster had just tried to crush a student inside neutral territory. Good. Too many competent people in one place created coordination costs.

Roan's left arm still burned from Vorath's pressure.

Not the skin. The pressure had ignored skin. It had entered through the gap between Vitalis and identity, pressing on everything in him that had a name. The Codex Shard had absorbed the first contact for a heartbeat, then inverted a fraction of the force. Useful. Also damaging. The crack along the shard had widened enough that even with the Vance Pendant stabilizing it, his spine felt warm in a way spines should generally avoid. He didn't need a prompt to know the field was listening.

That was the difference between being hunted and being indexed.

Hunted meant a predator knew one existed.

Indexed meant the predator had built a filing system.

"This is absurd," Brendt said quietly.

"Yes," Roan said.

"I'm not complaining. I just want that recorded. If I die under a bakery, I want someone to know I objected to the venue."

"Noted."

"You didn't write it down."

"I have a good memory."

Brendt looked at him.

Roan looked back.

"Usually," Roan amended.

That landed harder than intended.

Brendt's mouth closed. For a moment, the joke between them had nowhere to go. Roan disliked that. He disliked it because it was inefficient. He disliked it because Brendt noticed too much. He disliked it because a missing memory about a cracked practice slate had hurt more than several broken ribs, and that was bad accounting.

Kaelen glanced toward them, expression unreadable.

Drogan turned the old brass key in the bakery lock.

Roan watched the key.

"You have too many keys," he said.

Drogan didn't look back. "Your father stole too many doors."

"Borrowed," Brendt muttered.

Drogan paused.

Brendt froze.

After a long second, Drogan opened the door. "Don't encourage him."

The bakery was dark inside. The front room held display cases, empty racks, sacks of flour stacked behind the counter, and a smell that made Roan's stomach remember he hadn't eaten since before the café. Civilian spaces had an unfortunate habit of making survival logistics look rude. He ignored it.

Kaelen stepped in first.

That was informative.

Drogan allowed it. More informative.

Roan followed, then Brendt. The door closed behind them, cutting off the snow and streetlight. Drogan didn't turn on the lamps. He walked behind the counter, moved three sacks of flour, and pressed his palm against the brick wall.

For two seconds, nothing happened.

Then the bricks folded.

Not slid. Not opened. Folded, as if the wall had remembered a different shape and preferred it.

Behind it waited a descending stairwell lined with black stone.

Roan's Codex Shard pulsed once. `[Legacy access detected. Lineage compatibility: partial. External authority contamination: active.]`

Kaelen looked at him. "Don't push your shard down there unless you want Vorath to hear the echo."

"How close is he?"

"Not physically."

"That wasn't the question."

Kaelen smiled faintly. "Closer than I'd prefer. Farther than he'd like."

"Useless answer."

"Accurate one."

Roan considered pushing again, then chose not to. Kaelen was old and powerful, plus had saved his life less than an hour ago. Those facts didn't make him trustworthy. They did make him an expensive resource. Damaging the resource before understanding its cost was poor practice.

Drogan went down the stairs.

The passage beneath the bakery was older than the building above it. The walls were cut from the same black stone as parts of the Deep Rift temple, though here the material had been planed smooth and scored with narrow channels. Ward-light flickered within those channels, dim and pale, tracing lines that resembled veins more than runes.

Brendt touched the wall with one gloved finger, then thought better of it and pulled back.

"Good," Roan said.

"I learn. Slowly. Under duress."

"Most education is duress with furniture."

"That's a depressing view of schooling."

"We attend Black Spire."

"Fair."

The stairs ended in a circular chamber no wider than a dorm room. A metal ring lay set into the floor. Not decorative. Anchor interface. Three old chairs stood against the wall. One had a broken leg. A kettle sat on a cold iron plate in the corner.

Roan stared at the kettle.

Drogan noticed. "No tea."

"I wasn't asking."

"You were thinking."

"Common problem."

Kaelen walked to the metal ring and crouched. He pulled off one glove. The hand beneath was scarred across the knuckles, the old cuts white against darker skin. He placed two fingers on the ring.

The chamber tightened.

Pressure gathered under Roan's teeth. Not Vorath's pressure. This was older, narrower, less arrogant. It didn't push down. It listened.

Kaelen closed his eyes.

Drogan stood beside him, jaw tight.

Roan watched both men.

There were three useful lines of inquiry.

First: what was the second door?

Second: why had Aldric built or stolen access to it?

Third: why had Drogan hidden it even after learning Vorath had anchored a hunt field into the city?

The answer to the third question involved guilt. Guilt was rarely useful in emergencies, but it often preserved secrets in excellent condition.

"This door," Roan said. "Vance-made?"

Drogan said nothing.

Kaelen, eyes still closed, said, "Veyrn-made. Aldric adapted it. There's a difference."

Roan kept the distinction.

"Purpose?"

"Exit."

"From what?"

"Bad conversations."

"Annoying."

"Define yet."

The ring lit.

A thin silver line crawled around its circumference, then split into seven smaller lines. Roan's spine warmed sharply. He suppressed the reflex to answer with the Codex Shard.

Kaelen opened one eye. "Good."

"Don't grade me."

"Don't make it easy."

The silver light rose from the floor in a transparent sheet, forming a vertical oval. Not a Rift gate. Too stable. Too deliberate. The air inside the oval showed another place: a snowfield beneath a dark mountain, wind cutting across black stone, stars bright enough to look hostile.

Brendt took one step back. "That isn't under the bakery."

"No," Kaelen said. "That's Suthmere."

Drogan's expression did something complicated and unhappy.

Roan studied the image. "Why show me?"

"Because you need to know there are exits Vorath didn't build."

"And entrances."

"Those too."

"Can Vorath use it?"

"No."

"Can I?"

"Not yet."

Annoying.

"Define yet."

"When the shard stops treating you like cargo and starts treating you like a pilot."

Roan disliked the accuracy of that.

The Codex Shard was powerful. It was also selective. It answered some questions, withheld others, and occasionally sounded so much like Aldric that Roan wanted to cut open the system prompt and see what was underneath. A tool that decided when its user was ready wasn't a tool. It was a partner with bad communication habits.

Or a jailer with better branding.

"You said you could mask Vorath's anchor," Roan said.

Kaelen nodded toward the ring. "Door residue. Old Veyrn channels don't oppose hunt fields. They misfile them. Vorath will still hear you if you flare hard, but not completely. He'll get direction without distance, pressure without identity. Enough to irritate him. Not enough to target you through a wall."

"Cost?"

"The door wakes."

"Meaning?"

Drogan answered this time. "Meaning people who remember the doors may notice."

Roan looked at him. "People like Sirenne."

"Yes."

"People like the other five names."

"Maybe."

"People like the blank seventh."

The chamber went quiet.

Kaelen looked at him.

Drogan didn't.

That was answer enough to move the question from speculation to active concern.

"Interesting," Roan said.

"No," Drogan said. "Dangerous."

"Most interesting things are."

"Your father said that too."

"He had good habits."

"He died."

"Bad outcome. Not necessarily bad process."

Drogan took a step toward him. "Don't turn him into a theorem."

Roan considered the old man's face.

There was anger there. Not at Roan, not really. At repetition. At the shape of a dead man reappearing through a boy with too much calculation and too little fear. Drogan had spent months trying to keep Roan alive by teaching him violence, then looked surprised whenever Roan became better at surviving violent things.

People were contradictory when frightened.

Useful to remember. Dangerous to rely on.

"Then give me data," Roan said.

"After this."

"That usually means never."

"After this," Drogan repeated.

Kaelen placed his hand on the ring again before Roan could answer. "Argue later. He's leaning closer."

The silver lines flared.

Roan felt Vorath before anyone spoke his name.

The pressure came from nowhere and everywhere, thin at first, then sharpening as if a blade were being drawn through the idea of distance. It didn't carry killing force this time. It carried attention.

The hunt field had noticed the door.

Kaelen smiled without humor. "Predictable old bastard."

Drogan drew his blade.

"Can steel help?" Brendt asked, already raising his shield.

"No," Drogan said.

Brendt lowered the shield half an inch. "I appreciate the honesty less than expected."

Roan stayed still.

Movement didn't matter. Vitalis did. Identity did. The best move wasn't to vanish but to become unprofitable to target.

He slowed his breathing. Lowered circulation. Let pain remain pain. Let the Shard stay quiet.

Vorath's attention brushed the edge of the chamber.

The Vance Pendant, damaged and cold, pulsed once.

Kaelen pressed two fingers harder into the ring. Silver light crawled up his wrist like frost.

"You should know," he said conversationally, "that if this fails, the bakery explodes."

Brendt stared. "Why would you wait until now to say that?"

"Motivation."

"Terrible method."

"Effective one."

The pressure sharpened.

A voice entered the chamber.

Not through air. Through the ward lattice.

"Vance."

Vorath Greel's voice was calm enough to be polite.

Roan didn't answer.

Kaelen's eyes flicked to him. Approval, perhaps.

The voice continued. "You are carrying a dead man's door and pretending it makes you less trapped."

Roan looked at Drogan. "Does he always monologue?"

Drogan's mouth twitched despite the pressure.

Kaelen snorted.

The hunt field compressed.

One of the wall channels cracked.

Brendt flinched but held his ground.

Vorath said, "Three years, boy. I will give you three years. The Compact allows this much grace, no more. Grow. Learn. Gather whatever debts your father left behind. Then I will come again, and no failed remnant will place himself between us."

Roan considered several replies.

A threat would be wasteful. Vorath was beyond his reach.

A promise would be theatrical. Roan disliked theatrical promises unless an audience needed specific manipulation.

A question might gather information, but Vorath had chosen a statement rather than a negotiation. Fishing now would reveal hunger.

Silence, then.

The best answer to a deadline was accounting.

Roan took out his notebook.

Brendt stared at him. "Are you—"

"Yes."

Roan wrote: Vorath self-imposed interval: three years. Source: Compact or external constraint. Usable limit.

The pressure in the chamber paused.

Kaelen laughed.

Vorath's voice lost a fraction of its smoothness. "You think this is a ledger?"

Roan capped the pen. "Everything is."

The silver ring flared white.

Kaelen drove his palm flat against the floor.

The chamber rang.

For a heartbeat, Vorath's attention found Roan completely.

The Codex Shard convulsed. `[Foreign authority lock detected. Invert?]`

No.

Not yet. Not loudly. Not inside a hunt field built to detect him.

Roan let the pressure hit.

His vision narrowed. Blood filled his mouth. His left hand went numb. Somewhere near his spine, the Shard cracked another hair's width, then stopped, held by the pendant's failing anchor and his own refusal to answer.

The door swallowed the pressure.

Not blocked. Misfiled.

Vorath's presence slid sideways, caught in old channels that didn't deny him so much as redirect the question toward several impossible addresses at once. North. Below. Suthmere. Nowhere. Thirty years ago. Tomorrow.

The hunt field snapped back.

Silence fell.

One by one, the wall channels dimmed.

Kaelen removed his hand from the ring. His palm was bleeding.

"There," he said. "Masked. Poorly. Temporarily. Better than dead."

Roan swallowed blood. "Duration?"

"Depends how annoying you are."

"So short."

"Naturally."

Drogan sheathed his blade. The motion was slow. Controlled. His hand shook once before stilling.

Brendt noticed. So did Roan.

Kaelen stood. He wiped his bleeding palm on a cloth pulled from his coat, then took a folded note from an inner pocket and held it out.

Roan didn't reach immediately.

Kaelen sighed. "It's paper, not a curse."

"People keep underestimating paper."

"Aldric raised you through ghosts and bad habits."

Roan took the note.

The paper was thick and cream-colored, then folded once. Inside, in precise black handwriting, were nine words:

When you're ready, Suthmere. The mountain. K.

No explanation. No map. No date. Of course.

Roan folded it again and placed it inside his notebook.

"Why Suthmere?"

Kaelen pulled his glove back on. "Because your father left something there I refused to carry."

"What?"

"A choice."

"Cryptic."

"No," Kaelen said. "Just expensive."

The old man turned toward the stairs.

Drogan stepped into his path. "Kael."

That was new. Not Kaelen. Kael. An old name. A dangerous one, given the recently disgraced Greel grandson had the same shortened sound.

Kaelen stopped.

Drogan said, "You can't just walk back in and vanish again."

"Watch me."

"He needs answers."

Kaelen looked at Roan. "He needs years. Answers without years are just faster ways to die."

Roan disliked him more for being partially correct.

"Three years," Roan said.

Kaelen nodded. "Less, if you're foolish. More, if Vorath lied."

"Did he?"

"He always lies. The useful question is where."

That, unfortunately, sounded like advice.

Kaelen walked to the base of the stairs. Before ascending, he paused without turning around.

"Your father wasn't good," he said. "Don't let Drogan make him clean. Don't let Vorath make him monstrous. Both versions are easier than the truth."

Drogan said nothing.

Kaelen added, "Send word to the old Veyrn routes if you need me. I don't answer social calls."

Then he climbed the stairs and vanished into the dark.

A moment later, the bakery door above opened.

Snow wind moved through the hidden passage.

Then nothing.

Brendt exhaled. "I hate old people."

Drogan looked at him.

"Present company mostly included," Brendt added.

Roan leaned against the wall and closed his eyes for one second.

No more.

The hunt field remained outside, but blurred now. Vorath could still listen for a flare. He could still press diplomatic channels. He could still kill most people Roan knew by sending a subordinate with a phone call and enough money.

But he had given a deadline.

Three years.

A resource, if used correctly.

Roan opened his notebook and added one more line beneath the first.

Three years isn't mercy. It's a constraint to exploit.

Drogan read it over his shoulder and sighed. "Your father would have written the same damn thing."

Roan put the notebook away.

"Then he missed fewer variables than you think."

Drogan didn't answer.

That wasn't the same as having nothing to say.
