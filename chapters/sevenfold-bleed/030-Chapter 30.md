Chapter 30: The Ashford Rumor

The training yard smelled like iron and old sweat. Roan preferred it that way. Perfume meant politics. Politics meant conversations he couldn't calculate.

He rolled his left shoulder. No ache. The Codex Shard had finished its work on the Crimson Wake form the moment it began—the strain converted instantly into clean Vitalis gain. No lingering damage. No secondary process. The Inversion didn't keep accounts of what it had erased.

The yard was half-empty this early. Dawn had cracked maybe forty minutes ago. The light came in pale and flat across the stone, catching the moisture from the night's condensation and turning everything slightly grey. Good. Fewer witnesses to whatever he planned to run today.

He'd been Tempered for six days now.

Six days of deliberately terrible performance in morning drills. Six days of watching the rankings flicker with names that shouldn't be above him. Six days of Old Pell's awful coffee and Brendt's increasingly confused looks and Lyra's silence at the breakfast table, worse than her anger, that silence. She'd stopped asking when he'd gotten so strong. Now she just watched him with an expression he couldn't parse.

Right now he needed to focus on the asset in front of him.

The training dummy's outer shell was cracked along the left flank. Standard issue, reinforced wood over a steel frame, rated for Initiate impacts. Meaningless against anyone past Tempered. But the crack itself told a story: someone had hit it hard, recently, with something sharp and angled.

Not him. He hadn't touched the equipment since the morning he'd started wearing low.

He circled the dummy slowly. The crack ran from shoulder to hip on the left side, clean and deliberate. Whoever had done this knew what they were doing. No wasted motion. One cut, maximum structural compromise. This wasn't a student technique.

A Spire instructor, maybe. Running diagnostic tests on the equipment. Or,

"Staring at it won't make it talk."

Roan didn't turn around. He knew that voice.

Brendt Holloway leaned against the far wall, arms crossed, looking like he'd rather be anywhere else. Which was, Roan had calculated, true. Brendt had made it abundantly clear on multiple occasions that early morning anything was a personal affront to his continued existence.

"You're up early," Roan said.

"You've been skipping breakfast for three days straight. I figured something was wrong."

Right. Brendt had been watching. Not approaching, just observing, the way a tactical analyst observed terrain before committing to a plan. Roan filed that information away. Brendt wasn't a threat, but Brendt was paying attention, and paying attention was the first step toward understanding. Understanding was the first step toward leverage, even between friends.

Or especially between friends.

"I wanted to try something," Roan said. He still hadn't touched the dummy. "Alone."

"And instead you get me."

"Instead I get you." He turned to face Brendt properly. The morning light caught the angles of Brendt's face, tired, but alert. More alert than he'd been a week ago. Something had changed in the older boy's training recently. "When did you hit Tempered?"

The question landed exactly where he aimed it. Brendt's expression flickered, a brief tightening around the eyes that Roan recognized as surprise, followed immediately by calculation. Brendt was doing the tally. Figuring out how much to reveal. Figuring out why Roan was asking.

"Three days ago," Brendt said. "Why?"

The Vitalis reading Brendt gave off was elevated but controlled. Tempered threshold, maybe 260, 280. Natural talent, not accelerated. Which meant Brendt's family had been quietly funding something, supplements, private coaching, something above board enough to pass inspection but expensive enough to matter. The Holloway shipping business wasn't wealthy by Conclave standards, but it wasn't poor either.

Because Brendt had never had to hide what he was. Brendt had never had to calculate whether revealing his rank would get his family killed.

Roan envied that, in a distant and abstract way. The same way he envied people who didn't check exits before sitting down.

"Because I want to spar," he said. "And I don't want to hurt anyone."

Brendt stared at him. "You want to *not* hurt someone. By sparring with me."

"I've been holding back in group drills. It's getting difficult to maintain." This was technically true. Maintaining his false low ranking required active suppression of his Vitalis output, which cost concentration he could otherwise spend on more productive calculations.

"You've been holding back." Brendt's tone was flat.

"Significantly."

"For six days."

"Give or take."

Brendt's jaw worked. Roan watched him process it, watched the sequence of conclusions arrive in order: first, that Roan's public performance was a lie. Second, that this lie was deliberate and sustained. Third, that if Roan was deliberately performing below his actual ability, there was a reason, and that reason wasn't flattering to anyone currently ranked above him.

Fourth, and this part Roan was watching for most closely: Brendt would have to decide whether this information made Roan a threat or an opportunity.

The ledger, as usual, didn't lie. And Brendt was good at math.

"The dummy," Brendt said slowly, nodding toward the cracked shell. "That was you. Before you started pretending."

"Before I decided to be stupid about it, yes."

"You hit it once."

"Twice. The second crack is under the first. I hit it twice, then stopped."

Brendt walked over to the dummy. Ran a finger along the visible crack. Pulled his hand back, examined the residue, wood fiber, a faint metallic sheen from the underlying frame. "This isn't Tempered-level damage," he said quietly. "This is mid-Tempered. Natural talent, no supplements, clean technique." He turned back to Roan. "You're Tempered."

Roan said nothing.

"Six days ago. When everyone in cohort three was guessing you were still Initiate because your intake scores were garbage." Brendt's voice had dropped, not a whisper but close. "You were already Tempered six days ago, and you sat in remedial formation and smiled while Theon Mercer called you dead weight."

Mercer. The name landed like a small, precise cut. Theon Mercer had made three comments about dead weight in the past week. The third one, yesterday, had included a gesture toward Lyra.

Roan had noted the gesture. He'd noted the context. He'd noted that Mercer's Vitalis output had declined slightly over the past month, signs of a stimulant dependency, recreational and undisciplined. He had a running mental calculation of exactly how much damage would result from a single precisely-aimed strike to Mercer's left knee.

Not yet. But soon. When the balance supported it.

"I don't enjoy being insulted," Roan said. "But I enjoy the strategic value of being underestimated more."

Brendt laughed. It was a short, sharp sound, almost startled out of him. "Gods above. You're serious."

"I'm always serious."

"You're insane."

"I'm neither. I'm accurate." He stepped closer to Brendt. Close enough that Brendt would feel the Vitalis presence radiating off him, not suppressed anymore, not held back. Just present, the way it should be for a mid-Tempered student who had nothing to hide.

Brendt's eyes widened slightly. Not fear. Brendt wasn't the type to fear a friend's strength, but something adjacent. Surprise, maybe. Or the dawning recognition that the map he'd been drawing of the world needed a significant revision.

"Christ," Brendt muttered. "When you said you wanted to spar—"

"I meant I want to hit something without holding back. And I'd prefer it if the something could hit back."

Brendt rolled his shoulders. The motion was casual, but Roan caught the micro-adjustments, a fighter settling into stance. Brendt had been trained properly. Family money, good instructors, the works. He wasn't show-off strong, but he was sound. Functional. Exactly the partner Roan needed if he wanted to test the outer edges of his current capacity.

"One condition," Brendt said.

"Name it."

"After. You're going to explain why. Not everything, but some of it." Brendt's eyes were steady. "I'm not asking you to trust me with everything. But I can't spar with someone I don't understand, and you don't let anyone understand you. So either you start explaining, or we don't do this."

It was a reasonable demand. More than reasonable, it was the precise negotiation Roan would have proposed in Brendt's position. Information exchange, bounded by trust but not exceeding it. Smart.

Roan calculated the risk. Brendt knowing he was Tempered was already a variable he couldn't fully control. Brendt knowing *why* he was Tempered, knowing about the Inversion, or at least the broad strokes, introduced new risk vectors. But Brendt *not* knowing invited speculation, and speculation without data tended toward the worst conclusions.

Better to give Brendt a narrative he could believe than let him build one from fragments and fear.

"After," Roan agreed. "One explanation. Bounded."

"Bounded," Brendt echoed. He nodded once, sharp. "Alright. How do you want to do this?"

Right. Finally.

"Full contact," Roan said. "No ranked weapons. The dummy yard's empty and the nearest instructor station is two hundred meters east. We're alone."

"You're very concerned about privacy for someone about to commit to sanctioned combat."

"I'm concerned about narrative control. If people see this, they'll talk. If they talk, the wrong people will start asking the wrong questions." He settled into his own stance, lower than Brendt's, weight distributed toward the balls of his feet. Ready to pivot, ready to absorb, ready to strike. "I need to know what my limits are before I need to use them publicly."

Brendt assessed this. "You're planning something."

"I'm always planning something. That's not new."

"Something that requires you to know your upper limit precisely."

"Everything requires precision."

Brendt exhaled. Then he moved.

He was faster than Roan expected, not dramatically, but enough that the first exchange was closer than he'd calculated. Brendt's opening strike came in high, a testing jab that Roan deflected with his forearm, absorbing the impact and letting the Inversion register the minor Sidewound of contact.

[Sidewound absorbed.]
[Conversion: +3 Vitalis.]

Roan pivoted left, bringing his right hand up in a counter that Brendt blocked, barely. Brendt caught it on his forearm guard and shoved back, using the deflection to create distance. Smart. Brendt was a ranged fighter at heart, comfortable with staff and polearms, and he'd correctly identified that close-quarters was Roan's advantage.

But he wasn't wrong about wanting to test his limits.

Roan closed the gap again. This time he didn't telegraph, he just moved, letting his body drop into the instinct he'd built over the past weeks of brutal, self-directed training. His fist connected with Brendt's guard. Brendt absorbed it, rolled with it, came back with an elbow that caught Roan's shoulder.

Pain flared. Brief, sharp.

[Sidewound absorbed.]
[Conversion: +5 Vitalis.]

Right. The Inversion was working. Every impact fed back, converting the minor damage into raw Vitalis. This was the core of his advantage, not that he was stronger than his opponents, but that he could *profit* from every exchange. A normal Tempered student would exit a sparring match fatigued. Roan would exit stronger.

Brendt was good. Very good. His footwork was textbook, his form clean, his Vitalis expenditure controlled. He wasn't wasting energy on flourishes. Every movement served a purpose, and the purposes were layered, defense leading to counter, feint leading to positioning, positioning leading to pressure that tired opponents out.

But Brendt didn't know about the Inversion. Brendt was fighting Roan as if this were a normal exchange of violence, with normal stakes, win or lose, energy spent or conserved. He didn't know that every punch Roan absorbed was a small deposit, every guard he broke through a larger one.

Roan hit him with a combination, three strikes, each one flowing into the next, each one converting a small Sidewound. Brendt blocked the first two and caught the third on his ribs. His breath went out in a sharp huff.

"Gods—damn—okay—" Brendt backpedaled, holding up a hand. "Give me a second."

Roan stopped. He was breathing harder than he'd expected. His Vitalis, paradoxically, felt higher than before the exchange had started.

Brendt straightened, rubbing his ribs. "You hit harder each time. That's not normal."

Right. Brendt would notice that.

"I'm warming up," Roan said.

"You were mid-Tempered strength on the first exchange. That last hit was high-Tempered." Brendt's eyes were sharp, analytical. The look of someone building a model. "You're accumulating energy from the fight itself."

The columns didn't lie. Brendt was too smart to miss the pattern.

"Partial explanation," Roan said. "After. You said after."

Brendt held his gaze for a beat. Then he nodded slowly. "Fine. After." He reset his stance. "But if you're going to get stronger as the fight goes on, I'm not going to let it go long."

He came in harder this time. Roan recognized the shift. Brendt had decided to end the exchange before Roan's advantage compounded. Full commitment. Brendt threw a combination that was genuinely dangerous, not testing anymore, not probing. A real attempt to put Roan down before the Inversion made it impossible.

Roan took the first strike to his guard. Took the second on his forearm. Let the third graze his ribs, deliberately, calculating the angle and depth, and felt the shock of impact register through his Codex Shard.

[Sidewound absorbed.]
[Conversion: +8 Vitalis.]

He caught Brendt's fourth strike, twisted it aside, and delivered a palm strike to Brendt's solar plexus that doubled him over. Not hard enough to injure. Brendt would recover in seconds, but enough to end the exchange.

Brendt straightened slowly, hands on his knees. "Okay," he said, between breaths. "Okay. You win."

"You didn't let it go long enough to see the real limit."

"I don't need to. I saw enough." Brendt straightened up, brushing dust from his uniform. He looked at Roan with an expression that had shifted, still analytical, but with something warmer underneath. Curiosity, maybe. Or the beginning of respect. "Whatever you're doing, it's not in the curriculum. I've never seen anything like it."

"After," Roan said.

"After," Brendt agreed.

- break -

The changing room was empty. This time of morning, everyone who mattered was either at breakfast or in the early lecture halls. Roan sat on a bench with his shirt off, examining his shoulder in the mirror mounted above the sinks.

There was no bruise. The Inversion didn't leave marks. Every exchange had been converted instantly, the damage erased at the moment of impact. The cellular stress of a normal fight never got the chance to settle. He didn't need to heal faster—the Inversion simply never let the injury take hold in the first place. The Vitalis output from the spar was a single clean sum, no residual trace beyond the gain.

He was essentially self-sustaining in a fight. Every wound fed back. No wound ever truly remained.

This part he couldn't explain to Brendt. Not yet. Maybe not ever.

The door opened. Brendt walked in, freshly showered, carrying two cups from the canteen. He handed one to Roan without comment and sat on the opposite bench.

"Coffee's terrible," Brendt said. "But it's hot."

Roan took it. The warmth was welcome. His body temperature ran slightly below normal during Inversion cycles, the Codex Shard prioritizing energy conversion over thermal regulation. A minor inefficiency, but one he'd learned to manage.

"You said partial explanation," Brendt began. He wasn't looking at Roan, he was looking at the wall, the casual posture of someone who wanted to minimize the pressure of the conversation. "I'll start with a question. How long have you been Tempered?"

"Six days."

"And before that?"

"Ask what you want to ask."
