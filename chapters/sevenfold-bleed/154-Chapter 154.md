Chapter 154: A Narrow Bridge

Roan triggered the inversion.

[Inversion accepted.]
[Target: Brendt Holloway.]
[Path: wound reversal / contamination lock / structural restoration.]
[Quota status: 0/5 remaining.]

The force of the channel tore through Roan's frame. Not Brendt's pain. His own. The body was the bridge now, and every bridge buckled under enough load. He supplied blood through the palm he pressed against Brendt's chest, Vitalis through the contact, and intent through the Black Rite line.

First Refusal had been built to reject names.
Now he used it to reject an ending.

Brendt's wound tried to remain true. Torn vessels insisted they were torn. Rift contamination spread like black frost along the reinforcement channels. Muscle remembered being split. Bone remembered impact.

Roan told all of it no.
Not eloquently. Not with a heroic vow. No declarations about friendship or brotherhood or never letting anyone die. Those were theater, and theater didn't close arteries.

He refused the wound one layer at a time.

Blood flow reversed first. Not back into the body like cheap miracle nonsense, but into clotting, sealing, redirecting pressure through channels that could survive. The contamination stopped spreading. The torn reinforcement threads reknotted around the collarbone, ugly and incomplete but functional enough to keep the shoulder attached to the rest of the person.

Brendt arched once, teeth clenched so hard Roan heard something crack.

"Breathe," Roan said.

Brendt inhaled.

The wound closed by degrees.

Not healed. Stabilized. Alive.

Good enough.

Roan's vision grayed at the edges.

Something slipped.

For one second, Roan saw Greycross kitchen light across a cracked table. Lyra's hand stealing the larger piece of bread because she thought he wasn't looking. Mira coughing in the next room. Brendt's laugh from a different memory overlaying both, too loud and too alive.

Then the avenue returned.

The slip wasn't a debt. It was simple topography—the path of refusal ran both ways. He had pulled Brendt's ending off the table, and the road passed through the ground Brendt occupied in his life. The image was a side effect of the reach.

Roan's hand was still on Brendt's chest.

Brendt breathed.

The sovereign clicked.

Roan looked up.

Drogan had seen.

Only for a fraction of a second, between blade exchanges. The old man's mouth moved, not enough to become a smile by normal standards. For Drogan, it was practically public celebration.

Then the champion nearly took his head off, and sentiment returned to being inconvenient.

"Ines," Roan said.

"Already moving."

She slid beside Brendt, jammed a pressure patch over the sealed wound, and injected something from a black-capped vial into his thigh.

"What is that?" Roan asked.

"Temporary insult to death."

"Specific."

"You'll complain later if he's alive."

"Accurate."

Brendt coughed. Blood flecked his lips, but his eyes stayed open.

"Refund," he rasped.

Roan stared at him.

"For... Terrible last words."

"Denied."

Brendt's mouth twitched again.

Alive.

The Shard didn't offer comfort after the transfer. It offered a dry observation.

[External stabilization complete.]
[Residual contamination: contained.]

The rest was inference. Twenty-three minutes before the Rift contamination bound irretrievably to Brendt's marrow. A narrow treatment window.

Roan almost preferred when the system was vague. Vague numbers could be negotiated with optimism, ignorance, or violence. Precise numbers sat on the page like contracts already signed by other people. The inversion hadn't saved Brendt in any broad poetic sense. It had purchased time, badly and at premium rates.

Still alive.

That was the line that mattered.

He checked Brendt's pupils, breath rhythm, pulse at the throat. Brendt tried to raise his head and failed, then made an offended sound at his own body. Good. Offense indicated personality remained present. Roan placed Brendt's good hand over the pressure patch and pressed until Brendt's fingers closed around it.

"Hold that."

"It hurts."

"Excellent. You can feel it."

"Your bedside manner is a crime."

"Bill me."

Brendt's laugh came out as a wet cough. Roan disliked the sound and saved the dislike for future retaliation. Not against Brendt. Against the sovereign. Against Greel if Greel had touched the route. Against whoever had put dead-zone principles in a place where Hollowkin could learn from them. The list was becoming administratively inconvenient.

Drogan's blade rang behind him.

The champion pressed the old instructor harder now, as if the sovereign had fed it updated priorities through the broken clicking line. It no longer tried to kill Drogan quickly. It tried to keep him occupied while smaller bodies moved around his edges. That made it less a duel and more a lock. Locks could be broken, picked, bypassed, or left in place while one stole the wall.

Roan looked at the pylons again.

Old containment. Bad charge. Linked by buried cable beneath the ash.

A wall, then.

Not a good one.

But stolen walls didn't need to be good. They needed to belong to someone else five seconds ago.

Roan also noted the sovereign's reaction to the transfer.

It didn't rush. That mattered. A beast would have lunged the moment prey weakened. A commander watched the resource ledger change. The antlered thing had forced him to spend the last quota and then paused just long enough to confirm that the expenditure was real. No flare of offensive inversion. No Crimson Wake answer. No reversal gathering behind Roan's frame.

It could read absence.

Not perfectly, perhaps. The Vance Pendant still cooled against his chest, and Sidewound's resonance remained mostly private. But the sovereign had sensed enough to know the trap had succeeded. That meant the next attack would be shaped around Roan's limitations, not his public reputation.

Fine.

Reputation was usually wrong anyway.

The sovereign's army surged.

- break -

The first wave came low.

Four Husks, two on the ground, two along the walls, all aiming for Ines and Brendt. Not Roan. Not Drogan. The sovereign understood the team structure now. Remove the wounded and the handler, force Roan to guard, divide Drogan from the group, then let the champion occupy the strongest blade until exhaustion did the simple work.

A decent plan.

Roan ruined it with the pylon.

He had noticed the dead line of ward pylons when they entered the avenue. Old containment hardware, from whatever civilization had occupied the Hollowed City before it became a corpse. Most were cracked. One still held a faint charge. Not enough to function as designed. Enough to fail usefully.

He cut the support seam with Sidewound and kicked the pylon into the path of the low wave.

It fell slowly.

Too slowly.

Roan hated dramatic timing when it involved falling objects.

The lead Husk began to slip past.

Brendt, lying half-conscious on the ground, lifted his damaged shield with his good hand and shoved it into the Husk's legs.

The creature stumbled.

The pylon landed.

Old charge discharged into wet ash and Hollowkin tissue. Blue-white light snapped across the street. Two Husks convulsed. One burst. The fourth dropped from the wall directly toward Ines.

Ines shot it with the sidearm she hadn't mentioned carrying.

Roan looked at her.

She shrugged. "Emergency budget."

"We will discuss declaration rules."

"Survive first. Audit later."

"Accepted."

Drogan and the champion moved past them in a blur of short steps and exact violence. The champion pressed high, forcing Drogan's guard upward, then changed angle toward the knee. Drogan gave ground instead of blocking. Smart. Blocking would confirm the weakness. Retreating let him sell a lie.

The champion followed.

Drogan's blade reversed.

The creature lost two fingers and part of its wrist.

It didn't react like an animal. It looked at the missing digits, recalculated grip, and shifted the bone blade to a different line.

Roan disliked it more.

"Can Drogan win?" Ines asked.

"Yes."

"Will he win fast enough?"

"No."

"Plan?"

Roan looked at the gate.

The breach arch stood two hundred meters away now, gray light visible between ranks. The sovereign remained behind its army, controlling from elevation. Roan had no inversion quota. Peak Vitalis was still available, but using it without inversion support after the Brendt transfer would hammer his structural channels. Sidewound could cut seams, but closing on the sovereign required crossing bodies and command pressure.

Not ideal.

"We don't kill the sovereign," Roan said.

Ines blinked. "That's new."

"We make staying expensive."

"How?"

Roan pointed to the old pylon line running toward the gate. "Those were containment anchors. Dead, mostly. But not all. The sovereign is using bodies to block the avenue because it thinks terrain is neutral. Terrain is never neutral. It belongs to whoever invoices it first."

Brendt coughed. "That one... Was almost inspirational."

"Don't encourage him," Ines said.

Roan stood.

His spine protested. The Shard remained quiet except for the dull ache of empty quota. Good. He didn't need permission to be dangerous.

"Can you move him?" he asked.

Ines looked at Brendt. "Drag, yes. Carry, no."

Brendt lifted two fingers. "Can limp. Badly. With dignity."

"No dignity," Roan said. "Speed."

"Always the romantic."

Roan ignored that because Brendt was alive and therefore once again making bad jokes, which meant the inversion had achieved acceptable returns.

He cut the second pylon.

The army reacted faster this time.

Husks surged before the anchor fell, trying to reach him before he could complete the chain. Roan met the first with a knee to the throat and a cut through the shoulder seam. He didn't kill it cleanly. No time. He disabled, moved, cut the third pylon, and let momentum do the rest.

The second anchor dropped into the first's discharged field.

The avenue lit in crooked blue.

Old containment logic woke up wrong.

Energy arced from pylon to pylon, not strong enough to seal the street, not stable enough to hold, but violent enough to interfere with command pressure. The sovereign's clicking fractured mid-pattern.

The army hesitated.

One second.

Roan had built plans around less.

"Now," he said.

They moved.

- break -

Ines didn't stare at Roan. She was too disciplined for that, and too busy replacing Brendt's ruined shoulder strap with a length of binding cord pulled from her own belt. But her eyes flicked once to Roan's hand, where his blood and Brendt's had dried together into a black crust, then away. She understood enough to know that whatever he had done wasn't standard field medicine, not a Vance trick she could safely price, and not something she should ask about while standing inside an army's mouth.

Drogan understood more.

That was worse.

The old man's almost-smile hadn't been approval of a heroic choice. Drogan didn't approve of heroics. It had been recognition, and recognition carried history. Aldric had done something like this once, perhaps. Or failed to. Or chosen differently and spent thirty years becoming a sentence Drogan couldn't say without bleeding in places no one could bandage.

Roan didn't have room for that history now.

He compressed it into a later problem and put a knife on top of it.

Brendt breathed again. Shallow. Ragged. Present.

"Can you see?" Roan asked.

"Unfortunately," Brendt whispered.

"Good. Count fingers."

Roan held up three.

"Two," Brendt said.

"Close enough."

"That's not close."

"Medical standards are lower during ambushes."

"I want a better doctor."

"Survive and overpay one."

The exchange cost Brendt breath, but it anchored him. Consciousness responded to stupid conversation as well as pain, sometimes better. Roan had learned that in Greycross, sitting beside Mira during bad nights when medicine ran short and fear tried to make the room larger. Talk didn't heal. It kept people from drifting into places where healing arrived too late.

He didn't like remembering that.

He used it anyway.

The second pylon did more than discharge.

It remembered.

Only for a fraction of a second, but the Hollowed City had been built by people who didn't think of walls as inert objects. The ancient anchor drank the dying pulse from its neighbor and answered with a line of pressure that rose from the buried cable network beneath the ash. Not a barrier. A question in the language of containment: authorized passage, yes or no?

The Husks had no answer.

Neither did Roan, technically, but the Vance Pendant went cold against his chest and the pressure skimmed past him as if unwilling to examine the paperwork too closely.

The effect lasted less than three breaths.

In those three breaths, every Hollowkin body inside the pylon line staggered.

Drogan used the first breath to cut the champion's wrist tendons.

Ines used the second to drag Brendt upright with a sound that suggested she would be billing everyone involved.

Roan used the third to kill the nearest command relay.

It wasn't the sovereign. It was a smaller creature crouched on a broken lamp post, throat swollen with clicking organs, its whole body vibrating as it echoed orders down the avenue. Sidewound entered under the jaw and cut upward. The relay died without drama, which Roan appreciated. The army's left flank lost timing immediately.

"That helped," Ines said.

"It cost a pylon."

"I was thanking the pylon."

"It's dead infrastructure."

"It still has better manners than you."

Brendt, half hanging off her shoulder, lifted one finger. "Agree."

"You are concussed," Roan said.

"Doesn't make me wrong."

Unfortunately, no.

The sovereign clicked again, lower this time. Not angry. Adjusting. Its antlers drew a black arc through the mist as it lowered its head and began forcing the right flank into motion again. It had lost a relay and several bodies. It had also confirmed the pylons were dangerous but temporary.

Next wave wouldn't bunch near them.

Good.

That meant the path to the arch would spread, and spread enemies left gaps.

The run to the gate wasn't clean.

Brendt couldn't run properly. Ines half-supported, half-dragged him, swearing under her breath with impressive variety. Roan cut anything that came within arm's length and didn't chase anything that retreated. Drogan remained behind for five heartbeats longer than Roan liked, then disengaged from the champion with a cut that opened its armored chest from clavicle to hip.

Not lethal.

Educational.

The champion staggered. Drogan joined them, breathing hard, cheek bleeding freely now.

"You spent it," he said.

"Yes."

"Good."

"Don't make it sentimental."

"Wouldn't dare."

The sovereign recovered command pressure before they reached the arch.

It clicked once, deep and sharp.

Every remaining Husk in the avenue turned toward Brendt.

Roan almost admired the consistency.

Almost.

He cut the fourth pylon as they passed. This one didn't discharge. It collapsed into the avenue, forcing two Husks to climb. Ines shot one. Drogan threw a knife into the other. Brendt, pale and shaking, used his good arm to slam his shield edge into the knee of a third.

"Stop helping," Roan said.

"No."

"You're leaking."

"Temporary."

"That isn't how blood works."

"It's today."

The breach arch filled Roan's vision.

Gray light pooled inside the stone frame. The tether line still ran through it, taut and humming. Surface. Containment chamber. Bad air that was still ordinary air. Municipal incompetence. Ironholm. Black Spire. Hospitals. Problems with paperwork instead of antlers.

A Husk dropped from above the arch.

Roan threw Sidewound.

Throwing a specialized seam blade was a terrible idea in almost every context. This context made it merely expensive. The knife spun once, caught the creature under the jaw, and pinned it to the arch stone. Not a kill. Enough.

Drogan shoved Brendt through first.

Ines followed.

The sovereign crossed half the remaining distance in a blur of pale limbs and broken antlers.

Drogan turned to face it.

Roan grabbed Sidewound from the pinned Husk, tore it free, and stepped beside him.

"Go," Drogan said.

"No."

"Three seconds?"

"Two."

The sovereign reached them.

Its mask opened.

Inside, layered voices spoke in the old Hollow tongue.

Roan understood one word.

Debt.

Then Drogan hit it.

Not with a technique Roan had seen before. Not with the standard Black Spire curriculum—this was older. Smaller. A refusal compressed into a single step and a short cut across the sovereign's command throat.

The creature's clicking stopped.

Only for a breath.

Roan used it.

He cut the seam Drogan had opened, not deep enough to kill, not with enough strength to finish, but enough to make the sovereign flinch.

Then he and Drogan stepped backward through the gate together.

Gray swallowed them.

The last thing Roan saw in the Rift was the sovereign's cracked antlers lowering as the army's command pattern reformed.

Not defeated.

Delayed.

The bill remained open.
