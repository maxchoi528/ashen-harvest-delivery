Chapter 116: Brendt's Room

The corridor outside the examination wing smelled of antiseptic and anxiety. Roan stood three meters from the door, arms crossed, watching Brendt's fellow students filter past in clusters of two and three. They glanced at him, at the door, back at him. Nobody stopped to talk. Nobody asked why a fourth-year was waiting with a second-year cohort.

The ledger was straightforward. Brendt had been removed from classes two days ago for "observation." Mira had received no official notification, but old Pell had mentioned it at the market, gossip traveled faster than Sentinel dispatches in Greycross. Brendt's roommate had mentioned something about shaking, about waking up with blood on his pillow.

Roan had his own theories about what that meant.

The door opened. A pale youth in white robes emerged, supported by a woman who might've been his mother. She was saying something about hydration, about rest, about the body recovering in time. The boy nodded mechanically, eyes unfocused.

Standard pattern. Sidewound backlash from whatever they'd fed him in there.

Roan had seen it before, in himself, in the early days before the Inversion learned to sort signal from noise. The body didn't understand voluntary pain. It reacted to damage the way it had been wired to react for a hundred thousand years: by shutting down non-essential functions, by conserving resources, by flooding the system with chemicals designed to make the organism stop moving and start healing.

The difference was that Roan could invert the signal. Most people couldn't. They sat in examination rooms, let healers poke at their meridians, got told to rest and drink water and wait for the backlash to pass. Some of them never recovered full capacity. Some of them never recovered at all.

Brendt would be different. Brendt had to be different.

The door opened again. A stern-faced man in healer's robes looked at the remaining students, counted heads, then fixed his gaze on Roan.

"You." The word was clipped, professional. "Are you on the visitation list?"

"Brendt Holloway requested me specifically."

"Requested—" The healer glanced at a clipboard. His expression shifted from suspicion to something harder to read. "You're Roan Vance."

"Fourth year. Combat Applications."

"I know what year you are." The healer set down the clipboard. "His file says you've been cleared for a brief visit. Ten minutes. No physical contact, no sustained conversation without stress-inducing topics. He's fragile right now. Whatever happened in the western district—"

"I know what happened."

"—left marks that don't show on diagnostic imaging. Mental scarring. Emotional dysregulation. We don't fully understand the mechanism yet." The healer paused. "Nobody does, apparently. That's why we need to observe him. That's why we need to understand what you did to him."

Roan met the man's gaze without flinching. "I didn't do anything to him."

"That's what the file says." The healer's voice was flat. "It also says you're the only person he asked for by name. It says his stress markers spike when anyone mentions the western district, except when the context involves you specifically. It says—" He stopped, seemed to recalculate. "Ten minutes. Room seven. If you cause a regression, you'll be banned from this facility and subject to Academy disciplinary action."

"Understood."

The healer stepped aside.

Brendt's room was small and white, plus aggressively sterile. The bed took up half the available space; the other half held monitoring equipment that beeped in soft, irregular patterns. Brendt himself was propped up against pillows, thinner than Roan remembered, eyes fixed on the wall opposite the door.

He didn't turn when Roan entered. Didn't acknowledge the click of the latch or the footsteps on clean tile. He just stared at the wall like it contained something the rest of them couldn't see.

Roan pulled a chair over, sat down, and waited.

One minute. Two. The monitoring equipment chirped occasionally. Somewhere outside, a bird called, wrong for the height of the building, recording played through speakers to provide "natural ambiance."

"The walls are white," Brendt said, finally. His voice was hoarse, like he'd been screaming recently. "They want to keep everything calm. Low stimulation. But I can still feel it, you know? Underneath. Like there's a frequency just below hearing. They can't filter that out."

"What frequency?"

"The one that says something's wrong." Brendt turned his head and slowly, plus Roan saw the dark circles under his eyes, the slight tremor in his jaw. "I know it's not real. The healer explained. It's the backlash. The body expecting pain that didn't come, or came wrong. But knowing doesn't make it stop."

Roan considered the statement, ran the calculations. Brendt was describing the aftermath of forced breakthrough, the mechanism used on initiates who couldn't advance naturally. The process required was brutal, and the side effects were well-documented. Mental instability, chronic pain, personality shifts, occasional psychosis. Half of those subjected to it never recovered enough to function in normal society.

Brendt had been pushed through it anyway. By his own family, apparently, given the timing.

"The western district," Roan said.

"Don't."

"You need to process it."

"I need to forget it." Brendt's hands tightened on the blanket. "I need to not think about what they put in me, about what I felt when it woke up, about—" He stopped. Swallowed. "You were there. When it happened. You were the only thing that didn't feel wrong."

"Brendt."

"I'm not crazy." The words came fast, almost pleading. "I know how I sound. I know what they think. But I'm not. I'm not—"

"I believe you."

Brendt went still.

Roan had learned, over the past year, that certain phrases landed differently depending on context. He'd learned that "I understand" could mean anything or nothing, that "of course" was often a lie, that people responded to sincerity the same way they responded to surprise, viscerally, before the conscious mind had time to calibrate the reaction.

Brendt's conscious mind had been recalibrated a dozen times over the past week. But his visceral response was still intact.

"I believe you," Roan repeated. "Whatever you experienced in the western district was real. The sensation of something wrong, the frequency below hearing, the feeling that your body expected pain that didn't come, it was all real. It was your nervous system responding to the specific circumstances of your breakthrough."

"That's not—" Brendt blinked. "How do you know about the breakthrough?"

"Your family pushed you through forced advancement. I suspected it when your sister stopped attending morning drills. I confirmed it when I learned you were removed from classes for observation." Roan leaned forward slightly. "The technique they used on you, whatever variant, leaves traces. The body stores the memory of that kind of forcing. For weeks, sometimes months, you feel it beneath everything else. Like a second pulse."

"The healer didn't know that."

"Healers treat symptoms. I treat mechanics."

Brendt was quiet. The monitoring equipment chirped. Outside the window, which showed a painted terrain rather than actual sky, the fake bird called again.

"You're not supposed to know," Brendt said finally. "Nobody's supposed to know. The family—they made sure—"

"Your family's secrecy matters less than your recovery." Roan kept his voice level. "Right now, you need to understand what's happening to you. The pain you're feeling, the wrongness, the sense that something's waiting beneath your awareness, it's not a malfunction. It's your system recalibrating after forced expansion."

"It doesn't feel like recalibration. It feels like something's going to break through."

"It might." Roan met Brendt's eyes. "The techniques used in forced breakthrough aren't designed for safety. They're designed for results. Whatever they forced into your system is still settling. Still finding its place. Until it does, you might experience episodes, moments where the barrier between your normal consciousness and the breakthrough energy thins."

"Is that what's wrong with me?"

"No. What's wrong with you is that you were hurt by people you trusted." Roan paused, recalculated. "The rest, the shaking, the phantom pain, the sense that you're listening to a frequency nobody else can hear, that's the backlash. It's temporary. Your system will adapt or it won't. Either way, you need to stop fighting it."

Brendt laughed, a short, sharp sound that held no humor. "Stop fighting it. Right. Just let go. Just accept that my own family tried to—" He stopped. His jaw trembled. "They said it was for my own good. They said I had potential, that I was wasting it, that they were doing what had to be done. They had documents. Studies. Proof that forced breakthrough worked, that the risks were minimal."

"They lied."

"They didn't lie. That was the worst part." Brendt's voice cracked. "They believed it. They believed they were helping me. My mother broke when they held me down. She kept saying it would be over soon, that I'd thank them when I was strong enough to understand."

Roan had heard variations of this story before. He'd heard it from Lyra, whose own parents had tried something similar, before Mira had intervened. He'd heard it from kids in the lower dormitories, the ones whose families had money and desperation in equal measure. Forced breakthrough was an industry in certain circles. A way to skip the years of training, the months of meditation, the slow accumulation of power that came from natural advancement.

The industry was profitable. The industry was growing. And the industry didn't care that half its products ended up in rooms like this one, staring at white walls, waiting for a frequency to stop.

"The breakthrough," Roan said. "How far did they push you?"

"I don't know. They wouldn't tell me. After—" Brendt's face went distant, like he was trying to access something behind his eyes. "After the first session, I could feel something new. Like a second heartbeat. After the second session, it started moving on its own. By the third—"

"Third session?"

"They stopped after the third." Brendt's hands were shaking again. "Something went wrong. I heard—I heard something that wasn't me. I tried to tell them, but they said it was normal. That I'd adjust. That the voices would fade as the breakthrough settled."

Roan's jaw tightened.

The voices. The wrong frequency. The sense that something was waiting to break through.

Brendt hadn't just been pushed through forced breakthrough. He'd been pushed too far, into territory that most advancement techniques explicitly avoided. Whatever his family had paid for, whatever variant of the procedure they'd chosen, it had opened a door that wasn't supposed to open.

"You're going to be fine," Roan said.

"You don't know that."

"I know enough." He stood, pushed the chair back. "The thing you heard, the voice that wasn't you, it's going to stop. You're going to learn to filter it out, to treat it as background noise. It won't happen overnight. But it will happen."

"How can you possibly—"

"Because I've dealt with something similar." Roan moved toward the door, paused. "The healer is wrong about one thing. You're not fragile. You're recalibrating. There's a difference."

He left before Brendt could respond.

- break -

The corridor outside was empty now. The other students had gone, and the antiseptic smell had settled into something thicker, more oppressive. Roan walked quickly, mind running calculations.

Brendt's case was worse than he'd expected. The family had used a variant he recognized, a variant that shouldn't have been available outside military channels. The third-session effects matched descriptions from classified training documents, the kind that circulated in black markets and underground circles. Someone had paid a lot of money to break Brendt in this specific way.

Someone who wanted him functional but unstable. Functional enough to advance on paper. Unstable enough to be controlled in practice.

Roan had seen this pattern before. He'd seen it in recruitment files for shadow units, for black operations, for tasks that required operatives who could be trusted to follow orders without asking questions. The instability made them dependent on handlers. The functionality made them useful.

Brendt wasn't being hidden. He was being prepared.

The realization settled into Roan's chest like cold water. Brendt's family hadn't acted out of desperation or misguided love. They'd been contracted. Someone had paid them to produce a specific kind of product, and Brendt was the raw material.

Which meant the contract was still active. Which meant someone was monitoring his progress, waiting to see if the product performed as designed.

Which meant Brendt wasn't safe in this room. He wasn't safe anywhere in Greycross.

Roan reached the stairs, took them two at a time. His mind was already working through options, calculating angles of approach, assessing the resources he'd need to extract Brendt without triggering immediate retaliation. The cost was steep. Extracting a minor from his legal guardians required documentation and justification, plus time, time during which whoever had contracted the procedure could accelerate their plans.

But there might be another way.

The building's exit opened onto a courtyard, dappled with autumn light. Students crossed in small groups, heading to afternoon classes or lingering by the fountain. Nobody looked twice at Roan. He was just another fourth-year, walking with purpose, too ordinary to notice.

He let them think that.

His hand found the Vance Pendant beneath his shirt, warm, as always, pulsing with a rhythm that had become as familiar as his own heartbeat. The Inversion was quiet right now, conserving capacity, waiting for the next opportunity. It didn't need to be active for Roan to use what he'd learned from it.

The techniques. The calculations. The specific knowledge of how forced advancement worked, and how it could be countered.

Brendt needed more than time. He needed intervention, someone who understood what had been done to him, who could guide his system through the settling process without leaving him dependent on handlers or family. He needed someone who could make the voices fade without silencing him completely.

Roan might be able to do that. The knowledge was there, buried in notes he'd taken during his own recovery, in diagrams he'd reverse-engineered from Aldric's journals. He'd never tested it on another person. He'd never had reason to But he had reason now.

The courtyard fountain caught his eye, water arcing in predictable patterns, splashing into the pool below with mathematical regularity. Drogan had taught him to see systems, to break complex problems into component parts, to trace the flow of energy through interconnected mechanisms. Brendt's mind was a system. The breakthrough was a mechanism. The instability was a symptom with a specific cause.

Find the cause. Fix the mechanism. Let the system stabilize.

The arithmetic was ugly, but it wasn't impossible.

Roan pulled out his comm, scrolled to Brendt's contact. The message would need to be carefully worded, not so specific that it triggered monitoring filters, not so vague that it gave Brendt nothing to work with. He thought for a moment, then typed:

*Difficult today. Don't fight the voices. Treat them as background noise. I'll find a way to make them stop.*

He hit send.

Three dots appeared almost immediately. Brendt was awake, alert, still monitoring his messages despite everything. That was good. That meant the backlash hadn't progressed as far as it could have.

The reply came fast:

*How?*

Roan typed back:

*Something I learned. Not from books. Trust me.*

A pause. Then:

*Okay.*

The single word carried weight. Roan felt something shift in his chest, not warmth, not comfort, just a tightening that might've been resolve. Brendt was trusting him. Brendt was going to keep trusting him, even when things got worse before they got better.

That meant Roan couldn't afford to fail.

He pocketed the comm, started walking. The fourth-year dormitories were on the other side of campus, a ten-minute walk if he took the long route through the training grounds. He had papers to review, notes to cross-reference, a plan to construct from fragments of knowledge and educated guesswork.

He also had a meeting scheduled with Drogan in two hours. The Warden had been asking questions about the western district, about Brendt, about the specific timing of events. Questions Roan had deflected with partial truths and strategic silence.

He might need to offer Drogan more than partial truths. The Warden was the closest thing Roan had to an ally in the upper levels of the Academy, and Drogan had connections, resources, knowledge that Roan couldn't access alone. If Brendt's extraction required outside help, Drogan was the only option that didn't involve exposing the Inversion.

The math was changing. The variables were multiplying. But the core equation remained the same: Brendt had been hurt by people who should have protected him, and Roan had the skills to help him recover.

Everything else was negotiable.

- break -

The library's third floor was quiet at this hour, populated only by dedicated students and the occasional professor researching obscure texts. Roan had claimed a corner table near the eastern windows, stacks of books arranged in a careful semicircle around his laptop.

He was looking for references to breakthrough-Sidewound interactions, specifically, cases where forced advancement had produced auditory phenomena in the subject. The Academy's medical database was unhelpful; it contained descriptions of symptoms but no analysis of underlying mechanisms. The broader literature was more useful, though most of it was fragmented, incomplete, written by researchers who didn't fully understand what they were documenting.

One paper stood out. It was twenty years old, published in a journal Roan had never heard of, and written by someone named H. Tarn.

Lord the eastern signatory. The Eastern Tide.

Roan read the abstract twice, then the full text. The paper described a series of experiments, on unwilling subjects, though the author never used that word, involving accelerated advancement techniques. The subjects had been pushed beyond normal limits, into a zone the paper called "threshold crossing." Most of them had experienced what the author described as "residual consciousness bleed-through",a phenomenon where the breakthrough energy carried traces of previous experiences, previous owners, previous applications.

The paper concluded that threshold crossing was dangerous but useful. It recommended further study.

It also described exactly the symptoms Brendt was experiencing.

Roan bookmarked the paper, copied key passages, and kept reading. The deeper he went, the more the picture solidified. Lord Tarn hadn't just documented the phenomenon, he'd developed techniques to control it, to amplify it, to weaponize it. The experiments had continued for years, funded by unknown sources, conducted in facilities that appeared on no official records.

The final section of the paper was missing. Pages 847 through 852 had been removed, cut from the journal with surgical precision. Whatever conclusion Tarn had reached was gone.

But Roan didn't need Tarn's conclusion. He had his own.

The voices Brendt was hearing weren't his own mind fragmenting. They were something else, echoes, possibly, from the advancement technique itself. The procedure had been developed by Tarn, refined over decades, and somehow found its way into the hands of families in Greycross who were contracted to produce a specific kind of operative.

Brendt wasn't the first. He was one of many.

Roan closed the laptop, stared at the wall. The library's ambient noise faded into the background, replaced by the hum of calculations running beneath his conscious thought.

The Eastern Tide had been one of the Founding Seven. His territory bordered the Hollow Reaches, the domain where the Inversion archive had once thrived before being buried decades ago. The coincidence was tempting to dismiss, but Roan had learned, from his father's journals, that coincidences were often the residue of buried patterns.

Tarn's interest in breakthrough techniques. Tarn's proximity to the Hollow Reaches. The existence of the Inversion itself, with its ability to transform pain into power.

The threads were connecting. They were connecting in ways Roan didn't like.

He pulled out his comm, checked the time. Forty minutes until his meeting with Drogan. Enough time to review his notes, to formulate a strategy, to decide how much truth he could afford to share.

His eye caught movement near the library's main entrance. Selene Ardent, walking between the stacks with a folder of documents. She paused at a shelf, pulled a book, flipped through pages with the focused intensity of someone who was pretending not to notice him.

She was failing.

Selene had been watching him for weeks. Roan had noted her glances in the dining hall, her questions during group training, her presence at events where she had no obvious reason to attend. She wasn't subtle about it. She wasn't trying to be.

She was gathering data. The question was what she planned to do with it.

Roan returned his attention to his laptop, pretended to be absorbed in his research. Let Selene watch. Let her build whatever theories she was constructing. She wouldn't find anything actionable, not yet, not without proof.

And Roan was very good at not providing proof.

The comm buzzed. A message from an unknown number:

*Mr. Vance. We need to discuss your friend's condition. My people can help. Come to the address below at 9 PM tonight. Come alone.*

Below the message was a set of coordinates. An address in the industrial district, near the warehouses.

Roan read the message twice. Three times.

He didn't recognize the number. He didn't recognize the phrasing, too formal for a student, too casual for a Sentinel, too confident for someone who didn't know what he was dealing with.

The message was a trap. Almost certainly. The address was a location designed to give the sender home advantage, to isolate him from potential help, to create conditions where they could observe his response without engaging directly.

But they knew about Brendt. They knew about the western district. They knew enough to contact Roan directly, which meant they'd been watching long enough to identify him as relevant.

The tally was shifting again. New variables. New risks. New opportunities.

He could ignore the message. Block the number, increase his security protocols, assume the sender would try again through a different channel. That was the safe option. That was the rational option.

Or he could go. Walk into whatever they'd prepared, assess the threat firsthand, maybe learn something about who was buying and breaking people like Brendt.

That was the dangerous option. That was the option that fit his patterns.

Roan typed a reply:

*9 PM. I'll be there.*

He sent it before he could recalculate.

The response came in seconds:

*Good. We'll take care of your friend.*

Then the number vanished from his comm, along with the message. They'd used disposable tech, self-wiping, untraceable through normal channels. Whoever they were, they knew what they were doing.

Selene was still pretending not to watch him. The library was still quiet. Outside the windows, the afternoon was fading toward evening, shadows lengthening across the courtyard paths.

Roan closed his laptop, gathered his books, and prepared to leave. He had two hours until the meeting with Drogan. Two hours to decide how much to reveal, how to position Brendt's situation, how to navigate the conversation without triggering suspicion about his own capabilities.

And then he had a meeting at 9 PM with people who knew too much and wanted something he wasn't sure he could give.

The ledger was getting complicated. But it always was.

He stepped out of the library, let the door swing shut behind him, and started walking toward the fourth-year dormitories. There was work to do.

- break -

Drogan's office was cluttered in the way of someone who had stopped caring about appearances years ago. Books lined every wall, stacked in piles where shelves had overflowed. A half-eaten sandwich sat on the desk, abandoned and presumably forgotten. The window looked out over the training grounds, offering a view of students running drills in the failing light.

The Warden himself was standing by the window, arms crossed, watching the practice matches with the expression of a man who had seen too many promising fighters break themselves against bad fundamentals.

"You've been busy," Drogan said without turning. "The western district. The rescue. The paperwork that followed."

"Brendt needed help."

"Brendt needed a lot of things. Help was only one of them." Drogan turned, fixed Roan with the flat stare that had intimidated temperamental students for decades. "His family pushed him through forced advancement. You knew that before you went in. You planned the rescue accordingly."

"I planned it to minimize casualties."

"You planned it to get him out before they could finish what they started." Drogan's voice was hard. "I'm not criticizing. I'm noting. You took significant risk to achieve a specific outcome. That's not how you usually operate."

Roan said nothing. The room went quiet. "Brendt's file crossed my desk this morning," Drogan continued. "Diagnosis: breakthrough-Sidewound interaction, acute stage, cause unknown. Treatment: monitoring and observation. Prognosis: uncertain."

"Uncertain is better than dead."

"True. But also expensive." Drogan moved to his desk, picked up a folder. "The family has been contacted by representatives from Vellmark Conservatory. They're interested in Brendt's case, interested enough to offer significant resources in exchange for access to his medical files and ongoing observation rights."

Roan's jaw tightened.

Vellmark. House Vell. The Vell patriarch had been one of the Founding Seven, and House Vell controlled the northeastern territories along with a significant portion of the global pharmaceutical trade. Their interest in breakthrough-Sidewound phenomena wasn't coincidental.

"What did the family say?"

"They haven't said anything yet. They're considering." Drogan set the folder down. "Which means you have a window. Not a large one—House Vell moves fast when they see profit, but a window nonetheless."

"A window for what?"

"To decide what you want to do about your friend."

Roan met the Warden's gaze. Drogan wasn't offering help directly. He was offering information, context, strategic terrain that couldn't be accessed through normal channels. He was also, Roan realized, testing him.

The test wasn't about loyalty. It wasn't about capability. It was about judgment, about whether Roan would try to handle this alone or accept that some problems required resources he didn't have.

"I'll need to move him," Roan said. "Out of the Academy's medical facility, away from his family's jurisdiction."

"That's possible. Difficult, but possible. The paperwork alone would take weeks, but I have contacts who can expedite it." Drogan paused. "It would also require explaining why you're so invested in this particular student. Why a fourth-year with no family connections and no apparent personal interest suddenly cares enough to disrupt institutional processes."

"Would that be a problem?"

"That depends on what else you've been doing that I don't know about." The Warden's voice was flat. "There are rumors, Roan. About the western district. About a sequence of events that doesn't match the official report. About a student who fought like someone three ranks above his documented level and walked away without a scratch."

Roan kept his face neutral. "Rumors."

"Not the kind that reach official channels. The kind that circulate in quieter circles. The kind that make certain people wonder if there's something about the Vance family that wasn't in the public records." Drogan leaned forward slightly. "I'm not asking you to tell me everything. I'm asking you to tell me enough that I can help without stumbling into something that gets us both killed."

The office was quiet. Outside, the training grounds had emptied, students retreating to dormitories and dining halls. The light through the window had gone amber, then grey.

"Brendt was experimenteded on," Roan said finally. "Forced advancement using a technique that wasn't approved for civilian use. The technique has origins in documents that shouldn't exist outside classified archives. It's connected to House Vell, to other houses with interests in accelerated advancement."

"The Eastern Tide's research."

"You've seen it?"

"I've seen fragments. Enough to know that what House Vell is doing now is an echo of something that started decades ago." Drogan's expression was unreadable. "The Founding Seven destroyed an entire civilization to suppress certain knowledge. They failed, or they succeeded in ways that created new problems. The research you're describing is one of those new problems."

Roan processed this. Drogan knew more than he'd let on. Drogan had been carrying information that connected to Roan's own discoveries, to the patterns he'd been tracing since the beginning of his time at the Academy.

"The technique Brendt was subjected to produces specific symptoms," Roan said. "Auditory phenomena. Instability. The sense that something is waiting to break through. It's designed to make the subject dependent on handlers, functional enough to perform, unstable enough to follow orders."

"And you know this how?"

"Because I've studied the mechanisms." Not a lie. Not the whole truth, either. "The breakthrough energy doesn't integrate cleanly with forced advancement. It leaves gaps, resonances, traces of previous applications. The voices Brendt hears are echoes from those traces."

Drogan was quiet. Then he nodded, slowly, like a man who'd reached a conclusion he'd been approaching for some time.

"There's a facility," he said. "In the northern reaches. Officially, it's a rehabilitation center for veterans with breakthrough-related injuries. Unofficially, it's where House Vell sends the cases that don't fit their standard profiles."

"You want me to go there."

"I want you to know it exists. What you do with that knowledge is your decision." Drogan picked up a pen, began writing on a pad of paper. "Brendt's transfer can be arranged within forty-eight hours. The facility I mentioned is one option, the others are messier but workable. Whatever you decide, the window is closing. House Vell isn't patient when they see profit."

He slid the paper across the desk. An address. A contact name. A set of numbers that might've been access codes or coordinates.

Roan took the paper, folded it, tucked it into his pocket. "Thank you."

"Don't thank me yet." Drogan returned to the window, looked out at the darkening grounds. "Whatever's happening with Brendt, whatever's happening with House Vell and the research they inherited from the Eastern Tide, it's bigger than one student's recovery. The patterns you're describing, the connections you're tracing, they lead somewhere. I don't know where yet, but I've seen enough to know that following them gets dangerous."

"Dangerous how?"

"so dangerous where people who ask questions stop asking." Drogan turned, met Roan's eyes. "Your father disappeared asking questions. I'm not saying that to be dramatic—I'm saying it because it's true, and because I think you already know."

The room was silent. The weight of the statement settled between them.

"He didn't disappear," Roan said quietly. "He was killed. By people who didn't want the knowledge he had."

"Yes." Drogan's voice was heavy. "And now you're asking the same questions, walking the same paths, following the same patterns. The difference is you know someone who can falsify a transfer order. Don't push too hard on that difference."

The words hung in the air. Outside, the last light was fading from the training grounds, the equipment shadows stretching long across the grass.

Roan said nothing. He stood, moved toward the door. He paused with his hand on the frame.

"I'll have Brendt moved," he said. "Tomorrow. The facility you mentioned—I'll look into it."

"Good." Drogan turned back to the window. "And Roan? Whatever meeting you're planning for tonight, be careful. The people who do what House Vell does don't make casual invitations."

Roan didn't ask how the Warden knew about the message. He didn't need to He left the office, walked out into the evening, and started calculating the odds.

Nine PM. The industrial district. People who knew about Brendt, who had access to breakthrough research, who were confident enough to invite him to a meeting they controlled.

The trap was obvious. The opportunity was obvious.

The question was whether he could spring the trap on his own terms.

He pulled out his comm, checked the time. Four hours until 9 PM. Four hours to prepare, to plan, to set up whatever contingencies he could manage with the resources he had.

The risk was high. But that had never stopped him before.

Paper kept its word better than people kept theirs.

He started walking faster, mind already turning over possibilities, assessing angles, preparing for whatever waited in the industrial district.

Brendt's voice echoed in his memory: *Okay.*

One word. One trust.

Roan wouldn't waste it.
