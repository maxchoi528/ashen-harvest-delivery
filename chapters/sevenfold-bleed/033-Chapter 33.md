Chapter 33: Mind Trial

The morning after Lyra's outburst in the corridor, Roan found Brendt waiting outside the dormitory stairwell.

"You heard," Roan said. Not a question.

"Heard?" Brendt pushed off the wall. "The whole floor heard. Lyra doesn't yell. She never yells."

"People reach their limit."

"And your solution is to let her think you're—" Brendt stopped himself. Took a breath. "Never mind. I know better than to ask."

Roan descended the stairs without breaking stride. Brendt fell in beside him.

"Theon Mercer is asking questions," Brendt said quietly. "About the sparring match. About why you let Aldric beat you so badly, then somehow won the next three rounds."

"Tell him I'm inconsistent."

"I told him you're a mystery. He said mysteries are just problems that haven't been solved yet."

Roan glanced sideways. "Theon Mercer quotes riddles now?"

"After you beat Aldric three times in a row without taking a single solid hit? The man's obsessed." Brendt's expression shifted. "He's not the only one. I heard Selene Ardent talking to Warden Drogan after your match. She asked about your footwork. Specifically. She wanted to know where you learned to read weight distribution like that."

Roan's jaw tightened for half a second. He made it relax.

Selene Ardent was Crowned-tier. A third-year student on the fast track to Adamant. She didn't ask about first-years' footwork unless something about it was worth asking.

"Warden Drogan told her it was a family technique," Brendt continued. "Which was technically true, I guess. But Selene didn't look convinced. She looked like someone who'd just found a trail worth following."

They emerged into the courtyard. Morning drills were already underway, clusters of students running forms, pairs practicing strikes against practice dummies. The air smelled of sweat and the faint metallic tang of Vitalis exertion.

"Math doesn't change," Roan said. "People who look too closely eventually see what they expect to see. Not what's there."

"Unless you're interesting enough that they keep looking."

"Then I become a problem they need to solve. Problems that can't be solved get eliminated."

Brendt was quiet for a moment. "You're not worried."

"I'm calculating the probability distribution of outcomes. There's a difference."

Theon Mercer was waiting near the training yard's east entrance. He stood with his arms crossed, watching the drills with the detached focus of someone cataloging everything. When Roan and Brendt approached, his eyes tracked Roan like a targeting system.

"Vance." Mercer's voice was flat. "Walk with me."

It wasn't a request.

Brendt tensed. Roan's hand brushed his arm, *steady*, and he followed Mercer through a gap in the crowd toward the quieter yards near the Spire's outer wall.

"You fight like someone who's been in real combat," Mercer said without preamble. He stopped near a stone pillar, blocking casual view of their conversation. "Not sparring combat. Not tournament combat. Survival combat."

Roan said nothing.

"I've reviewed your record," Mercer continued. "Three years at Black Spire. Fourteen formal matches. Seven wins, seven losses. All losses to students ranked above you. All wins against students ranked below or equal to you. Nothing unusual." He paused. "Nothing interesting."

"Until yesterday."

"Until yesterday." Mercer's gray eyes were steady. "You lost the first round to Aldric Vell. Convincingly. Your form was sloppy, your footwork was lazy, you telegraphed every strike. I'd have ranked you Initiate-peak, maybe two months from Tempered breakthrough if you were lucky."

He let that sit.

"Round two, you were faster. Still losing, but faster. Round three, you started reading his patterns. Round four through seven—" Mercer shook his head slowly. "You weren't fighting Aldric Vell anymore. You were dismantling him."

"People have good days."

"People don't develop combat instinct overnight. Not from Initiate to something that felt like Tempered-peak in the span of twenty minutes." Mercer stepped closer. "My master told me there are two kinds of secrets in this world. The kind people keep because they want privacy. And the kind people keep because disclosure means death. You don't strike me as someone who values privacy."

Roan's expression didn't change. "You're perceptive."

"I'm practical. Someone at this school has a fast-track technique that hasn't been catalogued. Either it's in the restricted archives and you're stealing access, or someone is teaching you techniques that should be forbidden. Either way, the Conclave would be very interested."

The threat hung in the air.

Roan considered. Mercer was smart, smarter than average. He wasn't making idle accusations; he was establishing terms. The question was: what did he want?

"You could report me," Roan said. "But you haven't. You're standing here talking instead."

"I'm a problem-solver. I solve problems. But I also know the difference between a student hiding a technique and a student with a genuinely novel approach to combat." Mercer's voice dropped. "I know what it looks like when someone's working outside the standard curriculum. My master taught me to spot deviations. You're a deviation."

"That doesn't explain why you're telling me this instead of reporting me to the Conclave."

Mercer's mouth curved slightly. Not a smile. "Because I want to see what happens next. And because my master prefers to keep certain things off the records until they're fully understood. Consider that a... professional courtesy."

The morning bell rang across the courtyard. Students began filtering toward the main hall for the day's first lecture.

Mercer stepped aside, clearing the path. "I'm not your friend, Vance. I'm not your enemy either. I'm someone who collects information because information is power. But I don't sell information that isn't mine to sell."

Roan studied him for three full seconds. "What do you get out of watching me?"

"The satisfaction of solving an interesting problem." Mercer was already walking away. "Plus, if you're hiding something genuinely new, I want to learn it before the Conclave makes it classified."

He disappeared into the crowd of students.

Brendt, who had followed at a distance, appeared at Roan's shoulder. "What did he want?"

"Attention." Roan resumed walking toward the lecture hall. "He's curious about me. Someone with authority is watching from behind him, and Mercer just admitted he's the eyes. He didn't tell me who his master is, but he let me know I'm being observed. That's a gift, but also a leash."

"Whose authority?"

"Not sure yet. But Mercer's too smart to run a solo operation. He's reporting to someone high enough to keep the Conclave off his back." Roan paused at the lecture hall entrance. "The question is whether that someone is a threat or an opportunity."

They found seats in the third row, close enough to be visible, far enough to exit quickly if needed. The hall filled rapidly. Instructor Harwick took the podium, already shuffling notes.

"Last session, we discussed the basic principles of Vitalis circulation through the primary meridian channels. Today: complications."

Roan's attention sharpened. *Complications* meant *side effects*. And side effects were, in his particular case, considerably more interesting than they were for most students.

"Vitalis circulation isn't a simple pipeline," Harwick said. "It's a living system. It responds to stress, to training, to the methods you use to accelerate growth. Every technique that pushes your body beyond natural limits carries a cost. We call these costs side effects, and understanding them is critical to avoiding permanent damage."

The lecture turned to meridian strain, the accumulation of micro-trauma in channels that were pushed too hard. Harwick described the standard symptoms, aching joints, reduced circulation efficiency, the risk of scarring that could permanently narrow a pathway.

"Standard curriculum teaches you to manage these side effects through pacing," Harwick said. "Train hard, but not so hard that you cause lasting harm. The Conclave's position is clear: sustainable growth is better than explosive growth. A student who advances slowly but safely will ultimately outperform someone who burns bright and burns out."

Roan wrote nothing. He already knew all of this. The question was what Harwick would say next, the gaps in the standard curriculum, the assumptions that weren't true.

"There are, of course, techniques that promise faster results," Harwick continued. "The Bloodscourge line of catalysts. The Pyrebrand cycling methods. The Sevenfold Bleed discipline. These approaches can accelerate your growth by orders of magnitude." He paused. "They can also kill you."

A ripple of unease passed through the hall. Everyone had heard of these techniques. Everyone had heard they were forbidden.

"Meritocrats will tell you these methods are banned because they're too dangerous," Harwick said. "That's partially true. But the real reason—" He paused, scanning the room. "The real reason is something you'll need to figure out for yourselves. The Conclave's restrictions aren't just about safety. They're about control. About what happens when too many people advance too fast. I'll leave you to consider the implications."

Someone in the back muttered, "So they're hoarding techniques."

Harwick didn't confirm or deny. "I'm not telling you to seek out forbidden methods. I'm telling you that the decision to use them isn't just about danger. It's about what you're willing to sacrifice and why. Every technique has a cost. The question is whether the benefit justifies the price."

Roan's mind completed the logic before Harwick had finished the sentence. *Scarcity. The ranking system depends on scarcity. If every student could accelerate with unrestricted techniques, the value of slow progress would collapse. The old houses built their power on that exclusivity. Harwick just handed me the key piece.* He filed it away.

The lecture continued. Harwick described case studies, students who had pushed too hard and suffered permanent injuries, students who had used catalysts improperly and developed chronic meridian degradation, students who had died.

Roan listened to all of it. The patterns were useful. The specific details of what happened when things went wrong, those were what he filed away.

*Bloodscourge causes gradual meridian scarring if the cycling isn't completed within a specific window. Pyrebrand requires a minimum base Vitalis threshold or the heat will consume the user from inside. Sevenfold Bleed demands perfect balance between all seven pathways or the strain will cascade.*

None of these were secrets. Every trained cultivator knew the standard warnings. What they didn't know, what they couldn't know, was that for Roan, the costs were just different. Not eliminated, but inverted.

A Sidewound absorbed was a Sidewound *reversed*.

He had spent seventeen years thinking he was broken. Now he understood: the Codex Shard hadn't broken him. It had made him into something the system wasn't designed to account for.

And as long as he was careful, as long as he managed the Inversion properly, no one would ever realize what he really was.

The lecture ended. Students filed out. Brendt lingered at Roan's side.

"You looked like you were taking mental notes," Brendt said. "But you didn't write anything."

"Writing attracts attention." Roan stood, stretching meridian pathways that ached with yesterday's sparring. "Theon Mercer is watching me. Selene Ardent asked Drogan about my techniques. The less physical evidence I leave, the harder it's for anyone to build a case."

"Paranoid much?"

"Calculating." Roan headed toward the door. "There's a difference."

They stepped into the corridor. The lunch crowd was thick, students flowing between training yards and dormitories. In the distance, near the main staircase, a cluster of third-years surrounded someone.

Roan recognized the posture before he recognized the face.

Lyra.

She stood with her shoulders back, chin raised, facing down three students who were trying to intimidate her. Even from this distance, Roan's trained eye caught the tension in her stance, weight balanced, hands loose, ready to move in any direction.

She'd learned something since that night in the corridor.

He changed direction without breaking stride.

"—told you, I don't know what you're talking about." Lyra's voice carried. "I don't know anything about any shadow program."

"Your brother knows something," one of the third-years said. A tall boy with the build of a Hardened-tier fighter. "He's been getting instruction outside normal channels. Someone's been teaching him."

"Roan doesn't talk to me about his training."

"But you know things. You have access to the same resources. All we want is—"

"What you want is to steal something that isn't yours." Lyra's voice didn't waver. "The same way your house tried to steal from House Vance when my father died. You don't get to come crawling back now that you think there's something worth taking."

The third-year's expression hardened. "Careful, Vance. You're still Initiate-tier. I'm Hardened."

"Rank doesn't make you right. It just makes you dangerous to people you could hurt without consequence." Lyra's eyes flicked past him, toward Roan, who had arrived at the edge of the group. "It just makes you someone who picks fights they can win."

The third-year turned. His eyes met Roan's. "Warden Drogan's new favorite," the third-year said. "The one Mercer can't figure out."

"Garrick," one of the other third-years murmured. "Leave it alone."

"No." Garrick stepped forward. "He's been getting special treatment. Special access. And whatever technique he's using, it's not in any catalog. That means either it's forbidden, or it's new. Either way, the Conclave should know about it."

Roan's expression didn't change. "Report me, then. See what Drogan does when you interrupt his program."

"Drogan doesn't own this school. The Conclave does."

"The Conclave owns the curriculum. Drogan owns what happens in the yards after hours." Roan's voice was flat. "You're Hardened-tier. You know the difference between what you're allowed to do and what happens. Walk away, Garrick. You've already made an enemy of House Vance once. Don't make the same mistake twice."

The threat landed. Garrick's jaw tightened.

"Keep your secrets, Vance." He stepped back, gesturing to his companions. "But secrets have a way of coming out. And when yours does, you'll wish you'd never set foot in this school."

They left. The corridor crowd flowed back into the spaces they'd vacated, conversations resuming as if nothing had happened.

Lyra exhaled slowly. "Thanks."

"You handled it."

"I was about to make it worse." She looked at him, her gaze still carrying the remnants of anger, but something else was working its way through. She caught Garrick's back as he retreated, the tension in his shoulders, the way he'd looked at Roan. Not with contempt. With *wariness*. That look struck her harder than any threat. She realized, in that moment, what it must have cost Roan to make an enemy like Garrick look at him that way. The pieces clicked together—the corridor fight, the secrets, the distance he kept. He wasn't ignoring her. He was keeping the fire away from her.

"You knew that, didn't you?" she said, her voice quieter now. "You came over because you knew Garrick was about to push me into saying something I'd regret."

Roan said nothing. He didn't confirm. He didn't deny.

Lyra's expression flickered. "You've been protecting me," she said. "This whole time. Even when I screamed at you in the corridor. Even when I said—" She stopped. Swallowed. "I know you aren't ignoring me, Roan. Not really. I know you're... doing something. I don't know what. But you're not the brother I thought you were."

Roan's hand twitched. Almost reached for her. Stopped.

"I'm exactly the brother you thought I was," he said. "I'm just doing math you haven't seen yet."

"That's not an answer."

"No. It's not." He turned to leave.

"Roan." Lyra's voice stopped him. "Whatever you're planning, whatever you're hiding—I'm not going to expose you. I want you to know that. Even if I don't understand it. Even if it scares me. You're still my brother. That math hasn't changed."

He stood still for a moment. The noise of the corridor washed around them.

"Don't make promises you can't keep," he said finally. Then he walked away, disappearing into the crowd before she could respond.

Brendt caught up with him near the training yard entrance. "What was that?"

"Controlled information release."

"She just said she won't betray you."

"She said she won't betray what she thinks she understands. Lyra doesn't know what I'm doing. When she finds out, and she will find out, people always find out, she'll have to decide if her loyalty is to family or to the system she believes in." Roan's voice was distant. "I gave her time to prepare for that decision."

"And if she chooses wrong?"

The question hung between them.

Roan's expression didn't change. "Then I'll adjust the math."

He entered the training yard. The afternoon session was beginning, sparring matches, technique drills, the rhythm of bodies learning to be weapons. Somewhere in the crowd of students, Theon Mercer was watching. Somewhere in the faculty wing, Selene Ardent was thinking about weight distribution and family techniques.

And somewhere in the back of his mind, the Codex Shard waited. Patient. Ready.

*Sidewound absorbed. Capacity: 4/4. Inversion Yield: 100%.*

The tally never stopped. The calculations never ended. Every day brought new variables, new uncertainties, new threats that had to be accounted for.

But the foundation held.

Everything he'd built since his father's death, the mask of mediocrity, the careful calculations, the slow accumulation of power that no one could see, all of it served this moment. This preparation. This waiting.

The Conclave wanted to control how people grew. The old houses wanted to keep the old ways locked away forever. Garrick wanted to expose him, Mercer wanted to solve him, Selene wanted to understand him.

Let them want.

Roan Vance had been wanting for seventeen years. He knew the difference between desire and power.

He picked up a training staff and moved to the practice circle.

And somewhere, in the depths of the Codex Shard, a fragment of Aldric Vance's consciousness noted the irony: his son had become exactly the calculating, patient predator that the old houses had once feared.

*The Inversion works on side effects*, the fragment whispered. *But so do I.*

*Be careful what you reverse, kid. Some costs are meant to be paid.*

*— A.V.*

The warning faded. The training continued.

And outside the window of the lecture hall, a figure in a dark coat watched the yard, watched Roan. The coat's cuffs bore a sigil that wasn't part of any Spire crest—a coiled serpent, or maybe a root. The figure turned and walked away, the hem dragging just enough to brush the stone floor with a faint clink of metal. No one noticed.

- break -

Evening fell over Black Spire like a held breath.

Roan sat alone in his room, the Codex Shard resting on his palm. The day's events replayed in his mind: Mercer's observation, Harwick's lecture, Garrick's threat, Lyra's promise.

*Garrick reported to someone. Someone sent that watcher.*

He didn't know who. He didn't know why. But the mathematics were clear: he was drawing attention faster than he'd planned. The sparring match had accelerated a timeline that should have taken months to unfold.

*Adjust*, the Codex whispered. *Adapt. The ledger changes, you change with it.*

Or he could stop adjusting. Stop adapting. Stop being the careful, calculating shadow that everyone kept trying to shine light on.

The Codex Shard pulsed faintly against his skin.

*Your call, kid. But remember: once you step out of the dark, you can't step back in.*

*— A.V.*

Roan closed his fingers around the Shard.

Tomorrow, he'd train harder. Push the Inversion further. Find new techniques to absorb, new side effects to reverse.

Let them watch. Let them calculate.

The only math that mattered was his own.
