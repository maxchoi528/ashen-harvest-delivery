Chapter 46: Ironholm

The morning briefing room smelled of old sweat and nervous adrenaline. Fifteen initiates sat in metal chairs arranged in three rows, their posture varying from rigid to slouched depending on how many expeditions they'd survived. Roan took a seat in the back corner, second from the left. Optimal sightlines. Unobstructed exit.

Warden Drogan stood at the front, his Iron Hand resting on the briefing table. The prosthetic limb caught the overhead light, its joints gleaming with recent oiling. He'd been maintaining it more frequently lately. Something was wearing on the old man.

"Surface Rift alert in the Northern Reaches," Drogan said. His voice carried without projecting, a trick learned from decades of command. "Tier-2 incursion. We need a six-person sweep team to verify containment lines and extract any civilian stragglers."

Roan's eyes narrowed. Tier-2. Husks and Wraiths, mostly. Dangerous in quantity but manageable for a coordinated squad. The problem wasn't the Rift. The problem was the team composition.

"Initiates Holloway and Ashford, plus Vance, you're assigned. The rest of you—" Drogan gestured to the remaining twelve, "—maintain your regular training schedules."

Cyrus Ashford turned in his seat, a thin smile crossing his face. "Wonderful. Our resident Vance gets to play hero again. Don't embarrass yourself, Vance."

Roan didn't respond. He was calculating.

Three initiates. One Tempered Warden. Against a Tier-2 Rift that required six-person containment.

The ledger didn't add up.

- break -

The transport shuttle hummed through the Greycross industrial district, its windows rattling against the frame. Brendt sat across from Roan, his knuckles white where they gripped his thighs. Across the aisle, Cyrus had claimed the window seat, his posture deliberately relaxed.

"So, Vance," Cyrus said, not looking away from the window. "I hear your little sister is having trouble keeping up in her cohort. Shame about that. Vance bloodlines used to mean something."

Roan watched the other initiate's reflection in the glass. The taunt was amateur. Cyrus was testing for a reaction, fishing for leverage.

"She's fine," Roan said. "Focus on your own form, Ashford. The Ashford Spire isn't going to win itself."

Cyrus's jaw tightened. Good. The boy didn't like being reminded that his family weapon was a shadow of its former glory.

Brendt leaned closer to Roan, voice low. "This smells wrong. Tier-2 Rift, six-person minimum, and they send three initiates with one Warden?"

"Agreed," Roan said. "Keep your Vitalis circulation loose. Don't let them know you're ready, but stay ready."

The shuttle banked hard left, descending toward the Northern Reaches.

- break -

The Rift site was worse than the briefing suggested.

The containment lines had been breached in three places, the monitoring pylons toppled like broken teeth. The rift itself pulsed in the clearing ahead, a vertical tear approximately four meters wide, its edges crackling with sickly green light.

Drogan surveyed the damage, his expression unreadable. "Someone pulled the pylons down deliberately."

Roan catalogued the evidence. The pylon bases showed tool marks, not impact damage. Someone had dismantled the containment system.

"The civilians," he said.

"Missing," Drogan confirmed. "Three farmsteads reported evacuated yesterday. No confirmation they left."

Cyrus let out a low whistle. "So we're bait. Whoever sabotaged this wants something from the Rift, and they needed a distraction."

"Or a clean-up crew," Brendt added.

Roan shook his head. "Wrong angle. If they wanted the Rift cleared, they'd do it themselves. This is staged. Someone wants the Sentinel Bureau to look incompetent."

"Conspiracy theories later," Drogan cut in. "Holloway, take point. Ashford, rear guard. Vance, you're on Rift perimeter watch. We go in, we find survivors, we get out. No heroics."

The three initiates took their positions. Brendt moved toward the rift's edge, his hand drifting to the short sword at his hip. Cyrus hung back, positioning himself at the shuttle's boarding ramp. Roan circled wide, placing himself where he could observe all three points of entry.

Drogan approached the rift itself, raising his Iron Hand. The prosthetic's fingers spread, a soft glow building between them.

"Wraith presence confirmed," he called out. "Multiple signatures. Brace yourselves."

The rift shuddered. A Husk crawled through first, its body a grotesque fusion of animal and machine, plates of organic armor fused to a skeletal frame that moved wrong in seven different directions. Another followed. Then a third.

Seven Husks total. And behind them, circling in the corrupted air, three Wraiths, translucent figures of solidified Vitalis hunger, their forms flickering like dying flames.

Roan's jaw tightened. The briefing said "possible" Wraith presence. These were confirmed.

"Drogan," he called out. "That's too many for standard sweep protocol."

"I see it, boy." The Warden's voice was tight. "Fall back to the shuttle. All of you."

Cyrus was already moving, his retreat a smooth, practiced sprint. Brendt hesitated, his eyes on the advancing Husks. One of them hissed, a sound like steam escaping a cracked pipe, and charged.

Roan moved without thinking.

His body dropped low, rolling under the Husk's first swipe, his hand finding the combat knife at his belt. The creature's claws carved through the air where his skull had been. He came up inside its guard, driving the blade into the gap between two armor plates.

The Husk shrieked and buckled. Roan twisted the knife, ripped it free, and rolled again as two more Husks flanked him.

"BRENTT!"

The shout tore from his throat. His classmate was pinned against a fallen pylon, one Husk's claws raking across his shoulder, blood spraying in bright arcs. Another Husk was closing fast.

Roan ran. Not toward the shuttle. Toward Brendt.

Math time. Two Husks between him and his friend. Fifteen meters. Vitalis at eight hundred and change, enough for one full inversion cycle if he triggered it. The pain would register. The Codex Shard would convert.

He crossed five meters in a heartbeat.

The first Husk turned toward him. Roan feinted left, dropped into a slide, and drove his knife into the creature's exposed underbelly. Hot fluid sprayed across his forearm. The Husk convulsed and collapsed.

Eleven meters.

The second Husk lunged. Roan threw himself sideways, felt claws rake across his ribs, and the pain bloomed, sharp, immediate, real. A heartbeat.

[Sidewound absorbed.]

[Conversion successful: +187 Vitalis.]

[Capacity: 3/4.]

The pain vanished. The Husk stumbled, confused by prey that wouldn't stay hurt.

Roan came up inside its guard and buried the knife in its throat.

Brendt.

Six meters. Brendt was struggling to his feet, clutching his shoulder, blood matting his shirt. The third Husk was three meters away and closing.

Roan reached him as the creature pounced. He caught it mid-air, one arm taking the impact, and they crashed into the dirt together. The Husk's jaws snapped at his face. He shoved his knife hand up, catching it under the chin, and drove upward.

The blade punched through bone. The Husk went limp.

Silence.

Roan rolled the body off himself and stood. His ribs throbbed, phantom sensation, but the memory of real pain lingered. Across the clearing, Drogan was finishing off the last Husk with mechanical efficiency, his Iron Hand crushing what passed for the creature's spine.

The Wraiths circled overhead, but they hadn't descended. Something was keeping them at bay.

Then Roan saw it.

A figure stood at the rift's edge, barely visible against the haze. A man in a heavy Greycrest industrial coat, hood pulled low against the residual wind of the collapsing portal. He held a tablet in his gloved hands, the screen angled. Not recording the scene. Recording Roan.

The man's other hand rested on a device at his belt. Bronze casing. A pattern of interlocking claws embossed into the metal. Northern workmanship.

The man lowered the tablet. He looked directly at Roan. And nodded.

A nod of acknowledgment.

Then he touched the device on his belt. The Wraiths drew back, folding into the rift's glow. The man followed them, moving not through a portal, but into the smoke and debris kicked up by the Rift's contraction. He didn't vanish. He retreated behind a curtain of distortion and dying light.

The rift shrank. Sealed. Collapsed.

Gone.

- break -

Back on the shuttle, Brendt's shoulder was bandaged and bleeding through despite the pressure dressing. The wound was deep. He'd lost function in his rotator cuff. Weeks of recovery, assuming no infection.

Cyrus sat in the far corner, silent. He'd done nothing during the fight. Not one intervention, not one supportive action. He'd watched Brendt get mauled and done nothing.

That went into the ledger.

Drogan paced the cargo bay, his Iron Hand clenching and unclenching. "Someone sabotaged those containment pylons. Someone drew us into an active Rift scenario. And someone was watching the whole thing."

"He was taking notes on you specifically," Brendt said, his voice thin. "I saw the lens angle."

Drogan stopped pacing. "Northern metals. That bronze casing on his belt wasn't Bureau issue. Vance, you have something they want, or something they're afraid of. Your profile just went from 'initiate' to 'asset'."

Roan met the Warden's gaze without flinching. "The question isn't who. It's what they think I am. And whether they're satisfied with what they saw."

Brendt stirred on the bench, his face pale from blood loss. "Roan. The device. The claws. I've seen that pattern before. In your father's old storage. It's a Greel auxiliary mark."

Roan's stomach dropped. Of course. Brendt had been watching too.

The balance was getting clearer, and the equation was starting to look like a Greel signature.

- break -

Night. The medical ward was quiet, the only sound the soft hum of the sterilization arrays. Brendt slept fitfully, his Vitalis circulation sluggish from the blood loss. The healers had promised to check on him in four hours.

Roan sat in the chair beside the bed.

The blood on his sleeve had dried to a stiff brown film. He didn't move to clean it. Brendt's respirator cycled. A rhythmic count. The cost was sitting right there, wrapped in bandages, because the man with the bronze device had wanted to see what Roan would do.

The Inversion had worked. The arithmetic was clean. But the cost was sitting right there.

A soft knock.

"Enter."

Selene Ardent slipped through the door, her dark hair pulled back in a practical braid. She wore training clothes. She'd come directly from the archives, not the sparring yards.

"Vance." She nodded toward Brendt. "The hospital wing has a betting pool on how long he'll stay off duty. I put two weeks."

"Optimistic."

She pulled a second chair close and sat. She waited until the door sealed behind her before speaking again.

"The maintenance logs for those pylons don't match the standard rotation. I pulled the Bureau's internal dispatch from the Greycross depot. The work order was authorized three days ago. Signed by a regional coordinator. But the routing code is wrong. It routes through a private holding company based out of Ironholm."

Roan watched her. She wasn't guessing. She was reading from a data chip.

"That holding company has been eating my family's shipping contracts for the last six months. Quietly. Legally. Through paperwork and litigation. Someone is running a structured economic bleed in the Northern Reaches. Today they used a Rift scenario to test a Bureau response. They used you as the measurement."

"You're sure it's the same hands?"

"The routing codes match. The pattern matches. They don't want territory. They want information. They want to see who fights back." She looked at Brendt. "He fought back. Now he's in a bed. This is the cost of being an interesting data point."

"Why warn me?"

"Because if they hit the Bureau to get to you, they'll find a way to hit the Spire next. I don't want to be collateral damage in your math problem. But I do want whoever is signing those routing codes to bleed. So consider me an interested observer with a shared interest in finding the source."

She stood.

"Your father taught you how to read a room, Vance. Start reading the ledgers. The answer isn't in the Apex tiers. It's in the paper trail. The claws you saw on that device—they match the seal on the holding company's charter."

She passed him at the door, close enough that her voice carried without being overheard.

"Watch your back, Vance. You just became the most interesting variable in the North's equation."

The door closed behind her.

- break -

Dawn. The sparring yards were empty save for the cleaning drones and the skeleton crew of early risers. Roan stood at the eastern fence line, watching the sun breach the horizon.

The man had a Greel-marked device.
The Rift was a test.
The paper trail led North.
Selene's holding company was the conduit.

The arithmetic was still incomplete, but the constants were clearer now. He wasn't facing a faceless Apex conspiracy. He was facing the first ripple of the Northern Crown. His father's war wasn't ancient history. It was waking up, and it had just taken its first measured swing at him.

The Codex Shard pulsed once against his spine.

[They moved first. Now you move. — A.V.]

Roan exhaled slowly. The wind carried the scent of industrial smoke and wet pavement.

The man with the bronze claws had walked away confident in his data. He thought he had the measure of Roan Vance. He thought he knew the variables.

But data was a snapshot of the past. The Inversion system didn't follow known parameters. The arithmetic he calculated wasn't an equation to be solved by them. It was a code written in a language they hadn't learned yet, and Roan knew exactly which ledgers to open next.

He had forty-eight hours to prepare.

He was done being the interesting variable.

Time to become the constant.
