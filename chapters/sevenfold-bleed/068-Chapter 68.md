Chapter 68: The Rumor Mill

The Ashworth note was ticking. Roan had run the numbers a dozen times since midnight—principal, interest, the penalty for missed payment—and the total always landed on a sum he didn't have. If he didn't clear it by tomorrow's curfew, the debt collectors would find Mira before they found him. They had a timetable and a taste for theatrical ruin.

The Hollow Marrow Elixir wasn't ambition. It was arithmetic.

The gantry smelled like oxidized copper and the sweat of men who didn't know they were already dead. Roan lay prone against the grate, forty meters above the freight floor, counting. Guard One passed the northern stanchion with irritating regularity. Guard Two lingered by the cargo lift, smoking something that burned yellow and interfered with thermal imaging for eight-second windows. The arithmetic was simple: forty-three minus eight left a margin of thirty-five, but Roan needed thirty-nine to reach crate 7-C, pick the biometric seal, and verify the payload.

Thirty-five was smaller than thirty-nine.

He recalculated. The lift motor cycled once every two minutes, drawing thirteen amps and producing a localized magnetic distortion strong enough to blind the ceiling-mounted Vitalis sensors for four additional seconds. Thirty-five plus four equaled thirty-nine.

Exactly.

The arithmetic held. For now.

Roan's fingers found the edge of the gantry. He had checked his status before climbing, out of habit, out of necessity. The Shard was dormant—no active sidewound left to invert. He'd burned the last conversion three days ago, trading a shattered radius for the Hollowstrike Doctrine. The doctrine remained. The safety net didn't. Every injury he took today would stay taken. Every drop of blood would stay spilled.

He dropped.

The fall was twelve meters. He landed behind a condensation tank, knees absorbing the impact, converting downward momentum into a forward roll that carried him into the blind spot beneath the lift controls. Guard Two exhaled a lungful of yellow smoke. Roan didn't breathe at all.

Eight seconds.

He moved.

The freight floor was a grid of stamped alloy and spilled hydraulic fluid. Crate 7-C sat beneath a sodium lamp that painted everything the color of old bruises. The label read *Medical Precursors—Handle with Extreme Caution*. The mass stamp read fourteen kilograms with packing gel. Roan had memorized the shipping manifest at the exchange terminal six hours earlier. A full vial of Hollow Marrow Elixir massed eleven kilograms in suspension. Add three kilograms of cryo-gel and a steel transit sleeve, and the total matched.

He reached the crate. The biometric seal was a Greel-model rotary lock, third-generation, vulnerable to induced Vitalis resonance if you knew the frequency. Roan knew it. He'd stolen the copper filament and conductive wire from Black Spire's maintenance bay three weeks prior, calculating that the inventory discrepancy wouldn't be noticed until the next audit cycle.

The lock clicked.

He lifted the lid.

And froze.

The weight was wrong.

Not fourteen kilograms. Nine, maybe ten. The difference was subtle, enough that a freight handler would blame scale drift, enough that a thief would already be running. Roan didn't run. He let his eyes track the interior, cataloguing the absence. No cryo-gel. No amber suspension. Just a single brushed-steel cylinder the width of his thumb, set in shredded synth-fiber, and a folded slip of cream paper wedged beneath the padding.

"Wrong."

The word left his mouth as a whisper, barely louder than the lift motor cycling overhead. He didn't touch the cylinder. He lifted the paper with two fingers and read the handwriting, tight, angular, penmanship produced by someone who had learned to write with a combat stylus.

*For the boy who thinks he can swallow the world. K.G.*

The Greel envoy.

Roan's jaw tightened. Not in surprise. Surprise was for people who didn't model their enemies' behavior. He'd known House Greel was tracking the Hollow Marrow supply lines. He hadn't predicted that the envoy would replace the shipment with a message. The error was small, eight percent variance in his threat model, but eight percent was enough to fracture a metacarpal, enough to collapse a lung.

He examined the cylinder's base. A pressure trigger, ceramic and flush-mounted, designed to fracture an internal ampule if the lid was disturbed. Not explosive. Aerosol. Distilled Hollow spore or neurolytic vapor, something that would liquefy a man's nervous system faster than he could trigger an Inversion.

Roan had no active sidewound left to fuel the Shard's response. He was running on empty biology.

He disarmed the trigger with a twist of the copper filament, grounding the circuit to the crate frame. Then he took the cylinder anyway. Leaving empty-handed was a zero-sum loss. Math didn't tolerate zeros.

The alarm reached him as vibration through the floorplates before it reached his ears, a subsonic thrum from the security station two hundred meters east. The envoy hadn't just left a note. He'd left a beacon. The crate was a tripwire, and Roan had just pulled the cord.

Two figures rounded the southern stack. Tempered, both of them, moving with the loose-jointed confidence of men who'd been told the intruder was a student, an Initiate-level thief, maybe, soft-handed and desperate. The first carried a telescoping baton. The second had a kinetic pistol, low-caliber, enough to punch through ribs but not steel.

Roan had three seconds to decide: left corridor, which terminated in a blast door; right corridor, which led to the loading dock where the Hardened captain waited according to the duty roster he'd memorized; or through the two Tempered guards, which required impact trauma he couldn't convert.

The math was ugly.

He chose the guards.

Roan stepped into the sodium light, letting them see him, seventeen, bloodied already from the gantry descent, smaller than both. The first guard grinned and swung the baton in a lazy arc meant to intimidate. Roan didn't intimidate. He calculated.

Hollowstrike Doctrine: Reflected Blow.

He met the baton with his left forearm, not blocking but guiding, redirecting the kinetic vector down and across. The guard's own force, amplified by Roan's Vitalis, transferred into the second guard's sternum with a sound like a mallet striking a melon. The second guard folded, kinetic pistol clattering. The recoil snapped up Roan's arm and drove a fracture through his second and third metacarpals, dry, precise, unmistakable.

The pain was bright and structural, radiating from his right hand into the wrist.

Roan ignored it. He had two cracked bones. The guard with the baton had a broken nose and a disoriented center of gravity. The guard on the floor had a depressed sternum and no air capacity. The ratio was acceptable.

The standing guard lunged. Roan sidestepped, letting the baton whistle past his ribs, and drove his left palm into the man's philtrum. Bone gave way. The guard dropped.

Roan looked at his right hand. Swelling already, skin tightening around the knuckles. He flexed the fingers. They moved. Not well. Well enough.

He turned toward the exit.

The Hardened captain was already there.

She filled the corridor like a structural pillar, broad and immobile, her skin gleaming with a faint silver sheen. Adamant-weave subdermal mesh, illegal for private security, standard for House Greel enforcers. She held no weapon. She didn't need one. At Hardened tier, her bones could deflect low-velocity rounds. Her bare hands could compress a Tempered ribcage like wet cardboard.

Roan's effective Vitalis was roughly one-third of hers. His hand was cracked. His Shard was silent.

"Vance," she said. Not a question. A confirmation.

He didn't answer. Silence was a weapon too.

She advanced. Roan retreated, step for step, measuring the distance to the overhead crane. Twelve meters. Ten. Eight.

She threw a punch. He ducked. The air pressure alone split the synth-fiber crate behind him. He drew his knife, not the Sidewound, that came later, just a black-market ceramic blade, and used Crimson Wake: First Cut.

The technique opened a five-centimeter gash across her forearm, bypassing the mesh at the inner elbow where the weave was thinnest. Blood sprayed, black in the sodium light.

The Sidewound hit Roan immediately.

A matching line of fire opened across his own left deltoid, deep enough to part skin and nick the lateral head. Blood poured down his arm, hot and unrelenting. He tasted copper. His vision tilted.

One liter down. Maybe one-point-one.

No conversion. No reversal. Just hemorrhage.

He stumbled. The Hardened woman smiled, watching him bleed out from his own technique, and advanced.

Roan reached the crane controls.

He'd prepared the junction box forty minutes before dropping from the gantry, looping a filament around the magnetic brake. He triggered it now with a thrown knife, not the ceramic blade; his utility spike. The brake released. Two tons of alloy plating dropped from the overhead track with a shriek of disengaged gears.

The Hardened woman looked up.

She moved fast. Hardened reflexes, but the plate clipped her shoulder and drove her into the floor, not crushing her but pinning her, fracturing the clavicle beneath the mesh.

Roan didn't wait to see if she'd free herself. He ran.

The right corridor was clear. He took it, clutching the cylinder in his broken hand, blood dripping from his left shoulder in a rhythm that matched his heartbeat. The loading dock was unmanned, diverted by the alarm, another variable in the envoy's equation. Roan hit the street level and vanished into the Ironholm fog.

- break -

The drainage culvert beneath Hab-Ring Seven was six degrees Celsius, smelling of stagnant water and industrial solvent. Roan sat on a concrete shelf, stripped to the waist, threading a wire ligature through the wound in his deltoid. The bleed was venous, not arterial. Small mercies.

He tied it off and examined his hand. The second metacarpal was visibly displaced, the third likely greenstick. Without Inversion, the healing timeline was six weeks. Without medical intervention, the bone would knit crooked and compromise his grip geometry permanently.

He checked the cylinder again. The steel sleeve unscrewed with a quarter-turn. Inside: a glass ampule of murky black fluid, too viscous for pure Hollow Marrow. He held it to the dim light. The viscosity was wrong by seventeen percent. The surface tension suggested hydrocarbon contamination, rushed synthesis, cut corners.

Seventeen percent impure.

"Seventeen percent."

He set the ampule down. The note lay beside it, mocking. The Greel envoy had handed him poison dressed as progress. The trap wasn't the aerosol trigger. The trap was hope.

Roan tilted the ampule. Under the grease-pencil label, *Aldric Series, Batch V-7*, he found a watermark etched into the glass itself. A geometric sigil. Six intersecting triangles forming a hollow core.

His stomach tightened. Not from pain. From recognition.

The sigil matched the pattern embedded in his Codex Shard.

Hollow Marrow Elixir wasn't an Earth formulation. It was from the same civilization that had built the Inversion. The same culture the Founding Seven had exterminated. The Greel envoy wasn't selling contraband. He was reverse-engineering a genocide.

Roan leaned back against the concrete. The rib the Hardened woman had tagged with her air-pressure strike throbbed in counterpoint to his pulse. He took a shallow breath, testing the pleural space. No froth. Not punctured. Bruised, maybe cracked cartilage. He had seventy-two hours before the intercostal bleeding compromised lung expansion. Maybe less.

He needed the real Elixir. Not this poison. The real compound would force a marrow rewrite, push him from Tempered to Hardened, and the physiological cascade would heal the fractures, the bleed, the tissue damage.

But the real Elixir was gone. The envoy had it. Or had destroyed it. Unless.

Roan recalculated. If the contamination was hydrocarbon sludge, the active substrate was still viable. Seventeen percent impure meant eighty-three percent pure. Eighty-three percent of a Tempered-to-Hardened catalyst was still enough to trigger the Breakthrough.

The side effects would be catastrophic. Without Inversion to buffer the transition, drinking eighty-three percent pure Hollow Marrow would liquefy seventeen percent of his marrow and leave the rest fighting a war against necrosis. He'd be a paraplegic. Or dead.

Math didn't lie.

He capped the ampule and pocketed it.

- break -

The safehouse was a maintenance closet in the Spire's sub-basement, abandoned after the last HVAC retrofit. Roan had claimed it by virtue of picking the lock and never telling anyone. He had a folding cot, a water still, and a mirror cracked in three places.

He set his hand against the steel table edge and pushed.

The metacarpal snapped back into alignment with a wet click. He didn't scream. He exhaled, hard, once. His vision grayed at the edges. He bound the hand with stripped insulation tape and a titanium splint from his pack.

The mirror showed him a face he barely recognized, pale, blood-crusted, eyes too bright. Anemia. Shock. He catalogued the symptoms like a hostile witness: tachycardia, diaphoresis, reduced capillary refill. His Vitalis reading, if he'd had a meter, would show a baseline of 1,340. His effective output was maybe 920 and bleeding downward.

He sat on the cot. The Aldric Echo surfaced, unbidden—no display, no system prompt, just the resonance of the Shard pressing a truth he already knew.

[Absorption failed. Sidewound accumulation critical.]
[Backlash warning: physiological cascade in approximately three days.]

The Shard couldn't save him from his own failed biology. Only the Breakthrough could.

"Three days."

Close enough to be inconvenient. Close enough to be a deadline.

He needed the real Hollow Marrow Elixir, and he needed it before the cascade. The Greel envoy had moved the shipment, either to destroy it or to dangle it. The question was where.

The answer arrived with a knock.

Three raps, then two. Brendt's signal.

Roan reached for the knife with his left hand. The right was useless. "Enter."

Brendt Holloway slipped inside, hood up, smelling of rain and the ozone discharge of a cheap concealment charm. He looked at Roan's hand, at the bloodied shoulder, at the ampule on the table.

"You look like hell," Brendt said.

"I look like a broken equation."

Brendt picked up the ampule, held it to the light, and paled. "Is this—"

"Corrupted. Seventeen percent. The Greel envoy left it as a gift."

Brendt set it down like it was thermite. "The real shipment. I tracked it. It's not in Ironholm anymore. Greel's people transferred it to Black Spire's medical wing an hour ago. Filed under hazardous waste disposal. They're destroying it tomorrow at noon. Publicly. Something about 'unlicensed biologics.'"

Roan closed his eyes. The tally assembled itself. The envoy wasn't destroying the Elixir. He was displaying it. Baiting the trap with witnesses.

If Roan tried to steal it from the Spire, he'd face Drogan, the Wardens, and expulsion. If he tried to buy it, he'd need twelve thousand credits he didn't have. If he tried to intercept it during transport, he'd fight a Hardened escort with a broken hand and an empty Shard.

And if he did nothing, the Greycross debt would find Mira. The cascade would find him.

"The disposal form," Roan said. "It needs a witness signature from the Junior Hunters' Hall. A student observer. They always use one for hazmat ceremonies."

Brendt stared at him. "You want to sign up to watch them destroy the only thing that can save you?"

"I want to sign up to stand three feet from it while every faculty member in the building watches. The Greycross collectors don't negotiate. The Spire does. They have a reputation to protect. I can use that."

"If I drink it in front of them, they can't let me die. The scandal would stain the Spire's reputation. They'd have to intervene. Stabilize me. Treat the Sidewound."

"They'd also expel you for consuming contraband."

"Expulsion is reversible. The Ashworth note isn't. The cascade isn't."

Brendt was quiet. Then: "You're assuming the Greel envoy hasn't already told them you'd try this. You're assuming the vial they destroy is the real one."

"I'm assuming both. But math doesn't lie. If the Elixir at the disposal is fake, I learn the envoy's supply chain. If it's real, I get my Breakthrough. Either outcome moves me forward."

Brendt shook his head. "You're cracked, Roan. I mean literally. Your hand—"

"Will hold a blade again in six weeks. Or in six hours, if I'm Hardened by noon tomorrow. The timing is tighter, but the geometry is identical."

Brendt left ten minutes later, taking the corrupted ampule with him to dispose of. Roan sat alone in the dark, listening to the HVAC groan.

He checked his hand. The swelling had worsened. The fingers were stiffening. He had fourteen hours until the disposal ceremony, and seventy-one until his body shut down.

The tally overlapped. Barely.

- break -

He didn't sleep. He ran scenarios.

If the disposal Elixir was real, he'd need to ingest it, survive the pulse of raw Sidewound before the Codex Shard could convert the backlash. The timing was nanosecond-critical. He'd have to take the hit sober and conscious, perfectly aligned.

If the Elixir was fake, he'd need to bluff the ingestion, survive the faculty response, and extract the real location from whoever panicked.

Either way, he needed an audience.

Roan stood. He walked to the cracked mirror. In the reflection, behind his own shoulder, the pipework cast a shadow that looked almost like the sigil from the ampule. Six triangles. A hollow core.

His father had left him the Codex. The Greel envoy had left him the formula. The intersection of the two was a question neither man had answered.

Tomorrow, Roan would answer it.

He just wasn't sure yet whether the correct response was ingestion. Or arson. Or something that hadn't been invented yet.

The safehouse door clicked. Not Brendt, wrong signal. Roan reached for his knife with his left hand and turned.

The handle rotated.

Slowly.

Deliberately.

As if the person on the other side already knew he was wounded and unarmed, running on empty biology.

Roan calculated the distance to the vent shaft. Too far. He calculated the distance to the door. Two meters. He calculated his odds against an unknown hostile with a broken hand, a cracked rib, and a liter of blood missing from his veins.

The ledger came back negative.

He raised the knife anyway.
