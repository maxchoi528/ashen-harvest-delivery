Chapter 80: After the Recording

The arena wall felt colder than it should have.

Roan pressed his spine against the cut stone and counted heartbeats. The ventilation grates above his head pulled stale air from the lower tunnels, an engineering choice made decades ago when the Black Spire still pretended the quarterly Proving was a "regulated combat environment" and not a bloodsport machine grinding students into marketing material for weapon manufacturers.

Fourteen minutes since the semifinals ended. Fourteen minutes since the Greel observer had stepped off the sand, nodded to the crowd like a man acknowledging a debt that was owed, and walked toward the administrative wing without looking back.

No glance at Roan. No acknowledgment. Nothing.

That told him more than any interview could.

The Vitalis Reader on his wrist pulsed: 1,247. Up from the 1,103 he'd been showing two hours ago. The Proving's relaxed rules on pre-combat stimulants had let him take a diluted Ironbile dose, diluted enough that Brendt wouldn't smell the chemical tang in his breath, and let the Inversion handle the stomach cramps.

[Sidewound absorbed.]

[Conversion successful: +144 Vitalis.]

[Diluted. Sloppy. But you needed it. — A.V.]

He closed his eyes. Math didn't lie. He'd fought three matches today: two Tempered-tier opponents he'd handled with standard Crimson Wake pressure, and the semifinal against a Hardened student named Marv Colsett who'd had four centimeters of reach and a left hook that still rang in his jaw.

The figures said he'd needed every drop of that 144. Colsett had been a genuine threat, not Greel-tier, but close enough. The man had read his Sevenfold third-strike feint in the second round and nearly punched through his guard.

Nearly. But not.

Roan opened his eyes. "Third time," he muttered.

The Proving bracket had a quirk. The semifinal winners faced each other in the final, but the bracket also allowed for cross-tier exhibition matches between the quarters, a tradition designed to test lower ranks against upper ranks for evaluation data.

He'd been slotted into one such match. Tomorrow morning. Against a Forged-tier senior named Voss who the quarterly bracket had cheerfully placed there as punishment for finishing third in the qualifier.

"Welcome to the Black Spire," Roan said to no one. His voice came out flat. He was too tired for inflection.

The changing room door three corridors down slammed open. Footsteps, heavy, military, carrying the unmistakable rhythm of someone who walked like combat was their native language.

Drogan.

Right.

Roan stood, dusted off his training clothes, no point changing when he'd just get sweat on his good gear, and walked toward the approaching footsteps.

Drogan rounded the corner with Brendt half a step behind him. The Warden's prosthetic hand gleamed under the emergency lighting: a matte-black alloy that looked like sculpture and moved like flesh. His face was unreadable, which was itself a tell. When Drogan was angry, he smiled. When he was worried, he went still.

Right now, he was neither.

"Three matches," Drogan said. Not a question.

"Three."

"Your inversion quota?"

Roan checked the Shard-readout in his peripheral vision. "One left. Used one on Colsett's hook, the impact registered as a Sidewound during the feint phase. The Ironbile counted as the second."

"And the third round against that Hardened girl?"

"Didn't need it. She gassed in the third minute. I just waited her out."

Drogan nodded slowly. His eyes tracked something behind Roan's head, the Codex Shard's subtle warmth at the base of his neck. He never asked about it directly. Another tell.

"The Greel observer," Drogan said. "I watched his kinsman's match from the observation deck."

"Tell me."

"Faster than the footage suggested. His family's pressure technique. I've seen it called the Northern Crown's Wrath in some of the old texts, he's integrated it into a counter-strike system. He lets opponents commit to attacks, absorbs the Vitalis surge through his shoulder meridian, and releases it back with interest during the follow-through."

Roan's jaw tightened. "Like Hollowstrike, but inverted."

"Exactly like Hollowstrike, but inverted." Brendt pulled up his tablet, showed him a slow-motion capture. Greel's kinsman, mid-combat, taking a punch to the solar plexus and using the impact to drive his elbow into the opponent's guard. "His body processes incoming Vitalis as a charging mechanism. The harder you hit him, the harder he hits back."

The math rearranged itself. Seven seconds. He needed seven seconds to run the figures, check the calibration, adjust the Inversion threshold.

"He's watching you," Drogan said.

The words landed like a slap.

"What?"

"After his match. He walked to the observation gallery. Stood there for four minutes, looking at the bracket board. Specifically your name." Drogan's voice stayed level. "I need to tell you something else. I pulled the Greycross transport logs from the year your father died. The manifest had a name I didn't recognize. A ghost. We'll talk after the match."

"One more thing," Brendt said quietly. "The observer accessed Lyra's student file ten minutes ago. Contact information. Residential history. Just looking."

Roan closed his eyes and ran the scenario space. The observer wasn't scouting technique. He was scouting leverage.

Option A: Withdraw from the final exhibition match. Preserve operational security. Accept the reputation hit and live to fight another day.

Option B: Fight as-is. Show the world Tempered-tier numbers against a Forged. Win or lose, either outcome confirms hidden depth.

Option C: Push the Inversion harder. Spike his Vitalis reading to Hardened-tier at minimum, and hope the jump is large enough that the observer assumes a bloodline artifact.

The tally didn't like any of them.

"There's a fourth option," Brendt said quietly.

Roan opened his eyes. "No. There isn't."

"The Ardent girl."

Selene. She knew something was off. She also knew he'd saved her family's reputation during the Suthmere incident. That bought silence.

"Buys it for how long?"

Drogan raised his prosthetic hand. "Roan's right to be careful. But caution has limits. You're in a Proving. People expect anomalies. The question isn't whether to hide, it's how much to reveal."

Roan looked at the Warden. "You're suggesting I throw the exhibition match."

"I'm suggesting you calibrate it. Let Voss pressure you. Make it look hard. Show the world a Tempered-tier student surviving by inches against a Forged senior. That's the story they expect. Give them that story, and the anomalies become noise."

"And the observer?"

Drogan's expression shifted. Something cold moved behind his eyes. "The observer isn't the problem. Not yet. The Greel network plays long games. He won't move on whispers. He moves on confirmations. Right now, you're a statistical anomaly in a quarterly full of them. Keep it that way."

Roan exhaled. The ventilation system hummed above him.

The ledger, stripped of optimization pressure, said: *survive tomorrow. Don't expose more than necessary. Keep the observer in the dark.*

But underneath the balance, something older pulsed. The same something that had made him take the Bloodscourge in the first place. The same something that had made him push through the Sevenfold Bleed's third strike even when his instructors warned him it would shatter his meridians.

*Want.*

He wanted to know what a Forged-tier felt like under real pressure. Not the exhibition match pressure, the *other* pressure. The kind that came when you stopped hiding and started fighting.

"Tomorrow morning," he said. "Exhibition match. I'll calibrate it your way."

Drogan nodded. Brendt's shoulders dropped a millimeter.

Roan didn't tell them about the fifth option. The sealed note in his bag from the Greel observer. The meeting request for tomorrow. The use of both remaining inversions not to hide, but to make a statement that made the observer hesitate before crossing the line he was drawing.

He walked back to the changing room alone. The corridors were empty now, most students had cleared out after the semifinals, chasing dinner or revision or whatever normal seventeen-year-olds did in a combat academy.

The Codex Shard hummed against his spine. Warm. Patient.

[Three hours until reset. One conversion left. One opportunity. — A.V.]

His father had always said the most dangerous moment in any plan was the moment you believed your own calculations.

Roan reached the changing room door. Paused.

*What would a Greel observer do?*

The answer came instantly: *He wouldn't ask what his enemy would do. He'd already know.*

The observer had Sentinel Bureau access. He had family intelligence networks. He had the Spire's internal combat analytics.

And he'd already touched Lyra's file.

Roan pushed open the door. The changing room was dark, lit only by the amber glow of the Vitalis meters built into the lockers. His bag sat where he'd left it, untouched.

The sealed envelope lay on top of it. A student runner had delivered it during the briefing. The Greel crest. Inside, a single line of script: *Tomorrow. After the exhibition. The Warden's private gallery.*

The observer wasn't waiting for data. He was skipping to the conclusion.

Roan opened his bag. Pulled out the secondary he'd named the Tertiary, because naming weapons felt like tempting fate, and checked the edge. Still sharp enough. In a fight calibrated for theater, a slightly dull blade could mean the difference between a close call and a genuine injury.

*Tomorrow morning. Exhibition match.*

He had three hours to rest. One inversion left. And a meeting he couldn't avoid.

The Shard pulsed again.

[Your move, kid. — A.V.]

Outside, the tournament grounds hummed with post-elimination energy, sponsors closing data packages, students arguing about bracket predictions, security scanning for contraband Vitalis amplifiers. Somewhere in that noise, the Greel observer was running exactly the same kind of analysis Roan had just performed.

Two calculating machines, pointed at each other.

One of them would miscalculate first.

Roan sat down on the bench. Closed his eyes. Ran the math one more time, searching for the variable he'd missed.

Found nothing.

That, he decided, was either very good or very bad.
