Chapter 10: The Vance Cairn

The Codex Shard pulsed once against his spine. Not painful. Not pleasant. Just *there*, in a way it hadn't been before the Bloodscourge.

[Codex Shard active. Local capacity: 3/3.]
[Daily reset: 04:17.]

Roan kept his eyes open in the dark. The numbers hung in his peripheral vision like smoke that wouldn't dissipate. He'd been staring at them for four minutes now, testing whether they responded to willed focus. They did. He could shift them slightly, suppress them for three seconds before they reasserted. He couldn't make them disappear entirely.

The room smelled like dust and old paper. His mother's apartment on the fourth floor of a building that had seen better decades, curtains half-closed against the streetlight bleed from Greycross Avenue. Two blocks away, the steel mills were switching shifts. The rumble traveled through concrete and rebar like a second heartbeat.

He sat cross-legged on the mattress, hands resting on his knees. His lips were still slightly swollen from the Bloodscourge burn he'd absorbed yesterday. Not enough to alarm Mira, who'd been too exhausted from her double shift to notice. Just enough to remind him the Codex Shard had done something irreversible to his body.

Four hours of sleep. His mind kept running three tracks at once.

Forty minutes of silent testing. The rules so far, as he understood them:

First: the Codex Shard detected and converted *side effects*, not primary effects. The Bloodscourge's intended result, accelerated Vitalis generation, wasn't the target. The *cost* was. The ten-year lifespan reduction. The permanent scarring along the meridian channels. Those were what the system flagged and converted.

Second: the conversion required commitment. Brief contact with a side effect wasn't enough. The system needed a brief, ugly window of damage registering in his nervous system before it could latch onto the pattern.

Third: the Codex Shard had a daily capacity. Three conversions per day. He'd used one yesterday absorbing the Bloodscourge. That left two.

Fourth: the Codex Shard wasn't intelligent in any conversational sense. It processed patterns, executed conversions, tracked capacity. It didn't volunteer information. It responded to queries, physical queries, damage queries, with terse bracketed messages. The voice that sometimes accompanied those messages, the dry observational tone, came with a signature. *— A.V.*

Aldric Vance.

His father had built this system, or found it, or stolen it. The fact that Aldric's consciousness or echo or whatever remained embedded in the Shard suggested the latter two options were more likely. You didn't design a system to outlast yourself unless you'd planned to leave it behind for someone.

For him.

The thought sat in his chest like a debt he hadn't yet accounted for. He set it aside. There would be time to examine its implications. There would be time to decide what to feel about a dead man's ghost whispering tactical advice through his spine.

Right now, he needed to test boundaries. He bit the inside of his cheek. Hard enough to taste copper. Not hard enough to worry about bleeding out, a controlled bite, pressure applied with his molars through the thin mucosal tissue.

Pain registered. Bright and immediate. The Codex Shard warmed.

[Sidewound detected.]

His heart rate spiked. This was it. He held the bite steady, waiting for the conversion sequence to complete.

[Sidewound rejected: voluntary minor self-injury below threshold.]

The warmth faded. The taste of blood remained.

Right. The system had specifications. It didn't process every random injury, it processed *side effects*, which implied a context of deliberate action with known costs. Self-flagellation without the framework of training or combat didn't qualify.

He released the bite. The copper taste lingered another thirty seconds before his saliva diluted it to nothing. He tried again forty minutes later. Not a bite this time, a sewing needle from Mira's mending kit, heated over a match and pressed against his fingertip.

The pain was sharper, more localized. The Codex Shard registered it immediately.

[Sidewound detected.]

Here. This was the moment.

[Sidewound rejected: insufficient Sidewound complexity.]

The needle cooled against his skin. The red mark it left faded to a white line within two minutes.

Complexity. The system needed a side effect with multiple damage vectors, or at least a damage type it could analyze and process. A needle prick was too simple. One point of contact, one type of tissue, one duration. The Bloodscourge had been a cocktail, chemical burn, meridian disruption, cellular stress, lifespan erosion. That had enough data points to trigger conversion.

Voluntary injury didn't count. Minor injury didn't count. Simple injury didn't count.

He set the needle aside.

The conclusion assembled itself in his mind, piece by piece, like a lock mechanism clicking into alignment. The Codex Shard processed *real risks*. Real training risks. Real combat risks. Damage that came attached to deliberate actions with known costs and uncertain outcomes. Pill consumption. Technique execution. Battle injuries.

The system was designed for someone who trained hard enough, fought often enough, and consumed enough resources to generate side effects faster than his body could naturally adapt.

Someone like his father. Someone who had access to high-grade catalysts and forbidden techniques and ambition that burned through normal risk calculations.

Someone who had left this inheritance to a seventeen-year-old son he'd never properly trained, in a city where the closest thing to a proper academy was a three-month-away entrance examination.

His father had known. Or suspected. Or calculated that Greycross's proximity to the Black Spire meant his son would eventually find a path upward. The Codex Shard was waiting. The knowledge of what it could do had been written in margins and crossed-out passages, buried in a journal Roan had read cover to cover twice.

His father had left him a loaded weapon and a map to the ammunition depot.

He just hadn't expected Roan to find the map so early.

The circulation training began at half past four. Standard breathing pattern, the same one every Initiate at the Spire's feeder programs learned in their first week. Inhale for four counts, hold for seven, exhale for eight. Vitalis drawn from the air through the lungs, filtered through the meridian network, stored in the core abdomen.

Roan had practiced this pattern during his two years of self-directed study. His Vitalis base had climbed from the civilian baseline of twelve to something significantly higher, though he'd never had it professionally measured. The Codex Shard had been hinting at the number since last night. He could see it now, a persistent value in his peripheral vision: 250.

Today, with the Shard active and his meridians still faintly scorched from the Bloodscourge inversion, the training felt different.

The first cycle took seventy seconds. He tracked it with the second hand of Mira's alarm clock, visible across the room. Seventy seconds to complete what should have taken forty-five. His meridians were sluggish, still healing from yesterday's forced expansion.

The second cycle came faster. His body remembered the pattern. His meridians, despite their damage, were transporting more Vitalis per breath than they had before. The Bloodscourge had scarred them along the inside, yes. But it had also widened them. Made them more efficient at channeling flow.

The Codex Shard pulsed.

[Conversion successful: +12 Vitalis.]
[Daily Capacity: 3/3.]

He breathed through the third cycle. The same pulse.

[Conversion successful: +12 Vitalis.]
[Daily Capacity: 3/3.]

He opened his eyes and checked the clock. Four minutes had passed.

His Vitalis reading, according to the Codex Shard's persistent display, had climbed from the initial 250 to 286. Thirty-six points from a single training block.

He returned to the breathing pattern. Cycle after cycle. The Shard pulsed occasionally, warm against his spine, a reminder that it was still converting side effects from the Bloodscourge. By six in the morning, the sky over Greycross had shifted from black to charcoal. The steel mills were fully operational now, their smoke stacks exhaling industrial clouds into the lower atmosphere.

He sat at the window, watching the light change. His Vitalis had settled at 374. One hundred twenty-four points gained in approximately three hours of focused training.

The balance was straightforward. Every completed cycle generated a small conversion benefit. The benefit was modest, twelve Vitalis per cycle, but the rate at which he could execute cycles wasn't modest. He was processing nearly two cycles per minute now. That was roughly one hundred twenty cycles per hour. That was absurd. But the body had limits. The acceleration was real, but it tapered. After the first dozen cycles, the conversion pulse grew fainter. By the twentieth, it had settled into a background hum. The Codex Shard wasn't a license for infinite growth; it was a multiplier with a ceiling, a ceiling that rose as he rose, but never vanished.

His father's notes had called this "residual inversion benefit." The terminology had confused Roan yesterday. It made more sense now. The Codex Shard's primary function was side effect conversion. But the process of conversion left traces, efficiency improvements, pathway widenings, adaptation accelerations, that persisted even when the system wasn't actively converting.

The inversion changed him permanently. The obvious changes were easy to count: meridian size, Vitalis capacity, conversion quota. The real change sat underneath, in the rate at which he could improve. He would always learn faster than his peers. He would always recover more completely. He would always get more value out of every resource he consumed.

The question was what to do with that edge.

He thought about it for eleven seconds. Then he stopped thinking about it, because the answer was self-evident.
