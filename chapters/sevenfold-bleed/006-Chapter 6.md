Chapter 6: The Basement

The basement smelled of lime and old iron. The scroll was unrolled on the workbench, its edges yellowed, its ink faded to a rusty brown. Roan had traced the meridian diagrams a dozen times. He knew the paths by heart: Ruptured Sigh, Hollow Gate, Withered Root, Ashen Vein, Broken Toll, Drowned Passage, Silent Core.

The arithmetic was complete. The Sevenfold Bleed had a documented survival rate of seven percent in the general population. With Inversion, that number climbed to a theoretical twenty-two — dependent entirely on the speed and precision of the reversal.

Roan didn't care about the seventy-eight percent failure rate. He cared about the twenty-two percent chance of permanent enhancement. Those odds were better than a slow death in Greycross.

He rolled the scroll, tucked it into his jacket, and climbed the basement stairs.

- break -

The door didn't open.

It was *demolished*.

"Vance!"

Brendt Holloway stood in the doorway, one arm wrapped around a paper bag bulging with snack wrappers, the other still raised from the kick. His Vitalis flared bright, theatrical, performative, utterly unnecessary. The lock hadn't even been engaged.

"You were going to go to Spire without telling me, weren't you?"

Roan Vance didn't look up from the open textbook on his desk. The page was dense with anatomical diagrams of meridian pathways. Interesting material, mostly wrong, but the errors were instructive. Beneath the textbook, weighted by a loose brick, lay the faded scroll of *The Sevenfold Bleed*. He had just finished memorizing the final meridian route. "I didn't know I needed to report my movements."

"Man, I heard it from Theon. He said you were talking to Registrar Halvey about exam waivers." Brendt bulldozed into the room, kicked the door shut behind him with his heel, and dropped onto the corner of Roan's desk like he'd been doing it for years. He hadn't. They'd met six weeks ago. But some people had that effect, they inserted themselves into spaces like they'd always been there.

Roan turned a page. "Theon needs to learn when to stop talking."

"Theon's a gossip. That's different." Brendt rummaged in the bag, produced a foil-wrapped protein bar, and began unwrapping it with aggressive enthusiasm. "But you're not denying it."

"I don't see the point in denying something that apparently half the intake class already knows."

Brendt took a bite. Chewed. Swallowed. "So?"

"So what?"

"So are you going alone or not?"

Roan finally looked up. Brendt Holloway was exactly what his file suggested: middle-class background, solid if unimpressive Vitalis baseline (173, up from 161 last month, dedicated training regimen, hired help), and relentless social energy that made introverts tired by proxy. He was also, inexplicably, the first person in three years to walk into Roan's life without trying to extract something.

Maybe that would change. It usually did.

"I'm planning to take the exams, yes."

"Alone."

"Is there a group option?"

Brendt's jaw worked the protein bar to one side. "You know what I mean. Everyone's forming teams. Safety in numbers, split the resource costs, the whole thing. Old Mercer, you know, Theon's cousin, he's got four people already."

"I know." Roan had been tracking the alliance formations for two weeks. There were eleven active groups, thirty-seven standalone candidates, and one person Brendt hadn't mentioned: Calloway, who was quietly collecting the best candidates into a shadow network backed by House Ashford. That one wasn't in the public gossip.

"And you're not joining any of them."

"No."

"Why?"

Roan considered the question. Brendt was watching him with the intensity of someone who wanted an answer, not the polite kind, not the deflection. He wanted the real reasoning.

Math didn't work with more variables than necessary. More people meant more unpredictable inputs. More promises to keep. More variables that could break.

But that wasn't something you said out loud.

"Because I'm difficult to work with."

Brendt blinked. Then he laughed, a sharp, surprised sound. "Dude. That's the most honest thing you've said since I walked in."

"I'm not usually dishonest. Just selective with information."

"Same thing, different branding." Brendt finished the protein bar, crumpled the wrapper, and tossed it at Roan's waste bin. It bounced off the rim and rolled under the desk. Brendt didn't acknowledge it.

He was still watching Roan. Waiting.

The room went quiet. Roan filled it by returning to the textbook, but the words weren't registering. The presence of Brendt created a different kind of pressure, not hostile, not demanding, just *present* in a way that made it harder to calculate. The scroll beneath the textbook felt heavier than it should.

"You're still here," Roan said.

"Yep."

"That's disruptive."

"Noted." Brendt pulled out another snack, this one looked like dried fruit, stolen from a cafeteria he wasn't supposed to access. "So give me a real reason."

"Which I've already done."

"Which is a garbage reason and you know it." Brendt bit into the dried fruit. Chewed. "You don't want to owe people. That's what this is, right? I saw your face when Theon was talking about alliance debts. You looked like he suggested something criminal."

Roan's pen tapped twice against the desk. "Debt structures create dependency chains. I'd rather fail on my own terms than succeed on someone else's."

"Okay, but here's the thing." Brendt leaned forward, elbows on his knees, the dried fruit forgotten in his hand. "You're thinking about this like a transaction. What do I owe, what do I get, how do I balance the sheet. But alliances aren't just debt. Sometimes they're just, people. Working together. Because they want to."

"That's a naive framework."

"Maybe. But you're the one who looks lonely sitting here with a textbook."

The silence this time was different. Roan's pen stopped tapping. He set it down, aligned it parallel to the desk edge, and then looked at Brendt properly for the first time since the door had ceased to exist as a functional barrier.

Brendt was 178 centimeters, solidly built, with an open face that made people trust him before they'd earned it. His Vitalis signature was steady, no spikes, no fluctuations. He wasn't performing right now. He wasn't angling for anything.

He was just being Brendt.

"I appreciate the concern," Roan said. The words came out flatter than intended. He re-calibrated. "I'm not looking for a team."

"Didn't ask you to join mine."

"Everyone's asking. That's why there's eleven alliance groups."

"I'm not everyone." Brendt shrugged. "I'm just the guy who wants to know if you're planning to get yourself killed doing something stupid."

The question landed harder than it should have. Roan felt something shift in his chest, not warmth, not fear, just an awareness that Brendt had landed closer to the truth than he realized. The weight of the scroll beneath the textbook seemed to press against the wood of the desk.

"I'm planning to take an exam," Roan said. "The outcome depends on my preparation."

"And if your preparation isn't enough?"

"Then I'll adjust."

"From what?"

"From whatever variables present themselves."

Brendt stared at him. Then he shook his head, stood up, and walked to Roan's window, looking out at the courtyard below where students were filtering toward the dining hall. He didn't touch anything. Didn't snoop. Just stood there, being present, while Roan watched him watching nothing.

"My old man's a logistics officer," Brendt said, still looking outside. "Spent twenty years making sure the right supplies got to the right place at the right time. He used to tell me that the best plans aren't the ones that account for everything. They're the ones that have room to break."

"That sounds like an excuse for poor preparation."

"It's an excuse for being human." Brendt turned. "Plans break. People break. But if you're someone who can adapt when everything goes sideways, you're worth more than someone with a perfect strategy and no flexibility."

"And you think I'm that kind of person."

"I think you've been left alone long enough that you've had to become that person whether you wanted to or not." Brendt's expression shifted, something quieter underneath the usual noise. "I'm not trying to recruit you. I'm trying to tell you that you don't have to do this alone just because you always have."

The words sat in the room like something physical. Roan felt the weight of them, unfamiliar and uncomfortable, but not entirely unwelcome.

He filed that last part away and didn't examine it.

"I'll take it under advisement," Roan said.

Brendt nodded, apparently recognizing the dismissal for what it was: not an acceptance, but not a rejection. It was the most progress anyone had made with Roan Vance in three years.

Not that Brendt seemed to realize that. Or maybe he did, and just didn't care to make a thing of it.

"Right." Brendt moved toward the door, then stopped. "Oh, one more thing. Theon said you were talking about Initiate Draught suppliers. Old Pell's the guy, right? That shop in the Hollow Reaches market?"

Roan's pen moved before the thought finished forming. "Where did Theon hear that?"

"He talks to everyone. You know how he is." Brendt shrugged again, apparently his default gesture for things he didn't consider important. "Anyway, Old Pell's legit. My cousin used him. Bit of a character, but the goods are genuine."

"I'll keep that in mind."

"Cool." Brendt opened the door, then looked back one more time. Something flickered across his face, not concern, not calculation. Just an awareness that he was missing pieces of a puzzle and wasn't sure if he should be worried about what they'd reveal.

"Hey, Vance."

"Yes?"

"Whatever you're planning, you've got backup if you need it. Not an alliance. Not a debt. Just... Backup." Brendt tapped the doorframe once. "I'm just saying. It's an option."

Then he was gone, the door swinging shut behind him with significantly less violence than his entrance.

- break -

The room felt larger without Brendt in it. Roan sat still for eleven seconds, tracking his breathing, cataloging the conversation, looking for the angle.

He couldn't find one.

That was interesting. And concerning. And possibly, if he was being honest with himself, which he tried to be even when it was uncomfortable, something he'd forgotten existed.

Connection without transaction.

He didn't trust it. But he noted it.

Roan stood, crossed to the window where Brendt had stood, and watched the courtyard empty. The sun was starting its descent toward the outer wall of Greycross. Evening routines beginning. Students going to dinner, to training halls, to the library where rumors said someone had seen a Forged-tier instructor giving unsanctioned sparring sessions.

The Spire exam was in fourteen days.

But he couldn't think about the exam yet.

He moved back to the desk, pulled out a fresh sheet of paper, and wrote the training schedule for Brendt. He didn't hurry. He processed the data: Brendt's current Vitalis baseline, his documented strengths in endurance and social coordination, his weaknesses in structured combat and resource management. He outputted a framework that would maximize his test performance in the time remaining.

He wrote the schedule out in clean, precise characters, annotated with time allocations and expected outcome ranges. Then he added a note at the bottom: *If you follow this exactly, your probability of passing increases from 61% to 84%. Deviations reduce effectiveness. Questions accepted during lunch hours only.*

He didn't sign it.

Theon would deliver it, which was fine. Theon delivered everything, he had that kind of mind, hungry for information flow, incapable of keeping things to himself. Brendt would know it came from Roan. Brendt would laugh at the clinical precision of it. Brendt would follow the schedule anyway, because despite his noise, he understood value when he saw it.

Roan folded the paper, set it aside, and pulled the scroll from beneath the textbook.

Shadows pooled in the corners of the room.

The knock came. "Roan? Dinner's ready. Lyra wants you to come."

"In a moment."

"Roan."

"I'm reviewing material."

The pause was heavy with things neither of them said. Mira had stopped pushing two years ago, after the incident with the Vance estate, after she'd seen what happened when Roan decided to close a door. She still knocked. She still waited. But she didn't push.

"Seven minutes," she said. "Then I'm sending Lyra."

He heard her footsteps retreat.

Seven minutes.

Roan set the textbook down. The diagrams blurred slightly, eye strain, or maybe something else. He'd been at this for six hours. The human body wasn't designed for sustained concentration at this level. His father's notes mentioned techniques for optimizing neural recovery, but they required supplements Roan couldn't acquire yet.

Old Pell might have what he needed.

The name surfaced again. The vendor in the Hollow Reaches market that everyone knew about but nobody talked about openly. Supplied to people who couldn't afford official channels, or didn't want their purchases tracked. Grey market. Not illegal, exactly, but not clean either.

Roan had mapped the route three days ago. He knew the timing, the security rotations, the three most likely exit paths if things went wrong. He knew Old Pell's reputation: fifty years in the trade, specialized in rare catalysts, rumored to have connections to Forged-tier suppliers outside Greycross.

The figures said: go tomorrow. Early. Before the market opened.

The tally also said: this is the first concrete step toward a path that has a 67% probability of killing you before you reach Black Spire.

Roan pulled the top drawer of his desk open and extracted a folded paper, edges worn from handling. His Day 1 Plan. Not a metaphor. An actual document, numbered sections, contingency branches, resource allocation timelines. A plan that would make military strategists nod in approval and normal people question his sanity.

He unfolded it. Read the first line: *Objective: Reach Black Spire. Method: Whatever it takes.*

Underneath, in smaller text: *Don't die before doing what matters.*

He didn't remember writing that second line.

Didn't remember adding it.

But the handwriting was his.

Seven minutes.

Roan stood. He locked his door. He did not go to the stairs leading to the dining hall. He went to the basement.

The light of the single bulb was harsh, carving deep shadows around the workbench. He set the scroll down, opened it to the final plate. The seven meridians stared back at him, patient and lethal.

He stripped to the waist. The air was cold. His skin was cold. The Codex Shard lay warm against his sternum.

He sat in the center of the floor.

He did not hesitate. Hesitation was a luxury for people who had options. Roan had one path: forward.

He cycled the Void Vitalis. He fed it to the first meridian. The second. The third. He opened all seven channels at once, inviting the violation.

The world dissolved into a single color: agony.

His body rejected the command. His muscles locked. His spine arched. Blood vessels burst across his torso, painting his skin with fine red lines. The Sevenfold Bleed was not a technique designed for survival. It was a demolition tool.

Roan held it together.

In the blinding white of the pain, he felt the Codex Shard pulse. The Inversion did not bargain. It acted. It took the shattered channels, the ruptured arteries, the screaming nerves, and it folded them. Reversed them. Rebuilt them.

The cracks filled with liquid Vitalis instead of blood. The torn tissues reknit themselves into something stronger, denser, more receptive.

It took a single heartbeat.

The pain vanished. Roan's lungs expanded. The air tasted like victory.

A message carved itself across his mind, crisp and final:

**[Permanent enhancement. Father-sourced. Don't tell your sister. — A.V.]**

Roan stared at the words for a long moment. His father's handwriting. His father's voice, compressed into a sixteen-word message. His father had built this system for him.

He pressed a hand to his chest. The new channels hummed beneath his skin. The Sevenfold Bleed was no longer a theoretical path. It was a foundation.

He stood. Dressed. Washed the blood and dust from his face in the utility sink.

The man in the cracked mirror was the same, but different. The arithmetic had changed. The path was real.

Six minutes and forty seconds.

He climbed the stairs.

- break -

The dining hall was loud. Roan sat at the far corner of the family table, positioned with his back to the wall and his sightlines covering both entrances. Force of habit. Mira was explaining something to Lyra about fingering technique while Lyra pretended to listen while reading a technique manual disguised as a fantasy novel.

Mira caught his eye. Smiled. Didn't ask about the six hours he'd spent alone in his room.

That was the deal. He was here. He was present. That was enough.

Roan ate. The food was adequate. He tracked the nutritional content automatically, protein ratio slightly low, carbs appropriately complex, trace minerals present in acceptable ranges. His body was a machine. The fuel mattered.

He was halfway through the meal when Brendt walked past with his own dinner tray, heading toward a table where Theon and two other intake students were arguing about exam tactics. Brendt caught Roan's eye, raised his fork in a mock salute, and kept walking.

No expectations. No debt. No calculation required.

Just a guy who wanted Roan to know he wasn't alone.

Roan looked down at his plate. Something had shifted in his chest, not warmth, not fear, just an awareness. A deep, structural knowledge that the system worked. That his father had not abandoned him to a dead end. That the Sevenfold Bleed was not a suicide note, but a door.

He looked at Lyra, absorbed in her manual, and at Mira, watching him with that quiet, unreadable glance. The promise from the basement — *Don't tell your sister* — was a brand on his tongue. He could live with that. He would have to.

Old Pell opened at dawn. Tomorrow, the real work began.
