Chapter 182: Vorath's Rebuke

Kael Greel returned to his family under guard, which was the only reason he returned alive.

That wasn't speculation.

Roan read the transfer report from the infirmary bed while a healer rebuilt two bones in his left hand and complained that students with duel waivers treated internal bleeding as a scheduling inconvenience. The report had been sealed under Spire neutrality protection, then copied to Roan by Ines four minutes later with the subject line: *Educational Material*.

Kael hadn't been released to House Greel. That would've been idiotic. He had been placed in a neutral recovery suite under Black Spire custody, then moved under diplomatic escort to a sanctioned House residence in Ironholm for "temporary supervised consultation." The phrase meant Cole had compromised just enough to avoid immediate diplomatic seizure while keeping Spire observers close enough to make quiet murder awkward.

Awkward, not impossible.

Important distinction.

The healer pulled his fractured fingers straight.

Pain climbed Roan's arm like a ladder made of knives.

[Localized trauma detected.]

[Invert?]

No.

He had one quota, maybe zero, and no intention of spending it on fingers because a licensed professional was already doing something expensive and unpleasant.

"You are very calm for someone whose hand is being repaired," the healer said.

"Would screaming improve the procedure?"

"No."

"Then bill me for silence."

She stared at him for a second, then continued working. "I understand why Professor Drogan drinks."

"Correlation isn't causation."

"With you, I suspect both."

Roan almost respected that.

Across the room, Drogan sat in a chair too small for his shoulders, reading the same report on his own slate. He hadn't left since the duel. Not because he was hovering. Drogan didn't hover. He occupied space in a way that made threats reconsider geometry.

Selene stood near the window, speaking quietly into a secure line with someone from House Ardent. Her tone was polite enough to be dangerous. Iris had vanished entirely after sending three files, two warnings, and one invoice.

Brendt remained unconscious two floors below.

Kael remained alive somewhere across the city.

Vorath remained watching.

The last mattered most.

Roan's slate chimed with a new file.

He opened it one-handed.

The image showed Kael in a white recovery chamber, seated before a wall of black glass. His left wrist had been bandaged, but the gold-red oath mark bled light through the gauze. Two Spire observers stood near the door. A Greel physician hovered by the equipment. Kael's posture was straight despite exhaustion.

The glass reflected an older man entering the room.

White beard. Gold-red eyes. Northern posture. No visible weapon.

Vorath Greel.

The image froze there.

The file's existence was as important as its contents.

Ines didn't obtain things by accident. If she had received a partial transcript from inside a Greel-supervised recovery chamber, then either the Spire observer had been bribed, the observer wanted insurance, or Vorath had permitted a hole in the room's privacy. The first two were comforting in the way loaded weapons were comforting: dangerous but understandable. The third meant the old man had chosen to speak through the leak.

Roan preferred bribery.

The universe rarely prioritized his preferences.

He checked the metadata before reading further. Recording device: observer lapel pin, standard diplomatic issue. Encryption: intact until thermal distortion. Transfer chain: three hops, one scrubbed, one Ines-owned shell, one academic archival node that technically belonged to a botany department. Time from recording to Roan's slate: eighteen minutes.

Too fast for an accidental leak.

Drogan saw the conclusion on his face. "He wanted you to hear it."

"Yes."

"Then don't."

"That advice arrived late."

"I'm serious."

"So am I."

Drogan's eyes narrowed. "Vorath doesn't waste words."

"Neither do I. It creates scheduling problems when we correspond."

"This isn't funny."

"No. But if I treat him like a monster in every sentence, he gets free rent in the room."

Drogan didn't like that answer—but he didn't argue it.

Selene stepped closer. "What does 'the correction' mean in Greel language?"

"Punishment," Iris said from the doorway.

No one had heard her arrive, which was her preferred substitute for greeting.

She wore a dark coat over academy clothes and carried three folders under one arm. Her hair was damp from rain. Her eyes went first to Roan's braced hand, then the cracked pendant on the tray, then the transcript hovering above the slate.

"In Greel internal doctrine," she continued, "a correction isn't an apology. It's the act that proves the system was never wrong. If Kael failed, Vorath has to make Kael's failure serve Greel doctrine. Otherwise the doctrine admits weakness."

Roan nodded. That matched.

"So Kael becomes proof," Selene said.

"Or bait," Iris said. "Or a corpse with better paperwork later."

Drogan looked at her. "You always this cheerful?"

"No. Sometimes I invoice first."

She handed Roan one folder. He opened it one-handed.

Inside were preliminary market reactions, political statements, and three Greel-aligned financial vehicles dropping hard enough to make investors rediscover ethics. Useful, but not urgent.

The second folder contained copies of old Greel oath law.

The third contained a photograph of Kael at age twelve, standing beside Vorath in a snowfield, one wrist bare without oath mark visible. Roan looked at it for longer than expected.

Iris said, "The mark was added later."

"When?"

"Between fourteen and fifteen. Same year Kael entered northern closed training."

Selene's mouth tightened. "That supports coercive modification."

"It supports an argument," Iris corrected. "Proof requires medical imaging and testimony, plus someone willing to tell a Founding Seven patriarch that branding children is rude."

Roan closed the folder.

"Keep it ready."

"I already have six copies."

"Make seven."

Seven copies for six murderers and one survivor. The arithmetic was petty. It was also memorable, and memorable records had a way of surviving rooms where sensible documents died.

Iris smiled faintly. "Sentimental?"

"Pattern consistency."

"Of course."

Then a second file arrived: audio transcript, partial.

Ines had outdone herself.

Roan opened it.

Drogan looked up. "What?"

"Vorath reached Kael."

Drogan stood immediately.

Selene ended her call mid-sentence and turned.

Roan read the transcript aloud because forcing everyone to crowd around the slate would be inefficient.

*Vorath Greel: Stand.*

*Kael Greel: Grandfather—*

*Vorath Greel: I said stand.*

There was a pause in the transcript, marked by the recorder as movement and medical protest.

*Observer: Lord Greel, the patient is under—*

*Vorath Greel: Speak again and I will remember your name.*

Another pause.

Drogan's mouth became a flat line.

Roan continued.

*Kael Greel: I failed.*

*Vorath Greel: You confessed.*

*Kael Greel: The oath was compromised.*

*Vorath Greel: The oath was tested. You were compromised.*

Selene's fingers tightened around her slate.

Roan kept reading.

*Kael Greel: He inverted the recoil. He touched the seal. No Forged student should be able to—*

*Vorath Greel: Don't describe what everyone saw as if narration improves it.*

Roan paused.

That didn't surprise him.

Vorath was old and cruel, plus apparently allergic to useless explanation. Annoying. Competent villains were healthier for story structure and worse for everyone inside it.

He continued.

*Kael Greel: He spared me to use me.*

*Vorath Greel: Yes.*

*Kael Greel: Then let me correct it.*

*Vorath Greel: You are the correction.*

Silence.

The transcript marked six seconds.

That was a long time in a room with an old monster.

*Kael Greel: I don't understand.*

*Vorath Greel: That's why you are alive.*

The audio cut there.

Roan looked at the file metadata. Transmission interrupted by thermal distortion. Last frame corrupted.

Useful, incomplete, irritating.

Drogan said, "He let that leak."

"Yes."

Selene looked between them. "Why?"

Roan set the slate down while the healer wrapped his hand in a brace that smelled like antiseptic and expensive polymers.

"Several reasons," he said. "First, to show he reached Kael despite Spire custody. Second, to show he isn't panicking. Third, to make sure I know Kael remains under his control or at least within his reach."

"Fourth?" Drogan asked.

"To see whether I react to the phrase 'You are the correction.'"

Selene frowned. "What does that mean?"

"Possibilities: Kael is bait. Kael has been marked as expendable. Kael's confession triggers a larger Greel legal maneuver. Or Vorath intends to use Kael's survival to claim oath tampering by me."

"Can he?"

"Yes."

Selene's expression sharpened. "But the logs—"

"Show contact with an oath seam during a duel after Kael registered self-harm recoil. They help us. They also give him an argument."

Drogan grunted. "He'll say you attacked a House command seal."

"I did."

"Boy."

"Accuracy matters."

"The hell it does in court."

"Different kind of accuracy."

The healer finished the brace and stepped back. "You won't use this hand for forty-eight hours."

Roan flexed his fingers as much as the brace allowed.

She glared.

He stopped.

"Forty-eight hours," she repeated.

"Noted."

"I have been told that means nothing when you say it."

"People gossip."

"Your file gossips."

She left before he could respond.

Good. He had no response that improved the situation.

- break -

The public fallout began before Roan left the infirmary.

Not the official fallout. Official systems moved at the speed of frightened committees. Public systems moved at the speed of outrage and poor verification. The global martial feeds cut clips of Kael's confession into every possible length. Ten seconds. Thirty seconds. Full exchange. Side-by-side analysis. Slow-motion oath flare. Thermal charts. One commentator attempted to explain Inversion without knowing the word and concluded Roan had "weaponized frost psychology."

Brendt, had he been awake, would have enjoyed that.

Roan saved the clip for later mockery.

By evening, the Spire forum had stabilized enough to become useless again. The top thread was titled:

THIS IS HOW YOU KILL A HOUSE WITHOUT KILLING THE HEIR

Ines sent a screenshot with a note.

*I didn't start this one.*

Roan replied: *Unlikely.*

*Rude. Accurate, but rude.*

A second screenshot followed. It showed betting markets suspending all Greel-affiliated duel lines pending investigation. Resource contracts tied to House Greel had begun slipping in price. Two minor academies announced review of Greel exchange students. A northern equipment sponsor removed Kael's image from its promotional page.

Good.

Reputation converted into money faster when people were afraid of being photographed near contamination.

Roan catalogued the damage.

Greel public honor: severe hit.

Kael operational value: transformed from assassin to evidence and potential pawn.

Vorath exposure: named but not yet legally cornered.

Roan's condition: injured, pendant compromised, exposure risk elevated, quota unclear.

Black Spire position: publicly protective, privately annoyed.

Brendt: still unconscious.

Enemy escalation: inevitable.

The last item arrived at 8:09.

Not through Ines. Not through Selene. Not through official channels.

Through the Codex Shard.

Roan was alone in the infirmary room when the light flickered.

The monitors continued working. The ward lines remained stable. Drogan had gone to check the perimeter after threatening to tie Roan to the bed if he moved. Selene had left to secure Ardent testimony archives. Iris was wherever criminals went to invoice themselves.

Roan sat upright, damaged pendant in one hand, and examined the crack.

It ran through the center of the disk, almost invisible unless turned under light. Eckhart would be furious. Or quiet. Quiet would be worse.

The Shard pulsed.

Not hot.

Cold.

Text unfolded across his vision without the usual inner distance. Too close. Like someone writing on the inside of his eyelids with a needle.

[Throne-tier attention persists.]

[External anchor compromised.]

[Lineage concealment degraded: 14%.]

Roan's jaw tightened.

Four percent had become fourteen percent in the wrong direction.

Another line appeared.

[Message vector detected.]

[Source: hostile ancestral channel.]

[Open?]

That was new.

Also stupid.

Opening hostile messages through a damaged inheritance artifact ranked poorly on most survival lists. But refusing might not block the channel; it might leave it knocking. And if Vorath had sent something through a Greel oath interaction, understanding the vector mattered.

Roan considered calling Drogan.

Then he considered Drogan's likely reaction, which involved shouting and whiskey, plus maybe hitting the wall.

He set the pendant on the bed tray and placed his braced hand flat beside it.

"Open limited," he said.

The Shard hesitated.

That was never reassuring.

[Opening limited vector.]

The room vanished for a heartbeat.

He stood in snow.

Not physically. He could still feel the infirmary bed under him, the brace around his hand, the antiseptic smell. But layered over it came a second place: white plains beneath a black sky, wind cutting sideways, a long shadow cast by a man holding a glaive.

Vorath's voice arrived without sound.

*You have your father's hands.*

Roan didn't answer.

The snow pressed closer.

*He used them badly.*

Roan looked at the shadow's feet, not its face.

The Codex Shard trembled.

[Do not engage identity channel.]

Wise.

Vorath continued.

*The boy lives because I allowed it. The record exists because I allowed it. You think you have made a wound. You have made an address.*

The vision flickered.

Roan's nose began bleeding again.

Not much. Enough to be annoying.

He spoke one word.

"Invoice."

The snow paused.

Vorath had expected fear, anger, perhaps silence. Not accounting terminology.

Good.

Roan kept his gaze down. "If you are sending threats through damaged student oathwork, I assume Greel funds are already tight. I charge consultation by the minute."

The pressure increased.

The infirmary monitors began to beep.

[Identity pressure rising.]

[Close vector?]

Not yet.

Vorath's shadow leaned closer.

*Come north, Vance. Bring the shard. Bring the knife. Bring whatever remains of Aldric's arrogance. I will show you what he forgot to teach you.*

There it was.

Invitation. Threat. Bait. Confirmation that Vorath wanted the Shard enough to speak directly.

"Poor offer," Roan said.

*You will come.*

"Eventually."

*Sooner.*

"No."

The snow cracked.

For one instant Roan saw eyes through the shadow, gold-red and old enough to make the word human feel negotiable.

*Then I will come to you.*

The vector snapped shut.

Roan returned fully to the infirmary with blood on his lip and every monitor objecting.

Drogan burst through the door five seconds later with a knife in one hand and murder in both eyes.

"What happened?"

Roan wiped his nose with a tissue.

"Vorath sent a message."

Drogan went very still.

"What did he say?"

Roan looked at the cracked pendant.

"He's coming."

- break -

Kael's second transcript arrived after midnight.

This one came through official channels, which meant Cole wanted Roan to know and wanted a record that he had been told. The file contained no audio, only a written summary from Spire observers stationed at the Greel residence.

Vorath hadn't struck Kael. Not physically. He had stood in the recovery chamber for eleven minutes. He had dismissed the Greel physician. He had asked Kael to repeat the confession privately. Kael had done so. Vorath had then placed two fingers on the oath mark and closed the visible wound.

No healing signature recorded.

No known technique identified.

Kael lost consciousness immediately afterward.

Before leaving, Vorath made one statement to the Spire observers.

*Inform your Headmaster that House Greel considers Roan Vance a material witness in an internal matter of northern security. We will request lawful transfer.*

There it was.

The legal maneuver.

Not revenge yet. Procedure first. A Founding Seven patriarch didn't need to kick down the academy gates if he could make someone open them with stamped paper.

Roan read the line twice.

Drogan stood behind him.

Cole had joined them by secure projection, his face hovering above Roan's slate with the expression of a man whose week had been set on fire by teenagers and geopolitics.

Selene was back near the window. Iris had finally appeared in person, because the phrase "lawful transfer" smelled like contracts and therefore summoned her.

The conversation that followed was less a meeting than a collision between law and fear, plus people who had already done something illegal and wanted credit for it.

Cole moved the secure projection to a sealed conference channel. Hale joined from the arbitration office, still in her arena coat, hair immaculate despite the institutional explosion around her. Ines appeared as an audio-only participant because she claimed video compromised her current location, which meant she was inside a room she wasn't supposed to access. A Sentinel Bureau liaison attempted to join and was denied by Cole before the request finished loading.

Roan appreciated that.

"Status," Cole said.

Hale answered first. "The duel record is preserved in six official archives and, based on traffic patterns, more unofficial archives than I can count. Greel can't erase the confession. They can challenge context, mental integrity, oath interference, and chain of custody."

Iris leaned against the window. "They will challenge all four."

"Obviously," Hale said.

Ines's voice crackled through the speaker. "Thermal recoil data supports Kael's registered self-harm profile. Roan's contact with the oath seam occurred during active hostile thermal intrusion. That gives us self-defense framing."

Cole looked at Roan. "You planned that."

"Yes."

"Don't sound proud."

"I don't."

"He doesn't," Drogan said. "That's the problem."

Selene spoke next. "House Ardent can certify that Greel transfer paperwork was irregular. Not forged. Irregular. Someone accelerated it through old alliance channels."

"Can you name them?" Cole asked.

"Not yet."

"Can you by tomorrow?"

Selene's eyes sharpened. "Yes."

Roan remembered that. Selene under deadline sounded like a locked door learning regret.

Cole turned back to the slate. "The public record buys us time. Not safety. Vorath will move through institutions first because that lets him claim legitimacy. If that fails, he may attempt private contact."

Roan said nothing.

Drogan noticed immediately. "He already did."

Cole's projection froze for half a second.

Hale's eyes moved to Roan.

Ines stopped making background noise.

Roan considered lying. Pointless. The monitors had recorded the pressure event and Drogan had seen the aftermath.

"Through a hostile ancestral channel," he said. "Message content: threat, invitation north, claim that the record was allowed, statement that he will come to me."

Cole pinched the bridge of his nose.

Iris whispered, "Well, that's direct."

Hale said, "Did he exert coercive pressure?"

"Yes."

"Did you answer?"

"Yes."

Drogan made a sound.

Hale's mouth thinned. "What did you say?"

Roan looked at her. "I billed him."

For three seconds not one spoke. Then Ines laughed so hard the audio clipped.

Cole looked as if he was aging in administrative increments. "Vance."

"It interrupted his rhythm."

"That isn't a legal defense."

"No. It's a tactical note."

Hale, to Roan's surprise, wrote something down.

Cole said, "He will demand your temporary extradition for questioning."

"Extradition dressed as an interview," Iris said.

"Don't mix languages when committing treason," Cole told her.

"Noted."

Roan looked at the file. "Can he get it?"

Cole didn't answer immediately.

That was answer enough.

"Not through Spire code," Cole said at last. "Not cleanly. But he can apply diplomatic pressure, threaten funding channels, mobilize allied Houses, and claim Greel oath tampering constitutes an attack on sovereign family security."

"I did tamper."

Cole closed his eyes.

Drogan muttered something.

Iris smiled. "He did. Successfully."

Selene said, "The confession proves prior assassination intent."

"Yes," Cole said. "Which gives us grounds to refuse. It doesn't stop pressure."

Roan leaned back against the infirmary pillows. His body disliked the movement. His mind didn't care.

Vorath would escalate in layers. Legal demand first. Public northern outrage second. Pressure on Cole third. If refused, personal contact. The confrontation he had known was coming had begun assembling itself.

He looked at the cracked pendant again.

Eckhart would need to repair it, assuming the old weaponsmith didn't throw him out for breaking it within fifty chapters of receiving it. Roan had no guarantee the signal sink would hold against Vorath's next pressure. The Shard had warned concealment degraded. That meant future contact risked deeper recognition.

Resource ledger: weapon intact, pendant damaged, quota unstable, enemy exposed but alerted, legal shield active but stressed.

Next debt: survive the paperwork long enough to survive the man.

Cole said, "Until further notice, you remain inside Black Spire protected grounds."

"No."

Everyone looked at him.

Roan continued, "If I stay hidden, he defines the narrative. If I run, he gets fear. If I issue statements too early, I spend the confession before the legal demand arrives."

Drogan's eyes narrowed. "What are you suggesting?"

"Nothing yet."

"That worries me more."

"It should."

Roan picked up the official file and forwarded it to Ines, Selene and Iris, plus Drogan. Then he drafted one message to be scheduled, not sent.

Subject: Aldric Vance case.

Body: Six men killed my father. I have their names.

He didn't send it.

Not yet.

Timing mattered.

Outside the infirmary window, Ironholm's Rift-monitoring beams cut blue lines into the night sky. One of them flickered once, a brief distortion almost too small to notice.

Drogan noticed.

So did Roan.

The Codex Shard pulsed, faint and cold.

Roan saved the draft.
