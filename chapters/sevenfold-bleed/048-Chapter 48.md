Chapter 48: The Annex Siege

The corridor outside the Sparring Hall reeked of sweat and coagulated Vitalis. Roan paused at the junction where the east wing met the central atrium, cataloguing the ambient noise—elevated heartrates from at least twelve sources, the wet scrape of someone treating a wound two rooms down, the mechanical hum of the ventilation system running at capacity. Three of those heartbeats belonged to second-years who'd been circling his name on the rankings board for weeks. He didn't care about them. Not today.

Brendt's message had arrived forty minutes ago, timestamped 11:47 PM: *Library annex. Veyrn texts. Someone's been accessing them.* Three sentences. No elaboration. Which meant either Brendt was being careful, or someone had told him to be.

Roan reviewed his options. Heading to the library annex meant exposing himself to surveillance he couldn't fully map. Ignoring it meant losing whatever intelligence window Brendt had cracked open. The ledger pointed toward the annex. Brendt didn't send urgent messages. Brendt sent "let me think about this for a day" messages. Something had accelerated his timeline.

He took the eastern staircase, three steps at a time. The building's infrastructure groaned around him. Black Spire had been retrofitted twice in the last decade, and the joints showed it. His boots hit the landing. Left turn. Forty meters to the annex wing.

The corridor lights flickered. Not old wiring. Someone had modified the output.

Roan stopped walking.

A screening field. Standard practice for sensitive research, but the energy decay traced back at least two hours. Someone had known Brendt was going to access those texts. And hadn't stopped him. They wanted to see what he'd find.

The annex door was reinforced steel with a biometric lock. He studied the access panel. The ward hummed at a frequency that suggested Ines Carrow's work—elegant, restrained, expensive. If she was involved, this went deeper than a casual browse through restricted materials.

He pressed his hand to the panel, letting his Vitalis signature bleed through the detection field—a trick Drogan had shown him. Modest, useful, far from the techniques he'd already mastered. The lock chirped. Green light. The door exhaled open.

The annex was smaller than he'd expected. Twelve terminals arranged in concentric arcs around a central reference stack. Half the screens glowed with active searches. The other half sat dark, but their presence made a statement: *we know you've been looking.*

Brendt occupied the third terminal from the left. His posture had degraded since their last conversation—shoulders hunched, neck craned forward, fingers frozen over the keyboard like he'd been mid-keystroke when Roan entered. The skin beneath his eyes looked thin. Bruised. He'd been here for hours.

"You came," Brendt said. Not a question.

"You messaged."

"I wasn't sure you'd—" Brendt stopped. Swallowed. His gaze flicked to the darkened half of the room. "There's a problem."

"Define problem."

"The Veyrn texts. The ones I told you about, the ones that mention the Black Rite and its origin point." Brendt's voice had dropped to a murmur, which was idiotic given the privacy ward. Sound didn't travel through walls. But behavior communicated fear, and Brendt was broadcasting it in frequencies Roan could taste. "Three other people have accessed the same files in the last two weeks. Students. All from advanced tracks. All with family connections to the Conclave."

Roan processed that. Three students. Accessing origin texts for a forbidden technique. All with Conclave bloodlines. The probability of coincidence dropped toward zero.

"That's not the problem," Roan said. "That's the symptom."

Brendt's jaw worked. He'd always been a step behind in pattern recognition—not from lack of intelligence, but from an unwillingness to follow dark threads to their obvious conclusions. "The problem is that two of those students have since withdrawn from Black Spire. No explanation. No transfer paperwork. Their families just... removed them."

"Withdrew."

"Gone. As in, I checked the residential records this morning and their names don't appear in any active student database." Brendt pulled up a screen—faculty notifications, disciplinary logs, the administrative detritus of institutional life. "One of them was Kira Ashford. Cyrus's younger sister."

The name landed in Roan's mind like a stone in still water. Cyrus Ashford. The spear-user who'd been dogging Roan's ranking position for three months. Whose family had deep roots in House Ashford's Conclave operations. Whose younger sister had apparently vanished from existence after accessing restricted archives.

"When did this happen?" Roan asked.

"The withdrawals? Three days ago. But the access logs show they pulled the files two weeks prior." Brendt's voice cracked on the last word. "Roan, someone knew. Someone set this up. And whoever did it—"

The lights died.

Not flickered. Not dimmed. The privacy ward collapsed with a sound like tearing silk, and the darkness that followed carried weight—pressure against Roan's temples, his sinuses, the base of his skull. Someone was trying to probe him. Directly.

Roan moved before the thought finished forming. His left hand found Brendt's collar, yanked him sideways off the chair. They hit the floor behind the central terminal stack as a pulse of invisible force scoured the space where Brendt had been sitting. The terminal screens cracked. One exploded in a shower of sparks.

"Stay down," Roan said.

Brendt made a sound that wasn't a word.

The pressure intensified. Fingers testing a door for locks. Expensive training. The kind that came from Conclave black sites or Hollow Reaches operations.

Roan opened his defenses. The incoming pulse didn't encounter a wall to break. It encountered a vacuum. His Inversion caught it cleanly—stripped the harmful intention from the energy, fed the raw Vitalis into his reserves. No backlash. No debt. The attack's side effects dissolved on contact. The pressure wave rippled outward from his position, and he felt the attacker's signature flinch. Surprise. Then recalculation. Then a second pulse, stronger, more focused.

The battle shifted to a plane the room couldn't contain.

Roan's mental radius expanded to fill the annex, the corridor beyond, the edges of the atrium three floors down. He felt the attacker now, a presence operating from outside the building, off-campus, projecting through some kind of relay array. The signature read as female. Older. Controlled.

She pushed harder. A third pulse. A fourth. Probing for the breaking point where his defenses would fail.

Roan smiled. It wasn't a pleasant expression.

She couldn't know that every ounce of strain she projected was making him stronger. The harder she pushed, the more raw Vitalis pooled into his reserves. He let her believe she was winning. Let the pressure build until Brendt whimpered behind him, until the cracked terminals began to smoke. He let her pour everything into a final crushing wave.

Then he opened like a valve.

The attack's energy converted mid-flight. What should have been a mental assault became a pressure spike of his own—her weapon turned inward. A shockwave of inverted intention tore through her relay array like a blade through wet paper. Somewhere in the city, a building lost power. Somewhere in that building, a woman screamed.

The lights flickered back on. The privacy ward reasserted itself—Ines Carrow's signature, but shakier now, as if someone had just sent her an extremely alarming message.

Roan stood. His knees protested. His own Vitalis reserves were scraped to the bone—the Inversion had closed cleanly, but amplifying an inverted projection back through an array demanded raw output he didn't have to spare. He was running on fumes. The system owed him nothing.

But he'd gotten an answer.

"Who the hell was that?" Brendt's voice came from the floor, where he'd pressed himself into the narrow gap between the terminal stack and the wall. His face had gone the color of old paper.

Roan examined his hands. No visible damage. "Someone with access to Conclave projection equipment and a very poor understanding of risk assessment."

"She was trying to get inside your head."

"Yes."

"And you just—" Brendt gestured at the destroyed terminals, the scorch marks on the ceiling, the absolute devastation of the annex. "You turned it around."

"I converted the expenditure."

Roan stepped over the debris and retrieved Brendt's tablet from the wreckage of the third terminal. The screen had cracked but the data drive still functioned. He checked the file access logs. Two of the three students who'd accessed the Veyrn texts had withdrawn. The third—Marcus Vell, second-year, family ties to House Vell—remained enrolled. His most recent access had been forty minutes ago.

Right before Brendt sent the message.

Roan considered the timing. The attack came the moment he arrived. She wasn't trying to stop Brendt from finding the texts. She was watching the texts. She was waiting to see who came looking. The attack *was* the message.

"He's still here," Roan said.

"Who?"

"Marcus Vell. He hasn't withdrawn." The realization settled into his tactical map with the weight of confirmed intelligence. Someone had left him behind. Either as a witness, a fail-safe, or bait. "Which means we're not done."

Brendt pulled himself upright, one hand braced against the scorched terminal stack. His eyes had gone sharp—fear transmuting into the cold focus that preceded bad decisions. "What do we do?"

"We find Marcus Vell. We ask him very nicely who told him to access those files."

"And if he doesn't want to tell us?"

Roan paused at the threshold. The corridor beyond was empty.

"Then we make him an offer he can't refuse."

Brendt's jaw tightened. "You sure he's the next lead—or the next trap they set for you?"

It was a good question. The mathematical kind Roan appreciated. "That means the information's just as valuable either way."

Brendt didn't look convinced. But he followed.

The corridor stretched ahead, empty but alive with the pressure of distant observation. The woman with the projection array had failed. The failure communicated information too. Someone now knew that Roan Vance wasn't an ordinary student. They were recalculating.

Roan started walking. The ground under the problem had shifted. He needed to get to Marcus Vell before the shadow did.

He didn't look back. He could feel the shadow at the edge of his awareness.

It wasn't done.
