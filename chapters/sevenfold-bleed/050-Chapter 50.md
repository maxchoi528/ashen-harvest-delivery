Chapter 50: No Clubs

The Ironholm Cargo

The Greycross market district at dusk smelled like rendered fat and rust.

Roan moved through the crowd with his hood low, counting stalls the way other people counted coins. Three fishmongers. Two cloth traders. One man selling fraudulent Forged-tier manuals on expensive paper. The arithmetic of the market was simple. Find the leak, exploit the gap, get out before the balance reset.

He was a Tempered-ranked student at Black Spire now. Three months into his first year. The rules of the game were the same as Greycross. The only difference was the scale of the consequences.

The Codex Shard pulsed from his spine, a low warmth that settled into the bone. He had learned to read the pulse. This one meant attention.

The target was three stalls down. Esme. She dealt in ethically sourced enhancement supplements, which meant she bought stolen alchemical stock and sold it at a reasonable markup. She had a reputation for fairness, which made her the only person in Greycross Roan trusted with this transaction.

“Vance. The Spire actually let you in.”

“They had no choice. The trials were quantitative.”

Esme’s lips twitched. She set the scale down. “What do you need?”

“The Ironholm shipment.”

“That’s not a small request, Spire boy.”

“I’m not a boy.” Roan set a pouch on the counter. The weight made a solid sound against the wood. “I’m a paying customer.”

Esme tested the pouch without looking. “The shipment isn’t coming through the main roads. It’s being rerouted through the eastern mining tunnels. Three nights from now. Midnight. Four Tempered guards, one Hardened officer. They’re transporting enough Vitalis concentrate to force a Forged breakthrough.”

“Why the escort?”

“Because something is wrong with the cargo.” She looked up. “Your father asked me about shipments like this once. I warned him. He didn’t listen.”

“My father isn’t here.”

“No. He isn’t.” Esme took the pouch. “The tunnels. Midnight. Don’t let the Hardened get close.”

Roan nodded once and turned into the crowd.

The Shard pulsed again, stronger now. A warning that was not quite words—fragments of recognition his father had left behind in the system. *Locked… Containment protocol…*

“I’m listening,” Roan muttered, but the pulse faded into silence.

The walk home took twenty minutes through streets settling into their evening rhythm. Lamplighters climbed ladders. Children ran their last loops before dinner. The arithmetic of the raid resolved cleanly in his head. Direct confrontation against a Hardened and four Tempered was death. The only path through was the cargo itself.

The Shard pulsed. *Yes.*

“You don’t know what’s in it.”

The pulse repeated. *Danger. Leverage.*

“That’s not an answer.”

The warmth settled into his spine and stayed there.

Home. The smell of stew. Mira at the stove, Lyra at the table with a textbook open to a page on alchemical reactions. The greeting was a look, the silence an open question.

Dinner was quiet. When the bowls were empty and Lyra retreated to her room, Roan spoke.

“I need to go out. Three nights from now. After midnight. I won’t be back until morning.”

Mira’s hands stilled on the table. She did not ask where, or why, or whether he had considered the risks. She asked the question that mattered.

“Can you come back?”

“Yes.” The arithmetic did not have a column for absolute certainty, but she knew that.

“Then go.”

He left the table. Behind him, the water started running in the sink. His mother began to hum something slow. He didn’t recognize the tune.

The preparation took two days. Roan acquired a vial of Hasteburn Tincture, a cheap stimulant that burned through Vitalis reserves in exchange for twenty minutes of improved reaction speed. The side effects were nausea, temporary blindness, and cardiac strain. The Inversion would handle the side effects without a heartbeat of delay.

On the third night, he left at midnight.

The tunnel entrance east of Greycross was sealed with a lock that was newer than the official closure notice. Someone wanted the pretense of abandonment. Someone wanted private access.

The lock was poor quality. The pick turned in seconds.

Inside, the tunnel smelled like earth and copper and something else. Something that made his eyes water and the Shard pulse in a rhythm that matched his breath.

*Familiar.* The word formed where he could feel it—not a voice, but a shape in the resonance of the Shard.

“Your cargo?”

The pulse sharpened. *Release. Containment is failing.*

Roan moved forward into the dark, keeping to the collapsed support beams and the shadows they cast. The chamber opened ahead of him, a natural cavern that had been widened by excavation.

The container was the size of a coffin, marked with symbols he didn’t recognize and a company seal he did: the Ironholm Alchemical Consortium. Official suppliers to the Black Spire.

The escort was spread around it in a perimeter. Four Tempered. One Hardened standing directly beside the cargo.

But the Hardened’s hands were shaking.

Roan watched from the shadows, running angles. The shift change was in three minutes. It would open a gap. The gap was a trap if the Hardened recovered before he reached the container, but the Hardened was not recovering. The man was terrified.

The Shard pulsed against his spine. *The cargo. Use it.*

“I don’t know what it is.”

*It is what Inversion was built for.*

Before he could ask what that meant, a new sound echoed through the tunnel. Footsteps. Someone approaching from the entrance, walking with the confidence of someone who knew exactly where they were going.

The Hardened turned. Too slow. The newcomer reached the container and pressed their hands against the symbols.

The container began to open.

The arithmetic failed. The variable was incomplete. The Shard pulsed—recognition warring with warning—and Roan pressed deeper into the darkness as the seal broke and whatever was inside started to wake up.
