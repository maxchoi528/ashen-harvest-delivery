Chapter 110: Cyrus Advances

Morning light bled grey through the curtain gaps before Roan opened his eyes. The angles were wrong. The shadows fell left when they should have fallen right.

His hand found Sidewound's hilt before he was fully awake.

“Convenient hour to visit.”

Drogan Hale sat in the single chair by the wall. Broad shoulders. A sealed document tube balanced on his knee.

“You knocked.”

“Twice.”

“The door—”

“Is missing its hinges because you oiled them last month and never put them back.” Drogan stood. “Get dressed. Cole wants you. Cyrus is out. You’re in.”

Roan sat up. His Vitalis reading drifted at the edge of perception: 4,200. Normal. His real number, the peak he’d touched during the tournament bracket, was closer to 30,000. He hadn’t shown that to anyone except Brendt.

Cyrus Ashford. The coach’s golden boy. The one whose family had been leaning on the Greel envoy’s presence to justify their son’s spot. Fumbled a catch in the Vellmark match. Dropped three exchanges. The numbers weren’t kind. The coach had been forced to change the lineup.

Roan swung his legs off the bed. He dressed in the dark without fumbling: black training clothes, boots laced tight, Sidewound settled at his hip. The Vance Pendant hummed low against his chest, a quiet constant he no longer questioned.

The walk to the administrative wing took seven minutes. Roan spent them calculating.

Option one: refuse the call-up. Maintain the hidden profile, but risk being read as cowardice by the Greel observer.

Option two: accept, keep the Initiate mask. Cole would probe, find the inconsistency, and the trust between them would fracture.

Option three: accept, show the real number. Reveal Forged-tier. Gain leverage, but lose the advantage of being underestimated.

He was still running the variables when the office door closed behind him.

Cole’s office smelled of old paper and tonic. The headmaster sat behind a desk that looked older than the Spire itself, fingers steepled. A Veritas Stone sat in the center of the polished wood like an unasked question.

“Close the door.”

Roan obeyed.

“You know why you’re here.”

“Cyrus is out. The coach needs a replacement.”

Cole’s eyes narrowed. “The Greel envoy requested observation of the full tournament. He specifically requested to watch candidates from the Black Spire. I need to know what I’m fielding before I put it on that stage.” He tapped the stone. “Verify your tier. Officially.”

Roan looked at the sphere. The math was simple. The pencil was on the page.

He placed his hand on the stone.

Light flared. Not the soft blue of an Initiate, but something brighter, harsher. The number formed in the air above the sphere.

Forged-tier. Capacity: 4,000–30,000.

Cole’s expression didn’t change. It was the look of a man confirming a suspicion. “Thirty thousand capacity. Your official record says Initiate. No progression filings. No training evaluations. Nothing.”

“I didn’t falsify them.”

“You neglected to update them.” Cole leaned forward. “The Greel family doesn’t care about the paperwork distinction. Your father’s record was expunged for the same reason—because the system couldn’t account for what he was. Are you going to fight tomorrow, or are you going to let the envoy watch you withdraw?”

“I’ll fight.”

Cole slid a form across the desk. “The Rift tryouts are in one month. The envoy has been formally assigned to your observation group. You will participate in the remaining tournament matches, and you will represent the Spire in the Rift trial. That is not a request.”

Roan took the paper. The envoy. Watching. The arithmetic recalculated, variables shifting into new patterns.

“Your father was brilliant,” Cole said. “He assumed skill would protect him from politics. It didn’t. The environment hasn’t changed. Think about that before you step onto a stage where half the audience has already decided your ending.”

The door closed behind Roan. Drogan waited in the corridor.

“The envoy?”

“Watching.”

“Your father—”

“My father is dead. I’m not.”

Drogan’s jaw tightened. “You fight better when someone’s depending on you. Train with Brendt. The match is tomorrow.”

The training room on the third sublevel was cold and dark. Brendt arrived looking like he’d rolled out of bed and walked directly into a fight.

“You got the call-up?”

“I got the call-up.”

Brendt cracked his knuckles. “Good. Show me what Forged-tier looks like.”

They clashed. Blade against blade, body against body. The strain built across Roan’s right shoulder—a minor tear from compensating too hard on the recovery drills. He let it settle, let the flaw sink deep into the tissue.

The Inversion caught it.

[Conversion successful: minor strain reversed.]

No daily capacity counter. No yield ratio. Just the clean, efficient reversal of a harmful effect into stable energy. The torn fibers realigned. The energy slid back into his core, seamless and silent.

Brendt didn’t miss the pause. “You moved faster on the recovery.”

“I’m compensating.”

“No. You’re just faster.”

They went again.

The briefing room was full by noon. The proctor was a Senior Rift Hunter, grey-haired and hard-faced, spreading documents across the central table.

“The Rift tryouts are scheduled for one month from today. Your groupings will be determined by your performance in the remaining tournament matches.”

Roan scanned the list posted on the wall. His name anchored the column.

**Group Four:**
- Roan Vance (Black Spire)
- Brendt Holloway (Black Spire)
- Theon Mercer (Black Spire)
- Greel Envoy (House Greel)

The envoy had signed up for the same trial. The envoy had specifically requested Group Four.

The math didn’t care about coincidence.

The envoy wasn’t here to kill him tonight. The envoy was here to measure him, to test his reflexes, to report back to the North whether Aldric Vance’s son was a threat worth eliminating or a corpse waiting for the right moment.

The real trial wasn’t tomorrow’s match. The real trial was the audition.

Roan walked out of the briefing room into the cold grey light. Above him, the Spire’s flag snapped against a low sky.

In the stands, the envoy was already taking his seat.
