Chapter 87: Counterweight

The corridor outside the Sentinel Bureau's secondary archive smelled of dust and old paper. Not the unpleasant kind of dust, the archive clerks kept things clean, but the accumulated weight of records no one had touched in years. Decades, in some cases.

Roan walked three paces behind the clerk, counting the torches. Seventeen on the left wall. Seventeen on the right. The Bureau kept its main archive in the Central Tower, but certain records, older ones, contested ones, got stored down here. Officially to conserve space. Unofficially to keep them away from anyone who asked the wrong questions.

The clerk had given him a clipboard with a requisition form. Reason cited: "Comparative analysis of Tier 3 techniques for curriculum development." Reasonable enough. Black Spire instructors requested historical technique records all the time. The clerk hadn't even looked at the form closely, just stamped it and pointed him toward the stairs.

That was the thing about bureaucratic legitimacy. Nobody questioned paperwork that looked legitimate.

"You want the Tempered-era records," the clerk said over his shoulder. "They're in Section Seven. Third row from the left, down two levels."

"Understood."

Roan adjusted his grip on the clipboard. Section Seven contained the oldest combat records in the secondary archive, going back to the founding years of the Institute. Most of it would be useless: technique descriptions from a time when people still wrote technique manuals in poetic metaphor instead of concrete terms.

But some of it would be useful. Specifically, the records about the Rift Hunt of seventy-three years ago. The one that had supposedly killed a dozen students and ended the old expedition program.

The one Aldric Vance had allegedly survived.

Three months of digging, and Roan had found exactly one official reference to his father's participation in that expedition. A single line in a casualty report: *Vance, A. Status: missing, presumed deceased.*

Missing wasn't the same as dead. Missing was what you wrote when you didn't want to admit someone had walked out of a disaster that should have killed them.

The stairs down to Section Seven were narrower than the main corridors, lit by a single yellowed lamp every fifteen steps. The air grew cooler as he descended. Storage optimization, the Bureau kept the deep archives cold to slow paper decay.

Sensible. Also convenient for someone who didn't want to be found.

Roan counted steps. Forty-seven to the first landing. Thirty-two to the second. Another forty before the corridor widened into the archive proper. He noted the lamp positions in his mental map. Fourteen lamps in the main archive. Fourteen exit routes if he needed them.

Paranoid? Maybe. But Brendt's cousin had mentioned something during their last conversation, a throwaway comment about the Bureau's "archival access policies" that had made Roan reconsider how deep this particular rabbit hole went.

*Anyone who knows what to look for can pull records*, the cousin had said. *The trick isn't leaving a trail.*

Roan had spent three weeks figuring out how to enter this archive without a trail. The clipboard helped. The forged requisition helped more. But the most important part was timing: the secondary archive's security rotation left a six-minute gap at the top of each hour. Maintenance cycle. Everyone above ground, checking climate controls.

He'd chosen 11:43 AM. Plenty of time to reach Section Seven, find the relevant records, and be gone before anyone realized a student had no business being in the restricted wing.

The archive stretched before him, rows of metal shelving, drawers labeled with faded numbers. Section Seven was easy to find: a bank of cabinets marked with Roman numerals going back five centuries. He pulled the first drawer in the third row.

Records. Lots of them. Cardstock folders, handwritten labels, dates in the old calendar format.

He scanned the headers. Expedition logs. Patrol reports. Casualty lists. Request forms.

Then he found it.

*Black Spire Expeditionary Force. Rift Hunt Summary Report, 73 years prior.*

His hands didn't shake. That would be unprofessional. But his pulse kicked up half a beat as he pulled the folder from its slot.

The report was thick. Forty pages, give or take. The cover page bore an official header: *CONFIDENTIAL. EYES ONLY. SENTINEL BUREAU JURISDICTION.*

Roan settled against the shelf and opened to the first page. The report opened with a mission brief. Standard stuff: Rift detected in the Hollow Reaches, too dangerous for a standard patrol. The Bureau had assembled an expeditionary force, forty-two cultivators, ranging from Initiate to Hardened. Their goal was to investigate, assess threat level, and if possible, seal the Rift before it expanded.

Roan found the roster on page three.

He scanned the names. Most were unfamiliar, surnames that had since vanished from the Institute's rolls, either through extinction or deliberate erasure. A few stood out: *Hale, D. Rank: Tempered. Assignment: Vanguard*. Drogan's father, then. Or maybe an uncle. Either way, Drogan had family on this expedition.

He kept looking. Page after page of supply manifests, equipment lists, tactical formations. The expedition had entered the Hollow Reaches through the Western Gate. They'd encountered Hollowkin on day three. Casualties had started mounting by day seven.

The official death toll at the end of the report: thirty-eight dead, three missing, one insane.

One insane. Not dead, not missing, *insane*. Someone had survived the Hollow Reaches and come back, only to lose their mind in the process.

Roan flipped to the section on survivors.

The list was short. Four names, all marked with the same notation: *Returned to Institute, debriefed, classified.*

Three of the names meant nothing to him.

The fourth made his breath catch.

*Vance, A. Rank: Tempered. Status: Missing (see supplemental).*

See supplemental. Of course.

He turned to the back of the report, where a handwritten addendum had been clipped to the final page. The handwriting was shaky, script that came from someone writing quickly, or under duress.

*Supplemental Report. Vance, Aldric*

*Subject exhibited unusual resistance to Hollow Reaches contamination. Recommended for extended observation. Subject escaped custody on 14th. Current whereabouts unknown. Recommend redacting from primary record.*

*— S.B. File code 7719*

Roan read the paragraph three times.

Unusual resistance. Escaped custody. Unknown whereabouts.

His father hadn't survived the expedition and walked away. He'd survived, been captured, been studied, and then escaped. The official story, a missing soldier, presumed dead, had been a cover. A convenient fiction to hide whatever the Bureau had been doing to him.

His jaw tightened. The arithmetic here was incomplete, but the shape of it was clear enough.

Aldric Vance had been more than a promising student. He'd been something the Sentinel Bureau wanted to keep, and something they'd failed to keep.

Roan photographed the supplemental report with his hand device. Both pages. He needed copies, but not here. Not where someone might notice the time stamps.

He was about to close the folder when a sound reached him from above.

Footsteps. Multiple sets, moving quickly.

The maintenance rotation ended at 11:50. It was 11:47.

Three minutes early.

Roan slid the folder back into its slot with practiced speed. His mind ran calculations faster than his hands: fourteen lamps meant fourteen exit points, but only three would take him back toward the stairs. The rest fed into subsidiary corridors. If they were coming from above, they'd expect him to head for the main exit.

He wouldn't.

Section Seven had a ventilation shaft on its north wall, a relic from the old heating system, maintained but rarely accessed. The blueprints were public record. Roan had memorized the layout three weeks ago.

He moved between the shelves, staying low, staying silent. The footsteps grew louder. Voices now, clipped and professional.

"—checked the main archive. Clear."

"Section Seven?"

"Starting there."

Roan found the ventilation shaft thirty seconds later, a metal grate behind a filing cabinet, exactly where the blueprints said it would be. He pulled the grate free, set it against the cabinet, and eased himself into the shaft.

The metal was cold against his palms. He pulled himself forward, inch by inch, until the shaft curved upward. He'd be able to exit near the climate control room on the third sublevel. From there, he could reach the service elevator and ride it to the main floors, blending in with the maintenance staff.

Behind him, the archive doors opened. Flashlight beams swept across the shelves.

"Clear in here."

"Check the back rows."

Roan kept crawling. His breathing was steady. His muscles burned from the effort, but that was fine. Pain was fine. Pain meant he was alive and moving.

The shaft narrowed as it curved upward. His shoulders fit, barely. Another few meters and he'd reach the exit grate.

His hand device buzzed. Once. A text message from Brendt.

*Where are you? Cole's looking for you.*

Roan thumbed a response without stopping: *Handle it.*

Another buzz. Brendt again.

*He wants to talk about the Ashford situation. Says it's urgent.*

The Ashford situation. Right. Because Cyrus Ashford's challenge had been hanging over him for three weeks, and Headmaster Cole had been pretending it didn't exist.

Roan sent back: *Coming.*

He reached the exit grate, pushed it open, and dropped into a dim corridor. Maintenance staff uniforms were hanging on a rack to his left, he grabbed one, shrugged it on, and headed for the service elevator.

The game kept changing. That was fine. He'd adapt. Cole's office occupied the fourth floor of the Administrative Wing, with a view of the training grounds below. The Headmaster was standing at the window when Roan entered, hands clasped behind his back.

"Mr. Vance."

"Headmaster."

Cole turned. His expression was neutral, professional blankness that came from decades of managing difficult conversations. "You've been difficult to reach."

"I've been occupied."

"So I'm told." Cole gestured to the chair across from his desk. "Sit."

Roan sat. He didn't lean back. Didn't cross his legs. Just perched on the edge of the seat, weight forward, ready to move.

Cole studied him for a moment. "The Ashford situation has escalated."

"Define escalated."

"Cyrus has filed a formal grievance with the Disciplinary Council. He's claiming you assaulted him during the preliminary rounds of the tournament, specifically, that you used a prohibited technique that caused him lasting damage."

Roan kept his face neutral. "That's not what happened."

"I'm aware. But he has three witnesses, the other challengers from your bracket, who're willing to testify that you struck him after the match was called. They claim it was unprovoked."

"They're lying."

"Yes." Cole's voice was flat. "But they're lying consistently, and they have no prior history with the Council. You, on the other hand, have a pattern of..." He paused, choosing his words. "Incidents. The altercation with the Greel envoy. The confrontation in the Eastern Corridor. The matter with the supply officer last month."

"Every one of those was defensive."

"I understand that. The Council may not."

Roan processed this. The Ashford family had been building a case against him for weeks, picking up allies, preparing their version of events. Cyrus hadn't challenged him directly because he was confident. He'd challenged him because he knew the system would favor the established family over the orphaned nobody.

The math was ugly. But not unsolvable.

"When's the Council hearing?"

"Three days. You'll have the opportunity to present your defense, but I should warn you—Headmaster Cole isn't inclined to favor students without family backing. Especially students who've been involved in multiple confrontations."

"Even if those confrontations were provoked."

"Even then." Cole's gaze was steady. "I'm going to recommend you accept a formal censure and apologize publicly. It won't erase the Ashford grievance, but it will reduce the punishment significantly."

Roan considered the offer. Public censure. Apology. Two words that would haunt him through the rest of his time at the Institute.

"If I refuse?"

"The Council will hear the full case. If they rule against you, and based on the evidence as it stands, they likely will, you'll face suspension. Expulsion."

The threat wasn't subtle. Cole was giving him a way out, one that cost him his pride but kept him in the Institute. It was a reasonable offer from someone trying to protect an asset.

It was also exactly what the Ashfords wanted.

"May I have time to consider?"

Cole's eyebrow rose slightly. "Time?"

"Until tomorrow morning. I need to review the situation."

A pause. Then Cole nodded. "Tomorrow. First bell. I need your answer before I can file my recommendation to the Council."

Roan stood. "Thank you, Headmaster."

He was at the door when Cole spoke again.

"Mr. Vance. I knew your father."

Roan stopped. Didn't turn.

"He was one of the most promising students this Institute has ever produced. Before the expedition." A pause. "Whatever you're doing, the investigations, the questions you're asking, be careful. Some doors, once opened, don't close again."

Roan left without responding. The training grounds were empty at this hour. Roan found a bench at the edge of the practice arena and sat, staring at the reports on his hand device.

The supplemental file on Aldric Vance was damning. Not in an obvious way, no confessions, no smoking guns, but the implications were clear. The Bureau had considered his father a research subject. They'd held him, studied him, and then lost him. Escaped. The word appeared in three separate expedition reports, always in reference to something the Bureau wanted kept quiet. Roan had cross-referenced it with every database he could access. Nothing.

*Some doors don't close again.*

He scrolled through the Ashford grievance. Three witnesses, consistent testimony, formal charges. The evidence was fabricated, but fabricated well. The Council would have to choose between believing three established students and one orphan with a reputation for trouble.

The tally didn't favor him.

But math wasn't everything. The witnesses were lying, which meant they'd been coached. Which meant someone had prepared this case carefully. The Ashfords hadn't just filed a grievance. They'd built a trap.

Three days. Three days to find the flaw in their structure.

His device buzzed again. Brendt this time, a longer message.

*Heard about Cole. You need backup?*

Roan typed back: *Stay visible. Make sure people see you with me. Not here, somewhere public.*

*On it. The usual spot?*

*The usual spot.*

He closed the device and stood. The sun was beginning its descent toward the horizon, painting the training grounds in shades of amber and gold. Three days to unravel a conspiracy. Three days to find leverage against a family that had been building power for generations.

Impossible. But so was Inversion. So was surviving a mission that should have killed you. So was walking out of a Bureau facility with classified documents in your pocket.

Roan started walking. Somewhere in this Institute, the Ashfords had made a mistake. They just didn't know it yet.
