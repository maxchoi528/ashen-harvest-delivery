Chapter 156: The Greel Signature

Iris had chosen the ambulance bay because she understood optics.

That was the first useful thing about the ambush.

An attack in the containment chamber would trigger too many official logs. An attack in the service corridor risked killing Brendt before capture, and despite whatever Iris told herself, this operation wasn't built around simple murder. The ambulance bay sat in the middle category: close enough to the Rift exit that chaos was plausible, far enough from the containment ward grid that records could be edited, isolated enough that hired men could work before municipal response arrived.

Good venue selection.

Bad timing.

Roan catalogued the bay while Iris waited for his answer to an order he had already refused.

Ceiling height: six meters. Vehicles: three, two usable as cover, one black van with reinforced panels and probable suppression array. Exits: service corridor behind them, shuttered vehicle ramp ahead, personnel door on the north wall, maintenance hatch near the fuel cabinet. Lighting: poor, rain-gray through high windows. Civilians: Pellan, useless but witness-shaped. Brendt: alive, immobile, high priority. Ines: armed, low ammunition, standing close enough to Brendt to make her both guard and hostage risk. Drogan: injured but functional, attention on captain. Roan: Forged baseline, zero inversion quota, Sidewound stressed from door cut, Black Rite stable, exposure risk pending.

Enemies: four visible mercenaries plus Iris.

Unknown: hidden sniper, vehicle driver, suppressive wards, Iris's current emotional fracture pattern.

He looked at the black van.

There was a driver.

Heat distortion through the windshield, faint pressure behind the wheel, breathing too steady. Fifth mercenary. Likely support specialist.

Iris had also chosen her clothing for plausible deniability.

No House combat uniform. No Vell crest. The dark coat could belong to any rich student with a taste for dramatic rain and bad decisions. The cane-blade carried no external mark until activated. Even her earrings were plain silver instead of the family gold she normally wore during formal campus events. A witness could describe her and still leave room for lawyers to argue resemblance, coercion, or malicious misidentification.

Roan respected the preparation.

He would have respected it more if she hadn't brought Greel men with better contingency plans than hers.

"You keep looking at the van," Iris said.

"It keeps being interesting."

"It's transport."

"So is a coffin. Context matters."

Her fingers flexed once on the cane. She knew. Not everything, but enough to know the van wasn't hers. The realization had likely arrived in pieces before today: sealed communication channels she couldn't access, funds routed through Greel intermediaries, a captain who treated her orders as suggestions until they fit his own. Pride had made the pieces inconvenient. Crisis had made them visible.

Roan could use visible.

"Your grandfather outsourced you," he said.

Iris's face went flat. "Careful."

"You came here thinking you were recovering stolen assets. Valech came here prepared to remove witnesses, including you if necessary. Those are different missions."

"You don't know that."

"The underside array is angled through your retreat path. The driver has no line to protect you. Valech watches Drogan, not your safety. The support mercenary has looked at you twice: once for permission, once to see whether you were in his firing lane. Guess which look lasted longer."

Iris didn't turn toward her men.

Good discipline.

Her eyes did move, barely, toward the van's reflection in the wet concrete.

There.

She saw the underside bracket.

Valech saw her see it.

The captain's smile didn't change. That was more confirmation than any confession.

Roan stepped half a pace right, placing himself between Iris and Brendt without appearing to protect either fully. Ambiguity forced enemies to spend thought. Thought was cheap until everyone needed it at once, naturally.

"You can still walk out," he said.

"With you?"

"No. Separately. Preferably far away."

"And let you keep what you stole from the Rift?"

"I didn't steal it."

"Then what did you do?"

Roan looked at her for one second too long.

"Inherited."

The word hit harder than he intended.

Or perhaps exactly as hard.

Iris had inherited lies, debt, House obligation, and a grandfather who had turned affection into governance. Roan had inherited a Shard in his spine, a civilization's corpse, six enemies, and a father who apologized in thirteen words. Neither inheritance was fair. Fairness had never been offered as part of the transaction.

Iris swallowed whatever she had almost said.

Valech ruined the silence.

"How did you get the access lock revoked?" Roan asked.

Iris's eyes narrowed. "That's your first question?"

"No. It's the first one worth asking aloud."

The captain chuckled.

Drogan's gaze didn't leave him. "Name."

"Captain Valech," the man said. "Greel Contract Division."

Not Vell.

Iris flinched slightly at the name Greel. Tiny. Almost hidden.

Roan kept it. She disliked the association being spoken openly. Shame? Fear? Strategic annoyance? Perhaps all three. She hadn't fully crossed the line in her own mind yet. People committing betrayal often preferred euphemisms until the body count made them decorative.

"Greel," Drogan said.

The word landed heavy.

Valech inclined his head. "Former Black Squad instructor Drogan Kade. I was told you might be difficult."

"People keep saying that."

"They tend to survive longer when they do."

"Not today."

Iris's fingers tightened on the cane.

Roan watched her, not Valech. The captain was the blade. Iris was the hand that had chosen where to point it, and the hand was shaking in places she didn't understand.

"You shouldn't have come personally," Roan said.

"You shouldn't have gone into that Rift."

"I had business."

"With dead things?"

"Family business."

That got through.

Iris's composure cracked just enough to show the old wound beneath: the Vell patriarch's lies, the file Roan had given her, the possibility that everything she had been trained to defend had been built on deliberate omission. She hadn't forgiven him for revealing it. People often resented the person who gave them accurate pain.

"Then you found something," she said.

"Several things."

"Give them to me."

"No."

"Roan."

"Still no."

One of the Hardened mercenaries shifted his rifle toward Brendt.

Roan moved his eyes to the rifle.

Not his body. Just his eyes.

The man stopped.

He didn't know why. Good instincts, perhaps. Or enough experience to recognize that some teenagers covered in Rift blood weren't performance problems but workplace hazards.

Iris noticed.

Her gaze sharpened. "What happened to you in there?"

"Education."

"Your signal changed."

That was interesting.

She could sense it? Or the Greel equipment could? Roan adjusted his model of the ambush. Iris wasn't merely here to retrieve documents or capture him. She had expected a certain Roan Vance to exit: Hardened peak, dangerous but contained, likely depleted. Instead she saw something else and didn't yet have a category for it.

Useful.

"Everyone's signal changes after a Tier Three Rift," Ines said from behind him.

Iris looked at her. "Ines Carrow. You always did have expensive survival instincts."

"And yet I keep finding underfunded problems."

"Step away from Brendt."

"No."

"This isn't your fight."

Ines smiled without warmth. "It became billable."

Roan almost approved.

Almost.

Valech raised one hand. The mercenaries adjusted formation. Two split toward the sides, rifles angled. One stayed on Brendt. The driver inside the van moved. A suppression array began to hum from under the vehicle, low and unpleasant.

Not full dead-zone technology. A portable dampener. It would interfere with Vitalis projection, maybe disrupt weak wards, perhaps blur sensing. It wouldn't remove the Codex entirely.

Still inconvenient.

Drogan stepped forward.

Valech matched him.

The room understood the duel before anyone announced it. Drogan and Valech stood on a line that separated the ambulance bay into two tactical problems: master and captain in the center, Roan and the mercenaries around the edges, Brendt on the frame, Iris as command presence with emotional contamination.

"You don't want him," Roan said to Iris.

She blinked. "What?"

"Brendt. You pointed rifles at him because he's leverage. But you don't want him dead. You need me cooperative, not irrationally thorough."

"I know what you are."

"No. You know what the Vell patriarch told you I am. Different disease."

Her jaw set.

Good. Keep her attention. Keep the rifleman uncertain. Keep Valech from giving the shooting order while Drogan read his stance. Keep the driver focused on maintaining the dampener instead of calling reinforcements. Keep Brendt alive.

Many plates. Too few hands.

Roan preferred knives.

"You gave me forged documents," Iris said.

"No."

"You manipulated my response."

"Yes."

"You used me to expose my family's accounts."

"Yes."

"And you expect me to trust anything you say?"

"No. I expect you to check the parts you don't want to be true."

That landed.

For half a second, she looked seventeen instead of trained and sharpened and dressed in other people's plans.

Then Valech said, "Miss Vell."

One warning. Professional. He had seen the hesitation.

Valech's reaction to the word inherited was also useful.

He didn't react like a man hearing metaphor. His pupils tightened behind the half-mask. One finger tapped the spear grip twice, then stopped. Greel had briefed him on Vance inheritance, then. Perhaps not the full Codex. Perhaps not the temple. But enough to make the word operational.

Roan adjusted threat priority again.

Valech wasn't merely hired muscle. He was informed muscle. Those were rarer and much more expensive. Iris might think she had borrowed a captain. Greel had placed an observer with a spear.

"What did Greel tell you?" Roan asked him.

Valech smiled. "Enough."

"That's the slogan of men who signed bad contracts."

"And boys who survive on secrets."

"Mine pay better."

"For now."

Drogan's blade shifted a fraction toward Valech's throat line. "Stop flirting and fight."

"Jealous?" Roan asked.

"Tired."

"That's your default state."

"It gets worse when you talk."

Brendt made a weak sound from the frame. "He's not wrong."

The fact that Brendt could still participate in insults steadied the room in a strange way. Ines's shoulders lowered by a fraction. Pellan stopped staring at the blood and remembered how to breathe. Even Iris's eyes flicked toward the frame with something too complicated to name quickly.

Valech noticed and made the sensible choice: he attacked before the human element further contaminated the operation.

Iris's face closed.

"Take the documents," she said. "Alive if possible. Break his hands if necessary."

Roan glanced at Brendt.

"See?" he said. "That's a worse plan."

The room moved.

Roan preferred when rooms moved one piece at a time. This one chose every piece at once, which was discourteous and, worse, inefficient in every expensive direction available at once, naturally.

- break -

The support mercenary's first mistake was assuming Ines was pinned by Brendt.

She was, in the sense that she wouldn't abandon the frame. She wasn't pinned in the sense of being harmless. Ines had arranged three tools along the underside rail during the corridor retreat. Roan had seen two. The third revealed itself when the support mercenary tried to circle behind the med-rig.

A wire snapped out from the frame's lower bracket and wrapped his ankle.

He looked down.

Ines pressed a switch.

The wire retracted.

The mercenary's foot left the floor, and his helmet struck the side of the ambulance with a noise that improved Roan's opinion of physics.

"That was expensive," Ines said.

"His helmet?" Brendt rasped.

"My wire."

"You're all terrible."

"Alive enough to judge," Roan said. "Good trend."

Brendt's answering gesture was obscene and weak, plus therefore reassuring.

Iris's eyes flicked toward him again.

There was a moment when she could have ordered a shot while Roan's attention sat on Brendt. She didn't. That gave Roan a clearer model: Iris wanted control, retrieval, proof, perhaps revenge. She didn't want Brendt dead for its own sake. Valech, on the other hand, would shoot Brendt if it moved the operation forward by half a meter.

Two enemy command structures.

Excellent. Enemy disagreement was the cheapest reinforcement available.

Roan angled his next step to increase that disagreement. He put himself where Iris's blade could reach him if she lunged, but where Valech's support line had to shoot past her to hit Brendt. She noticed. Her nostrils flared once.

"Stop using me as cover," she said.

"Stop standing in useful places."

"I can move."

"Then you concede the frame."

Her cane-blade lifted.

She didn't move. The tip traced a shallow arc—not a loss of control, but a question her pride couldn't yet admit to the room.

Good.

People with pride could be positioned if one found the right insult and let them hold it.

Valech attacked Drogan first.

That confirmed his quality.

Amateurs waited for the younger combatants to create chaos. Professionals removed the veteran before the veteran simplified the room. But the duel wasn't a simple veteran check. Roan recognized the delay for what it was: the dampener needed sustained focus from the driver to cycle into full suppression. Valech wasn't looking for a quick win. He was holding the center line, locking Drogan's attention in close quarters while the field calibrated for a wider sweep. Valech crossed the center line with a short spear that unfolded from his forearm guard, its head matte black and etched with Greel runework. Not a flashy weapon. Designed for armor gaps, tendon lines, and close suppression.

Drogan met it with his short blade.

The first exchange produced no sparks.

Only a flat, ugly sound as edge met warded metal and both men adjusted before impact fully resolved.

Roan didn't watch the second exchange. Drogan could survive for the next ten seconds. Brendt might not.

The nearest rifleman fired.

Ines shoved Brendt's medical frame sideways. The round tore through the frame's upper rail instead of Brendt's throat. Roan crossed the distance in three steps and cut the rifle seam with Sidewound.

The weapon split.

The mercenary dropped it instantly and drew a sidearm. Competent. Roan disliked competent enemies slightly less than incompetent allies but only slightly.

No inversion quota. No Crimson Wake. No easy reversal.

Forged baseline would have to do.

Roan stepped inside the sidearm line, struck the wrist with his left palm, and drove Sidewound's pommel into the man's mask filter. The filter cracked. The man tried to headbutt him. Roan let the helmet glance off his shoulder, hooked the ankle, and put him down behind the medical frame.

Not dead.

Alive enemies created noise.

Roan cut the man's thigh reinforcement seam and moved on. The mercenary wouldn't stand quickly.

Second mercenary came from the left, blade and pistol combination. Hardened peak. Good footwork. Too focused on Roan's right hand. Tournament footage again.

Roan let him see the right-hand feint.

The man bit.

Roan drew the standard knife with his left and drove it through the inside of the elbow. The pistol dropped. Sidewound followed across the abdomen reinforcement, shallow but disabling. The man collapsed with a controlled breath, trying to roll away.

Roan kicked him behind the knee and took his pistol.

"Borrowing," he said.

The mercenary swore.

Fair.

Ines fired from behind the ambulance. Her sidearm barked twice. The Tempered support near the van ducked behind cover. Good. He was the dampener tech or guarding the driver. Either way, pinning him helped.

The ambulance bay punished large movements.

Vehicles created blind corners. Rainwater made the concrete slick near the ramp. The overhead lights flickered whenever the suppression equipment drew power, slicing motion into unpleasant fragments. Every time a rifle fired, the sound bounced off metal doors and returned from the wrong direction. It wasn't a duel hall. It was a garage with too many people trying to perform surgery with weapons.

Roan used that.

He kept Iris between himself and the van whenever possible, forcing the support mercenary to choose between clean shots and political suicide. He kept his left side closer to Brendt's frame because Iris had seen him favor right-hand lines in the tournament footage. He let the stolen pistol hang visible for three exchanges longer than necessary, then dropped it under the ambulance with his foot, where Ines could reach it if she needed a surprise.

She did.

A mercenary lunged around the rear wheel, knife angled toward the stabilizer feed. Ines's hand came up with the pistol Roan had left there and fired into the man's knee at contact range.

He went down.

"Borrowing," she said.

"Usage fee applies," Roan replied.

"Put it on my tab."

"Your tab has structural issues."

"So does his knee."

Brendt wheezed something that might've been a laugh.

The sound made Iris hesitate again.

Small hesitation. Useful hesitation.

When the dampener sputtered, the room became legible in layers.

Roan felt the mercenary on the left shifting weight to his rear foot. Felt the driver behind the van door holding breath before firing the suppression device. Felt Pellan trying not to run. Felt Ines's knife hand tighten over the frame rail. Felt Brendt's pulse stutter against the stabilizer patch.

And Iris.

Iris's Vitalis had always been polished. House-trained, well-fed, layered with technique instead of desperation. Now the polish had hairline cracks. Not weakness. Fracture. Her power moved through forms taught by people who had never expected her to question the hand that fed them. Roan knew the shape because he had built his own life around questioning every hand.

Her blade came in fast, cutting toward the stabilizer again.

He didn't block the blade directly.

He cut the question underneath it.

Sidewound found the point where her technique committed to a harmless-looking wrist turn. Roan touched that seam with the flat of the knife. Her Vitalis sheath skipped. The blade passed below the stabilizer instead of through it, slicing a strap but not the line.

Iris recovered beautifully.

Annoying.

"Someone trained you better," Roan said.

"Someone tried."

"Was it the Vell patriarch?"

"No."

Truth.

"Good. He seems like a poor teacher."

"He taught me not to trust boys who weaponize files."

"Then he taught one useful thing."

That almost got a laugh out of her. Almost. Then Valech's spear rang against Drogan's blade and the moment died.

Moments did that around Greel men.

Iris moved.

The cane wasn't a cane.

It extended into a narrow thrusting blade wrapped in pale gold Vitalis. Vell House technique. Her line was elegant, controlled, aimed not at Roan but at the stabilizer on Brendt's medical frame.

Smarter than attacking him.

Roan intercepted with the stolen pistol.

He didn't fire at her body. Too much political cost, too much uncertainty, not enough need. He fired at the cane blade.

The round struck the gold Vitalis sheath and flattened, but the impact changed the line. Iris's blade missed the stabilizer by two inches and carved a line through the frame padding.

She looked at him.

"You would shoot me?"

"I shot the cane."

"That isn't an answer."

"It was a warning. Answers cost more."

Her eyes hardened.

She attacked again.

Iris was better than he remembered.

But she had learned from the last months, not better than Selene in raw blade discipline or better than Cyrus in formal duel conditioning. She didn't overcommit. She aimed at equipment, movement, leverage. She tried to cut straps, sever stabilizer lines, force Roan to choose between blocking and keeping Brendt's frame intact.

Good.

Too good to treat as a confused rich girl.

Not yet enemy enough to kill.

That middle category was irritating.

Roan gave ground, using the ambulance corner to limit her angles. Ines dragged Brendt's frame backward toward the second med-rig. Pellan, to his credit, had found a spine and was helping push.

Drogan and Valech crashed into the black van.

The van's side panel buckled inward.

The dampener sputtered.

For one heartbeat, Roan's senses cleared.

He felt his new Forged baseline fully for the first time outside Rift interference. Four thousand stable, organs reinforced, senses sharper along heat and pressure, plus intent. Peak far above that, but peak without quota would bite.

Iris felt something too.

Her eyes widened.

"You broke through."

"Yes."

"In there?"

"Poor timing to ask."

She retreated half a step before she meant to. The fear wasn't large. Iris had too much pride and training for that. But it existed.

Roan didn't press.

Pressing her now might force a lethal exchange, and he didn't want lethal. Not because killing Iris would trouble his sleep. She was over fourteen and armed, then had hired Greel mercenaries to block a medical evacuation. The ethical paperwork was manageable.

But Iris alive was a pressure point on the Vell patriarch, a witness to Greel involvement, a fractured heir who might still split from her grandfather at a useful moment.

Dead, she was an incident.

Alive, she was leverage.

Roan preferred leverage.

The driver opened the van door and aimed a compact suppression device at Brendt.

Roan threw the stolen pistol.

It hit the driver's wrist. The device fired into the ceiling, bathing the lights in violet static.

Ines shot the driver in the shoulder.

"Nonlethal?" Roan asked.

"Mostly."

"Define mostly."

"Not now."

Valech kicked Drogan away from the van and rolled his shoulder back into place with a sound that made Pellan gag.

Drogan wiped blood from his mouth.

"You're trained," he said.

Valech inclined his head. "By men who studied men like you."

"Then they taught you old mistakes."

The captain smiled.

Then he attacked harder.
