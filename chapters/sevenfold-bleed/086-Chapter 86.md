Chapter 86: The Practice Hall Trap

The morning after the Ashford mess, Roan found Brendt in the training yard before dawn.

Brendt had the look of someone who had slept in his gear. Dark circles under his eyes, jaw set tight. His Vitalis readings flickered at the edge of Roan's perception, unsteady, a candle in wind.

"You're up early," Brendt said.

Couldn't sleep. Roan didn't say it. He'd spent four hours staring at the ceiling. The Codex Shard had been cycling strange readings all night. Not painful. Noisy. Like a radio tuned to static in his spine. Every attempt to focus on a clean inversion vector scattered against the interference.

He needed to move. Sitting still with that many unknowns was worse than forward motion.

"Training," Roan said. "Figured you'd be here."

Brendt shrugged. The gesture was too controlled. Brendt performed casual like a second language, and this morning the performance was fraying.

"Warden Drogan sent word," Brendt said. "He's requesting both of us at the Hollow Reaches checkpoint. Something about the last assignment's intel being outdated."

Roan's eyes narrowed. The Hollow Reaches checkpoint was a Sentinel-run waystation sixty kilometers south of Ironholm. It processed Rift incursions, maintained supply lines, and served as a buffer between the city's civilian sectors and the deeper danger zones.

A mundane posting, in theory. In practice, it meant contact with active Hollowkin samples, exposure to Rift contamination, and a forty-eight-hour rotation where the rules of engagement loosened considerably.

Roan had been angling for a Hollow Reaches posting since he'd arrived at Black Spire. The data density was unmatched. Actual Hollowed Cities. Unclassified salvage. Real threats.

But Drogan requesting both of them? Together? Right after the Ashford incident?

The cost-benefit on coincidence was terrible. Nothing Drogan did was accidental.

"When?" Roan asked.

"Tomorrow morning. Dawn transport."

Eighteen hours to prepare. Enough to optimize his loadout, review protocols, and figure out what he was missing. Not enough for proper reconnaissance on Drogan's actual agenda.

"What aren't you telling me?" Roan said.

Brendt's jaw did that tightening thing again. His weight shifted, ready to move, or ready to deflect.

"The dispatch order mentioned Elevated Threat Protocol," Brendt said.

ETP. Reinforced team composition. Mandatory equipment checks. A hard extraction limit. It meant the Sentinels had seen something on their sensor nets that wasn't in the official briefing packet.

"How elevated?" Roan asked.

"Tier 3 minimum. Tier 4 possible."

Tier 4. Crowned-tier Hollowed Sovereigns. Entities that required full Sentinel strike teams and still left the survivors writing eulogies.

Roan held the column in his head: Brendt at Tempered, roughly 1,200 Vitalis on a good day. Roan himself was pressing into the Hardened threshold, his surface reading a modest 800, burst capacity closer to 1,200. Against a Tier 4 Sovereign, even with a full squad, the math was a rear-guard action, not a fight.

The smart play was to beg off. Claim illness. Claim prior commitment. But Drogan had specifically requested both of them. This wasn't a voluntary rotation; it was an evaluation.

"What did you tell Warden Drogan?" Roan asked.

"I told him we'd be there."

Brendt didn't flinch from fights. It was admirable. It was also going to get them killed if the threat profile was accurate.

"I need to make some calls," Roan said.

Brendt's eyebrows rose. "Calls about what?"

"Equipment. The kind that doesn't go through official channels."

- break -

Theon Mercer was in the armory, running maintenance on a set of standard-issue riot shields. His Vitalis readings were steady. Tempered-tier, all natural talent and minimal family name. A legacy student coasting on reflexes.

"Roan." Theon set down his tools. "Didn't expect to see you here. After yesterday's thing with Cyrus, I figured you'd be lying low."

"I need equipment. Off-record."

Theon went still. He knew what off-record meant. It meant Roan was planning something that couldn't survive scrutiny.

"What kind of equipment?" Theon asked carefully.

"The kind that makes a Hardened threshold scrape look like something that can survive a Tier 4 engagement."

Silence. Theon stared at him, the implications running behind his eyes.

"That's not small," Theon said finally. "The people who hold that kind of gear, they're not the kind you walk away from."

"I have something to trade," Roan said. "Information. About the checkpoint operation."

Theon's eyes narrowed. "You know something about the Hollow Reaches assignment?"

Roan's pieces had been assembling since the dispatch order. Drogan's timing. Brendt's exhaustion. The Sentinel Bureau's silence on the actual threat assessment. Someone at the checkpoint had seen something that the official channels were trying to bury, and Drogan was sending them in not because it was a standard rotation, but because he needed eyes he could trust in a position where normal oversight couldn't reach.

"I know enough," Roan said. "And the people who need that kind of information will pay for it."

Theon weighed this. His Vitalis readings cycled through calculation, risk assessment, a flash of something that might have been greed before settling back into controlled neutrality.

"There's a woman who runs the salvage depot near the southern dockyards," Theon said finally. "Elara Voss. If you have something she wants, she'll have something you need."

Voss. Roan had seen her name in old market logs. A salvage operator with reach into the Sentinel Bureau's logistics chain. High-end boutique work.

"What does she want?" Roan asked.

"Pre-Cataclysm artifacts. Expedition logs. First-generation survey data. She fills gaps in the historical record."

Roan's mind moved to his father's notes, compressed on the data chip in his jacket. Coordinates. Nothing that compromised his core inheritance, but enough to prove he had access to a valuable information stream.

"How do I make contact?"

Theon gave him the address and the window. Midnight to four AM. The dockyards at their emptiest.

- break -

The dockyards smelled of brine and machine oil. Rust-streaked warehouses lined the water's edge. The air carried something chemical underneath that Roan didn't want to identify. His Vitalis senses prickled at the edge of perception, background contamination from the nearest Rift access point, maybe a kilometer south, leaking its influence into the local environment like poison into groundwater.

Voss's operation occupied a converted customs building. One red lantern. Two guards at the entrance. One mobile on the eastern wall. All Tempered-tier. Professional spacing. Overlapping fields of observation.

Roan approached. A woman with close-cropped hair and a scar that ran from her temple to her jaw stepped into his path.

"Closed for the night."

"Theon Mercer sent me."

The woman's expression didn't change, but her Vitalis readings shifted, a verification against an internal list. She disappeared inside, returned with a nod.

"Second floor. Third door on the left."

Elara Voss wasn't what Roan had expected. She was old, but not elderly. Sixty years of hard edges under a worn coat. Her Vitalis readings were strange, suppressed, but with a core density that suggested she was operating well above the visible surface.

"Sit," she said.

Roan sat. The chair was positioned so the lamp lit his face while leaving hers in shadow.

"Theon says you have information about Hollow Reaches operations. That's dangerous cargo."

"I'm not asking for charity. I'm trading."

"You talk like a Vance," she said. "Quick to the arithmetic. Slow to the trust."

"You knew my family."

"I traded with your father's operators on occasion. He had the same habit. Sitting like he was ready to move in any direction without committing to any of them." She tilted her head. "What do you need?"

"Hollow Reaches checkpoint. The Sentinel Bureau is burying something in their threat assessment. I need equipment that shifts the arithmetic."

"What are you offering?"

Roan produced the data chip. "Updated coordinates on seventeen pre-Cataclysm sites in the southern sectors. Swept from the Vance expedition logs. Third series."

Voss took the chip, turning it over in her fingers. Her Vitalis readings flickered with recognition.

"This is real."

"The information is real. Terms?"

"I don't have heavy ordnance in stock. That level of gear requires authorization I can't move in a single night." She rose, crossed to a cabinet, and returned with a small wooden box, lacquered black and sealed with a wax stamp.

Inside, resting on velvet, was a glass vial containing liquid that shifted between silver and black depending on the angle of the light.

"Wraithspite," Voss said. "Concentrated Hollowkin suppressant. One dose gives you approximately thirty minutes of immunity to Tier 4 and below Hollowed influence. After that, your system needs twelve hours to clear the compound naturally."

The arithmetic was immediate. Thirty minutes of immunity. It wasn't a kill shot, but it was a massive correction to the survival probability curve.

"What do you want for it?"

"The coordinates are a down payment. I want a share of the data from the checkpoint. Anything unusual. Anything the Sentinel Bureau tries to bury."

"You want an asset on the inside."

"I want eyes where the Bureau is actively lying. The checkpoint is being used for something. I don't know what yet. When you find out, I want to know."

Roan weighed the ledger. A gray-area trade. Not betrayal, but a commitment to a woman he'd met twenty minutes ago.

The column said the risk was acceptable. The column also said he was out of time.

"Deal."

Voss smiled, thin and cold. "Good. The Wraithspite will be delivered to the transport drop point by tomorrow morning. Don't waste the dose."

She slid the box across the table. Roan took it, tucked it into his jacket, and stood.

"One more thing," Voss said.

Roan paused.

"Drogan Hale survived your father's expedition because he made choices that the other survivors didn't. If you're going to walk into whatever he's planning, you should decide whether you're walking as his student or as his heir. Those aren't always the same thing."

She turned away, dismissing him. Roan walked out into the cold morning air, the Wraithspite vial cold against his chest, and tried to calculate whether he'd just made a deal or just made a mistake.

- break -

The transport left on schedule.

Dawn light painted the Sentinel compound in shades of gray and amber as Roan and Brendt boarded the armored carrier. Reinforced plating. Twin mounted turrets. A medical bay currently empty except for a rack of emergency supplies.

Drogan was inside, checking systems. His Iron Hand of Hale caught the light as the mechanical fingers flexed.

"You made contact with Voss," Drogan said. Not a question.

"She had what I needed."

"She's useful. She's also compromised in ways she doesn't fully understand. The information you gave her will reach people who are actively looking for Vance artifacts. When it does, they'll come looking for you."

Roan had calculated that risk. He'd decided it was acceptable because the alternative was walking into a Tier 4 engagement without contingency gear.

"I understand the risk," Roan said.

"Do you?" Drogan's expression didn't change. "Your father understood risks too. He calculated them perfectly, every time. And he still ended up dead because the one variable he couldn't account for was what he'd have to sacrifice to keep the people he cared about alive."

The transport lurched into motion. Through the armored windows, Ironholm slid past, the academy's towers giving way to industrial districts, then suburbs, then empty road stretching toward the southern checkpoint.

Brendt had been quiet since they'd boarded. Now he spoke up, his voice careful.

"What's the actual threat assessment? Not the official one. The real one."

Drogan looked at him. Then he reached into his jacket and produced a data chip, older than the one Roan had traded, more worn.

"Three weeks ago, a Sentinel patrol went dark near the southern sector. Standard twelve-hour rotation. No reported anomalies. When they didn't check in, a rescue team was dispatched. They found the patrol's equipment, their transport, and no bodies."

"No bodies," Brendt repeated.

"No blood. No signs of combat. The Vitalis traces were present but fading. Like someone had been there and then ceased to be present."

"What caused it?" Roan asked.

"That's the question." Drogan held up the chip. "The rescue team uploaded their findings to this before they went dark too. Same pattern. No bodies. No blood. No combat. Just absence. The Sentinel Bureau has been trying to suppress the reports, but the Hollow Reaches checkpoint has been receiving intermittent signals from the affected area. Signals that match human vocal patterns but don't correspond to any recorded language."

"Shadow talk," Brendt said quietly.

Roan knew the term. Shadow talk was what Hollowkin used when they were preparing something. A corruption ritual. A territorial expansion. A coordinated incursion. It was the linguistic equivalent of footsteps in an empty room.

"How many signals?" Roan asked.

"Thirty-seven over the past three weeks. All from the same sector. All at intervals that suggest increasing activity."

Thirty-seven signals. A pattern that was accelerating. A Bureau that was burying the reports because they didn't understand what they were looking at.

Drogan pocketed the chip. "We're not going in there to fight. We're going in there to observe. Gather data on what the Hollowkin are preparing and report back before we trigger any response. If we encounter an active threat, we withdraw. Immediately. No heroics. No confrontations."

"And if withdrawal isn't an option?" Roan asked.

Drogan's Iron Hand of Hale flexed once, a long, slow curl of mechanical fingers.

"Then I pray the Wraithspite you bought from Voss is worth the debt you owe her."

The transport drove south, into the Hollow Reaches.
