Chapter 56: Tier One Rift

The knock came at seventh bell.

Roan was already awake. Had been for an hour. He'd spent it at the small desk by the window, running numbers on three different purchase orders that wouldn't reconcile no matter how he arranged them. The discrepancy was nine hundred credits—not a rounding error, not a mispost. Someone was using procurement to cover a movement trail, and the pattern didn't match any legitimate student operation. He needed to confirm the paper trail before the weekly audit.

The knock came again. Three sharp raps. Not Brendt's rhythm. Too deliberate.

He crossed the room and opened the door.

Ines Carrow stood in the corridor, dressed for travel. Her coat was heavier than the weather called for. She had a satchel over one shoulder and her hair pulled back tight. Her eyes swept past him into the room, cataloguing the desk, the scattered papers, the half‑empty cup of cold tea.

"You're awake," she said. Not a question.

"I sleep lightly." He leaned against the doorframe. "It's almost seventh bell. You're not supposed to be here."

"I know what time it is." She shifted her weight. "I need to talk to you. Privately."

Roan considered the options. Ines Carrow didn't come to his room at dawn for casual conversation. Everything about her posture said urgency—the too‑heavy coat, the packed satchel, the way her hand kept drifting toward the strap. She was planning to leave, or planning to do something she couldn't take back.

He stepped aside.

Ines entered and stopped in the middle of the room. She didn't sit. Her eyes moved over the papers on his desk, and he watched her register the columns of numbers, the purchase orders, the margin calculations. She filed that information and dismissed it.

"There's a job," she said. "Off‑books. I was approached yesterday."

Roan closed the door. "Define off‑books."

"Black Spire isn't the client. It's an individual. Private contract. The pay is—" She paused. "Significant."

"How significant."

"Fifty thousand credits. Half up front."

Roan did the ledger. Fifty thousand was more than most students saw in two years. "For what kind of work?"

"Retrieval." Ines met his eyes. "There's an artifact in the Hollow Reaches. A sealed section that hasn't been accessed in decades. The client wants what's inside."

"The Hollow Reaches." He repeated the words flatly. "Tier Four territory."

"Yes."

"You're Tempered. I'm Tempered. The Hollow Reaches is Forged minimum, and even then you'd want a full party." He crossed his arms. "Who is this client?"

"I don't know his name. That's part of why I'm here." Ines's jaw tightened. "He approached me through a dead drop. Left instructions in a location I check for work notices. He knew I was looking for off‑books contracts. He knew my rates. He knew—" She stopped.

"Knew what?"

"He knew I had a debt."

Roan's expression didn't change. That information wasn't public. Ines owed money to someone, he had suspected from the way she handled credits, the careful rationing, the moments when she went quiet in the dining hall. But he hadn't confirmed it.

"How did he know about your debt?" he asked.

"That's what I want to know." Ines pulled a folded paper from her coat pocket and held it out. "He left this. The drop location was in Greycross, outside the district proper. The seal is Carrow family wax."

Roan took the paper. The wax seal was authentic—three ravens of House Carrow pressed deep into the red. Someone with access to Carrow property had prepared this.

He unfolded it.

The handwriting was precise, almost mechanical. No signature. The message was brief:

*The Carrow debt can be cleared. The Hollow Reaches artifact is yours. Come alone or don't come. You have until the end of the month.*

No return address. No further instructions.

"This is bait," Roan said.

"I know."

"Someone knows about your debt, prepared a message using your family's seal, and is offering you a sum large enough to clear it in exchange for entering Tier Four territory with no support." He looked up. "That's not a job. It's a death trap with extra steps."

"Or it's a genuine opportunity that someone is trying to make look like a trap." Ines's voice held steady, but her hand had moved to the satchel strap again. "I need that money, Roan. The debt is worse than you think. My father signed a blood‑bound agreement before he died—if I default, everything House Carrow still owns gets executed. Not just my problem. My family's entire remaining property. The current holder is threatening to sell the documentation to House Greel. If that happens, they'll own me. Everything I know, every contact I have."

Roan studied her. The tight jaw. The controlled breathing. The way she'd positioned herself between him and the door. She wasn't afraid of him. She was afraid of what she might do if he said no.

"What's inside the sealed section?" he asked. "The artifact. Do you know what it is?"

"No. The client didn't specify. He said it's been sealed since the last Forged expedition, forty years ago, and that it requires Tempered‑level access or higher to open the lock."

A sealed Forged‑era chamber, untouched for decades. The artifacts inside could be expired, degraded, worthless—or something the Conclave had buried deliberately. The Founding Families had hidden things they didn't want found. Black Spire had its own sealed sections, not opened on student requests.

"You've already decided," he said. "You came here to ask me to come with you."

"Yes."

"Why me? You're Tempered. I'm Tempered. You have Carrow family resources. You have other contacts." He set the paper on the desk. "You came to me specifically."

Ines was quiet for three seconds. Four. Five.

"Because you have a reputation," she said. "People talk. They say you're cold in a fight. That you don't panic. That you can calculate your way out of things other people can't." She paused. "And because you owe me nothing. I can trust someone who owes me nothing to make a rational decision about their own survival."

Roan remembered that day. Cyrus had cornered him in the east corridor after the preliminary rankings. Ines had stood at the turn for half a second—long enough to see Cyrus step back. She'd seen it all.

"Two problems," he said. "First, I can't just walk into the Hollow Reaches on a rumor. I have my own obligations—training, rankings, the Hardened threshold. Second, even if I agreed, we'd die. Two Tempered in Tier Four. The balance doesn't support it."

He counted the remaining Inversion slots in his head. Three full conversions since the last cycle. If the sealed section held something that matched his father's standards, three wouldn't be enough for a full fight, but they might open a path. *Marginally.*

"The client specified that the sealed section has reduced threat parameters. Residual seal work from the original expedition. Whatever's in there, it was considered important enough to contain."

"Or dangerous enough to bury, and the seal is failing."

Ines's expression tightened.

Roan picked up the paper again. The seal. The precise handwriting. The specific knowledge of her debt. Someone had done research. Someone with access to House Carrow. Someone who knew her well enough to predict she'd take this bait.

"The debt," he said. "Who holds it?"

"I can't—"

"You came to me for help. Help requires information. Who holds the debt?"

Ines was silent. Then: "My father. Before he died, he borrowed from people he shouldn't have. The debt transferred to me when I enrolled here. It's been accumulating interest for three years." Her voice was flat. "The current holder is threatening to sell the documentation to House Greel. If that happens, they'll use it to pressure me. They'll own me."

House Greel. Of course.

Roan saw the trap now. Ines was being squeezed from two sides—the debt holder threatening to sell, and this mysterious client offering a solution that would almost certainly get her killed. Someone wanted her in the Hollow Reaches. Dead, or compromised.

"Why would House Greel want to own you?" he asked.

"I don't know. I haven't figured it out yet."

"You will. Give it time." He folded the paper and handed it back. "Your debt is a tool. The holder can leverage it because it's documented. The answer isn't fifty thousand credits from a suicide mission. The answer is removing the documentation."

"I can't afford to remove it."

"Then find leverage against the holder. Find what they don't want exposed. Every secret has a counter‑secret." He watched her face. "You're smart enough to know this."

Ines took the paper. She didn't put it away. She held it in both hands, staring at the Carrow seal.

"The job is in eleven days," she said. "The client expects a response by day five. If I don't show, he moves on."

"To another Carrow?"

"Or he sells the information about the Hollow Reaches location to someone else. Whoever's inside that sealed section, someone wants them found. Badly enough to spend real money on a setup."

Eleven days. Not enough time to train significantly. His Inversion capacity was the only edge he had, and even that had limits. He'd already used a lethal inversion on the Hollow Marrow Elixir. If they hit something that required that level of intervention, he'd be out of options.

"What do you need from me?" he asked.

Ines looked up. "I need you to think about it. Really think about it. The immediate survival math is not enough. The long‑term math matters too." She gripped the paper. "If Greel gets that documentation, they own me. They own anything I know. Any connection I have. That's worse than dying in the Hollow Reaches."

Roan saw the logic. A debt was a leash, and House Greel had long arms.

"I'll think about it," he said. "But I need more information. The client's communication method, the dead drop. Can you trace it?"

"I can try. The location was a rental locker in the Greycross outer district. The drop was placed three days ago. Anything useful is gone by now."

"Do it anyway. Check the surrounding lockers. See if there were other drops. See if anyone was watching." He moved back to his desk. "And find out who else knows about your debt. Start with immediate family, expand from there."

Ines nodded slowly. "You're helping me investigate."

"I'm gathering data. What I do with it depends on what the data says."

"That's not a yes."

"It's not a no either." He sat down. "Eleven days is a window. I'm not committing until I understand the shape of the trap."

Ines studied him for a moment. Then she tucked the paper into her coat and moved to the door. She paused with her hand on the frame.

"Thank you for listening," she said.

"I'm not your friend, Ines. I'm your data source."

"I know." She opened the door. "That's why I came to you."

She left. The door clicked shut.

Roan sat at the desk for a full minute, running numbers in his head. The variables were incomplete. The client unknown. The destination dangerous. The stakes high. Somewhere in the structure of the problem there was an answer, if he could find the right angle.

He pulled a fresh sheet of paper toward him and began a new column:

*Client identity, priority one.*  
*Debt holder, priority two.*  
*Greel connection, priority three.*  
*Inversion capacity backup plan, priority four.*

He capped the pen and set it down. The tea had been cold for an hour. He drank it in two swallows—didn't taste it.

---

Nine hours later, he had the first answer.

Brendt appeared at his door shortly after evening meal, carrying a scroll tube under one arm. His expression was complicated, part excitement, part wariness.

"I found something," Brendt said, pushing into the room without waiting for an invitation. "It's nothing. It's probably just coincidence. But I checked the archive index like you asked, and—"

"Spit it out."

Brendt unsealed the tube and produced a printed listing. "Three days ago, a researcher requested access to the Carrow family seal registry. Historical records only, no physical access to current seals. The request was approved and processed."

Roan took the listing. The requester's name was redacted, but the date and authorization code were visible. "Historical records of a family seal. Why would someone need that?"

"To forge a message. Or to verify the correct pressure and depth for an authentic seal." Brendt pulled out his own notes. "The archive doesn't track who uses approved access. But the timing matches. Three days ago, the drop was placed. And at the same time, someone was studying the Carrow seal specifically."

Roan looked at the authorization code. A standard researcher credential, mid‑tier, nothing that would trigger alerts. "Can you trace the credential?"

"I tried. It's registered to a temporary account. The researcher listed an address in the outer district, and when I checked the cross‑reference, that address is a storage rental facility."

The Greycross outer district. The same area as the dead drop.

"That's not a coincidence," Roan said.

"No." Brendt hesitated. "There's one more thing. The authorization code was registered under a name that doesn't appear in any student or faculty database."

"A false identity."

"Yes. But the handwriting on the registration form—the signature—it matches something in the historical seal registry."

Roan looked up. "Matches what?"

"Forty years ago, there was a Forged expedition to the Hollow Reaches. Tier Four. The expedition was sealed, and the records were classified. But someone in the archive left a handwritten note in the margin of the classification order." Brendt's voice was careful. "The note was from a researcher named Vance. Aldric Vance. He was documenting the seal methodology used on the expedition."

Roan's jaw tightened. His hand moved, absently, to the base of his skull—the seventh vertebra, where the Codex Shard sat beneath the skin. A faint warmth answered, like the echo of a resonance he hadn't consciously called. Aldric's handwriting. Forty years old. Connected to the same Hollow Reaches site that someone was now targeting. Probability of coincidence: negligible.

"This isn't about Ines," Roan said quietly. "It's about my father."

Brendt didn't respond immediately. He was watching Roan's face, reading the shift in his expression.

"I don't know what it means yet," Brendt said finally. "But whoever's behind this, they're using Ines's debt as the hook. They knew you'd get involved."

Roan set the listing down. His hands were steady. They always were in situations like this. The anger came later, in private, processed and filed.

"The client wanted me in the Hollow Reaches," he said. "Ines was the bait. Her debt was the pressure. But the real target—"

"Is the sealed section. And whatever your father put in it."

Roan looked at the authorization code on the listing. A temporary research credential. A dead drop. A message sealed with House Carrow wax. Forty years of preparation, and someone was finally moving.

"Eleven days," he said.

Brendt's eyes narrowed. "Eleven days until what?"

Roan didn't answer. He was already calculating the new variables, running the revised math. The trap had a shape now. It was bigger than he'd thought. And it had been set for him specifically—not Ines, not the debt, not any of the surface details.

The target was Aldric's secret.

The trap was the Hollow Reaches.

And he was going to walk into it anyway. Because Aldric had sealed something in that chamber. Something worth killing for, worth dying for. And if Roan didn't find it first, whoever had set this trap would have it forever.

He picked up his pen.

"Get me everything on the Vance expedition," he said. "Forty years ago. Every name, every detail, every piece of equipment they carried. I don't care if it's classified. Find it."

Brendt nodded once and headed for the door.

Roan started a new column on his paper.

*The real client, priority zero.*

He had eleven days to find the answer.
