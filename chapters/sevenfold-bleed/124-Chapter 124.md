Chapter 124: The Visitor Pass

The morning air carried the metallic tang of approaching autumn, and with it came the summons that changed everything.

Roan found the notice pinned to the training hall board at five-thirty, when the corridor lights were still flickering through their morning warm-up cycle. The parchment was official Spire stock, cream-colored, edged in black, and the seal pressed into the bottom left corner was unmistakable.

House Ashford.

He read it twice. Then a third time, running the calculations.

*Regional Qualifier Invitational. Hosted by House Ashford at the Ironhaven Competition Grounds. Spire students of Hardened tier and above are eligible to compete for ranking points toward the Winter Championship.*

Ranking points. The currency of advancement at Black Spire, more useful than credits in some contexts because they couldn't be traded, only earned. And House Ashford hosting meant Cyrus Ashford would be there. Would be competing.

*Presumably not hiding anymore.*

Brendt appeared at his shoulder, still wearing the rumpled shirt from yesterday's dungeon crawl. His hair was doing something architectural that suggested he'd slept on it wrong and then made it worse by trying to fix it.

"You're looking at that notice like it's a contract for your firstborn," Brendt said.

"It might as well be." Roan folded the parchment carefully, making sure the Ashford seal remained visible. "When's the qualifier?"

"Three weeks."

Brendt's voice dropped as he leaned in, sliding a folded sheet from his pocket. "I pulled the incident logs from last year's qualifier while you were staring at the seal. You know what 'Visitor Pass' means around Ashford events?"

Roan's fingers stilled. He hadn't heard that term before.

"Civilian family members of competing students get informal visits after the tournament," Brendt continued, keeping his tone low. "Not from Spire marshals. Unmarked enforcers. They call it a Visitor Pass—one warning, delivered to your family's door before the real trouble starts. Three documented cases last year. Two dropped out before the final round. One ended up in the infirmary for a month." He met Roan's eyes. "You sure you want to play in a game where the audience gets targeted before the players even step on the field?"

Roan absorbed the information, adjusting the calculation. A new variable. A risk he hadn't fully weighted. "Then we make sure we're not the ones who need the warning." He tapped the folded parchment. "The deadline for registration is in nine days."

"Also noticed." Brendt studied his face with the careful attention of someone who'd learned to read his silences. "You're thinking about asking me."

"I'm thinking about the arithmetic." Roan turned away from the board, already walking. "Come on. We need to talk somewhere that isn't a hallway."

The private study room Brendt had reserved for the semester cost him forty credits a month, money Roan had loaned him during a tight month and that Brendt insisted on repaying with interest in the form of access privileges. The room was small and soundproofed, equipped with a training array that could simulate mid-tier combat conditions.

Roan activated the privacy ward as they entered. The faint hum of the barrier settling into place was reassuring.

"Talk," Brendt said, dropping into the chair.

"Three weeks until the qualifier. Nine days to register. If I enter solo, I'm capped at whatever ranking the Spire assigns me through the standard channels, which won't be high, because I'm supposed to be at Initiate baseline." Roan's fingers drummed against the table's edge. "If I enter as a pair with someone strong, I can angle for a better bracket position. And if that pair happens to include a Thorne Valley scholarship student with a well-documented reputation for taking every competition seriously, the odds of someone questioning my sudden Hardened-tier competitiveness drop significantly."

Brendt's eyebrows rose. "You want me as your cover story."

"I want us as our cover story. You're strong enough that people expect you to compete. I'm strong enough to not embarrass you. Together, we're a pair of mid-tier students chasing ranking points." He leaned back. "Separately, I'm a nobody who shouldn't be here and you're a scholarship kid who needs every credit he can get."

"And together?"

"Together, we're just two kids trying to climb the ladder. Nothing suspicious about that." Roan's mouth curved, not a smile. "And if something goes sideways during the competition, we have a ready-made reason to explain why a Hardened-tier student is fighting like he's had actual combat experience."

Brendt was quiet for a moment, his fingers tracing the grain of the table. "You're not telling me everything."

"I'm not telling you everything because I don't know everything yet." That was true enough. The plan had formed in the thirty seconds since he'd seen the notice, but the specifics required more data. "What I know is this: the Ashfords are hosting an event that grants ranking points. I need ranking points. You need credits. And the last time we worked together in a competitive environment, we won."

The Rift Hunt. Brendt's expression flickered, the memory of danger, of blood, of things that had bonded them in ways that academic collaboration never could.

"You're not going to tell me what you're planning."

"I'm going to tell you what I can. Which right now is: I want to compete. I want to win. And I want to do it without exposing exactly how much I've advanced since enrollment." Roan met his eyes. "Can you work with that?"

"Depends." Brendt's tone sharpened. "Does 'without exposing' mean I'm going to be lying to people? Because I can handle a cover story, but I can't handle actively misrepresenting my capabilities to Spire officials."

"No. You'll be exactly what you are, a Hardened-tier student with a solid record. I'm the one who's misrepresenting. By a lot." He spread his hands. "Your story is true. Mine is a lie. If anyone asks questions, you tell them what you know, which is that I'm stronger than I should be. Let them draw their own conclusions."

"They'll draw the conclusion that you're cheating."

"Some will. The smart ones will draw the conclusion that there's something unusual about me and file it away for future reference." Roan's voice was flat, matter-of-fact. "I can live with that."

Brendt studied him. Then he exhaled, the tension draining from his shoulders. "Fine. But I'm not lying to officials. If they ask me directly whether I think you're hiding your real tier, I'll tell them I have no idea what you're hiding."

"Fair."

"And if something goes wrong, if this gets us expelled or worse, you're explaining everything to Headmaster Cole."

"That won't be necessary."

"Roan."

"It won't be necessary because nothing will go wrong." He stood, moving toward the door. "I'll draft the registration documents tonight. We submit tomorrow, before the deadline. That gives us three weeks to prepare without anyone getting suspicious about last-minute entries."

"Wait." Brendt caught his arm. "You haven't told me about the actual competition format. What are we fighting? People? Rifts? Teams?"

Roan paused. The information had been in the notice, but he'd been so focused on the implications that he'd skipped the details. He pulled the parchment from his pocket and smoothed it out on the nearest flat surface.

"Regional qualifier. Three-stage format. First stage is a timed gauntlet, individuals through a preset obstacle course with embedded combat challenges. Second stage is the pairs competition, random bracket, single elimination. Third stage..." He stopped.

Third stage was elimination rounds between the top eight pairs from stage two. Best two-out-of-three format. Lethal force technically permitted but practically discouraged by heavy liability clauses.

"It's not a friendly sparring match," Brendt said quietly.

"No. It's a proving ground." Roan's jaw worked. "House Ashford doesn't host events to make people feel good about themselves. They host them to identify threats and eliminate them through legally sanctioned channels."

"And you want to walk into that."

"I need to walk into that." The distinction mattered. "Cyrus Ashford has been playing games around me for months. Summoning me to his estate, watching me in training, dropping hints about my father. He's waiting for something, and I don't know what." Roan met Brendt's eyes. "If I wait for him to make his move, I'm reactive. If I enter this competition and place high enough to matter, I'm demonstrating capability. It's harder for someone to dismiss you as a target when you've proven yourself in official competition."

"That assumes you survive the competition."

"It does." Roan's voice didn't waver. "That's why I need a partner I trust."

Brendt was silent. The privacy ward hummed softly in the background, a steady pulse that filled the spaces between words.

"You're asking me to risk my scholarship, my future, on a plan you won't fully explain."

"I'm asking you to compete in a tournament with me. That's all." Roan's tone was precise. "The risk to your scholarship exists whether you partner with me or not. You take the same risk every time you accept a Rift assignment. The difference is that this time, you know the stakes going in."

"The stakes are different."

"The stakes are always different. That's not an argument against this. It's a statement of fact." He began folding the parchment again. "I won't force you. If you say no, I'll find another partner. Someone who asks fewer questions."

"Someone like Theon?"

The name landed like a stone in still water. Roan didn't react, had trained himself not to react to deliberate provocations, but the calculation that followed was instantaneous. Brendt was probing. Testing. Trying to determine how much weight Theon's betrayal still carried.

"Theon is useful for certain things. Not this." His voice was level. "He's too visible. Too many people watch him. And he doesn't have the same pressures you do, he's not dependent on scholarship money or ranking points."

"But he also doesn't have the same loyalty you think I have."

"Loyalty is the wrong word. I'm not asking for loyalty. I'm asking for mutual benefit." Roan's eyes were steady. "You need ranking points. I need a partner who can compete at a level that won't raise flags. You help me, I help you. That's the arrangement."

"Until it isn't."

"If it stops being beneficial for either of us, we renegotiate. That's how partnerships work." He moved toward the door again. "Think about it. Let me know by tomorrow morning. I need to prepare the registration documents regardless, and if you're not available, I'll adjust."

He left before Brendt could press further. There was no point in staying, the conversation had reached the point where further words would only muddy the waters.

The corridor outside was quiet, the morning light filtering through the high windows in dusty columns. Roan walked without hurrying, cataloguing everything he passed. The training schedule board showed a group session at noon that he could attend without arousing suspicion. The cafeteria entrance had a line forming already, students grabbing quick meals before morning classes. The administrative wing was sealed for another hour, which meant he couldn't file the registration documents until after lunch.

Three weeks. Nine days to register. And somewhere in that timeline, Cyrus Ashford would be preparing his own strategy.

*Let him prepare. Let him think he's the one controlling the board.*

The thought settled in Roan's mind like a seed finding purchase. He had learned enough about Ashford politics to know that the family operated on a framework of control and leverage. They didn't just compete, they engineered outcomes. If Cyrus was hosting this qualifier, it was because the Ashfords wanted something from it: identification of threats, elimination of competitors, or the exercise of power over a major event.

Any of those possibilities worked to Roan's advantage, as long as he kept his own objectives clear.

*Rank high enough to matter. Not high enough to attract the wrong attention.*

The balance was delicate. But Roan had spent years learning to navigate delicate balances.

He reached his room and sat at the desk, pulling out a fresh sheet of paper and beginning to draft the registration documents. The Spire's official format required detailed information: names, tiers, team designation, training history summary. Most of it could be fudged through the bureaucratic gaps, training history especially, since the Spire's own records were a mess of conflicting data entries and outdated assessments.

His current public record showed Initiate tier with a Vitalis reading of 180, far below the Hardened threshold. To compete legitimately, he would need that record updated, which meant someone with authority would have to re-assess him. Which meant his actual tier would become visible to whoever performed the assessment.

*The assessment isn't done by people. It's done by the arena itself.*

The qualifier's combat zones had automatic tier-verification systems, part of the safety protocols for allowing lethal-force scenarios. Anyone who entered would have their tier confirmed by the system, regardless of what their student file claimed.

Which meant the lie would last exactly until the moment he stepped into the first combat zone.

*Not a lie. A deliberate underestimation.*

The distinction mattered legally. Incompetent students were weeded out quickly. Deliberately deceptive ones were expelled and prosecuted. But students who were misassessed? Who had improved faster than the system tracked? They were a gray area, problematic, but not automatically criminal.

The risk was that misassessment followed by unexpected performance would trigger an investigation. And investigations involved interviews, record reviews, even direct examination by Spire officials.

*Which is why I need Brendt. His genuine reputation provides cover for my performance. "I didn't know he was that strong either" is a more believable defense than "I didn't know I was that strong."*

The registration form asked for a team name. Roan considered options, something generic enough to be forgettable, distinctive enough to be memorable if they placed well.

*Hollow Vein.*

Brendt's family name and his own chosen element, combined. It said nothing and everything, depending on how you interpreted it.

He wrote it at the top of the form, then began filling in the rest of the fields with careful precision. Brendt's information was straightforward, his actual name, his verified tier, his scholarship status. Roan's was more carefully constructed: name, birth date, enrollment year. The training history section was deliberately vague, "independent study under informal mentorship, combat application through authorized Rift assignments."

It wouldn't stand up to serious scrutiny. But it didn't need to. It just needed to pass the initial administrative review, which would be conducted by an overloaded clerk working through hundreds of forms before the deadline.

By the time he finished, the morning had shifted into late morning. His stomach reminded him that he'd skipped breakfast. He gathered the documents and headed for the cafeteria, calculating the line length and the time until his next commitment.

The food was adequate, protein-heavy, nutritionally balanced, absolutely devoid of taste. He ate mechanically, running through scenarios for the qualifier's first stage. The gauntlet was designed to test endurance and adaptability, pushing competitors through a timed course where speed and accuracy both counted. Combat challenges were embedded throughout, meaning he would be fighting in conditions that prevented thorough preparation.

*The gauntlet doesn't allow for long-term strategy. It's pure reaction. Which means whoever I'm paired with in stage one needs to be able to keep up.*

Brendt could keep up. His combat instincts were solid, his training recent, his willingness to push himself well-documented. But there was a risk in partnering with him, one that Roan hadn't fully articulated, even to himself.

Brendt had watched him fight in the Rift assignment. Had seen what he could do when pushed. If they competed together and Roan performed at the level he was capable of, Brendt would know for certain that something unusual was happening. The suspicion he already carried would become certainty.

*And certainty means questions. And questions mean eventually someone tells Brendt enough truth to matter.*

The Codex Shard pulsed faintly against his spine, a sensation he'd learned to interpret as a warning about mental strain. Not dangerous, not yet. But present.

[A.V.: Your friend is more observant than you give him credit for.]

*I know.*

[The conversation you're avoiding will happen eventually. The only question is whether you control the timing.]

*When I control the timing, I control the terms.*

[Spoken like your father.]

Roan's jaw tightened. The Aldric Echo had been more active lately, offering commentary, pushing observations, occasionally making what might've been jokes if dead men could appreciate humor. The increase in activity coincided with Roan's own advancement, as though the Codex Shard was responding to the pressures he was under.

Maybe it was. The device was designed to adapt to its user, after all.

He finished his meal and headed for the training hall, documents secured in his bag. The group session at noon would provide cover for his presence, showing up was expected, and his participation would establish an alibi for the weeks ahead. If anyone asked, he was preparing for the qualifier like everyone else. Nothing unusual.

The training hall was busier than he'd expected. Word about the qualifier had spread quickly, and students were clustering in groups that reflected their competitive ambitions. He recognized some faces from his courses, mid-tier students who rarely made waves, now suddenly animated with the possibility of ranking points. The air crackled with tactical chatter; a pair of Hardened-tier seniors were arguing over gauntlet strategies near the weapon racks, their voices carrying across the floor.

Cyrus Ashford wasn't present. That was notable, the Ashford scion rarely missed opportunities to be seen in spaces where his status could be reinforced. His absence could mean he was preparing elsewhere, or it could mean he had other commitments.

Roan found a corner and began the warm-up routine, letting his body settle into the patterns while his mind mapped the competition terrain. Nine days until registration closed. Three weeks until the first stage. Somewhere in that span, he needed to prepare without revealing how much he needed to prepare for.

The equation remained honest. But sometimes the math was incomplete.

He had just begun a second set of stretches when he caught movement near the hall's main entrance. Cyrus Ashford walked through the doors, flanked by two students Roan recognized as House Ashford retainers. He wasn't in training gear—he was wearing a formal coat, as if he'd just come from a meeting. And beside him, a woman in a grey Sentinel Bureau uniform, her sharp eyes scanning the room.

They exchanged a few words near the entrance. Cyrus nodded once. The Sentinel officer left, her heels clicking against the stone floor.

Then Cyrus turned, and for a moment, his gaze found Roan across the crowded hall. No smile, no greeting. Just a brief, deliberate recognition—the acknowledgment of a piece already in motion.

Roan held the stare for exactly one second before he looked away, returning to his stretch.

*He knows I'm here. He knows I've seen the notice. And he's already positioning his pieces.*

The pressure of the qualifier shifted from abstract calculation to immediate weight. Cyrus wasn't just hosting the event. He was watching the board.

Roan's fingers tightened against his palm. Then he forced the tension loose.

*Let him watch. I'm still the piece he hasn't learned to read.*
