Chapter 130: A Bad Offer

The corridor outside the examination chamber smelled of iron and the dust of old preservation wards, the residue of stone that had held violence for decades. Roan leaned against the wall, arms crossed, watching the door like it owed him money.

Inside, Selene was answering the Sentinel Bureau's questions. Theon had already left. Theon didn't have secrets worth keeping. Selene did.

Roan ran the arithmetic for the third time.

Two weeks ago, Selene had walked into the Veyrn Sanctum. She had seen the inscription above the altar, the Inversion script, the words his father had carved into stone seventy years ago. She had seen it, and she hadn't reported it. She had seen the blood she had left at the altar, and she had asked Roan why the House Ardent sigil appeared there.

She hadn't asked because she didn't know what it meant.

She had asked because she wanted to hear him say it.

The door opened. Selene stepped out, composed as ever, her dark braid still intact, her expression neutral. She looked at Roan for half a second longer than necessary, then kept walking.

"Selene."

She stopped but didn't turn.

"Whatever you told them—" He paused. Measured. "—you didn't have to."

Now she turned. Her eyes were the same pale grey as weathered granite, unreadable. "I told them what a Tier 2 Rift incursion looks like when the Sentinel Bureau does its job properly."

"They'll want to follow up."

"They will." She tilted her head slightly. "The Black Spire has been cooperative. Headmaster Cole has already made that easier."

"Selene."

"What, Roan?"

"The inscription in the Sanctum. The altar." He held her gaze. "You knew what it meant when you saw it."

Her composure held, but a line tightened at the corner of her mouth, a seam where calculation stopped and something deeper began. "I knew what it looked like. The characters are a dialect from the old northern records. Pre-Founding records." She let that sit. "I also know what happens to people who possess materials from that era."

"It's not illegal."

"No. But the Founding Seven disagree about what it *is*." She took a step closer, lowering her voice. "House Greel has been asking the Sentinel Bureau for months about artifacts recovered from the Hollow Reaches. Specifically, artifacts that predate the Rift Wars."

"Greel's interested in pre-Rift materials."

"Greel is interested in anything that challenges the current order." She glanced down the corridor, empty, but her awareness of that fact told Roan she was being careful. "My family has been careful for four generations, Roan. Careful means not knowing things."

"And yet you went into the Sanctum."

"I went into the Sanctum because you were bleeding on the floor and Drogan was screaming for a medic." Her voice didn't rise. "I didn't go in *looking* for answers. But I found some anyway."

Roan weighed the angles in her silence. The math wasn't clean here. Selene was too smart to risk exposure without a reason. She had covered for him in that room, and now she was drawing a line, acknowledging the secret without stepping into it. That was a particular kind of intelligence. It meant she wanted an exit, or she wanted leverage, or she wanted both.

"You want something," Roan said.

"I want you to be more careful." She pulled something from inside her jacket, a folded piece of paper, heavy stock, the kind used for official correspondence. She set it on the windowsill between them. "The Bureau is assigning a junior investigator to the Rift task force. Someone young. Someone the Black Spire can manage."

"That's a gift?"

"That's a warning dressed as a gift." She retrieved her hand. "He's coming from Greycross."

The name landed like a stone in still water. Greycross. His hometown. Mira and Lyra were in Greycross.

"The Bureau has protocols for assigning investigators near their home regions," Selene continued. "Exceptions can be made. But not if the investigator has personal connections worth investigating."

"You're telling me to stay away from home."

"I'm telling you that someone is going to look at your family before he looks at the Rift." She turned to leave, then paused. "Your mother called the Ardent estate last month. She asked about you. My mother thought it was a courtesy check."

A cold seam closed behind Roan's ribs. "She shouldn't have done that."

"No. She shouldn't have." Selene's tone carried no judgment, only data. "The Ardent family routed that call through three intermediaries, but Sentinel Bureau monitoring catches the pattern. Someone in the Bureau noticed that a Vance woman is making unusual contact with an old Conclave family."

"Unusual how?"

"Seven families were exterminated in the Rift Wars. House Vance was one of them. Officially, the bloodline ended with your father." She held his gaze. "Officially, you don't exist in any Conclave record."

"Roan Vance exists in the Black Spire enrollment."

"Roan Vance exists as an anomaly. A student with no verifiable family history, no Conclave registration, and an unusually rapid progression through tier." She shook her head slowly. "It's a good cover, Roan. It's just not a complete one."

He said nothing. The tally was suddenly very simple, and it was terrible.

Selene left.

Roan stood in the corridor for a long moment. The paper on the windowsill sat undisturbed, official Bureau letterhead, a name printed in clean type: *Sentinel-Apprentice Caden Marsh, Rift Task Force, Greycross Division.*

Sentinel-Apprentice. Not a full Sentinel. Young enough to be managed, she had said. Or young enough to be pushed into something he didn't understand.

Roan picked up the paper, folded it, and put it in his pocket.

The library at Black Spire was a working space, not a showpiece. Three levels of worn wooden shelves, the smell of old paper and low-grade Vitalis preservation wards, study alcoves carved into the walls like niches in a tomb. Roan had claimed one in the northeast corner weeks ago, three walls, a single entrance, sight lines to every staircase.

He sat with the paper unfolded in front of him.

Caden Marsh. Greycross native. Tier level unconfirmed but likely Tempered or early Hardened—junior investigators didn't get assigned to Rift task forces at Initiate. Age mid-twenties. Bureau career track: fast but not spectacular.

Roan pulled three other files from his satchel, the ones he'd copied from the administration office two months ago, when he'd been mapping the security architecture of Black Spire. Marsh's file was thin. Official record: enrolled in the Sentinel Bureau Academy at nineteen, graduated at twenty-two, assigned to Greycross patrol for three years, promoted to Sentinel-Apprentice eight months ago.

Personal additions: transferred to Ironholm six months ago. Lived in Sentinel housing near the eastern district. Trained in single-blade combat form.

The gap was interesting. Eight months as a Sentinel-Apprentice, then quietly transferred to the Ironholm division and assigned to a Rift task force. That wasn't promotion. That was relocation.

Someone had pulled him out of Greycross.

Roan looked at the name at the bottom of the assignment notice. *Reviewed and approved by: Sentinel Warden Halric.*

Halric. He knew that name. Drogan had mentioned it once, a Sentinel Warden who had been in Ironholm for twenty years, solid reputation, careful, politically neutral. A man who signed papers and kept his head down.

But Halric had been the one to approve Marsh's transfer. And Halric had approved Selene's investigation room access. And Halric was the one who would have reviewed the footage from the Sanctum before the Bureau did.

Roan closed the files.

The figures said: Halric was moving pieces. Caden Marsh was a piece. Selene was a piece. The investigation was a piece. They all fit together in some pattern he couldn't see yet because he didn't have enough data.

That meant more data. That meant waiting. That meant not going to Greycross, staying visible at Black Spire, doing nothing unusual, letting them watch him and think he didn't know he was being watched.

He hated that it meant not warning Lyra.

Not directly. Lyra was twelve. Mira was a civilian. If the Bureau was monitoring contact between the Vance house and old Conclave families, any unusual communication would be flagged. A phone call home saying *be careful* would be a pattern.

Roan drummed his fingers on the table. Four beats. Stop.

What he could do: pull Caden Marsh's personnel file from Greycross city records. The public kind, not Bureau confidential. Name, address, next of kin, civil registration. If Marsh had family in Greycross, that family would be in the same district as Mira and Lyra. Proximity was a risk and an opportunity.

What else: observe. Marsh would be arriving in Ironholm within the week. The Bureau would want to debrief him, introduce him to the local infrastructure, show him the Rift sites. He would appear in places where Sentinel presence was visible: the Spire's administrative buildings, the eastern district patrol routes, the market quarter near the Hollow Reaches access points.

Roan could manufacture a reason to be in any of those places.

What else: Selene's behavior. She had covered for him in the Sanctum. She had delivered this warning. She hadn't asked for anything in return, which meant she would ask for something later, and it would be something she wanted badly enough to risk four generations of careful neutrality.

He owed her a debt. Debts compounded.

Right. Fine.

Roan folded the paper again and slid it into the Codex Shard's housing strap beneath his shirt. The Shard sat against his spine, warm, quiet. It had been quiet since the Hardened breakthrough. Aldric's echo was still there, still occasionally surfacing in system prompts, but something had shifted. The resonance was different. Like a radio tuning to a new frequency.

*Your mother's call was flagged eight weeks ago. I'm not supposed to tell you that. A.V.*

Roan's hand went still on the table.

*I'm dead, Roan. I don't have to follow the rules. A.V.*

"You're supposed to follow the rules that keep me alive."

*I am following that rule. You needed to know. A.V.*

He waited. The Shard offered nothing more. Aldric's echo was selective about timing: the revelation would land, then silence, as though the man had said his piece and retreated behind a closed door.

Eight weeks. The call had been flagged eight weeks ago. Selene's information was two weeks old. That meant the Bureau had known about Mira's contact with the Ardent family for six weeks before anyone from the investigation team was assigned.

They had been watching. Building a case. Marsh wasn't a junior investigator randomly assigned. Marsh was a scalpel, not a hammer. Someone had chosen him specifically because he was from Greycross.

Roan's fingers curled against the table. Lyra was twelve. Mira was a civilian. And someone in the Sentinel Bureau had decided to use them as a pressure point.

He stood. Left the alcove. Walked through the stacks without looking at anyone.

The courtyard outside was grey with early evening light. Students moved in clusters, heading to the dining hall, the dormitories, the training yards. Normal patterns. Everything normal.

Roan crossed to the equipment locker near the eastern wall. Eckhart had left a message there two days ago. Sidewound needed maintenance, the edge geometry could be optimized for the Crimson Wake form. He'd been too busy to retrieve it. The locker was cold iron, standard Bureau manufacture, keyed to his Vitalis signature.

He opened it. The blade sat in its magnetic cradle, dark steel without inscription. Eckhart had done good work: the bevel angles were tighter, the balance shifted two millimeters forward. Better for cutting. Better for a strike that consumed blood to gain power.

He closed the locker without taking the blade.

Caden Marsh would be in Ironholm within days. The Bureau would brief him. Marsh would ask about the Rift sites. He would ask about the Sanctum. He would ask about the student who had triggered the alarm at two in the morning and bled on the floor of a restricted archive.

And somewhere in those questions, Marsh would mention Greycross. He would mention the Vance house. He would mention a woman named Mira and a girl named Lyra who lived three doors from his own family on Cedar Street.

Roan locked the locker. Walked back across the courtyard.

The dinner bell rang. Students shifted toward the dining hall. Brendt was already inside, holding a seat, eating whatever the kitchen staff had managed to make edible this time. Normal life. Normal pressure.

Roan walked toward the dining hall.

He sat across from Brendt. He ate. He said nothing about Marsh, about Selene, about the paper in his pocket. Brendt talked about the morning's combat drills, about a new sparring partner from Suthmere who fought like someone had taught him to fight and then told him to forget everything he'd been taught.

Roan made the right noises. Laughed once. Left half his meal.

Back in his room, he pulled the paper from his pocket and smoothed it flat on the desk.

Caden Marsh. Greycross. Cedar Street.

Roan stared at the name for a long time.

Then he picked up a pen and began to write, not a letter, nothing that could be intercepted or read. A list. Locations in Ironholm where a Sentinel-Apprentice from Greycross would be visible. Times. Routes. The mathematics of intersection.

Caden Marsh would be in Ironholm within the week.

Roan would be ready.

The knock came at his door at half past eleven. Three sharp raps, no rhythm, no courtesy knock. Someone who wanted to be heard.

Roan opened the door to find Theon Mercer standing in the hallway, his dark hair unwashed, his eyes too bright. He looked like he hadn't slept in two days. He smelled like the alchemy laboratory near the Black Spire's east wing: sulfur and something acidic.

"You've been in the lab," Roan said.

Theon pushed past him into the room. "I need you to not ask questions for thirty seconds."

He set something on Roan's desk. A glass vial, small, maybe three inches tall, filled with liquid that shifted between pale gold and deep red depending on the angle of the light. The liquid moved on its own, not settling, but crawling up the sides of the glass, searching for a way out.

"Pyre-Heart Tincture," Theon said. "Combat grade. I've been working on a synthesis variant for nine weeks. Bureau classified. Extremely classified."

Roan looked at the vial, then at Theon. "You've been synthesizing classified Bureau alchemy in the Black Spire lab."

"Yes."

"That's stupid."

"I know." Theon's voice cracked on the word. "But I'm out of time. Cyrus Ashford has been asking questions about my work. Started three weeks ago. Small questions. Who I buy reagents from. How long I spend in the lab. Whether I've synthesized anything that might have combat applications."

Roan's expression didn't change. "He's building a case."

"He's building a case against *you*." Theon grabbed Roan's arm, his fingers tight. "He doesn't care about me. I'm not the target. I synthesize things you might use. If he can prove I'm synthesizing restricted alchemical materials for a fellow student—"

"He uses it to implicate me."

"He uses it to get you expelled. Or detained. Or both." Theon released Roan's arm. "The Pyre-Heart Tincture—I've already inverted one batch. The side effects are gone. What remains is pure Vitalis amplification without cost. It's the most useful thing I've ever made. I'm giving it to you because if the Bureau raids my room and finds it, I need you to have a copy."

"You need me to have the formula."

"No." Theon shook his head violently. "No, I'm giving you the *tincture*, not the formula. If you have the tincture, you can reverse-engineer it, but that's *your* problem. If they search my room, they find a vial, not a formula. A vial I claim I was synthesizing for Bureau review."

"That argument holds for about four seconds."

"I know." Theon's laugh was brittle, sharp-edged. "I know. But four seconds is better than zero, and right now I have zero options."

Roan picked up the vial. The liquid crawled against his fingers through the glass, warm, eager, almost alive. The Codex Shard at his spine registered the proximity and began its quiet hum.

*Pyre-Heart Tincture. Cardiovascular stimulant. Side effects include: acute tachycardia, capillary rupture, temporary blindness, cardiac hypertrophy. Conversion rate: 150% at Hardened tier. A.V.*

Roan set the vial down. Looked at Theon. "What does Cyrus know?"

"He knows I've been synthesizing restricted reagents. He knows I've been spending time in the Sanctum. He knows—" Theon stopped. Swallowed. "He knows I was there the night you were injured. He didn't see you, but he saw me leaving the Sanctum corridor at two in the morning."

"When was that?"

"Three weeks ago."

Roan closed his eyes for one second. When he opened them, the ledger was already done. Three weeks ago. Right after the Sanctum incident. Selene had covered for him in the examination room. But Selene hadn't been the only one in the corridor. Theon had been there too, and Selene hadn't known, or had chosen not to mention it.

"You didn't say anything," Roan said.

"You were bleeding out. I wasn't thinking about who saw me."

"You should've been."

"Probably." Theon ran a hand through his hair, leaving it standing at odd angles. "I'm a decent alchemist, Roan. I'm a terrible spy."

Roan held his gaze. Theon was terrified, not of Cyrus, not of the Bureau, but of the way the walls were closing in. Nine weeks of synthesis work. Three weeks of surveillance. One night in a corridor that shouldn't have mattered and did.

The vial sat heavy in his hand. The liquid inside kept crawling.

"Keep the tincture," Roan said.

"What?"

"Keep it. If Cyrus pushes, tell him you synthesized it for Bureau review, that you were planning to submit it through official channels. That's not a lie—you were. The fact that you haven't submitted it yet is a bureaucratic delay, not a conspiracy."

"He won't believe that."

"He doesn't have to believe it. He has to have *reasonable doubt* before he goes to the Bureau with an accusation. Theon Mercer, promising young alchemist, synthesizes a restricted combat tincture and intends to submit through official channels—that's a story that holds. It's boring. Boring stories don't get investigated."

"You're saying I tell him the truth."

"I'm saying you tell him a *version* of the truth that doesn't lead back to me." Roan set the vial down on the desk, gently, precisely in the center. "Cyrus wants to destroy me. That's his goal. Everything else is secondary. He's been building this case for three weeks and still has nothing concrete. That makes him frustrated. Frustrated people take the first path that looks like a win." His voice was flat, factual, the way he described mathematical certainties. "Give him the boring story. Let him feel like he's winning."

Theon stared at him for a long time. Then something shifted in his expression, the brittle edge softened, replaced by something that looked almost like relief. "You really have thought about this."

"I'm thinking about it right now." Roan stood. "Leave the vial. Go back to your room. Act normal tomorrow. Cyrus will push. Give him the story. He'll take it."

"And the tincture?"

Roan picked up the vial. The liquid inside was still crawling, warm, eager. He could feel the Codex Shard registering its presence, logging the side effect profile, cataloguing it for future inversion. A pure Vitalis amplifier with no cost. Theon had done something remarkable, and he was terrified, and he had come to Roan first instead of running.

Roan weighed the vial in his palm. Two hundred milliliters. Maybe less. Enough for one significant dose.

He put the vial in his desk drawer and locked it. "I'll hold onto it. Insurance."

"For what?"

"For the day Cyrus stops wanting to feel like he's winning and starts wanting to win." Roan opened the door. "Go to bed, Theon."

Theon left. Roan closed the door. Locked it.

The room was quiet. The vial sat in the locked drawer, warm through the wood. The paper with Caden Marsh's name sat on the desk, folded, undisturbed. Two problems. One night.

He sat at the desk. Unfolded the paper. Spread it flat.

Marsh. Greycross. Cedar Street. Three doors from home.

Roan picked up the pen and continued his list.

Outside, the Black Spire settled into its nighttime rhythm: the distant clang of the training yards, the hum of the preservation wards, the wind moving through the old stone. Somewhere in Greycross, Mira and Lyra were asleep. Somewhere in Ironholm, Caden Marsh was packing for a move that would put him in the same city as a family he didn't know he was being sent to surveil.

Somewhere in the Sanctum, beneath the stone and the old blood, the inscription waited, words his father had carved in a dead language seventy years ago, words that explained how pain could be turned into power and power into debt, and debt into leverage that made people like House Greel very, very nervous.

Roan capped the pen. Looked at the list.

Three days. Maybe four. That was the window before Marsh arrived in Ironholm.

He stood. Walked to the window. The city spread out below, dark and vast, the lights of the eastern district glittering like scattered coins. Somewhere out there, Selene was being careful. Theon was lying awake. Cyrus was plotting. Halric was moving pieces on a board Roan could see only half of.

And somewhere in Greycross, his mother was wondering why her son had stopped calling.

Roan pressed his palm flat against the cold glass.

*Numbers were harder to bribe than people. But the ledger doesn't tell you everything, either.*

He stayed at the window for a long time.
