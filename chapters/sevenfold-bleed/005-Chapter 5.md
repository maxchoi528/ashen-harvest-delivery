Chapter 5: The Registration

The registration center in Greycross was a long grey building with a faded sign that read *Ironholm Auxiliary Intake*. It looked like a place that processed things, not people. A queue of candidates snaked across the sidewalk, a collection of nervous eighteen-year-olds from every tier of the district. Rich kids from the hill with new gear. Poor kids from the lower quarter in patched jackets. Roan was somewhere in the middle, invisible enough to pass.

He felt the Codex Shard warm at the base of his skull, a steady pulse he was still learning to ignore. The pendant was gone, dissolved into the architecture of his own spine on the night he drank the Bloodscourge. He didn't reach for it anymore. There was nothing to reach for. Just a new center of gravity that hummed quietly beneath his thoughts.

The line moved slowly.

Roan watched the other candidates. Some whispered strategies. Some stared at their feet. One boy had vomited into a gutter and was now being comforted by a girl in an academy jacket. Normal people. Normal fear.

He didn't feel normal anymore. The Inversion had done something to his perception. He could see the tension in their shoulders, the calculation in their eyes. He could feel the Vitalis ripples beneath their skin. Weak. Moderate. Here and there a flicker of something more.

None of them shone the way the Codex Shard did.

*Control it*, he told himself. *You are a poor kid from the lower quarter. You got lucky on a reading once. That's all.*

The door opened. A proctor in a grey uniform gestured him inside.

---

The intake hall smelled of antiseptic and old paper. Fluorescent lights hummed overhead. A medical assistant took his name, his district registration, his family history. Questions Roan answered with the economy of someone who had rehearsed them for weeks.

*Father's occupation?*  
*Deceased. Former Sentinel.*

*Cause of death?*  
*Rift casualty.*

*Any prior medical conditions?*  
*No.*

The assistant handed him a form and pointed to a room at the end of the hall. "Vitalis assessment. Place your hand on the inscription plate and hold still."

Roan entered the room.

It was empty except for a stone pedestal with a circular diagram carved into its surface. The same markings that had been on the pendant. The same patterns that now lived inside him.

He placed his hand on the cool stone.

The response was immediate. A flare of light. The runes on the pedestal began to glow, counting upwards in a sequence of soft chimes. Roan watched the numbers climb past five hundred, past eight hundred, past one thousand. He breathed slowly, deliberately, trying to suppress the flow. The Codex Shard resisted him. It wanted to be seen. It wanted to announce itself.

He forced it down. The glow stabilized.

1,247 Vitalis.

Proctor Venn, a fifty-year-old man with tired eyes and a practiced bureaucratic frown, looked at the number. Then he looked at Roan. "You are aware that this is a high base score for a first-generation registrant? Most kids from your district struggle to break six hundred."

"I read a lot," Roan said. "My father left books."

"A thousand credits' worth of books might buy you theory, but it doesn't buy you a body that projects twelve hundred. The reading is clean. Pure."

Venn stared at him.

Roan met his eyes without flinching. He didn't try to look innocent. He just looked tired. A kid who studied too hard and didn't sleep enough. "What does the physical assessment say?"

"We're about to find out."

---

The physical assessment took place in a smaller room with a training golem and a Sparring Examiner who looked bored.

Roan took the stance he had practiced with his father a thousand times. Then he deliberately forgot it. He moved slowly. He blocked late. He took a hit to the ribs that the golem delivered at moderate force, a blow that should have been trivial to dodge with his Vitalis level. He let it connect. The pain flared, clean and sharp.

The Inversion stirred.

*Accept the harm*, it whispered. *Convert the impact into speed. Show them what you are.*

He ignored it. He swallowed the bruise and continued his clumsy dance.

The Sparring Examiner tabulated the results without emotion. "Theoretical score: 94/100. Combat application: 23/100. Recommendation: Standard Initiate Track. Tier One."

Standard track. The path for average students. The slow path. The safe path.

Perfect.

As Roan left the assessment room, he felt the weight of a gaze that wasn't the proctor's. He didn't turn around. He walked straight to the exit, his registration packet tucked under his arm, his spine warm with the Codex Shard's silent presence.

---

Behind him, in a monitoring booth, Sentinel Halric rewound the assessment footage. He watched Roan's so-called combat application three times. The block that was half a second too late. The stumble that looked genuine but had perfect recovery lines. The hit to the ribs that should have been dodged.

"He's hiding," Halric murmured.

"Sir?" The technician beside him looked confused.

"His Vitalis score is first-tier. Potential to hit Hardened in three years with proper training. But he fights like he's never held a sparring weapon. The numbers don't match the body."

"Maybe he's just a bookworm?"

"Bookworms don't score 1,247 on their first stone reading. That takes body conditioning. The dosage tolerance alone requires a healthy frame. This boy is lying, and badly." Halric pulled up the background file the terminal offered. "Roan Vance. Father: Aldric Vance. Deceased. Black Squad."

The blood left the technician's face. "The Aldric Vance?"

"The same. His son registers with a monster Vitalis base and a combat score that wouldn't pass a district gym. He knows we're watching. He's telling us exactly what he wants us to see." Halric tapped the screen. "Flag him. High priority. Content monitoring only. Don't spook him. I want to know who taught him that suppression trick."

---

Roan stepped out of the Registration Center.

The air was cool. The sky was overcast. He felt the phantom eyes on his back, the tingle of state observation settling into his skin like a second coat.

*Step one: complete.*

He turned down the alley towards the lower quarter. Halfway home, a figure emerged from a doorway. Drogan Hale. The old blacksmith from District Edge. He didn't look surprised to see Roan.

"You made it," Drogan said.

"I got the packet."

"I know. Your father's old contacts have ears. Word travels fast when a Vance walks into the system." He studied Roan with the same tired, measuring gaze that his father had worn. "The registration is just paper. The real test starts tomorrow. My forge. Don't be late."

"Why?"

Drogan didn't answer. He just turned and walked away, his broad shoulders vanishing into the grey morning.

Roan stood alone in the alley, registration packet in hand, the warmth of the Codex Shard resting against his spine. The Sentinel Bureau was watching. Drogan Hale was reaching out.

The game was real, and it had already begun.
