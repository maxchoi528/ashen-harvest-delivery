Chapter 90: The First Public Bet

The training yard smelled of iron and sweat. Fourteen cadets stood in two rows, breathing in the cold morning air, waiting for Warden Drogan to finish his inspection walk.

Roan was third from the left. He'd counted the others the moment he'd entered, fourteen, plus Drogan, plus the two senior disciples monitoring from the observation platform. Seventeen people who might be watching him at any given second.

The figures were plain. The risk was manageable.

"First form. Sevenspine posture."

Drogan's voice carried across the yard. The cadets moved. Roan moved with them, spine curved at precisely forty-seven degrees, arms locked in the mid-stance pattern. Sevenfold Bleed's foundation stance. The one that looked identical to the standard academy form.

Identical, but not identical. Drogan had taught him the variations three weeks ago. Weight distribution shifted two degrees left. Breathing cadence dropped half a beat. Small enough that no examiner would notice. Large enough that executing the technique for ninety minutes would cost him twelve percent more Vitalis than the standard version.

Roan didn't care about the extra cost. He cared about the extra pressure. Every deviation from the textbook version was a small, private rebellion, proof that he was learning, adapting, becoming something more than a number on a ranking sheet.

The Codex Shard hummed faintly against his spine. Not active. Just… Present. A reminder that his body was no longer entirely his own.

"You."

Drogan stopped three meters in front of him. The warden's iron hand glinted in the early light. "You're holding your shoulder wrong."

No greeting. No name. Just the accusation.

Roan didn't blink. "I'm holding it the way the manual specifies."

"The manual specifies a forty-five degree angle."

"And I'm holding it at forty-four point eight." Accurate to the decimal. He'd measured himself in the mirror last night.

Drogan's expression didn't change. The other cadets weren't looking anymore, they'd learned that watching the warden's corrections meant risking their own. But Roan could feel their attention anyway. Seventeen people recalculating his threat level.

"The manual is a suggestion," Drogan said. "Your body is the reality. Adjust."

Roan adjusted. Two-tenths of a degree. The difference was negligible. The message was clear.

Drogan moved on.

- break -

Two hours later, Roan was in the secondary training room, the one with the reinforced floors and the scorch marks that no one bothered to clean. The official reason was that he'd requested supplementary drills. The real reason was that the main yard had too many eyes.

Brendt was already there, stretching against the wall. He'd been nursing a pulled muscle from a drills accident the week before. The Ashford family had filed an official complaint about their continued proximity in training rotations. The complaint had been acknowledged but currently sat in formal review.

"Their lawyer sent another letter," Brendt said without looking up. "Third one this week."

"Let it sit."

"He wants a response within seven days or they're escalating to the Sentinel Bureau."

"They can try." Roan dropped his bag beside the bench. "Cyrus signed the sparring waiver. The injury was ruled accidental. There's no legal angle here."

"Ashford lawyers don't need angles. They need persistence." Brendt finished his stretch and stood. "My father's getting nervous."

"Your father should have thought about that before he let you fight a Greel."

The words came out sharper than he'd intended. Brendt's jaw tightened, a single beat of tension that Roan filed away and dismissed. The conversation had moved into uncomfortable territory.

Roan adjusted his approach. "What's the letter demanding?"

"Separation. They want me transferred to a different training cohort, away from you." Brendt shrugged. "Standard harassment tactic. Make my daily life difficult until I choose to leave."

"Will you?"

"Leave?" Brendt laughed. The sound was hollow. "I'm not built for leaving. Ask my mother. She tried to send me to Aldermere when I was fourteen. I refused. Packed my bags for Greycross instead."

"You're stubborn."

"I'm stupid." Brendt walked to the center of the room. "Same thing, depending on who you ask."

Roan followed. They moved into the starting positions, standard sparring distance without weapons. The floor was cold beneath his feet.

"Rules?"

"Same as last time. First to yield loses." Brendt settled into his stance. "No Inversion. Just technique."

No Inversion. The agreement had been Brendt's idea, he didn't want to risk another injury, and more importantly, he didn't want to become dependent on Roan's system. The logic was sound. You couldn't learn to fight if you were always compensating for abilities that wouldn't be available in a real engagement.

Roan nodded. Brendt moved first.

The opening strike was a standard feint, high left, then dropping to the ribs. Roan's block came automatically. He parried, shifted his weight, and countered with an open-palm strike aimed at Brendt's shoulder.

Contact. Brendt rolled with the impact, absorbing the force instead of blocking. Good. He'd learned that Roan hit harder than his size suggested. Better to redirect than to brace.

They circled. The floor creaked beneath them.

"How long until the trial?" Roan asked.

"What trial?"

"The Sentinel Bureau hearing. For your combat certification."

Brendt's expression flickered. "Word travels fast."

"I have sources." Roan pressed forward. A low kick, swept toward the ankle. Brendt jumped it easily. "The hearing is in eleven days. Your combat simulation scores need to be above the eighty-fifth percentile to avoid automatic reassignment."

"You've been reading my file."

"I read everyone's file." The truth. He'd memorized the academy ranking structures within his first week. Knowing where you stood meant knowing who was watching you, and who you could ignore. "Your last simulation was seventy-two. That's eleven points short."

"I know."

"You're going to fail."

Brendt's next punch came faster. Roan dodged left, felt the wind of the strike pass his ear. Brendt was pressing now, abandoning the defensive rhythm in favor of aggression. The anger was showing.

Roan sidestepped, brought his elbow down onto Brendt's wrist. The joint bent past its comfortable angle. Brendt hissed but didn't break position.

"Failure isn't the end of the world," Roan said quietly. "It just means you're in the wrong test."

"I'm not failing anything." Brendt surged forward. His fist connected with Roan's forearm, a solid block without damage taken. "I earned my place here. The Ashfords didn't give it to me. My scores did."

"Your scores." Roan countered with a palm strike to the sternum. Brendt absorbed it, pushed back. "The same scores that will be reviewed by a Sentinel Bureau that Ashford lawyers are already pressuring?"

The point landed. Brendt's rhythm faltered, half a second of hesitation. Roan capitalized immediately. A sweep to the knees, a controlling grip on the shoulder, and Brendt was on the ground, his back hitting the reinforced floor with a dull thud.

Roan didn't finish the hold. He stepped back, letting Brendt get up. The sparring rules were clear, yield or tap. Brendt hadn't yielded.

"Again?" Brendt asked, breathing hard.

"Your choice."

They reset. The room was quiet except for their breathing.

"Eleven days," Brendt muttered. "Eleven days to move eleven points."

"Spatial reasoning is your anchor," Roan said. "Eighteen percentile. Situational judgment is forty-one. The east wing simulator can give you unlimited pattern recognition drills. It's been broken for three months. I can fix it."

"You know how to repair academy equipment?"

"I know how to fix anything with a circuit board and a manual." Roan settled back into his stance. "The simulator's issue is a power regulation failure. I can bypass it in about twenty minutes. Then you get unlimited access to the drills."

Brendt studied him. "Why?"

"You're my friend. And the Ashfords want you gone. Which means keeping you here is the right move."

The room hummed with tension. Brendt's expression shifted, not a smile, but something close.

"That your way of saying you care?"

"My way of saying I don't want to recalculate the social dynamics of my daily routine." Roan rolled his shoulders. "Friends are expensive. I'm not finding a replacement."

"Right." Brendt's stance tightened. "Again?"

"Again."

- break -

The simulator room was cold and abandoned, tucked into a corner of the east wing where the heating ducts didn't reach. Roan had been here twice before, once to assess the equipment, once to gather the tools he needed. Tonight was the third visit.

The cover panel came off easily. Inside, the circuit board was exactly as he'd predicted: a voltage regulator had burned out after years of inconsistent power supply. A bypass would take longer than the standard fix, but it would be more stable. More permanent.

He worked in silence, his fingers moving through the pattern of connections. Aldric had taught him the rhythm at twelve: read, touch, read again. Trust the diagram more than the fingers. Fingers lied if you let them. The screwdriver was cheap, academy property, but it did the job. The replacement regulator wasn't academy property. He'd paid forty credits for it at a parts dealer in Ironholm's lower district. Out of his own funds.

Brendt would pay him back. The details of repayment hadn't been discussed. Roan didn't mind. The investment was sound. Brendt passing the trial meant Brendt staying in the cohort. Brendt staying in the cohort meant someone else watching his back during the exercises that Drogan designed to break people.

The Codex Shard pulsed once. A small warmth against his lower spine.

[Sidewound type unrecognized. Pattern logged: prolonged cold exposure, minor joint strain. Yield calculation pending. — A.V.]

The notification was unexpected. Aldric's residual consciousness rarely spoke during non-combat situations. The shard was tracking his body's state, calculating the minor stress of working in the cold room.

Roan ignored it. He didn't need a system to tell him his fingers were stiff.

The bypass took half an hour. When he finished, the simulator's power light glowed steady green, no flicker without warning indicators. He'd run three test cycles to confirm the fix. The response times were perfect.

He wiped his hands and stood. The room was exactly as he'd left it, no one had entered without one had noticed. Standard academy maintenance wasn't his responsibility, so no one would ask questions.

On his way out, he passed the window overlooking the central courtyard. The lights were still on in the administrative building. Someone was working late. The shadow on the third floor moved like a clerk, not a warden.

Roan noted the time and moved on.

- break -

Morning training was brutal. Drogan had designed the session to break through accumulated fatigue, drill that pushed until your body screamed, then pushed further. Forty cadets had started. When the whistle blew for the final rest period, only thirty-eight remained standing.

Roan was standing. Barely.

His arms trembled as he held the recovery position. Around him, the other cadets sprawled on the training mats, their breathing ragged. The air tasted like copper and exhaustion.

Brendt was two positions to his left, also standing. They'd exchanged no words during the session, communication required energy that neither could spare. But Brendt had caught his eye twice during the final segment, and each time there'd been something like acknowledgment. Something like gratitude.

The simulator fix had worked. Brendt's training scores had improved by four points in two days. Eleven days left. The ledger was still difficult, but it was no longer impossible.

"Werewolf."

The name cut through the exhausted silence. Every head turned toward the voice.

Cyrus Ashford stood at the edge of the training yard, flanked by two senior disciples. His posture was casual, almost relaxed, but his eyes were sharp. Calculating.

"Werewolf," he repeated. "That's what they're calling you now. The students from the outer districts. They say you fight like an animal. No honor without form. Just teeth and instinct."

Roan didn't move. His body wanted to rest. His mind wanted to analyze. He let both impulses settle.

"Aren't you going to respond?" Cyrus stepped closer. "Your friend Brendt taught you to be silent, but I expected more from the Vance heir. Your father had a famous temper."

The name hit like a physical blow. Roan's jaw locked.

[Careful. — A.V.]

The warning was Aldric's. The shard knew what the provocation was designed to do. The Ashfords were escalating, pushing beyond legal harassment into personal assault. They wanted Roan to react, to break, to give them an excuse for a formal complaint.

The game was transparent. The stakes were manageable.

Roan let the silence stretch. One heartbeat. Two. Three.

When he spoke, his voice was flat. Controlled. "Your simulation scores from last month. Combat retention, seventy-two. Spatial reasoning, fifty-eight. Situational judgment, forty-four." He paused. "Those numbers won't get you into the Sentinel Bureau either, Ashford. They'll just get you into a different kind of failure."

Cyrus's expression flickered. The cheap shot had landed, a reminder that Roan knew things, tracked things, and would use that knowledge when it served him.

"You think you're clever." Cyrus stepped back, his senior disciples closing behind him. "But cleverness doesn't stop a blade. Your father learned that. Eventually."

He walked away.

The training yard stayed silent for another thirty seconds before the cadets began to move again. The moment had passed. Another skirmish in a war that wouldn't be declared until much later.

Brendt appeared at his side. "You shouldn't have engaged."

"He wanted me to attack him. I didn't."

"You insulted him."

"I gave him information. What he does with it's his problem." Roan rolled his shoulders, feeling the strain in his muscles. "The Ashfords are losing leverage. Cyrus is desperate. Desperate people make mistakes."

"Mistakes that could get us both killed."

"They won't kill us." Roan looked at the spot where Cyrus had stood. "They'll try to break us first. Failure is more satisfying than death, to people like him."

Brendt didn't respond. He didn't need to. The afternoon session began. Drogan's whistle cut through the air.

Roan moved.

- break -

By dinner, the yard had already given the exchange a name. The score-knife trick. The senior disciples in the observation tower called it a bet, in the precise tone of bookkeepers watching a ledger get longer.

Drogan logged the incident in his evening report under a heading he didn't file: *Vance R. Confirmed faculty-database access, declined to deny.*

Iris Vell, two corridors away, opened a private slate, marked a small wager beside a name she hadn't yet decided to favor, and closed it before anyone saw the figure.

- break -

The library was quiet after hours. Most cadets didn't know about the extended access protocol, the one that let you stay past ten if you'd been approved for supplementary research. Roan's approval had come through three weeks ago, after he'd filed the request for access to historical combat records.

The records were a front. What he really wanted was the archive room in the back, the one that held the academy databases on international advancement politics. The founding families. The treaties. The bloodlines.

He found the file under "Greel, House (Northern Federation)." It was three pages long. Most of the information was public record. House Greel's rise, the family's political influence, their control over the northern training academies. Nothing useful.

He tried "Ashford, House (Western Alliance)." Four pages. Cyrus's grandfather had been a Sentinel Bureau commander. His father was a lawyer. The bloodline was old but not exceptional, a side branch of the main family tree, distinguished enough to matter but not powerful enough to dominate.

The Vance file was shorter. Four lines. Aldric Vance, former instructor, deceased. No further details.

Roan stared at the screen.

The system had flagged his access. A small icon in the corner, a warning symbol he'd never seen before. Someone had noticed his query.

He closed the file, cleared his history, and left the library.

The corridors were empty. The lights had been dimmed for the night cycle. Roan moved through the shadows without sound, his footsteps absorbed by the stone floors.

Three floors up, the same window where he'd watched the administrative building the night before. The light was still on. The shadow had moved.

He paused, counting the seconds.

The shadow stopped. Turned.

Looked directly at the window where Roan stood.

The moment stretched. Neither of them moved.

Then the shadow withdrew. The light went out.

Roan stood in the darkness for another ten minutes before he walked away.

The equation remained honest. Someone was watching. Someone knew he was searching.

The question was whether they were an enemy or something else entirely.

The answer would come. It always did.
