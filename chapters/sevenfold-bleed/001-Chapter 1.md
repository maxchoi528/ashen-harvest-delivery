Chapter 1: Six to Go

The dead man had very white teeth.

Roan noticed this because the man's mouth was open, slack, loose, the way jaws went when the tendons stopped holding them shut, and the teeth caught what little light remained in the Hollow Reaches, gleaming with a faint, almost medical cleanliness against the blood that had pooled beneath his head. Not his blood, mostly. Roan had made sure of that.

He looked away from the teeth and down at his own wrist.

The Codex Shard projected its usual readout above his skin, faint blue text hovering in the air like a ghost that had learned grammar.

[Codex Shard: idle. Capacity: 0/6.]

Zero out of six. He'd used the last one three minutes ago, converting the frostburn from that Throne-tier's death-aura into enough Vitalis to crack the man's ribcage from the inside. A neat trick. It made economists weep and battlefield medics retire early. He was going to feel it tomorrow, or whenever tomorrow came, in a place where the sky cracked open and leaked starlight the color of old bruises.

Roan squatted.

Not out of respect. The dead didn't deserve respect, not this one. He squatted because dead men shouldn't witness what happened next, and the man's eyes were still open, pale gray, fixed on the purple-gray fissure tearing through the heavens above the ancient battlefield. Roan reached out and pressed the eyelids shut with two fingers. The skin was already cooling, slipping slightly under his thumb like wax left too near a flame.

"Right," he said.

No one answered. There was no one else left standing on the ridge, not among the shattered stone and the older shattered bone. The battle had taken eleven hours by his count, though time moved strangely here. Five of his allies were dead. Three were dying somewhere below, their Vitalis readings fading. The rest of the enemy, the ones who'd come in waves before this last one arrived, lay scattered across the slope. Roan didn't bother counting them. The count didn't matter anymore.

He'd won.

That wasn't true. He felt the bone-deep exhaustion that came from cycling Vitalis past the redline, a tiredness that sat behind his eyes and made the columns on his Shard readout blur. He felt the cold seeping through his coat, which was more holes than fabric now, the wind finding each gap with the precision of a needle. He felt the weight of the knife in his boot, the one he hadn't needed to use.

Roan stood slowly. His left knee didn't want to unbend. He let his gaze drift across the battlefield. The ridge was a mess of craters and glassy black stone where Vitalis had burned hot enough to melt rock. Below, the slope was littered with corpses. He'd faced worse odds before, in deeper places. That wasn't what made this different.

The difference was the man with the very white teeth.

The deputy had stepped through the fissure thinking he'd find a wounded boy and something broken. Instead he'd found Roan Vance, standing in a crater of his own making, with five uses of Capacity remaining and a smile that wasn't a smile at all.

Roan looked at the dead man's hands. They were large hands, callused in the way that came from holding a glaive for thirty years, the Northern Glaive, a signature pattern passed down to deputies and lieutenants who dreamed of inheriting more than steel. The hands were empty now. The glaive lay three meters away, its blade snapped cleanly in two, the break so precise it might've been cut by a machine rather than by a seventeen-year-old boy who'd figured out how to invert a Throne-tier's defensive aura into a localized gravity well.

"Wonderful."

The word came out flat, barely a whisper, tasting of copper and dust. He'd lost count of how many times he'd said it sarcastically since leaving Greycross. Roan could tally it without moving his lips: six years since his father's death, two years since the Shard had first spoken to him, eight months since he'd entered the Spire's records as a Tempered candidate while operating at Hardened output, and now, today, the first Throne-tier kill. The progression was clean. Almost too clean. So clean it made him check his calculations twice, three times, looking for the error that had to be hiding somewhere in the margins.

He walked to the glaive's broken blade and nudged it with his boot. The metal was still warm, humming with residual Vitalis signatures that his Shard could read if he had any Capacity left. He didn't. He kicked the blade over instead, watching it clatter down the ridge, tumbling past a dead Drake's still-smoking ribcage before coming to rest against a half-buried Husk skull.

He took inventory of himself.

Left shoulder: dislocated, popped back in during the eighth minute. Still functional, though raising his arm above chest height sent a bolt of pain down his spine.

Right hand: three fingers numb. A feedback burn from channeling too much Vitalis through too small a channel. He'd feel it in his grip for weeks.

Ribcage: two cracked, breathing shallow. The Sovereign's punch had done that, the heartbeat before the Inversion answered. He'd coughed blood afterward, pink foam flecking the dirt, before his flow finally stanched the bleeding. He'd need a healer when he got back. One who didn't ask questions.

Face: untouched. That mattered more than it should. People remembered faces longer than battle reports, and Roan still had business that required him to be forgettable.

He noted his pulse: finally below ninety. The medical literature did not cover post-Throne-tier baselines for seventeen-year-olds. He took some private satisfaction in being a footnote.

He checked his pockets. The emergency vial had shattered. Whatever liquid had soaked into his coat lining smelled faintly of almonds and formaldehyde. No use now. His mind was already as resilient as a locked vault. What he needed was physical repair, and for that he needed to walk.

Walk out of this place. Walk back through the portal they'd used to enter, assuming it still worked after the detonation. Walk three hundred miles to reach the city without being intercepted by patrols who might wonder why a seventeen-year-old student was wandering out of a restricted zone with blood under his fingernails and a dead man's ring in his pocket.

The logistics were annoying.

Roan sat down. His knee was grateful. The rest of his body registered the cessation of movement with a collective sigh that wasn't audible but felt like one.

His hand found something in his pocket. Not the ring, something smaller, colder. A fragment of metal no bigger than a coin, curved, still bearing the faint grooves of a hydraulic joint.

He didn't remember picking it up.

That bothered him more than the injury in his ribs. Roan remembered useful objects. He remembered angles, footsteps, debt, exit routes. If his hand had taken this without permission from the part of his mind that kept records, then the last eleven hours had cost more than he had admitted.

He closed his fingers around the metal fragment. It left an imprint in his palm.

A debt, then. Another one.

He would identify the owner when he had time to breathe and enough Capacity to make touching unknown battlefield salvage less stupid.

He looked at the dead man again. The deputy. The northern lord's man. The one who'd announced himself with a title that started with "Arch-Lord" and included at least three geographic claims Roan had never heard of. A title that took longer to say than the fight had lasted.

The fight itself. Roan replayed it in his head, the way he replayed everything, filing the data under "Throne-tier engagement #1" for later analysis.

The man had opened with a standard Apex Sovereign opener: Vitalis aura compression, creating a defensive shell dense enough to deflect artillery. The shell had shimmered gold-white, the color of old money and older bloodlines, and Roan had felt the pressure of it from fifty meters away, a crushing weight that made his teeth ache and his ears pop like a descending elevator.

He'd walked into it.

No one would believe that part, if anyone ever wrote a report. He'd walked forward, step by step, letting the aura's pressure squeeze his chest, compact his lungs, slow his heartbeat. He'd let it hurt. He'd let the frostburn, the deputy's secondary effect, a localized temperature inversion that flash-froze anything touching the shell's outer layer, coat his left arm in rime ice that cracked his skin and froze his fingers into claws.

A breath.

The Inversion didn't reward flinching. It needed the wound to become real first, not a threat, not a warning, but damage with teeth in it. Roan had learned that timing the way a safecracker learns tumblers: by bleeding through every mistake until his body understood the lock.

The Shard kindled along his spine.

Then the frostburn inverted.

The cold became heat. The pressure became expansion. The deputy's own defensive shell, compressed to maximum density by the man's confidence and Roan's baiting, detonated outward in a localized gravity well that pulled everything toward the deputy's center of mass, including the deputy's own ribcage, which folded inward with a sound like a wet book slamming shut.

The man had looked surprised. That was the part Roan remembered most. Not the death. Not the blood. The surprise. The way the deputy's eyes, those pale gray eyes now safely shut, had widened fractionally, too late, too slow, the realization arriving after the ribcage had already compressed his lungs into paste.

"Arch-Lord" something-something had died confused. Roan found that appropriate.

He looked at his left hand. The frostburn had inverted completely, leaving his fingers with a faint afterglow of warmth. The Inversion didn't heal, it converted. The damage became power. The pain became fuel. His current Vitalis reading, if he'd had Capacity to check, would show a spike in the low thirties. Forged-tier output from a body still registered as Tempered. The discrepancy was his edge. The discrepancy was his mask. The discrepancy was why men with titles longer than their lifespans kept underestimating a boy from Greycross who shopped for discount Catalysts in alleys.

The sky groaned.

He looked up. The fissure above him, the one that had opened when the Hollowed Sovereign died and its Core detonated, was beginning to knit itself closed. Not healing, exactly. More like the world was remembering how to be whole, stitching the wound shut with threads of violet light that moved too slowly to be healing and too deliberately to be natural. Whatever threshold he had crossed by killing a Throne-tier in their own domain, the Hollow Reaches were reacting. Closing the door he'd kicked open.

Six to go.

The thought came without invitation, crisp and quiet as a shutter clicking. Six hostile signatures remained on a ledger his father had buried so deeply that even the Shard revealed it one bruise at a time. Six people, families, or powers tied to Aldric Vance's death. Six debts that had outlived the man who recorded them.

Someone had believed that killing one inconvenient scholar would make the problem smaller.

They had been wrong.

Roan had proven that today, though the proof felt less like triumph and more like arithmetic. One down. Six remaining. The ratios were manageable. The costs were acceptable. The timeline—he stopped himself. This was how it started, the recursion his mother had warned him about before she'd stopped speaking to him entirely. *You become what you hunt, Roan. You become the tally.* He'd been eleven when she'd said that. He hadn't understood her then. He understood her now, perfectly, which was worse.

He looked at the iron fragment in his palm. The imprint was still there.

The Codex Shard flickered.

[Processing...]

Roan's hand stilled. The Shard hadn't shown that message in months. Not without a threshold being crossed. Not without internal recalculation that usually meant a new cost had been discovered after the payment had already left his account.

Blue text formed above his wrist.

[Target ledger updated.]

[Unresolved hostile signatures: 6.]

[Do not die before the count reaches 3. — A.V.]

Roan stared at the last line.

The notation was his father's. Not proof of presence. Not a conversation. Just a recorded habit embedded in the Codex, surfacing at the least convenient possible moment with the emotional tact of a tax notice.

His eyebrow twitched.

"Helpful," he said.

The Shard offered no apology. It never did.

Six unresolved signatures. Six remaining debts. No names displayed, no context offered, no explanation for why one death had been enough to wake the ledger and not enough to make it generous.

That was fine. Names were useful only after routes existed. Roan had a ring, a broken weapon, a battlefield full of cooling evidence, and a portal that might close before he finished bleeding into his coat.

Information could wait.

Extraction couldn't.

Roan reached down and took the ring from the dead man's hand. A simple band, platinum, warm even in this cold, still holding residual Vitalis from its owner, thrumming faintly against his palm like a second heartbeat. He'd take it to Eckhart when he got back. The old smith had a fondness for trinkets, and a sharper fondness for paying in information rather than credits. Information about who'd sent this man. About his employer's movements. About the security patterns that would need to change now that one of their lords had died screaming in a gravity well of his own making.

Roan pocketed the ring and straightened his collar—ridiculous, given the state of his clothes, but old habits from Greycross died harder than Throne-tier enemies. He looked once more at the dead man with the very white teeth, now safely shut-eyed and silent, a minor lord in a greater war who had happened to stand in the wrong place when Roan Vance came through.

The Hollow Reaches breathed around him, cold and mineral and wind-scoured.

Roan turned toward the horizon, where the first true stars—not the bleeding, fissured light of the wounded sky, but real, distant, indifferent stars—were beginning to show through the healed cracks. He counted them automatically, the way he counted everything. Seven visible. One for each name on his list, if he'd been someone who believed in signs.

He wasn't.

"Six to go," he said.

The wind took his words and gave nothing back.

[Six to go. — A.V.]
