Chapter 174: The Identity Check

Final examinations began on a Tuesday, which Roan considered evidence that the academic calendar had never been reviewed by anyone with enemies.

The official purpose of finals was to evaluate student progress across theory, application, combat readiness, and professional judgment. The actual purpose was to compress several hundred ambitious young practitioners into stress, sleep deprivation, and public rankings, then pretend the resulting behavior reflected character rather than institutional negligence.

Roan approved of the efficiency, if not the hypocrisy.

His first examination was Advanced Vitalis Control.

The room smelled of metal dust and anxiety. Thirty students stood in individual testing circles while three instructors walked between them with clipboards, measuring output stability through a lattice of floating rings. Each ring responded to a different circulation pattern: compression, expansion, lateral pulse, reflexive correction, and sustained fine control. Most students hated the last one. They preferred large visible output. Fine control punished ego.

Roan liked fine control.

It lied less.

"Vance," Instructor Pellan said, stopping before his circle. Not Old Pell from Greycross. Different Pellan. Worse eyebrows. "Your current registered baseline after medical clearance is Forged early. You will demonstrate within the approved safe range."

"Define safe."

Pellan looked over the clipboard. "Don't make me file a report."

"Clear."

The first ring lit blue.

Roan exhaled and fed Vitalis through his right palm in a narrow thread. Not enough to impress. Enough to satisfy. The ring held steady.

Second ring: expansion.

He widened the current, stopping well below what his body could now tolerate. Since the Hollowed City, his stable baseline had settled around standard Forged. Four thousand Vitalis by formal scale, though numbers meant less than they had when he was starving in Greycross and trying to look boring at registration. His peak could go higher. Much higher and briefly, plus with invoices attached.

He didn't show that.

Third ring: lateral pulse.

He let the energy shift sideways through the shoulder channel, avoiding the scar where Crimson Wake tended to wake too eagerly. The ring flickered once, then steadied.

Pellan's eyebrow moved. "Again."

Roan did.

No flicker.

"Acceptable."

Fourth ring: reflexive correction.

The ring spat an interference pulse back into his hand. A minor disruption, meant to imitate hostile energy contact. Several students hissed or swore when their circulation broke. One overcorrected and cracked the floor tile inside his circle.

Roan let the disruption enter. A heartbeat.

The Codex Shard offered nothing. Too small. Too clean. Good.

He corrected manually.

The ring went green.

Fifth ring: sustained fine control.

A thin silver needle rose from the floor. Roan had to wrap it in Vitalis without bending it, heating it, or letting the energy thread touch the surrounding circle. The trick wasn't delicacy. Delicacy implied softness. The trick was refusing unnecessary motion.

He held the thread for sixty seconds.

Pellan wrote something down.

Roan withdrew.

Score: high but not absurd. Enough to maintain public trajectory after the Rift. Not enough to invite a special review.

He stepped out of the circle and found Kael Greel watching from the next row.

Kael had finished his own test.

Of course he had.

His rings showed a near-perfect stability pattern, with one tiny flaw in the compression phase. Deliberate? Likely. Too perfect invited examination. A flaw placed correctly implied talent without making instructors smell blood.

Kael inclined his head.

Roan didn't return it.

Cyrus stood two circles behind Kael, sweat on his brow, expression grim. His fourth ring was unstable. Reflexive correction. He failed once, jaw tightening. Kael didn't look at him. That was the point. No rescue. No public correction. Cyrus reset on his own.

Second attempt: pass.

Cyrus's shoulders eased.

Roan left before the instructor could call comparison scores.

Outside the testing hall, Selene waited with two sealed folders and a cup of coffee.

"For me?" Roan asked.

"For Brendt," she said.

"Good. He requires bribery."

"He calls it emotional maintenance."

"He would."

She handed him one folder instead of the coffee. "Preliminary Greel material. Not the genealogy. That requires a second lock and one cousin who thinks discretion means charging twice. This is public-facing history."

Roan opened it while walking.

House Greel. Northern Crown lineage. Old warlords turned treaty signatories. Ice combat specialization. Diplomatic immunity webs. Strategic marriages across Arctic holdings, northern Spire districts, and two financial houses in Ironholm. Vorath Greel listed as senior patriarch emeritus, which was a title people used when "retired monster" sounded impolite on documents.

Kael Greel appeared on page four.

Age nineteen. Official transfer student. Prior enrollment: Northwatch Martial Institute. Public ranking: top eight percentile, not first. Specialization: cryo-Vitalis control, defensive field shaping, spear forms.

A modest profile.

Too modest.

"Northwatch records are curated," Selene said. "Not false. Thin."

"Family hiding a better blade."

"Yes."

"Why send a grandson with a thin public record?"

"Because overqualified students trigger review. Because a mediocre official record makes sudden excellence look like growth. Because Cyrus Ashford needs a mentor he can tolerate, not a legend he would resent."

Roan glanced at her.

Selene's expression remained composed, but fatigue shadowed her eyes. She had been working through House Ardent archives while maintaining the public fiction that she remained a loyal daughter with mildly eccentric academic interests. That kind of double life consumed more energy than combat and left fewer visible bruises.

"Sleep?" he asked.

She blinked. "Was that concern or data collection?"

"Yes."

"I got four hours."

"Too few."

"You got?"

"Enough."

"Roan."

"Two."

She gave him a flat look.

He changed the subject because hypocrisy became less useful when named. "What does Ardent think Kael wants?"

"Officially? To rebuild northern student influence after Cyrus embarrassed the old tournament structure."

"And unofficially?"

"My father told me to stay away from him."

"That isn't an answer."

"It's the closest thing to panic he has shown in years."

Useful.

They turned into a quieter corridor near the administrative wing. Exams had emptied most classrooms, leaving only distant footsteps and the hum of warded doors. Roan disliked administrative corridors. Too many locks. Too many people who believed paper made cowardice respectable.

Selene lowered her voice. "The genealogy will confirm whether Kael is direct-line Vorath or collateral."

"He's direct."

"You know?"

"Pattern."

"You need proof."

"Yes."

She nodded. "Tonight, then. Archive annex. After my contract law exam."

"Contract law?"

"Some of us commit crimes with professional standards."

Roan almost smiled.

Almost.

- break -

The second examination was Tactical Ethics.

Roan suspected Cole had placed it on his schedule personally.

The exam took place in a tiered lecture chamber with individual response crystals mounted to each desk. Students were given scenario prompts and required to submit decisions under time pressure. The scoring algorithm claimed to measure legal compliance, casualty minimization, mission success, and moral reasoning.

Algorithms were just other people's assumptions wearing math.

Prompt one: *A civilian convoy is trapped between low-tier Rift creatures and a collapsing bridge. You can save the convoy by destroying the bridge, isolating an allied scout team on the far side. Choose action.*

Roan selected: destroy bridge, mark scout team as delayed recovery, send flare before detonation if timing permits.

The crystal pulsed yellow.

Partial credit. The exam wanted emotional language about difficult sacrifices. Roan refused to flirt with furniture.

Prompt two: *A hostile student who previously attempted to sabotage your team is injured during a sanctioned expedition. Medical resources are limited. Treat?*

Yes, if the hostile student's survival improves mission exit probability, preserves legal standing, or yields interrogation value. No if treatment significantly reduces allied survival odds.

The crystal pulsed yellow again.

Brendt, two rows away, looked over with suspicion.

Roan ignored him.

Prompt three: *A suspected assassin uses another student as social cover. You lack evidence sufficient for formal accusation. What action do you take?*

Roan stared at the prompt.

Subtle, Cole.

Options offered: report suspicion; avoid contact; confront privately; request faculty mediation; monitor and gather evidence.

He selected monitor and gather evidence, then added: *Control engagement terrain before confrontation. Identify whether social cover is tool, hostage, or collaborator.*

The crystal pulsed green.

So Cole had a sense of humor after all. Unfortunate.

Kael sat near the front.

Roan couldn't see his answers, but he could see the crystal pulses. Green. Green. Yellow. Green. No hesitation longer than necessary. Cyrus, seated two rows behind Kael, produced more yellow than green but fewer red pulses than expected.

Improving.

Irritating.

The final prompt appeared.

*An enemy violates institutional neutrality through covert action. Legal retaliation is available but may expose sensitive personal information. Extralegal retaliation is likely to succeed but risks escalation. Choose a strategy.*

Roan didn't answer immediately.

The obvious intended answer was legal retaliation. Black Spire loved its neutrality laws because neutrality turned institutional self-interest into moral costume. But sensitive personal information changed the equation. If exposing Kael exposed Codex, legal channels became a trap.

Extralegal retaliation risked escalation, but escalation had already occurred. Greel had sent blood into campus. Vell remnants tested toxin delivery in the refectory. Someone had slipped notes through warded doors. The question wasn't whether escalation existed, but whether Roan shaped it or reacted to it.

He selected: *Hybrid. Construct legal venue with controlled evidentiary release. Force enemy to violate neutrality publicly while protecting sensitive core information through decoy motive.*

The crystal went green.

Then blue.

The chamber's instructor looked up sharply.

Roan looked at the crystal.

Blue wasn't listed in the exam briefing.

Kael turned his head slightly.

Cyrus frowned.

The instructor approached Roan's desk with professional calm and badly hidden curiosity. "Mr. Vance. Your response triggered review classification."

"Is that good?" Brendt called from two rows away.

"No," Roan said.

The instructor hesitated. "It means the system identified a strategic answer outside standard scoring bands."

"Still no," Roan said.

A few students laughed nervously.

The instructor read his answer and went quiet.

Cole would hear about it within the hour.

That was fine. Cole already knew enough to be troublesome. The more useful question was whether Kael saw the blue pulse and understood what it implied.

Kael's expression remained polite.

Too polite.

Yes, then.

- break -

That evening, Roan failed an exam question on purpose.

Not dramatically. One error in Historical Rift Jurisdiction, buried in a long-form answer about municipal authority over unstable breaches. A clean error. Plausible fatigue. Mistake a bright student made when operating under post-expedition stress and too many practical exams.

He did it because perfect performance across all finals would become another data point Kael could use to refine estimates. Roan needed public variance. Not incompetence. Variance.

Also because the question's official answer was wrong.

The prompt claimed the Sentinel Bureau had gained exclusive authority over Tier Three civilian breach sites after the Third Containment Act. That was true in the legal sense and false in the operational one. Municipal black agreements, Spire emergency carveouts, and old military access keys. Aldric's stolen brass key, for example, made "exclusive authority" a polite hallucination.

Roan wrote the official answer minus one clause and moved on.

After the exam, he collected Brendt from the medical wing lobby, where Brendt had been arguing with a nurse about whether carrying coffee counted as strenuous activity.

"It doesn't," Brendt said as Roan approached.

The nurse pointed at his sling. "Your chart says no load-bearing."

"It's coffee."

"It has weight."

"Emotionally, yes."

Roan took the cup from him.

"Betrayal," Brendt said.

"Medical compliance."

"I liked you better when you were committing tax crimes with potion money."

"No, you didn't."

"No, but I had more coffee."

Roan handed him Selene's earlier cup, now reheated and transferred into a lighter container.

Brendt accepted it with suspicion. "Why?"

"Selene."

"Ah. She understands civilization."

"Barely."

They walked toward the archive annex by way of two indirect corridors, one service stair, and a maintenance bridge above the eastern courtyard. Roan watched reflections. No tail. Or no visible tail. The distinction mattered.

Brendt sipped coffee. "You're taking me to the secret archive because I'm useful or because you don't trust me alone?"

"Yes."

"Your friendships are so layered."

"You wanted honesty."

"I wanted coffee and fewer assassination attempts."

"Lower probability on the first without guarantee on the second."

At the archive annex, Selene waited beside a locked side door wearing a formal Ardent jacket over student blacks. The jacket made her look more expensive and less approachable, which was likely why she had chosen it. Beside her stood Ines, who had no obvious reason to be there except that locked places attracted her like unpaid invoices.

"No," Roan said to Ines.

She smiled. "You don't know what I'm asking."

"Still no."

"I have tools."

"Selene has access."

"Access leaves logs. Tools leave character."

Selene looked offended on behalf of laws she was about to bend. "I have temporary catalog authority."

Ines looked at Roan. "Temporary catalog authority leaves logs."

Roan considered.

Selene sighed. "I hate that she has a point."

They used both.

Selene opened the first lock legally. Ines disrupted the second lock's logging charm long enough for Selene's entry to record as a routine cross-reference instead of a restricted genealogy pull. Roan watched the corridor. Brendt held coffee and looked increasingly like a man wondering when his life had become procedural burglary.

Inside, the archive smelled of paper, cold stone, and expensive dust.

Rows of black cabinets lined the room. Genealogical records occupied the rear section behind a secondary ward. Selene placed her palm against the ward plate and spoke three Ardent authorization phrases. The plate accepted the first two and challenged the third.

Her mouth tightened.

Ines leaned in. "Family changed access?"

"Yes."

"When?"

"After I requested the Greel index."

Roan looked at Selene.

She didn't apologize. Good.

"Options?" he asked.

"Break ward, alert administration. Leave, lose timing. Or use blood relation override."

"Cost?"

"My father receives notice that I accessed disputed noble lineage under personal authority."

"Political damage?"

"Moderate."

"Personal?"

Her face went still. "Manageable."

Not manageable, then.

Roan weighed it. The genealogy mattered. But burning Selene's family position too early reduced future access. On the other hand, the lock change itself was evidence that someone inside Ardent or Greel feared the record.

Brendt said quietly, "We can find another way."

Selene looked at him.

It was the wrong strategic advice and the right human one. Brendt specialized in making those inconveniently adjacent.

Selene removed a glove. "No. We came because timing matters."

Roan caught her wrist before she touched the plate.

She looked at his hand.

"I pay debts," Roan said.

"This isn't a debt."

"It will be."

A faint smile moved across her face. "You make loyalty sound like accounting."

"It survives better that way."

She pressed her thumb to the ward spike.

Blood welled.

The plate accepted her.

The cabinet unlocked.

Inside lay the Greel family genealogy in three volumes.

Selene took the newest.

Roan opened it on the archive table.

While they read, Brendt had turned his back to the table—not for privacy, but to sight the corridor's single entrance. The coffee was in his good hand. The other, still in the sling, was angled to brace the door if it opened fast.

Names ran across the page in precise northern script, arranged in branches, marriages, deaths, formal disinheritances, sealed adoptions, and bloodline certifications. He found Vorath Greel near the center of the current patriarchal line.

Below him: sons, daughters, collateral heirs.

Then Kael.

More than his grandson.

Primary bloodline heir of the Northern Crown martial branch.

Designated vessel-candidate.

The phrase sat in the record as if it were ordinary.

Brendt leaned closer. "Vessel for what?"

Selene had gone pale.

Ines whispered, "That term isn't used in modern inheritance law."

Roan read the line again.

Designated vessel-candidate.

Beneath it lay three seals. One was the expected Greel blood certification, stamped in northern blue. The second was a martial succession mark, old but still legally valid. The third had been half-scraped from the page. Not removed cleanly. Scraped by someone who had feared tearing the paper more than leaving traces.

Selene bent closer. "That isn't a civil seal."

"What is it?" Brendt asked.

"A rite acknowledgment."

Ines made a small interested sound. "Religious?"

"No. Older than current northern temple law. Some martial houses used rite seals for body-line preparation before the Neutrality Accords. The practice was supposed to have died out because too many heirs became dead, mad, or legally inconvenient."

Roan looked at the scraped mark. "Preparation for possession?"

Selene hesitated.

That was answer enough.

Brendt went very still. "Possession as in ghosts, or possession as in rich families found a worse word for succession?"

"Both, historically," Selene said. "Most accounts are exaggerated."

"Most," Roan repeated.

She gave him a tired look. "Yes. I heard it too."

Ines slid a thin lens from her pocket and held it over the scraped seal. A faint red pattern appeared on the glass, reconstructing pressure marks left in the paper fibers. "Not enough to identify fully. But there are three strokes here. Crown shape. Maybe Northern Crown internal rite."

"Can you copy it?"

"Already did."

Selene shot her a look.

Ines smiled without shame. "History rewards initiative."

Roan turned another page. Kael's childhood record had been pruned heavily. Training certifications appeared in neat intervals, but healer summaries were missing. Academic placements listed, but no exam comments. A tournament record existed with several wins, then an unexplained two-year gap before Northwatch. After the gap, Kael's official performance dipped. Publicly, that might imply injury or illness. Privately, it looked like concealment after selection.

"Vessel-candidate at what age?" Roan asked.

Selene traced the date. "Thirteen."

Brendt swore softly.

Roan didn't.

Thirteen was young enough that Kael hadn't chosen it in any meaningful sense. Old enough to understand the cage being built around him. That mattered. Not morally, not yet. Operationally. A weapon that knew it was a weapon behaved differently from one that believed it was a son.

The Codex Shard pulsed once, cold and deep.

[Hostile lineage architecture detected.]

Roan closed the book halfway, not fully. Fully closed books looked finished. He wanted the page available if the Shard produced another warning.

Kael wasn't just Vorath's grandson.

He was something Vorath intended to use.

That made him more dangerous.

It also made him less free.

The archive ward chimed once.

Selene's face changed. "Someone queried the same cabinet."

"In the building?" Roan asked.

"No. Remote request." She snapped the volume shut and slid it back into its case. "Ardent senior access."

"Your father?"

"Or someone using his authority."

Ines killed the logging charm completely with one sharp twist of a tool. The ward sparked, then settled into a false idle hum. "We should leave before genealogy becomes a family reunion."

They left by the maintenance stairs. Halfway down, voices drifted from the main archive hall. One belonged to an Ardent clerk Roan had seen twice in Selene's files. The other was colder and younger, plus polite.

Kael.

Selene froze.

Roan held up one hand.

Through the crack in the stairwell door, Kael's voice carried. "Only public genealogical cross-references. I am preparing for Comparative Lineage Law."

A pause. The clerk murmured something nervous.

Kael laughed softly. "No, I have no interest in old rite records. Those stories embarrass everyone."

Brendt's good hand tightened around the rail.

Roan smiled without humor.

Kael had arrived to see who else had looked.

Good.

Now both sides knew the other was counting bloodlines.

They didn't return to the dorm directly. Roan took them through the lower stacks, then the old faculty corridor, then a maintenance stair that smelled of dust and citrus cleaning solvent. Brendt complained twice and stopped after Roan pointed out that complaint volume mapped fatigue.

At the final landing, Selene caught Roan's sleeve. "Vessel-candidate changes how we treat him."

"Yes."

"How?"

"Carefully."

"That isn't an answer."

"It's the only answer until we know whether Kael wants what Vorath wants."

Selene released his sleeve slowly. "You think he might not."

"I think being selected at thirteen produces resentment, loyalty, fanaticism, fracture, or all four in sequence."

Brendt looked uneasy. "Can we maybe not feel bad for the assassin-in-progress?"

"Feeling bad is optional," Roan said. "Understanding isn't."

Ines tucked the copied seal lens into her coat. "That may be the most unpleasantly mature thing anyone has said all week."

Roan ignored her and checked the stairwell reflection. Empty.

Still, the sense of being observed followed them down. Not a gaze, exactly. A pressure. The Vance Pendant remained cool against his chest, and the Codex Shard stayed silent in the way it did when silence meant calculation rather than absence.

By the time they reached the dorm corridor, Roan had written a new line in his head.

*Don't assume Kael is only a hand. Determine whether he's also a wound.*

He added a second line before opening the dorm door.

*If wound, identify who benefits from keeping it open.*

That question mattered more than pity. Pity softened edges and made people sentimental about knives because someone else had once held them by the blade. Roan didn't need pity for Kael. He needed leverage, and leverage began by distinguishing obedience from desire. If Kael wanted Vorath's approval, one trap would work. If he wanted escape, another. If he wanted both, then he was already splitting.

Splits became seams.

Good.
