Chapter 45: The Northern Name

He heard the footsteps before he saw the shadow.

Roan pressed his back against the alley wall and counted. Two sets. Different weight distribution. The heavier one moved with the deliberate cadence of someone who had killed before and expected to kill again. The lighter one followed three paces behind, a follower, not a leader.

He had fourteen seconds before they reached the junction.

The arithmetic resolved cleanly. Two opponents. One exit behind him. His Vitalis read 1,247. Initiate tier, exactly where he wanted it on the public boards. The Tempered reclassification threshold of fifteen hundred sat ahead of him like a line he intended to cross cleanly. The Codex Shard hummed against his spine at a comfortable 3/4 capacity. Enough. Barely.

- break -

The Black Spire courtyard at dusk smelled like iron filings and overcooked protein paste. Roan Vance stood in the third position from the gate, exactly where the ranked seating chart said he belonged, and listened to Instructor Marrek call out the morning sparring assignments.

"Carrow versus Holloway."

Brendt shuffled past him toward the arena, offering a quick nod that communicated *not my problem today*. Roan returned it with a fractional tilt of his chin. They had an understanding. Brendt kept his mouth shut about certain things, and Roan made sure those certain things never became Brendt's concern.

"Cassian versus Vance."

Right.

He walked to the arena floor with the same pace he'd used for every spar since arriving at the Black Spire three months ago. Not slow enough to seem eager. Not fast enough to suggest anxiety. The Vitalis readout embedded in the arena's eastern pillar flashed green as he passed: 1,247. Visible to everyone. Intentionally mundane.

Cassian Aldmere was already waiting.

The Aldmere boy stood at the center mark with his practice blade balanced across both palms, a formal gesture, the kind instructors rewarded with bonus points. He was Hardened tier, eighteen months older than Roan, and carried the brand of confidence that came from growing up in a household where someone had paid for every advantage.

"Sparring to first clean touch," Instructor Marrek announced from the judge's platform. "Match begins on my mark."

Cassian's eyes tracked Roan's approach. There was no hostility in the look, only the mild, impersonal assessment of someone categorizing a future victory. His Vitalis readout pulsed on the pillar: 2,680. More than double.

The mark dropped.

Cassian moved first, which was expected. His opening was a standard Aldmere family pattern, high thrust, baiting an upper block so he could hook the descending arm and pull his opponent off-balance. Textbook. Effective against anyone who had learned to fight from the same textbooks.

Roan blocked at a thirty-degree angle that served no defensive purpose whatsoever. The practice blade glanced off his forearm guard with a clean crack. He staggered backward, half a step, exactly what the hook required. His shoulder dropped. His weight shifted forward.

Cassian committed.

*—The hook catches. The boy pulls. I drop my center just ahead of his pull vector, which means—*

The mathematics of it finished before Cassian understood the bait.

Cassian's blade swept through empty air where Roan's arm had been a heartbeat before. The Aldmere heir's momentum carried him forward, hips overextending by twelve degrees, which was enough. Roan's practice blade tapped the inside of Cassian's forward knee, not hard enough to bruise, hard enough to count.

"Point to Vance."

Instructor Marrek's voice carried the faint undertone of surprise he was too professional to express. Cassian straightened, blinking once, and reset his stance with a tightness around his jaw that Roan catalogued without caring about.

The match continued.

Six exchanges. Each one followed the same pattern. Cassian attacked with increasingly sophisticated combinations drawn from his family's archives. Roan defended with the minimal necessary motion, occasionally counterattacking in ways that scored points without ever seeming like a genuine threat. His Vitalis readout stayed perfectly stable at 1,247. His body language telegraphed effort, concentration, barely-controlled anxiety.

The shape held without needing to be held.

Cassian won the match 7–4. Standard points differential for someone playing below their actual capability against a Hardened-tier opponent who needed to feel like they'd earned the victory. The Aldmere heir walked off the arena floor with his shoulders back, nodding to his section-mates, already composing the version of this story he would tell at dinner.

Roan sat on the bench, towel around his neck, and stared at the ceiling tiles.

Four.

That was the count of students who had looked at his Vitalis readout during the match. Cassian, confirming his advantage before the opening exchange. Instructor Marrek, checking the numbers after each exchange to validate scoring. Sera Cole, the principal's daughter, who sat in the front row and watched everything, reading his stable baseline and dismissing him as a data point. And the fourth.

The fourth had been standing in the equipment alcove, twenty meters back, where the angle of the light made it hard to read the pillar display.

Brendt Holloway.

Brendt had watched the entire match with an expression Roan couldn't parse. Not concern. Not curiosity. Something closer to confirmation, like a man watching a lock open and feeling neither satisfaction nor surprise, only the quiet recognition of a mechanism functioning exactly as designed.

They hadn't spoken about Inversion. Not once in the six weeks since Brendt had walked into the eastern training hall at an hour when no one trained alone, seen the Codex Shard's glow through Roan's shirt, and walked back out without a word. But Brendt had been different since that night. Quieter. More observant. He no longer asked Roan questions about sparring tactics or exam prep. He watched.

*Watching what?*

Roan tightened his jaw.

It didn't matter. Brendt wasn't a problem. The math there was clean. A friend who knew your secret and said nothing was an asset, not a threat, provided the friend lacked the ambition or the leverage to convert that knowledge into something useful. Brendt had neither. His family was comfortably middle-tier, his own Vitalis plateaued at 1,100, and his ambitions extended to graduating in the top third of their cohort and securing a junior Sentinel posting. Small goals. Manageable people. And the Holloway family had a quiet reputation for keeping bargains sealed with silence—Brendt’s father had once held a mercenary contract through two regime changes without a single word leaking. Brendt had inherited that instinct.

Roan folded his towel into a precise rectangle and stood.

*—Cassian Aldmere. The Aldmere family has a standing contract with House Vell for low grade catalyst refining. They purchase approximately 3,000 units per semester from the Spire's approved vendor list. That's a 15% margin on resale. I could undercut them by 8% and still clear 12 credits per transaction if I had access to the supply chain—*

He was three steps toward the locker room when Sera Cole appeared in his path.

She stood exactly where he needed to walk, arms crossed, head tilted at the precise angle that suggested she was running a calculation of her own. Her Vitalis readout pulsed at 3,400, top quartile of their entire cohort, and she was only seventeen. The Cole family produced instructors the way other families produced merchants: reliably, with high expectations attached.

"You dropped your center on his third combination," Sera said. Not a question.

Roan stopped walking.

*She noticed.*

"Your weight shifted left before his hook connected," Sera continued. "The stagger was consistent with insufficient lower-body training. Standard deficiency for Initiate-tier students transitioning to mixed sparring."

She had the tally. She always had the columns.

"Right," Roan said.

"You recovered faster than the stagger warranted." Sera's eyes narrowed by exactly two degrees. "Your reaction time in exchanges four through six averaged less than a blink faster than your first three exchanges. That's a 23% improvement within a single match."

*She noticed that too.*

Roan let a small crease form between his eyebrows, the expression of a student who had been caught out and didn't know why. It was, he reflected, not entirely an act. Sera Cole's analytical precision was genuinely inconvenient.

"Shaved my hair this morning," he said. "The weight difference affects my balance."

Sera held his gaze for a full three seconds.

"Hm," she said, and walked past him toward the arena.

She didn't look back. She didn't need to. The message had been delivered: *I see more than you think I see. I am choosing not to act on it. For now.*

Roan stood in the corridor and listened to her footsteps fade, and calculated. The excuse was a placeholder, not a solution. He added the exchange to a mental column marked *countermeasures*. Tomorrow he would have Brendt casually mention a recovery diet shift—some plausible error in his nutrition schedule that could explain the odd recovery pattern. A secondary narrative for her to find. If she was choosing to wait, he would make sure the wait delivered her a satisfying dead end.

Sera Cole wasn't a problem today. She wasn't even a problem this week, or this month. But the Cole family's relationship with the Sentinel Bureau meant that Sera had access to monitoring protocols, Vitalis verification systems, and, most dangerously, institutional database that could flag statistical anomalies in a student's progression curve.

If she ran a pattern analysis on his sparring data over the next semester, she would find the variance. The way his recovery times tightened when his back was against the wall. The slight acceleration in his strike speed during exchanges that his Vitalis baseline shouldn't support. Small discrepancies. Individually meaningless. Collectively a signature.

He needed to flatten the curve.

*—Three options. One: maintain current performance, accept the statistical drift, hope she loses interest. Probability of exposure over 90 days: 34%. Unacceptable. Two: strategically injure myself during sparring to introduce random variance that masks the inversion-linked improvements. Recovery cost: 8–12 days. Unacceptable, too visible. Three: accelerate publicly. Move from Initiate to Tempered in the next evaluation cycle. A visible, explicable jump that buries the micro-variance in the macro-improvement.*

Option three was the tally.

The next evaluation cycle was nineteen days away. The eastern training hall was empty at 2 AM, which was the point.

Roan sat cross-legged on the mat with his shirt off, the Codex Shard resting against his sternum like a second heartbeat. The black iron absorbed the faint light from the hallway sconce and gave back nothing. No glow. No warmth. Just weight, more weight than the size suggested, a fact he had long since stopped questioning.

He pulled a small vial from his jacket pocket. Wraithmilk Tincture. The label was handwritten: *Impurity: moderate. Market value: 180 credits. Side effect profile: 23% probability of persistent auditory hallucination for 48–72 hours post-consumption. Severity: manageable.*

He had bought the batch from a third-year student who needed tuition money and didn't know what he had. The impurities in this particular batch were the kind that other students avoided, too much variance, too unpredictable. For Roan, variance was just noise in a signal he knew how to read.

*Twenty-three percent chance of hearing voices that aren't there.*

He uncorked the vial.

The Wraithmilk Tincture tasted like copper and ozone. The first swallow hit his stomach and sat there, a cold weight that spread outward through his intestines. By the second swallow the cold had reached his joints, and by the third it was climbing his spine like frost on glass.

He set the vial down and waited.

The Codex Shard warmed against his skin.

[Sidewound detected.]

[Type: Neurologicaltempus. Profile logged.]

[Conversion: 100%.]

[+87 Vitalis.]

The cold in his joints reversed direction. Instead of spreading outward it drew inward, pulling toward his center like water finding a drain, and where it passed the tissue felt, *different*. Tighter. Denser. The sensation wasn't pain, exactly. It was the absence of a looseness he hadn't known he was carrying.

His Vitalis readout climbed: 1,247... 1,261... 1,298... 1,334.

Stable. The Wraithmilk Tincture had pushed him 87 points above his baseline. For a standard Initiate-tier student, that would cost 23 credits at the campus pharmacy. For him, it had cost the vial and a burst of auditory static, barely noticeable in an empty room at that hour.

*Inventory check.*

He had four Wraithmilk vials remaining. Three Hollowstrike lesson slots available from Drogan before the next evaluation. And nineteen days.

Nineteen days to move from Initiate baseline to Tempered classification. Eighteen percent Vitalis improvement minimum required for a clean evaluation. He could hit that with three more Wraithmilk cycles, provided the hallucinations didn't compound, but that was the floor, not the target.

He needed the ceiling.

*The Sevenfold Bleed. First Form.*

The thought arrived without invitation. He pushed it down.

Not yet. The Sevenfold Bleed was a sledgehammer, and he needed a scalpel. The technique's Sidewound profile was dramatic, seven Vitalis channels simultaneously burning would generate enough Vitalis yield to push him past Tempered and halfway to Hardened in a single cycle. But the cost was visible. Blood in the urine for six hours afterward. A tremor in his dominant hand that took two days to fully resolve. Drogan had warned him: *The Sevenfold Bleed isn't a training technique. It's a controlled catastrophe. You use it when the alternative is worse.*

This wasn't that moment.

The alternative was nineteen days of careful, methodical improvement. Pills bought from students who didn't know their value. Techniques practiced in empty rooms at 2 AM. Numbers nudged upward by percentages that seemed trivial until they compounded.

*Patience.*

He stood, pulled his shirt back on, and felt the Codex Shard settle into its familiar position against his spine. The weight was comfortable now. Almost familiar.

Brendt had seen the glow. Sera Cole had seen the figures. The Aldmere heir had felt his blade meet resistance that didn't match the readout and chosen not to examine it closely, because examining it would require admitting he'd been outmaneuvered by someone he had categorized as beneath notice.

All of it within acceptable parameters.

*For now.*

He turned off the light and walked back through the dark corridor toward his dormitory, footsteps measured, mind already running the calculations for tomorrow's training session. If he pushed his sparring intensity to 68% of actual capability, up from the current 61%, he could claim the improvement was anxiety-related underperformance correcting itself. It would cost him two matches he would have otherwise won, but the statistical narrative would be cleaner.

Sera Cole was a variable. He would need to introduce a second variable to compensate. A distraction. A competing pattern for her to chase.

*Find someone whose numbers are worse than mine.*

The corridor opened onto the courtyard. The sky above the Black Spire was the color of old iron, stars swallowed by the city's ambient glow. Somewhere in the dormitory wing, Brendt was sleeping with a book open on his chest, the habit of a man who read before bed and never remembered the pages. Somewhere in the east wing, Sera Cole was running her evening analysis protocols, building models, searching for the pattern.

And somewhere in the faculty quarters, Instructor Marrek was filing his sparring report. *Cassian Aldmere defeats Vance, 7–4. Consistent with tier differential. No anomalies flagged.*

No anomalies flagged.

Roan walked up the dormitory stairs, counting each step. Fourteen to the second landing. Eight to his room. Door on the left. Bed on the right. Small window facing the courtyard where the evening shift posted their training scores on a public board.

He sat on the edge of the bed and looked at the board through the glass.

His name wasn't on it. Vance didn't rank high enough to appear on the evening board, not yet.

*Soon.*

He lay back and closed his eyes, and the tally kept running in the dark behind his eyelids like a river that never stopped.

*19 days. 87 Vitalis per Wraithmilk cycle. Three cycles remaining before impurities become unmanageable. Sevenfold Bleed at day 14 as a forcing function. Target: 1,580 Vitalis by evaluation. Minimum threshold for Tempered reclassification: 1,500. Buffer: 80 points.*

*Margin of error: thin.*

*But thin margins are where I operate.*

Sleep came without fanfare, and he dreamed of nothing, which was the best kind of dream, because nothing meant no hallucinations, no inversions without complications. Just the quiet dark of a mind running its calculations in the background, solving problems it wouldn't encounter for another nineteen days.

But even in the quiet dark, he held the purpose. The Northern name wasn't a title he claimed. It was a debt he was building the capacity to collect.
