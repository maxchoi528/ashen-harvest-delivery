Chapter 198: Ironholm Rooftop

End of term lasted eleven seconds, which was four seconds longer than Roan expected.

The Headmaster's office didn't panic well.

That wasn't an insult. It was a structural observation. Cole's office had been designed to impress and intimidate, plus survive assassins, but it hadn't been designed for a sentence like *Aldermere Academy just reported a ward anomaly in their historical gallery. It concerns a portrait of Vorath Greel.*

Most rooms reacted to danger by becoming noisy. People shouted. Wards chimed. Assistants appeared with tablets and expressions administrators used when pretending their procedures had anticipated something they very much hadn't.

Cole's office did the opposite.

The windows darkened by two shades. The ceiling array sealed with a soft click. A suppressive field settled over the floor, not strong enough to hinder movement, just enough to discourage sudden Vitalis output. The desk swallowed the open slate into a recessed compartment. Three wall panels shifted by less than a finger-width, exposing no weapons Roan could see, which meant the weapons were either better hidden than expected or the panels were bait. Bait.

Useful office.

Roan closed his notebook.

Lyra.

The name didn't need to be said, but saying nothing didn't make it less present. It sat in the room like a glass dropped on stone, still intact only because everyone was watching it fall in slow motion.

Drogan pushed off the bookshelf. "How old is the report?"

Cole looked down at the message line, though Roan suspected he had already read the timestamp three times. "Just under five minutes. Aldermere security flagged a historical ward disturbance at nineteen-fourteen local. Their duty archivist delayed escalation because the portrait has triggered minor false positives before. The escalation moved to me because the name Greel was attached to an existing protective advisory."

"Minor false positives," Selene repeated.

Her voice had gone very even. Not calm. Even. House training did that. It polished panic until it reflected other people's faces instead.

Cole's mouth tightened. "Aldermere's phrasing. Not mine."

Roan stood still because moving would have wasted time. The first impulse was simple: leave the room, get to Aldermere, remove Lyra from the gallery, destroy the portrait, and bill Vorath's estate for the inconvenience after death made collection difficult.

Simple impulses were rarely optimal.

Aldermere was outside Black Spire jurisdiction. Travel time from Ironholm under normal conditions: seven hours by express wardline, three by emergency aerial courier with authorization Cole might obtain if he wanted three governments and two academy boards asking why a Forged first-year was being moved toward a ward anomaly connected to a Throne-tier foreign magnate. Direct action created a visible route. Visible routes could be blocked, observed, or used as bait.

Vorath had just failed to break Roan directly.

Attacking Lyra was obvious. Too obvious, unless the obviousness was the point.

Roan looked at Cole. "Who has eyes on her?"

"Aldermere campus security. Dormitory floor guardian. Historical gallery staff. I am requesting a senior warder now."

"Requesting."

"I don't command Aldermere."

"Then ask someone who does."

Cole's gaze sharpened. "I am."

Good. Not enough.

Roan turned to Selene. "Ardent contacts at Aldermere."

"Two instructors, one assistant bursar, one family scholarship officer," she said immediately.

Prepared. Good.

"Use the scholarship officer. Administrators move faster when tuition optics are threatened."

"Already drafting."

Her phone was in her hand before she finished speaking.

Drogan watched Roan. "You're not running."

"Not yet."

"Good."

"Don't sound surprised."

"Then stop surprising me."

Roan ignored that and opened his own phone.

He had three secure channels with Lyra. One official, monitored badly by Aldermere's student safety systems. One private, set up by Brendt after the first tournament threats. One emergency-only, routed through two dead commercial relays and a Greycross repair shop account Mira didn't know was still active.

He used the official channel first.

Not because it was safe. Because anyone watching expected him to avoid it. Public channels made useful bait when paired with private action.

The call connected on the fourth pulse.

Lyra answered with her camera angled too low, showing the edge of her chin, a blue Aldermere scarf, and the blurred ceiling of what looked like a corridor. Her breathing came too fast, then hitched as she tried to control it.

"Roan?"

Several things in the room changed at once. Drogan looked away, granting privacy without leaving. Cole's fingers paused above his slate. Selene stopped typing for half a second, then resumed faster.

Roan kept his voice flat. "Where are you standing?"

Lyra swallowed. The camera jerked, showing polished stone, a brass plaque, then her shoes. "East hall. Outside the gallery. They told us to wait while security checks the—"

"Who is with you?"

"Mara and Professor Ellan. Two ward guards. I think more are coming. Roan, why is Headmaster Kellan's office asking for my dorm location?"

Aldermere had received Cole's advisory. Good.

"Because bureaucracy gets lonely. Are you hurt?"

"No."

Too fast.

"Lyra."

She exhaled through her nose. "No blood. No burns. My ears rang when the portrait moved."

Cole's eyes lifted.

Roan didn't look away from the screen. "Moved how?"

"It didn't move like a person. The paint shifted." Her mouth tightened. "The man in it looked at me."

The suppressive field in Cole's office felt suddenly thin.

Roan's spine warmed.

[Remote resonance report detected.]

[Hostile image-anchor suspected.]

[Lineage proximity vector: collateral.]

Collateral.

The word entered the ledger and immediately demanded blood.

Roan didn't move.

"Describe the room," he said.

"What?"

"Room. Exits. People. Distance from portrait. Don't look back at it."

Lyra's eyes flicked off-screen, then stopped. Good. She had almost looked and corrected herself. "Long hall. Marble floor. Two rows of portraits. The Vorath one was near the north wall, under a bronze lamp. I'm outside now. Gallery doors behind me are closed. Two guards at the door. Professor Ellan is arguing with someone. Mara is crying quietly, but she does that when scared, not when injured."

"Useful distinction."

"I learned from you."

"Then learn the next part faster. Don't say his name again. Don't describe his face aloud. Don't let anyone record you describing it."

Lyra's face changed. Fear, yes, but also focus settling over it. She was fourteen, not stupid. Roan had spent most of her life making sure those two conditions didn't become confused.

"Why?"

"Because if the portrait reacted to you, speech may be a second trigger."

"That's insane."

"Yes."

"That's not you disagreeing."

"Correct."

The corner of her mouth twitched despite everything. It vanished quickly.

Cole muted his own slate, then held up three fingers. Three minutes until Aldermere senior staff joined. Or three layers of jurisdiction now involved. With Cole it could be either.

Roan switched the call to audio-only.

Lyra made a sharp sound. "Why did you turn off video?"

"Because images can be doors."

Silence.

Then, smaller, "Roan."

"I am here."

"That isn't the same thing."

No. It wasn't.

The office gave him no useful answer. Neither did the skyline beyond the darkened glass. Ironholm spread beneath Black Spire in winter lights and ward towers, each blue beam pretending civilization was a solved problem.

Roan hated distance. Not emotionally. Emotion was imprecise. He hated distance because it converted action into requests. Requests required other people. Other people had response times, hidden incentives, and inconvenient bodies.

"Listen carefully," he said. "You are going to do four things. First, keep your eyes on the floor until a senior warder confirms the gallery is sealed. Second, don't let anyone separate you from Professor Ellan unless they give you a written order and you send me a picture of it without faces. Third, move to a room with no portraits, mirrors, polished windows, ceremonial shields, or water basins. Fourth, switch to emergency channel after this call ends and keep the phone in your sleeve, not your hand."

"My sleeve?"

"Hands get searched. Sleeves get forgotten."

"That isn't comforting knowledge."

"It's useful knowledge."

"I know." She breathed once, forcing the rhythm flat. "Mara is looking at me like I'm having a criminal lesson."

"Tell Mara to learn too."

A muffled voice from Lyra's end said something Roan couldn't make out.

Lyra repeated, "Mara says thank you and also what the hell."

"Acceptable order."

Drogan's mouth twitched. Selene didn't look up, but her typing rhythm changed in a way that suggested she had nearly smiled. Cole looked profoundly tired for a man who had invited some of this by keeping a school full of weaponized teenagers.

"Roan," Lyra said, quieter now. "I didn't know it was him at first. The portrait plaque said Northern Crown Benefactor, not Greel. Then the paint changed and the beard—"

"Stop."

She stopped.

Good.

The Codex Shard pulsed with a heat that didn't reach his skin.

[Alias confirmed: Northern Crown Benefactor.]

[Historical image-anchor likely pre-positioned.]

[Transmission path uncertain.]

Roan wrote the phrase in the margin of his notebook.

Northern Crown Benefactor.

Public history was a weapon people left lying in museums and called education.

"Did it speak?" Roan asked.

Lyra hesitated.

Every useful answer had a cost. Hesitation identified the cost before language dressed it up.

"Not out loud," she said.

Cole's face went still.

Roan's voice didn't change. "How, then?"

"I heard a word in my head. Maybe not a word. It was like someone pressed meaning against my teeth." She swallowed. "It said *found*."

The room disappeared to a narrower set of variables.

Found.

Not Roan. Lyra.

Maybe because of bloodline proximity. Maybe because the portrait had been built to notice Vance-line contact. Maybe because Vorath had seeded recognizers across academies decades ago, waiting for Aldric's children, debts, students, or mistakes to walk close enough.

The old world had loved traps that aged well.

Roan preferred traps with expiration dates. Easier accounting.

"You aren't found," he said. "You are tagged. Tags can be removed."

Lyra gave a short laugh with no humor in it. "That's your reassurance?"

"Yes."

"It's terrible."

"It's accurate."

"That helps," she said after a moment, surprising him. "A little."

Roan adjusted Lyra's threat category upward. Not because she was in more danger. Because she handled the danger better than his preferred model had allowed. Models that underestimated family got people killed.

"Professor Ellan is waving at me," Lyra said. "Senior warder is here. Tall woman. Silver badge. Looks angry."

"Name?"

A pause. "Warder Ilyas."

Cole's hand moved over his slate. A confirmation blinked green.

"Ilyas is reputable," Cole said.

Roan nodded once. "Put her on."

Lyra's audio rustled. A woman's voice came through, clipped and irritated in the way competent people often sounded when forced to explain themselves to strangers during emergencies.

"This is Senior Warder Mara Ilyas of Aldermere Academy. Who am I speaking with?"

"Roan Vance."

Another pause.

Shorter than expected.

"Black Spire's problem child," she said.

"Depends on which memo you received."

"All of them, apparently." Paper shifted. "Your sister is physically unharmed. The gallery has been sealed. The portrait is under a blind cover and a dampening cage. We have no current evidence of a live conduit."

"Current evidence."

"I am not in the habit of lying to worried relatives, even difficult ones."

Reasonable. Perhaps useful.

"Was the portrait one of yours?"

"No. Donation piece. Northern cultural exchange set, acquired twenty-nine years ago."

Twenty-nine years.

After Aldric came to Earth. Before his death. During the years his enemies would've been searching for everything he had touched.

"Remove it," Roan said.

"I will isolate it. Destruction requires board authorization."

"Then isolate the board near it and wait."

Cole closed his eyes.

Warder Ilyas made a sound that might've been a cough hiding a laugh. "I understand the sentiment."

"It was a suggestion."

"A bad one."

"Only legally."

"Your sister will be moved to a warded dormitory suite without reflective surfaces until we understand why the artifact responded. She won't be questioned without faculty present, and I will personally review any request to access her. Does that satisfy you?"

"No."

"I appreciate honesty. What would satisfy you?"

"Vorath Greel's head in an evidence bag."

Drogan's gaze flicked to him.

Cole's office remained quiet.

Ilyas was silent for a second. "That's outside Aldermere's current response capacity."

"Then do the suite."

"Done."

"No portraits."

"No portraits."

"No mirrors."

"No mirrors."

"No ceremonial shields."

A pause. "You have had unusual experiences."

"Yes."

"No ceremonial shields. Anything else?"

"No old water."

"Define old water."

"If it has been sitting in a basin, fountain, sealed decorative vessel, ceremonial bowl, or ancient kettle, remove it."

"Ancient kettle," she repeated.

"Especially those."

"I am writing that down against my professional judgment."

"Professional judgment improves with survival."

Ilyas exhaled. "Your sister wants the phone back."

"One more thing."

"Of course."

"Don't use her full name near the artifact."

This time the silence lasted longer.

"Why?"

"Names are handles."

"Is that theory or experience?"

"Yes."

"I hate that answer."

"So do I."

The phone rustled again. Lyra returned. "Roan?"

"Emergency channel in thirty seconds. Sleeve. Floor. No names. No faces."

"I heard." She paused. "Are you coming?"

There were lies that comforted. There were lies that bought time. There were lies that trained people to wait for rescue instead of moving.

Roan avoided the third kind.

"Not immediately," he said. "If I move toward you now, whatever is watching may learn more about how we protect you. Cole and Selene are moving pressure through channels faster than I can travel. Warder Ilyas is competent. You are safest by making yourself boring and sealed, then hard to discuss."

Lyra was quiet.

Then, "That's a very Roan way to say no."

"Yes."

"I hate it."

"Also yes."

Another pause.

"But you're right?"

"Probably."

"That's not comforting."

"Comfort is Mira's department. Mine is error reduction."

Lyra laughed once. It came out thin, but real enough to count.

"Fine," she said. "Error reduce me."

Roan's fingers tightened around the phone for half a second. He released before the casing cracked.

"Don't look at him," he said. "Don't talk about him. I'll handle it."

"You always say that."

"I am often correct."

"Often isn't always."

"Improving."

"Roan."

"Yes?"

Her voice dropped. "I saw the picture change before anyone else. Mara thought I was imagining it. Then the ward alarm went off. I wasn't."

"I know."

"No, I need you to understand. It looked at me like it recognized me."

The Codex Shard pressed heat against bone.

Roan looked at his reflection in the darkened office window. The glass showed a seventeen-year-old boy with too little sleep, too many enemies, and a room full of adults waiting to see which direction his violence would take.

The reflection was incomplete.

Good.

"Then it made the first mistake," he said.

"Which is?"

"It let you know."

Lyra was silent for a moment.

"That sounded less reassuring than you think."

"It wasn't meant for you."

"Who was it meant for?"

Roan looked at the sealed skyline beyond the window.

"Anyone listening."

He ended the official call.

For three seconds no one spoke. Then Cole said, "There are therapists at both academies who would pay to study your definition of reassurance."

"They can invoice Vorath."

"I suspect they would prefer living clients."

Selene looked up from her phone. "Ardent scholarship office has acknowledged. They're contacting Aldermere's deputy head and requesting a written safety protocol. I phrased it as concern over donor confidence."

"Good," Roan said.

"It felt disgusting."

"Also good. Disgust means the wording was credible."

Drogan walked to the window. "Vorath found the family line."

"No," Roan said.

Drogan turned.

"He found a signal," Roan said. "There's a difference. Signals can be misdirected."

Cole rubbed the bridge of his nose. "I am almost afraid to ask what that means."

"Then don't."

"Vance."

Roan slipped his phone into his pocket and opened the emergency channel with Lyra. A single coded ping came back: three taps, pause, one tap. Alive. Moving. Compliant.

Good.

He wrote: *Keep sleeve. Count exits. Breathe through nose.*

Lyra replied: *Mara says stop giving field instructions to minors.*

Roan typed: *Mara can send better ones.*

A five-second delay.

Then: *Mara says rude but fair.*

Roan closed the phone.

"What are you going to do?" Cole asked.

Roan considered lying, then discarded it. Cole had placed resources on the board. He deserved partial truth. Partial truth was cheaper than future misunderstanding.

"I am going to make the portrait less useful as bait."

"How?"

"By deciding what Vorath thinks I should do and not doing that."

"Which is?"

"Run to Aldermere. Expose route. Split protection. Enter a jurisdiction where Black Spire's shield weakens and his old cultural donations become terrain."

Cole's expression shifted by a fraction. "You think this wasn't an attack on your sister."

"It was an attack on my sister," Roan said. "But not primarily to hurt her. Primarily to move me. Hurting her remains an acceptable secondary outcome for him."

Drogan's hands closed at his sides.

Selene's face went cold.

Cole said nothing.

Roan appreciated that. People often interrupted clean analysis because they disliked how clean it sounded. Reality didn't improve when described gently.

"So," Cole said finally, "you stay in Ironholm."

"Tonight."

"And tomorrow?"

Roan looked toward the north, though the office windows faced west.

His father's cairn stood in Greycross cemetery, a stone marker over an empty grave. The coat buried beneath it had no body. The name carved into it had become more useful after death than it had been permitted to be in life.

Vorath had offered him a card in the café. A polite rectangle of heavy stock bearing the Greel crest, a private number, and arrogance rich men folded into stationery.

Roan had kept it.

He hadn't yet decided whether that had been instinct or calculation.

Now he knew.

The cairn didn't answer, which put it ahead of most living authorities.

Roan had visited it often enough as a child to know which rituals belonged to Mira and which had belonged to him only because he had been too young to refuse. Mira brought flowers on the anniversary. White ones when money allowed, whatever the market discounted when it didn't. Lyra had once left a drawing of the four of them under a stone and asked whether rain counted as delivery. Roan had stood beside them with his hands in his pockets and tried to decide whether talking to a stone was kinder or stupider than saying nothing.

He had eventually chosen maintenance. Cleaning the moss from the carved letters at twelve, tracking the drunk who damaged the row at fourteen, stopping his visits at sixteen when Mira's attention grew too sharp to withstand without giving away something he hadn't yet named.

Now he came with a dead man's enemy burned into his pocket and a relic in his spine that treated family grief like a permissions system.

Progression, apparently.

"Tomorrow," he said, "I settle a small account."

Cole closed his eyes for one second. "Is this account likely to create international consequences?"

"Not immediately."

"That answer was worse than yes."

"Then ask narrower questions."

Drogan gave him a long look.

Roan met it.

The old instructor understood before the others did. His expression tightened, not in objection, but in recognition. The cemetery. The cairn. Aldric's empty grave.

Drogan didn't tell him not to go.

That was either trust or exhaustion.

Acceptable either way.

Roan walked toward the office door.

Cole's voice stopped him. "Vance."

Roan looked back.

"If Aldermere reports further movement, I will notify you immediately."

"No," Roan said. "Notify Drogan and Selene too. Single-point alerts are failure points."

Cole looked as if he disliked receiving security advice from a student almost as much as he disliked agreeing with it.

"Done."

Roan left.

He didn't go straight to his dorm.

Dormitories were for sleeping, lying to resident monitors, and letting enemies assume one had retreated into a predictable container. Roan used them when useful. Tonight, useful meant height.

Black Spire had exterior observation terraces on every administrative tier. Students called them balconies because students were optimistic and bad at architecture. The one below Cole's office jutted from the western face of the tower, open to wind and snow, shielded by a waist-high wall and three layers of anti-fall wards. It was high enough to see Ironholm's old districts and low enough that the Spire didn't classify standing there as an aviation hazard.

Roan stepped onto it and let the door seal behind him.

The city spread beneath him in cold grids. Blue ward beams marked the campus perimeter. Red traffic lights moved along the elevated tramlines. Farther east, Greycross blurred under industrial haze, too stubborn to look peaceful even from a distance. North, the mountains were only a dark pressure under cloud. Somewhere beyond that line, Suthmere waited. Somewhere south-east, Aldermere held Lyra in a room stripped of mirrors because a portrait had spoken without using a mouth.

Distance again.

He put both hands on the stone rail and counted practical options until the first emotional answer lost shape. Aerial courier: fast, visible, interceptable. Secure shuttle: slower, logged, politically noisy. Covert route: possible, but would require Ines, an aircraft, and three crimes she would enjoy too much. Remote pressure: immediate, distributed, hard to block.

Remote pressure won.

He sent four messages in order: one to Mira, stripped of details but clear enough to stop her from calling every hospital in Ironholm; one to Brendt, asking for a clean copy of the emergency relay map; one to Ines, offering salvage priority on non-Codex historical artifacts in exchange for any record of Northern Crown Benefactor donations; one to an old Greycross number that still belonged to Dren's courier network.

The last message contained only three words.

*Find white paintings.*

Dren would complain. Then he would look. He liked money more than comfort, which made him reliable in the narrow ways that mattered.

Roan watched the messages leave and waited for the Codex to object.

It didn't.

That was almost approval. Or the Shard had learned that arguing over logistics wasted energy. Personal growth came in strange forms when one was dealing with a spine-bound relic.

A thin snowflake landed on the back of his hand and melted. For a moment the water traced a line across the tendons where Sidewound's grip had opened his palm in the Rift. Old wound, new scar.

The emergency channel pinged once in his sleeve. Lyra had complied.

Roan released a breath he hadn't scheduled.

Then he went back inside before anyone could mistake the pause for sentiment.

Outside the office, Black Spire's upper corridor was empty and polished, then too quiet. The suppressive field fell away behind him. His spine warmed again, then steadied under the pendant's cold weight.

[Hostile image-anchor identified.]

[Collateral vector: Lyra Vance.]

[Directive: protect collateral asset.]

"Asset," he said softly.

The hallway didn't answer.

The Codex Shard pulsed once, as if reconsidering the word and deciding not to apologize.

Roan resumed walking.

Protect asset was correct. Incomplete, but correct.

Tomorrow morning, he would go to a grave with no body and burn the calling card of a man who had just made his second mistake.

The first had been letting Lyra know.

The second had been reminding Roan that revenge wasn't an emotional project.

It was logistics.
