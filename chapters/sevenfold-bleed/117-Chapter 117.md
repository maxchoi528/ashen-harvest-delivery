Chapter 117: Afterimage

The message arrived at dawn.

Roan found it pinned to his door with a black iron dart, the kind used by the Rift Hunters Guild for official summons. The parchment bore the wax seal of House Vell, and the handwriting inside belonged to the Vell patriarch himself.

*Vance. The Syndicate has questions about your settlement. Third bell. The Hollow Keg. Come alone.*

He read it twice, then a third time.

The Hollow Keg was a mid-tier tavern in the Outer Wards, neutral ground where disputes got settled over ale and blade-work. Calling it a summons instead of a challenge was diplomatic language for: *we know you owe us, and we're done waiting.*

The equation left little room for comfort. Three months ago, Roan had purchased a batch of Hollow Marrow Elixirs from a Vell subsidiary, eighteen vials, heavily discounted because they were expired and considered unstable. He'd inverted every single one, turning trash into treasure. The subsidiary had gone bankrupt six weeks later when the actual buyers figured out the market had been flooded.

House Vell traced the supply chain back to Roan within a month. They'd been patient. Three months of patience, which meant they'd been building leverage.

Brendt appeared at the corner of the hallway, already dressed in his mission gear. "You look like someone just dropped a debt notice on your door."

"That's exactly what it is." Roan folded the parchment. "House Vell wants a meeting. One hour."

"The Hollow Keg? That's their territory." Brendt's jaw tightened. "That's not a negotiation. That's a collection."

"I know."

"I'm coming with you."

"No." Roan's voice carried no room for argument. "This is my debt. My problem. If you show up, they'll use you as leverage before the first round of drinks arrives."

Brendt wanted to argue. The pressure in his shoulders said he wanted to argue for the next ten minutes. But he'd learned, over the past year, when Roan was immovable.

"One hour," Brendt said. "If you're not back by the second bell—"

"I'll be back."

He wasn't sure that was true. But saying it helped Brendt stay behind.

\- break -

The Hollow Keg sat on the corner of Rustmarket and Ironbone Alley, its facade stained with decades of smoke and old blood. The building itself was a converted warehouse, two stories tall, with barred windows on the ground level and a single door reinforced with sheet iron. Nothing about it said *welcome*. Everything about it said *you've made a mistake coming here.*

Roan pushed through the door at the third bell.

The interior was exactly what he expected: long tables, smoke-thick air, and a dozen faces that turned toward him with predatory interest. Most were Forged or Hardened, dressed in the leather-and-chain fashion of lower-tier bounty hunters. The kind who worked for whoever paid and didn't ask questions about ethics.

The Vell patriarch sat at the back table, flanked by two bodyguards.

He was a thick man in his forties, with a face that had been ugly since birth and had grown uglier with power. He wore his Vitalis like a costume—heavy rings on every finger, chains around his neck, a coat lined with iron plates. He looked like he was trying to convince everyone he was tougher than he was.

That was a mistake Roan filed away. People who over-compensated had gaps.

"Vance." The Vell patriarch's voice carried across the room without shouting. He didn't need to shout. "Come. Sit."

Roan crossed the room. He took the chair across from the Vell patriarch, not the one beside him. Positioning mattered. If he'd sat beside the Vell patriarch, he'd have been subordinate. Across put them as equals.

A serving girl appeared with two tankards. She set one in front of each of them and vanished without a word. The ale smelled like cheap grain and cheaper ambitions.

"You've been busy," the Vell patriarch said. He wasn't talking about the last three months. "I've been reading reports. The Greycross prodigy. Beat a Vell-sponsored student in the Northern Trials. Took first place in the junior Rift hunting rankings."

"Lucky streaks."

"Lucky." The Vell patriarch smiled. It didn't reach his eyes. "That's one word for it. The word *I* keep hearing is *suspicious*. How does a Tempered-tier student consistently outperform Hardened competitors? How does someone from a fallen House take first place in sanctioned trials?"

"Lots of training."

"Lots of *something*." The Vell patriarch leaned back. "My nephew competed against you. Dorian Vell. You broke three of his ribs."

Dorian Vell was a name Roan recalled with clinical precision. A spoiled teenager with more money than sense, who had underestimated Roan's blade work and paid for it. "It was a legal match. The officials ruled it clean."

"Legal. Yes. Everything you do seems legal." The Vell patriarch picked up his tankard and drank. "My nephew spent four months in recovery. Four months of therapy. Four months of watching his competitors advance while he was stuck in a medical ward."

"He chose to fight. I chose to win. That's not a crime."

"No. It's a pattern." The Vell patriarch set the tankard down. "And now there's this business with our subsidiary. Eighteen vials of Hollow Marrow Elixir. Sold at a discount. Then sold again, flooding a market we controlled. The subsidiary went under. Dozens of jobs lost. Families ruined."

Roan kept his face neutral. "I bought those vials legally. From a licensed dealer."

"You bought expired medication that should've been disposed of. Instead, you—what was the word? *Reconditioned* it—and sold it back into circulation at prices that undercut our entire supply chain." The Vell patriarch's voice dropped. "Do you know what happens to people who damage House Vell interests?"

"They get hurt?"

"They get *corrected*." The Vell patriarch gestured to his bodyguards. Neither moved. "But I'm a reasonable man. You've got talent. Potential. You could go far, if you made the right friends. So I'm offering you a way out."

"Go on."

"Work for me. One year. You complete five missions for House Vell. Rift operations, mostly, the kind we can't put our name on officially. After that, your debt to the subsidiary is cleared. We forget about the market manipulation. And you walk away with a letter of recommendation from a Vell scion."

Roan let the offer settle. "That's a good deal."

"I'm generous."

"You're fishing." Roan's fingers drummed once on the table. "You want to know how I restore expired medications. You want to replicate the process. Build your own subsidiary. Corner the market on reconditioned goods."

The Vell patriarch didn't flinch. "You're paranoid."

"I'm observant." Roan met his gaze. "You didn't come here to settle a debt. You came here to recruit me. The debt is just the leash."

Silence settled between them. The Vell patriarch broke it first. "You're smarter than I expected. That's good. Smarter people are more useful." He leaned forward. "I'm not going to pretend otherwise. What you do, this *reconditioning*, it's useful. The Guild doesn't have the resources to handle half the expired stock that comes through Ironholm. If someone could reliably restore it, process it, resell it..."

"You'd corner a market nobody else knew existed."

"We'd corner it *together*." The Vell patriarch spread his hands. "Fifty-fifty split on all profits. House Vell provides capital and connections, plus legal cover. You provide the technique. We split the revenue. And your debt disappears overnight."

The arithmetic was tempting. Fifty percent of a profitable black market still beat zero percent of nothing. And having House Vell's backing would make the next year significantly easier.

But there was a cost. The Vell patriarch knew too much already. He'd researched Roan's background, tracked down the subsidiary incident, built a profile. If Roan worked for him, he'd be surrendering control—becoming a tool instead of an operator.

"I want sixty percent," Roan said.

The Vell patriarch's eyebrows rose. "That's greedy."

"That's my price." Roan leaned back. "You want my technique, you pay for it. Fifty-fifty means I work for you. Sixty means we negotiate as partners."

"You're forgetting who called this meeting. You're the one who owes money."

"I'm also the one with the skill. You can find other alchemists. You can't find other people who can restore expired medications at that rate." Roan's voice was flat. "The math doesn't change just because you have muscle."

The Vell patriarch studied him.

Then he laughed. It was a genuine sound, surprised, almost delighted. "You're either very confident or very stupid."

"I'm very good at calculating risk-to-reward ratios." Roan stood. "Sixty percent, my terms, or I walk. Your debt stays unpaid, your leverage stays useless, and you spend the next year trying to find someone else who can do what I do."

"You think I'm bluffing about hurting you?"

"No." Roan's hand rested on the table, near his blade. "I think you came here looking for a profitable investment, not a fight. If you wanted to hurt me, you'd have ambushed me outside the tavern, not invited me in for ale."

The Vell patriarch's smile widened. "You're smarter than I expected."

They negotiated for another hour.

By the end, they had an agreement: sixty-two percent to Roan, thirty-eight to House Vell, with a twelve-month contract. Roan would handle all reconditioning for Vell-affiliated subsidiaries. In exchange, his debt was cleared, he received a letter of credit for fifty thousand Spire Credits, and House Vell would stop investigating his methods.

It was a win. Not clean, not total, but a win.

Roan walked out of the Hollow Keg as the sun reached its apex, feeling the weight of the contract like chains around his ankles. He'd bought himself time, but he'd also painted a target on his back. If House Vell decided to betray him, they'd have legal grounds.

And there was the other problem. The Vell patriarch's nephew, Dorian Vell, was still recovering from their match at the Northern Trials. The Vell patriarch would remember that. Would bring it up at an inconvenient moment.

Brendt was waiting outside the gate when Roan emerged. "You're alive."

"For now." Roan handed him a copy of the contract. "Read this. Tell me if I missed anything."

Brendt scanned the document, his expression shifting from concern to something darker. "You made a deal with House Vell."

"I made a deal that keeps them from making a deal with someone worse." Roan started walking. "The Vell patriarch wants to use me. I want to use him. Neither of us trusts the other. That's fine. Trust is expensive. Leverage is cheap."

"This puts you in his debt."

"No. This puts us in *business*. There's a difference." Roan's jaw tightened. "House Vell has resources. Contacts. Access to materials I can't get elsewhere. If I play this right, I turn this contract into a profit center instead of a leash."

"And if you play it wrong?"

"Then I deal with the consequences." He stopped walking, turned to face Brendt. "I need you to do something for me. Find out everything you can about Dorian Vell. Medical records, recovery timeline, recent activities. If he's still angry about the match, he'll try something. I want to see it coming."

Brendt nodded. "I'll ask around."

"Thank you."

They walked back toward Black Spire in silence. The contract was signed, the deal was done, and Roan had just made himself more useful—and more vulnerable—than he'd been an hour ago.

The Vell patriarch had called him smart. Roan wasn't sure that was a compliment.

Smart people got used. Smart people got targeted. Smart people made the right enemies at the wrong time.

But the alternative was worse. The alternative was watching House Vell burn his reputation to the ground, blacklist him from half the markets in Ironholm, and force him to fight his way through every barrier they put up.

So he'd made a deal. Signed a contract. Painted a target.

The figures said it was the right call.

The figures said a lot of things. Sometimes the tally was incomplete.

He could only hope this was one of the times it was right.

Back in his room, Roan set the contract on the desk and let his Vitalis settle into a controlled cycling rhythm. The negotiation had been clean—he'd read the patriarch's tells, held his ground, extracted favorable terms—but the exposure was a problem he couldn't invert away. House Vell now had a formal window into his methods. The patriarch had seen enough to know the reconditioning wasn't alchemy. He'd seen enough to be curious.

Curious people dug. Digging people got buried.

Roan unclenched his jaw and let his breath cycle through a slow Tempered rhythm. The numbers still held. The contract had a non-compete clause locked to reconditioned goods only, and the definition was narrow enough to slip through. He'd written the loophole himself, buried in the addendum, and the Vell patriarch hadn't caught it.

That was the difference between power and precision. Power came in rings and chains and iron-plated coats. Precision came in knowing where to press.

Before he could sit, a folded note slid under the door.

He picked it up. The seal was Drogan's.

*Warden Hale requests your presence at the Veyrn Sanctum. Immediately. Bring your blade.*

No explanation. No context. Just the summons.

Roan read it twice, feeling the timing settle into his ribs like a blade point. Hours after he signed with House Vell. Hours after he'd painted a larger target on his back.

It could be coincidence. Drogan had summoned him before—training sessions, debriefings, the occasional urgent matter. This could be routine.

But the phrasing was wrong. *Bring your blade* meant trouble.

He gathered his equipment, strapped the sheath across his back, and felt the familiar weight settle against his spine. Whatever waited for him at the Sanctum, he wouldn't face it unprepared.

The arithmetic of the day had been clean. The arithmetic of the night was still unwritten.
