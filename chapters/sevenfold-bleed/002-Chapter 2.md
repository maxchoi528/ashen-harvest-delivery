Chapter 2: Don't Get Caught Again

Six years before the dead man's teeth, before the count of six, before the Codex Shard learned to spell his name. Roan Vance was eleven, and the chair was too big.

That was the first thing he noticed, not the fluorescent light buzzing overhead, not the smell of disinfectant and recycled air, not the uniformed woman behind the desk who'd stopped talking to her screen and started talking to him. The chair. He sat in it like a man drowning in furniture, feet dangling above the floor, and he thought about the physics of it. Leverage. Center of gravity. The way his mother's kitchen stool had a lower step because Lyra couldn't reach the counter otherwise.

"Roan Vance." The woman. Officer something, badge number 7-3-4, read from her terminal. "Age eleven. Caught on camera entering the Ashworth residence at 14:23 yesterday. Recovered property includes one Vitalis resistance band, grade two, and one manual dexterity sphere set. Estimated value: forty-eight Spire Credits."

She said it like a verdict.

Roan said nothing. His hands stayed on the armrests. Not fidgeting. Just present.

"Your guardian has been contacted. He'll be here within the hour." She paused, waiting for something, a tear, a protest, a child's excuse. When none came, she returned to her screen.

An hour. He'd calculated the walk from their apartment. A short walk at adult pace. Shorter if the lights at Foundry Junction were kind. His father hadn't walked here. He'd run.

That was interesting.

Roan catalogued the room. Two exits: front desk, back hallway marked RESTRICTED. One camera in the corner, red light blinking. The woman was Initiate-tier, he could tell by the way she held her shoulders, the slight asymmetry of a Vitalis-improved tendon compensating for old injury. She could have taken him down before he cleared the chair if he presented a threat. He didn't present a threat. He presented a problem, and problems went to paperwork.

Time dragged. Then dragged longer. The clock on the wall had a crack at the seven, third stroke from the top, and Roan found himself timing its pendular motion. Thirty-one swings per minute. Something to do with the mass of the hand, the length of the arm, the gravitational pull of Greycross's lower atmosphere. Math didn't lie.

Eventually, the door opened.

Aldric Vance didn't look angry. That was the second thing Roan noticed. His father walked in with the same unhurried gait he used at the grocery store, at the hardware counter, at the morning market where vendors tried to overcharge for rank-boosting herbs and Aldric would stare them into honesty. He wore the gray jacket with the small tear near the left pocket. Roan had offered to mend it; Aldric had said it wasn't worth the thread, and his eyes moved across the room the same way Roan's had. Assessed. Catalogued. Dismissed.

"Mr. Vance." The officer stood. "Thank you for coming so quickly."

"Is he hurt?"

"No, sir. He cooperated fully. That's—" She hesitated. "That's unusual for juveniles."

Aldric nodded like this was expected information. He walked to Roan's chair and put a hand on his shoulder. Not heavy. Just there.

"Go home."

Roan slid off the chair. His feet hit the floor. Aldric didn't look at the officer again.

They walked out into Greycross.

- break -

The industrial district at five in the afternoon smelled like ammonia and hot metal and the sweetness of unfiltered reactor exhaust that nobody bothered to filter anymore because filtering cost credits and the plants that needed clean air were three districts east, near where the rich people lived. The sky was the color of an old bruise, purple-gray, streaked with orange from the furnace stacks that never stopped burning. Somewhere behind them, a billboard displayed a woman's face enlarged fifty feet tall, her Vitalis readings floating beside her head in green pixels: 847. She'd just won something. A tournament. Or a ranking. The top of something.

Aldric walked beside Roan without holding his hand. They never held hands in public, not because of shame, but because Aldric said it made you slower. Easier to track. Easier to grab.

"Foundry Junction," Aldric said, as they approached the intersection. "What does the sign say?"

"Walk. Don't Walk. Flashing Walk."

"Flashing Walk means what?"

"Run."

Aldric almost smiled. Almost. "And Steady?"

"Stop and assess."

"Good."

They crossed. A delivery truck blew through a yellow light, close enough that Roan's jacket rippled in its wake. He didn't flinch.

"Ashworth's residence," Aldric said, once they'd reached the other side. "What did you take?"

"Resistance band. Sphere set."

"Why?"

Roan had prepared for this question. "The spheres have progressive resistance scaling. Grade two means they adjust to user Vitalis output in real time. I wanted to see if I could replicate the mechanism with lower-grade materials."

"And the band?"

"Collateral."

Aldric didn't ask what that meant. He already knew. The spheres were the target; the band was what Roan had been carrying when he climbed through Ashworth's window. He hadn't needed it. He'd taken it anyway because leaving empty-handed after breaking in seemed like a waste of opportunity.

"You broke a window to get in."

"Latched window. The latch was corroded. I applied pressure at the friction point and it released."

"You broke it coming out, too."

"I didn't have time to open it again."

They walked in silence for two blocks. Past the pharmacy with the handwritten SUMMER CLEARANCE sign that had been there for three years. Past the newsstand where old man Garrett sold week-old international papers for half price. Past the squat building with no sign where Roan knew, without being told, that men went in and didn't always come out the same.

"Hungry?" Aldric asked.

"Yes."

"There's a dumpling cart near the market. If we take the long way."

Roan considered. The long way cost him time. His mother would be home by then, which meant his father would have to explain why they were late, which meant his father would have to decide how much to tell her. Or they could go straight home and his mother would be asleep, third shift again, back ache, the double dose of stamina boosters she'd been taking that made her hands shake when she came back at dawn.

"The long way," Roan said.

Aldric nodded. They went the long way.

- break -

The dumpling cart smelled like ginger and pork and the char of a pan that never fully cooled. The woman running it was Hardened-tier, at least, you could see it in the way she moved her hands without looking, the way her Vitalis radiated a faint warmth that Roan could almost feel on his skin even though he couldn't activate it yet. Initiates had Vitalis but couldn't feel it. Tempered could feel it but couldn't see it. Hardened and above could see it in others and project it in themselves. This woman had that settled, effortless control. She was selling dumplings to industrial workers for two credits each.

"Two pork," Aldric said. "No cilantro for the boy."

The woman looked at Roan. Something flickered in her eyes, curiosity, maybe. She'd seen something in him. A posture. A stillness. The way he'd tracked her hands without being told.

"You got a future, little one," she said, handing over the dumplings in wax paper. "Don't waste it on small crimes."

Aldric paid. They walked.

The dumplings were good. Aldric ate his in three bites; Roan ate his in twelve, savoring each one, which meant Aldric finished first and waited and didn't rush him. That was the third thing Roan noticed about his father. He never rushed.

"Mom works tomorrow?" Aldric asked.

"Second shift. Back at six AM."

"I'll be home."

Roan nodded. He didn't ask where Aldric went during the days. The answer was always "work", a word that covered everything from the logistics firm downtown to the errands he ran for people who didn't appear on any ledger. Roan's mother thought Aldric was a midlevel supply chain coordinator. The truth was more complicated, but Roan had learned early that some truths weren't his to tell.

They reached the apartment building as the streetlights clicked on, one after another, a chain of amber that lit their path up three flights of stairs where the carpet was worn to threads and the hallway smelled like cooking oil from eight different apartments trying not to smell like each other.

The door was unlocked. Lyra sat on the living room floor, homework spread around her like a paper explosion, her tongue poking out the corner of her mouth as she concentrated on fractions.

"Mom's out," she said without looking up. "She left money for pizza."

Aldric went to the kitchen. Roan heard the clink of a glass, the pour of water, the sound of his father thinking, which was silence, broken only by the ambient noise of a building full of people trying to live.

"Lyra," Aldric called. "Come here."

She came. Her ponytail was lopsided, she'd done it herself, in the bathroom mirror, which meant it was always lopsided on the left side because her left hand was dominant and she couldn't see what her right hand was doing. She sat at the kitchen table, legs swinging.

"Roan," Aldric said. "Sit."

Roan sat. His feet still didn't touch the floor.

Aldric poured a glass of milk and put it in front of Roan. Then he put a second glass in front of Lyra. Then he sat across from them both and waited until they were looking at him.

"I'm not angry."

The words hung there. Lyra's eyes went wide; she'd expected a lecture, maybe, or worse. Roan felt his shoulders drop a fraction of an inch. Not relief. Just... Permission to stop calculating escape routes.

"I should have given you better tools."

Roan didn't understand. But he logged the sentence somewhere deep, in the place where things waited until they were ready to be understood, not then or fully.

"Don't get caught next time."

That was the third sentence. Aldric said it without emphasis, the way he'd say the time or the weather or the name of a street. It wasn't permission. It wasn't permission denied. It was a correction of method.

Lyra giggled, nervous. "Dad, that's so—you can't say that—"

"Dad's joking," Roan said.

"I'm not," Aldric said. "But I'm also not angry. You understand the difference?"

Roan nodded.

"Go finish your homework, Lyra."

She went, casting a suspicious look back at them both.

Aldric looked at Roan. The kitchen light was bad, always bad, a single bulb that flickered every forty seconds, and in that flickering light, his father's face was half shadow, half something else.

"You wanted to know if you could do it," Aldric said. "The break-in. You wanted to test yourself."

"Yes."

"Results?"

"Positive. I identified three security vulnerabilities and accessed the target in under four minutes. The recovery was—" Roan paused. "The recovery was suboptimal."

"You got caught."

"I got caught."

Aldric leaned back. The chair creaked under his weight. "You're eleven."

"Yes."

"Most eleven-year-olds want to play games. You want to test systems."

"Games are systems."

This time, Aldric did smile. Small. Brief. Gone before Lyra could see it from the living room.

"Eat your dumpling," he said.

Roan looked down. He'd been holding the wax paper, folded, untouched. He unwrapped it. The dumpling inside was cold but still good. He ate it in six bites this time.

- break -

Two days later, Aldric left.

He said he had a business trip. A client meeting near the Hollow Reaches, a shipment that needed inspecting. He kissed Mira on the forehead and told her he'd be back in a week, maybe less. He knelt down and hugged Lyra, who held on a little longer because she always held on a little longer when he left, even though he always came back. He put a hand on Roan's shoulder and looked at him and said nothing.

Roan understood later that the look had been an apology. But then, he was seventeen the first time he understood that, and by then the look had faded into memory, and memory is a poor substitute for a word you should have said.

Aldric walked down the stairs. The building's front door opened and closed. The sound of his footsteps faded on the landing, and then there was only the building again, the radiator's hiss, the television through the wall, the smell of cooking oil and the silence of an apartment missing one person.

He didn't come back in a week.

He didn't come back.

Years later, Roan would remember the lesson before he understood the cost. Better tools. Cleaner exits. Never confuse survival with permission.
