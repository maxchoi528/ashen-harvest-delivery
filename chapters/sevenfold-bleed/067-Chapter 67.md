Chapter 67: Selene Watches

The atrium's ceiling had collapsed in a geometry that offended Roan's sense of structural probability. Slabs of ferrocrete angled against one another like a child's ill-fitted blocks, creating a triangular void that shouldn't have stayed upright for seventy seconds, let alone seven years. He counted the stress fractures anyway. Seven major, thirteen minor. Load-bearing column sheared at the base. The Hollowed City didn't obey civil engineering, but it obeyed weight, and weight was math.

He crouched behind a fallen transit kiosk, breath shallow, knife held reverse-grip along his forearm. The target was twenty meters ahead.

A Husk. Brute-Alpha classification, Tier-2 Surface Rift standard. It shuffled between the shadows of two tilted spires, shoulders hunched under calcified plating, dragging a fist that had fused into a blunt club of bone. Guild bounty paid five thousand credits for the marrow. Roan needed it. The Hollow Marrow Elixir wouldn't brew itself, and Eckhart didn't take requests from paupers. Worse, the Spire's autumn tournament was weeks away—distant enough to feel like a deadline, close enough to tighten his chest. He needed Hardened bone by winter. The elixir was the shortest path.

Roan had been Tempered for weeks. His displayed Vitalis sat at the number he showed the Spire's census scouts when they bothered to look. Six hundred twelve. A respectable climb from his Initiate baseline. A number that made instructors nod and move on.

His real output was a different equation. One he didn't solve for anyone.

He watched the Husk's gait. Left leg stiff. Old injury, or natural asymmetry. Either way, it turned in a twelve-second loop, guarding something near the base of the western spire. Roan mapped the path. Three seconds to close. One to cut the tendon. Two to pry the sternum and extract the cryo-vial. Five seconds total. The Rift's local stability clocked at forty minutes remaining before phase-shift. Plenty.

He shifted his weight.

The Husk stopped.

It straightened. Not the jerky motion of a distracted beast, but the precise, spinal alignment of a fighter centering their balance. The calcified shoulders rolled back. It turned.

Roan froze.

The Husk wore his coat. Identical. Every fray, every stain. It held a knife the same length as his. And when it looked at him, it had his face.

Wrong.

Right.

The thing smiled with teeth too even to be Roan's.

Roan didn't need the Shard to tell him what he was looking at. The Hollow Echoes were Rift myths until they weren't. Mimics. Predators that absorbed kinetic memory from invaders and wore their shapes like Sunday suits. He'd read the Guild briefings. Tier-2 encounters, rare but documented. Usually copied standard troops. Sometimes copied Hunters who'd died in the breach.

He'd been in the Rift for ninety minutes.

Apparently, that was enough.

The Echo moved.

Not like a Husk. Like him. Low slash, reverse grip, aiming for the brachial artery. Roan parried. Steel scraped steel. The impact rattled his elbow, perfectly calibrated to his own muscle mass. He pivoted, kicked for the knee. The Echo pivoted with him, mirroring the kick. Their shins cracked together. Pain flared white up Roan's leg. Real pain. Enemy damage. The kind the Codex wouldn't touch.

He disengaged, rolling behind a tilted slab. The Echo didn't pursue immediately. It stood in the clearing, head cocked, studying. Learning.

Roan touched his shin. Not fractured. Bruised deep. He ran the math. If it copied his mass, his reach, his preferred openings, then standard engagement was a mutual destruction loop. Two identical equations solving for the same zero. Worse, the Echo didn't tire. Its Vitalis wasn't metabolizing. It was a closed system, recycling energy until the Rift collapsed.

Unless he introduced a variable it couldn't copy.

The Shard pulsed at the base of his skull.

[Suggestion: your self-esteem is not a weapon. Your self-harm, however, remains on the table. — A.V.]

Roan ignored the dead man's humor. He sheathed his knife.

The Echo tilted its head. Then it sheathed its own.

Roan exhaled. He activated Pyrebrand Cycling.

Heat ignited in his femoral arteries, spreading like spilled accelerant. The technique burned blood for speed, a foundational offense he'd honed since his second month at the Spire. The Sidewound was immediate. Capillaries seared, plasma thickening in his calves. He counted. One Mississippi. Two Mississippi.

At three-tenths, the Codex devoured the damage.

[Sidewound absorbed. Conversion: +12 Vitalis. Capacity: 3/4.]

The Echo watched. Then it copied.

Its skin flushed. Steam vented from its collar. It shot forward, fast, blurring through the same acceleration Roan had just purchased. Roan braced.

The Echo's fist caught him in the sternum.

He'd expected speed. He hadn't expected heat. The thing replicated Pyrebrand perfectly, burning blood for force. The impact hurled him into the transit kiosk. Ferrocrete crumbled against his spine. Air left his lungs in a sharp, involuntary hiss. He hit the ground, rolled, came up spitting dust.

The Echo stood where he'd been, regarding its own fist. Smoke curled from the knuckles. The skin was blackening, peeling back from metacarpals that gleamed like wet stone. It had copied the technique, but not the Inversion. It had burned its own blood and kept the damage.

Roan tasted copper. Not from injury. From opportunity.

The Echo looked at him. For the first time, its expression flickered. The glassy eyes narrowed, the head twitching side to side as if rereading a page that no longer made sense.

Wonderful.

Roan didn't give it time to recalibrate. He charged, unleashing Sevenfold Bleed: First Strike.

The technique carved through seven meridians simultaneously, trading structural integrity for penetrating force. Pain detonated in his forearm, white-hot, as the channels fractured under the strain. One Mississippi. Two.

[Sidewound absorbed. Conversion: +34 Vitalis. Capacity: 2/4.]

He drove his knife toward the Echo's throat.

The Echo matched him. Same strike. Same angle. Same suicidal meridian burn. Their blades crossed with a shriek of metal that echoed off the tilted spires.

But Roan's arm held. The Echo's didn't.

The Sidewound stayed in its channels. Tendons popped like snapped wires. Its elbow joint bent backward, a geometry that offended even this place's structural logic. The knife tumbled from spasming fingers. Black fluid sprayed. Not blood, but concentrated Rift essence, pressurized and wrong.

Roan didn't hesitate. He shifted inside its guard, reversed his blade, and plunged it into the hollow beneath the jaw.

The Echo gurgled. It clawed at his coat. At its coat. Their coat. He twisted the knife. The body convulsed, then lost cohesion like wet paper in rain, sloughing into black sludge that ate at the ferrocrete.

Roan stepped back. His meridians hummed, rebuilt, fortified by the Inversion. The sludge bubbled, evaporating into Vitalis mist that the Rift absorbed.

He checked his hands. Steady. He'd used two conversions in under eight seconds. His reserves sat at half capacity. The tally was still favorable, but the ribcage the Echo had targeted ached with each breath. Not fractured. Close.

He turned toward the western spire. The Husk, the real one, hadn't moved. It lay dead near the base, throat opened by the Echo before Roan had even arrived. The mimic had been hunting the same bounty, or guarding it. Efficient.

Roan knelt. He worked the sternum with practiced efficiency, prying the calcified plates until he reached the marrow cavity. The tissue was fibrous, iridescent with trapped Vitalis. He drew the cryo-vial from his belt and scraped the sample. Three grams. Enough for one elixir dose.

He sealed the vial. The Rift groaned around him. Dust fell from the ceiling void. Phase-shift incoming, ten minutes ahead of schedule. Unscheduled variances in Rift behavior were never good. They preceded Tier-3 bleed-throughs, spatial inversions, or Wraith swarms.

He ran.

The geometry of the Hollowed City shifted as he moved. Streets he'd mapped on entry lengthened. Corridors compressed. He didn't look at the architecture. He looked at the negative space, the paths gravity preferred, the routes water would take. Exit protocols for Tier-2 Rifts were straightforward: follow the decay gradient. Old things clustered near the breach points.

He found the rift-gate shimmering in what had once been a subway terminus. The arch of distorted air hummed, unstable, flickering at the edges. He stepped through without breaking stride.

- break -

The Guild transfer station hit him with fluorescent light and antiseptic smell. Ironholm's autumn chill replaced the Rift's stagnant humidity. Roan squinted, letting his eyes adjust, letting his body reorient to Earth's denser physics.

The attendant booth was empty.

Not unusual for the late shift. Junior Hunters often skipped paperwork, especially when the heating system was on the fritz. Roan walked past the scanner, letting his badge ping the log. He needed a shower, a protein block, and twelve hours of quota recovery before he considered the next step.

He was halfway to the lockers when he stopped.

The bulletin board near the exit had been updated. A single sheet, printed on Guild letterhead, pinned over the duty roster with a brass thumbtack that caught the light like an accusation.

ATTENTION: QUARANTINE ORDER

Rift Designation: H-771 (The Shattered Atrium)

Status: CONTAMINATION EVENT

All extractors from H-771 within the last 4 hours report to Sentinel Bureau for mandatory debrief.

Unauthorized possession of biological material from H-771 is a Class-3 infraction.

Roan read it twice.

His hand drifted to the cryo-vial in his pocket. Still cold. Still sealed. But the quarantine hadn't been posted when he'd entered. Not long before. Someone had declared it while he was inside. The paper wasn't even curled at the edges. Fresh.

He turned. The station's security feed camera above the door swiveled. Red light blinking. Tracking.

Not good.

He walked anyway. Not running. Running triggered pursuit algorithms in the station's automated defense suite. He kept his pace to the calculated threshold of innocent behavior. One point four meters per second, shoulders loose, gaze forward.

The camera followed him to the exit.

Outside, Ironholm's drizzle masked the street in gray gauze. He turned left, away from the Spire, away from his dorm. Toward Eckhart's workshop. If the Guild flagged the marrow, he needed it stashed somewhere that wouldn't trace back to his residence. Eckhart's back room had a false wall and a refrigeration unit that ran on independent generators. The smith owed him for the Pyrebrand schematic Roan had recovered last month.

He made it half a block before the sedan pulled alongside.

Black, armored without insignia. Bulletproof glass with a faint Vitalis sheen that bent the streetlights into rainbows. The rear window descended with a hydraulic whisper. The man inside wore a charcoal suit that cost more than Roan's annual credit stipend. Silver at the temples. Hands that had never held a blade for labor, only for threat. Roan didn't recognize the face, but he recognized the posture. Shoulders back, chin tucked, the slight forward tilt of someone who expected violence to arrive from the front and intended to meet it with superior firepower.

"Vance," the man said. A statement of fact, not inquiry.

Roan stopped. The sidewalk was empty. Rain dripped from his hair into his eyes. He didn't blink. He mapped the street. Two cameras. One on the liquor store corner, thirty meters back. One above the transit stop, fifteen meters ahead. The sedan blocked the curb-side escape. The alley to his left was a dead end. The alley to his right led to a main thoroughfare with foot traffic. If he moved fast, he could vanish into the crowd before the man exited the vehicle.

But the man wasn't exiting. That meant he had a gun, or a caster, trained on Roan's center mass from inside the car. Firing from a seated position, limited arc. Easier to dodge if Roan dropped low.

Still, dodging a Vitalis-charged round at two meters was poor math. Especially with bruised ribs and half his Inversion quota spent.

"The marrow from H-771," the man continued. His voice was cultured, Eastern European dulled by decades of Anglophone polish. "It carries a biological marker. That sample is spoken for."

Roan said nothing. His mind ran the probabilities. The Husk had been seeded. The Echo had been a guard dog. And he'd walked out with the bone.

"You have two options." The man held up a tablet. On the screen, Roan's Guild file. Photo from six months ago. Vitalis reading: 612. Public record. "Surrender the sample now. We compensate you for the inconvenience. Five thousand credits, same as the bounty. Or we file a contamination report with the Sentinel Bureau, and you spend the next eighteen months explaining to Headmaster Cole why you stole from a restricted Rift."

The rain kept falling.

Roan looked at the tablet. Six hundred twelve. The man was leaning on it, confident that a mid-tier Tempered student would fold under the threat of institutional consequence.

Roan almost smiled.

He reached into his pocket. Drew out the cryo-vial. Held it up between two fingers, letting the streetlight catch the iridescent sludge inside.

"You said it's tagged," Roan said.

The man's eyes narrowed.

"Let's verify the marker. Right here, under the camera at the transit stop. I'll call the Sentinel Bureau myself. Because if that tag is registered, you've just confessed to seeding a Tier-2 Rift with tracked biologics without Guild authorization. Class-1 infraction. If it isn't registered, you're lying, and I've got a clean extraction log plus two camera angles of you threatening me with an unlicensed firearm."

The man's fingers tightened on the tablet. A micro-expression. Left corner of the mouth tightening, the skin around the knuckles whitening.

"You're drooling," Roan said.

The man's hand twitched toward his lapel. Weapon there. Concealed, Vitalis-charged, intended for executions, not duels.

Roan didn't move. He didn't need to. He'd already counted the street cameras. Two. Both within line of sight. The man wouldn't fire. Not here.

The window rose. The sedan pulled away, merging into traffic with a low hum that vibrated in Roan's teeth.

Roan stood in the rain. The vial was still in his fingers. His heart rate hadn't exceeded seventy beats per minute. But his jaw ached from clenching.

He pocketed the sample and turned toward the alley. As he did, a flicker of motion across the street caught his eye. A woman stood beneath the awning of a shuttered bookshop. Red coat, dark hair plastered by the damp. Too still for the cold. Academy posture, not civilian.

Selene Ardent. Third-year, Aldermere, Red Mage. He'd seen her face in the tournament briefing packets.

She met his gaze for a fraction of a second, then turned and walked into the side street. Not following. Observing.

He filed it and moved.

- break -

The workshop smelled of ozone and hot steel. Eckhart didn't look up from his grinding wheel when Roan entered through the service hatch. The old smith had a rule. If you knew about the hatch, you were welcome. If you didn't, you'd never find it. Sparks cascaded from the bench in a steady orange waterfall, illuminating the rust-pocked walls.

"You're bleeding on my floor," Eckhart said.

Roan looked down. A thin line of red traced his boot from the shin impact. Minor. "I'll mop."

"Don't. I'll bill the Spire." Eckhart killed the wheel. The whine decayed into a whir, then silence. He pushed his goggles up, leaving pale rings around his eyes. "You get it?"

Roan tossed him the vial. Eckhart caught it one-handed, held it to the overhead light.

"Hollow Marrow," the smith grunted. "Good density. Iridescence reads clean from here. Where'd you pull it from?"

"Shattered Atrium."

Eckhart's expression flickered. Just a twitch of the left eyelid. Too controlled, too fast. "That place went quarantine an hour ago. Contamination event. Guild's already talking about bumping it to Tier-3."

"So I saw."

"You were inside during?"

"I was the event."

Eckhart studied him. Then he turned to his workbench, drawing a scanning wand from a leather holster. He passed it over the vial. The device beeped. Once. Twice. A low, ugly trill that made the hair on Roan's neck stand up.

Eckhart's jaw tightened.

"What?" Roan asked.

The smith didn't answer immediately. He turned the vial, examining the bottom edge where the calcified tissue met the glass. Then he held it under a magnifier lamp and beckoned Roan over.

Roan looked. Beneath the magnification, the marrow wasn't uniform. Threaded through the iridescent tissue were black filaments, fine as hair, arranged in a lattice that pulsed with its own Vitalis rhythm. Not random. Structured.

"There's a second signature in here," Eckhart said. "Embedded in the mineral lattice. Not Husk. Not Echo. Something that was feeding on the marrow before you extracted it. Growing in it."

Roan went still. "Define feeding."

"Parasitic Vitalis. Dormant, but structured. Like a seed waiting for water." Eckhart looked at him. "You planning to brew the elixir with this?"

"That was the idea."

"Bad idea."

Eckhart set the vial down. "This isn't raw Hollow Marrow. It's been cultured. Whoever dropped that Husk into the Atrium was doing more than tracking it. They were growing something inside it. A delivery system." He tapped the glass. "And if you drink this, you are getting more than marrow. You're getting the payload. Whatever blooms in your bones when the elixir activates."

Roan stared at the iridescent sludge. His exit from the Rift. The sedan. The quarantine order pinning him to a timeline he hadn't chosen.

It wasn't about the marrow. It was about what was in it.

He reached out. Took the vial back. His fingers were steady.

"Can you refine it out?"

"Maybe. Half the sample, maybe less. You'd need a sterile catalyst lab. Black market rates. Three, four days to set up." Eckhart paused. He wiped his hands on a rag that left more grease than it removed. "Or you could use it as-is. Most Tempered wouldn't survive the standard Hollow Marrow backlash anyway. Liquefied bone marrow isn't a joke. A parasite on top of that? Certain death. Slow, screaming, certain death."

Roan turned the vial. The liquid caught the workshop light, throwing rainbow patterns across the wall. Beautiful. Poisonous. A resource that existed only because someone else had decided it shouldn't.

The Shard pulsed.

[Organic analysis: Hollow Marrow precursor. Foreign parasitic signature detected. Fatality estimate: 73%. — A.V.]

Roan closed his fingers around the vial. Warm. Pulsing faintly, like a second heartbeat that had learned his name.

"Set up the lab," Roan said.

"Three days. Maybe four."

"Do it."

The smith looked at him. Roan looked back.

"You realize," Eckhart said, "that even if I scrub it clean, the elixir itself might still kill you. Hollow Marrow isn't a training tonic. It liquefies bone in half the kids who try it. The other half wish it had."

"I know."

"And you're still going to drink it."

Roan pocketed the vial. He thought of the Echo wearing his face. The sedan sliding away. The quarantine order. The woman in the red coat who had watched him from across the street. The ledger.

Math didn't lie.

But sometimes the ingredients were incomplete.

"Three days," Roan said. He turned toward the hatch. "I'll be back with the catalyst fee."

He climbed out into the alley. The rain had stopped. The air smelled like wet ash and approaching winter. He pulled his coat tighter, the real coat, not the Echo's imitation, and walked into the dark.

Behind him, the workshop light clicked off. But the vial in his pocket kept pulsing, faint and insistent, like a countdown he couldn't hear.

- break -

Roan didn't go back to the Spire.

He took the maintenance tunnels beneath the Ironholm transit grid, navigating by the hum of the third rail and the gradient of water stains on the walls. The tunnels were a map he'd memorized in his first month, escape routes calculated against every conceivable threat. Sentinel sweep, academy expulsion, civil unrest. He moved through them like an equation solving itself in real time, each intersection a variable, each dead end a discarded hypothesis.

The safehouse was an old utility closet two levels beneath a shuttered laundry. He'd paid the lease in Black Market Tokens, untraceable, renewed through a cutout in the Calloway district. He locked the door, wedged the chair under the handle, sat on the concrete floor with his back to the wall.

He drew the vial.

In the dark, the marrow glowed. Faint, bioluminescent blue. The parasitic signature Eckhart had mentioned wasn't visible to the eye, but the Shard felt it. A thready, malignant rhythm underlying the main pulse, syncopated and hungry.

He had three days to decide. Brew the elixir with Eckhart's help, burn half the sample, and maybe still die from the standard backlash. Or wait. Find another source. But someone had cornered the Hollow Marrow market in Tier-2 Rifts. That was common knowledge in the Guild undercity. The Shattered Atrium had been his best shot, and they'd turned it into a trap.

He set the vial on the floor.

His ribs ached from the Echo's strike. His shin throbbed in time with his pulse. Real damage, not Sidewound. He couldn't Invert what others gave him. Only what he took upon himself.

He reached into his pack. Drew out his canteen. The water was flat, lukewarm. He drank anyway, letting it settle in his stomach like a promise he couldn't keep.

The Shard hummed.

[Daily capacity: 2/4. Quota resets: 11h 14m. Reserves: 847/1000.]

One hundred fifty-three points. A week's grinding in the Spire's training pits. Or one perfect inversion of a high-yield Sidewound.

Or one sip of the marrow. Enough.

Roan capped the canteen. He lay back on the cold concrete, staring at the dark ceiling, listening to the city's arteries thrum around him. The vial pulsed beside him, a tiny blue star in the underground.

Someone knocked on the door.

Two raps. Sharp. Deliberate.

Roan didn't move. His hand found the knife on his hip. The maintenance tunnels had no neighbors. The laundry above was shuttered for the season. The lease was under a dead man's name, paid through a trust that wouldn't trigger for another year.

The knock came again. Three raps this time. Patterned. Familiar.

Brendt.

Roan rose, crossed the room, and opened the door.

Brendt Holloway stood in the corridor, hair plastered to his forehead with rain or sweat, eyes wide, breath fast and shallow, hands shaking.

"They raided the workshop," Brendt said. "Twenty minutes after you left. Sentinels. Not Guild. Sentinel Bureau proper. They tore Eckhart's place apart. Took his scanners, his wands, everything in the refrigeration unit."

Roan's grip on the doorframe tightened. Wood splintered under his fingers.

"They didn't find anything," Brendt continued. His voice was fast, breathless. "Eckhart must have flushed it. But Roan. They had a warrant with your name on it. Bureau-level. Eckhart said that kind of paper doesn't move without someone high up pushing it."

Roan looked at the vial on the floor. The blue glow flickered once, as if answering a distant signal.

He looked back at Brendt.

"The sample," Roan said. "I need it in Drogan's hands. Not mine. They find me with it, it's evidence. They find you with it, you're a courier running a favor."

Brendt's jaw tightened. "You sure you trust me with this?"

"I'm asking you because you're the only one I trust with it." The words came out flat, but the calculation beneath them was stone-cold. Brendt knew the tunnels. He had no record with the Bureau. And after the Kael business, he had reason to owe. "Trust cuts both ways."

Brendt stared at him for a long second. Then he nodded, reached out, and took the vial. "I owe you for Kael. I'm not letting you go alone out there. But I can get this to Drogan."

"Don't use the transit grid. Don't use your phone. Stick to the pedestrian underpasses. If anyone tails you, lose it in the market district."

"Got it." Brendt pocketed the marrow and turned to leave, then paused. "Roan. You're the heat?"

"I'm the heat."

The footsteps in the far tunnel had grown louder. Vitalis-charged lanterns bobbed in the dark, painting the walls in hard white circles.

Roan stepped into the corridor, closing the safehouse door behind him. He turned the other way. Toward the deep tunnels. Toward the old Rift-sewer junctions that even the Bureau feared to patrol after midnight.

He didn't look back.

But as he descended into the lower levels, he felt the weight of a gaze that wasn't the Sentinels'. Somewhere above, in the grid of streets and alleys, Selene Ardent was still watching. He didn't know what she wanted yet.

That was a problem for later.

The equation remained honest.

But tonight, the balance had a body count.
