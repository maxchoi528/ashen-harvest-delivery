Chapter 71: Ines Carrow

The elixir sat in its glass vial like a promise wrapped in a threat.

Roan turned it over in his fingers, watching the way the light caught the silver-tinged liquid inside. The apothecary had called it *Hollow Marrow Elixir*, third-tier catalyst, normally reserved for Hardened cultivators attempting the transition into Forged. The standard dosage was a single drop. He'd bought the entire vial. Twelve drops. Enough to kill a Tempered cultivator three times over, if the side effects didn't get him first.

*Bone marrow liquefaction. Permanent paralysis. The slow dissolution of the skeletal framework from the inside out.*

He closed his eyes. The balance wasn't complicated.

Standard approach: take one drop, hope the body adapted, pray the marrow reformed before it turned to soup. Recovery time: three to six months, assuming no permanent damage. Assumption being the operative word.

His approach: take everything, let the Codex Shard process what it could, pray the Inversion didn't decide this particular poison was beyond its scope.

*Sidewound type: Tier 3 catalyst. Severity: mortal. Classification: bone-systemic dissolution.*

The system prompt had appeared an hour ago, after he'd uncorked the vial and let the fumes hit his nostrils. The Shard had analyzed and categorized, then rendered its judgment without his permission. It always did that now, pre-scanning anything he intended to put in his body, any technique he considered learning, any wound that might become interesting.

[Awaiting input. Processing time required: minimum four hours. Capacity cost for full vial: seven days.]

Seven days. He'd burned through two weeks of capacity in the past month alone. Another seven-day hit would leave him walking a razor's edge for the next month. One serious fight, one unexpected injury, and he'd be out of reversal options entirely.

Unless the reward justified the risk.

*Cross-tier bone hardening. If it works.*

He set the vial down on the workbench beside the other materials he'd accumulated over the past three months. The Black Spire's underground market had been good to him, better than good. The Vance name still carried weight in certain circles, weight he hadn't earned but could certainly spend. Brendt had made introductions. Drogan had looked the other way when certain transactions occurred. And somewhere along the way, Roan had accumulated enough low grade catalysts, recovered techniques, and stolen—*liberated*, he'd correct himself—resources to build a stockpile that most Tempered cultivators wouldn't see until they hit Hardened.

The problem was accessing it. Most of those catalysts required a body stronger than his to survive. The techniques demanded a Vitalis foundation he hadn't built yet.

The elixir was the key. If it worked, he'd have the bone structure to handle the rest. If it didn't. He picked up the vial again.

It didn't matter. The arithmetic was clear. Staying at Tempered meant staying invisible, and invisible meant safe, and safe meant dead in the ground within two years when House Greel's long game finally reached its conclusion. He'd seen the projections. He'd done the calculations. The window was narrow, the competition brutal, and the resources required to close the gap were not available through standard channels.

This was the only path.

He uncorked the vial. The first drop hit his tongue like liquid fire.

Roan suppressed the flinch through sheer will, letting the catalyst slide down his throat with controlled precision. His body immediately registered the intrusion, the stomach lining convulsing, the bloodstream flooding with compounds designed to reshape bone structure at the cellular level. Pain was secondary to the sensation of *wrongness*, as if every skeletal joint in his body had decided to rebel against its own existence.

The Codex Shard woke.

[Sidewound detected. Initiating Inversion protocol.]

[Conversion successful: +89 Vitalis. Yield: 100%.]

[Processing: Tier 3 catalyst. Estimated capacity cost: most of one day.]

He set a mental timer. Thirty seconds between drops. That was the recommended interval for standard dosage, though he had no idea if it applied to an idiot taking twelve times the normal amount. The research had been thin. The sample size nonexistent. Anyone else who'd tried this had either died or never tried at all.

Second drop.

The pain shifted, focusing inward. He could feel his spine now, every vertebra, every disc, every nerve cluster, as the elixir began its work on the marrow inside. Standard effect. The bones were supposed to soften, become malleable, then reform under the catalyst's guidance. A controlled demolition of the skeletal framework, followed by reconstruction at a higher tier of hardness.

The Inversion caught the transition.

[Conversion successful: +94 Vitalis. Processing: marrow liquefaction phase one.]

Third drop.

His knees buckled. Roan caught himself on the workbench, knuckles whitening around the vial's base. The room swam briefly before stabilizing. His vision had developed a faint silver tint that he'd learned to associate with the Shard's active processing. Somewhere inside his body, bone was becoming liquid, and the Codex was drinking that process like wine.

*It doesn't care that this should kill me. It only cares that it's interesting.*

Fourth drop. Fifth.

[Warning: Processing load approaching threshold.]

[Inversion capacity: 3/4 remaining.]

The silver tint deepened. He could taste iron in the back of his throat, his body bleeding internally, the soft tissues surrounding the liquefying bones unable to adapt quickly enough. The elixir didn't care about muscle, about blood vessels, about organs. It wanted marrow. It would take marrow, even if it had to dissolve everything in its path to get there.

Roan kept counting.

Six. Seven. Eight.

[Sidewound absorbed.]

[Conversion successful: +103 Vitalis.]

[Capacity: 2/4 remaining.]

The pain crested somewhere around the ninth drop. He found himself on his knees, both hands pressed flat against the stone floor, breathing in shallow gasps that didn't fill his lungs. His ribs had softened. He could feel them moving wrong, pressing against organs that hadn't evolved to handle that kind of pressure.

*This is what dying feels like.*

The thought arrived without panic. He'd considered this outcome. Calculated the probability. The Codex Shard had given him a sixty-three percent survival rate, better odds than most standard breakthrough attempts, if less comfortable. The problem wasn't dying. The problem was dying without purpose, dying without leaving something behind, dying in a basement room while the world above continued its slow grind toward war.

Ninth drop.

Tenth.

[Capacity: 1/4 remaining.]

[Warning: Mortal Sidewound threshold exceeded.]

The Shard was supposed to handle this. The arithmetic had assumed a standard curve. The tenth drop pushed it past the edge of prediction. For a fraction of a heartbeat, the silver tint stuttered—and Roan understood, with cold clarity, that the Shard hadn't budgeted for this. He'd calculated wrong.

[Initiating emergency protocol.]

The Codex Shard did something it hadn't done before.

Roan felt it reach deeper than the surface-level conversions, past the bone dissolution, past the internal bleeding, into something fundamental. The Shard had always been a passive observer, converting what it could and noting the rest. Now it was *intervening*. Reinforcing. Taking the structural collapse happening inside his body and stabilizing it with energy it had stored from previous conversions.

*It's spending savings.*

The realization arrived with a strange clarity. The Shard had accumulated a reserve, not in Vitalis, but in something harder to quantify. The residual power from every reversal, every converted Sidewound, every moment of pain turned sideways. It had been saving that reserve for exactly this situation.

Eleventh drop.

The silver tint vanished. In its place came a sensation of *tightness*, as if someone had grabbed every bone in his body and pulled them taut. The liquefied marrow didn't reconstitute—it transformed. Calcium deposits formed in patterns that felt deliberate, architectural, as if the Codex was designing a skeletal framework specifically optimized for a cultivator of his potential.

[Emergency protocol complete.]

[Capacity: 0/4 remaining.]

[New status: Mortal conversion complete. Bone structure upgraded. Cross-tier hardness achieved.]

Twelfth drop.

He swallowed the final drop of the vial and collapsed forward onto his hands. The stone floor was cold against his palms. That was the first thing he noticed. The second was that he could feel the individual grains of the stone, the microscopic imperfections in its surface, with a clarity that hadn't been there before. His sense of touch had always been good—the Inversion had been slowly improving it for months—but this was different. He could feel the temperature differential between where his hands touched and where his forearms rested. He could feel the vibration of his own heartbeat in the bones of his wrists.

He pushed himself upright.

The movement was wrong. Everything was wrong. His center of gravity had shifted, his muscle attachments had changed, and the way his ribs expanded had altered. He took one step toward the workbench's mirror and nearly fell, catching himself on the edge with a hand that felt too strong, too precise.

His face in the reflection looked the same. That was almost disappointing—he'd half-hoped for some visible marker of what had just happened, some external sign that matched the internal revolution. But the body was subtle about these things. It didn't announce transformation. It *was*.

He rolled his shoulders. The joints moved with a smoothness that spoke of chrome-hardened cartilage, of bone surfaces polished to molecular perfection. When he flexed his fingers, the knuckles cracked with a sound like distant thunder.

*Cross-tier bone hardening.*

The Codex confirmed it. He'd expected to find numbers, some quantifiable measure of his new status. Instead, there was only the sensation of solidity, of being built from better materials than before.

[Breakthrough achieved.]

[Rank: Hardened.]

[Note: This was the stupidest thing you've ever done. Congratulations on surviving it. — A.V.]

Roan let out a breath that might've been a laugh.

The math had worked. Not cleanly, not safely, but it had worked. The twelve-drop approach had given the Codex enough data to reverse-engineer the entire process, turning a lethal cascade failure into a controlled upgrade. The emergency protocol had eaten through the rest of his capacity reserve, leaving him exactly where the projections said he'd be: one serious fight away from disaster, but significantly harder to kill.

He looked at the empty vial on the workbench.

Three more like this one, and he'd have the foundation to attempt Forged. Assuming his body survived the preparation process. Assuming House Greel's people didn't find him before then. Assuming a dozen other variables that were currently beyond his control.

The tournament registration opened in six days. His name was on the list, his rank officially recorded as Tempered, a status he'd maintained through careful misdirection and expensive bribes to the testing administrators. If he entered as Tempered and fought like Hardened, the questions would start. The investigations would follow.

Unless he lost on purpose.

Unless he made sure his victory looked expensive enough to explain, painful enough to excuse, messy enough to deflect scrutiny.

The tally was getting complicated again.

A knock at the door.

Roan was across the room before he consciously registered moving, the new speed still startling him. He pressed his back against the wall beside the frame, knife appearing in his hand through reflex rather than intention.

"Roan?" Brendt's voice, muffled through the wood. "You in there? Ines Carrow came by looking for you. Something about your credit allocation for the tournament."

He let out the breath he'd been holding.

"Give me a minute."

He looked around the room. The empty vial. The scattered notes from his calculations. The bloodstains on the floor that were already fading as his body processed the remnants of the internal bleeding. All of it evidence, and evidence was liability.

He scooped the vial into a cloth, pocketed the notes, and kicked a bucket of water over the bloodstains. The dilution was imperfect but sufficient. By the time anyone looked closely, the stains would be gone entirely—his improved bone structure included a minor enhancement to healing factor, a side benefit he hadn't expected.

He opened the door.

Brendt's eyes went immediately to Roan's hands, then to his face, then back to his hands. The knife was still there, half-concealed in his sleeve. Brendt's jaw tightened.

"You look like hell," he said.

"I've been working on something."

"That much is obvious." Brendt leaned in, studying Roan's eyes with the intensity of someone who'd learned to read Vitalis fluctuations through necessity. "Your reading is... Different. Higher than yesterday. Significantly higher."

Roan didn't confirm or deny. "The tournament registration."

"Right. Ines Carrow stopped by the dormitory in person. She said the credit allocation paperwork needs your signature tonight or your name gets pulled from the bracket. She's in her office." Brendt paused. "Also, Selene Ardent stopped by the training halls asking for you. Twice."

*Interesting.*

"What did she want?"

"Didn't say. Just asked if you'd been seen recently and whether you'd mentioned anything about your family background." Brendt's expression shifted to something more guarded. "House Ardent doesn't make social calls, Roan. Selene especially. She's the daughter of the current head, not some second cousin playing politics. If she's asking questions about the Vance name..."

"Then she's either suspicious or she's been told to be suspicious by someone who already knows the answers." Roan stepped past Brendt into the corridor. "Let me worry about Selene. You focus on the registration."

"Roan." Brendt caught his arm. "Whatever you're doing, whatever you've been doing, the window for keeping it quiet is closing. The tournament is going to put you in front of people who have resources and connections, plus very long memories. People who remember your father."

Roan looked at Brendt's hand on his arm, then up at Brendt's face. The concern there was genuine. The worry was legitimate. Brendt had stuck with him through three years of careful mediocrity, never asking questions he didn't want answered, never pushing when Roan changed the subject.

That loyalty had limits. Everyone's did.

"I'll be careful," Roan said.

"That's not—"

"I'll be *strategic*." He pulled his arm free gently. "There's a difference."

He walked away before Brendt could respond, but instead of heading toward the training wing, he cut across the administrative courtyard. Ines Carrow making a personal visit was out of protocol. A signature could have been sent through a runner. She wanted a conversation.

Her office was tucked between the auditing department and a disused records archive, a single lamp illuminating the desk where she sat. She didn't look up when he opened the door, only gestured for him to close it.

"I'm not supposed to see students past curfew."

Roan sat. She pushed a document across the desk—his tournament registration, the credit allocation already adjusted in steady handwriting.

"Baseline wasn't enough. I pulled from the academic reserve. It's legal, barely. The trail exists, but it's clean."

"You're showing me the trail."

"Because someone will follow it if they look. Selene Ardent came by yesterday asking for your family file. I told her it was under Warden Covenant. She didn't push, but she'll come back with more weight next time."

Roan read the document. The increase was significant—forty percent above standard allocation, drawn from a clause he hadn't known existed. An unasked favor.

"Why tell me this?"

"Because I don't do favors without return, Vance. If you survive the tournament, you owe me a straight answer about what you found in your father's legacy."

The offer was clean and transactional. "And if I don't make it?"

"Then the investment was bad and this conversation never happened."

Roan signed the document. Pressed his thumb into the inkpad, then onto the parchment. The mark registered his new Vitalis signature.

Ines studied the fresh print without changing expression. "You're stronger than you were at enrollment. Stronger than you were this morning, if the readout is accurate. The tournament evaluators will notice. Edit your stance, your recovery speed, your visible Vitalis expenditure. Don't let them see the gap."

Roan tucked the pen back into its stand. "That's specific advice."

"Your father paid for three pieces. That was the second. You have one left."

He left without counting.

Outside, the corridor lamps were dimmed for the late hour. Ines Carrow was an asset, but a conditional one. Her loyalty was to the process, not to him. As long as he fit the process, she'd smooth his path. The first time he deviated in a way that threatened her position, she'd close the account.

He entered the eastern training wing and locked the door behind him. The room was empty at this hour, as he'd anticipated. He set his bag against the wall and stood still, letting his mind settle.

*Selene Ardent.* Her questions weren't random. The variable was the vector: personal ambition or House instruction. A scout for a larger interest, or an independent player building her own leverage with the Vance name as a building block. The distinction changed the counterplay. Either way, she needed monitoring.

He filed it under active observation and turned his attention to the bones in his arm.

He struck the reinforced training post with gradually increasing force, cataloging the rebound impact as it registered against his forearms. The bone hardening was real. His skeleton wasn't just harder—it was *designed* differently. The internal architecture optimized for absorbing and distributing kinetic force, the marrow producing something that drew energy from his Vitalis flow to maintain integrity at a level he hadn't evolved to achieve.

*Cross-tier hardness.*

The Codex had done more than reverse the damage. It had rebuilt him using principles from Aldric's notes, the same principles that had allowed Inversion archive cultivators to survive forces that would shatter normal bones. This was their legacy. The knowledge they'd killed an entire civilization to suppress.

And now his son was sitting in a training room, running diagnostic tests on a skeletal structure that had been illegal to possess for seventy years.

The tournament was six days away. Selene Ardent was asking questions. And somewhere in the administration tower, Ines Carrow was watching to see if the Vance name was worth the debt she'd just bought.

The ledger was still complicated. But for the first time in months, the variables were shifting in his favor.
