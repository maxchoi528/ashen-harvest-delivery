Chapter 13: The First Haul

The hallway outside the lecture hall smelled like old sweat and the cheap disinfectant the cleaning crew used between classes. Forty-two students filing in, all of them posturing—shoulders back, chins up, pretending they belonged here.

Roan counted them. Classified them. Moved through the crowd with measured steps, not fast, not slow, just precise—each gap taken the moment it appeared.

Three Tempered in this section alone. One Hardened. The Hardened was a girl with black hair and a scar across her knuckles, and she was looking at Roan like he was something she hadn't catalogued yet.

He didn't look back. The arithmetic didn't require eye contact.

The Codex Shard sat quiet at the base of his skull. No readout. No whisper. He was grateful for the silence.

He found a seat near the back. Three rows from the exit.

Brendt would be here in six minutes. Theon Mercer would approach him in nine. Garrick would make a comment about Bloodscourge in twelve, and Roan would have to decide whether to laugh or correct him.

He ran the scenarios.

Scenario one: laugh. Let it pass. Preserve the mask. Stay invisible.

Scenario two: correct him. Establish superiority now, while the stakes were low. Risk making an enemy of someone who might become useful.

Scenario three: do neither. Redirect. Make Garrick feel like the conversation was his idea.

He chose three. It always felt like the right choice.

“Roan.”

Brendt slid into the seat beside him. “You're quiet.”

“I'm always quiet.”

“You're quieter quiet.” Brendt's eyes swept the room, catalogued the exits, found nothing that concerned him. The man had no idea how useful that habit was. “You good?”

“Good is relative.”

“Translation: no.” Brendt settled back, crossing his arms. “Wanna talk about it?”

No.

“I had a dream.”

Not technically a lie. The Codex Shard had pulsed three times last night, and the voice—Aldric's voice—kept bleeding into his REM cycle. Something about patience. Something about the seventh strike. Something that felt like a warning wrapped in memory.

Roan didn't trust memory. Memory was a ledger that always favoured the debtor.

“About what?” Brendt asked.

“Old things.”

Brendt didn't push. That was the thing about him—he knew when to let silence do the work. Most people thought silence was an invitation. Brendt understood it was a wall, and walls were sometimes necessary.

“Alright,” Brendt said. “Let me know if you need anything.”

He did. That went into the ledger. Brendt's willingness to offer unprompted help. Useful data. Noted.

The lecture hall filled. Professor Martin Hawkes stood at the front with a pointer and a slide showing the twelve-tier Vitalis hierarchy.

“Welcome to Tier Theory 101.”

The room went quiet. Even the most arrogant students straightened.

“Good. You understand hierarchy. Some of you think you understand what it means.” Hawkes's eyes swept the room. Stopped, briefly, on Roan.

Roan felt it like a touch.

“This is a mistake,” Hawkes continued. “Understanding hierarchy isn't about knowing the tally. It's about knowing what breaks when the columns change.”

He clicked to the next slide. A chart showing Vitalis thresholds for each tier.

“Standard progression expects twelve years from Initiate to Forged. Natural talent gets you there in eight. Exceptional talent in five.” His voice carried no inflection. Just facts. Just math. “The Black Spire's record is three years, set in 1987 by a student named Vellmont. He died in a Rift incident four months after graduation.”

Chuckles. Nervous laughter. The reminder that talent and survival weren't the same thing.

“In this room, approximately—” Hawkes's gaze swept again, “—eighty percent of you won't see Forged tier before you graduate. Twenty percent will make it, but half of those will wash out within five years due to injuries, burnout, or better financial opportunities in the private sector.”

Cold. Clinical. True.

Roan watched the students react. Some angry. Some scared. Some calculating whether they'd chosen the wrong path.

This was good teaching. This was teaching that would make students work harder because they'd been shown the cliff first.

“And the remaining five percent?” a student in the front row asked. Cocky. Hardened tier, if the Vitalis reading was accurate. Overconfident.

“The remaining five percent.” Hawkes smiled. It didn't reach his eyes. “They understand that the chart is a lie.”

A pause. The room waited.

“Thresholds exist, yes. You need approximately four thousand Vitalis to break into Forged. But thresholds aren't ceilings. They're floors. And some floors are made of paper.”

He clicked again. A new slide. Numbers Roan recognized—the standard readings for each tier.

“These are the public numbers. What the textbooks teach. What the Sentinel Bureau tests for.” Hawkes leaned against the podium. “What they don't teach you is that Vitalis readings are averaged. They smooth out peaks and valleys. They make a messy world look clean.”

Roan understood. He'd seen the Codex Shard's projections. The actual capacity requirements for true breakthrough were lower than reported, much lower, because the system was designed to gatekeep. To keep people dependent on institutions, on approved methods, on anything other than their own calculation.

“The public threshold for Forged is four thousand Vitalis.” Hawkes paused. “The actual minimum is twenty-two hundred. You could be walking around with thirty-eight hundred Vitalis and still be classified as Hardened, because you haven't triggered the breakthrough event yet.”

The room shifted. Students exchanging glances. Doing math.

Roan did the math. He'd tested at twelve hundred Vitalis yesterday. The Codex Shard said he was holding eighteen forty-seven in inverted capacity. If thresholds were lower than reported, he could breakthrough to Hardened within six months. Maybe sooner.

“How?” The hardened kid in the front row again. Demanding. Desperate.

“By understanding what the breakthrough is.” Hawkes straightened. “It's not a wall you break through. It's a door you open. The wall is real, yes. But doors have hinges. And hinges can be persuaded.”

He looked at Roan again.

Roan kept his face neutral. His Vitalis reading was still showing as twelve hundred. Low for a second-year. Not impressive enough to warrant attention.

But Hawkes was watching him like he was something to be solved.

The lecture continued. Hawkes outlined the mechanics of breakthrough—how Vitalis accumulated in the meridians, how pressure built until something gave, how the body restructured around the new baseline.

It was all standard theory. All public knowledge. The things he wasn't saying hung in the air like invisible architecture.

By the end, Roan had seventeen pages of notes. Twelve were legitimate. Five were his own calculations about what Hawkes had deliberately left out—but most of those calculations were still tentative. He'd need to test them before he could call them verified.

Brendt leaned over. “You look like you just found a map to buried treasure.”

“Something like that.”

“Can you share?”

Roan considered. Brendt had proven reliable. Trustworthy. But trust was a resource, and resources were finite.

“Later,” he said. “I need to verify some things first.”

“Fair enough.”

The lecture ended. Students filed out. Roan stayed.

Hawkes was packing up, moving with the deliberate efficiency of someone who'd done this a thousand times and saw no reason to rush.

Roan approached. Not directly—diagonally. Something about direct confrontation felt aggressive, and aggression invited resistance. Diagonal was exploratory. It gave both parties room to retreat.

“Professor Hawkes.”

Hawkes looked up. “Mr. Vance.”

Roan stopped. Two meters away. Respectful distance.

“I have a question.”

“I noticed you had several.”

“You said the public threshold was a lie.”

“I said the chart was a lie. Thresholds are real. The figures are just smoothed.”

“Right.” Roan processed. “If thresholds are floors, and floors are made of paper—”

“Then some people punch through faster than others.” Hawkes met his gaze. Held it. “Why are you asking me this?”

“Because the tally says I should be tier-limited by standard progression methods.”

“And you don't accept limits.”

“I accept constraints. There's a difference.” Limits were external. Constraints were everything—the rules that governed what was possible. Roan had learned to work within constraints, not against them. Working against constraints was inefficient. Working within them was just problem-solving.

Hawkes studied him. That look again—evaluating, calculating, deciding.

“You're testing well above average,” he said finally. “Top five percent in your cohort.”

“Bottom fifteen percent in the official rankings.”

“Official rankings measure compliance, not capability.”

Roan filed that. Another data point. Another hint that Hawkes was more than a professor giving basic lectures.

“I'm not going to tell you how to accelerate,” Hawkes said. “The system isn't set up to reward that kind of knowledge. The institutions need you dependent on their timelines, their methods, their approval.”

“I know.”

“Then what do you want?”

Roan thought about it. What did he want? The obvious answer was power—more Vitalis, faster breakthrough, the ability to stop being weak. But that was the surface answer. The ledger beneath it was more complex.

He wanted to understand the machine before he tried to break it.

“A framework,” he said. “Not instructions. Just the shape of the thing. So I can figure out my own path.”

Hawkes was quiet. The lecture hall was empty now. Just the two of them and the hum of the ventilation system.

“You're more dangerous than I expected,” Hawkes said. “At your age, most students want shortcuts. You want understanding.”

“Shortcuts are for people who don't have time to do the arithmetic.”

“And you have time?”

Roan thought about the Codex Shard. The weight of it in his blood. The voice that sometimes whispered from the base of his skull, offering cryptic warnings and colder truths.

“I have more than most,” he said. “And I'm going to use it.”

Hawkes nodded slowly. “Then I'll give you one thing. Free of charge, no strings attached.” He stepped closer, voice dropping. “Breakthrough isn't about accumulating enough Vitalis to break through. It's about reducing the resistance of your meridians until the existing Vitalis can do the work itself.”

Roan processed that. “Concentration versus pressure.”

“Exactly. Most students try to push more Vitalis through their meridians. They hit resistance, they get frustrated, they push harder. And the resistance just grows.” Hawkes's voice was lower now, meant only for him. “But if you focus on clearing the channels instead—expanding capacity, reducing friction—breakthrough becomes inevitable instead of effortful.”

“Standard teaching says to build pressure.”

“Standard teaching is designed to keep you dependent on institutions for breakthrough assistance. It's not malicious—just fits the economic incentives.”

Roan thought about the Codex Shard. The Inversion system didn't build pressure—it converted resistance into energy. The sidewound was, essentially, resistance. And the Inversion turned that resistance into Vitalis.

But the Inversion required an existing harmful effect. He couldn't just clear healthy meridians. He'd need to create a sidewound first—maybe push his vital channels to the point of micro-damage through extreme training, then reverse that damage to reduce overall resistance. The principle fit, but the trigger had to be real.

That changed the timeline. But it didn't break the math.

“That changes things,” Roan said.

“It changes everything.” Hawkes straightened. “Now go. I have office hours in fifteen minutes and students to disappoint.”

Roan nodded. Turned to leave.

“Roan.”

He stopped.

“You came to me with a question. That's good. But the better question is: what are you going to do with the answer?”

Roan considered. “Calculate. Test. Verify.” He paused. “Adapt.”

“Good. Those are the only three steps that matter.” Hawkes picked up his materials. “Don't let me down, Mr. Vance.”

He said it like he meant it. Like there was something at stake beyond academic performance. Like he knew exactly who Roan was and was waiting to see what Roan would become.

Roan left. The hallway felt different now—full of possibility, but also full of edges. He'd been given a tool. Now he needed to figure out how to use it without cutting himself.

Brendt was waiting by the courtyard entrance.

“You look like someone just handed you a loaded weapon,” Brendt said.

“Metaphorically, yes.”

“Want to tell me about it?”

Roan thought about it. Brendt had proven trustworthy. The math suggested Brendt would remain trustworthy. But trust was still a resource, and resources were still finite.

“Later,” he said again. “I need to run some tests first.”

“Tests. Right.” Brendt fell into step beside him. “Can I help?”

Roan considered. Having Brendt as a testing partner could accelerate verification. Could also expose the Inversion system if he wasn't careful.

But the alternative was slower. And slower meant more time for enemies to notice, for institutions to intervene, for the carefully constructed timeline to slip.

“Yes,” Roan said finally. “But I need to know something first.”

“What?”

“If I show you something unusual—something that doesn't make sense with standard theory—can you accept that without demanding full explanations right away?”

Brendt was quiet for a moment. Thinking. Roan appreciated that he thought instead of just reacting.

“I can try,” Brendt said slowly. “I won't promise to stop asking questions, but I can accept that some things don't get answered on my schedule.”

Good enough. The hesitation was healthy. A friend who accepted everything blindly would be a liability.

“Tomorrow,” Roan said. “After morning classes. Meet me at the east training grounds. The ones by the old wall.”

“Those are usually empty.”

“Exactly.”

Brendt nodded. “I'll be there.”

They parted ways. Roan headed toward the library. He needed to cross-reference Hawkes's framework with known breakthrough mechanics. Verify the theory. Find the edges where the official story didn't hold together.

The Codex Shard pulsed at the base of his skull. No words. Just a pulse—a reminder that it was there, waiting.

*Patience. The third strike comes when you're ready, not when you're desperate. — A.V.*

Third strike. Sevenfold Bleed had seven forms. He could perform the first. The second was still theory. The third—he didn't know what the third was yet. But he would find out.

He entered the library. Found a quiet corner. Opened his notebook to a fresh page.

The numbers waited.

That night, in his dorm room, Roan sat on his bed with the Codex Shard's weight settling into his meridians like ice water in warm glass.

He'd reviewed the framework. Marked the assumptions that needed testing. Designed the first experiment.

Tomorrow, he would begin clearing his meridians—not by accumulation, but by reduction. He would push his channels to the edge of tolerance during a controlled training session, creating localized sidewounds. Then he would use the Inversion to reverse those micro-damages, converting each point of resistance into a step forward.

The official path expected three years to Hardened. Four more to Forged.

His projections said eighteen months to Hardened. Maybe less.

But the balance also said something else. Something he kept circling back to, unable to resolve.

Hawkes had given him the framework. But Hawkes had given it too easily. Too freely. Like he was waiting for Roan to find it on his own, so Roan would trust it when he did.

And that meant Hawkes knew something Roan didn't.

About the Inversion. About the Codex Shard. About the seven forms and what came after.

Roan closed his eyes. Listened to the silence at the base of his skull.

“You are testing me while you teach,” he said.

No response. Just the weight. Just the waiting.

He opened his eyes. Looked at his hands.

Tomorrow, he would start building the foundation for something much larger than simple breakthrough acceleration. He would start building toward the third strike.

And he would find out what Hawkes was really waiting for.

The Codex Shard pulsed once more. A single word, bleeding into his thoughts like a drop of ink in water.

*Good.*

Then nothing.

Just the weight. Just the arithmetic. Just the understanding that everything had changed, and he was only starting to understand what that meant.

He lay back. Closed his eyes.

Sleep came fast. No dreams. Just the ledger, waiting for him to fill the next line.
