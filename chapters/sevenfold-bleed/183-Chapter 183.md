Chapter 183: Vorath Moves

The infirmary became a courtroom because Cole understood architecture.

Not the decorative kind. The practical kind. Most people thought a room was defined by walls, windows and furniture, plus whether the chair looked expensive enough to justify an administrative title. Cole treated rooms as pressure devices. He moved people into them, decided where they could stand, determined what they could see, and used the arrangement to make lies work harder.

Roan approved in principle.

He approved less when the room chosen for the first post-duel conference was still the one where his ribs hurt.

Medical lattice light hung over his bed in pale bands, each strip cycling a slow diagnostic pulse across his torso. The cracked Vance Pendant sat on a tray beside him, sealed under transparent wardglass. The outer casing still held enough resonance to blur remote observation, though the core had long since merged into his spine. With it broken, the next recognition event became more expensive. Sidewound lay across his lap because several healers had tried to remove it and discovered that Roan's tolerance for medical necessity stopped before disarmament. His left hand had been braced in a flexible splint. Three fingers remained numb in a way the healer described as temporary with the careful voice professionals used when temporary meant expensive.

Drogan stood near the door. Not leaned. Not paced. Stood. A barricade with a pulse.

Selene occupied the visitor chair, posture perfect, face composed, one sleeve hiding a hand that had been clenched since the word extradition entered the conversation.

Iris had stolen a rolling stool from medical storage and sat backward on it, forearms crossed over the back, using irreverence as armor. Ines stood near the window, not because the view mattered, but because reflections let her watch everyone at once. Headmaster Cole attended through a projection crystal on the bedside table. The projection had no dramatic shimmer. The man's face appeared, flat and sharp, like a legal document that had learned disappointment.

Brendt wasn't present. He was awake, conscious and irritated, then under healer instruction not to leave bed for forty-eight hours. He had sent one message asking whether the legal emergency was "the normal kind or the everyone-is-being-stupid kind."

Roan had replied: *Yes.*

Brendt had sent back seventeen dots and no further words.

That was healthy.

Cole finished reading the first Greel statement. He didn't read it aloud. He had projected it onto the far wall instead, which was worse. Spoken nonsense dissipated. Written nonsense lingered where everyone could examine the grammar of cowardice.

House Greel condemns the unlawful coercion of a minor heir under compromised arena conditions.

Minor heir. Kael was eighteen and Forged, then trained to kill under a command oath. Legally minor under northern House compact inheritance law until formal bloodline emancipation. Convenient. Roan admired the structure the way one admired a knife hidden in a sleeve: professionally, not emotionally.

The confession extracted during the duel can't be considered valid testimony.

Extracted. Good word. It made Kael sound mined.

Roan Vance is requested for cooperative inquiry under Northern Compact jurisdiction to determine the presence of oath-tampering, foreign bloodline interference, and prohibited anti-sovereign techniques.

Requested.

Roan looked at Cole. "They used inquiry instead of custody."

Cole nodded. "Inquiry sounds voluntary to people not paying attention."

"It also avoids triggering the Spire protection clause."

"Yes."

"Sloppy or deliberate?"

"Deliberate."

Iris rolled the stool half an inch forward. "Grandfather's people used that phrasing in finance investigations. You invite someone for cooperative inquiry. They decline. Then you say refusal implies consciousness of guilt. Then you petition to freeze assets, revoke privileges, or demand third party intervention."

"House law by debt collection," Ines said.

"Most House law is debt collection with older coats."

Drogan grunted. "They can't take him."

Cole's projected eyes moved to him. "Not cleanly."

"I hate that qualifier."

"You should. It's where most bodies accumulate."

Roan shifted against the pillows. Pain moved through his ribs in a slow sheet. The pendant's shell had taken the worst of Kael's frost pressure and cracked for the privilege. Without it, Vorath's remote observation might have recognized more than Codex surface bleed. The broken casing meant the next recognition event carried higher cost.

Resource ledger: legal confession, public sympathy, damaged anchor, unstable quota, exposure risk.

Enemy ledger: Vorath had lost deniability through Kael but gained justification through the method of exposure. He wouldn't argue Kael was innocent. That argument had failed on camera. He would argue process. Powerful people loved process after evidence arrived.

Rule ledger: Spire neutrality protected enrolled students from external custody unless the school recognized jurisdictional misconduct. Greel needed Cole to either surrender Roan voluntarily or allow enough ambiguity for a higher compact board to compel review. Cole would refuse. Vorath knew that. Therefore the request wasn't designed to succeed.

It was designed to create the next step.

"He wants refusal," Roan said.

Everyone looked at him.

Cole didn't. Cole continued looking at the statement as if he had already reached the same conclusion and disliked that a concussed student had done so from a hospital bed.

Drogan's eyes narrowed. "Explain."

"If Cole accepts, Vorath gets me. Good outcome. If Cole refuses quietly, Vorath gets a public narrative: Black Spire shelters illegal oath attackers. Good enough. If Cole refuses loudly, Vorath can escalate by claiming insult to northern sovereignty. Also useful. The demand isn't a door. It's a wedge."

"Between whom?" Selene asked.

"Cole and external governance. Black Spire and neutral law. Students and faculty. House donors and administration. Anyone who prefers stability to truth."

Iris's mouth tightened. She had spent enough time around donors to understand the species.

Cole steepled his fingers within the projection. "Correct."

"That's irritating," Drogan said.

"Usually," Roan said.

Cole ignored both. "At 09:00 tomorrow, I will receive formal diplomatic representatives from the Northern Compact, House Greel counsel, two neutral Spire auditors, and one Sentinel observer. They will request your temporary transfer for inquiry."

"No."

"I haven't finished."

"I was saving time."

Cole's projected expression didn't change, but several degrees left the room. "You aren't attending."

Roan considered several replies.

The most obvious was also the least useful: *You can't stop me.* False. Cole could stop him in at least six ways, some legal and several pharmaceutical.

The second was: *I need to hear them.* Also weak. Ines could record. Selene could summarize. Iris could translate aristocratic poison into usable venom.

The useful reply wasn't about his desire.

"If I am absent," Roan said, "they control my shape in the room."

Cole's gaze sharpened.

Roan continued. "If I attend, they must speak while the person they want to take is visibly injured by the heir who confessed. Cameras may be barred, but records remain. Refusing to let me attend makes sense if you want order. It doesn't make sense if you want refusal expensive."

Drogan muttered, "There it is."

"You can barely stand," Selene said.

"I don't need to stand well. I need to sit visibly."

"That isn't reassuring."

"It wasn't meant to be."

Ines tilted her head. "He's right."

Selene looked at her.

Ines shrugged. "I hate it when that happens too. But he's. Greel's best move is to abstract him into a procedural threat. Put him in the room with a splinted hand and cracked pendant, and they have to explain why the assassination target is the dangerous one. That costs them phrasing."

"Phrasing," Drogan said.

"Phrasing is where legal murder hides."

Cole's projection stayed silent.

That mattered.

Roan used the silence. "Vorath's next move after refusal will be personal attendance."

Drogan went still.

Selene's face lost another layer of color.

Iris looked at him sharply. "You are guessing."

"Yes."

"Based on?"

"Remote observation failed. Kael failed. Legal inquiry will fail. He has already risked enough reputation that delegation looks weak. Also he wants to see what I am before choosing the next pressure point."

"And you think he will come himself."

"Not to kill me immediately."

Drogan's laugh had no humor. "Comforting."

"To classify me."

The Codex Shard pulsed faintly against Roan's spine, as if the word had touched something alive.

[Hostile classification vector probable.]

[Concealment degraded.]

[External anchor repair recommended.]

Roan didn't react.

Cole saw something in his face anyway. The man missed little and disliked missing anything.

"Your pendant?" Cole asked.

"Compromised."

"Can it be repaired?"

"Eckhart can try."

Drogan's mouth flattened. "We aren't sending you across Ironholm while Greel pressure is active."

"No. We send the pendant."

"Eckhart will ask questions."

"He already knows enough to ask worse ones."

Ines pushed away from the window. "I'll take it."

Drogan said, "No."

She looked amused. "That was quick."

"You are competent enough to get there and too curious to avoid making things worse."

"Accurate but rude."

"I'll take it," Selene said.

Everyone turned to her.

The proposal was strategically poor for several reasons and politically elegant for one. House Ardent carrying an item connected to Roan Vance into an old weaponsmith's shop during a Greel legal attack would create traceable movement, but it would also signal that at least one major House considered Roan worth open procedural risk.

Selene knew that.

So did Cole.

"No," Roan said.

Selene's eyes narrowed. "You don't get to reject every useful risk taken by someone else."

"I do when the risk gives Ardent a claim over the evidence chain."

"That isn't why I offered."

"No. But it's what your aunt's counsel would use if this becomes a House matter."

She had no immediate answer.

Good. Correct answers often arrived after the first objection died.

Iris lifted one finger. "I can carry it."

"No," three people said at once.

She smiled. "Progress. Shared trust."

Roan looked at Ines. "Send it by courier under decoy."

"Eckhart will refuse anonymous work."

"Not anonymous. Paid."

"That's not better."

"It often is."

Drogan rubbed a hand over his face. "I'll take the damned pendant."

Roan looked at him.

The old instructor glared back. "I can still walk across a city."

"That isn't the concern."

"I also know when not to talk to Eckhart."

That was more useful.

Cole nodded once. "Fine. Drogan repairs the anchor. Vance remains under medical watch. Tomorrow morning, I refuse transfer."

"In public record," Roan said.

"Yes."

"And I attend."

"No."

Roan waited.

Cole continued, "You will attend as an injured student under institutional protection, seated, silent unless addressed, with medical staff present. You won't speak unless I permit it."

"That last term is unnecessary."

"It's the only term I care about."

Roan considered.

Speaking at the wrong time would waste pressure. Cole's refusal carried institutional authority. Roan's voice should be saved for a larger audience. A public statement after Greel's demand failed would hit harder than commentary during the demand.

"Accepted," he said.

Cole looked mildly surprised.

"You thought I would argue."

"I priced the possibility."

"Correctly?"

"Apparently not."

Roan allowed himself half a breath that could almost be a smile.

\- break -

For the next hour, the infirmary became less a place of healing than a place where people discovered new ways to underestimate paperwork.

Roan preferred paperwork to most forms of violence. Violence had a tendency to make noise and leave witnesses with opinions. Paperwork moved quietly, multiplied in drawers, and survived long enough to embarrass descendants. Greel understood this, which was why the first House statement hadn't called for punishment, revenge, or blood. It called for inquiry.

Inquiry was a polite word for opening a box and deciding afterward whether the person inside had ever agreed to climb in.

Iris built the first response tree on a slate propped against Roan's blanket.

"If they frame oath destabilization as bloodline assault," she said, "they can request compact intervention without proving assassination intent. If they frame the duel as compromised medical procedure, they can demand arena logs. If they frame your method as foreign anti-sovereign technique, they can pull in Sentinel Bureau under public safety review."

"Can they prove foreign?" Selene asked.

"No."

"Can they imply it?"

"Easily."

Roan watched the branches expand. Iris wrote like someone who had been taught to hide knives inside accounting categories. That made sense. Vell finance had done much worse with less ink.

"What do they fear most?" he asked.

"Disclosure," Iris said immediately.

Selene nodded. "Kael's oath."

Ines said, "And how it got through enrollment."

Cole's projection remained on the bedside table, but his eyes sharpened. "That implicates internal processing."

"Not necessarily corruption," Roan said. "Could be privilege assumed to be harmless."

Cole's expression suggested he disliked that because it was probable.

House students carried unexamined exceptions the way other people carried keys. A clerk seeing Greel documentation might not inspect a bloodline seal closely. A registrar might accept northern certifications because challenging them meant three departments of argument and one expensive complaint. A medical office might note unusual oath resonance and file it under heritage variation rather than compulsion.

Systems didn't need traitors when they had fatigue.

"Find the path Kael used," Roan said.

Cole said, "That's an internal investigation."

"Yes."

"You aren't part of it."

"I didn't request inclusion."

"You were about to."

"No. I was about to request results be preserved before anyone loyal to convenience edits them."

Cole stared at him for a second, then looked away as if annoyed by the reasonableness of the sentence. "Done."

Ines made a note. "Resource gained: enrollment chain preservation."

Roan looked at her.

She shrugged. "RoyalRoad mode. Rules matter. Also, writing it down annoys people."

"Continue."

She smiled.

Drogan didn't. "This isn't a game."

"No," Roan said. "Games reset."

That ended the comment thread.

A healer entered ten minutes later with a sedative patch and the cautious face of a woman approaching a patient who had already argued with three licensed professionals and one headmaster.

"Mr. Vance," she said, "you need sleep."

"I need clear cognition."

"You need both. One is currently less negotiable than you think."

Roan looked at the patch. "Side effects?"

"Reduced pain response, slower reflexes, suppressed Vitalis spike, eight to twelve hours of enforced sleep."

"No."

Drogan said, "Take the patch."

"No."

"You are running on duel shock and spite."

"Spite has acceptable burn efficiency."

The healer's eyes narrowed. "Your left hand almost froze from the inside."

"Almost isn't a diagnosis."

"It's when I write the report."

Roan considered the political value of being documented as noncompliant. Moderate. The physiological cost of refusing all recovery. High. The danger of being sedated while Vorath's declaration remained pending. Higher.

"Half dose," he said.

The healer looked offended. "This isn't a market stall."

"It's now."

Iris, traitorously, said, "Half-dose stabilizer without reflex suppression exists."

The healer glared. "It also tastes terrible and requires monitoring."

"I am surrounded by monitors," Roan said.

"Those are political parasites."

Ines put a hand to her chest. "Recognized at last."

Cole closed his eyes.

Five minutes later, Roan accepted a liquid stabilizer that tasted like burned mint and formal regret. It dulled the rib pain by perhaps ten percent and left his mind clear enough to continue planning.

Acceptable.

The healer left after telling Drogan to make him sleep.

Drogan waited until the door closed. "You heard her."

"Yes."

"And?"

"She overestimated your influence."

Drogan looked at the ceiling, perhaps asking it for patience. The ceiling wisely didn't answer.

Selene's slate chimed. She checked it and frowned.

"House channels are splitting," she said. "Smaller Houses dislike Greel pressure but won't say so publicly. Larger Houses are waiting to see whether Black Spire folds."

"Market behavior," Iris said.

"Political behavior."

"Same disease. Different clothing."

Roan lifted his good hand. "Which Houses move first if Cole refuses?"

Selene projected a list.

House Ardent sat in the middle, marked *constrained support*. House Vale appeared with a question mark. House Mercer was neutral. Three northern Houses aligned near Greel. Two coastal Houses had no public position but heavy scholarship contracts with Black Spire.

"Scholarships are pressure points," Roan said.

Cole's face hardened. "I know."

"Greel may threaten student funding."

"Yes."

"That makes this bigger than me."

"It never was only about you."

The answer landed with more weight than expected.

Roan looked at the House list again. If Greel made Black Spire pay through scholarships, students with no Houses would suffer first. Greycross children. Border-zone candidates. Brendt, if not for Roan's business accounts. People whose lives could be redirected by a donor's signature.

Vorath would know that.

Roan added a note.

If Greel threatens scholarships, publish donor leverage map.

Iris leaned over and read it upside down. "That map will get you sued by fourteen people."

"Only if inaccurate enough today."

"No, especially if accurate enough today."

"Then accuracy remains essential."

Selene looked at him for a moment, and Roan couldn't tell whether she disapproved or understood. Both.

His slate chimed with a family channel.

Lyra.

The message contained no greeting, no panic, and no punctuation wasted on politeness.

*Mom saw the duel clip. I told her you were alive because otherwise she would call you seventeen times. You should call her before she decides to come to Ironholm and embarrass everyone.*

Roan stared at the message.

Several strategic threats in the room immediately became less interesting.

Drogan saw his face. "What?"

"Family logistics."

Selene's expression softened by a fraction. Iris looked away with the tact of someone who had learned it late and used it sparingly.

Roan typed back: *I am injured but stable. Don't let her travel.*

Lyra replied: *Say that yourself, idiot.*

Roan considered ignoring the instruction. Then he considered Mira Vance arriving at Black Spire during a Greel diplomatic crisis.

He called.

The conversation lasted less than two minutes.

Mira didn't cry. Roan preferred that and hated noticing. She asked whether he could breathe properly, whether his hand would recover, whether Brendt was alive, whether the people who sent Kael were still dangerous, and whether Roan would tell her the truth if the answer was no.

He answered in order: yes, yes and yes, plus no.

There was a long silence after the last answer.

Then Mira said, "Your father bought himself time with the same dry precision when he did not want to answer the real question."

Roan looked at the far wall.

"I know."

"Roan."

"Yes."

"Don't let dead men decide every room you enter."

He didn't answer immediately.

In the infirmary, everyone pretended not to listen. Badly.

"I'll account for the risk," he said.

"That isn't the same."

"No."

Another silence.

Then Mira sighed. "Your sister says you are being an idiot."

"She's fourteen."

"She's often right."

"Unfortunately."

"Call tomorrow."

"I will."

The call ended.

Roan set the slate down.

Lyra sent one final message before he could put the slate away.

*Don't die before calling tomorrow. Mom will be mad and I will sell your old coat.*

Roan looked at the message for three seconds.

The coat in question had been too small for him for two years and worth perhaps twelve credits if sold to a blind buyer during bad weather. Lyra knew that. The threat wasn't economic. It was domestic jurisdiction, the kind no House compact could appeal.

He typed: *Keep the coat.*

She answered: *Earn it.*

He saved the exchange in a folder that had no label and no backup. Then he put the slate face down.

For a moment, the legal room, the House charts, the declarations, and the cold pressure of Vorath's approaching name all moved one step farther away. Not gone. Never gone. Just displaced by a kitchen in Greycross, by Lyra standing near the sink with crossed arms, by Mira holding a phone too tightly and pretending seventeen-year-old sons could be managed with tone.

Then the projection crystal chimed.

Cole's eyes shifted to an incoming message visible only from his side. His face changed by so little that most people would have missed it.

Roan didn't.

"What?" Drogan asked.

Cole looked back at the room.

"Vorath Greel has just issued a personal declaration through Northern Compact channels."

Ines swore softly.

Selene's spine straightened.

Iris's fingers tightened on the stool back.

Cole projected the declaration onto the wall.

It was shorter than the House statement.

That made it better.

Roan Vance has attacked my House, compromised my blood, and defamed my name under the protection of Black Spire walls. If the Spire won't deliver him for inquiry, I will attend in person to seek lawful satisfaction.

No mention of Kael's confession.

No denial of the assassination order.

No threat phrased as threat.

Just attendance.

Roan read it twice.

The Codex Shard burned cold along his spine.

[Hostile approach confirmed.]

[Vorath Greel: active vector.]

[Recommendation: gather witnesses.]

Roan looked at the declaration.

"Good," he said.

Drogan stared at him. "Boy."

"He committed to movement."

Selene's voice was low. "That's what worries us."

Roan touched the empty space on his chest where the pendant's casing should have been.

A classified threat was dangerous. A moving threat had schedule, route, protocol and ego, plus witnesses. Movement turned monsters into logistics.

Logistics could be taxed. Properly.

"Then," Roan said, "we start charging him."
