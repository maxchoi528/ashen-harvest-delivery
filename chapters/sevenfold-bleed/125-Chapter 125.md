Chapter 125: A Message Under Seal

The first thing Roan did after becoming famous was lock the door.

The second thing was check whether the lock mattered.

It did, technically. The dormitory door carried a standard student privacy ward, a mechanical deadbolt, a sensor strip tied to Black Spire housing security, and a cheap override latch that had been installed by someone who had never lived in a building with enemies. Against ordinary students, it would hold. Against a determined House operative, it would provide enough delay for Roan to decide where to place the body. Against House Greel, it was likely decorative.

Still, decorative barriers had value.

They told intruders where contempt began.

Roan set a chair under the handle anyway.

Brendt would have called that paranoid if Brendt had been present. Brendt wasn't present because Roan had sent him to eat, answer none of the reporters' questions, and sleep in a room where Drogan had already placed two wards and one threat disguised as advice. Brendt had objected to being managed. Roan had pointed out that Brendt had almost walked into three separate camera clusters while trying to process the rankings update. The objection had weakened.

Ines had vanished with copies of public telemetry and the expression of a woman who intended to monetize other people's confusion.

Drogan had escorted Roan back to the dormitory in silence, stopped outside the door, and said, "If anything happens, you call."

Roan had said, "Define anything."

Drogan had looked at him.

Then he had said, "Your father asked that too."

Not useful.

Very useful.

Roan had gone inside before asking the wrong question and getting no answer.

He didn't begin with mysticism.

That was how people got themselves killed by artifacts while feeling deep.

Roan began with controls.

First, he placed three mundane markers around the desk: a pencil balanced on a cup, a strip of tape across the drawer seam, and a coin on the floor near the chair leg. If the room distorted, moved, or lost time, one of them might show it. Not guaranteed. Reality had more ways to lie than tape had adhesive. But cheap controls were better than reverent staring.

Second, he opened a local recording pane disconnected from the network. It would capture video, audio and temperature, plus Vitalis fluctuation inside the room. If the Shard ate the file, that was information. If the file survived but contradicted memory, that was better information.

Third, he wrote his current memory anchors on paper.

Mira. Lyra. Brendt. Drogan. Aldric. House Greel. Greycross. Black Spire. Cyrus down in seven seconds. Mother said eat.

He looked at the final line, then added:

Half ration bar on desk.

Specific anchors beat emotional ones. Emotional anchors changed under pressure. A ration bar was either there or not.

Fourth, he tested voluntary access.

Status.

Nothing.

Codex.

Nothing.

Inversion quota.

Nothing.

Sidewound.

A faint internal pressure, then silence again.

That one mattered. The Shard recognized the concept even before the weapon existed. Sidewound wasn't only a term. It was a category inside the archive. Categories had history. History had owners.

Roan wrote: *Shard selectively suppressing response. Not inactive.*

Now the room was quiet.

Too quiet for a night when half the academy had learned his name and the other half was deciding what to do with it. Outside the window, Black Spire's tournament lights still cut through the clouds in rotating bands of gold and black. Distant cheers drifted up from the lower courtyards. Somewhere, students were celebrating a victory they hadn't earned but intended to borrow. Somewhere else, Cyrus Ashford was waking with chest pain and a ruined mythology.

Roan sat at his desk and opened three panes.

Public reaction.

Private messages.

Physiological log.

He checked the last first.

Body temperature: slightly elevated.

Heart rhythm: stable.

Vitalis circulation: high strain in seven primary channels, resolving.

Pyrebrand residue: moderate.

Marrowforge integrity: stable.

Sevenfold micro-tears: present, minor.

Inversion quota: full.

Codex Shard: active, nonresponsive.

That last line was new.

The Shard had never been inactive, exactly. It pulsed, warned, calculated, mocked him in his father's dry cadence, and occasionally withheld information with bureaucratic malice. Nonresponsive meant Roan could feel it humming against the seventh vertebra but couldn't draw a prompt, status fragment, or even an insult out of it.

He tested the gap three times, then deliberately closed the connection. *Projection preparation. Or a security blackout tied to the name anchor.* A silent system after hostile recognition was bad.

He opened public reaction next.

Mistake.

Not operationally. Emotionally, perhaps, though Roan disliked granting forums that much power.

The student boards had turned feral.

SEVEN SECONDS.

VANCE JUST RETIRED A BLOODLINE.

CYRUS WAS ALWAYS OVERRATED, DISCUSS.

IS ROAN USING A FORBIDDEN METHOD? FULL FRAME ANALYSIS THREAD.

GREYCROSS KIDS BUILT DIFFERENT.

The last one had three thousand replies, half of them from people who had never been within ten districts of Greycross and considered poverty an aesthetic. Roan closed it before becoming tempted to correct them.

The professional feeds were worse in a cleaner way.

Analysts argued about his technique. Sponsors speculated about whether his brand value fit "disciplined violence with underdog appeal." A retired Eastreach fighter called him "dangerously efficient," which was one of the few comments approaching accuracy. A Vale-affiliated commentator implied the match should be reviewed for illegal spinal artifact use.

Roan saved that clip.

Not because it threatened him. Because it revealed the first official line of counterattack.

Illegal artifact.

Predictable. Useful. If Vale pushed too hard without evidence, they would make themselves look desperate. If they had evidence, Roan needed to know what they thought counted.

Private messages filled the third pane.

Most were useless.

Congratulations from students who had mocked him in earlier terms. Sponsorship inquiries. Training invitations. Two threats from anonymous accounts. One marriage joke from Brendt that Roan deleted without reading past the first line. A message from Lyra containing only:

*Eamon says you were less awful than expected. This isn't praise. Also: good job.*

Roan looked at that longer than the others.

Then he moved it into the protected folder.

Mira had sent one message.

*I saw the end. Your father would have asked whether you ate afterward. Eat.*

Roan stared at the sentence.

He hadn't eaten.

Annoying.

He stood, retrieved a ration bar from the drawer, and ate half while reviewing threat messages. The first threat had poor grammar and poorer operational planning. The second contained a photograph of his dormitory building taken from the west courtyard.

That one he saved.

The angle suggested someone with student access, not a professional.

Good. Amateur threats were information-rich.

At 11:37 p.m. The room temperature dropped.

Not enough to fog breath. Enough to tighten skin along his forearms.

Roan closed the panes.

The Codex Shard stopped humming.

The silence became physical.

He didn't stand. Standing would signal alarm to any observer, internal or external. He placed the remaining half ration bar on the desk, aligned it with the edge because his hands wanted a task, and waited.

Pain touched his spine.

Not sharp.

Familiar.

Like the first Bloodscourge Draught in Garrick's back room. Like the Sevenfold Bleed opening all seven channels at once. Like Wraithmilk tearing through his mind and leaving three seconds missing. Like the moment House Greel's cold attention swept across him after the final and the Shard answered before Roan told it to A pressure built behind his eyes.

For a moment, Roan considered calling Drogan.

The thought arrived cleanly, wearing practical clothing. Drogan had known Aldric. Drogan might stabilize the situation. Drogan had warned him to call if anything happened, and this qualified under even the narrowest definition unless one was Roan being difficult on purpose.

Then Roan examined the impulse more closely.

Calling Drogan would bring a witness. A witness would bring questions. Questions would bring emotional contamination before Roan understood the mechanics. Worse, Drogan might react to the projection as a man seeing a dead brother rather than as a field operative evaluating an unstable archive event. That reaction could damage him, the projection, or both.

Brendt was worse. Brendt would help immediately and think later, which was why he remained alive in some situations and endangered in others.

Ines would try to record everything and steal the air if it appeared useful.

No.

First contact belonged to Roan.

Not because he deserved it.

Because he had the fewest external variables.

He set the local recorder to redundant capture with a thirty-second delay. If the room suffered a memory wipe, the delay might preserve pre-event frames in buffer. Might. The word carried little weight, but it carried more than prayer.

Then he placed the tournament finalist token beside the paper anchors.

Metal on wood. Small sound. Real.

He had won it today.

Cyrus had fallen.

House Greel had watched.

The Shard had awakened something wearing Aldric's face.

The sequence mattered.

He wrote one more note: *Projection follows hostile recognition + public name anchor.*

Then he crossed out "projection" and wrote "residual."

The Shard had been very clear.

Don't trust what you want to hear.

Roan underlined it twice.

[Name anchor strengthened.]

The prompt appeared without his asking.

The letters didn't sit in his vision as usual. They hung deeper, as if written on the inside of bone.

[Hostile recognition recorded.]

[External lineage threat: confirmed.]

[Archive fragment integrity: unstable.]

[Residual imprint rising.]

Roan's left hand closed around the desk edge.

"Define residual imprint," he said.

The room didn't answer.

The Shard did.

[Do not move.]

Roan went still.

That wasn't a recommendation. The Shard used recommendation when it wanted compliance without admitting urgency. Warning when a threat had teeth. Don't move was new.

The pain in his spine became heat.

Then light.

Not visible light. The room remained dark except for the city glow beyond the curtains. But something inside Roan's perception opened, not outward like a vision, but inward like a door cut into a memory he didn't own.

A cup of tea on a metal table.

A black coat hanging over a chair.

A woman laughing in a language Roan had never heard and somehow almost understood.

Seven lines carved into white stone.

A hand, his father's hand, younger, unscarred, writing symbols that rearranged themselves before Roan could read them.

Then blood.

Too much blood.

Roan's breath shortened.

He forced it steady. A heartbeat.

The pain crossed the threshold where Inversion usually offered itself.

No prompt came.

The Shard didn't offer reversal.

It was using the pain.

Roan disliked being used by systems, dead civilizations, and fathers in approximately that order.

"Explain," he said.

The heat sharpened.

[Archive fragment integrity: 41%.]

[Name anchor sufficient for projection attempt.]

[Memory source: Aldric Vance.]

[Warning: imprint is not consciousness.]

[Warning: consciousness may be simulated under grief response.]

[Warning: do not trust what you want to hear.]

Roan stared at the final line.

That was the cruelest useful warning he had ever received.

He respected it immediately.

A second memory struck, sharper than the first.

Aldric in a room Roan had never seen, older than the first image but younger than Greycross. His father stood over a basin filled with dark water. A child slept on a cot in the corner. Not Roan. Too small, hair too light. The memory trembled before Roan could decide whether it belonged to Aldric, the Shard, or someone Aldric had failed to save.

A woman's voice said, in that almost-understandable language, *They will follow the wound.*

Aldric answered, *Then I will make many.*

The memory broke.

Roan's chair creaked under his grip.

From the desk, a faint scrape. He didn't look down.

The coin on the floor rolled half an inch without being touched.

He looked at it.

No wind. No tremor. No visible ward flare.

Room distortion confirmed.

He wrote the note with his left hand because his right had gone numb from the spine down.

The letters came out uneven.

Good. Physical evidence of impairment.

The Shard pulsed again.

[Do not chase memory fragments.]

"Then stop throwing them."

No answer.

A knock sounded outside the door.

Roan didn't move.

The knock came again. Soft. Two taps, pause, one tap. Not Brendt; Brendt knocked with guilt. Not Drogan; Drogan generally made doors reconsider their careers. Not Ines; she would have sent a message first unless she wanted to be dramatic, and Ines preferred profitable drama.

A voice followed.

"Roan?"

Lyra.

His body reacted before his mind approved: shoulders tightening, breath catching, hand moving toward the door.

The Shard flared.

[False auditory lure detected.]

[Do not move.]

Roan stopped.

The voice came again, smaller. "Roan, open up."

The imitation was good.

Not perfect.

Lyra never sounded that willing to ask twice.

Roan sat back slowly.

"Try Mother next," he said.

Silence.

Then the sound outside the door peeled away, not fading but losing the logic of speech. For a moment it became static, then a wet clicking, then nothing.

Roan's hand stayed half-open for a count of five before he closed it over the pen.

He wrote: *Projection stabilization includes external lure test. Voice mimicry available. Source unclear.*

His hand didn't shake.

It wanted to That was different.

The false Lyra voice changed the emotional math.

Until then, the event had been artifact behavior: dangerous, intimate, but classifiable. The voice meant either the Shard was testing his susceptibility, the hostile recognition had opened a channel through which something else could probe, or Aldric's archive fragments included defensive measures that considered cruelty acceptable if it prevented greater compromise.

The third possibility sounded like his father by reputation.

That wasn't comforting.

Roan reviewed the lure.

It had used Lyra, not Mira. That might indicate the system reached for the most immediate protective reflex. Or the attacker, if there was one, had access to recent emotional profiles. Or Roan's own mind supplied the voice under stress and the Shard classified it as false because grief was an internal hostile environment.

He disliked that last possibility most because it couldn't be stabbed.

The paper anchors helped.

He read them silently again.

Mira. Lyra. Brendt. Drogan. Aldric. House Greel. Greycross. Black Spire. Cyrus down in seven seconds. Mother said eat. Half ration bar on desk.

Still intact.

The half ration bar had moved.

Roan stared at it.

It now lay one inch closer to the projection than before.

No visible hand had touched it. The desk surface showed a faint scrape through crumbs. Movement vector: toward the Shard manifestation.

Not a hallucination unless the scrape was false too.

He added: *Minor object displacement toward projection. Unknown mechanism.*

Then, because he was still himself despite the room becoming increasingly unreasonable, he picked up the other half of the ration bar and put it in his mouth.

The taste was chalk and salt, plus industrial regret.

Real.

Good.

The face above the desk continued forming.

The air above the desk bent.

At first it looked like heat distortion rising from an invisible flame. Then the distortion gained edges: shoulder line, head angle, the suggestion of a hand resting on the table where no table existed except Roan's cheap student desk. The shape flickered, failed, rebuilt itself from pale threads drawn through the room's darkness.

Roan didn't move.

His body wanted to That was irritating.

He had trained through poison, bleeding channels, broken ribs, mental pressure, public humiliation, and the unpleasant experience of Brendt trying to cook. His body had remained mostly cooperative. Now a half-formed projection made the muscles in his throat lock like a child's.

He noted the reaction.

Classification mattered.

The local recorder failed at 11:41.

It didn't shut down. That would've been too honest. The timer kept counting. The audio bar kept pulsing. The temperature readout still showed a steady drop. But the video pane froze on the last frame before Aldric's jawline fully resolved, trapping the room in an image that no longer matched reality.

Roan looked at the pane, then at the projection.

"Selective capture failure," he said.

The projection's eyes hadn't yet formed properly, but Roan had the distinct impression it was aware of the comment.

He wrote the observation down.

Paper still worked.

For now.

The ink darkened oddly as it dried, each letter sinking deeper into the page until it looked carved rather than written. Roan stopped writing and tore the corner of the paper. It tore normally. Good. Visual corruption, not material transformation.

He placed the torn corner beside the coin.

If later the corner returned to the page, he intended to be extremely annoyed.

A third memory fragment surfaced.

Aldric sitting beside a hospital bed in Greycross, older now, watching Mira sleep. His hand rested on a black iron pendant Roan remembered from a drawer years later. Aldric whispered, in English this time, "Not yet."

The memory ended before Roan could determine whether he had spoken to Mira, the pendant, or himself.

The room smelled more strongly of tea.

The projection didn't appear.

It negotiated with failure.

One moment Aldric's shoulders were too broad, built from an old memory of Drogan standing in a doorway. The next, they narrowed and took on the slope Roan remembered from childhood evenings when his father came home from clinic work with exhaustion folded into his coat. The hands formed with surgeon's precision, then blurred into a stranger's hands marked by thin white scars. The scars corrected themselves and didn't. Some belonged to the father Roan remembered. Some belonged to the man Roan didn't.

The face was worst.

Memory tried to help, and memory was an unreliable collaborator. Roan remembered Aldric kneeling to tie Lyra's boot. Aldric washing blood from his fingers after a late clinic shift. Aldric standing in the kitchen doorway while Mira argued with a creditor and smiling in a way that made the creditor leave confused and slightly poorer. Aldric's face in the recording from Chapter 79, voice saying Roan would be difficult with a tenderness that had cut worse than any technique.

The Shard pulled from all of it.

For a breath, the projection looked exactly like the father in Roan's memory.

Then it changed, becoming older, harder, less human in ways too small to name. A man who had run for seventy years under false skies. A man whose apologies had contingency plans.

Roan preferred the second version.

It was more likely to be true.

He checked the paper anchors.

Mira. Lyra. Brendt. Drogan. Aldric. House Greel. Greycross. Black Spire. Cyrus down in seven seconds. Mother said eat. Half ration bar on desk.

All present.

The half ration bar was still on the desk.

The pencil had fallen from the cup and now lay pointing toward the projection.

Roan marked the angle without looking away.

A face began to appear.

Not fully. The projection didn't have enough integrity for fine detail. The hair was wrong at first, too long, then corrected. The jawline sharpened. The eyes remained blank light for several seconds before darkness gathered into them. The nose resolved last.

Roan knew the face from photographs and memory, plus absence that shaped a household more thoroughly than furniture.

Aldric Vance looked younger than he had in Roan's last clear memory.

That made sense. The imprint might've been recorded before Greycross, before Mira's hospital years, before whatever kind of running turned a man from another civilization into a quiet doctor with a fake grave waiting.

The projection flickered.

Roan's desk cracked under his grip.

He released it before the sound became obvious.

[Projection coherence: low.]

[Speech capacity: limited.]

[Do not interrupt first stabilization.]

Roan's mouth remained closed.

Outside, someone shouted in the courtyard, drunk on victory or proximity to it. The sound felt impossibly distant. The city lights moved across the curtains. The dead plant in the corner leaned in its pot, still dead and therefore honest.

The projection's head lifted.

For one impossible moment, it looked directly at Roan.

Not through him.

At him.

The Shard pulsed once, hard enough that Roan's vision whitened around the edges.

[Residual imprint stabilized.]

[Identity match: Aldric Vance.]

[Transmission margin: minimal.]

Limited.

Of course.

His father had apparently rationed even haunting.

Roan waited.

The projection opened its mouth.

No sound came.

It flickered violently, collapsing at the shoulders, rebuilding around the eyes.

Roan's hand moved before he decided to move it.

He stopped one inch above the desk.

Don't move.

The instruction remained.

He obeyed.

Not because he was a good son.

Because the system had a reason and because breaking the projection through impulse would be stupid.

The air smelled faintly of tea.

Roan had not made tea.

The projection tried again.

This time, sound gathered.

Not words yet. Breath. A dry, damaged inhale from a man who didn't have lungs in the room.

Roan sat very still in his cheap chair, half a ration bar on the desk, tournament blood dried under his wrist tape, global fame burning through the feeds he had closed, and one of his father's killers perhaps still in Ironholm under a ridiculous hat.

Aldric Vance looked at him from the broken light of the Codex Shard.

The first word hadn't yet arrived.

Roan watched the projection's mouth shift through silence. When the first word finally dropped, he let it land, and counted every syllable that followed. Thirteen exactly, drawn out of the archive's failing grip before the image dissolved. He wrote the number down.
