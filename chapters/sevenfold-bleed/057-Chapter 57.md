Chapter 57: Brendt's Leg

The training hall smelled of old sweat and iron. Three floors beneath the Black Spire's public levels, where the Junior Hunters' Hall officially ended and the real work began, the air carried an undercurrent of something else: blood residue from the practice dummies, the faint chemical tang of vitality-improving compounds that never fully dissipated, and something older. Stone that had absorbed decades of desperate effort.

Roan counted the seconds between each impact as he worked through the Hollowstrike forms.

Thirty-six. Thirty-seven. Thirty-eight.

His knuckles split on the forty-third strike, skin separating the way it always did when he pushed past his body's preferred rhythm. The practice dummy didn't move. It never did. But the feedback column embedded in its chest cavity registered the hit with a soft chime, and the number on its display updated: 847.

Two hundred and twelve above his baseline from six weeks ago.

He straightened, rotating his wrist to work blood back into the joints. The cut would close in forty minutes if he let it heal naturally, or in twelve if he spent Vitalis accelerating the process. Both options carried hidden costs. Natural healing meant the micro-tears would accumulate into scar tissue that would eventually limit his grip strength. Accelerated healing consumed resources he couldn't afford to waste on something this minor.

The numbers were plain. Let it heal. Document the rate of degradation. Adjust technique to distribute impact across harder areas of his hand.

He retrieved the small notebook from his back pocket and recorded the observation in the shorthand he'd developed over the past three months: *H-S22, knuckles, 43rd rep. Split pattern consistent with grip arc deviation. Compensating.*

"Still writing in that pig Latin?"

Brendt's voice echoed down the corridor, bouncing off stone walls with the resonance that came from three floors of underground architecture. He appeared a moment later, ducking through the low entrance to the secondary practice chamber, his broad shoulders nearly scraping the doorframe. The boy had grown another inch since the semester began, a fact Roan had noted and catalogued not out of sentiment but because Brendt's increased reach was becoming a factor in their sparring sessions.

"Cipher," Roan said, without turning around. "There's a difference."

"Uh-huh." Brendt dropped a training bag near the door and stretched, his joints popping in sequence. "Warden's asking for you. Said something about your application for the Hollow Reaches expedition."

Roan's pen stopped moving.

The Hollow Reaches expedition. Third-year students only. Required minimum of Tempered rank, which he'd formally achieved two weeks ago. A six-day journey into a stabilized Surface Rift northwest of Ironholm, with the stated goal of mapping new growth patterns and clearing any Husks that had wandered beyond the standard containment zones.

In practice, it was a profit opportunity. Rift-adjacent zones contained plants, minerals, and occasionally artifacts that couldn't be found anywhere else. The school took a thirty percent cut, but what remained was substantial for anyone with the ability to identify useful materials and the discipline to extract them without dying.

"I'm Tempered," he said. "The application requirements—"

"Specify one year as an enrolled student. You're three months short."

Of course they were.

Roan closed his notebook. The arithmetic on this situation had multiple variables he didn't have values for yet. Warden Drogan's opinion of him. The actual likelihood of the expedition being approved for someone with his enrollment timeline. The probability that this was a test, a trap, to see if he'd push too hard, too fast, in exactly the way his father's notes warned against.

"What exactly did he say?"

"That you should see him. Before the administrative deadline." Brendt shrugged, the motion pulling at his training shirt. "That's literally all. I was heading to the mess hall anyway. Figured I'd save you the walk down later."

The administrative deadline. For the expedition applications.

Roan reviewed what he knew: the deadline was in thirty-one hours. Applications required instructor endorsement. Without Drogan's signature, he couldn't submit. Without the expedition, he couldn't access the restricted materials that only appeared in active Rifts, the ones the school's purchasing department refused to stock because the demand was too erratic.

No expedition meant no Hollow Marrow Elixir until he found another source. No elixir meant his timeline for reaching Hardened rank slid from the end of the current semester to sometime in the following year.

The pencil settled and didn't need to move again. It rarely did.

But the math didn't tell him why Drogan would summon him now, three months before his enrollment anniversary, when any competent administrator would reject the application on procedural grounds alone.

"Thanks," he said, which covered both the information and the implicit offer of cover. Brendt nodded, collected his bag, and headed for the door. He paused at the threshold.

"He's in a weird mood. Just… warning you."

Weird mood. That was Brendt's way of saying he'd seen something he couldn't interpret. Drogan Hale wasn't a man who displayed moods that his students could read, not on purpose, not accidentally, not unless something had shifted in whatever equation Drogan used to navigate the world.

Roan kept the observation and began the methodical process of wrapping his knuckles. The training hall's ambient lighting cast no shadows, optimized for revealing every detail of form and movement. The walls were bare stone, deliberately stripped of any artwork or decoration that might distract from practice.

He thought about his father's notes on Drogan.

*Warden Hale understands cost. He knows what it takes to survive the Discipline in a world that rewards caution. Find ways to work with him, not around him.*

Working with required information. Information he didn't have.

He finished the wrap, tested his grip, and walked toward the corridor that led to the administrative levels.

---

The Warden's office occupied a corner of the Black Spire's Crown Tier, a designation that referred to the building's highest practical floor rather than any actual monarchy. The space was modest by institutional standards: a desk built from compressed ironwood, two chairs positioned at precise angles, walls lined with case files that Roan recognized as student records.

Drogan sat behind the desk, his prosthetic hand resting on a document. The iron fingers caught the light from the tall windows, their articulation still the most advanced engineering Roan had ever seen. Three years of studying the joint mechanics, and he still couldn't reverse-engineer the alloy composition from observation alone.

"Vance. Sit."

The voice carried the flatness that Drogan used when he was managing information flow, reducing emotional cues, keeping the conversation on specific rails. He'd employed the same tone during their first meeting, three months into Roan's enrollment, when he'd delivered the news about his father's death with the emotional register of a weather report.

Roan sat. The chair was hard, positioned to make extended conversations uncomfortable. A deliberate choice.

"Your application." Drogan slid the document forward. "For the Hollow Reaches expedition."

Roan didn't reach for it. "You've seen it."

"I see everything that crosses my desk." The iron hand tapped the paper once. "You're three months short on enrollment. The automated system would reject this in four seconds if I didn't pull it first."

So the trap was real. He'd been about to submit an application that would disappear into administrative limbo, never reaching the review committee, wasting the Vitalis he'd spent preparing the supporting documentation.

"I wasn't aware the submission window was restricted by manual review," Roan said. "I'll withdraw."

"Stop."

He stopped.

Drogan's expression didn't change—if it ever changed—but something in the quality of his stillness shifted. A predator deciding not to strike, or perhaps deciding to wait for a better angle.

"I don't play games with student applications, Vance. If I'm discussing your submission, it's because I have a decision to make."

The implications cascaded through Roan's analysis. Drogan had pulled the application manually. Drogan was making a decision. This meant the standard rejection protocol wasn't the only possible outcome.

"The expedition has a forty percent casualty rate," Drogan continued. "Among enrolled students with full qualifications. You've been training for three months past Initiate rank. Your vitalis readings show aptitude consistent with accelerated advancement. But aptitude doesn't survive the Hollow Reaches."

"I'm aware of the statistics."

"You're aware of the number. Awareness and understanding aren't the same thing." Drogan leaned back in his chair, the wood creaking under his considerable weight. "I've watched you train. The way you push and recover. The way you push when you think no one is watching. Your father did the same thing."

The name landed like a stone in water. Ripples spread outward, though Drogan showed no sign of registering them.

"Aldric made choices that this institution couldn't support. He operated outside protocols that existed because previous students died. Some of those students were his friends." The iron hand lifted, fingers rotating through a complex sequence that Roan had never been able to interpret. "I don't know what he told you about this place. I don't know what you think you're going to find in the Hollow Reaches. But I know the pattern. I've seen it before. Talented kid, missing father, operating just inside the edges of acceptable risk."

"You think I'm going to follow his path."

"I think you're going to try. The question is whether you try smart or try dead."

Roan's jaw tightened. He forced it to relax. The urge to defend himself, to explain his reasoning, to justify his choices—those were emotional responses. He didn't trade in emotional responses. He traded in information and calculation.

"What would working smart look like?"

Drogan's expression didn't change, but something in his posture shifted. A slight rotation of the shoulders. The iron hand returning to rest on the desk. These weren't conscious tells—the man was too disciplined for that—but they were data points nonetheless.

"Endorsement requires demonstration of three competencies. Surface Rift survival protocols. Team coordination under threat. Resource extraction under time pressure." A pause, weighted. "I've arranged a practical assessment. Tomorrow morning, dawn. Sublevel Four. If you pass, I sign the application. If you fail, I file it as rejected and we pretend this conversation never happened."

The terms were precise. No ambiguity, no room for post-hoc reinterpretation. Either he passed or he didn't.

"What's the failure condition?"

"You die, I fill out paperwork. You quit, I fill out paperwork. You exceed the time limit, I fill out paperwork." The ghost of something that might've been humor crossed Drogan's face. "The paperwork is always the same. The difference is whether you're alive to sign it."

Roan ran the calculations. Dawn tomorrow. Sublevel Four, a restricted training area used for high-risk simulations. The competencies listed matched standard expedition requirements, which meant he'd be tested on exactly the skills he needed for the actual mission anyway. Passing benefited him regardless of the expedition outcome.

"What resources can I use to prepare?"

"None. This assessment is designed to measure current capability, not potential. Preparation would defeat the purpose." Drogan stood, the motion smooth despite his size. "I'm not trying to destroy you, Vance. I'm trying to determine whether you're worth the paperwork. There's a difference."

"Understood."

"Do you?" The question wasn't rhetorical. Drogan's eyes—still human, still warm in a way that his prosthetic suggested he'd sacrificed—held Roan's with uncomfortable directness. "Your father asked the same question. He answered it by disappearing into the Hollow Reaches two weeks before his graduation exam. He came back different. Weaker, then stronger, then gone. I've spent eleven years wondering if I should have stopped him."

The information was new. Significant. Aldric had entered the Hollow Reaches before his official graduation, without institutional sanction. The timing matched the gap in his father's notes, the three-month period where the entries shifted from cautious optimism to something darker.

"Why are you telling me this?"

"Because you need to understand that I know exactly what you're doing. The edge you're walking. The shortcuts you're taking. The way you measure risk against reward like it's a ledger and not a minefield." Drogan moved toward the door, his weight distributed evenly despite the asymmetry of his limbs. "Your father was brilliant. His calculations were better than anyone else's I've known. And he still ended up dead in a place that doesn't appear on any official map."

"A place that doesn't appear—"

"Get some sleep, Vance. You'll need it."

The dismissal was clear. Roan stood, but didn't move toward the door immediately.

"Warden. The assessment tomorrow. If I fail, will you tell me why you really pulled the application?"

Drogan paused, his hand on the doorframe. The iron fingers flexed once, a mechanical whisper.

"Because Aldric asked me to watch for you. And I've spent three months watching you do exactly what he did, with exactly the same pattern of risk assessment." He didn't turn around. "I don't know if that makes you predictable or stupid. Tomorrow will tell me which."

The door closed behind him.

---

Roan returned to his quarters in the students' residence hall and sat on the edge of his bed without turning on the lights. The window faced east, away from the setting sun, and the room had already shifted into shadow.

His father's voice echoed through the fragments of memory he still carried: *Drogan understands cost. Find ways to work with him.*

Working with Drogan meant accepting that the man had information Roan didn't have. It meant accepting that Aldric had been monitored, had perhaps been helped, and still ended up dead. It meant accepting that his own calculations might be incomplete in ways he couldn't anticipate.

The notebook in his pocket contained seventeen different approaches to the Hollowstrike forms, all refined through months of iteration. He'd calculated optimal strike angles, recovery paths, and energy expenditure curves. He'd tracked his vitalis recovery rate after each session and adjusted his training intensity to maintain consistent gains without triggering the degradation patterns he'd documented in earlier weeks.

He ran it once, ran it back, and found nothing he hadn't already known.

But Drogan had said Aldric's math was sound too.

He pulled out the notebook and began writing. Not cipher this time, his actual analysis, the internal monologue that he usually compressed into shorthand. The assessment tomorrow would test three competencies. Surface Rift survival meant recognizing and avoiding environmental hazards, managing oxygen consumption in low-oxygen zones, and maintaining directional orientation when visual reference points disappeared. Team coordination meant trusting others with his safety and theirs. Resource extraction under time pressure meant identifying useful materials quickly and accepting trade-offs between yield and risk.

None of these were things he could learn tonight. They were things he should've been learning over months of supervised practice, the way Brendt was learning them, the way every other student with normal enrollment timelines was learning them.

He was three months short because he'd pushed through Initiate rank in four months instead of the standard eighteen. He'd used Inversion to absorb the side effects of accelerated training compounds, converting the damage that should have slowed him into vitalis that accelerated his growth. The tally worked. His body had responded. His rankings had improved.

But the ledger didn't account for Drogan Hale, who had watched Aldric do the same thing and seen it end in a place that didn't appear on any map.

The Codex Shard embedded in his spine pulsed once, a faint warmth that had become familiar over the past months. System notification or Aldric's residual presence, he couldn't tell anymore. Sometimes the distinction seemed meaningless.

He opened his desk drawer and retrieved the small vial of bloodscourge draught he'd purchased from the underground market two weeks ago. The liquid inside was murky, inconsistent with the batch consistency he'd seen in quality-controlled samples. Either the seller had lied about the source, or this was a deliberately degraded product designed to produce weaker effects.

The balance on degraded bloodscourge: reduced vitalis gain, but also reduced side effects. If he inverted the dose tomorrow morning, he'd enter the assessment with a partially filled vitalis reserve. If the assessment included any physical combat component, that reserve could mean the difference between passing and failing.

But degraded product also meant unpredictable conversion rates. He couldn't calculate exact yields because the active compound ratios were unknown.

He closed the vial and put it back.

Tomorrow. Dawn. He'd face whatever Drogan had designed without chemical enhancement and without preparation time.

The arithmetic was incomplete. That happened sometimes. The answer wasn't to pretend the math was complete anyway. It was to act on incomplete information and adapt faster than the situation deteriorated.

He lay back on the bed and stared at the ceiling, running through Hollowstrike sequences in his mind. The forms were clean, memorized, optimized. He'd trained until his body could execute them without conscious direction.

But training wasn't assessment. Training was controlled conditions and repeatable parameters. Assessment was someone else's design, measuring something he couldn't predict.

His father's voice, from a memory that had become more vivid as the months passed: *The Discipline isn't about being the strongest. It's about being the most adaptable. Strength is a resource. Adaptability is the skill.*

Roan closed his eyes.

Thirty-one hours until the deadline. Nineteen until the assessment.

The columns didn't comfort him, but they didn't need to. Comfort was an emotional response. Numbers were data. Data could be processed, analyzed, acted upon.

He slept.
