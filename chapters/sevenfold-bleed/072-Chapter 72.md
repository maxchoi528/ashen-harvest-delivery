Chapter 72: A Forbidden Recommendation

The vial on the steel table held exactly four milliliters of fluid, enough to liquefy the marrow of a Tempered practitioner and leave him paraplegic for whatever remained of his shortened life. Roan had tested the viscosity, the particulate suspension, the gray sheen that separated light into unhealthy spectra. The dealer in the Hollow Reaches exchange had called it a bone-wash elixir for Hardened frame maintenance. The Codex Shard, fed the chemical trace an hour ago, had logged the pattern without error.

That meant the Sidewound was calculable.

Roan rolled his shoulder. The Shard embedded at his seventh cervical vertebra sat against his spine like a cold stone, dense and inert. He reached inward, not with his hand, but with the silent calibration he kept of its state. It was stable. The Inversion from Drogan's session had absorbed a tendon backlash cleanly. The Warden had noticed nothing beyond a grunt of approval. Roan had walked away with a concealed limp that vanished in a heartbeat.

Which meant the Hollow Marrow Elixir was the next variable. A Tier 2 catalyst poured into a Tempered skeleton would overshoot his classification by a full tier. The Shard would classify the incoming damage as mortal-level. It would demand authorization. The conversion was instantaneous, but the window was the risk—if consciousness failed before the sidewound completed its cycle, the feedback loop broke and the damage stuck.

In a month when Sentinel Bureau patrols were seizing unregistered catalysts by the crate, when House Greel's youngest heir had been asking pointed questions in the Junior Hunters' Hall about independent supply chains, and when Drogan had scheduled a live-combat assessment in five days' time, a fatal-tier gamble wasn't a luxury. It was the only arithmetic that didn't leave him irrelevant.

Roan looked at the vial. Then at the restraint rig he had bolted to the floor that morning.

He hadn't built the rig out of fear. Fear was a distraction, and distractions skewed timing. He had built it because liquefied marrow meant structural collapse. If his femurs turned to jelly and he folded sideways before the Inversion resolved, the Shard might misread the positional trauma as catastrophic injury rather than sidewound. Misclassification meant conversion failure. Conversion failure meant four milliliters of gray fluid would finish its work, and Roan Vance would never walk again.

He strapped his ankles first. Leather and steel. Then his wrists, tight enough to cut off capillary refill. A final strap across the forehead, pinning his skull to the chair's high back. The buckles clicked with a sound like chambers loading.

Acceptable.

He unstopped the vial. The smell hit immediately, rusted iron left in boiled vinegar, with an aftertaste of ozone that seemed to reach the eyes rather than the nose. He tipped it back. The fluid was thicker than water. It coated his tongue and slid down his throat in one viscous lump.

He swallowed.

- break -

The first sensation was cold. Not surface cold. Deep cold, as though the elixir had bypassed his stomach and poured directly into the hollow shafts of his long bones. His tibias ached, then froze. For a moment, nothing existed except a faint mineral taste at the back of his palate.

Then the fire started.

It began in his sternum, a pressure, a widening crack, and radiated outward along the osseous lattice. Roan's jaw locked against the bite guard. The straps held. His spine arched, not by choice, but because the vertebrae were spasming as the marrow inside them turned to acid. Liquid. His body was trying to collapse into itself, bone by bone, beginning with the largest reservoirs and working inward.

*The figures said the damage had to be real before the Inversion would touch it.*

*This felt like a bad joke told by a butcher.*

His vision tunneled. The ceiling fixture, a single bare bulb, blurred into a white smear. Sound narrowed to the rush of his own blood, too loud, the pitch wrong, as if his heart were pumping sludge instead of plasma. The Codex Shard was supposed to register Sidewound as it crossed the threshold. If the damage was too slow, the conversion didn't qualify. Too fast, and the damage became permanent before the Shard could read the pattern. He had trained his pain tolerance to precision. But this wasn't pain. This was erasure. His skeleton was being unwritten.

He forced his eyes open. If he lost consciousness, the timeline skewed. Unconsciousness wasn't death, but it was disconnection. The Shard read active Vitalis signatures. A blank mind meant a blank reading.

His left femur buzzed. A vibration so deep it didn't register as sound, only as structural instability. The bone wasn't snapping. It was dissolving from the inside out, the marrow chamber filling with gray solvent that ate trabeculae like water meeting sugar.

[Sidewound detected: Hollow Marrow liquefaction.]
[Pattern recognized. Yield classification: Tier 2 catalyst ingested by Tempered host.]
[Warning: Fatal-tier conversion will generate anomalous energy signature.]

The prompts appeared not in his vision but in the architecture of his perception, cold and rectilinear. Roan couldn't speak to acknowledge them. His tongue felt like a slab of lead resting on a swelling throat. The next prompt would be the demand for authorization. Aldric's architecture had built in a consent gate for mortal-tier conversions, one moment of directed intent, one clear push of will.

Roan focused on the Shard's weight. The node at C7. He pushed intent toward it the way he'd push a blade through resistance, not a thought, not a word, a direction.

*Take it.*

[Mortal Inversion authorized.]

The world inverted.

The cold became heat. The dissolution became reweaving. The gray fire eating his bones didn't stop, but its direction reversed. Where marrow had liquefied, the Codex forced conversion, the Sidewound pulled inward, devoured, translated into pure Vitalis. The channels of his skeleton, moments ago collapsing, began to glow with redirected force. Liquid marrow re-solidified, not into its old architecture, but into something denser, laced with compressed Vitalis conduits that branched like alloyed wire through the cortical bone.

[Mortal Sidewound absorbed.]
[Fatal-tier conversion registered.]
[Conversion successful: +250 Vitalis.]
[Anomaly flagged: Host signature variance exceeds standard Tempered threshold.]

*That was stupid. Effective. But stupid. — A.V.*

Quiet.

Roan sagged against the straps. Not from relief. From mass. His bones were heavier. The conversion hadn't merely healed the liquefaction; it had reforged the marrow architecture entirely, packing the cavities with channels that hadn't existed before. He could feel the density in his skull, his ribs, his pelvis. The chair's steel bolts groaned as his weight settled, a low metallic protest that hadn't been there when he'd sat down.

He spat the bite guard into his palm. It was cracked. He cracked his neck. The sound was wrong, too deep, like a mallet striking hardwood rather than the wet pop of cervical release.

Unstrapping took longer than it should have. His fingers were precise, but the tendons moving them sat in heavier grooves now, anchored to fulcrums that had shifted by millimeters. When he finally stood, the floorboards split. Not splintered, cracked, a sharp report like ceramic under pressure. He looked down. The reinforced concrete beneath the floor's surface layer had spiderwebbed beneath his bare heel.

Roan took a step. His gait was off. The femurs were denser, longer by perhaps two millimeters, or the connective tissue had tightened to accommodate the new load. He compensated, recalculating his center of gravity on the fly. A shadow-boxed jab. The air popped. Not from speed, from the mass behind the strike. His fist had become a blunt instrument carrying the inertia of a sledgehammer.

He walked to the steel table. Placed his palm flat against its surface. Pressed down. The metal dented. Not deeply, but enough to hold the print of his palm in cold-pressed steel.

Right.

- break -

The shower ran gray for eight minutes. Not blood. Impurities, the slag of his old marrow expelled through sweat and capillary bleed, mixed with the residue of the elixir's passage. Roan watched the water spiral down the drain and ran the new diagnostic.

The Shard was stable. The conversion was a clean burn. But the anomaly it left behind was a beacon. A Tempered body reading like a Hardened frame. Any standard probe would flag the variance. Any alert would trigger a sweep.

This month, Sentinel patrols were confiscating unregistered elixirs by the crate. The Greel envoy had been asking questions in the Junior Hunters' Hall about independent supply chains. And Drogan had scheduled a live-combat assessment in five days' time—an assessment where his overclocked signature would be read in plain sight.

Roan shut off the water. Dried off. The towel dragged across his skin with unfamiliar friction; his pores seemed to have tightened, the epidermis bonding more firmly to the denser dermis beneath. He dressed in loose clothing that wouldn't chafe against the unfamiliar topography of his shoulders and hips. The collar of his shirt sat differently now, stretched by the thickened musculature of his upper back.

He checked the window. Alley below. Empty.

Three sharp knocks. Institutional rhythm.

He didn't move. The knocks repeated, then a voice, male and bored: "Sentinel Bureau. Open up."

Roan looked at the chair. The restraint rig lay on the floor like shed skin. The empty vial was already sealed in a shielded pouch inside his jacket. No obvious evidence. But his Vitalis signature was a flare. A Hardened frame crammed into a Tempered classification, or so any probe would suggest.

He crossed the room. Unlocked the deadbolt. Pulled the door open.

Two Sentinels. The lead was a woman in a gray municipal coat, Hardened by the steady pressure she gave off, unremarkable, neat, dangerous because of its very tidiness. Her badge read Halric. Not the same Halric from the Rift incident in Greycross. Different branch, same family maybe, same blunt efficiency. Her eyes swept him, paused on his collarbones, his stance, the way his shirt hung.

"Roan Vance?" she asked.

"Yes."

"You're in possession of an unregistered Tier 2 catalyst." She didn't draw a weapon. She didn't need to. "We have a witness statement and a transaction record from a seized dealer. You'll come with us. Quietly."

Roan considered the doorway. The stairwell behind them. The drop from the window. His new weight made the window escape slower. The partner, a broad man with a Vitalis dampener at his hip, already had a hand on the device. The dampener would suppress energy flow, currently irrelevant, but it wouldn't touch his new bone density. Still. Two Hardened Sentinels in a narrow corridor. No margin for a fight that confirmed the anomaly.

"Who named me?" Roan asked.

The woman's smile was thin. "You can read the report at the bureau."

Roan stepped forward. Let them flank him. The partner clipped a suppression cuff around his left wrist, standard procedure, not combat restraint. The metal was cold. Roan walked between them down the stairs, counting exits, counting angles, recalculating his new body mass into strike physics. If he fought now, he confirmed the variance. Compliance bought time.

The figures said comply.

Outside, the street was wet from an earlier rain. A black transport waited at the curb, Sentinel markings dulled by the damp. As the partner opened the rear door, Roan glanced across the street.

A figure leaned against a lamp post. Tall. Blond. Expensive coat that hadn't been cut by any Ironholm tailor. The Greel envoy watched from beneath the brim of a charcoal hat, and when Roan's eyes found him, he raised one hand in a languid wave. Not a threat. A receipt.

The dealer hadn't just talked. He'd been instructed.

Roan ducked into the transport. The door slammed. Through the mesh-reinforced window, he watched the envoy turn away, walking into the crowd as if he'd never been there at all.

The transport pulled into traffic. Roan sat back. The suppression cuff dug into his wrist. The Greel envoy had set the clock, but he had underestimated the weight of the piece he had just offered up.

The Shard was full. The architecture was stable. The problem was the beacon he now broadcast. A cell, an interrogation room, a signature that didn't match his file. The Sentinels would prod. The cuff was standard. The holding cell would be standard.

He ran the new math. His body was heavier. His bones were harder. His options were narrower. But the foundation had shifted. The balance was no longer borrowed from a system of charges and limits. It was his own, forged from a four-milliliter gamble that had just paid out.

The numbers didn't care. But the scale had finally tipped in his favor.
