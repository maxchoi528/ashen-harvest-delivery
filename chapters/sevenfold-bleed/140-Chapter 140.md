Chapter 140: Map Rights

Drogan didn't tell the story like a confession.

That would've been simpler. Confessions had shape. Admission, regret, explanation, some ritualized hope that the listener might forgive or at least understand. Drogan spoke as if he were reading from an incident report he had memorized badly and hated accurately.

Roan preferred that. Reports contained fewer decorative lies.

The signal station held around them with a faint blue hum. Outside, the Husks had withdrawn beyond the outer ward light but not beyond hearing.

Before Drogan began, Roan made him wait.

That was deliberate.

The old man had finally agreed to speak, which meant every impatient question would now make him retreat into shorter answers. Better to turn the station into a controlled room first. Roan had learned the value of rooms in Greycross. People thought negotiations happened between people. They were wrong. Negotiations happened between a person, a chair, an exit, a visible weapon, a clock, and whatever sound came through the walls.

So he adjusted the room.

Brendt's cot went against the inner wall, farthest from the door and below the least damaged shutter. Not because it was safest, nothing in the station deserved that word, but because anyone checking Brendt's wound would have a clear line to both entrances. Ines's map projection moved from the control panel to the floor, where Drogan couldn't avoid seeing it when he spoke. Roan placed the sealed ash-thread sample beside the projection, not for analysis, but as a reminder that the Rift was already inside one of them in trace form.

Drogan noticed every choice.

"You setting a courtroom?" he asked.

"No. Courtrooms pretend procedure equals truth."

"Interrogation room, then."

"No restraints."

"Generous."

"Temporary."

Brendt muttered, "I preferred him before he discovered furniture as intimidation."

"No, you didn't," Ines said. "He was doing it already. You just didn't have enough blood loss to notice."

Roan let them talk. Light pressure in a room helped. Silence sharpened too much too quickly; humor dulled the first edge and made the second one cleaner.

Only when Drogan sat beneath the map did Roan ask about the orders.

Their claws clicked from alleys and rooftops, plus the dry rail yard below. Waiting. Watching. Occasionally tapping the boundary as if counting how long it would last.

Brendt sat on the cot with his injured foot elevated on a folded pack. He had stopped pretending the wound didn't hurt. That was progress. Ines had restored partial access to the station's survey cache and now projected three overlapping maps onto the wall: official route grid, old Black Squad overlay, and current distortion model. Drogan sat beneath them, elbows on knees, eyes on the floor.

Roan stood because sitting made other people think a conversation had softened.

It hadn't.

"Start with the orders," he said.

Drogan's mouth tightened. "You don't ask small questions."

"Small answers waste time."

"The orders came through Sentinel command. Black seal. Founding Seven authority attached but not named. We were told the Hollowed City still contained pre-collapse archives. Dangerous archives. Our job was to destroy them before Rift entities could access them."

"That makes no sense," Ines said.

Drogan looked at her.

She shrugged. "Rift entities don't read municipal archives. Even intelligent ones usually don't care about human filing systems. If command wanted destruction, they were afraid humans would find the records, not monsters."

"Yes," Roan said.

Drogan's version came in fragments at first, and Roan let the fragments remain fragments until they contradicted one another.

There had been five members on the ground team, not counting the remote observer. Aldric Vance, listed as tactical specialist despite being the person everyone followed. Drogan Hale, breach and extraction. Talen Mercer, wards and structural logic. Lysa Harrow, field medic and rifle. Garran Pike, heavy shield. Names without bodies in Roan's life until that night. Now each acquired a position on the map, a likely death angle, a debt attached to Drogan's voice.

Roan asked for roles, not feelings.

Roles survived memory better.

"Aldric shouldn't have been lead," Drogan said. "Officially."

"But he was."

"In every way that mattered. Command tolerated it because missions succeeded. Then they resented him because missions succeeded without needing them."

"Common management failure."

"He said that too. Less politely."

"Good."

Drogan's mouth almost moved into a smile and stopped. "Mercer trusted structures more than people. Lysa trusted people more than structures. Garran trusted shields and breakfast. I trusted Aldric. That was our operating doctrine for too long."

"What changed?"

"The Hollowed City."

That wasn't enough, and Drogan knew it, because he continued before Roan asked.

"Before this mission, Aldric's secrets were inconveniences. After the tower, they became gravity. Everything bent toward them. Command, the western signatory, the Hollowkin, the Founding Seven. We went in to burn archives and found out we were inside someone else's old war."

Roan looked at the map. Someone else's old war had become his present inheritance. That seemed rude but unsurprising.

Drogan nodded once. "Aldric reached that conclusion before we crossed the gate."

Of course he had.

Roan disliked how often his father's competence arrived after his death in the form of other people's delayed admiration. It was an inefficient form of inheritance.

"Why go in?" Brendt asked.

Drogan gave him a look.

Brendt raised both hands. "I know. Orders. But you said Aldric knew."

"Aldric wanted the archives," Roan said.

Drogan didn't deny it.

Useful.

"He suspected they were connected to him," Roan continued. "Or to whatever he was before he became Aldric Vance."

Drogan's eyes lifted.

There. Not surprise. Confirmation arriving unwillingly.

"He never told me what he was before," Drogan said.

"But you knew there was a before."

"Everyone in Black Squad had a before."

"Not like his."

Silence.

Outside, something scraped along the wall and moved away.

Drogan looked at the map. "He didn't age like he should have. He knew old Rift dialect fragments no academy taught. He could identify ward logic from civilizations that officially never had contact. He healed wrong. Bled wrong. Lied badly about all of it."

"Badly?" Brendt asked.

Drogan's mouth twitched. "Badly to people who slept in the same mud for three years. Well enough for command."

That went into the ledger.

Aldric had hidden from more than enemies. He had hidden among allies and imperfectly, plus been accepted anyway. That mattered. It meant his father had either trusted Black Squad enough to be careless or been too exhausted to maintain the lie.

Both possibilities were inconvenient.

"The tower," Roan said.

Drogan pointed to the old overlay. A black mark pulsed near the inner district, north of the dry canal, east of the statue plaza.

"Here. Survey designation C-17. We called it the bell tower because Mercer wouldn't stop making jokes about churches. It wasn't a church."

"Mercer," Roan said.

"Talen Mercer. Theon's uncle."

Roan absorbed that.

Theon Mercer, their former roommate, had mentioned an uncle once. Missing, not dead. Family preferred missing. Missing allowed bureaucrats to postpone grief.

"Did he die here?" Roan asked.

Drogan's expression flattened. "No."

Again too quick.

"Then what happened?"

"Later."

"Drogan."

The old man's gaze sharpened. "I am telling you route-critical history. Not thirty years of graves."

Roan considered pressing.

Rejected. The name mattered, but not immediately. Talen Mercer could wait behind Brendt's ankle, the Husk pack, the tower, and Aldric's chamber.

For now.

"What was inside the tower?" Ines asked.

Drogan reached up and touched the projected map. The old overlay flickered, expanding an interior note so corrupted half the text had become symbols.

"Clean hall," he said. "No dust. No bodies. Six doors sealed by blood and something else. Center stair going down. Markings on every wall. Aldric recognized them and ordered us not to touch anything."

"Did you?" Roan asked.

"No."

Truth.

"Who did?"

Drogan looked away.

There it was.

"Command relay," he said. "We had a remote observer attached. Sentinel analyst. She wasn't supposed to have override authority. She used it anyway. Opened the first seal from outside."

"Name."

"The western signatory."

The Codex Shard struck Roan's spine like a hammer tapping glass.

Not painful. Precise.

He didn't move.

Years earlier, no, months, though it felt like a different person wearing his bones. Roan had awakened from a Wraithmilk inversion and found a word written in unfamiliar handwriting.

The western signatory.

At the time, it had been a fragment. A leaked memory. A name without a face.

Now it had a function.

Remote observer. Sentinel analyst. Override authority. Connected to Black Squad. Connected to Aldric's blood chamber.

Roan looked at Drogan. "Lady Sirenne is the western signatory?"

Drogan's eyes narrowed. "You know that name."

"The Shard leaked it once."

Brendt looked at the ceiling. "I continue to dislike how many sentences in this friendship are terrifying when taken literally."

Ines's expression had gone still. "The western signatory isn't just an analyst. House Sirenne controls three archival courts and half the legal memory trade in the western districts."

"Thirty years ago she was younger," Drogan said.

"People often are," Roan said.

Drogan gave him a flat look.

"Continue."

The old man did.

The first seal opened. The tower woke. Not fully. Enough. The walls began speaking in a language none of them knew except Aldric, who went white under the grime and told Drogan to cut the relay.

Drogan had tried.

The relay didn't cut.

The western signatory spoke through command crystal and ordered them to secure all artifacts. Aldric ordered the team to retreat. For exactly fourteen seconds, Black Squad had two commanders.

Fourteen seconds was enough.

The Hollowkin came from below.

Not Husks. Not mindless. Drogan described them without embellishment: tall, gray-skinned, masked in bone, carrying hooked glaives and rifles built from materials no human quartermaster could identify. They moved like soldiers. They targeted the relay first.

The western signatory screamed when the connection destabilized.

Aldric laughed.

Not because it was funny, Drogan said. Because he had found something he expected and dreaded.

"What?" Roan asked.

Drogan shook his head. "He said one sentence in that language. The Hollowkin stopped shooting for half a breath. Then their leader answered, and Aldric went down the stairs."

"Alone."

"He told us to hold."

"And you did."

Drogan's hands tightened.

"Yes."

Roan let that sit.

The old man had survived because he followed Aldric's order. Or because Aldric gave him one that kept him above while Aldric entered the real chamber. Survival and obedience often shared paperwork.

"How long was he gone?" Ines asked.

"Nine minutes."

"Exact," Roan said.

"I counted because the walls were bleeding."

No one commented on that.

Drogan continued. "When he came back, his hands were red up to the elbows. He carried a black case. The Hollowkin leader let him pass. Then the tower started collapsing inward. Not down. Inward. Aldric told us the mission had changed.

We fought out.

Mercer was taken."

Roan went still. "Taken where?"

"I don't know. The Hollowkin took him. Alive."

The silence crystallized.

"Lysa died at the fountain. She was trying to save the navigation crystal. Garran lost an arm covering the breach. I carried Aldric for the last hundred meters because he couldn't feel his legs."

Roan's fingers rested against the Vance Pendant.

Aldric had bled wrong. He had known the language. He had carried a black case. He had later built a Shard that lived in Roan's spine.

The line from tower to son was becoming less metaphorical.

"What was in the case?" Roan asked.

Drogan looked at him.

"Your pendant," he said. "Or something like it. And a shard wrapped in black cloth."

The signal station seemed to become very small.

The Codex Shard didn't pulse this time.

It listened.

Brendt swallowed. "So the thing in Roan's spine came from here."

"Possibly," Ines said.

Roan said, "Probably."

Drogan said nothing.

Outside, a Husk tapped the ward once.

Roan turned toward the window slit.

The street beyond was mostly dark, but the outer blue ward cast enough light to show shapes moving along the platform. The pack hadn't left. It had rearranged.

No longer pressing. No longer clicking.

Waiting for them to decide whether fear outweighed curiosity.

It didn't.

"We go to the tower," Roan said.

Brendt looked at his ankle. "Of course we do."

Drogan shook his head. "No."

Roan turned back.

"No?"

"The tower collapsed inward. There may be nothing left."

"Then we confirm."

"Your friend is injured."

"My friend is listening."

Brendt sighed. "I hate when you include me correctly."

Roan looked at him. "Can you move?"

"Slowly. With complaint."

"Can you hold shield?"

"Yes."

"Can you run?"

Brendt hesitated.

Good. Honest.

"Not well," he said.

Roan nodded. "Then we don't plan on running."

"That's the worst solution to that problem."

"Only possibly."

Ines folded the map projection and brought up the route. "There's an alternate path to C-17 along the dry canal ridge, then through an administrative quarter. It avoids the market and most roof-heavy streets. It passes within two hundred meters of the statue fountain."

Drogan's face tightened at fountain.

Lysa died at the fountain.

Roan saw the reaction and marked it.

"Threats?" he asked.

"Marker constructs, Husk pack, distortion pockets, possible clean sites," Ines said. "Also whatever controls the station speaker."

"Station speaker used Drogan," Roan said. "It may not track us if we leave its domain."

"Or it tracks us better."

"Yes."

Brendt raised a hand. "I would like to raise an ethical objection to plans where yes is the answer to every danger."

"Denied," Roan said.

"Can you deny ethical objections?"

"Efficiently."

Drogan stood.

For a moment, Roan expected another refusal. Instead the old man walked to the map and touched the dry canal route.

"We leave at first gray," he said. "Not dawn. This place doesn't have dawn. We move before the pack changes position again. Brendt carries half load. Ines handles route. Roan, you don't touch any tower markings without telling me first."

"Agreed."

Drogan squinted. "That was too easy."

"I said I would tell you first. I didn't say I would wait for permission."

"There it is."

Brendt covered his face with one hand.

Ines looked amused despite herself.

Drogan stared at Roan for a while, then grunted. "Your father was worse."

"People keep saying that as if it helps."

"It helps me."

Roan also asked about extraction, because people liked to end old stories at the moment that made them look least guilty.

Extraction hadn't been clean. The official report said Black Squad exited through the northern breach under heavy pressure and destroyed pursuit with demolition charges. Drogan's version replaced destroyed with delayed and heavy pressure with everything behind us learned our names. Aldric was conscious for half the route and delirious for the rest. He kept trying to remove the black case from his own pack and give it to Drogan, then taking it back before Drogan touched it.

"Why?" Roan asked.

"He said it would choose wrong if I carried it."

The Codex Shard pulsed at that, once, very soft.

Roan wrote: Shard precursor had carrier preference before implantation.

"Did he say who should carry it?"

Drogan looked at him. "No."

Not truth. Not lie. A door with furniture against it.

Roan let it stand because Brendt was awake, Ines was listening, and some answers became less useful when forced in front of witnesses. But he marked the pause. Later, when Drogan had fewer walls and Roan had more leverage, they would return to it.

The route discussion after Drogan's story took longer than anyone wanted and shorter than it should have.

The old overlay showed six possible approaches to C-17. Three no longer existed, assuming Ines's current model wasn't lying in a particularly artistic way. One passed through the market of clean lanterns. One passed under the administrative vaults where the map displayed a blank square and refused to render the surrounding streets. The last followed the dry canal, crossed the fountain plaza, and entered the inner district from the north.

"Last route is obvious bait," Brendt said.

"Yes," Roan said.

"We are taking it?"

"Yes."

"I miss when obvious bait was something we avoided."

"We avoid concealed bait. Obvious bait can be priced."

Ines tapped the blank square on the map. "What about this?"

"No," Drogan said.

"You didn't look."

"I remember."

That made everyone look.

Drogan's expression didn't invite questions. Roan asked one anyway. "What is it?"

"A records annex. Or was. Mercer tried to stabilize the outer wall while we were retreating. The inside was full of shelves. Empty shelves. Every label had a person's name."

"Whose names?" Ines asked.

"Ours."

Brendt lowered his voice. "I vote dry canal."

"So do I," Ines said, all archival hunger temporarily extinguished.

Roan studied the blank square. A room of shelves labeled for visitors before they entered. Memory architecture, then. Or identity bait. The western signatory would have loved it, which was another reason not to go there first.

"Dry canal," he agreed.

Drogan seemed relieved for exactly half a second. Then he noticed Roan noticing and became annoyed, which was better than guilt.

"Stop collecting my expressions," he said.

"Make fewer useful ones."

Brendt, half-asleep and pale, pointed vaguely at both of them. "For the record, this is what cooperation looks like in nightmares."

"Rest," Roan said.

"I am resting judgment. Different thing."

"Do both."

"Terrible bedside manner," Brendt murmured, but his eyes closed again. The room kept humming around them. Dead systems, pretending to be alive.

The old man turned away before Roan could answer.

Drogan also remembered the sound the tower made before it fell.

He had avoided describing it until Roan asked him to separate structural collapse from Rift distortion. That forced the issue into technical language, where Drogan could operate without drowning in ghosts.

"Bells," he said. "But not metal. Seven tones. Low to high. The floor answered each tone. Aldric counted them under his breath. On the sixth, he told Mercer to stop listening. On the seventh, the stair opened."

"Opened how?" Ines asked.

"It had already been there. We just started being allowed to see it."

Brendt closed his eyes. "I preferred doors."

"No, you didn't," Roan said. "Doors lie with handles."

"You aren't helping."

Seven tones. Seven lines. Seven injuries. Roan wrote it down and felt the Codex Shard remain deliberately silent. That silence had developed texture. It wasn't absence of data. It was refusal or protection. Both. His father's systems had inherited his father's habit of giving help one bruise late.

"Did Aldric bleed before the stair opened?" Roan asked.

Drogan's eyes sharpened. "Yes."

"Voluntary?"

"He cut his palm on the fifth tone. Said it would be worse if the tower took blood first."

Brendt looked at Roan's bandaged palm from Crimson Wake.

Roan ignored him.

Blood before threshold. Voluntary injury as key. Inversion architecture loved symmetry in the way tax collectors loved penalties.

"Then if we find an entrance," Roan said, "we assume the first blood decides terms."

Drogan's voice went hard. "You assume nothing."

"Fine. We model it."

"Roan."

"That was a concession."

"It was vocabulary laundering."

"Efficient vocabulary laundering."

Brendt looked at Ines. "Is this how he talks to everyone trying to keep him alive?"

"Mostly," she said. "You should feel included."

They slept in pieces.

Roan took the last useful watch, which wasn't the same as the last scheduled watch. Drogan pretended to rest and failed. Ines slept like someone who had trained herself to wake before anyone entered knife range. Brendt slept badly, jaw clenched, one hand resting near the shield latch even while unconscious.

The wound held.

Residual gray remained around the punctures but didn't spread. Roan checked it every thirty minutes until Brendt woke and told him to stop looking at his ankle like a disappointing investment.

"It's a disappointing investment," Roan said.

"I am injured and still emotionally available for offense."

"Noted."

Outside, the Husks withdrew shortly before the light changed.

Not sunrise. The sky didn't brighten so much as become less committed to darkness. The mist thinned near ground level, revealing the rail yard below the station platform. Husk tracks covered the ash in layered patterns.

Roan crouched beside the outer ward while Ines packed the last marker kit.

The tracks told a story.

The pack had surrounded the station, tested the wards, waited, then retreated in three groups. One toward the market. One toward the dry canal. One north, exactly along the route toward C-17.

Not following. Pre-positioning.

"They know where we're going," Roan said.

Drogan looked at the tracks. "Yes."

"Because of you?"

"Maybe."

"Because of me?"

Drogan didn't answer.
