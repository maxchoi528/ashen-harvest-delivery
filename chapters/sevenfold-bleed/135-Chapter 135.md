Chapter 135: The Vanishing Point

- break -

Tournament done. The blood washed off. Next objective.

The entrance to the Tier Three Rift sat under an abandoned rail station three districts east of Black Spire, which Roan considered rude.

Dangerous places ought to look dangerous. It saved time. The Greycross alleys of his childhood had understood that basic courtesy. Broken glass, piss-stained walls, men with knives who watched your shoes too carefully, everything announced its intentions. Even the worst black-market shops had the decency to smell like bad decisions.

Ironholm's eastern transit depot looked merely closed.

The old station squatted between two warehouse blocks, its windows sealed with gray panels, its sign stripped of letters until only rust shadows remained. Rainwater gathered in shallow puddles across the concrete forecourt. A chain-link fence surrounded the property, decorated with official warnings in three languages and one unofficial tag that read: *RIFT COPS EAT TEETH.*

Brendt stared at the tag.

"Do they?"

"No," Ines said. "They fine you first."

Drogan unlocked the gate with an old brass key.

Not a digital pass. Not a Spire-issued access token. A brass key.

Roan looked at it.

Drogan noticed. "Problem?"

"Several."

"Pick one."

"Why do you have a private key to a sealed municipal Rift entrance?"

"Because asking the city for permission would involve paperwork."

"That explains why you want one. Not why you have one."

Drogan pushed the gate open. "Your father stole it."

Roan paused.

Brendt made a small sound. "I'm sorry. Did you just say—"

"No," Drogan said.

"You did."

"I said nothing useful."

Roan stepped through the gate. "Aldric stole municipal infrastructure access."

"He borrowed it."

"For thirty years?"

"He was thorough."

That was one word for it.

The gate clanged shut behind them.

Ines adjusted the strap on her rifle case and looked around the forecourt with professional disinterest. "External surveillance?"

"Disabled," Drogan said.

"By you?"

"By time."

"That isn't reassuring."

"It wasn't meant to be."

Roan scanned the building. Three visible cameras, all dead. Two ward nodes above the old ticket hall, one flickering. Rust on the western service door but not the hinges, suggesting recent use. No fresh footprints visible in the rain except theirs and one set of boot marks near the loading ramp, partially washed out. Heavy tread. Adult male. Could be municipal maintenance. Could be someone watching the same entrance.

"Someone came through within twelve hours," Roan said.

Drogan didn't look surprised. "Yes."

"You knew."

"Yes."

"And brought us anyway."

"Yes."

Brendt exhaled. "Wonderful. Excellent. Love the honesty arriving after the fence closes."

Drogan turned. "If I thought they were waiting inside, you wouldn't be here."

"That implies many comforting things about what you do think," Brendt said, "and none of them are comforting."

Roan crouched near the boot print. Water had blurred the edges, but the depth remained uneven. The person had carried weight on the left side, or favored the right leg. The heel pattern had a split triangular notch. Military surplus. Not Spire issue.

"Scavenger?" Ines asked.

"Possibly."

"Guild?"

"No visible tag."

"Illegal, then."

"Most useful people are."

"Flattery this early?"

"Classification."

She smiled.

Drogan opened the service door.

Stale air breathed out.

The inside of the station smelled of old dust, wet concrete, and faint ozone. Rows of turnstiles stood frozen under dead advertisement panels. Someone had stripped the copper from the ticket machines years ago. A map of the transit network remained on one wall, half peeled away, the colored lines leading to stations no one used anymore.

The Rift pressure began at the stairs.

Roan felt it behind his teeth before he saw anything. A thin vibration, not sound exactly, more like the sensation of biting foil while someone whispered through bone. His Vitalis responded by tightening along his ribs.

The Vance Pendant cooled against his chest.

[Ambient distortion detected.]

[Signal bleed reduced.]

[Recommendation: do not remove anchor.]

Roan had not planned to Good to have consensus.

Brendt rolled his shoulders and deployed the first shield frame. It unfolded from his pack with a metallic snap, expanding into a curved rectangular guard braced along his forearm. Not elegant. Practical. The frame made him look broader, which was usually a poor combat choice but useful when his job was to stand between fragile people and teeth.

Ines removed three small brass markers from her coat and placed one on the wall beside the stairs.

"Route tag one," she said. "Surface access."

Drogan nodded. "Masks on at the second landing. No talking after the gate unless necessary. Necessary doesn't include commentary."

Brendt looked wounded.

Roan fitted his filter mask around his neck but didn't raise it yet.

They descended.

The stairs went deeper than the station's architecture justified. That was normal for Rift-contaminated spaces. Reality near a stable breach tended to compromise with geometry the way politicians compromised with ethics: loudly and badly, plus at public expense.

First landing: old maintenance offices. Empty. Dust thick enough to show no recent movement except a single dragged line along the floor, too narrow for a body. Cable, maybe.

Second landing: the lights failed.

Drogan raised one hand. Everyone stopped.

Roan listened.

Drip of water. Low electrical hum. Brendt's breathing, too controlled to be natural. Ines's coat shifting as she lowered one hand near her knife. Drogan's pulse, steady. His own heartbeat, slower than it ought to be.

Then, beneath all of it, the Rift.

Not a sound. A pressure.

Like a door thinking about opening.

Drogan touched the wall. A row of emergency lights flickered on, dim red, leading down.

"Convenient," Brendt murmured.

Drogan looked at him.

"Necessary commentary," Brendt whispered.

Roan raised his mask.

The air changed after the third landing.

Gray mist clung to the steps. It didn't move like normal vapor. It gathered in corners, slid away from their boots a moment too late, and reformed behind them as if reconsidering. Roan extended a thread of Vitalis toward it, barely more than a touch.

The mist tasted like rust and old rain.

The Codex Shard pulsed once.

[Low-grade Hollow residue.]

[No inversion value.]

[Do not inhale.]

Roan stopped testing.

At the bottom of the stairs stood a blast door.

It had been installed by people who understood fear professionally. Three meters high, layered alloy, ward bands across the frame, mechanical locks, Spire seals, municipal seals, Sentinel Bureau seals, and beneath all of them older scratch marks where something had once tried to get out.

Brendt looked at the marks. "Those are on the inside."

"Yes," Drogan said.

"Just confirming."

Drogan placed his palm against the central lock.

For a moment nothing happened.

Then the door recognized him.

That was the only way Roan could describe it. The wards didn't merely accept a key or passcode. They stirred, tasted Drogan's Vitalis, and withdrew with something like reluctance.

Another old access.

Another old silence.

The blast door groaned open.

The Rift gate waited beyond.

It was smaller than Roan expected.

No giant portal. No dramatic vortex. No vertical wound in space throwing purple lightning across the chamber, despite what cheap documentaries liked to show. The breach looked like a circular depression in the air about two meters wide, filled with slow gray light. Its edges were wrong, frayed into threads that vanished before the eye could track them.

Around it, the containment chamber had been built in concentric rings. Ward pylons. Sensor towers. Emergency shutters. Burn marks.

On the far wall, someone had painted over an old warning.

The paint had peeled.

Underneath, seven narrow lines formed part of a circle.

Roan stopped.

Drogan noticed but said nothing.

Ines followed Roan's gaze. Her face didn't change. Her eyes did.

Brendt saw all three of them see it and sighed. "I assume that's bad."

"Not confirmed," Roan said.

"That means bad."

"It means not confirmed."

"Roan."

"Fine. Bad."

"Thank you."

Drogan stepped in front of the Rift gate. "Last chance to back out."

No one moved.

Brendt looked at the breach, then at Roan, then at Drogan. "For the record, I am not too proud to back out. I know Roan would go anyway, Ines would go because profit has apparently eaten her survival instinct, and you would go because you're old and allergic to explaining yourself. So my safest option is staying close to the idiots with weapons."

"That was almost touching," Ines said.

"It was meant to be insulting."

"Needs work."

Roan checked his gear one final time. Sidewound at the back. Standard knife left hip. Sealant. Flares. Chalk. Cord. Notebook sealed in inner pocket. Pendant cold. Quota full: five usable inversions, assuming no major overdraw. Vitalis stable below peak. No headache. No memory slip.

Good enough.

Drogan looked at each of them in turn.

"Rules," he said. "One: if I say run, you run. Two: if I say leave someone, you leave them."

Brendt stiffened.

Roan looked at Drogan. "No."

Drogan's eyes hardened. "This isn't negotiable."

"Yes, it is."

"No, boy. It isn't."

Roan stepped closer. "If you say leave Ines because retrieving her kills all four of us, I'll evaluate. If you say leave Brendt, I'll evaluate and conclude you're wrong. If you say leave me, I'll assume you're trying to be noble and ignore it. Precision matters."

The containment chamber went quiet.

Brendt looked down.

Ines's expression turned unreadable.

Drogan stared at Roan. "Your father hated orders too."

"Did he survive them?"

"No."

"Then I'll continue."

Drogan's jaw tightened. Then he gave a short, humorless laugh. "Fine. Rule two: if I say leave someone, you have three seconds to make a better plan."

"Accepted."

"Rule three: no heroics."

Roan tilted his head. "Define heroics."

"Doing something stupid because it feels right."

"I don't do that."

Brendt coughed.

Roan ignored him.

Drogan looked unconvinced but moved on. "Rule four: anything that speaks with a human voice in there's lying until proven otherwise."

"What if it's us?" Brendt asked.

"Especially then."

That wasn't ideal.

Drogan turned toward the gate. "Masks sealed. Weapons ready. Touch the tether line as you cross."

A black cable ran from a pylon beside the breach into the gray light. Physical tether, warded. Thing people used when they didn't trust maps, compasses, or space to keep its promises.

Drogan went first.

The Rift swallowed him without drama.

Ines went second, one hand on the tether, eyes forward.

Brendt took a breath. "I hate this."

"Yes," Roan said.

"That was your motivational speech?"

"You know the risks. You're still here. Motivation would be redundant."

Brendt stared at him for a second, then smiled despite himself. "You're terrible at friendship."

"I'm efficient at it."

"No. You're really not."

Brendt stepped through.

Roan remained alone in the containment chamber for one breath.

The painted symbol on the far wall seemed to watch him through layers of old paint and institutional denial.

The Codex Shard pulsed.

[Lineage marker proximity increasing.]

[Proceed.]

Roan placed his hand on the tether.

Cold ran up his arm.

He stepped into the gray.

- break -

Transition through a Rift didn't feel like movement.

It felt like being measured by something that didn't approve of the result.

For a fraction of a second, Roan's body existed as a list of disputes. Bone density. Blood pressure. Vitalis circulation. Scar tissue. Foreign shard. External pendant. Weapon resonance. Intent.

The Rift touched each item and tried to decide whether to let it pass.

The Codex Shard answered first.

Not with power. With identity.

The gray pressure recoiled.

Roan landed on one knee in wet ash.

His left hand hit cracked stone. His right hand went to Sidewound before his vision cleared.

Air scraped through his mask with a mineral taste.

He looked up.

The Hollowed City rose around them.

No briefing had prepared him for the scale.

The containment chamber on the Ironholm side had been underground, industrial, human. The Rift side opened beneath a sky that had forgotten the sun. Gray mist hung between towers of black stone and bone-white concrete. Streets spread in broken grids, half-buried under ash. Buildings leaned against each other like exhausted men. Windows stared without glass. Bridges arched overhead from one impossible angle to another, some ending in open air where sections had ceased.

In the distance, a tower rose above the rest, split down the middle by something that hadn't been a blade.

Everything was quiet.

Not empty.

Quiet.

Roan stood slowly.

Drogan was already scanning the street, one hand raised. Ines placed a second route marker against a nearby wall. Brendt had landed badly but recovered, shield frame deployed, eyes too wide.

"Welcome to Tier Three," Drogan said.

His voice came through the mask filter, flat and close.

Brendt looked at the dead city. "I prefer the station."

"Most people do."

Roan turned in a slow circle.

The Rift gate behind them resembled a vertical pool of gray light suspended in a ruined archway. The tether vanished into it. Around the arch, old carvings had been scraped away. Not eroded. Removed deliberately.

Clean stone in a dirty ruin.

Eckhart's warning returned.

Don't trust clean ruins.

Roan crouched beside the arch and ran gloved fingers near the scraped surface without touching it.

Recent? No. The stone edges had softened under ash and Rift moisture. Years at least. Perhaps decades. Someone had removed symbols long ago.

Or something had.

The Vance Pendant cooled further.

[Signal bleed reduced.]

[Ambient recognition risk: moderate.]

Recognition risk.

Roan disliked that phrase.

Ines knelt beside him. "Find something?"

"Absence."

She looked at the scraped wall, then nodded. "Someone removed markings."

"Carefully."

"Which means they feared the markings more than the effort."

"Or valued them."

"Both?"

"Likely."

Drogan's voice cut in. "We move."

Roan stood.

The team formed without discussion. Drogan front. Brendt slightly behind and right, shield ready. Ines center-left, map case open, tagging route points. Roan rear, because the rear saw what followed and because Drogan trusted him to kill quiet things.

That was either confidence or delegation of bad luck.

The street underfoot wasn't stone. Not entirely. The surface had once been paved, but ash had fused into it, creating a brittle crust that cracked under pressure. Every step mattered. Too heavy, and sound carried. Too light, and footing failed. Brendt adjusted fastest after Roan, which wasn't surprising. Carrying too much gear through Greycross markets had taught him what rich students learned from balance coaches.

They passed the first building.

Inside, rows of desks sat under a collapsed ceiling.

School? Office? Administrative hall?

On one desk lay a cup.

Ines stopped.

Roan saw it too.

The cup was clean.

White ceramic. No ash inside. No cracks. Sitting upright in a room where the roof had fallen thirty years ago or three hundred, depending on how Rift time had decided to lie.

Drogan's hand closed into a fist.

Everyone froze.

The cup didn't move.

Brendt's voice whispered through the mask. "That's extremely not normal."

Roan looked at Drogan.

The old man stared at the cup with a face like locked metal.

"You've seen this before," Roan said.

"no."

Lie.

Not complete. Drogan wasn't lying about the cup. He was lying about the category.

Roan looked back at the clean ceramic.

"Do we destroy it?"

"No," Drogan said.

"Why?"

"Because we don't know who notices."

Reasonable.

Annoying.

They moved on.

The city watched them with empty windows.
