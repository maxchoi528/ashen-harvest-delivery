Chapter 31: Vitalis Trial

The bracket went up at ninth bell.

Roan was late on purpose. The central hall would be packed with four hundred students craning at the ranking board, and four hundred students meant four hundred data points he didn't need. He'd learn his draw secondhand, process it without the crowd's noise distorting his reading, and arrive after the first wave of reactions had already peaked and crested.

The hall's stone colonnades echoed with the low thrum of voices by the time he slipped through the eastern arch. The ranking board covered the entire northern wall, a grid of names and seedings in thick black ink, the committee's handwriting precise as a surgeon's cuts. Roan didn't look at it. He positioned himself near the back pillar, where the shadows pooled thick, and watched the crowd instead.

Brendt was three rows from the board, arms crossed, jaw set in that way that meant he'd already found his own name and was pretending he hadn't. Kira Fenwick was written beside Roan's seeding number. He read it once. Confirmed it twice. Moved on.

Kira Fenwick. Tempered tier, House Vell, right-hand dominant. Solid fundamentals. No remarkable kills on record, which meant she fought by the book and won or lost on whether her opponent could exploit the gaps between her standard forms. A manageable match. Twenty seconds if Roan pushed. Forty if he played it cautious and let her extend.

The problem with a quick match was that forty seconds of easy dominance still showed too much.

He ran the figures. His projected bracket path: Fenwick in round one, then a second round against whoever survived the matchup two slots below. That bracket was light, a courtesy seeding for someone the committee expected to lose in the qualifiers. If Roan handled Fenwick clean, he'd face another Tempered or low-Hardened in round two and slide into the quarters without pressure. Clean. Political. Exactly what House Vance's reputation needed after the scandal.

*Too clean*, a voice in the back of his mind noted. *Which means they'll change it.*

"There you are."

Selene Ardent's voice, low and controlled, came from his left. She stood three feet away, close enough for conversation, far enough to maintain plausible deniability. Her expression was composed, the way it always was when she was calculating something. Theon Mercer flanked her, arms folded, radiating discomfort.

"Your first match is Kira Fenwick," Selene said.

"I know."

"The committee already moved it."

He caught the shift before she finished the sentence—a micro-tension in her shoulders that hadn't been there a moment ago, the kind of adjustment you made when you were about to deliver news you knew would land badly. Roan kept his posture easy, his expression neutral, but the balance in his head recalibrated. He looked at her directly.

"Moved it how?"

"Re-draw. Her opponent was changed after the initial posting." Selene's gaze flicked to the board and back. "Your new opponent will be announced in ten minutes. The committee received a formal request from House Vell's section of the tournament committee to adjust bracket assignments for 'competitive balance.' The request was approved."

House Vell. The Vell patriarch, whose name sat on the Conclave's founding charter. Whose daughter had qualified for the global bracket with a war-hammer and a kill count that made veteran students step back from the practice ring.

"Competitive balance," Roan repeated.

"You're a Tempered-tier student facing a Tempered-tier opponent in round one. That's not balanced by their math." Selene's voice dropped another degree. "Someone decided you were too comfortable."

Brendt materialized beside them, his face a shade darker than Roan's own mood.

"I heard," he said. "They're changing your draw?"

"They're considering it," Selene corrected.

"The consideration has already been approved," Theon muttered. "I saw the document. It left the committee office an hour ago."

Roan let the silence stretch. Three seconds. Four. Enough for Brendt to shift his weight and for Selene's composed expression to develop the faintest hairline fracture of uncertainty.

The administrative officer's voice cut through the hall's noise. A crowd began gathering near the board, and Roan's name appeared on the updated bracket alongside a new opponent.

Iris Vell.

Hardened tier. House Vell. The war-hammer that had cracked a practice dummy's spine in the qualifying rounds. The daughter who had made a Tempered opponent spit blood in under thirty seconds during the previous regional heat.

Selene exhaled once, slow. "They didn't even wait for the formal ten-minute window."

Brendt said something Roan didn't process. His attention had narrowed to a single point: the name on the board, and the arithmetic that had just detonated his entire bracket strategy.

Iris Vell. Hardened tier. Twelve hundred Vitalis baseline, two thousand if she pushed to peak. Roan was at one thousand four hundred, his own peak after the breakthrough, still settling into the new tier's ceiling. A six-hundred-unit gap. She would hit harder, move faster, and sustain longer than he could match through conventional exchange.

The balance on a fair fight was brutal.

He walked through it anyway, because walking through the arithmetic was all he had.

Sidewound was rated for a thousand units of sustained throughput before its edge began degrading. He was at eight hundred from the qualifier rounds. If he activated Inversion at the trigger threshold, absorbing her first real strike, converting it to Vitalis gain, he'd eat roughly three hundred units of kinetic damage before the conversion completed. His Vitalis channels would dilate. His capacity would shift by one, dropping from five available to four. Four charges remained for the tournament's harder rounds. Spending one against a first-round matchup that should've been a formality wasn't the play. The answer came fast: he could win this fight without Inversion, but only if he changed his approach entirely.

*Not the approach. The goal.*

He wasn't trying to beat Iris Vell. He was trying to beat her in a way that revealed nothing about what he could do.

The war-hammer was a power weapon. She would want to end exchanges fast, two or three strikes, maximum. Anyone caught in her arc took blunt trauma that bypassed surface defense and rattled internal organs. A Tempered student had collapsed from internal bleeding after she caught him in the ribs during regionals. She didn't fight long. She didn't need to. Against that profile, the correct counter wasn't to counter.

He needed to survive her window, the first ninety seconds when she would push hardest to secure a quick kill, and extend the match into the middle round, when her stamina would begin its natural Hardened-tier decline. Most Tempered students couldn't survive that window. They tried to trade strikes with her, matched her pace, and were crushed in the first exchange.

Roan didn't need to match her. He needed to stay out of her arc until she was frustrated enough to change her approach.

The Crimson Wake's first principle: bleed forward. Not literal blood, the technique's flow state that traded small, survivable cuts for accelerated movement. If he bled *toward* her instead of away, closing the distance with the Crimson Wake's burst mechanics, she'd have to adapt to a target inside her power arc where the hammer's momentum became a liability.

It was a terrible plan built on one good idea. The terrible parts: it required him to take at least one clean strike from the hammer. The good idea: it might work.

The blade's edge was still at eighty-four percent integrity—serviceable, but the degradation curve meant he was burning through its tolerance faster than he liked, with seven hundred and twenty units of load already logged against a thousand-unit threshold. He had three weeks. The bracket had been re-drawn, but the matches hadn't started yet, there was a gap for students to prepare, to review footage, to adjust training regimens. Three weeks wasn't nothing. It wasn't enough to close the six-hundred-unit gap through conventional training, but it was enough to optimize his existing resource base.

The Sevenfold Bleed's first strike widened his Vitalis channels. Wider channels meant faster Vitalis throughput during combat, a benefit that compounded over a long fight. If he activated it during the match, he'd take the standard Vitalis channels strain and convert it through Inversion to channel dilation. The cost was one charge. The benefit was a permanent upgrade to his combat baseline that would persist past the tournament.

The Crimson Wake gave him mobility. The Sevenfold Bleed gave him staying power. Combined, they let him close the gap, eat one strike, and then outlast her in the middle round when her frustration cost her precision.

Sixty-two percent probability of clean execution. Thirty-one percent probability of needing to activate Inversion as a backup. Seven percent probability of everything going wrong.

Roan catalogued the seven percent.

Iris Vell was aggressive. If she saw a Tempered opponent closing the gap, she'd switch from power strikes to sweeping arcs, using the hammer's weight differently, as a containment tool rather than a kill tool. The sweeping pattern would close his approach window and force him into a defensive posture. Defensive posture against a Hardened war-hammer user meant losing Vitalis to blocking, which meant his stamina would crater before hers did.

He needed a third option. Something that broke the pattern before she could establish it.

Hollowstrike's reflected blow mechanic: when he took a strike, a fraction of the kinetic force was returned to his opponent as a pulse through their own skeletal structure. In theory, a clean Hollowstrike counter on the hammer's handle would send a vibration through her wrists that would compromise her grip for the next two seconds.

Two seconds. Enough to close, deliver one Crimson Wake strike, and reset.

The combination wasn't elegant. It was three techniques forced into a sequence that their creators had never intended. But the individual components were sound, and the math held: if he executed the sequence cleanly, he looked like an aggressive Tempered fighter who had gambled on closing the gap and gotten lucky. If he didn't execute cleanly, he still had Inversion as a reserve.

It wasn't a plan. It was the best approximation of a plan achievable with the information available.

Roan blinked. The hall was still full, but the crowd around the bracket had thinned, most students had found their matches and filtered away. Brendt was watching him with that expression he wore when he wanted to say something but was trying to decide if it was his place. Selene had stepped back, her composure restored.

"I need to go," Roan said.

"Where?"

"Training hall. I have three weeks."

Brendt's jaw worked. "They're trying to kill you before round one."

"Fourteen minutes," Roan corrected. "The formal announcement was fourteen minutes ago. If they wanted to kill me, they'd have put me against Cyrus." He paused at the edge of the colonnade. "Speaking of which, you're in his path."

"I know."

"Lose clean. Don't give him footage."

Brendt's expression shifted into something harder than Roan had seen in months. "And if you get hurt because some Conclave bastard moved your draw to cover an assassination with paperwork?"

"Then you do whatever you were going to do regardless." Roan turned. "Win your match first."

He walked out before Brendt could respond, the colonnade's shadow swallowing his back.

- break -

The training hall was empty at tenth bell. Most students were in the common areas, arguing about bracket changes and trade rumors, processing the tournament drama through the only outlet available to them. Roan walked the perimeter alone, running the Iris Vell scenario through every iteration he could construct.

The file on her was sparse. House Vell kept their qualifiers private, no footage of her regional matches had been released through normal channels. Everything he knew came from secondhand accounts: the war-hammer, the aggressive opening, the three-second finish against a Tempered opponent who had reportedly underestimated her.

The underestimate was the key. She had a reputation for quick kills, which meant people expected her to open with overwhelming force and end the match fast. That expectation was a vulnerability. Anyone who expected overwhelming force would try to survive the opening. Anyone who tried to survive the opening would play defensive. Anyone who played defensive against Iris Vell would be swept into her arc and broken.

Roan wasn't going to play defensive.

The Crimson Wake was the only technique in his arsenal that explicitly rewarded aggression. The Sevenfold Bleed required him to take damage to activate, the first strike's Vitalis channels strain came from his own technique, not from the opponent. The Inversion would convert that strain, but only if he triggered the Sevenfold first.

The sequence, then: Crimson Wake approach. Take one hammer strike. Sevenfold Bleed activation, absorb his own Vitalis channels strain. Use the Inversion window to widen his channels mid-combat. Hollowstrike counter. Reset. Exploit the middle round.

Sixty-two percent. Thirty-one. Seven.

He wasn't comfortable with those numbers. The seven percent scenario was where everything aligned against him: Iris Vell adjusted her strategy faster than his model predicted, the Hollowstrike counter failed to disrupt her grip, and he was forced to activate Inversion under conditions where the tournament observers were already watching him specifically because of the draw change.

If that happened, they'd see him absorb kinetic damage that should have crumpled his ribcage and walk out of it with wider channels and more Vitalis than he entered with. The Inversion would be visible. Not as a system, not as a mechanism, just as a man who should have lost and didn't, for reasons that wouldn't map to any known technique.

Math didn't lie. But sometimes the tally revealed that the situation was worse than the tally suggested.

He started the first form. The training load registered at eighty units—manageable, but the six-hour recovery estimate meant he'd need to pace himself. The Crimson Wake's first stance: weight forward, blade low, the body aligned to bleed toward the opponent rather than away. He held it for thirty seconds. Adjusted. Held it again. The muscle memory was sound, the qualifier rounds had embedded the forms deep enough that he could run them without conscious direction.

The question was sequence. Crimson Wake into Sevenfold Bleed: the approach would trigger her response, the strike would land, the technique would fire. The timing was tight, one narrow beat between Sevenfold activation and the Inversion trigger. Too slow, and he'd already taken the Vitalis channels damage without the conversion. Too fast, and the Inversion wouldn't register the Sidewound.

He ran the timing drill. Once. Twice. Five times.

On the seventh repetition, the hall's door opened.

Drogan Hale stood in the frame, his Iron Hand catching the training hall's low light. The warden didn't speak immediately, he watched Roan hold the Crimson Wake stance, watched the adjustment, watched the seventh repetition fail to clean up a wrist angle that would have compromised the follow-through.

"You're early," Drogan said.

"The bracket moved."

"I heard." He crossed the hall in four steps, his gait deliberate, the Iron Hand hanging at his side with the casual menace of something that could crush stone. "Iris Vell."

"Yes."

"You'll need the Hollowstrike counter for the grip disruption. But that's not your problem." Drogan stopped three feet away, close enough that Roan had to look up. "Your problem is that House Vell didn't move your draw for competitive balance. They moved it because someone paid them to."

Roan's expression didn't change. But his mental model of the situation underwent a quiet revision.

Selene's words: *The committee received a formal request.* House Vell's section of the committee. Not the main committee, a faction within it. Faction-level coordination meant resources beyond a single family's political interest.

"You think it's House Ashford," Roan said.

"I think it's someone who benefits from House Vance looking weak in a public forum. The scandal already damaged your standing. A first-round loss to a Hardened opponent confirms the narrative." Drogan studied him with the flat, assessing gaze of a man who had seen every combination of tournament politics and combat technique. "Or it confirms you're worth killing."

"Killing requires paperwork."

"Killing through paperwork is still killing. They just let the tournament committee hold the knife." Drogan gestured toward the stance Roan was holding. "Show me the sequence."

Roan shifted into the Crimson Wake first form. Drogan watched without comment until he reached the transition point, the moment where the approach became the strike became the Inversion trigger.

"Stop."

Roan stopped.

"Your wrist breaks before the Inversion window closes. You're bleeding off forty milliseconds of conversion time because you can't lock your joint angle under stress." Drogan's Iron Hand moved, not attacking, just demonstrating. The motion was slow enough to track, fast enough to be correct. "Fix it. Again."

Roan reset. Ran the sequence. Fixed the wrist angle.

"Again."

Thirty-two repetitions. The wrist stopped breaking. The conversion window held. The Hollowstrike counter locked cleanly into the sequence, and for the first time, the ledger felt less like a desperate approximation and more like something that might work.

Sixty-two percent.

Still not comfortable. But better.

Drogan watched the thirty-second repetition in silence. When Roan finished, the warden didn't offer praise or correction. He nodded once and turned, then walked back toward the door.

"Your father fought a similar match in the '92 Conclave trials," Drogan said, not turning around. "They moved his draw the night before. Put him against a Hardened opponent three tiers above his rank."

"What did he do?"

"He won. Barely. The wrong people were watching, and they understood what they'd seen." Drogan paused at the threshold. "He spent the next eight years being hunted by people who wanted the mechanism he was running."

He left.

The training hall settled into silence. Roan stood in the Crimson Wake first form, wrist locked, conversion window clean, the numbers in his head cycling through their endless permutations.

Sixty-two percent clean execution.

Thirty-one percent needing Inversion.

Seven percent everything going wrong.

One additional variable he hadn't factored: the wrong people might already be watching. The draw change was too direct, too public, too obviously political. Someone had wanted to see what happened when a Vance fought a Vell in the first round. Someone who might be tracking the outcome not for bracket drama, but for something older. Something with a name that had been buried with the scandal but apparently hadn't stayed buried.

Roan held the stance for ten more seconds. Then he reset and ran the sequence again, the training load settling at four hundred and eighty units—a recovery cost that would take fourteen hours to clear—but he didn't plan to stop.

[Inversion capacity: 4/5.]

[Match countdown: 21 days.]

The figures didn't care. But the balance didn't tell the whole story either, and somewhere in the gap between what the tally said and what was happening, something was moving that Roan couldn't see yet.

He ran the sequence again.
