Chapter 79: Mira's Recording

Mira Vance lay in the same bed Roan had seen her in three weeks ago. Same monitors. Same oxygen cannula. Same grey light through the window. But something had changed. She was sitting up. Her eyes were clear. And on the bedside table, next to the plastic water pitcher, lay a small voice recorder.

Roan stopped in the doorway.

"Close the door," Mira said.

He closed it.

"Sit."

He sat in the chair beside her bed. The same chair from his last visit. The same scratch on the armrest where some previous occupant had carved an initial and given up.

Mira reached for the voice recorder. Her hand trembled slightly, the monitor's pulse trace flickered in response. She held the device out to him.

"Your father left this with me the night before he died."

Roan didn't take it. "I know."

"He told me to hold it until you were ready. I didn't think you'd be ready at seventeen." Her mouth moved. Not quite a smile. "But you found his pendant. You awakened his system. You walked into a Spire full of enemies and made them nervous." She set the recorder on the edge of the bed, close enough for him to reach. "You're ready."

Roan looked at the recorder. Small. Black. Unremarkable. The kind of device that could be bought at any electronics vendor for fifteen credits. But the data it carried had been waiting twelve years.

"How long is it?"

"Four minutes. Maybe five." Mira's voice was steady. She had already heard it. She had already processed it. "He recorded it in his office, after everyone left. He knew he wasn't coming back."

Roan picked up the recorder.

It was lighter than he expected.

"I'll be outside," Mira said. She swung her legs off the bed, slow, careful, her IV stand rattling as she moved. "Take as long as you need."

She left. The door clicked shut.

Roan sat alone in the hospital room, holding his father's voice in his hand.

He pressed play.

A breath of static. Then:

"Roan."

The word hit like a physical force. Aldric Vance's voice. Not a memory, not a reconstruction. The actual sound of his father speaking, recorded twelve years ago, preserved in digital amber.

"If you're hearing this, I'm dead."

A pause. The sound of Aldric adjusting his position, the creak of a leather chair Roan remembered from the basement office.

"I wanted to tell you a lot of things. I wanted to tell you that I was proud of you. I wanted to tell you that I was sorry for leaving. I wanted to tell you that the work I was doing mattered, even though it cost me everything. But I didn't know how to say any of it without making it sound like an excuse. So I'll say this."

Static. A breath.

"The Inversion system isn't a weapon. It's a door. I built it so that someone could walk through the pain and come out the other side still able to fight. That someone was supposed to be me. It turned out to be you."

A pause. The recording quality shifted, Aldric moving closer to the microphone.

"The men who killed me are dangerous. They're also predictable. They believe in power. They believe in control. They believe that the world runs on fear and currency and the careful management of information. They don't believe in impossible things. That's their weakness."

Another pause. Longer.

"I love you, Roan. I love your mother. I love your sister. And I believe that if anyone can finish what I started, it's you."

The recording clicked off.

00:04:17.

Roan set the recorder down.

The hospital room was the same. The monitors beeped. The oxygen hissed. The grey light through the window hadn't changed.

But Roan had changed.

He sat in the chair for a long time, hands resting on his knees, looking at the small black device that had just delivered his father's last words. He was not crying. He was not speaking. He was breathing, slow and steady, the way he always did when the world rearranged itself around him.

Then his chest tightened.

It came without warning. A pressure behind his ribs that had nothing to do with Vitalis. A weight in his throat that he couldn't swallow past. He had not cried since his father's funeral. He had trained himself out of the reflex, replaced it with calculation and distance and the careful management of emotion as a resource.

He let this one arrive without an audit. He let it break through without a plan.

He bowed his head.

A single tear fell. One. It landed on the back of his hand, and he looked at it with the detached surprise of someone who had forgotten their own body was capable of saltwater.

Then he closed his eyes. Sat with it. Let the wave pass through him without resistance, because resisting would have been more expensive than letting it end.

Forty-five seconds. That was how long he allowed himself.

Then he opened his eyes. Drew a breath that was steadier than it had any right to be. Picked up the recorder, turned it over in his hands, and slipped it into the inside pocket of his jacket, next to his heart.

He stood.

He opened the door.

Mira was waiting in the corridor, leaning against the wall with her IV stand beside her. She looked at him. Her eyes moved from his face to the small bulge in his jacket pocket, then back to his face.

"You okay?"

Roan met her gaze.

"I will be."

She nodded once. That was all the acknowledgment she gave. Then she pushed off the wall, took his arm, and walked with him back to her room without another word.

Behind them, the voice recorder sat in his jacket, carrying Aldric Vance's last message like a second heartbeat.
