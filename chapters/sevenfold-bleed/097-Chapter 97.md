Chapter 97: A Letter From Lyra

The first thing Roan noticed about the Ashford delegation was the smell.

Not literally, but the way they moved through the Ironholm campus carried a certain unmistakable air. Three students, each wearing the navy-and-silver crest of House Ashford on their combat uniforms, cutting across the central courtyard like they owned the pathway itself.

The second thing Roan noticed was that they were heading directly toward the training grounds. Toward him.

He set down the catalyst vial he'd been examining. Wraithmilk Tincture, third-rate quality, would barely net him thirty Vitalis after Inversion, and let his hand rest on Sidewound's hilt. Not a threat posture. Just... Readiness.

Brendt was three meters to his left, running through Hollowstrike forms. He'd noticed too.

"That's Tarn Boyce," Brendt muttered between stance transitions. "Third-year. Hardened rank. House Ashford's current champion in the junior rankings."

Roan ran the math. Tarn Boyce. Hardened meant Vitalis in the four-digit range. A natural talent who coasted on raw stats and never needed to ask why.

The three Ashfords stopped at the edge of the training ground. Tarn Boyce, tall, dark-haired, with shoulders that suggested heavy lifting since childhood, stepped forward.

"Roan Vance." His voice carried. Not shouting, but loud enough to draw attention from the dozen other students scattered across the grounds. "I have a proposal."

Roan didn't move. "I'm listening."

"Sparring match. Here. Now." Tarn's jaw was tight. "Nothing formal. Just a test of technique between two students of similar age."

Similar age. Right.

Roan's gaze slid to the two Ashford companions. One was recording on a crystal tablet, documentation. The other was positioned with a clear sightline to intercept anyone who might try to intervene.

A staged encounter. Someone had paid or arranged for this.

"You're offering to spar a Tempered student when you're Hardened," Roan said. Not a question. "That's generous of you."

Tarn's expression flickered. He'd expected pushback, but not this kind, straightforward, almost conversational. "House Ashford values practical demonstration over academic ranking. I'm curious whether the Vance name carries any weight beyond historical reputation."

There it was. The actual insult wrapped in plausible deniability. *The Vance name*, as if Roan's family was nothing but a footnote in history books now. The wording was too precise. Tarn was a straightforward fighter, a bruiser, not a wordsmith. Someone had written his lines. That thread was more dangerous than Ashford's money.

Brendt had stopped his forms. His Vitalis reading was spiking slightly, not aggressive, just alert.

Roan considered the angles.

Refusing meant accepting a narrative of cowardice. Accepting meant fighting someone two full ranks above him.

"You want terms," Roan said slowly. "First to five clean touches. Both combatants fight at Initiate-level output only. You're Hardened. If you can't win with your vitalis capped at Initiate ceiling, that's your answer. And if I win, House Ashford pays my next semester's tuition, full scholarship without conditions."

Silence.

Brendt made a small sound that might've been a suppressed laugh.

The two Ashford companions exchanged glances. The one with the tablet didn't lower it, documentation of their own was ongoing.

Tarn's expression went cold. "Initiate-level cap. First to five."

"Agreed."

"Today. Now."

"Today. Now." Roan turned to Brendt. "Witness?"

Brendt straightened. "I'll count touches and stop the match if either combatant crosses the legal target boundary. Standard sparring rules observed." His voice carried, not for Tarn, but for the other Ashfords. Making sure there was no room for later disputes.

The training ground's handful of observers had noticed the gathering. A small cluster was forming at the edges.

Roan stepped into the cleared space. Sidewound stayed sheathed, this was about capability.

Tarn Boyce took his position opposite him. His stance was solid, traditional Ashford style, heavy on defense and counter-strike. Waiting for the opponent to commit, then punishing the opening.

Solid. Predictable.

Roan settled into his own stance. Not a named form, a neutral guard that revealed nothing.

The recording student raised a hand. "On my mark."

Tarn's weight shifted slightly onto his back foot. Anticipating a blitz.

The hand dropped.

Roan didn't move.

Tarn's eyes narrowed. A feint?

One second. Two.

"You're stalling," Tarn said.

"I'm fighting at Initiate level." Roan's voice carried no emotion. "Initiate speed is what you're seeing. If you can't find openings at that pace, the cap isn't the problem."

Tarn's jaw clenched. He stepped forward, a probing strike toward Roan's shoulder, testing distance.

Roan shifted slightly. The strike passed two centimeters from his sleeve. No touch.

"First," Brendt announced. "No contact."

Tarn recalculated. He struck again, a thrust toward Roan's torso.

Roan twisted. The strike grazed his shirt.

"Second," Brendt called. "Slight contact."

Tarn pressed. A combination, high-low-high, testing Roan's defensive patterns.

Roan gave ground. Two steps. Three. Each retreat was minimal, just enough to avoid contact. His eyes tracked every element of Tarn's body positioning, the rhythm of his breathing, the micro-tensions in his shoulders that preceded each strike.

Pattern recognition. Tarn had three primary combinations he rotated through. All heavily weighted toward the right side. Someone had matched him to Roan because he was a physical counter—reach, mass, aggression. But they hadn't accounted for this step.

"Third," Brendt said. "Tarn Boyce."

The crowd had grown. Fifteen students now. Black Spire colors. A few other house insignias.

Tarn was breathing harder now. His offense required more energy than Roan's defense.

"Your defense is adequate," Tarn said. "But you're not landing any touches."

"I'm not trying to land touches." Roan shifted his stance. "I'm demonstrating that you can't touch me at this level. It's different."

Tarn's eyes flashed. "Then I'll make you attack."

He dropped his guard. Leaving his torso exposed. A trap.

Roan considered it for half a second.

Then he stepped in.

Not a strike, a single forward step that closed distance. His right palm extended toward Tarn's solar plexus, moving at exactly Initiate-level speed.

Tarn threw his counter-strike. Roan's palm stopped two centimeters from contact.

"Fourth," Brendt said quietly. "Roan Vance."

The training ground went silent.

"You knew where I'd be." Tarn's voice was tight. "That wasn't speed."

"You cycle through three combinations. The third ends with a right cross that covers three meters. I adjusted my position to that." Roan's expression didn't change. "You wanted proof the Vance name carries weight. This is what it carries. Not power. Prediction."

Tarn's face hardened. "The match isn't over."

"No. It's four to three. You need two touches to win. I need one."

Tarn struck. Not one of his combinations, a raw, straightforward right hook, delivered with everything he had. The Initiate cap meant the speed was lower, but the commitment was maximum.

Roan ducked under it. A pivot that moved him to Tarn's blind side.

He tapped Tarn's shoulder blade with two fingers. Soft. No force.

"Fifth," Brendt said. "Match to Roan Vance."

The training ground stayed silent.

Tarn stood with his fist still extended in the space where Roan's head had been. His shoulders were shaking.

"You were playing with me. The whole time."

"I was fighting at your level. That you couldn't handle it isn't my concern."

Someone in the crowd exhaled sharply. The Ashford recording student was already walking away, his tablet dark.

Tarn's two companions looked at each other. Then they left without waiting for their champion.

"You'll regret this," Tarn said. His voice was quiet now. "House Ashford doesn't lose gracefully."

"House Ashford lost gracefully when my father killed three of your elders in a single afternoon." Roan's voice carried no heat. "This is considerably more respectful than that standard."

Tarn turned and walked off the training ground. His back was straight. Defeat without collapse.

The crowd dispersed slowly. By dinner, everyone at Black Spire would know.

Brendt walked over. "That was reckless."

"No. It was calculated." Roan retrieved his catalyst vial from the bench. "Tarn Boyce was a tool. Someone sent him to test my response patterns. Now they have their answer."

"Which is?"

"That I'm not afraid to fight above my rank. They won't send probing attacks anymore. They'll send actual threats." Roan turned the vial over in his hands. "Which means I need to be ready for actual threats."

Brendt was quiet for a moment. "You made a joke earlier. About your father killing three Ashford elders."

"I don't joke about mathematics. The elders died in a legal challenge. House Ashford has spent two decades trying to prove otherwise."

"And you just gave them motivation."

"I gave them clarity." Roan's eyes swept the dispersing crowd. "They already had motivation. They were just hoping I'd stay quiet. Now they know I won't."

He started walking toward the armory. "I need to talk to Eckhart. And I need to know who paid Tarn Boyce to challenge me today."

"That's not public information."

"No. But there's a paper trail." He glanced back. "Start with Tarn's former roommate. Find the social connections. Someone at Ironholm knows who gave him his marching orders."

Brendt nodded. "I'll start tonight."

"Start now. The faster we have information, the faster we can adjust."

Alone now, Roan allowed himself to stop calculating. He touched the folded paper in his inner pocket. Lyra's letter was still warm against his ribs.

*The people looking for Father aren't done digging.*

He needed to be harder to kill. Faster. More capable. The Ashfords would escalate. And whoever was writing their lines had already found his family.

The tally always ran deeper.

---

The armory was quiet at this hour.

Eckhart was at his workbench, hammering something that sparked green when it struck metal. The smell of hot iron and catalyst residue hung in the air.

"Warden Vance." The old weaponsmith didn't look up. "I heard about your match."

"News travels fast."

"News about House Ashford losing travels faster." The hammer struck again. "Sit. I'll be done in a minute."

Roan sat on the tool bench's edge. The armory's single window showed the last light of afternoon.

Eckhart finished his work, quenched the metal in oil, and set the hammer aside.

"The Ashford boy you fought—Tarn Boyce—I've worked on his equipment before. He was a sharp tool. A tool that gets blunted in public doesn't get fixed. It gets tossed away." Eckhart wiped his hands on a rag. "And the one who broke it gets put on a list. Ashford keeps lists."

Roan nodded. "I suspected as much."

"The coach won't be obvious. They have people inside the Spire who aren't officially connected to the house. Administrative staff. Support personnel. Someone in a position to identify targets." Eckhart set the rag aside. "They don't lose gracefully. Tarn's a broken file now. They'll strike his name. But you—you're the anvil they've been looking for. They'll swing harder next time."

"Justification for what?"

"Formal challenges. Academic complaints. Access restrictions. Financial audits." Eckhart's expression was grim. "And if those don't work, they'll look for something more direct."

"The formal challenge route is most likely," Roan said. "If they can force a legal match against one of their higher-ranked students, they can reset the narrative."

"You'd have to accept. Refusing would damage your standing."

"But accepting means fighting someone Hardened or stronger. With House Ashford controlling the match conditions."

"Unless you find a way to refuse while appearing confident." Eckhart's eyes held something like approval. "You're thinking about it already."

"I'm always thinking about it."

Eckhart made a sound that might've been a laugh. "Your father used to say that. Usually right before he did something that made everyone else nervous." He turned back to his workbench. "I have a delivery coming in tomorrow. Catalyst batch from the eastern markets. Low quality, but sufficient quantity. If you're still buying from the grey channels, now would be a good time to stock up."

A subtle offer. Eckhart was telling him to prepare for a period of reduced access.

"I'll be here tomorrow morning."

"Good." The hammer rose again. "Now get out. I have work to do."

Roan left the armory as the last sunlight faded. The Ironholm evening was cold.

He had maybe eighteen hours before the Ashford response took shape. The ledger was running. But the balance was incomplete.

He didn't know who had coached Tarn Boyce. He didn't know whether House Greel was involved. House Greel had been quiet lately, which made Roan more nervous, not less.

He pressed his thumb against the Vance Pendant beneath his shirt. The Sidewound dissolved into the reservoir. +34 Vitalis. The conversion was clean. Degraded catalyst, stable output. Good. He needed the supply chain verification. If Ashford was going to cut him off, he had to know how low-grade he could go and still profit. This wasn't random enhancement. It was logistics.

He touched the letter in his pocket.

The unknowns were dangerous. But Roan Vance had never needed certainty to act.

He needed probability. Margin. Options.

---

The message arrived at midnight.

Brendt's courier handed over a sealed envelope and left without speaking. Efficient. Untraceable.

Roan opened it in his dormitory room, alone.

The handwriting was Brendt's. Precise, functional. No wasted words.

*Spoke with Tarn's former roommate. Tarn received payment three days ago, 5,000 Spire Credits, deposited to a holding account. Source traced to external benefactor, not internal Ashford channel.*

*The benefactor's identity is protected by privacy seals, but I found someone who owes me a favor. The seal signature matches House Greel's administrative files.*

*House Greel paid Tarn Boyce to challenge you. The timing coincides with House Greel's return from the northern territories.*

*Also: Tarn's roommate says Tarn mentioned that someone gave him specific instructions about how to phrase the challenge. Advice about making you angry. Making you fight predictably.*

*The instructions came from a third party, not Greel, not Ashford. Tarn didn't know who. But the person was familiar with Ashford internal politics.*

*More investigation needed. Be careful.*

Roan read the letter twice. Then he held it over the small trash incinerator.

The paper caught, curled, and disintegrated to ash.

House Greel and House Ashford, working together or in parallel. The two families that had suffered most from his father's actions.

But the third party—someone with inside knowledge of both houses, capable of coordinating an attack without official connection—that was the real problem. Anyone could pay Tarn. Not anyone could design the exact approach that would provoke a response while appearing legitimate.

Someone had studied him. His patterns. His likely reactions.

He looked at Lyra's letter, still unfolded beside his lamp. *The people looking for Father aren't done digging.*

He touched the pendant.

[New pattern logged.]

[Threat vector identified: coordinated multi-house action.]

[Root cause: likely connected to father conflict, pre-existing grudge.]

The Aldric Echo's signature. Occasional. Accurate.

Roan stared at the glowing embers of the burned paper.

Greel, Ashford, and a shadow. They had targeted his family. They had set a trap in the training grounds. They thought they understood his patterns.

The math was incomplete. His reaction wouldn't be.

Roan Vance stood up, folded Lyra's letter into his inner pocket, and walked out into the Ironholm night.

He wasn't going to wait for the board to reveal itself.

He was going to shake it.
