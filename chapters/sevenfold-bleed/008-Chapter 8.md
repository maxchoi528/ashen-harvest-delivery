Chapter 8: I Know

Kid, this'll burn your veins to ash."

The words came from behind a counter cluttered with unmarked vials and devices Roan didn't recognize. Garrick, sixtyish, built like someone who'd stopped caring about his body twenty years ago, set a dark bottle on the stained wood between them. The liquid inside seemed to swallow the candlelight.

"Most kids your age want the good stuff. The kind that makes you feel invincible." Garrick's grin showed yellowed teeth. "This doesn't make you invincible. This makes you permanent."

Roan studied the bottle. Red-black, dense, swirling with something that moved against the light. Bloodscourge Draught. The same compound that had been slowly destroying Vance combat students for three decades. One sip and the body rejected its own Vitalis channels. The meridian network would scar. The lifespan would shorten. The pain would last for weeks.

Everyone who drank it and failed ended up broken.

Or dead.

"Fifty credits," Garrick said.

Roan met his eyes. "Thirty."

Garrick laughed. "Kid, you don't haggle with a death sentence."

"You do." Roan's voice stayed level. "Especially with a death sentence."

The older man leaned back, studying him. Something shifted in his expression, curiosity replacing amusement. "You're Vance's kid."

"Yes."

"The one who put Corvin in the infirmary last week."

"Word travels."

"In Ironholm? Word travels before the person who caused it gets home." Garrick tilted his head, gaze moving over Roan's face like he was reading something written there. "Yeah. You look like him. Same jaw. Same eyes."

The room went quiet. "Your old man used to come through here," Garrick said. "Before he died. Bought the same stuff."

Roan didn't react. "Did he."

"Didn't drink it himself though. You understand? Bought it for someone else."

The words landed in Roan's chest like a blade between ribs. He let them sit there. Didn't pull them out. Didn't examine them in front of Garrick.

"Forty credits."

"Thirty-five."

"Thirty-eight. That's my final offer."

Roan considered. The negotiation was a formality. The contract already gave him a claim on aging stock. He could invoke it and leave. But the assessment was part of the work: measuring the value of what he was about to consume, even if he wasn't paying cash.

He could see it in the dust on the bottle's surface, the way Garrick hadn't polished it before laying it out. Unsold inventory. Stock that had been sitting long enough to gather grime.

"Thirty-eight," Roan said. "Applied against the contract."

Garrick's hand stopped. The clause. First pick on aging stock. The old man's jaw tightened, but he didn't argue.

He slid the vial across the counter. "Drink it somewhere private, kid. The screaming tends to disturb the neighbors."

Roan pocketed the bottle. No credits exchanged hands. The cost was a draft on a contract he'd written weeks ago, a death risk already converted into leverage.

Tomorrow's income from classes and whatever Brendt could scrounge could go to equipment, not survival. The economic equation had shifted.

It would be enough.

"One more thing." Garrick's voice stopped him at the door.

Roan waited.

"Your old man. He was buying for someone else, like I said." The vendor rubbed at the counter, a nervous habit. "But here's the thing. Whoever he was buying for, they weren't local. Nobody in Ironholm had access to stuff Aldric was moving. Ashford contacts, maybe. Maybe even Greel."

Roan said nothing.

"The point is—" Garrick stopped, shook his head. "Never mind. Forget I said anything. Get out of here, kid. And don't come back unless you're buying. I don't do refunds on this kind of merchandise."

Roan walked out.

The Ironholm night swallowed him whole. Black Spire's towers rose against a sky that never showed stars, just the perpetual haze of a city that burned too much coal and killed too many people who complained about the smoke. Roan moved through streets he knew like the veins in his own arms, past the shuttered markets and the vendors who were just starting their night shifts.

His hand stayed on the vial in his pocket. Solid. Real. Heavy in a way that had nothing to do with weight.

A draft on a contract. A tool his father had used to supply people Roan had never known existed.

The walk home took forty minutes. He used every minute of it.

By the time he reached the edge of the Vance district, a neighborhood that had once meant something and now meant nothing, his mind had built three new frameworks around the information Garrick had dropped. Framework one: Father hadn't been working alone. Framework two: The network Father had worked with still existed, or had left resources behind. Framework three: the white-bearded man might be closer to this story than Roan had assumed.

All three frameworks created more questions than they answered.

He didn't care. Questions were just unsolved equations.

The Vance house sat dark and quiet. Mother worked the night shift at the textile mill, sixteen credits a week for labor that aged her hands before their time. Lyra was somewhere in the city, doing whatever seventeen-year-old girls did when they wanted to pretend their family name still carried weight. Mira had died three years ago. Aldric had died one year before that.

The house held two living people and two sets of ghosts.

Roan locked his bedroom door behind him. Small room. Smaller bed. Desk covered in notes he'd been collecting since he was fourteen, research on Bloodscourge, on the students who'd survived it, on the three who'd died from it. Charts of meridian pathways. Calculations of what the Inversion might do if his theory was correct.

He pulled the vial from his pocket and set it on the desk.

Red-black liquid. Heavy with potential. Tomorrow this would go into his body and either kill him or change him. No middle ground. No third option.

He sat in the darkness and stared at the bottle for a long time.

His hand found the key around his neck, small, brass, useless-looking. It opened the lock on his desk's bottom drawer, where he kept the documents that would matter if he died tonight instead of tomorrow. Not a will. Just a letter. Addressed to no one.

To the person who finds this:

If you're reading this, the experiment failed. Don't waste time looking for someone to blame. The ledger was sound. The execution had flaws. That's how it works.

Roan picked up a pen.

He wrote nothing. Set the pen down.

The letter could wait. The letter had always been able to wait. Every time he'd drafted it before, he'd found reasons to stop. Not because he was afraid of dying, he'd accepted that possibility the moment he'd started this path.

He stopped because dying now meant nothing would change.

Bloodscourge wasn't the end. Bloodscourge was the beginning, the proof that the Inversion worked, that his father's system held together, that everything he'd been building toward wasn't just the fantasy of a seventeen-year-old who'd read too many books and believed too many dead men's notes.

If he died tomorrow, that possibility died with him.

If he lived, then the real work started.

Roan opened the drawer. Pulled out a sheet of paper. Set it in front of him.

He wrote: "Lyra. The desk. Everything there's yours."

One sentence. Simple. Clean. Nothing else.

He folded the paper. Slid it beneath the drawer where it would stay if he didn't come back to move it.

The vial sat on the desk, waiting.

Tomorrow he would drink it. Tomorrow he would learn if the Inversion could absorb the Bloodscourge's damage and convert it to something useful. Tomorrow he would find out if Aldric Vance had left his son a system that worked or just another set of notes full of dead ends and false hopes.

Tomorrow would settle the theory.

Or nothing did.

His hand closed around the vial. Lifted it. Weighed it. The glass felt colder than it should have, the liquid inside denser than water.

Not yet.

He set it back on the desk. Picked up the letter to Lyra, looked at it for a moment, then slid it into the drawer and locked it.

A draft on a contract. Eight months unsold. His father's voice echoing through a dead man's notes.

Garrick's words repeated in his head: *Someone else.*

Someone else had taken what Aldric bought. Someone else had used the Bloodscourge or the Hollow Marrow or the Ironbile Resin — all the poisons Roan was planning to poison himself with. Someone else had walked this path before him.

The figures said that person had survived. The figures said the Inversion could work.

- break -

He ran three checks before sleep.

First: inventory. One Bloodscourge vial, no quality seal, glass weight consistent with old stock rather than fresh counterfeit. The stopper hadn't been resealed; the wax was cracked but continuous. That lowered the odds of recent tampering and raised the odds of long term degradation.

Second: environment. Door locked, window latched, chair wedged beneath the handle at an angle that would buy four seconds if someone forced entry. Four seconds wasn't much. It was enough to swallow something, break something, or hide something.

Third: failure case.

Roan opened the bottom drawer again and checked the paper for Lyra. One sentence. No apology. Apologies created obligations for the living and offered no useful instructions. The money he had left was marked in the margin: twelve credits under the loose floorboard, three in the coat lining, one emergency chit taped behind the bedframe.

He considered adding Mira's name. Removed the thought before it formed a sentence. His mother would find a way to make the grief her fault, and no arrangement of words from him would stop that.

That was an ugly calculation.

It was still true.

The vial waited on the desk.

He didn't pray. Prayer was a negotiation with an entity that had never shown up to sign the contract.

Instead, he wrote two numbers on a scrap of paper: expected pain onset, expected channel collapse.

Thirty seconds. Five minutes.

The Inversion was a switch. It would either recognize the sidewound or it wouldn't. If it failed, the other numbers wouldn't matter.

Tonight he slept.

Tomorrow he tested the theory.
