Chapter 113: Terms of Courtesy

Roan flexed his right hand. The knuckles popped in the wrong order, third, second, first, a reversed cascade that started in his spine and ended somewhere behind his eyes. The Marrowforge Sutra had been rewriting his skeleton for eighteen hours. Eighteen hours of osteoclastic fire, of marrow boiling and resetting, of his own body turning against him in microscopic increments. He hadn't inverted the ache. Not because he was saving his quota, though he was, but because the sutra's Sidewound was structural and gradual, plus the Codex Shard only registered damage that arrived faster than three-tenths of a second. Slow poison didn't trigger the ledger.

Two out of five.

That was the number glowing behind his eyelids when he checked the Shard's ledger.

[Daily capacity: 2/5.]

Three conversions had already bought him passage through the preliminaries. An Ironbile Resin backlash in the first round. A hairline fracture Sidewound from an Eastreach bruiser's knee in the second. The residual nerve-burn of his own Pyrebrand Cycling in the third. Each had been a clean transaction. Pain in, Vitalis out. But the ledger didn't care about his schedule. It only counted down.

The stone bench beneath him was cold. Greycross granite, shipped south to Ironholm for the Black Spire's arena annex. He knew the thermal conductivity. He knew the humidity in the prep cell, forty-two percent, just enough to make sweat linger. He knew Eamon Cole's public Vitalis reading from the Aldermere registry: five thousand eight hundred. Forged, early phase. Number that turned Tempered fighters into cautionary tales.

Roan's public reading, submitted under duress by the Spire's administrative staff, sat at nine hundred and eighty. Tempered, borderline. A clerical error that was, in fact, a lie he'd orchestrated two months prior.

The balance didn't care about reputations. But the arithmetic, right now, was incomplete. He didn't have Eamon's actual peak output, his combat form preferences, or the depth of his Mental Radius. Aldermere students didn't publish their failures. They buried them in perfect transcripts and old-money discipline.

A knock. Three raps, Brendt's pattern.

"You're up in six," his friend said through the door. The wood muffled nothing; Brendt's voice carried the tension of a man who'd watched Roan's previous three matches and knew the difference between winning and surviving. "Crowd's thick. Cole's already on the sand."

Roan stood. His spine fit a series of clicks that sounded like someone loading a rifle. The Marrowforge again. He rolled his shoulders, feeling the new density in his ribcage. If Eamon hit him there, the impact geometry would be different than it had been yesterday. Whether that meant his ribs would hold or shatter in a new, interesting way remained to be calculated.

"Right," he said.

- break -

The Aldermere Academy colors, deep green and old gold, hung from the western rafters, heavy as wet canvas. Eamon Cole stood across the sandpit, thirty meters of polished quartz that would reflect Vitalis signatures like a mirror. He was twenty, maybe twenty-one, with stillness that came from trained patience rather than Roan's brand of calculated restraint. His hands were empty. Aldermere didn't permit weapons in single combat; they considered it a confession of insufficient discipline.

The arbiter's voice boomed from the elevated platform. Combatants ready.

Roan didn't take a stance. He let his arms hang loose, fingers angled toward his thighs, weight shifted sixty-forty to the back foot. Eamon's eyes tracked the posture, cataloguing it. Roan could almost see the man's internal ledger. Tempered, likely defensive, baiting a trap.

The gong sounded.

Eamon didn't charge. He walked, each step pressing a shallow crater into the quartz as his Vitalis bled downward in controlled threads. Rooting, they called it. The Aldermere Verdant Discipline. Within three seconds, the sand in his half of the ring became a sensory net. He could feel Roan's weight through the vibrations, predict shifts before they happened.

Roan didn't move. Let him read. Let him calculate.

At ten meters, Eamon's right hand flicked up. Not a strike. A test. A lance of compressed Vitalis, bark-hard and spinning, screamed toward Roan's leading knee. Low, disabling, designed to end mobility and force a desperate response.

Roan sidestepped. Not fast, he didn't have the Vitalis margin for speed, but precisely, letting the lance pass two centimeters from his patella. The wind of it tugged his pant leg. He didn't counter. He reset his weight and waited.

Eamon's eyebrow lifted a fraction. "You're quieter than your file suggests, Vance."

"Files are averages," Roan said. "Averages lie."

Eamon smiled. It didn't reach his eyes. Then he rooted deeper.

The quartz beneath Roan's boots shifted. Microscopic filaments of Eamon's Vitalis, thin as root hairs, threaded through the sand and gripped. Not an attack. A tax. Every movement Roan made would now cost more energy, every pivot would meet resistance. Within a minute, the ring would become a swamp dressed as stone.

"Wonderful," Roan said.

- break -

The first minute passed in slow increments. Eamon circled, probing with low-output strikes, palm thrusts, knee feints, a spinning back elbow that Roan ducked by exactly four centimeters. Each time Roan refused to commit, Eamon's expression tightened by another degree. A Tempered fighter should've been exhausted by now. The rooting field drank stamina like sun on ice.

Roan let Eamon hit him.

It was a glancing blow, a ridge-hand strike to the floating ribs that Eamon disguised as a feint toward the jaw. Roan saw the geometry three frames early and chose not to dodge. The impact came, bark-wrapped Vitalis shredding the fabric of his shirt, and his newly Marrowforged ribs took the load. The bone didn't break. It sang, a low ceramic note that only Roan could feel. He let his body fold around the strike, exhaling hard, painting the picture of a man who'd just had the wind crushed out of him.

He stumbled back two steps, one hand pressed to his side. The crowd murmured. Eamon pressed forward, confident now, and threw a follow-up cross aimed at Roan's temple.

Roan slipped it. Barely. He made it look like desperation, his head snapping aside, his own counter, a short elbow to Eamon's exposed ribs, traveling at Tempered speed. Weak. Predictable. Eamon's free arm swept down to block.

The elbow stopped short. Roan had never intended to land it. He used the aborted momentum to pivot inside Eamon's guard and drove his forehead into the Aldermere student's nose.

A wet crack. Eamon staggered.

No Vitalis output on the headbutt. Pure physics. The tournament sensors wouldn't flag an impact that carried less than three hundred units.

Eamon's hand went to his face. Blood seeped between his fingers. The rooting field trembled, then stabilized as he forced his composure back into place.

"You fight like a dockyard thug," Eamon said, voice thick.

"Your nose didn't disagree."

Eamon's eyes narrowed. He abandoned the probing. His Vitalis spiked, a visible crest of green-gold rising at his shoulders, and he closed the distance with a proper Forged-level burst. Five thousand eight hundred, unleashed without reservation. A palm strike aimed at Roan's sternum that would fold a Tempered fighter in half and pulp the organs behind the ribcage.

Roan had exactly half a second to choose.

He couldn't dodge. Eamon had learned the range, adjusted for Roan's tendency to slip right. He couldn't block. A Tempered block against Forged output would shatter the arms and trip every sensor in the arena.

Stone Breath.

He triggered the Marrowforge technique. His ribs turned to stone-density for exactly two-thirds of a second. Eamon's palm connected.

The sound was a church bell dropped from a height.

Shockwaves rolled through the quartz. Eamon's eyes went wide. His hand met resistance that hadn't been there a moment before, and the recoil snapped his wrist backward. He stumbled. Roan let the impact carry him off his feet, flying backward in a controlled arc, hitting the sand in a roll that hid the fact that his ribs were intact.

The Sidewound hit immediately.

His vision went white. Not darkness, white, searing, total. Stone Breath had taken his sight as payment for the defense. The Codex Shard pulsed against his spine.

[Sidewound detected.]

Three-tenths of a second. The window ticked down. If he let the damage anchor past the threshold, the blindness would become permanent. If he inverted it, he burned one of his two remaining conversions.

Eamon was breathing hard six meters away, cradling his wrist, trying to understand why a Tempered ribcage hadn't collapsed.

Roan made the choice.

[Conversion successful: +247 Vitalis.]

His sight snapped back, restored, then sharpened. The arena resolved into a lattice of Vitalis signatures. Eamon's green-gold network blazing in Roan's improved vision, the arbiter's muted blue, the crowd a dull grey wash. He could see the gap. Under Eamon's left arm, where the Verdant Discipline's rooting feedback loop terminated, there was a node of compressed Vitalis no larger than a grape. A structural flaw. Aldermere's perfect technique, imperfectly closed.

[Daily capacity: 1/5.]

"One left," Roan said.

Eamon heard the voice and turned, still half-blind with his own pain. He raised his good arm to guard.

Roan didn't have the luxury of a drawn-out finish. He had one arm that worked properly, one conversion in reserve, and a sensor ceiling of roughly fifteen hundred Vitalis that he couldn't exceed without exposing his rank. He needed a strike that would end the fight without looking like it had ended a fight.

Sevenfold Bleed. First Strike.

He channeled it through his right fist, clamping down on the Vitalis output so hard it felt like trying to breathe through a collapsed lung. The technique wanted to roar. He let it whisper. Seven meridians in his forearm ignited, shredding themselves under the restrained load. The pain was immediate, absolute, a white-wire lance from elbow to fingertips. He didn't invert it. Couldn't afford to. The meridians tore, scorched, narrowed under the self-inflicted wound.

His fist traveled in a straight line and clipped the grape-sized node under Eamon's arm.

The effect was immediate. Eamon's Vitalis network short-circuited. The rooting field collapsed inward, the green-gold threads snapping back into his body with nowhere to go. He spasmed, dropped to one knee, and toppled sideways into the quartz.

The arbiter's count began. Ten. Nine.

Eamon didn't rise.

Roan lowered his fist. His right arm hung at his side, meridians smoldering. The sensors above the ring tracked his peak output during the exchange: one thousand one hundred and forty-seven. Under the wire.

The math held.

- break -

The medical bay smelled of copper and antiseptic. Roan sat on the examination table, shirt off, while a Black Spire physician traced a Vitalis scanner over his ribs. The woman frowned at the readings, tapped the device, and tried again.

"You're fine," she said. It didn't sound like a compliment. It sounded like an accusation. "Bruising. Minor tissue compression. For a direct hit from a Forged striker, you should have a punctured lung."

"Good bone density," Roan said.

She stared at him. He stared back. After a moment, she snapped the scanner off and marked the chart. "You're cleared. Try not to collect any more headbutts. Your skull isn't made of granite."

It was, getting closer.

She left. Roan rolled his shoulder, testing the damage in his right arm. Seven meridians, scorched. The Vitalis flow through his forearm felt like grit through a clogged pipe. He'd taken the Sidewound raw, and now he was paying for it in function. A day of rest would help. He might get twelve hours.

The door opened. Drogan filled the frame, the Iron Hand of Hale gleaming where it caught the overhead light.

"You look like you picked a fight with a wall and lost," Drogan said.

"I won."

"You survived. Different equation." Drogan stepped in, letting the door hiss shut behind him. His eyes went to Roan's arm. "That last strike. You felt wrong. Not slow. Constricted. Like you were fighting with a hand wrapped around your throat."

Roan said nothing.

Drogan watched him. Then he grunted. "The committee's reviewing the match footage. Nothing formal. Just whispering about how a Tempered brat with a nine-eighty reading ate a Forged palm strike and walked away." He paused. "They're calling it a fluke. Lucky chemistry. Aldermere's already filed a complaint about the headbutt. Unsanctioned technique."

"It was my forehead."

"Unsanctioned technique," Drogan repeated. He turned to leave, then stopped. "You got another match tomorrow. Quarterfinals. Get some sleep."

He left. Roan sat in the quiet, looking at his hands. The right one trembled slightly when he tried to close it.

He reached for the Codex Shard's ledger, wanting to confirm the numbers. The Vitalis gain from the blindness inversion. The remaining capacity.

The Shard pulsed before he could focus.

[Sidewound detected: Meridian micro-fracture.]

What?

[Conversion successful: +89 Vitalis.]

[Daily capacity: 0/5.]

Roan went still. The Sevenfold Bleed damage hadn't registered in the arena. Adrenaline, maybe. Or the Marrowforge density had masked the tear until his pulse slowed. Now, three minutes after the fight, the micro-fractures in his forearm meridians had crossed the three-tenths-second threshold. The Shard had processed them automatically.

He hadn't authorized it. But the Codex didn't ask permission for damage that lingered.

He had nothing left. Zero conversions until the reset.

"Huh," he said.

The door burst open. Brendt this time, face pale, papers in hand.

"They moved the schedule," Brendt said. "Quarterfinals are tonight. Not tomorrow. Some political garbage about broadcast rights and inter-academy scheduling."

Roan's jaw tightened. He flexed his right hand. The fingers moved half as far as they should have.

"How long?" he asked.

"Forty minutes."

Brendt stepped closer, lowering his voice. "And Roan, the committee just issued a new mandate. Every bracket upset over a tier difference gets a live blood audit before the next match. They're calling it 'competitive integrity verification.' They want a sample. Before you step back on the sand."

Roan looked at the medical cart. At the syringes in their sterile packs. At his own arm, where the meridians were scorched and the blood would carry the residue of Inversion. Blood didn't lie. A live audit would read the Marrowforge in his marrow, the Shard's presence in his chemistry, the true depth of his Vitalis. The discrepancy between nine hundred and eighty and the truth would unfold under a microscope.

He looked at his hand again. Zero capacity. Damaged pathways. An audit waiting.

The equation had gaps.

Brendt held out the bracket sheet. At the bottom, in the slot for Roan's next opponent, a name had been handwritten in a sharp, deliberate script. Not Eamon. Not another Aldermere student.

House Greel.

Roan's fingers stopped trembling. He went very still.

The audit request hadn't been random. The signature authorizing the mandate was a name he recognized from his father's journal.

Greel.
