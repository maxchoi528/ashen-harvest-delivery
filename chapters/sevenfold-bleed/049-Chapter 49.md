Chapter 49: The Cheapest Courses

The morning session dragged.

Three hours of Crimson Wake drills had the junior cohort breathing through their teeth, and Instructor Marshburn kept circling like a hawk with opinions about their footwork. Roan held position seven in the second row, running the blade forms at half-speed while his mind ran calculations at full. Theon Mercer two positions ahead had been sloppy on the transition from First Cut to Second Guard, a habit he hadn't corrected despite three corrections. Silas Deckard on his left bled from a split knuckle he'd tried to hide by keeping his hand in his pocket between sets. That was new. That meant sparring after drills.

That meant opportunity.

"Breathe through the hilt, Deckard," Marshburn called. "You're strangling it."

"Yes, Instructor."

Roan watched Deckard's jaw tighten. The knuckle was already swelling. Theon caught his gaze and looked away, which was interesting. Usually Theon was the type to stare back and manufacture a reason. Today he was deflecting.

Something had shifted in the social geometry of the cohort overnight. He filed it and returned to the form.

The Seventh Cut of Crimson Wake was a lateral sweep designed to open the torso for the follow-through. Most students treated it as a single motion. Marshburn had corrected that twice this morning: separate the sweep from the commitment, feel the half-second of exposure before you commit, use that half-second to read your opponent's weight shift. To Roan, the Seventh Cut's architecture mapped cleanly onto the Inversion logic he carried. The weapon was the side effect. The commitment was the inversion point. He kept that recognition sealed behind his expression. If you could read the moment between, you controlled the kill.

Roan had been doing this instinctively for weeks. Marshburn was now trying to make it explicit for everyone else, which meant the cohort was about two months behind where they'd need to be for anyone other than a handful to use it effectively. That was fine. Math didn't lie. Two months of drilling plus natural talent plus cohort culture wouldn't close a gap that wide for most of them.

The bell rang.

- break -

Lunch in the refectory was a controlled market. Roan collected his tray, protein paste, rice, synthetic greens, and found his usual seat near the eastern service door. Brendt was already there, eating with the mechanical precision of someone who'd learned to treat meals as fuel rather than event. He had a bruise along his jaw that was yellowing at the edges.

"Training accident?" Roan asked.

"Fell out of bed."

"You sleep on the floor."

"Fell harder, then." Brendt speared a forkful of greens. "Marshburn's been drilling that Seventh Cut for a week. Something's got him pushed."

Roan chewed. The protein paste was consistent with last week's batch, which meant the procurement office was buying from the same vendor at the same price point, which meant they weren't competitive shopping. The Spire was hemorrhaging credits on inefficiencies that a competent administrator could close in a quarter. This was neither here nor there for him, but it irritated the part of his brain that catalogued waste.

"Selene Ardent came to observe this morning," Brendt said, keeping his voice low. "Sat in the back for twenty minutes, left without talking to anyone."

Roan's chewing slowed by half a second. "What did she watch?"

"You. Specifically."

He swallowed. Let that sit for a moment. Selene had been distant since the incident at the Hollow Reaches boundary, polite, professional, giving him space. That was fine. Space was useful. But her suddenly re-engaging and watching specifically him meant something had changed in her information set. New data. New conclusion.

"Did she look at anyone else?"

"Theon Mercer. Noted something about his stance after the Fifth Cut."

Theon again. Interesting.

Roan took another bite, considered. Selene's interest in Theon could mean a dozen things. Recruitment, the Ardent family was always looking for promising students to sponsor. Intelligence gathering. Theon's father had ties to House Vell, and Selene's family had complicated history with the Vells. Or simple pedagogical concern. Theon was good, and Selene was tracking him as a future asset.

But she was tracking Roan too. Both at once meant the two were connected in her assessment.

"Why would she care about Theon's stance?" Roan asked.

Brendt shrugged. "He's been outperforming since the rankings reset. Top five now. Maybe she wants to poach him."

"Before he's ranked high enough to matter?"

"He's not going to climb much higher. Theon's got a ceiling, technique is solid but his tactical layer is thin. He'll plateau in two months." Brendt paused. "Unless someone's been coaching him."

Roan set his fork down. "You're saying someone is deliberately pushing his ranking."

"I'm saying he's been consistently sharper for three weeks, and no one in this cohort improves that fast without help."

That tracked. Theon Mercer had been competent but unremarkable six weeks ago. Since then, his reaction times were measurably better, his form execution cleaner, his composure under pressure noticeably tighter. Traditional training couldn't account for that rate of improvement. Something was supplementing it.

He ran the possibilities. Private tutoring from an outside instructor, expensive, detectable, inconsistent with his family background. Experimental stimulant regimen, plausible, but Marshburn ran drug screens and Theon hadn't flagged. Technique transfer from a senior student, possible, but the sparring logs showed no unusually frequent sessions with anyone above him in ranking.

Another possibility: Theon was getting help from someone inside the Spire who had access to training resources above his pay grade. That meant sponsorship. Someone with a reason to develop him.

The Ardent connection kept surfacing.

"Why would Selene care about Theon specifically?" Roan asked.

"I've been thinking about that." Brendt leaned closer. "His family has land holdings in the Vellmark corridor. Shared border with Ardent territory. There was a dispute two years ago over mineral rights, the Ashford tribunal ruled against the Mercers."

"So Theon's family got screwed by a tribunal that Selene's family influenced."

"Which means either Selene is trying to make amends by grooming the son, or she's trying to keep him close because he's a useful piece on the board."

Roan weighed both. The first was sentimental and unlikely. The second fit Selene's pattern, she accumulated debts and connections the way others accumulated credits. Theon being visibly in her orbit was a leash whether he knew it or not.

"What's the play?" Brendt asked.

"I need to spar with him."

Brendt blinked. "Why?"

"Because I need to see his real output. Rank tells you what's happened. Sparring tells you what's happening now." He picked up his fork again. "And because Selene is watching both of us, which means the connection between us is interesting to her whether we make it interesting or not. Better to define it on my terms."

"You want to fight him publicly."

"I want to fight him in a context where the outcome tells us both something. Public enough that she notices. Private enough that she has to read between the lines."

Brendt considered this. "That's a lot of chess for a lunch period."

"It's the only time I have." Roan finished his greens. "Marshburn's been pushing Seventh Cut drills because something's coming. The senior cohort is cycling through preparation courses, which means there's a combined exercise scheduled. Either a live exercise or a ranking evaluation."

"How do you know about the senior cohort?"

"Tanner in logistics talks when he's drunk. He was complaining about equipment requisitions yesterday." Roan stood, collecting his tray. "Combined exercises mean mixed-rank sparring. Mixed-rank sparring means Theon gets exposed to Hardened-tier pressure. If someone's been coaching him to punch above his weight class, this is when we'll see whether the investment pays out."

Or whether it collapses under real load.

- break -

The afternoon session was weapons-integrated drilling, live blades, reduced impact settings, padded contact zones. Marshburn had rearranged the cohort into matched pairs based on ranking proximity. Roan was paired with Silas Deckard, which was either a reward for his performance this morning or a punishment for something he hadn't done yet. Deckard's split knuckle was wrapped but his grip looked steady.

They ran through a standard rotation: open, exchange, score, reset. Deckard was aggressive in the opening, high guard, forward pressure, trying to dictate the terms of engagement before Roan could establish tempo. That was a known pattern for fighters with mediocre technique trying to compensate with aggression. You could read it from the first exchange. His shoulder dropped a quarter-second before his blade did whenever he committed to a strike.

Roan adjusted. Let the shoulder drop. Let the commitment happen. Then opened with a lateral deflection that turned Deckard's force back across his own centerline.

The score went to Roan, clean contact at the ribs, within the padded zone parameters. Deckard's eyes narrowed.

They reset. Deckard came in harder, which meant the psychological adjustment was working but the tactical one wasn't. More aggression, less structure. Roan's blade found the gap between his guard and his elbow twice more in the next exchange. First score was a deflection bonus. Second was a genuine opening he didn't exploit because drawing blood in training was diplomatically complicated.

Deckard was breathing hard by the fourth exchange. His technique was breaking down under pressure, which was normal for someone with a hand injury, pain was flooding his working memory, reducing the cognitive bandwidth available for real time calculation. He wouldn't quit. He wasn't the type. But he was leaking points at a rate that would make the final tally embarrassing for him.

Marshburn called the rotation. Roan stepped back.

Deckard was staring at his wrapped hand. Something had changed in his expression, not frustration, which was the expected emotional line, but something more specific. He was looking at his hand the way someone looked at a problem they didn't know how to solve.

"Your form broke down after the third exchange," Roan said quietly, not looking at him. "Weight shift went lateral instead of forward. Your shoulder compensation got worse because the knuckle was limiting your grip, which meant you were overcompensating with arm strength, which meant your reach advantage disappeared."

Deckard looked up.

"You're not injured enough to explain the drop. Which means either the injury is worse than you're showing, or something else is degrading your performance." Roan retrieved his tray. "Not my business either way. But if it's the second one and you need a sparring partner who won't exploit it publicly, I have an open slot tomorrow after the evening meal."

He walked toward the service door before Deckard could respond. The man's expression would tell him what he needed to know, whether this was a genuine injury issue or something connected to whatever was pushing Theon up the rankings. If Deckard took the offer, there was a pattern. If he deflected, it was just an injury.

The eastern corridor was quieter at this hour. Roan moved through it at a measured pace, cataloguing the ambient noise, distant foot traffic from the upper levels, the hum of the ventilation system, two voices in conversation near the junction ahead. He adjusted his trajectory to pass within earshot without obviously eavesdropping.

"— rankings reset in nine days. I'm telling you, the top ten is going to look completely different after the combined exercise."

"Different how? Ashford's still going to be top three."

"Ashford's been plateaued for a month. Vance is the one everyone's underestimating."

Roan's jaw tightened by approximately two millimeters. He kept walking.

"Vance doesn't have the family backing. Ashford's father bought him three private tutoring cycles last term alone."

"Family backing doesn't win fights. Vance's Crimson Wake is cleaner than anything I've seen from the top five, and he's not even ranked yet because he keeps pulling from scheduled matches."

"He's pulling on purpose. I've been tracking his match history. Every time he's within striking distance of ranking, he tanks a match and drops back."

The second voice, a woman, younger, a second-year, laughed. "Why would anyone do that?"

"Because ranking is a trap. The moment you're in the top twenty, you get targeted by everyone trying to make a name. Vance knows that. He's been playing low."

Roan turned the corner before they could see his face. Playing low. His reputation was developing a reputation. That was useful for misdirection but dangerous if the narrative got specific. "Playing low" was a fine story as long as people attributed it to lack of ambition rather than strategic calculation. If someone with analytical skills started asking why, what was he hiding, what was he building toward, the story got complicated.

He needed the narrative to stay one step behind his actual position at all times. Any gap wider than that and the pattern became visible to the people who mattered.

The corridor opened into the central quad. Afternoon light came through the high windows at an angle that made the training floors look warmer than they were. A group of senior students was running rope drills along the far wall, synchronized, punishing, drill that made onlookers wince reflexively.

Theon Mercer was standing near the equipment alcove, watching them.

He had the posture of someone who was watching not because he was interested in the drill itself but because he was studying a specific person within it. Roan followed his sightline. The target was a tall senior woman with close-cropped hair executing a perfect skip-roping form, technically flawless but unnecessarily aggressive in her movements, showing off for the audience.

But Theon wasn't watching her form. He was watching her hands. Specifically, the way she transitioned between the rope patterns. His eyes moved a half-beat ahead of her actual movements, which meant he was predicting the pattern rather than reacting to it. That was a trained skill. That wasn't something picked up from general drilling.

Roan filed it. Theon was learning something specific from watching senior students, and it wasn't standard curriculum. He was developing a pattern-reading ability that didn't match his ranking position or his family's training resources.

Someone was teaching him to read fights the way Roan did. That narrowed the field considerably.

- break -

Dinner service was busier. Roan found a seat at the edge of the long tables, positioning himself with sightlines to both the main entrance and the secondary service corridor. Brendt joined him halfway through the meal, sliding a folded piece of paper across the table.

Roan unfolded it under the table. A requisition form, equipment checkout records from the training auxiliary. Someone had been pulling gear from the restricted store, specifically gear used for advanced sensory drills. Weighted vision obscurers. Auditory deprivation chambers. Tools that simulated stress conditions for tactical training.

The checkout signature was smudged but readable. Instructor authorization: Marshburn, T.

Marshburn was personally authorizing sensory training equipment for someone in the cohort. Marshburn, who was drilling the Seventh Cut with unusual intensity. Marshburn, who had rearranged the pairings this afternoon to put Roan against Deckard. That wasn't random. Marshburn was running a dual-track training program, the public curriculum for everyone, and a shadow curriculum for specific students.

Roan thought about the implications. Marshburn had been at the Spire for eleven years. His background check was clean, family of minor functionaries, no Conclave connections without political affiliations. He was exactly the instructor who could be quietly turned by someone with resources, because no one important watched him closely enough to notice the deviation.

Selene Ardent had been at the Spire for two days. She was tracking Theon Mercer and Roan Vance simultaneously. Marshburn was running equipment that improved sensory pattern recognition. Theon's performance had improved at a rate that suggested specialized coaching.

The arithmetic was assembling itself. Not a proof yet, a working hypothesis. Something to test.

He refolded the paper, passed it back to Brendt. "Put this somewhere safe."

"What is it?"

"A theory. I need more data before I know if it's the right one."

Brendt pocketed it without looking. "You're being very calm about this."

"I'm being very focused. Calm is a side effect of having too much information to process emotionally. I'll deal with the emotional processing later." He pushed his tray forward. "Right now I need to find out if Marshburn is running a private coaching ring, or if Selene is running Marshburn, or if there's a third player I haven't identified yet."

"What about Theon?"

"Theon is a piece. If I understand the board correctly, he's a pawn that's been promoted beyond his actual rank by someone with a plan. The plan is what matters. The piece is just the delivery mechanism."

Roan stood. "I'm going to the auxiliary gym. Open training hour. Theon goes there after dinner when he thinks no one important is watching."

"You want to get close to him."

"I want to see if he recognizes what I'm doing. Pattern readers spot each other. If he knows what I am, he'll react. If he doesn't, he'll do something stupid." He collected his tray. "Either way, I'll know whether to treat him as a player or a piece."

Brendt studied his face for a moment. "You're enjoying this."

"Calculating patterns isn't the same as enjoying complexity. I'm optimizing for accuracy, not satisfaction."

"That's not what I said."

Roan paused. Brendt was right, something in his chest was moving faster than it should, a physical sensation that a less analytical person might have called anticipation. He didn't examine it further. That way lay inefficiency.

"I'll report back," he said.

- break -

The auxiliary gym was a converted storage space on the lower level, officially designated for independent study but practically used as an overflow training area when the main halls were occupied. During open training hour it was dim and hot, plus loud with the sounds of self-directed improvement, the slap of palms against leather, the rhythmic grunt of lifters, the sharp whistle of form drills.

Theon was there. Of course he was.

He was working a heavy bag in the far corner, and he was very deliberately not good at it. His form was sloppy in ways that seemed unconscious at first, a dropped elbow here, an overextended reach there, but Roan recognized the architecture of deliberate inefficiency. Theon was making himself look less skilled than he was. He was playing low.

Theon Mercer was playing low.

The realization landed with a dull weight. This changed the readout significantly. If Theon was consciously underperforming in public while improving in private, then the trajectory Roan had mapped, the natural ceiling, the plateau in two months, was a manufactured limitation. Someone had coached him to appear weaker than he was, exactly the way Roan was doing.

Two players on the same board, running the same strategy.

Roan moved to the free weights along the opposite wall. Loaded the bar with a weight that looked impressive but was well within his working range. Began a slow, visible progression through a routine that would read as intermediate to any observer who didn't know what to look for. He kept his peripheral vision on Theon.

Theon's rhythm on the bag shifted. Subtly, his third combination was cleaner than his first two. He'd noticed Roan. And he'd adjusted, unconsciously or deliberately, by making his next sequence slightly worse than the one before. He was matching Roan's performance calibration.

Two people playing low at the same table, each trying to stay one step behind the other.

Roan added weight to the bar. Made the next set visibly harder. Theon responded by accelerating his bag work, then catching himself and slowing down to a pace that looked slightly fatigued. They were in a silent negotiation, each testing the other's commitment to the act.

It was, Roan realized, the most interesting conversation he'd had in weeks.

Theon finished his set. Wiped his face. Walked toward the rack to return his equipment, which meant passing within three feet of Roan's station. He paused.

"Vance."

"Mercer."

Long pause. Theon was calculating. Roan could see it in the micro-expressions, the slight narrowing of the eyes, the tension along his jaw, the way his weight shifted two degrees toward retreat before settling back to neutral. He was running the same assessment Roan was running: Who is this person? What do they know? What is the optimal play?

"Interesting form on those deadlifts," Theon said. "You're compensating through the lumbar."

"I'm aware."

"Must be the injury."

Roan tilted his head. "What injury?"

Theon's mouth twitched. Not a smile. "Never mind."

He walked away. Roan reracked the weight and considered the exchange. Theon had made a probe, testing whether Roan would acknowledge an injury that didn't exist. Roan's deflection had been correct, which meant Theon now knew he was dealing with someone who maintained cover under direct questioning. That was useful information for both of them.

But there was something else. Theon had called it an injury because he expected an injury. That meant he had a template, he was looking for the pattern Roan was running because someone had told him what to look for. Someone who knew about deliberate underperformance as a strategy.

Selene. Marshburn. The third player.

The board had another layer he hadn't seen yet.

Roan finished his routine. Left the gym. The evening corridor was cool after the gym's heat, and the light along the walls was shifting toward the amber of the low-power cycle. He walked back toward his quarters with his mind running the new data.

Theon's pattern matched Roan's. That wasn't coincidence. That was a signature. Someone was training Theon to be a mirror of Roan, same strategy, same timing, same cover. Which meant someone was building a counter-Roan. A second piece on the board that looked like the first but wasn't.

The question was why. And whose design it served.

He reached his door. Stopped. Someone had left a folded note in the small gap between the frame and the hinge.

He opened it with the edge of a credit chip, keeping his fingers outside the fold line. Unfolded it slowly, under the light.

Three lines, handwritten, in precise block capitals:

THE BOARD IS LARGER THAN YOU THINK.

MERCER'S PROMOTION ISN'T THE MOVE.

CHECK YOUR SEVENTH.

No signature.

Roan read it three times. The handwriting was deliberately generic, no markers of origin, no distinctive slant or pressure patterns. The paper was standard issue from the Spire supply closet. The message was a warning dressed as instruction.

"Check your Seventh" could mean the Seventh Cut drill Marshburn had been running. Could mean the seventh ranked student. Could mean something else entirely that the writer assumed Roan would decode.

Or it could mean the Seventh inversion capacity. The seventh day. The seventh rank.

Someone had been watching long enough to know about the Seventh Cut focus. Someone was tracking Marshburn's dual-track program. Someone was close enough to leave a note at his door without being seen, which meant access to the residential corridor, which meant either staff clearance or a rank high enough to walk freely.

Theon was a piece. Selene was a player. Marshburn was a tool.

Who was moving the pieces?

Roan folded the note, tucked it into his waistband, and entered his quarters. The room was exactly as he'd left it, bed made with military corners, desk clear, training kit in its designated corner. Nothing out of place. No sign of entry.

He sat on the bed and stared at the wall for four minutes, running the board.

The note writer knew his strategy. Knew Theon's strategy. Knew about the Seventh Cut focus. Either they were inside the operation, Marshburn's program, Selene's tracking, or they were a third party watching both and choosing to warn him rather than exploit him.

Warning him suggested alignment. Alignment suggested a player with goals that conflicted with whoever was promoting Theon.

A player who wanted Roan to succeed.

The board was larger than he thought. That was almost certainly true. The question was whether the new player was an ally, a second layer of deception, or something he hadn't modeled at all yet.

He lay back on the bed. Stared at the ceiling. Let the silence settle around the problem.

Tomorrow he would spar with Deckard and read the injury pattern. Tomorrow he would watch Theon in the morning drills and see whether the note had changed his behavior. Tomorrow Marshburn would run the Seventh Cut again, and Roan would watch for the seventh thing, the seventh student, the seventh detail, the seventh deviation.

But tonight, the board was larger than he'd thought, and he was one move behind whoever had left the note.

He closed his eyes. He slept without dreams, cognitive processes running below the surface, calculating through the dark.

Somewhere in the Spire, someone was watching.

They were watching back.
