Chapter 121: The Third Day

The roar hit the arena walls and came back three times louder.

Seven thousand seats, every one occupied. The Greycross Municipal Tournament Grounds had been expanded twice since the last Ashford-hosted event, and still people clustered three-deep along the railings, pressed into the merchant stalls ringing the outer concourse, perched on rooftops with borrowed spyglasses. The afternoon sun threw long shadows across the sand-coloured floor, but the crowd's heat made it feel like noon in a furnace.

From the competitor's pavilion stairs, Roan watched Cyrus Ashford descend with the bearing of a man who had already decided how the afternoon would end. The Ashford Spire rested across his shoulders, the haft resting against his neck, tip pointed toward the sky with the casual arrogance of a flag claiming territory. His dark coat had been replaced by the close-fitted combat silks of House Ashford, black and silver, with silver stitching that caught the light like scales.

Behind him, two attendants carried a lacquered case.

Roan counted the steps automatically. Forty-seven. Cyrus was pacing himself, making the descent last. Letting the crowd get hungry. Letting the silence build. Roan had known for three years that Cyrus Ashford understood optics better than most soldiers understood their own heartbeat.

He spotted Brendt in the crowd, three rows back from the barrier. Brendt's jaw was tight, his hands gripping the railing with white knuckles. Lyra stood beside him, her eyes fixed on the arena floor.

Cyrus reached the sand. The crowd noise crested, then fell, not to silence, but to that particular hush that follows a held breath. The attendants opened the lacquered case. Inside, nested in black velvet, lay a secondary weapon. A shorter spear, no longer than a longsword, with a flared butt and a tip designed for closer work.

Roan recognized the configuration. The Ashford Shortspire. A backup weapon for when the primary lance lost range advantage.

Cyrus lifted it from the case, tested the balance, set the butt against the sand like a man planting a flag.

Then he turned toward the pavilion stairs and waited.

The second roar hit the arena, louder this time, because this time it was genuine.

Roan Vance descended the stairs with none of the ceremony Cyrus had employed. No attendants. No lacquered case. No grand pacing. He walked the steps at an even clip, hands loose at his sides, and if anyone had bothered to measure his stride length against Cyrus's, they'd have found Roan's shorter and more efficient. Less energy wasted. Numbers were harder to bribe than people.

Sidewound hung sheathed at his left hip. He'd oiled the blade this morning, the edge gleamed like a sliver of mercury under the arena lights. His combat silks were grey and black, Vance colours, and someone in the crowd had started a chant that spread like wildfire: *Vance. Vance. Vance.*

Cyrus's smile didn't waver. If anything, it widened.

Roan reached the sand. The crowd had found a rhythm now, a call-and-response that shook dust from the upper rows. *Vance. Ashford. Vance. Ashford.*

Cyrus extended the Shortspire toward Roan in a formal challenge-gesture. "You came."

"Didn't I always?" Roan's voice carried across the arena without effort. Quiet, but clear. "You seemed concerned I wouldn't."

"I was." Cyrus set the Shortspire butt-first into the sand, leaned on it like a man at a garden party. "The preliminary rounds were impressive. That match against the Vellmark girl, particularly creative. But preliminary rounds don't test a man. They test his tolerance for mediocrity."

Roan didn't respond to that. His eyes moved to the lacquered case, then back to Cyrus.

"You brought two."

"Three," Cyrus corrected. The Ashford Spire slid from his shoulders into his grip. "You seem to be missing that number."

"Two hands."

"Two hands." Cyrus laughed, a clean, pleasant sound that the crowd echoed back at him. "And the arrogance to match. Tell me, Vance, when did you last sleep a full night?"

The crowd's energy shifted. Roan caught the edge of it: a ripple of uncertainty. Cyrus was probing something.

Roan tilted his head. A fractional movement. "Why? You planning to let me?"

"Planning." Cyrus spun the Ashford Spire once, butt-planted it beside the Shortspire. "No. But I am curious. You've been running hot for three weeks. Burning bright. So bright that either produces results or produces collapse. Which are you today?"

Three weeks. That was the window between the Global Tournament semis and now. Three weeks of training past midnight, swallowing catalysts, running cycles until his Vitalis readings bounced like a fever chart. The arithmetic didn't add up—not for a Hardened-tier cultivator. Something was sustaining him that shouldn't exist.

Cyrus knew. Or suspected. And he was building the question into the arena like a trap.

Roan didn't answer immediately. He walked the circle's edge, studying the sand, the weapon placements, the distance between himself and Cyrus. When he spoke again, his voice had dropped half a register.

"You want to know if I'm running on fumes."

"I want to know if you're running at all." Cyrus's smile sharpened. "Or if you're just falling with style."

The arena went quiet.

Roan had stopped at the circle's edge, face tilted toward the sun like a man taking a reading. The light caught his eyes, grey and flat.

Then Roan smiled.

It wasn't a pleasant expression.

"Alright," he said. The Referee raised his hand. The crowd noise died like a blade.

"Match rules: first blood or yield. Weapon lethality: tournament standard. Healing mages on standby." The Referee's voice echoed across the arena. "No killing intent registered. Any questions?"

Cyrus shook his head. His eyes never left Roan.

Roan raised his hand. "One."

The Referee paused. "Vance?"

"The preliminary rounds. My match against Kira Vell." Roan's voice carried flat and clean. "The girl I allegedly 'creatively' defeated. Where is she now?"

The Referee's face did something complicated. In the stands, a ripple of conversation spread like a stone dropped in water.

Cyrus's smile didn't change. "Kira Vell withdrew from the tournament citing illness. She's recovering at her family's estate."

"Withdrew." Roan nodded slowly. "After I ruptured her spleen in the third exchange. Yes. Recovering."

"Vance." The Referee's voice carried a warning edge. "The medical records are sealed under—"

Roan said nothing for half a breath. He hadn't known the girl's age before the match. That was a gap in his intelligence, and he'd already closed it. The wound on her abdomen was severe, but not irreversible—House Ashford's archives contained regenerative draughts that could clear the scar in a month. The question was whether they'd pay that cost.

"Medical records." Roan's head tilted. "She's fourteen years old. She'll carry a scar for months unless House Ashford provides the top-tier regenerative draught it promised. Because a Tempered-tier competitor entered a Hardened bracket and no one checked the weight classes." He paused. "But that's not the question I asked."

Cyrus's smile thinned by a quarter of a millimetre.

"House Ashford sponsored her admission to this bracket," Roan continued. "House Ashford provided her family with a healing draught after the match, under the table, of course. And House Ashford is now positioning itself as the voice of tournament safety." He paused. "That's the question I asked."

The arena had gone completely silent.

Cyrus lowered the Ashford Spire until the butt struck sand. The sound rang across the crowd like a bell.

"You're accusing House Ashford of—"

"I'm stating facts." Roan's voice didn't rise. Didn't need to. "If you want to call them accusations, that's your business. But you asked me a question about whether I slept well, and I wanted you to know I had plenty of time to think about other things while I was lying awake."

The silence stretched like a wire. In the stands, Brendt's hands had come off the railing. He was gripping the barrier with white knuckles now, his jaw tight. Lyra's face had gone pale. Her eyes were locked on Roan.

Cyrus set the Ashford Spire across his shoulders again. When he spoke, his voice had lost its garden-party warmth.

"You're making accusations in the arena."

"I'm making observations." Roan's hand dropped to Sidewound's grip. "There's a difference. But you seem to prefer accusations to observations, so I'll be happy to accommodate."

"Such as?"

"Such as asking whether you brought three weapons because you're afraid of what happens when a Hardened-tier opponent gets inside your reach." Roan's eyes tracked to the Shortspire planted in the sand. "Such as asking why House Ashford's spymaster was in Greycross three weeks ago, two days before a warehouse fire destroyed the Vance family estate's remaining records." Another pause. "Such as asking whether anyone has checked the Vitalis readings on the healing draughts being distributed to injured competitors."

The Referee's face had gone grey.

Cyrus's smile hadn't wavered, but something behind it had shifted. A calculation. A reassessment.

"You have an interesting theory," Cyrus said. "I look forward to disproving it."

Roan drew Sidewound.

The blade caught the afternoon light and threw it across the sand like a streak of frozen lightning.

"So do I," he said.

The Referee dropped his hand.

Cyrus moved.

The Ashford Spire came down from his shoulder in a sweeping arc that covered the distance between them in a single breath, eight feet of haft, a lance-tip moving at a speed that made the air scream. Against a normal Hardened competitor, it would've been an immediate answer. A knockout blow in the first exchange.

Roan wasn't normal.

He flowed inside the arc.

Sidewound came up, not to block, but to guide. The lance-tip screamed along the blade's length, metal shrieking against metal, and Roan's off-hand caught the haft an inch behind the spear-head. His grip twisted. The Ashford Spire wrenched sideways, and for one frozen instant, the weapon's length became a liability instead of an advantage.

Cyrus released and pivoted in the same motion. The Shortspire came up from the sand, butt-first, slamming toward Roan's exposed ribs.

Roan twisted. The butt glanced off his floating ribs, a glancing impact, but enough to jar his stance. The Codex Shard caught the impact and converted the force into something usable, but the buffer dipped—one more hit like that and he'd feel the thinning.

He rolled with it, releasing the haft, Sidewound carving a horizontal cut that forced Cyrus back a half-step.

They separated. Five feet of sand between them. The crowd noise had become a single sustained note, thousands of voices compressed into a wall of sound that shook the arena's bones.

Cyrus spun the Shortspire once, settled into a low stance. His eyes had gone flat. Business-flat. No more garden party.

"You've been training with Drogan Hale."

"Officially?" Roan's voice came out even. No gasp, no catch. His ribs ached where the Shortspire had landed, but the pain was already fading—not fast enough, but enough. "No. He's a Warden. I'm a student. We have no contact outside of official channels."

"Unofficially?"

"Unofficially, I don't discuss my training methodology in the arena." Roan's eyes tracked the distance between them, measuring Cyrus's stance, the angle of the Shortspire, the reach advantage. "But I'll tell you something freely."

Cyrus's jaw tightened.

"You're telegraphing your second strike. Every time." Roan's voice dropped half a register. "The Shortspire is faster, but you overcompensate on the recovery. You're used to opponents who don't know how to close. I'm not going to make that mistake."

Cyrus moved.

The Shortspire came in fast, a blur of silver and black, butt-end leading this time, a stabbing motion aimed at Roan's solar plexus. Faster than the first exchange. Significantly faster. A technique designed to exploit exactly the inside-close that Roan had used against the Ashford Spire.

Roan back-stepped. The butt passed an inch from his sternum. He pivoted, Sidewound coming around in a low arc, and stopped three inches from Cyrus's hamstring.

The Ashford Spire blocked. Not the Shortspire. The Ashford Spire had come around in a reverse grip, haft horizontal, catching Sidewound's cutting edge against four feet of lacquered wood. The impact drove Roan back another step.

Cyrus pressed.

The Shortspire came in again, low this time, sweeping toward Roan's knee. He dropped, twisted, the sweep passing under his centre of gravity. Sidewound flicked out, a jab rather than a cut, tip seeking Cyrus's wrist.

Cyrus twisted. The jab grazed his sleeve, a scratch of fabric, nothing more. He flowed backward, creating distance, and for three heartbeats the two men circled each other in the sand.

The crowd had gone quiet again. The silence stretched like a wire.

Cyrus settled into his stance again. The Shortspire held low, the Ashford Spire balanced across his shoulders. Two weapons. Two metres of reach advantage even without the lance-length.

"You're faster than I expected," Cyrus said. His voice had gone flat again, but this time it carried an edge of something else. Something that might've been respect. "The preliminary rounds didn't show this."

"Deliberate."

"You've been hiding your actual capability."

"Playing low." Roan's eyes didn't leave Cyrus's hands. "There's a difference. Hiding suggests shame. Playing low suggests strategy."

Cyrus's smile thinned. "And you're sure this is a strategy you can sustain?"

Roan tilted his head. A fractional motion.

Then he moved. The Codex Shard pulsed as he closed the distance—the buffer was softer now, but still enough. He had maybe three more exchanges before he'd need to end it.

Sidewound came in low and fast, a cut aimed at Cyrus's forward knee. Cyrus pivoted, the Ashford Spire sweeping down to intercept, and Roan was already inside the arc before the interception arrived. His shoulder hit Cyrus's chest. Not a strike—a pressure test. A measuring contact.

Cyrus grunted. The Shortspire came up, butt-end driving toward Roan's spine.

Roan twisted. The butt glanced off his lat muscle, a grinding impact that would have dropped a lesser man. He absorbed it, drove forward, and Sidewound carved an upward cut that forced Cyrus's guard high.

The Ashford Spire chopped down. Roan rolled under it, came up on one knee, and his free hand drove three knuckles into Cyrus's floating rib.

Cyrus staggered. His breath went out in a single explosive burst. The Shortspire swung wild. Roan ducked it, felt the wind of its passage ruffle his hair, and Sidewound came up in a rising cut that opened a six-inch slash across Cyrus's forearm.

The crowd screamed.

Cyrus retreated three steps, blood sheeting down his sleeve. His eyes had gone wide, not with pain, but with something rawer. Something closer to fear.

Roan rose from his crouch. His left arm hung at an awkward angle, something torn in the shoulder, the rotator cuff from the Shortspire's earlier impact, but Sidewound stayed steady in his grip.

"First blood," Roan said.

Cyrus straightened his bleeding arm and smiled. No. Not smiled. Grinned.

"First blood," Cyrus repeated. His voice cut through the crowd noise like a blade. "Not first advantage. Not first strike. Just first blood." He rolled his shoulder, worked the arm, watched the blood drip onto the sand. "You've been running hot for three weeks, Vance. Burning bright. And you just spent half your reserve closing distance on a Hardened-tier opponent."

Roan's expression didn't change.

"You've got nothing left," Cyrus said. "And you know it."

The second exchange lasted four minutes.

Roan learned three things.

First: Cyrus Ashford was faster at range than any Hardened-tier competitor the archives recorded. The Ashford Spire moved like a serpent with a grudge, each thrust and sweep calibrated to keep Roan at exactly the distance where Sidewound couldn't close without eating a lance-tip.

Second: the Shortspire was designed to counter exactly the inside-close Roan had used in the first exchange. Its balance was wrong for a normal spear, too much weight in the butt, too much flex in the shaft, but against a single weapon opponent trying to get under a longer reach, those design choices became a killing measure. Every time Roan pressed inside the Spire's arc, the Shortspire answered with a stabbing precision that forced him back.

Third: Cyrus wasn't fighting to win.

He was fighting to exhaust.

The realization crystallized in Roan's mind like ice forming on still water. Cyrus wasn't pressing advantages. Wasn't taking risks. Every exchange was designed to cost Roan energy—physical and mental—plus whatever else kept a Hardened-tier cultivator operating past the point where the math should have stopped working.

By the end of the fourth minute, Roan's legs burned. His shoulder had gone from painful to numb. Three separate cuts decorated his arms, his ribs, his left thigh—a catalogue of shallow wounds that would have healed cleanly in a day but accumulated into a tide of small damage that kept his Inversion cycling.

The crowd had stopped screaming. They were on their feet, but silent now, sweating, breathless, watching two men circle each other on a floor of sand that had turned dark with mixed blood.

Cyrus's left arm hung at his side. The cut had been cleaned and clotted by the arena's ambient Vitalis field, a tournament courtesy, but the muscle underneath had torn in a way that wouldn't fully heal until the match ended. He'd lost range on his forward grip. The Ashford Spire moved a fraction slower than it had at the start.

Roan had lost more. But he'd lost it strategically.

The shape was almost finished settling.

Cyrus circled left, the Ashford Spire held in a mid-guard. His breathing had slowed—not controlled, but forced into an approximation of calm by sheer will. The grin had faded. Something colder sat behind his eyes now.

"You know what I find interesting?"

Roan circled with him. Sidewound held low, tip angled toward Cyrus's knee. "Tell me."

"That you're still standing." Cyrus's voice carried across the sand like a thrown stone. "You've taken more cumulative damage in the last four minutes than most Hardened competitors absorb in a full tournament bracket. Your shoulder is functional at maybe sixty percent. You've bled from nine separate points." He paused. "And you're still circling. Still calculating. I can see it in your eyes."

Roan's expression didn't change.

"What's sustaining you, Vance?" Cyrus's voice dropped. "That's the question. Not the tournament. Not the rivalry. Not even the accusations you threw at me at the start." He tilted his head. "What resource is a Hardened-tier Vance burning that shouldn't exist?"

The arena had gone silent.

In the stands, Brendt had stopped breathing. Lyra's hands gripped the barrier with white-knuckled intensity.

Roan smiled.

It wasn't a pleasant expression.

"Math doesn't lie," he said.

Then he moved. The Codex Shard caught the next exchange hard—the buffer narrowed to a thread. He had one more, maybe two, before the system caught up with itself.

Sidewound came in high, a feint, not a cut, and when Cyrus brought the Ashford Spire up to intercept, Roan was already inside the arc, sliding under the haft on his back foot, shoulder driving into Cyrus's midsection with every ounce of force his exhausted legs could produce.

Cyrus buckled.

Roan drove forward, carrying the man three steps before they hit the ground together. Cyrus twisted, the Shortspire coming up in a desperate stab. Roan caught the wrist, twisted, felt something grind in his own torn shoulder as pain flared white-hot through his vision.

The Shortspire clattered to the sand.

Cyrus reached for it with his bleeding arm. Roan's boot came down on the haft, pinning it, and Sidewound's tip settled against Cyrus's throat with an accuracy that didn't require thought. Just calculation. Just math.

The arena went silent.

Cyrus lay on his back in the sand, blood streaming from his arm, the Ashford Spire three feet away. Roan's boot pinned the Shortspire. Sidewound rested against his Adam's apple with a pressure that promised the next breath would be the last if the next breath was a word.

"First blood," Roan said. "First yield. First question."

Cyrus's eyes, wide, flat, calculating even now, tracked up to meet Roan's.

"Who," Roan said softly, "is Ines Carrow?"

Cyrus flinched.

The reaction lasted less than a quarter-second. A micro-expression—a tightening around the eyes, a fractional tension in the jaw. But Roan had been watching for it for three weeks. Had built three weeks of playing low and training past midnight and swallowing catalysts around the edges of a question that didn't have an answer.

Until now.

"She's watching," Roan said. "Up in the private boxes. Third row from the top, fifth from the left. She was watching before the match started. She watched every exchange." He pressed Sidewound's edge a millimetre deeper. "And when you started fighting to exhaust instead of fighting to win, her face did something very interesting."

The crowd couldn't hear the words. But they could see Cyrus Ashford—Hardened-tier, Ashford heir, tournament favourite—lying pinned in the sand with a blade at his throat, and they could see Roan's lips moving, shaping questions that made the boy under the blade go pale.

Cyrus swallowed. The movement pushed his throat against Sidewound's edge.

"You're making a mistake," he managed.

"Probably." Roan's voice stayed soft. "But you asked what was sustaining me. You asked if it was a resource that shouldn't exist." He paused. "It's not a resource. It's a debt. And debts have a way of making men reckless."

Sidewound lifted from Cyrus's throat.

The arena exploded. The referee was running across the sand, his hand raised, shouting something about rules and yields and medical attention. Roan stepped back, released the Shortspire, let the arena's ambient Vitalis field wash over him with its cheap healing grace. His shoulder screamed. His ribs throbbed. His legs trembled with exhaustion that came from running a body past every marker the manufacturer recommended.

Cyrus rose slowly. The Ashford Spire had been retrieved by an attendant. His arm hung at his side, professionally immobile, the blood flow stopped but the underlying damage left for proper healers to address later.

He didn't reach for the Shortspire. He didn't reach for anything.

Instead, he looked up at the private boxes, third row from the top, fifth from the left, and his face did something that Roan filed away with the same clinical precision he'd used to file away every other observation of the past three weeks.

Cyrus smiled.

It wasn't a pleasant expression. Not a garden-party smile. Something colder, harder, with an edge that cut.

"You've won the match," Cyrus said. His voice carried across the arena despite the crowd noise. "Congratulations, Vance. I'm sure it feels like victory."

Roan didn't respond. He was watching the private boxes. Third row from the top. Fifth from the left.

The box was empty.

"She's gone," Cyrus said. "You'll note the timing. She left before your last exchange. Before you pinned me. Before any of this became certain." He rolled his shoulder, the damaged one, and smiled again. "She's been gone for four minutes, Vance. Whatever you think you learned from that question, she already knew you were going to ask it."

The arena noise had shifted again. The crowd had found a rhythm, a chant building from the Greycross section and spreading through the stands like a brushfire. *Vance. Vance. Vance.*

Roan looked down at Cyrus. The man was still smiling. Still talking. Still performing even now, even with blood drying on his sleeve and the taste of a controlled humiliation coating his tongue.

"She's gone," Cyrus repeated. "And you'll never find her before she finds you."

Roan considered this.

Then he sheathed Sidewound, turned his back on the Ashford heir, and walked toward the pavilion stairs.

The crowd chanted his name. Brendt's jaw had finally unclenched. His hands were still white-knuckled on the railing. Lyra watched Roan climb the stairs with an expression he couldn't read from this distance.

He didn't try.

The pavilion doors cut off the arena noise, and for three heartbeats Roan stood in the silence with his hand still on Sidewound's grip and his lungs burning with exhaustion and his mind already three moves ahead of his body.

Ines Carrow.

House Carrow.

The debt.

The tally.

The question.

The medical station door opened. Roan walked through.

Behind him, in the private boxes, third row from the top, fifth from the left, empty now, the curtains drawn, the seat cooling from a weight that had departed four minutes before the match ended. A single card lay on the velvet cushion.

White. Unsigned.

And on its face, in handwriting that Roan wouldn't recognize but would never forget:

*You're getting warmer. Don't let it burn you.*

The card sat in the empty box for seven more minutes before an attendant found it and reported it as a mystery.
