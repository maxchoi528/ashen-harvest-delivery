Chapter 103: Drogan's Debt

Drogan's office smelled of old leather and older regret.

Roan had been here before, enough times to map the room by scent alone: the polish on the Warden's Iron Hand, the dust on the file cabinets that hadn't been opened in years, the faint metallic tang of the confiscated enhancement materials locked behind the cabinet doors. But tonight, something was different.

The bottle on the desk.

Drogan sat behind the slab of treated oak, his Iron Hand resting on a file he had not opened, his flesh hand wrapped around a glass that was half-empty. He was not in uniform. No insignia, no rank markers, just a civilian shirt with the sleeves rolled up, revealing the scarred forearms of a man who had spent his life in combat.

"You're late," Drogan said.

"You said 2100. It's 2104."

"I said 2100." Drogan's voice was rougher than usual. "Four minutes is late."

Roan closed the door behind him and stood with his back to it. He had seen Drogan in many states: angry, calculating, exhausted, even, once, afraid. He had never seen him drunk. Drogan was not drunk now. But he was working on it.

"Sit."

Roan sat in the chair across from the desk—the same chair he always sat in, the crack from decades of use still traced through the wood.

Drogan looked at him for a long moment. The kind of look that preceded something that could not be taken back.

"I've been carrying something for six years," Drogan said. "I've been waiting for the right time to set it down."

"You could have set it down before tonight."

"I could have. I didn't." Drogan took a long drink. Set the glass down. His hand stayed around it. "Your father was my brother."

Roan's expression didn't change. He had known this, in the way that he had known many things about his father's life: through fragments, inferences, the careful assembly of data from unreliable sources. But hearing it spoken aloud was different.

"Aldric Vance saved my life twice," Drogan continued. His eyes were fixed on something Roan could not see, a point in the middle distance where memory and regret overlapped. "First time was in the Hollow Reaches. We were on a survey team, mapping a new Rift tier that had opened near the northern ridge. The terrain collapsed beneath us. I fell into a pocket of spore gas that should have killed me in under a minute. Aldric carried me two kilometers to the extraction point with a broken leg and a punctured lung."

He paused. Drank again.

"Second time was in a Conclave safe house, three years later. Someone had sold our location. Six assassins, all Forged-tier, waiting in the basement. Aldric walked in first, saw the trap, and didn't stop walking. He took three of them down before I even cleared my weapon. The other three ran. They were smart."

Roan said nothing. He had heard versions of these stories before, from Brendt, from the fragments in his father's notes. But never from Drogan. Never in the Warden's own voice.

"We called ourselves the Black Squad," Drogan said. "Me, Aldric, and a man named Kaelen. We were the Conclave's solution to problems that couldn't be solved through official channels. Assassinations, extractions, information retrieval. The work that kept the world running while the politicians pretended it didn't exist."

"Kaelen," Roan repeated. The name was familiar. He had seen it in his father's notes, referenced in the same passages that mentioned the Founding Seven.

"Kaelen the Unbroken. Third member of the Black Squad. Best combatant I've ever seen, and I've seen Aldric in full Inversion surge." Drogan's jaw tightened. "He disappeared after your father died. I don't know if he's alive."

The room went quiet. The ventilation system hummed. Somewhere in the corridor outside, footsteps passed and faded.

"And the six names," Roan said. "The execution order. You were there."

Drogan's hand tightened on the glass. "I was not in the room. I was ordered to attend. By the time I arrived, Aldric was on the ground and the six had already left. Six chairs around a table. Six glasses. Six signatures."

"Ordered."

"I was late because I stopped to help a junior officer who had been injured during a training exercise." Drogan's voice was flat. "I thought I had time. I thought the formalities would take longer. I was wrong."

The room felt colder.

"I've spent six years trying to remember how to have a brother," Drogan said. His voice was barely audible. "Aldric was the closest thing I had. And I was late."

Roan sat with the silence. He did not offer comfort. He did not offer absolution. Those were not his to give.

"Kaelen," he said. "Where is he?"

"Suthmere. The Unbroken Peak. That's where he went after Aldric died." Drogan's eyes finally focused on Roan. "If he's still alive, he's there. And if he's there, he knows more about the Seven than I do."

The name settled into place. Roan had already known, before tonight, that his path would eventually lead beyond the Spire. Now it had a direction.

"I'm going to Suthmere."

"I know."

"You could stop me."

"I could." Drogan set the glass down. It was empty now. "I'm not going to."

"Why?"

The Warden met his eyes. For a moment, the mask slipped, and Roan saw the man beneath the title. The survivor beneath the Warden.

"Because someone needs to survive what's at the top of that mountain," Drogan said. "And I'm too old to be the one who tries."

The silence returned. Roan stood.

"Thank you," he said.

It was not gratitude. It was acknowledgment. The recognition that Drogan had given him something that had cost more than the Warden would ever admit.

Drogan nodded once. That was all.

Roan left the office. The corridor was dark. The lights were dim. He walked through the empty halls of the Black Spire with the weight of his father's ghost pressing against his spine, and the name of Kaelen the Unbroken burning behind his eyes.
