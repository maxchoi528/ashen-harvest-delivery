Chapter 11: Wraithmilk Tincture

The grey light of predawn filtered through the grimy window, catching dust motes as if even the air in Ironholm was slower than everywhere else. Three weeks into the Spire routine. Three days since his last dose of Bloodscourge. Three days since the pendant had stopped being a pendant and started being something else.

Roan Vance lay on his narrow bed in the lodging house on Tanners Row, flat on his back, eyes open. He hadn't slept through a single night since the ritual. Not a single full cycle. Sleep was submission, and he had learned in Greycross that submission was a tax he couldn't afford.

The Codex Shard sat fused to the seventh vertebra of his spine. He knew this because he had felt it settle there like a second heartbeat, quieter than the first but steady. The sensation wasn't painful. That was the problem.

Something that powerful should hurt.

He ran the arithmetic on the morning of the Spire's third week the way he had been running it every day since the ritual. Capacity: two uses left today. Tomorrow morning, three again. The system reset at midnight, Aldric's ghost had confirmed that much, though confirmation from a dead man's echo felt less like comfort and more like an audit.

Sidewound absorption rate: one hundred percent at current tier. Every pain the body sustained was processed and converted. More than converted, the math worked out to a net gain. His Vitalis readout yesterday had been one hundred and twelve. This morning it stood at one hundred and nineteen.

One hundred and nineteen. Against a class average of perhaps eighty for first-years who hadn't ingested anything stronger than basic Awakening Tonic. He had gained thirty-nine points in two days without training. Without anything except sitting with his back against the cold wall of his room, running the columns.

The first dose had been a gamble on his biology. Today's target was a gamble on something else entirely.

[Codex Shard: 2/3 daily capacity. — A.V.]

"You could be less smug about it," Roan said to the empty room.

The silence that followed had the texture of something that wasn't silence. The Codex Shard didn't respond. It never did unless he was actively absorbing damage, and right now the most dangerous object in the room was the draft from the window.

He sat up. Swung his legs over the side of the bed. The floorboards were cold. Ironholm was always cold unless you burned something expensive, and the coldness of late autumn in a city built on a river that smelled of rust and old blood. Greycross had been quieter. Greyer in a different way. This city ate sound and replaced it with industry.

Today's schedule:

Morning lecture, The Discipline and Its History. Professor Callen, who talked about Vitalis pathways as if describing plumbing. Boring. Useful only if you cared about theory, and Roan didn't have the luxury of caring about theory. Theory was for students who had mothers not working double shifts at a textile mill and sisters not waiting in a rented room for you to somehow make tuition appear.

Midday: Physical conditioning, the Spire's standard second-session. Mandatory for all first-years. The instructors would run them through forms, basic stances, nothing exotic. Observe. Standard evaluation. He had seen the records. The instructors had no reason to pay special attention to Roan Vance, registered Initiate, tier one reading of eighty-two on intake.

Eighty-two. Even that number was a lie. He had held back during intake assessment. Deliberately. No point in drawing attention from a kid with no money and no family name that meant anything anymore. The numbers held: be mediocre enough to be ignored, fast enough to be lethal when it mattered.

Afternoon: free. The Spire gave students afternoons to study, train privately, or work off-campus. This was the time slot he would use to acquire the next reagent. Wraithmilk Tincture. A failed mental stimulant that burned out the pathways it was supposed to strengthen. The Spire's pharmacopoeia listed it as a dead end. The Inversion saw it as fuel.

Old Pell's contact had given him the address for the first dose—a vendor in the Ironveil Market who counted credits and asked no questions. That had been the Bloodscourge. Today, a different contact. Varro. A warehouse in the Smelt quarter. The kind of place where the Spire's oversight was measured in absences.

Right.

He stood. Dressed. The same grey training clothes he had worn for three days straight because laundry cost credits he couldn't justify spending on appearance. His boots were cracked at the toe. He didn't care about the boots.

He cared about the fact that his body felt different. Not dramatically, not yet, but the difference was there if he paid attention, and Roan paid attention to everything. His reaction time in the morning had improved by a measurable fraction. His muscle recovery between conditioning sessions was faster. His appetite had tripled, which was inconvenient but logical: the system was demanding more input to process the output it was generating.

This was the critical phase. Day one to day seven after activation were when the body adapted to the Codex Shard's presence or rejected it. Aldric's notes had been unambiguous on this point. Most initiates who tried to force an Inversion without preparation either burned out or killed themselves. Roan had not forced anything. The pendant had been waiting for him since he was eleven, worn against his skin for six years, and the moment he had absorbed real side effects from the Bloodscourge, the Shard had recognized him.

Recognized him. As if it had been waiting.

His father had left him a weapon disguised as a piece of cheap jewelry and a set of instructions written in a dead man's handwriting. Typical.

Roan pulled on his jacket. It fit him now. Bought from a second-hand stall near the docks for eleven credits. It was too warm for the season but the mornings were cold and he had learned years ago that comfort was a luxury you purchased only after security was guaranteed.

He checked the room. Nothing had been moved. The window latch was intact. He had scratched a mark into the frame where it met the wall, a small thing, meaningless to anyone else, invaluable to him. Someone had opened that window in the night at some point during the past week and he hadn't known until he had checked this morning. Now he knew. Now he would know if it happened again.

Little things. The tally was built from little things.

He locked the door behind him, descended the narrow stairwell, and stepped out onto Tanners Row.

The street was narrow and ran downhill toward the river. The buildings on either side were four stories of timber and brick, most of them leaning slightly inward as if conspiring to block out whatever the sky was trying to do above Ironholm. The smell hit him immediately: tannery runoff, cheap cooking oil, coal smoke from the forges that ran day and night in the industrial quarter to the east. The people who lived on Tanners Row were the people the Spire produced but never claimed. Laborers. Junior craftsmen. A few initiates who couldn't afford better accommodations.

No one looked at Roan twice. That was correct. That was the math holding.

He walked. Twenty minutes to the Spire's main campus, an additional ten to the lecture hall if he cut through the Ironveil Market. He cut through it. The market was narrow lanes between canvas stalls and stacked crates, the air thick with the sound of haggling in three languages. The vendors were already setting up. Roasted chestnuts. Dried fish. Cheap Vitalis tablets that claimed to boost intake efficiency but did nothing at all.

One of those stalls was where he had bought the first Bloodscourge. The vendor hadn't asked what he intended to use it for. The vendor had counted his credits and handed over a small vial of something that looked like old wine and smelled like a wound. Roan had drunk it in an alley behind the market, felt his veins catch fire long enough for the Codex Shard to wake for the first time.

[Sidewound absorbed.]
[Conversion successful: +41 Vitalis.]
[Capacity: 2/3.]

He still had the empty vial. Not because it had any further use. One dose, one Inversion. But because keeping it reminded him of the balance. Forty-one points from a single swallow. If he could maintain a supply of Bloodscourge, the compounding rate would be significant.

But Bloodscourge was a body reagent. The Wraithmilk Tincture was a mind reagent. If the Inversion could process mental damage as cleanly as physical damage, he would have access to an entirely different layer of growth.

He reached the Spire's outer gate at eight-fourteen. Early, but not suspiciously so. The gate was a functional thing, iron-banded oak, wide enough for a wagon. Two Sentinels flanked it, not the junior kind that patrolled campus but actual graduates employed by the Institute, men who looked like they had stopped needing to try hard years ago. They checked student IDs without making eye contact. Routine.

Roan showed his ID. The Sentinel glanced at it, glanced at him, waved him through.

No reaction. Eighty-two on intake. Unremarkable. The Codex Shard sat at his spine and broadcast nothing, and the body's Vitalis reading was close enough to what it had been before the ritual that the Spire's internal sensors registered him as the same underperforming student he had been three weeks ago.

This, too, was correct.

The campus was larger than the street-level impression suggested. The Spire's central tower rose above the lower buildings like a blackened tooth against the grey sky, but around it lay training courts, lecture halls, a dormitory complex that housed two thousand students, and, at the rear, the conditioning grounds. The grounds were gravel and packed earth, bordered by high walls to contain the sound of impact.

Roan had been here every day for three weeks. He knew the schedule. He knew the instructors. He knew which students were serious and which were playing at it. The latter outnumbered the former about six to one, and the serious ones were almost uniformly from families with resources. You could spot them by their gear, their confidence at the start line, the way they didn't flinch when the instructors barked commands.

He wasn't serious-looking. He had done the arithmetic on that, too.

He walked to the conditioning grounds and found a spot near the back of the morning group. Roughly eighty students stood in rows, arranged by intake cohort. Roan was in cohort nine, late summer intake, the "catch-up" batch that the Spire accepted to fill capacity. Most of them had come from regional academies or, like Roan, from nothing at all. They looked tired. They looked hungry in the way that came from not sleeping enough rather than not eating enough.

A few of them glanced at him. He gave them nothing back. Neutral expression. Neutral posture. He was someone who disappeared in a crowd and that was precisely the point.

The instructor for this session was a woman named Kendra. Hard-faced, mid-forties, hands that looked like they had broken things and moved on. She didn't introduce herself. She reviewed the formation with the dispassionate efficiency of someone who had reviewed formations eight thousand times.

"Basic stance. Weight centered. Arms loose."

The group shuffled into position. Roan mirrored the others, feet shoulder-width, knees bent slightly, arms at their sides. The posture was elementary. He had held it a hundred times in Greycross, practicing alone in the yard behind their house while his father's notes lay open on the bench.

Holding a stance wasn't difficult. What was difficult was holding it while your legs burned and your lungs worked and the instructor walked the line looking for weakness. The stance wasn't about the form. It was about the duration, and what duration revealed.

Roan's legs didn't burn.

That was new. Three weeks ago, the same stance would have produced standard lactic acid buildup by the four-minute mark. Now, at six minutes, his legs felt nothing more than mild warmth. Not supernatural. Tempered-tier initiates could hold basic stances for thirty minutes without strain. But it was progress.

Progress he couldn't show.

He lowered his center fractionally. Adjusted his breathing to match the instructor's rhythm, in, hold, out, and kept his Vitalis reading locked at a number that looked like a student with decent natural reserves who wasn't trying. Sixty-eight. Seventy. The numbers drifted in a range that was plausible, nothing that would prompt a second look.

Around him, the cohort was thinning. Students who had come from better academies held their positions with visible effort. Students who had come from nothing were already shaking. The instructors made notes on slates, no names, just cohort numbers, tracking who could endure and who couldn't.

Roan endured. That was his entire strategy. Endure until the instructors stopped looking, then endure more.
