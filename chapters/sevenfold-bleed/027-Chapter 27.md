Chapter 27: Black Spire

Roan stood before the bracket board with his hands in his pockets. The posting area had thinned out over the last hour as students drifted toward the dormitories, but a cluster of thirty-odd remained, clustered around the main bracket display where names blazed in luminous blue script against black stone.

The preliminary bracket was a pyramid. Twenty-four names at the base, twelve in the second tier, six in the third, three in the semifinals, then the final. Each match was a best-of-three format, efficient enough to keep the tournament moving, brutal enough that no one could coast on a single fluke.

His eyes moved to the left column. Round One, Match Seven. Roan Vance vs. Darian Holt. Preliminary Arena B.

He filed the name, weighed it, discarded it. He didn’t recognize it, which meant the opponent was either someone who scraped through qualification by luck, or someone good enough to have stayed off his radar entirely. Either way, the ledger was the same: he would have a result within forty minutes.

Brendt appeared at his shoulder, exhaling sharply. “Roan.”

“See it.”

“You’ve got four matches to get through before you even hit the main bracket. Whoever set this bracket wanted you to earn every step.”

“Or they wanted to bury me early with opponents chosen specifically to counter my style.” Roan’s gaze swept the preliminary bracket again. “But look at the placement. If I win all four in the preliminary bracket, I’m seeded into the bracket at position nineteen. Which means my first main-bracket opponent is determined by whatever chaos happens in the middle section.”

“You’re reading conspiracy into a bracket arrangement.”

“I’m reading probability into an arrangement that conveniently keeps me away from every Ashford and Vell for the first three rounds.” He tapped the bracket board with one finger. “Cyrus Ashford is in the main bracket as the number two seed. His projected path, assuming he wins his first two matches, puts him in the semifinals on day four. I’m projected to reach the main bracket by day three if I don’t stumble in prelims.”

“So?”

“So the bracket separates us until the final.” He pulled his hand back. “Either this is coincidence, or someone is engineering a specific collision. I don’t believe in coincidence.”

Brendt studied the bracket, then shook his head slowly. “Your problem is that you assume everything is engineered.”

“My problem is that assuming nothing has gotten people killed. But fine. Assume I’m wrong. Say the bracket is clean and I’m being paranoid.” He turned away from the board. “I’ll still need to fight four matches in two days. That means I need to manage my capacity across a compressed timeline. That’s a logistics problem, not a conspiracy.”

“Still not a reason to be happy about it.”

“I’m not unhappy.” He started walking. “I’m calculating. There’s a difference.”

They moved down the corridor, past the main bracket board, past the smaller boards flanking the entrance to the training yards. A cluster of students was gathered around a handwritten betting ledger tacked to the wall—someone had started informal odds on the prelims. Roan’s name was listed at 4:1 for advancing to the main bracket. The line next to Darian Holt sat at 7:5. A short, weedy kid with a counting tablet was taking copper coins and scribbling names.

“Your odds are better than his,” Brendt noted.

“The odds reflect public knowledge. The public doesn’t know what I know. That makes the odds useful only for reference, not for prediction.”

They stepped out into the open air. The training yards were emptying as dusk settled over Black Spire. Lanterns flickered to life along the covered walkways, casting pools of orange light across the packed earth where sparring circles were etched into the ground. A few students still drilled—forms, strikes, footwork drills that would continue until curfew. Tomorrow morning, half of them would be watching matches instead of training. The other half would be fighting them.

Brendt kept pace. “You need a plan for the prelims. Four matches in two days is—”

“Six matches in two days if I make the main bracket and win my first match there.” Roan corrected. “That’s nine potential fights across forty-eight hours. I can fight nine times. What I can’t do is spend the same energy on round one that I save for round six.”

“So you plan to—”

“Lose the first few exchanges. Gather data. Conserve quota for the matches that matter.” He glanced at Brendt. “Or I just fight clean and walk through the whole bracket. The question is which approach creates the better outcome.”

“What does your gut say?”

“My gut says the preliminary bracket was designed by someone who wants to see me perform at full strength in front of the entire student body before the main bracket begins. They want a show. The question is whether they want to watch me succeed or fail.”

They passed a side arena where two second-year students were still running light sparring. The taller one caught Roan’s eye and held his gaze for a moment, then looked away. Not a threat. Not a signal. Just the kind of baseline surveillance that came with being a new variable in a closed system.

Brendt was quiet for a moment. “You could pull out. Claim illness, injury, anything. No one would force you to compete.”

“I could.” Roan continued walking. “But then I’d never know what the bracket was. Was it a trap? A test? A genuine opportunity? Pulling out tells me nothing.”

“You’re going to fight four matches you think might be a setup.”

“I’m going to fight four matches that will tell me exactly what I’m dealing with. That’s not the same thing.”

They reached the covered walkway that led back toward the residential towers. Overhead, the sky had deepened into violet-black, a color that reminded him of the sky over the Hollow Reaches before a storm. The air carried a faint metallic taste—ozone, or something older. He kept that too.

“Roan.” Brendt’s voice dropped. “If this is a trap, if someone arranged the bracket specifically to exhaust or expose you before the main tournament, what’s the counter?”

“The counter is that I’m not the only person in this tournament who was placed by someone else’s agenda.” He stepped around a puddle of spilled water from the evening washdown. “Every competitor has a sponsor, a family, a faction. Cyrus Ashford has his father. Iris Vell has her house. Selene Ardent has whatever secret arrangement she maintains with the Conclave. If someone engineered a bracket to trap me, they also engineered brackets for a dozen other competitors. Which means the tournament is a web of competing agendas, and the only advantage that matters is knowing whose agenda aligns with whose.”

“And yours?”

“Mine is simple.” He didn’t elaborate. Brendt had been patient enough.

The corridor to the junior dormitories split ahead. Brendt’s tower to the left, Roan’s to the right. They stopped at the junction.

“Tomorrow morning, seven sharp,” Roan said. “Don’t be late.”

“I’ll be there.”

“Take a meal. Rest well. If the bracket follows a pattern, Match Seven will be the day’s third or fourth bout. Which means I fight around midday.” He paused. “Which gives whoever arranged this eight hours to decide whether they want to intervene before my fight starts.”

Brendt studied his face. “You’re not even going to pretend to relax, are you.”

“Relaxation is a resource allocation problem. I’ll rest when the tournament tells me I can afford to.”

He turned toward his tower. Brendt watched him go.

The room was cold. He’d left the window cracked, a habit from Ironholm days when stale air felt like a cage. The Codex Shard sat quiet beneath his skin, inert, its interface dark. He hadn’t called it up in two days. The weight on his spine had become familiar enough that silence created its own discomfort, like a phantom limb aching for sensation.

Four matches. He sat on the edge of the bed and ran the numbers.

At Tempered, his reserves could handle roughly four clean inversions before recovery thinned noticeably. The safe estimate was four, but the actual number depended on sidewound severity. He’d never pushed the limit on a lethal-scale conversion—his instincts put a week’s gap as the minimum before attempting something that catastrophic. Currently he was at full reserves, his body quiet, no accumulated fatigue.

If he used all four safe uses in the prelims, the main bracket matches would be fought with diminished capacity. If he conserved, he risked losing early and never reaching the main bracket at all.

The columns didn’t lie. But the balance was also incomplete. He needed information about his opponents before he could solve for the correct distribution.

He lay back on the bed. The ceiling above was stone, same as every room in every dormitory tower in Black Spire. The same walls. The same institutional sameness. The difference was what happened inside those walls—the balance, the planning, the relentless compression of variables until only the essential remained.

Tomorrow. Seven matches maximum. Nine if he made the main bracket and advanced.

He closed his eyes.

—

The morning air was sharp enough to cut. Fog clung to the training grounds in thin sheets, diffusing the early light into something gray and pale. The Preliminary Arena was a half-buried amphitheater carved into the hillside below the main spire, twelve fighting circles arranged in a rough crescent, each ringed by tiered stone seating that could hold two hundred spectators per arena. Not large by any measure. Not formal. Which meant the fights would be close and personal, plus visible to anyone who cared to watch.

Roan arrived at six forty. Brendt was already there, sitting on the stone steps of Arena B with a folded packet of something wrapped in cloth.

“Eat,” Brendt said, holding it out.

He took it. Flatbread with spiced meat, still warm. He ate standing, watching the other competitors file into the arena grounds. The preliminary bracket drew a different crowd than the main tournament would—fewer spectators, more scouts. Faculty members stood at the perimeter, some with notepads, some with nothing at all. Drogan wasn’t among them. Neither was Headmaster Cole.

“Scouts,” Brendt observed quietly.

“A handful. Not enough to make this a performance.”

“The faculty not being here is—”

“Neutral information. Could mean they’re not interested. Could mean they’re watching from somewhere else. Could mean the preliminary bracket doesn’t register as worth their attention, which would favor whoever is being tested here.”

Brendt exhaled. “You’re impossible.”

“No. I’m consistent.” He finished the flatbread, wiped his hands on his trousers. “My opponent. Darian Holt. Who is he?”

Brendt pulled a crumpled paper from his pocket. “I asked around last night. Darian Holt—he’s a late-enlisted first-year, spent five years in a frontier militia before he qualified for Spire entry. Tempered tier. Ranked sixty-three in the incoming class standings. Solid but unremarkable. Primary style is Ironbound Fist, close-range grappling with a focus on ground control.”

“Age?”

“Nineteen.”

“Physical profile?”

“Average height, above-average reach. Stocky build—he carries his weight in his shoulders. Fighting stance is square, favoring stability over mobility.”

Roan processed. “He’s a grappler. That means his path to victory is closing distance, controlling the clinch, taking it to the ground. Which means his primary vulnerability is anyone who can maintain space and punish the approach.”

“You can maintain space.”

“I can.” He tucked the information away. “But if he’s trained specifically to close distance against strikers, he might have tools I haven’t seen yet. I need to watch his first match before mine.”

They found a vantage point on the upper seating of Arena B. The crowd was sparse—perhaps eighty people scattered across the stone benches. A handful of names Roan recognized from the ranking lists. A few faces he couldn’t place. The faculty presence was minimal. No Drogan. No Cole. A man in gray robes he didn’t recognize stood near the eastern entrance, hands clasped behind his back, expression unreadable.

Match one ended quickly. A Hardened-tier student dismantled a Tempered opponent in under two minutes. No surprises there. The gap between tiers was too large to close without a significant skill differential or a catastrophic mistake.

Match two was longer. Both competitors were Tempered, both built around mid-range striking. The bout stretched past four minutes before one of them landed a clean liver shot that ended the fight. Darian Holt wasn’t in either match.

Match three. Darian Holt stepped into the ring.

He was exactly as Brendt had described—square shoulders, stable stance, eyes tracking his opponent with the patient focus of a predator that knew it only needed one opening. His opponent was a girl with a slender build and a long reach, a striker who tried to maintain distance from the first exchange.

She failed. Darian closed in the first thirty seconds, got inside her guard, and spent the next two minutes grinding her down against the ring floor. It wasn’t elegant. It was effective. He won by referee decision at the four-minute mark, the girl unable to answer the restart after being dominated in ground control.

“Ground specialist,” Brendt murmured.

“Confirmed.” Roan watched Darian rise from the mount, nodding to the referee. No celebration. No wasted energy. “He’s not fast, but he doesn’t need to be. He trades mobility for control and he’s willing to invest time to execute it.”

“Can you keep it standing?”

“Depends on how good his reactive takedowns are. If I circle, he’ll pressure forward. If I strike and move, he’ll time a shot. The counter is to make him commit to a shot I can read, then punish the extension.”

“You’re planning to let him take a shot on purpose.”

“I’m planning to see if his shot is telegraphed enough to exploit. If it is, I counter. If it isn’t, I adjust.” He checked the bracket display. “Match five. I’m after the next one.”

“Rest.”

“I’ve been resting. What I need now is movement.” He stood, stretched his shoulders. “Stay here. Watch the next match. If something changes in the bracket or the timing, find me.”

“Find you where?”

“Arena B. Wherever Darian Holt is—if he moves between arenas for his own observation.”

Brendt gave him a long look. “You’re scouting your own opponent mid-tournament.”

“I’m gathering data. There’s a difference.”

He walked down toward the preparation area, a flat stone platform behind the arena ring where competitors waited before being called. Other students clustered there—some stretching, some sitting with their heads bowed in what might have been meditation or anxiety. A few watched him pass. He recognized one face, a Vell associate, someone from House Vell’s satellite network. The boy looked away when Roan’s gaze crossed him.

Interesting. Not useful yet, but interesting.

He found a clear section of the stone platform and began moving through his pre-fight sequence. Slow rotations at the ankles and wrists. Controlled breathing. The mechanics were the same regardless of opponent—warm the joints, settle the heart rate, clear the mental slate. The strategy would come from observation. The execution would come from the moment itself.

Four minutes later, the call came.

“Match Seven. Preliminary Arena B. Darian Holt. Roan Vance.”

He stepped into the ring.

Darian was already there, hands wrapped in gray cloth, stance low and wide. His eyes tracked Roan’s approach with the same patient focus he’d shown in his first match. No taunting. No pre-fight bravado. Just evaluation.

The referee, a faculty appointee—a woman in her thirties with a Hardened-tier read on his mental radius—called them to the center.

“Standard rules. Three-minute rounds. Best of three. Referee stop, tap, or knockout. Clinch timeout after ten seconds. Touch gloves.”

They touched. Darian’s hands were rough, calloused, built for gripping.

“Begin.”

Darian moved forward immediately. Not fast—methodical. He wanted to pressure, cut off angles, make Roan back up.

Roan stepped laterally and fired a probing jab. Darian didn’t parry it—he absorbed it on his shoulder, closed the distance, shot for a tie-up.

There. The shot was slightly telegraphed. A fraction of a second of weight shift on the front foot before he committed. Exploitable.

But exploiting it required commitment. If Roan wanted to counter the shot, he’d need to commit to a strike powerful enough to disrupt the takedown, which meant burning energy on an exchange that should have been neutral.

The figures said: probe again. Gather more data. See if the telegraph was consistent or situational.

He jabbed again. Darian absorbed, pressed, shot.

This time Roan saw the telegraph clearly. Weight shifted half a second before commitment. Front foot turned slightly inward. The tell was small but unmistakable—a habits-based movement pattern that would fire whether Darian was in peak condition or fatigued.

He needed to confirm it under stress. One more exchange.

He jabbed, circled, fired a teep kick to Darian’s lead leg—not powerful, just enough to disrupt his base. Darian adjusted and pressed, then shot again.

Telegraph confirmed.

Roan sidestepped, loaded his right hand, and fired a counter hook the moment Darian committed to the shot.

The connection was clean. Darian’s temple met the hook as his head dropped toward the takedown, and his momentum broke. He didn’t go down, but his rhythm shattered.

Roan didn’t press. He reset, circled, let Darian find his feet. The crowd murmured. That wasn’t a knockout finish. That wasn’t a dominant position. That was a single clean shot that suggested a threat without delivering a decision.

Darian’s eyes narrowed. He understood what had happened. His game plan—pressure, clinch, control—had a hole in it, and Roan had found it in the first exchange.

The second minute was a chess match. Darian tried to change his approach—higher stance, more striking to probe range—but his body defaulted to pressure every time Roan created distance. The telegraph was consistent. Every time Darian tried to shoot, his weight shifted forward with the same tell.

Roan punished it twice more. Not enough to end the fight, but enough to bleed Darian’s confidence. Each failed takedown cost him energy and composure. By the end of the third minute, Darian was starting to hesitate before committing, which made his shots slower and even easier to read.

Round one ended without a finish. The referee called the break.

Brendt was leaning over the railing, eyes wide. “You’re toying with him.”

“I’m mapping his pattern.” He rolled his shoulders, settled his breath. “He has one plan. I’ve identified the flaw in it. Now I’m deciding whether to exploit it fully or let him think he can adapt.”

“Let him think he can adapt.”

“Correct.”

Round two began. Darian came out with a new approach—wider stance, more head movement, attempting to change the angle of his pressure. It was a decent adjustment. Against a striker who relied on set angles, it might have worked.

Roan wasn’t a striker who relied on set angles. He was a calculator who used strikes as punctuation.

He let Darian circle for thirty seconds. Let him think the adjustment was working. Let him invest in the new approach. Then, when Darian committed to a combination—a jab-cross intended to create an opening for the shot—Roan slipped the cross and fired a liver kick that dropped Darian’s guard by half a step.

But Darian didn’t reset as expected. This time, he used the missed shot as a feint. Instead of committing to the full takedown, he stopped his penetration step short and dropped to a low single-leg catch that Roan hadn’t read in the set-up. The shift was abrupt enough to catch Roan mid-circle, and suddenly he was ankle-deep in Darian’s grip.

The crowd snapped to attention. Darian drove forward, lifted Roan’s leg, and forced him to hop backward. It wasn’t clean, but it was close. Roan felt the ground control threat compress. He slammed a palm strike into Darian’s ear to break the grip, but Darian was already switching to a double-leg re-shot, driving Roan off balance.

They hit the ground. Roan’s back touched the padded stone. Darian settled into half-guard, heavy chest pressure, grinding elbows.

The figures recalculated. This was the moment he’d deliberately risked when he let Darian think he could adapt. He’d wanted to see if Darian had another layer. He did.

[Sidewound: compressive pressure — moderate — ambient.]

[Conversion successful: +8 Vitalis. Yield: 80%.]

The Inversion smoothed the strain in his hips, but the sensation was a reminder: he was burning capacity on a position he shouldn’t have been in. He worked his guard, framed under Darian’s chin, and bucked to create space. Darian suppressed the escape with weight and shoulder pressure.

Stalemate.

Roan took a breath. He’d wanted data. He had it. Darian wasn’t a one-trick grappler—he could set traps. But the trap relied on Roan expecting the same shot every time. Now that Roan had seen the variation, the pattern expanded: Darian’s real tell was the fraction of weight shift before any commitment, whether full shot or feint. The telegraph was still there—it just came from the same origin.

Time to seal.

He built his base, waited for Darian to shift weight to improve position, and exploded upward into a technical stand-up. Darian clung, but Roan’s leverage was better from the post. He shook the grip, created an arm’s length, and was back on his feet in half a second.

The exchange cost him a clean Inversion use and thirty seconds of the round. It was still a net win: he now knew Darian’s entire pressure map.

Roan reset. “Nice adjustment.”

Darian said nothing, but his breathing was slightly heavier. The ground scramble had cost him energy too.

From that point on, the fight was a formality. Darian tried to mix in the feint again, but Roan read it from the same weight shift and stuffed the entry with a knee. The telegraph became a liability—each committed shot drew a counter that landed harder than the last. By the end of the second minute, Darian’s face was flushed with accumulated punishment. His stance had lost structure.

Roan waited for one more committed shot. It came. He sidestepped, loaded his right hand, and fired a clean hook directly onto Darian’s jaw as he committed his weight.

Darian’s body went rigid. He dropped lateral, hit the ground, and didn’t move.

The referee waved it over. “Winner by knockout. Roan Vance.”

The crowd noise was louder than before—not explosive, but appreciative. A Tempered-tier knockout finish wasn’t rare, but the way it had come—a calculated dismantling followed by an adaptation and a seal—drew more eyes. Roan caught sight of the gray-robed faculty member on the east side, who had shifted his attention from the match to the space directly behind Roan. The man was making notes on a small ledger.

Roan walked back to the preparation area. Brendt met him halfway.

“You got taken down.”

“I let myself get taken down. There’s a difference. I needed to see the feint layer before I closed the plan.” He felt the expended Inversion residue in his spine—a faint warmth that signaled one safe use gone. “Two conversions total, one minor, one clean. I’m still in range.”

“Your takedown escape was tight.”

“It was tight because he had a good trap. Now I know. The next three matches won’t see the same pattern—he’ll take that knowledge out of the bracket with him.” He checked the bracket display. “Preliminary round two is tomorrow morning.”

Brendt nodded. “Who’s next?”

“Tomas Wyre. Also Tempered. Ranked fifty-eight in the incoming standings. The bracket keeps placing me against mid-tier opponents—low enough to be beatable, high enough to look credible if I win all four.”

“You think that’s deliberate.”

“I think it’s consistent with the hypothesis that someone is engineering my path.” He folded the paper and tucked it away. “But it’s also consistent with random distribution that happens to place me in the deep bracket. The difference matters for how I approach the next three fights.”

“If it’s engineered, approach how?”

“If it’s engineered, I fight exactly as I did here—methodically, conservatively, gathering information. Because engineered paths are designed for an audience, and an audience wants to see competence, not dominance. Dominance attracts scrutiny. Competence attracts interest.”

“And if it’s random?”

“Then I still fight the same way, because four matches in two days is still a logistics problem regardless of whether someone arranged it.” He started walking toward the exit. “Either way, the approach is the same. The difference is only in what I assume about what happens after.”

Brendt fell into step beside him. “You’re going to win the whole tournament, aren’t you.”

“I’m going to win every match I fight. Whether that constitutes winning the tournament depends on how many matches I’m asked to fight.” He pushed through the arena gate into the afternoon light. “But I won’t get ahead of myself. Three more in the prelims. Then the main bracket. Then whatever’s behind the main bracket that—”

He stopped.

In the crowd near the main bracket board, a tall figure with Ashford’s crest on his collar was standing with his arms crossed, staring directly at Roan. He didn’t look away when their eyes met. He raised one hand, touched two fingers to his own throat, and pointed at the arena.

A promise. A threat. A confirmation that someone was watching—and that the next match wouldn’t be anonymous.

Roan held the gaze for a beat, then turned and kept walking.

“What was that?” Brendt asked, his voice lower.

“That was my next question being answered before I asked it.” He let the comment settle. The tally were still the same. Win four. Rest. Win two more. Rest. Fight whoever the bracket placed in front of him. Repeat until no one remained.

But now he knew that somewhere in that bracket, someone had already studied today’s performance.

Roan Vance.

Result: Win by KO, Round 2.

Vitalis: 1,520 (peak).

Inversion reserves: roughly two-thirds remaining.

Three more to go.
