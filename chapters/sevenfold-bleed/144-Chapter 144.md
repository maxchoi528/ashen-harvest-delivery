Chapter 144: The Cost of Entry

The temple wanted a thing Roan couldn't remove.

That was inconvenient, but not unprecedented. Several people wanted parts of Roan they couldn't remove. Most of them lacked stone doors, ancient guardians, and a Husk audience, which made their negotiations less urgent.

He looked at the blood-mark on the stele.

The shape had changed again. His palm impression remained, but the seven lines had pulled inward around a hollow center, forming a smaller version of the Codex's first icon. The mark wasn't bleeding. It was waiting. That was somehow worse.

The guardian's skull remained visible through the vertical slit.

"Codex wakes," it repeated. "Door remembers."

Roan flexed his left hand. The reopened cut stung. Ordinary pain. Useful pain. Blood gathered but didn't drip; the temple seemed uninterested in fluid once the wound-pattern had been copied.

The obvious methods for letting the Shard "touch" the stele were all stupid.

One: carve into his back until the Shard was exposed. Rejected. Too slow, too fatal, and likely to make Drogan violent.

Two: drive Vitalis from the Shard through his palm into the mark. Possible. Dangerous. The Codex sat in his spine, not his hand, but every Inversion used pathways that crossed blood and nerve, plus Vitalis. If he could route a Shard signature without triggering full inversion, the temple might accept it.

Three: use Sidewound as a conductor. The weapon had resonated with the door. It recognized Veyrn patterning. It also enjoyed cutting seams, including the seams inside him. Useful and not reassuring.

Four: lie.

Roan considered lying for perhaps half a second. Ancient identity machines were usually poor audiences for fraud, but not always. Old systems could be literal. If he said a statement shaped like access, perhaps the door would accept form over truth.

Then the Codex Shard pulsed.

[Do not lie to a door that remembers your father.]

Roan's mouth twitched.

"Helpful timing," he murmured.

Brendt leaned closer from behind his shield. "Was that at us or the door?"

"The hereditary parasite."

"Right. Glad we clarified."

Drogan had gone very quiet.

That quiet mattered more than the guardian. Roan turned enough to look at him.

The old man stood at the boundary where ash met clean stone. His blade was in hand now, though he hadn't drawn it with any visible motion. His left knee was locked. His eyes were on the slit, but his attention was split, guardian, Husks, Roan's hand, the temple door, route behind them. Counting ways to fail.

Roan understood the impulse.

He didn't indulge it.

"What happened when Aldric touched the tower?" Roan asked.

Drogan's gaze snapped to him.

"There was no time," Drogan said.

"There's never time. Answer anyway."

The guardian breathed.

The Husk pack clicked beyond the avenue.

Ines lowered her lens. Even she understood this wasn't a recording moment.

Drogan's jaw worked once. "He put his hand on a wall. The wall opened. Mercer said it was a vault. Your father said it was a classroom. Then the bells started and everything that had ignored us stopped ignoring us."

"Did it hurt him?"

"Yes."

"How?"

Drogan's eyes flicked to Roan's spine. "Like something was pulling his bones into words."

That was unpleasantly specific.

"Did he invert it?"

"I don't know."

"Guess."

"Yes."

The answer was too fast to be speculation. Drogan had seen enough.

"And after?"

"He couldn't remember my name for six minutes."

Roan absorbed that.

Memory loss. exposure risk. Shard activation through Veyrn architecture. Aldric, stronger and more experienced, had paid in identity recall. Roan's Shard was fragmented and partly shielded by the pendant. Better in some ways. Worse in others.

"Good," Roan said.

Brendt stared. "Good?"

"Known cost category."

"Roan."

"Unknown costs are worse."

"They aren't worse than forgetting names."

"Depends on the name."

Brendt opened his mouth, then closed it. Perhaps he remembered that Roan had already forgotten pieces of time before. Perhaps he remembered that arguing near blood doors rarely improved them.

Ines did, however. "Can you anchor against memory slip?"

"Somewhat."

"With what?"

"External reminders and pain, plus people irritating enough to reassert context."

Brendt raised his hand. "I have never been more ready."

"Moderately ready," Roan corrected. "Too much irritation creates murder incentives."

"Also grounding."

"Briefly."

Drogan stepped closer to the boundary. "If you forget more than names, we leave."

"If I forget tactics, drag me."

"If you forget us?"

Roan looked at him.

There were kind answers. There were useful answers. They occasionally overlapped, but not often enough to rely on.

"If I forget you," Roan said, "Brendt tells me I owe him money."

Brendt blinked. "Do you?"

"No."

"That might work better if it were true."

"It will annoy me either way."

Ines nodded thoughtfully. "He has a point."

Drogan looked like he wanted to object and couldn't find a better plan. That was becoming a theme.

Roan turned back to the stele.

"Guardian," he said. "Define acceptable Codex contact."

The skull behind the slit angled downward.

"Touch through wound. Touch through mark. Touch without chain."

Chain.

The Vance Pendant cooled.

Roan looked down at his chest.

The pendant was a signal sink. It reduced recognition risk. It also interfered. The guardian wanted contact without chain. Either it considered the pendant a chain, or it objected to something else restraining the Shard.

Removing the pendant near a severe recognition marker seemed like taking off armor because a knife asked politely.

"No," Roan said.

The guardian went still.

Drogan made a low sound. "No?"

"Yes."

"To the temple?"

"To removing the pendant."

"You understood that?"

"Likely."

The guardian's voice deepened. "Chain dulls proof."

"Proof remains proof if it survives interference," Roan said. "A door worth opening should recognize signal through noise."

Brendt put one hand over his face. "He's negotiating authentication standards again."

Ines whispered, "I am enjoying this more than is appropriate."

The guardian's skull withdrew slightly.

The stele mark darkened.

For a moment, the air pressed against Roan's ears.

Then the guardian said, "Chain permitted. Proof burden increased."

That was interesting.

Not forbidden. Increased burden.

A secure system allowed degraded signal if enough other factors matched. The temple wasn't rigid. It could evaluate. Good. Also bad, because evaluation implied intelligence.

Roan looked at his palm.

Blood. Wound. Mark. Shard. Chain permitted.

He needed to move a signature from his spine to the stele without inverting the pain into some unrequested permanent change. He needed the Shard awake enough to authenticate, not awake enough to broadcast.

He drew Sidewound.

The door brightened immediately.

The guardian's skull snapped forward.

Drogan's blade rose half an inch.

"Not attack," Roan said.

"That isn't always comforting when you hold a knife," Brendt said.

He set the blade down again before committing.

That earned him a slight tilt of the guardian's skull, which Roan chose to interpret as either curiosity or disappointment. Both were survivable.

"Anchor protocol," he said.

Brendt blinked. "We have a protocol?"

"We do now."

"That's my least favorite kind."

Roan pointed to Brendt first. "You use debt. False but irritating. Keep the number specific and inconsistent if I fail to respond."

"Specific and inconsistent?"

"Say twelve hundred first. If I ignore it, say eight hundred. If I accept either amount, assume memory compromise."

Brendt absorbed that with a seriousness that looked strange on his normally expressive face. "Because you would dispute both."

"Immediately."

"Good. I can do that."

"Ines."

She straightened slightly. "Yes?"

"You ask for first access to the western signatory material."

Her eyes narrowed. "Why that?"

"If I give it freely, assume judgment impairment."

"Flattering."

"Accurate. Also ask whether I promised you first refusal on non-Codex salvage."

"You did."

"Then I should answer yes with irritation. If I answer no, I may have lost recent contractual memory."

Ines nodded. "Understood."

She added, "If the contact tries to rewrite your contract memory, I have the original Wraithmilk agreement with your signature. Only you know which margin note I added as a test."

Roan glanced at her. "Good. Use it if the identity bleed gets structural."

Drogan's expression had darkened with each instruction.

Roan turned to him last. "You use Aldric's sentence."

"No."

"Yes."

"No."

The refusal was immediate and too rough.

Roan held his gaze. "If the Shard contact affects deep identity, ordinary cues may not reach far enough. Aldric's projection did. Use the thirteen words."

Drogan's face closed so hard it looked carved.

Brendt looked away.

Ines lowered her eyes to the floor.

The guardian waited.

Roan didn't soften the instruction. He couldn't afford soft. If he came out of the contact believing himself something else, someone needed a blade long enough to reach the buried place. Aldric's sentence had already done that once.

Drogan finally said, "You remember them."

"Yes."

"Say them."

Roan's jaw locked for half a second.

Then he said, "I'm sorry. I'm proud. Don't become me."

The words didn't echo.

They didn't need to Drogan nodded once, the smallest movement a human neck could make and still count. "Fine."

"Also, if I forget your name again, don't look wounded until after we leave."

"I didn't."

"You did."

"I will throw you into the wall."

"Good. Anger improves recall."

Brendt made a strangled sound that might've been a laugh trying to survive in hostile conditions.

Roan picked up Sidewound again.

The extra minute had helped. His pulse was slower. The pain in his palm had settled into a clean line. The Shard's attention remained at his spine, awake but not expanding. The Vance Pendant pressed cold against his sternum, muting the outward bleed.

Preparation didn't make the act safe.

It made failure less creative.

He ran one more check. Daily capacity held. The team was positioned. The door was open in principle. The arithmetic resolved cleanly. Proceed.

"Don't pull me off contact unless I stop answering anchors, begin speaking a language none of you understand, or the temple starts writing through my bones."

Brendt raised one finger. "How do we identify writing through bones?"

"You'll know."

"I hate that answer."

"It's traditional."

The guardian spoke. "Blood-mark prepares like one hunted."

Roan looked at it. "Was Veyrn not hunted?"

"Veyrn prepared like one certain it deserved survival."

That was a good sentence and a bad epitaph.

Roan filed it.

Roan reversed the grip and laid the flat of Sidewound against his bleeding palm.

The blade's central line drank the shape of the wound. It didn't open the skin further. It didn't need to It remembered the previous Crimson Wake. It remembered the Husk seams. It remembered the temple steps.

The Codex Shard stirred.

[Resonant circuit possible.]

[Warning: Codex exposure through Sidewound may trigger identity bleed.]

[Invert?]

No.

Roan refused before the prompt completed.

The Shard pulsed again, sharper.

[Manual contact route unstable.]

[Proceed?]

Yes.

Not Inversion. Activation.

The distinction mattered. The Shard didn't reverse harm. It extended identity along harm's path.

Pain climbed from his palm through wrist, elbow, shoulder, spine. It was thin pain, surgical rather than blunt, as if someone had threaded wire through every old injury and was testing which bells still rang. Bloodscourge. Sevenfold. Wraithmilk. Pyrebrand. Hollow Marrow. Marrowforge. Crimson Wake. Sidewound. The Codex knew the order better than he did.

Roan pressed Sidewound's point to the blood-mark on the stele.

The stone accepted the blade without a scratch.

Not into the stone. Through it.

For a heartbeat, the temple touched the Shard.

Roan's vision fractured.

A city under a red sky. Seven towers bleeding light upward. People walking backward through falling ash, wounds closing only when they remembered why they had opened. A man with Aldric's face but not Aldric's age standing before a black gate with one hand on a child's shoulder. A white-bearded man without the beard, younger and smiling, holding a glaive that hadn't yet been named Northern.

Then pain.

Not physical.

Categorical.

The temple looked at Roan Vance and found the name shallow. It looked deeper and found Aldric's blood. Deeper and found Veyrn. Deeper and found the Codex Shard, cracked and hiding in bone. Deeper and found the world around him, wrong-shaped, conquered, renamed.

The Shard answered.

Not in words.

In refusal.

[Codex contact established.]

[External archive challenge accepted.]

[Identity bleed detected.]

[Anchor required.]

Brendt moved first.

"Roan owes me twelve hundred credits," he said loudly.

Roan's eyes snapped open.

"No, I don't."

"That was fast," Brendt said, voice tight.

Drogan had crossed the boundary.

The ash line didn't stop him. Or perhaps the temple allowed it because the proof was already underway. His hand closed on Roan's right shoulder, not pulling, not yet, but anchoring with blunt human weight.

"Vance," Drogan said.

The name hit differently.

Not because it was truer. Because it was chosen.

Roan held the blade to the mark.

The guardian began speaking. Not to him. To the door.

The language scraped through the avenue. The step inscriptions answered in sequence, lowest to highest, seven clusters per step lighting in black-red order. The forty-nine steps became a ladder of wounds.

Husks screamed beyond the boundary.

Not shrieked. Screamed.

The pack outside recoiled from the avenue as if something had finally noticed them.

Ines, because survival hadn't cured her of scholarship, whispered, "Forty-nine-step activation. Sequential acknowledgment. Route archive opening."

"Less notes," Drogan said.

"More accuracy," she replied.

The temple door split.

No explosion. No grand sound. The black stone opened inward along seven seams, each folding away with the slow care of a hand unwrapping a bandage. Cold air breathed out.

It smelled of tea.

That was the first impossible thing inside.

Not rot. Not ash. Not old blood. Tea. Bitter, floral, slightly oversteeped.

Drogan went rigid.

Roan didn't turn.

The contact wasn't finished.

The Shard and stele remained linked through Sidewound, and the temple was still reading. Images pressed against the edge of thought, too many to parse. A classroom. A surgery. A battlefield. A child hiding under a table while bells rang. Aldric's hands, younger, shaking as he wrote in a language Roan could now almost read.

The guardian's skull lowered.

"Codex wounded," it said.

"Obviously," Roan managed.

"Codex partial. Heir partial. Door partial."

"How encouraging."

"Enter partial. Learn enough not to die."

The mark released Sidewound.

Roan pulled the blade back and nearly fell.

Drogan caught him before his knees buckled. That was irritating mostly because it was necessary.

The first memory anchor failed before anyone noticed.

It wasn't a clean failure. Clean failures were visible. A clean failure would've been Roan forgetting Brendt's name or accepting a false debt. Instead, while the Shard remained in contact with the stele, Roan briefly believed the temple corridor behind him had always been part of Black Spire.

Not literally. Not as a thought he could examine and reject. More like a background assumption sliding into place. Black stone became lower annex architecture. The guardian became a Spire examiner with unusual posture. The Husks outside became distant training hazards. The absurdity lasted less than a second.

Long enough.

Roan bit the inside of his cheek.

Blood filled his mouth. Ordinary blood. Ordinary pain. Present tense.

The false continuity broke.

[Memory integrity fluctuation detected.]

[Anchor engaged: self-inflicted sensory correction.]

[Exposure risk: moderate.]

Noted.

He didn't tell the others yet. Reporting during contact would split attention and perhaps make them say useful things at the wrong time. Better to let the anchor protocol continue naturally.

The temple pushed again.

This time it offered a different arrangement. Aldric had not died. Aldric stood somewhere beyond the open door, annoyed that Roan had taken so long, ready to explain everything with that dry marginal tone the Shard kept imitating. A practical lie. A tempting lie, because it didn't ask Roan to become soft. It merely offered a better briefing.

Roan held the blade against the mark and counted costs.

If Aldric lived behind the door, Drogan's grief was false, Mira's silence was worse, House Greel's recognition was misread, the projection's thirteen words were theater, and every hard-won inference about the Six collapsed into melodrama.

Too expensive.

He rejected it.

The Shard steadied.

Brendt's voice came from behind him, too loud and too near. "Roan Vance owes me eight hundred credits."

"No," Roan said.

"Why not?"

"Because last time you said twelve hundred."

Brendt released a shaky breath. "Good."

Ines followed immediately. "First access to the western signatory materials?"

"Reviewed access after screening for loyalty and blackmail, plus sentimental stupidity."

"Still rude," she said.

"Still accurate."

Drogan didn't speak.

For one moment, Roan wondered if the old man had frozen. Then Drogan's voice came low, and the room tightened around it.

"I'm sorry. I'm proud. Don't become me."

The temple paused.

Not Roan. The temple.

The words struck the contact route like a second key turning inside a lock. Aldric's sentence didn't belong to Veyrn exactly. It belonged to Aldric Vance, which wasn't the same as Aldric Veyrn, and the distinction mattered. The temple had been measuring blood, archive, exile, debt. Drogan's words introduced choice. A surface name. A father. A failure trying to warn a son away from repetition.

For the first time since contact began, the guardian lowered its head.

"Surface witness accepted," it said.

Roan's grip tightened around Sidewound.

That was new.

Not Veyrn witness. Surface witness. The temple had accepted Drogan, not as debt-bearer alone, but as someone whose memory of Aldric anchored Roan's current name.

Roan Vance was shallow, perhaps.

But shallow things could still keep a man from drowning.

The door's seven seams deepened.

The Shard didn't relax. Neither did Roan.

A third memory pressed in, colder than the first two.

A red sky. A hand much larger than his own closing around a child's wrist. Not Aldric's hand. The white-bearded man before the beard. House Greel smiling as bells rang. A voice saying a name Roan couldn't hear because the memory had been cut around it.

The buried name.

The temple wanted to offer it.

Or the contact did. Or the enemy had left a hook in the archive.

Roan didn't reach.

A name was a handle. Handles were for people who intended to pull.

He thought, very: not here.

Sidewound's edge vibrated against the stele.

The hidden name withdrew.

Something beyond the door clicked once, deep inside the stone.

The guardian said, "Heir refuses bait."

"Frequently," Roan said through clenched teeth.

Brendt whispered, "Was that a joke?"

"Later," Ines said.

Roan kept the blade steady until the mark stopped pulling.

[Codex contact ended.]

[Daily Capacity: unchanged.]

[Identity bleed: contained.]

[Exposure risk: moderate.]

[Memory integrity: unstable — anchor advised.]

Roan blinked.

For one moment, he couldn't remember the name of the man holding him.

Gray coat. Scar through left eyebrow. Bad knee. Former Black Squad. Drinks cheap whiskey. Loved Aldric. Lies by omission. Taught Hollowstrike. Would die before saying anything useful directly.

"Drogan," Roan said.

The old man's grip loosened by a fraction.

"Again," Drogan said.

"Drogan."

"Again."

"Stop wasting my oxygen."

Drogan let go.

Brendt exhaled hard enough to fog the inside of his mask. "That counts."

Ines had lowered the lens completely now. Her expression wasn't greed anymore. It was calculation after seeing a price tag too large to steal casually.

Roan looked at the open door.

Inside, darkness waited in a corridor too clean for the dead city. Warm yellow light glowed somewhere deeper, steady and domestic. The scent of tea remained.

The guardian withdrew from the slit. For a moment, Roan thought it was gone. Then the main doorway framed it.

It stepped into view.

Tall. Almost human in arrangement if one had never liked humans much. Its skin was dark gray and plated along the shoulders, the plates cracked and repaired with black resin. Its head was a long eyeless helm of bone fused to a narrow neck. Seven vertical cuts marked the brow. Its hands had three fingers and one thumb, each ending in blunt black claws. Around its throat hung a chain of small metal tags, each carved with a name Roan couldn't read.

The guardian carried no weapon.

That didn't mean it was unarmed.

Brendt whispered, "That isn't a door monster."

"No," Ines said softly. "That's a person."

The guardian tilted its head toward her.

"Remnant," it corrected.

Then it looked at Roan.

"Blood-mark passes. Carriers pass. Debt-bearer passes under watch."

Drogan's mouth tightened.

The guardian turned and walked into the temple.

Roan sheathed Sidewound, flexed his left hand, and checked his memory by force.

Mira. Lyra. Brendt. Drogan. Ines. Aldric. House Greel. Eckhart. Veyrn.

All present.

Good enough.

Brendt limped to the bottom step. "Do we follow the ancient remnant into the tea-smelling death temple?"

Roan looked at the open door.

The efficient answer was yes.

The honest answer was also yes.

"Obviously," he said.
