Chapter 23: Sentinel's Second Pass

The old man behind the counter didn't blink.

That was the first problem. Most merchants in the Ironholm outer market blinked when Roan placed the small glass vial on the worn counter between them. Some blinked and leaned back. The smarter ones blinked and called for security.

Old Pell just looked at the vial, then at Roan, then back at the vial.

"Sablefang," he said. Not a question.

"Fifty-fifty."

"Twenty percent chance you'll be dead before the pill finishes dissolving."

"And eighty percent chance I'll gain thirty percent Vitalis within the hour."

Old Pell scratched his beard. He was perhaps sixty, perhaps older, hard to tell with scarring that came from decades handling reagents in a market where the quality inspectors took bribes and the merchandise took fingers. His left hand was missing two digits. His right eye had the milkiness that came from a chemical burn gone wrong.

"You've done this before."

"A few times."

"And you're still here."

"Numbers were harder to bribe than people." Roan tapped the counter once. "The odds favor me. That's all the explanation you need."

Old Pell picked up the vial. Held it to the weak light from the single grimy window. The liquid inside was the color of old copper, not the bright copper of new coins, but the dark oxidized shade of metal that's been through something. Metal that's been tested.

"Huh," he said.

That was it. Just "huh."

Roan waited.

Most people filled silence with noise. Brendt filled it with questions. The academy instructors filled it with warnings. His mother would have filled it with worry. Old Pell just looked at the vial like it was a mildly interesting stone he'd found on a beach, then set it back down on the counter.

"Fifty credits."

"Forty."

"Forty-five."

Roan considered. The vial contained perhaps three doses. The market rate for a Sablefang Venom Pill was fifteen credits per dose, if you could find someone stupid enough to sell them, and stupid enough to buy. Most people who bought Sablefang were either desperate or stupid. Desperate people didn't have forty-five credits lying around. Stupid people didn't live long enough to become repeat customers.

Old Pell wasn't stupid. He'd survived sixty years in a market that killed three generations of merchants. That kind of survival required either extraordinary luck or a complete absence of sentiment. With him, it was the latter.

"Forty-two."

"Fifty."

"Forty-five, and you tell me where you got it."

A pause. Not long, maybe half a second, but Roan caught it. The pause before agreement meant Old Pell had something to hide, and the price of that information was lower than fifty credits.

"Forty-five," Old Pell said. "And that's the end of it."

"Done."

Roan counted out the credits. He'd budgeted sixty for this trip, which left him eighteen for food and another three for the toll at the bridge crossing back into the academy district. Eighteen credits wouldn't buy much, but it would buy enough.

The vial went into his inner pocket, the one sewn into the lining of his jacket, where a pickpocket's fingers wouldn't find it. He'd sewn the pocket himself, using thread bought from a street vendor who didn't ask questions about why a seventeen-year-old wanted reinforced clothing.

Standard merchant practice. No one blinked at seventeen-year-olds with sewn pockets. The outer market was full of them.

Old Pell swept the credits into his drawer without counting. Another tell. He knew exactly how many credits were in that pile, either from years of practice or because he'd counted them before Roan finished talking.

"Walk straight out," Old Pell said. "Don't stop at corners. Don't look at the food stalls."

"Why?"

"Because the kid from House Ashford is three stalls down, and he's looking for someone to buy him something interesting."

Roan kept his expression neutral. House Ashford. Cyrus's family. The same House Ashford that had been quietly pressuring the academy's equipment office to deny Roan access to the better training implements. The same House Ashford whose heir had publicly declared, three weeks ago, that Roan Vance was "a scholarship case who didn't belong in a room with real warriors."

Cyrus Ashford was seventeen like Roan. Also Tempered tier, first-stage. Also came from money, though his family's money was newer and shinier, plus considerably more aggressive.

"His credit good?" Roan asked.

"Better than yours."

"That isn't saying much."

Old Pell almost smiled. Almost. "Go out the back. I don't want trouble in my stall."

"Since when?"

"Since the last time trouble came through, it cost me two fingers and my good eye. You going to pay for my new ones?"

Roan counted the cost in his head. Two fingers, one eye, and whatever the next round of trouble would cost. Old Pell was a survivor, which meant Old Pell was a coward, the useful kind, the kind who survived by calculating risk-to-reward ratios with the precision of a merchant reviewing a ledger.

The back of the stall led through a narrow alley that smelled of old oil and newer desperation. Roan navigated it without hurrying. Hurrying attracted attention. He kept his pace steady, his hands visible, his eyes moving in the pattern he'd learned from watching city patrol routes three months ago.

The alley opened onto a side street that ran parallel to the main market thoroughfare. Three blocks east, a bridge. Four blocks north, the academy gate.

Roan made it to the bridge without incident. The toll collector was a bored woman with a scar across her throat who didn't bother checking whether students had paid the market tax, a tax that existed on paper but rarely got enforced on anyone who looked like they belonged in the academy district.

The academy gates were twelve feet tall, made of black iron that had been painted so many times the metal underneath had started to rust through the paint. The emblem at the center showed a spire rising from a broken circle, the old symbol of the Black Spire Combat Institute, before the school had become a training ground for Conclave families and the emblem had quietly been updated to show the spire surrounded by seven stars.

Roan walked through without stopping.

The inner district was different from the outer market. The streets were paved instead of packed dirt. The buildings had windows that weren't boarded up. The air smelled less like oil and more like the chemical tang of training grounds where students practiced combat forms against reinforced training dummies.

His dormitory was on the third floor of a building that housed forty-seven students. His room was on the south-facing side, which meant it caught the afternoon sun and stayed warm until dark. The warmth was useful in winter. In summer it was a problem, but summer was three months away and Roan had learned to think in terms of immediate problems rather than distant ones.

He locked the door behind him. Checked the window, clear. Checked the wall where the seam met the floor, no new shadows without sounds from the neighboring room. Then he sat on his bed, pulled out the vial, and held it up to the fading light.

Sablefang venom came from the fangs of a breed of serpent that lived in the Hollow Reaches, the deep rifts, the ones that ordinary people didn't visit. The venom was fast-acting, which meant it could kill a person before their body had time to mount an immune response. But fast-acting also meant fast-metabolizing. The venom burned through a person's system in minutes, either killing them or being processed and excreted.

The pill form was an attempt to moderate that speed. Someone had taken the raw venom and mixed it with binding agents that slowed the release, hoping to give the body time to adapt. The adaptation would either strengthen the user's Vitalis channels or kill them.

Fifty-fifty odds.

Roan turned the vial in his fingers. He could feel the faint hum of the liquid inside, not a sound, exactly, but a vibration. The venom was active, even in suspension. It wanted to do something. It was waiting.

Inversion would handle it. The Codex Shard embedded in his spine would detect the side effects when the venom started working, convert them into pure Vitalis, and convert the death risk into a strengthening of his system.

But Inversion had limits. The Codex could convert side effects, not substance. If the venom was too concentrated, if it hit his system faster than the Codex could process, the conversion would lag. The damage would become real before the system caught up.

Three conversions this month. He'd used one on a low grade stimulant that was supposed to help with focus but gave him a migraine instead. Inversion turned the migraine into a brief surge of clarity that lasted four hours. Useful, but not dramatic.

The second conversion had been a training injury, torn ligament in his left knee from overextending during a sparring session. The Inversion converted the pain into an accelerated healing period that cost him two days of training but left the knee stronger than before.

The third had been a bad meal at the academy's dining hall. Food poisoning, from the fish, from the fish after Brendt mentioned he'd eaten the same fish and spent the following morning vomiting.

Three conversions. Three more available, at least according to the internal calculation the Codex provided. The daily capacity was a soft limit, it could be exceeded in emergencies, but exceeding it meant paying a different kind of price. Pain that didn't convert. Damage that stayed. Recovery time that extended from hours to days.

He'd done it twice in the past year. Both times, the aftermath had been bad enough that he'd sworn never to do it again.

The vial sat in his palm. Small. Unremarkable. Thing that could be found in any market stall run by anyone who didn't care whether their customers lived or died.

Which was exactly what made it profitable.

He uncapped the vial. Tilted it until one drop of the copper-colored liquid rolled out and onto his tongue.

The taste was wrong. Not bitter, not sweet, something else. Something that felt like the absence of a taste, a hole in his sensory map where a flavor should've been. His body registered the introduction of the foreign substance and began its response sequence.

Side effect detected.

The Codex Shard's response was automatic now, a cascade of input-output that he'd learned to read the way other people read facial expressions. The venom was making contact with his stomach lining. The binding agents were failing. The raw venom was hitting his bloodstream.

[Side effect detected. Beginning analysis.]

The analysis phase was the dangerous part. The Codex needed to identify the side effects, what the venom was *doing* to his system, before it could begin converting. In those seconds of analysis, the damage was real. The venom worked without permission.

Roan's vision narrowed. His hands went cold. The heartbeat in his ears became the only sound in the world, and it was slowing, each beat a little further apart than the last, as if his body was trying to decide whether to continue at all.

[Converting.]

The first wave hit him like a punch to the sternum. He saw white. He smelled copper. His knees buckled and he landed on the floor of his dormitory room, one hand catching the edge of his bed, the other pressed flat against the cold boards.

[Conversion in progress. Estimated yield: negligible Vitalis increase.]

Not thirty percent. Not even ten. The pill was old, which meant the venom had degraded. The binding agents had failed early, which meant the full dose hit his system before the Codex could modulate the intake rate. The result was a conversion that was more expensive than useful — he'd taken the full side effect of a near-lethal poison for a gain that wouldn't register on any test.

Furious. He was absolutely furious.

Roan lay on the floor of his dormitory room, breathing through the aftereffects, and felt the rage build in his chest. Not hot rage, cold rage, the kind that came from mathematics. He'd paid forty-five credits for a pill that gave him less than one percent gain. He'd risked his life for a number that wouldn't make a difference in any calculation he was running.

The columns were wrong. The ledger should have worked.

[Conversion complete. Yield: negligible. Daily capacity: 2/4.]

The Codex's notification came with a faint echo of something else, not a voice, not a sensation. Something that might've been amusement, if the dead could be amused.

[You got cheated. The vial was past its shelf date. — A.V.]

His father's voice. Or the echo of it, the pattern that the Codex had learned to reproduce when it detected certain emotional states. Aldric Vance had been dead for six years, but the Codex carried enough of his patterns to simulate the deadpan commentary that Aldric had been known for in his letters.

Roan sat up. His hands had stopped shaking. The cold had receded from his extremities. The venom was gone, converted, processed, excreted through channels he couldn't identify. He'd survived the fifty-fifty. He'd beaten the odds.

And it had cost him forty-five credits and accomplished nothing.

"Cheated," he said to the empty room.

The word sat in the air. Someone had sold Old Pell a degraded product, and Old Pell had sold it to Roan without checking, because checking would have cost time and time was money. The merchant chain from Hollow Reaches to the outer market was long enough that multiple opportunities for fraud existed at every link.

Roan wasn't someone who got cheated. Roan was someone who did the cheating, who found the gaps in systems and exploited them. Who saw the balance and ran it correctly while other people made errors.

Someone had made an error here. Someone had cheated the system. And he was going to find out who.

He got to his feet. The room looked the same as it had before, narrow bed, small desk, window with a view of the training yard below. Nothing had changed. The world hadn't shifted to accommodate his failure.

The world didn't do that. The world kept moving, and you moved with it or you got left behind.

Roan opened his notebook to the page where he tracked his resources. Forty-five credits spent. Expected return: thirty percent Vitalis gain. Actual return: a rounding error. The gap between expectation and result wasn't a gap, it was a chasm. It was the difference between a useful investment and a waste of money.

He needed to find out where the degradation had happened. He needed to trace the supply chain back to its source. Not because he expected compensation — he did expect compensation — but that wasn't the primary motivation. The primary motivation was that someone had successfully deceived him, and deception was an error in the system. Errors were opportunities.

If Old Pell had sold him a degraded product knowingly, that was fraud. If Old Pell had bought it from someone who had degraded it, that was a gap in the merchant's quality control. Either way, there was information to be extracted. There was leverage to be gained.

And if the trail led to House Ashford, as Old Pell's comment about "the kid from House Ashford" suggested... Roan closed the notebook.

Then he opened it again.

The trail might lead to House Ashford. Or it might not. The connection between Cyrus Ashford and the degraded Sablefang was thin. Old Pell had mentioned the Ashford heir in the context of a warning about being seen in the wrong market, not in the context of product sourcing.

But thin connections were still connections. And House Ashford had been pressuring the academy's equipment office. And Cyrus Ashford had publicly declared that Roan Vance didn't belong.

Circumstantial. Coincidental. A pattern that might mean something or might mean nothing. A pattern that required investigation.

[Your father would have traced it to the source. — A.V.]

The Codex's echo was persistent today. Aldric had been dead for six years, but the patterns lived on, in the device embedded in Roan's spine, in the letters that Roan kept in a locked box, in the memory of a conversation about math tests and better tools.

Don't get caught next time.

Roan tapped his pen against the notebook page. The investigation would take time. It would also take resources — credits he didn't have, access he might not be granted, information he didn't know how to find.

But he had something that most people didn't have. He had the Codex Shard. He had a system that could convert the side effects of anything into usable gain. He had a daily capacity of conversions that he hadn't fully explored.

And he had a dead man's voice telling him to trace it to the source.

He closed the notebook. Crossed the room. Locked the vial back in the hidden compartment in his jacket lining — one dose remaining, degraded or not.

Then he went to find Brendt.

His fellow student would know something about the outer market's supply chains. Brendt came from a merchant family, even if his family wasn't in the same league as House Ashford. He understood how goods moved through the city, where the gaps in quality control existed, how to trace a degraded product back to its origin.

It wasn't much. It was a start.

Roan walked down the dormitory stairs and out into the evening air. The training yard below was empty — most students were at dinner — which meant Brendt was in the dining hall. Roan could intercept him there. He could also wait outside Brendt's door. He could also ask one of the other students where Brendt had gone and adjust his approach accordingly.

He had options. He always had options. That was the advantage of thinking in terms of systems rather than commitments.

The question was whether to pursue this investigation now or to file it away and return to it later. The question was whether a forty-five credit loss and a rounding-error gain was worth the effort of a full inquiry.

The answer, computed quickly, was yes.

The arithmetic supported it. Not because the math was kind — the tally didn't care about kindness — but because the figures said that fraud was a gap, and gaps were opportunities, and opportunities were the only way forward when you were seventeen years old with no family name and no safety net.

The figures said yes.

And the columns didn't lie.
