Chapter 29: The Invitation

The aftermath of breakthrough was always worse than the breakthrough itself.

Roan Vance sat in the small cleaning chamber adjacent to the training hall, his back against the cold stone wall, watching the thin line of blood trace a slow path down his forearm. The wound had already closed. Codex Shard processing at its usual efficiency, but the phantom sensation of the split remained, a memory of tissue and vein that existed only as echo now.

[Sidewound absorbed.]

[Conversion successful: +87 Vitalis.]

[Daily capacity: 2/4. — A.V.]

Eighty-seven points. That was what a clean, controlled split netted. Not the explosive yields he'd seen in his father's notes, those described conversions in the hundreds, but still better than nothing. His body was still recalibrating to the new baseline. Tempered felt different than he'd imagined. Not stronger, exactly. More *architected*. As if his muscles and tendons had been replaced with versions that understood their own structural logic.

"Still breathing?"

Warden Drogan's voice came from the doorway. The old man stood with his arms crossed, his Iron Hand glinting in the torchlight. He'd been there for ten minutes already. Roan had heard him pacing, but had waited until the conversion notification faded before speaking.

"Breathing and thinking," Roan said. "That's the problem."

Drogan's weathered face creased. "Breakthrough anxiety. Classic."

"It's not anxiety."

"It's always anxiety."

Roan looked up. "It's calculation. I broke through a week early. Cyrus Ashford will have noticed. The ranking board will update in the next two days. Everyone who was watching me", he ticked them off on his fingers, "will recalibrate their threat assessment, and I will lose every single advantage I've spent six months building."

Drogan leaned against the doorframe. "So don't let the ranking update."

"The ranking board is automatic. Sensor array reads Vitalis output and—" Roan stopped. Considered. Reconsidered.

Drogan watched him work through it. The old man had the patience of a glacier and the same warmth. "You're not wrong that people will notice. But notice *what*, exactly?"

"That I'm Tempered."

"And?"

"And I was showing Initiate-level output three weeks ago."

"Correct."

"So I'm either hiding potential—" Roan's voice flattened, "—which means I'm a threat worth eliminating early, or I had help, which means I have a resource worth taking. Neither option ends well for me."

Drogan was silent for a moment. Then he reached into his coat and produced a small glass vial, setting it on the stone floor between them. The liquid inside was pale blue, almost translucent. "Vitalis Masking Agent. Academy-issued. Drops your displayed readings by sixty percent for six hours. Side effects include mild vertigo and a twelve-hour cooldown before you can consume anything else."

Roan stared at the vial. "You're giving me a cheat code."

"I'm giving you a tool. There's a difference." Drogan's voice hardened. "The difference is that you're smart enough to know what it costs. Masking agents work until they don't. Someone with better sensors, or someone who watches you fight, will see through it eventually. But 'eventually' can buy you months."

"Months of what?"

"Months of being overlooked while you figure out what your real power is." Drogan's eyes narrowed. "You broke through early. Good. Now prove it wasn't an accident. Show me you belong at Tempered before anyone asks how you got there."

Roan picked up the vial. The glass was cool against his palm.

"Three weeks," Drogan said. "The junior rankings refresh on the first. That's when every ambitious student in this institute makes their move. If you want to survive the culling, you need a plan before then."

He left without waiting for acknowledgment.

Roan held the vial up to the torchlight. Academy-issued, his ass. This was Drogan's personal stock, probably cooked in an off-hours workshop and logged under a dead student's name. If an unannounced sensor sweep swept the training wing tomorrow, or if an instructor with better sensors took an interest in his sudden compliance, the trail led straight to both of them. He pocketed the vial anyway. The cost of getting caught was high. The cost of the ranking refresh without it was death. The arithmetic resolved cleanly.

Right.

Three weeks to build a strategy that accounted for visibility and resources, plus enemies he couldn't fully map yet. The ledger was incomplete, too many variables, too many unknown inputs. But the framework was familiar. He'd done this before, in smaller ways, when his father was still alive and the Vance name carried weight instead of a target.

The difference now was that the weight was heavier, and the target was his.

- break -

The afternoon sessions were split between combat drills and theory lectures. Roan chose combat drills, specifically, the intermediate sparring block that attracted Tempered students from across the cohort. The theory lectures would've been safer, but safety wasn't the objective. The objective was information.

He arrived at the training grounds early, positioning himself in the observation alcove where he could watch the current sparring matches without being immediately visible. The space was carved into the stone walls of the main arena, elevated and shadowed, designed for instructors to assess without interrupting. Most students never used it. Most students weren't trying to avoid being assessed.

From his vantage point, he catalogued the current participants.

Four Tempered students. Three of them he recognized from the monthly rankings: Maren Voss, Kell Ashton, and Drew Colton. All mid-tier, all competing for the same pool of advancement slots. The fourth was unfamiliar, tall, red-haired, moving with the deliberate economy of someone who'd been in real fights rather than just academy matches.

A sparring ring opened. Maren stepped out, blood on her lip, having just finished a bout. She scanned the area for an opponent, her eyes passing over the observation alcove,

,and stopped.

Roan felt the weight of her gaze. She hadn't seen him but she'd sensed something. Vitalis output was low enough to ignore in the shadows, but the Masking Agent wouldn't hide everything. Body language. The way he held himself. The micro-hesitations that marked someone either far above or far below the level they were pretending to be.

She turned away. Found another partner.

Not yet. But soon.

His phone buzzed. A message from Brendt.

*Where are you? Training hall's packed today. Something about the rankings refresh.*

*I'm observing.*

*From where?*

*Overhead.*

A pause. Then: *That's creepy. Also, smart. Want company?*

Roan considered. Brendt was useful as a second set of eyes, but he was also conspicuous, a known associate made Roan conspicuous by association. The calculus wasn't favorable.

*Stay where you are. Look normal.*

*Define "normal."*

*Like you're not looking for me.*

*You mean "like I usually do."*

Roan put the phone away. Brendt's concept of discretion was, charitably, a work in progress. But he'd manage. Brendt always managed, somehow, despite every indicator suggesting otherwise.

The unfamiliar red-haired student finished his bout. Quick win, two strikes, one guard break, opponent submitted. The crowd around the ring murmured. Someone called out a name: "Garrett, you've been holding out on us." The student smiled without showing teeth and walked toward the equipment area.

Garrett. Not in the top twenty. Not in any of the current sparring rotations. He'd positioned himself to be seen, but not watched. Strategic visibility.

Roan kept that. Another variable. Another potential threat or resource.

He mapped the floor like a tactical chart. Pairings repeated—Maren and Kell circled each other with rehearsed courtesy. Drew Colton collected wins against opponents who weren't trying. The real architecture lived in the boundaries. The top five held a loose court near the center. Cyrus Ashford wasn't present—Roan checked twice, and the absence was itself a data point. Cyrus didn't avoid things. He only appeared when he had an advantage to leverage.

He was still cataloguing exit routes when a voice came from behind him.

"You hide well."

He turned. The red-haired student. Garrett, was leaning against the alcove entrance, arms crossed, head tilted. His Vitalis readings were moderate. High Tempered, maybe, or low Hardened trying to look less than he was.

"I'm not hiding," Roan said. "I'm watching."

"Those aren't mutually exclusive." Garrett's accent was northern. Greycross, maybe, or one of the border territories. His tone carried a practiced neutrality, the kind that came from learning not to reveal regional affiliations in mixed crowds.

"What do you want?"

Garrett's expression shifted slightly, surprise, maybe, or calculation. He'd expected deflection. The standard response. "I want to know why someone who can fight is sitting in the observer's alcove instead of making a name for himself in the ring."

"Making a name yourself is also an option."

"I have a name." Garrett pushed off the wall, taking a single step closer. "I'm just looking for someone who won't report it to House Ashford the moment I turn around."

There it was. The real question underneath the casual approach.

Roan measured the distance between them. Four meters. Garrett hadn't positioned himself for combat, he was still in conversation stance, but his weight was balanced, his hands loose. Trained instincts.

"You think I'm an Ashford informant."

"I think a lot of people are Ashford informants. The Ashfords make it worth someone's while."

"They make it worth everyone's while if the price is right." Roan kept his voice even. "That's not news. That's just economics."

"Then we're agreed. Economics." Garrett's eyes searched his face. "So the question is: which economics are you playing? Ashford's, or your own?"

The calculation ran fast. Garrett was offering a lateral move, a potential non-enemy in a field crowded with them. But the offer itself was a probe. Answer wrong and he became either a threat to be neutralized or a tool to be leveraged.

"I'm playing my own," Roan said. "That's not a declaration of alliance. It's a statement of fact. If you want to know what that means for you, figure out what you're offering first."

Garrett stared at him. Then, slowly, the corner of his mouth twitched.

"Huh."

"What?"

"Nothing. Just—" He shook his head. "Most people either jump at an offer like that or run from it. You did neither. You made me define my own terms."

"Someone has to."

"Fair enough." Garrett stepped back, creating space. "I'll think about it. You do the same."

He left without another word.

Roan watched him go, noting the route, the pace, the way he avoided the main exit in favor of a side passage that led toward the dormitories. Northern training. Efficient. No wasted motion.

Another variable. More incomplete math.

- break -

The evening class was theory. Battle Dynamics, a course Roan usually tolerated because it involved minimal physical exertion and maximum logical analysis. Professor Aldwood spoke in measured tones about force vectors and momentum transfer, pausing occasionally to sketch diagrams on the transmutation board. The room was half-full, students scattered across the tiered seating in loose clusters of alliance and proximity.

Roan took a seat in the back corner. Brendt was there before him. Brendt was always there before him, despite claiming to run late every morning, and immediately leaned over.

"Saw you talking to the red-head," Brendt muttered. "New friend?"

"Unknown quantity."

"That's a 'no.'"

"It's a 'not yet.'"

Brendt's eyebrows rose. "You have a 'not yet' for everyone. What's his deal?"

"Claims he's not Ashford-aligned. Could be true or could be a trap." Roan opened his notes, scanning the previous lecture's material. "I've had six months to map the obvious threats. Garrett is the first non-obvious one I've encountered since the semester started."

"Non-obvious how?"

"He doesn't fit the pattern." Roan tapped his pen against the page. "Ashford-aligned students advertise it, subtle signals, language markers, training affiliations. Garrett has none of those. He fights like he's from the border territories. His Vitalis reads high but not peak. He positioned himself for visibility today, but he's not angling for rank advancement. He's *looking*."

"For what?"

"For someone who isn't looking back. Yet." Roan's pen stilled. "Or he's bait. Set up to find students who answer that question honestly."

Brendt was quiet for a moment. Then: "That's paranoid."

"That's survival."

"It's also lonely."

Roan didn't respond. The lecture resumed, Professor Aldwood moving into a discussion of tactical positioning and resource allocation. Standard material. Roan's attention drifted, running probability calculations on the new data points.

Garrett's offer was either genuine or staged. The cost of assuming genuine was exposure, the risk of being tracked, tagged, mapped. The cost of assuming staged was the same exposure, just faster and more directed. Either way, the balance pointed to the same conclusion: engage cautiously, extract information, maintain deniability.

The only variable he couldn't account for was intent. Garrett wanted something. Everyone wanted something. But the specific shape of that want, the thing he'd pursue if his primary angle failed, remained unclear.

Clarity would come. It always did, given enough observation.

The question was whether he had enough time before the ranking refresh.

- break -

Night training was optional, but Roan never skipped it.

The small private room behind the equipment bay was technically reserved for advanced students and upper-year seniors. Drogan had arranged his access two months ago, citing "developmental potential" and a forged signature on the authorization form. The room was barely larger than a closet, mat on the floor, weighted dummy on the wall, ventilation grate in the ceiling, but it was private, and privacy was the rarest commodity in a combat institute.

He ran the Sevenfold Bleed sequence. First form. The strike pattern was embedded in muscle memory now, the seven-point split requiring no conscious navigation. He moved through the sequence three times, watching the system notifications scroll in the corner of his vision.

[Sidewound absorbed.]

[Conversion successful: +94 Vitalis.]

[Daily capacity: 1/4.]

Ninety-four points. Slightly better than this morning, his body was adapting, finding efficiency in the conversion process. He noted the increase and filed it for future analysis. The rate would plateau eventually, limited by his current tier and the natural ceiling of the Codex Shard's processing capacity. But until then, every session added to the cumulative total.

Two more conversions available. He'd used them sparingly since the breakthrough, hoarding the capacity for emergencies. The ranking refresh would create exactly the situation that justified hoarding, but it would also create opportunities, if he was patient enough to wait for them.

His phone buzzed. Another message from Brendt.

*Ashford's recruiting. New offers going out tonight. Heard they're targeting mid-tier Tempered students, specifically the ones who haven't picked sides yet.*

Roan stared at the screen. Mid-tier, non-aligned. That was exactly the category he'd been positioning himself in for months. The timing wasn't coincidental. Someone had noticed something, his absence from the public rankings, maybe, or Garrett's conversation had been observed and reported, or Drogan's early-morning visit had been tracked by Ashford's intelligence network.

The offer would come within days. Maybe tonight.

He had to decide, before it arrived, whether to accept, deflect, or provoke.

Accept: immediate safety, long term binding. Ashford protection came with Ashford strings. He'd seen what happened to students who accepted and then tried to leave, the ones who "retired" to family estates, the ones who transferred to branch institutes, the ones who stopped appearing in rankings without explanation.

Deflect: buy time, maintain deniability, hope the offer wasn't genuine. But deflection required a credible excuse, and credible excuses were hard to manufacture under pressure.

Provoke: escalate, force a response, use the confrontation to extract information about Ashford's real intentions. But provocation risked making him a target rather than asset.

The third option had the highest upside and the highest cost. The first had the lowest cost and the highest long term debt. The second was the coward's path, technically viable, but only if he was willing to run the readout every time a new offer arrived.

Roan set the phone down. Looked at the weighted dummy. Looked at his hands.

The columns didn't lie. But they also didn't tell him what he wanted.

He ran the sequence again. Fourth time. Fifth.

[Sidewound absorbed.]

[Conversion successful: +91 Vitalis.]

[Daily capacity: 0/4. — A.V.]

Capacity depleted. He was out of conversions for the day, standard reset would occur at dawn, assuming no interruptions. He was also out of time for deliberation. The ranking refresh would happen in eighteen days, not three weeks. The message had been wrong, or deliberately misleading, or both.

Someone was accelerating the timeline.

The question was who, and why.

His phone buzzed again. Unknown number.

*Roan Vance. House Ashford requests your presence at the Eastern Pavilion, 9 PM tonight. Attendance is appreciated. C.A.*

C.A. Cyrus Ashford.

The invitation was polite. The implication wasn't.

Roan looked at the clock on the wall. 8:47 PM.

Thirteen minutes to decide whether to walk into a potential trap, or to confirm his status as an enemy before he was ready.

The arithmetic pointed toward attendance. Refusal would be interpreted as hostility, which would escalate into action, which would reduce his options to survival and evasion. Acceptance gave him time, time to assess, time to calculate, time to find the leverage that turned a trap into an opportunity.

But time wasn't the same as advantage. And Cyrus Ashford wasn't someone who invited enemies to tea.

Unless he wanted them to know they were outmatched.

Roan stood. Adjusted his training clothes. Checked the small blade he kept in his left sleeve, not for use, but for the weight of it, the reminder that he had options beyond the ones presented.

He had thirteen minutes. Not enough to prepare, but enough to choose.

The Eastern Pavilion was a thirty-second walk from the training room. He could be there in eight minutes if he moved fast, leaving five minutes to observe the location before entering.

Or he could arrive exactly on time, demonstrating neither eagerness nor reluctance, letting Cyrus make the first move while he gathered data on who else would be present.

The latter was the better choice. The former was the safer one.

He made his decision, and then he made himself move.

The night air was cool as he stepped outside. The institute's towers rose against the sky, lights scattered like stars in the wrong configuration. Somewhere to the east, Cyrus Ashford was waiting. Somewhere in the walls, eyes were watching.

Roan walked. Not fast, not slow. Just forward.

The math was incomplete. The odds were unfavorable. The timing was almost certainly wrong.

But he hadn't survived this long by running from the columns.

He walked toward the Pavilion, and the decision he hadn't made yet.
