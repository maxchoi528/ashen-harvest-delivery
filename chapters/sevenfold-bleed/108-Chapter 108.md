Chapter 108: The Sanctum Note

The water in the basin had gone still. Not frozen, still. As if someone had pressed pause on the world and forgotten to unpause it.

Roan stood three feet back, Sidewound held in a neutral grip, and watched House Greel's body twitch on the stone floor of the Veyrn Sanctum. The old man's chest still rose and fell. Barely. Each breath came with a sound like paper tearing, and the air around him shimmered with a faint, sickly luminescence that had nothing to do with the Sanctum's rune-lit walls.

The Northern Crown was still alive. Barely conscious. And the black iron anchor beneath Roan's shirt was burning against his sternum like a coal pulled fresh from a forge.

*Not good.*

He ran the balance. Three options, clean as arithmetic. One: finish House Greel now while the man couldn't fight back. Clean kill, no witness complications, House Greel's house collapses without its patriarch and the Greel grip on the Summit loosens. Two: take him alive. Drag the most powerful political asset in the Northern Territories back to Ironholm and figure out the optics later. Three: walk away and let the Sanctum finish what the lightning hadn't.

None of the options were simple. That was the problem with being right. You could calculate a perfect strike, a perfect counter, a perfect kill, and still end up with a situation that didn't resolve cleanly because people weren't equations. House Greel was a living treaty. A living war. Kill him and every Greel-aligned house would demand satisfaction. Keep him and the same houses would spend the next decade trying to leverage his captivity for maximum political gain.

And walking away was its own kind of problem. House Greel had seen him here. Had seen him use the Sevenfold Bleed, *the* Sevenfold Bleed, a technique the Greels had been trying to recreate for decades. Had seen him carry a Codex Shard embedded in his spine.

That last part was the real issue.

Roan sheathed Sidewound. The sound of steel sliding home was very loud in the Sanctum's silence.

"You're going to have to make a decision." He said it to the room, to the walls, to the ghost of his father whose presence he could feel pressing faintly against the edges of his awareness. "And I'd appreciate it if you could stop gloating long enough to be useful."

The Shard pulsed. Warm. Almost amused.

*A decision? I thought you already made it. You're standing here talking to me instead of cutting his throat. That's your answer, kid.*

"That's not an answer. That's a hesitation."

*Hesitation is also a decision. The worst kind. The kind you have to live with.*

House Greel coughed. A wet, terrible sound. He rolled onto his side and retched blood onto the ancient stone, and the shimmer around him flickered, that sickly light pulsing brighter for a moment before dimming again. His eyes opened. Clouded. Searching. They found Roan and didn't move.

"You," House Greel rasped. The word came out like gravel scraping bone. "You're the boy. The Vance boy."

Roan didn't answer. He crouched instead, bringing himself to House Greel's eye level, and studied the old man's face without blinking. The Greel patriarch looked like a man who had been carved from ice and left too long in the sun, still recognizable as what he'd been, but melting at the edges. His left arm hung at an angle that suggested the shoulder had separated. His Vitalis signature, that monstrous, world-shaping pressure that Roan had felt from across the battlefield, had collapsed down to something almost readable. Maybe two thousand, three thousand. A shadow of a sovereign.

The shimmer around him pulsed again. Something was keeping him alive. Not Vitalis. Not any technique Roan recognized. The Sanctum's runes, maybe. Old Pell had mentioned the Veyrn Sanctum had preservation properties, that the altar could sustain life in extremis.

Or the Shard itself. The Codex Shard was reacting to House Greel the way a compass reacted to north — and violently, with purpose.

*Your father made him once,* the Shard whispered. *In this room. Same floor. Same blood. Ask him what happened.*

Roan's jaw tightened. He didn't ask.

"You know what this is." House Greel's voice was clearer now. Surviving had always been the Greel specialty. The old man lifted his right hand, the one that still worked, and pointed a shaking finger at the black iron anchor beneath Roan's shirt. "That object. I've seen drawings. Schematics. The Conclave spent forty years trying to—" He stopped. Coughed. "Forty years."

"I don't care what the Conclave spent."

"You should." House Greel smiled. It was a terrible smile, more blood than teeth. "You think you're the only one who knows about Inversion, boy? You think your father invented this? The Conclave has *records*. Fragments. Enough to know that the Inversion archive left behind more than just a graveyard of techniques. We've been looking for you for seventy years." His eyes glittered. "And you've been wearing one around your neck this whole time."

Roan absorbed that. Filed it. The arithmetic updated: the Greels didn't have a Codex Shard, but they had *information*. Enough to know what they were looking for. Enough to have been hunting.

"Here." House Greel's voice was barely a whisper now, but his eyes were sharp. Sharper than his body had any right to be. "Let me die in peace and I'll tell you where the others are. All of them. Every lock the Invisible erected."

It was almost perfectly tempting. A trade: the location of the archive's surviving anchors in exchange for a clean death. No political fallout. No body to dispose of. No leverage that could be turned against him. Just information and an ending.

The Codex Shard pulsed inside his spine, faster now. Urgent.

*Don't.*

"I'm not negotiating with a man who had my father killed," Roan said. His voice was flat. He watched House Greel's face for the tell, the micro-expression that would confirm or deny. "You're going to tell me about the anchors because you're going to tell me everything, or you're going to die here and I take whatever's left in your head apart piece by piece until I find it anyway."

House Greel laughed. It turned into a cough. Then a wheeze. Then silence.

"You're your father's son," he said, when he could breathe again. "He said the same thing. Right there. On that altar." His eyes drifted to the raised stone platform at the center of the Sanctum, the same platform Roan had stood on when he'd first activated the Sevenfold Bleed. "He was standing exactly where you're standing now. And I was exactly where I am. And he made me the same offer."

The Sanctum's runes flared. The shimmer around House Greel intensified.

Roan stood. Stepped back. His hand found Sidewound's hilt.

"I don't make offers," Roan said. "I state terms."

"Terms." House Greel tasted the word. "Fine. Then hear mine." He pushed himself to sitting, ignoring the way his broken shoulder screamed, ignoring the blood running from his temple. The shimmer around him pulsed in time with his heartbeat — or no. In time with something else. Something in the walls. Something in the runes. "The Veyrn Sanctum isn't just a training ground. It's a *lock*. Your father came here to study it. To understand what the Invisible buried here." The old man's bloody smile returned. "He succeeded. He also found it."

The temperature in the Sanctum dropped. Not gradually — all at once, like someone had opened a door to a place where temperature didn't apply.

Roan felt it in his spine first. Not the Codex Shard — the Shard was silent now, cold in a way it hadn't been since the night Aldric died. He felt it in the bone, a deep-freeze ache that spread outward from his seventh vertebra.

"What did he find?"

"Ask the Shard." House Greel slumped back against the wall
