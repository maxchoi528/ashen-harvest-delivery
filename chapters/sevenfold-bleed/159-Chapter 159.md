Chapter 159: The Medical Wing

Roan didn't fall.

That was the important part.

Falling would have forced everyone else to react, and everyone else was already doing something useful or pretending to. Drogan was sealing the exit corridor with a temporary ward line that looked like it had been made from anger and old habits. Ines was stripping the dead mercenaries of anything useful, incriminating, or likely to explode. Pellan was backing up his recording onto a second crystal while muttering prayers to three different data integrity standards. Brendt was trying to remain conscious and failing in a slow, stubborn way that made Roan want to hit him and stabilize him in equal measure.

So Roan stayed upright.

The pain from Third Refusal didn't strike like a wound. It audited him.

Seven micro-wounds, each smaller than a fingernail, each placed in lines his body had accepted as temporary lies. Now the lies demanded recognition. The one under his lung line became a sharp hitch in breath. The one beneath the liver became heat. The stress thread behind his right eye became a pulsing white point that made the world tilt if he looked too quickly to the left. Four others woke along the Sevenfold pathways and began arguing about whether movement should remain possible.

[Uncatalogued technique terminated.]

[Structural debt manifesting.]

[Recommended action: rest, nutrition, medical evaluation, avoiding further stupidity.]

Roan blinked once.

The prompt remained.

The Shard had inherited Aldric's tone. That was inconvenient evidence against several comfortable theories about dead fathers and impartial systems.

"Roan," Drogan said without turning around.

"Functional."

"That's not what I asked."

"It answers the useful part."

Drogan swore softly and finished the ward line.

A heavy impact struck the far side of the sealed corridor a second later. The temporary ward flared yellow. Something had tested the path Iris used and found it closed. Whether that something belonged to Iris, Greel, or the Rift itself remained unclear.

Unclear didn't mean irrelevant.

"Time," Roan said.

Ines looked up from Valech's corpse. "Five minutes before that ward fails if they press. Two if they use charges. Less if the Rift decides geometry is a hobby."

"Medical evacuation?"

"Brendt shouldn't be moved. Therefore we move him immediately, because this location is indefensible and smells like murder." She removed a ring from the dead captain's finger and dropped it into a lead-lined pouch. "Also this is a Greel command seal. Counterfeit or deniable, but useful."

"Keep it sealed."

"I know."

"Do you?"

She looked offended. "I steal professionally."

"You've been exposed to temptation."

"Also professionally."

Brendt's eyes opened a sliver. "Are we alive?"

"Provisionally," Roan said.

"That sounds like one of your contracts."

"Better terms."

"Doubtful."

His voice was thin. Too thin. The stabilizer had done enough to keep him from dying in the Rift corridor, but not enough to reverse blood loss and shock, plus the damage caused by being carried through terrain that had considered Euclidean space optional. His shoulder was sealed under layers of medical foam. His ribs had been braced. One side of his abdomen glowed faintly where Ines's emergency graft patch kept internal bleeding from becoming external failure.

Roan had spent his last inversion quota keeping Brendt alive.

He didn't regret it.

That was the problem.

Regret had uses. Regret identified bad calculations and marked them for correction. Its absence meant either the decision had been correct or Roan was becoming less capable of noticing when efficiency had been replaced by preference.

He had chosen Brendt over killing the Hollowkin sovereign.

Then he had created Third Refusal to kill two Hardened without quota.

Both decisions had worked.

Successful bad habits were the most dangerous kind.

Drogan crossed the room and crouched beside Brendt. His hands moved with brutal gentleness, checking straps, seals, circulation. "We move in ninety seconds. Pellan, can you open the surface tram access?"

Pellan looked up from the recorder. He was a thin man in a gray transit engineer's coat, face chalky from several hours of realizing his retirement plan didn't include Greel mercenaries. "Not directly. The old tram shaft is locked from the municipal side. But there's an inspection lift. Manual. Slow. Also not rated for stretchers, armed fugitives, or whatever category all of you occupy."

"Open it," Drogan said.

"That wasn't a yes. That was a structural warning."

"Open it anyway."

Pellan looked at Roan, perhaps expecting the younger, bloodier person to be less unreasonable.

Roan said, "If it breaks, we reduce load by dropping corpses first. Then nonessential gear. Then opinions."

Pellan stared.

Brendt whispered, "Please classify me before opinions."

"You're expensive cargo."

"Friendship."

"Insurance category."

"Still counts."

Pellan made a sound somewhere between a laugh and despair, then hurried toward the service panel beside the old rail doors.

Drogan stood. "You aren't carrying anyone."

Roan had expected that. "I can carry—"

"No."

"You are injured."

"And you are held together by spite and bad theory."

"Spite has performed consistently."

"Theory hasn't been reviewed."

"Neither has your teaching license."

Drogan's stare suggested the old man was considering whether this was the moment to test if Forged skulls made better sounds against concrete.

Ines saved him the trouble. "I can rig the stretcher to the pack frame. Brendt's weight splits between me and Drogan. Roan takes rear guard because he will ignore any other assignment and because his new suicidal trick works better when people misjudge him."

"I dislike being summarized accurately," Roan said.

"Then become less predictable."

"Later."

They moved.

Not well.

Well was unavailable. They moved with brutal compromise that got people out of bad places: straps tightened too hard, gear abandoned without ceremony, bodies searched only once, evidence split between pouches so one lucky shot couldn't erase everything. Roan took Valech's payment ledger, the memory solvent ampoule, two intact ward-pistol cartridges, and the dead captain's left glove because the inner lining carried pressure imprints that might identify whoever had issued the command seal.

Ines looked approving when she noticed the glove.

Roan didn't need her approval.

He pocketed it anyway, metaphorically. Assets had categories.

The first charge hit the ward line as they reached the inspection lift.

Yellow light flashed behind them. Concrete dust shook from the ceiling.

"Two minutes was generous," Ines said.

Pellan slapped the manual release. The lift doors opened halfway, jammed, then opened another foot after Drogan kicked them. Inside waited a metal platform with railings, old cables, and a posted weight limit that had been optimistic even before everyone involved became armed and traumatized.

Brendt looked at the sign. "We exceed that."

"By equipment weight," Ines said.

"That isn't better."

"It means equipment can be dropped."

"I am glad my organs aren't equipment."

Roan glanced at the medical stabilizer. "Some are currently rented."

"I hate that I know what you mean."

They loaded Brendt first. Drogan and Ines braced the stretcher frame. Pellan took the control lever. Roan remained outside until the second charge struck the ward.

The corridor flashed white.

The temporary ward cracked.

Beyond it, shapes moved.

Not mercenaries. Too low. Too many joints.

The Rift had sent collectors after the noise.

Of course it had.

"Roan," Drogan said.

The lift began to rise.

Roan stepped backward onto the platform as the floor moved under him. The doors didn't close. Manual lift. Open shaft. Excellent for ventilation, poor for surviving pursuit.

A Husk forced its head through the failing ward line and opened a mouth full of layered teeth.

Roan threw one of the ward-pistol cartridges.

Not fired. Thrown.

It bounced once, rolled under the Husk's jaw, and detonated when the collapsing ward sent a feedback pulse through the corridor.

The blast was small and blue, plus vicious. It didn't kill the Husk. It removed enough of the front wall to bury the creature under municipal concrete, which was almost as useful and less expensive.

Pellan stared at him. "Those cartridges aren't impact stable."

"I noticed."

"You noticed before throwing it?"

"During."

"That's worse."

The lift groaned upward through darkness.

Roan stood at the open edge, Sidewound in hand, watching the shaft below. His vision pulsed with the stress thread behind his eye. Every vibration in the platform traveled into his ribs and informed him that Third Refusal had a repayment schedule.

The first Husk entered the shaft two levels below, crawling along the wall.

Then another.

Then a third.

Brendt lifted his head. "How many?"

"Three visible," Roan said.

"Visible is doing work in that sentence."

"Yes."

Drogan shifted, but Roan held up one hand. "Don't move the stretcher."

"You have no quota."

"Correct."

"Your new form nearly dropped you."

"Correct."

"Then use your brain."

Roan looked down at the shaft.

The Husks climbed fast, claws punching into old concrete, teeth clicking in the one-three-one pattern that had followed them since the city. Not random. Pack command echo, even without the sovereign nearby. The coordination persisted. Either the sovereign had survived and remained linked, or the Hive pattern was more distributed than Drogan's briefing admitted.

Useful and bad.

Roan counted shaft supports.

Four visible crossbeams. Two rusted. One cracked. Lift cable running along the western rail. Emergency brake housing above. Manual counterweight chain to the left. The Husks climbed the eastern wall because the concrete there had more grip and less old oil.

He didn't need to kill them.

He needed to alter the climb.

"Pellan," Roan said. "When I say stop, stop the lift. Not before."

Pellan swallowed. "Stopping between floors voids safety compliance."

"So does being eaten."

"Fair."

Roan waited until the first Husk reached the cracked crossbeam.

"Stop."

The lift jerked.

Everyone swore except Drogan, who saved it for later.

The sudden halt made the counterweight chain snap taut. Roan cut it with Sidewound.

The chain screamed through its housing.

The counterweight dropped.

Not onto the Husks. That would have required luck, and luck was an unreliable employee. It dropped along the western rail, smashing through two auxiliary brackets and yanking the emergency brake arm sideways. The lift lurched, tilted three degrees, and locked in place.

Below, the cracked crossbeam took the shifted load.

It broke.

The eastern wall section peeled away.

Three Husks lost their grip and fell with several tons of concrete.

The sound went on longer than expected.

Pellan's face had gone gray. "We are now stuck."

"Temporarily," Roan said.

"You cut the counterweight chain."

"Yes."

"That's the opposite of temporary."

Drogan looked up at the emergency ladder running along the inside of the shaft. "We climb."

Brendt closed his eyes. "I miss being unconscious."

"You can be unconscious upstairs," Roan said.

"Motivational."

The climb was slow and miserable, plus technically successful.

Drogan went first with a rope. Ines followed, then they hauled Brendt's frame level by level while Pellan operated emergency locks and apologized to the lift as if machinery cared. Roan took rear guard. Twice, something clicked in the darkness below. Twice, nothing reached them.

By the time they forced open the service hatch into the sealed station above, Roan's vision had narrowed to a tunnel around the next useful action.

Open hatch.

Scan platform.

No immediate hostiles.

Move Brendt.

Secure evidence.

Close hatch.

Break locking mechanism.

Pellan collapsed onto the platform and began laughing quietly. Not healthy laughter. Stress leaving through a poorly chosen exit.

Drogan ignored him and lifted Brendt again.

The station above smelled like dust and rain, plus human infrastructure. After the Rift, it was almost offensive in its normality. Emergency lights flickered along the old rail line. The sealed municipal doors stood forty meters ahead.

Between them and the doors stood Iris Vell.

Alone.

No mercenaries. No cane blade drawn. One hand pressed to a bleeding cut on her cheek, the other holding a small data crystal.

Everyone stopped.

Roan didn't raise Sidewound. He didn't lower it either.

"This isn't seventy-two hours," he said.

Iris looked past him, toward Brendt on the stretcher. Something moved behind her eyes and vanished.

"Greel already sent a recall ping," she said. "Valech's death triggered it. If I go dark for seventy-two hours, they cut every channel I have. If I answer now, I can lie once before they decide which lie to believe."

She held out the crystal.

"This is the brief the Vell patriarch gave me. Not the Greel one. Mine. Thirty-six hours early."

Roan didn't take it immediately.

"Why?"

Her mouth tightened. "Because Valech had memory solvent."

"That explains anger. Not cooperation."

"Because he had enough for two doses."

Silence.

Pellan stopped laughing.

Iris's fingers whitened around the crystal. "One for you. One for me."

Roan took the crystal.

Their fingers didn't touch.

"I still meant what I said," she told him. "I will come back."

"Yes."

"Not for the Vell patriarch. Not for Greel."

"Then improve before you do."

For a second, she looked as if she might laugh. Or stab him. The two options seemed closely related.

Instead she stepped aside.

Drogan carried Brendt past her.

Ines followed, watching Iris with open interest.

Roan remained last.

Iris said, very quietly, "You're not the only one they lied to."

"No," Roan said. "But I charge interest."

He walked past her into the rain.

The rain outside the station was normal rain.

Roan took one second to appreciate that. Water fell from clouds, struck concrete, ran downhill, and didn't whisper in dead languages, form clean circles, or arrange itself into symbols from his father's murdered civilization. A low standard, perhaps, but after the Hollowed City, competence from weather deserved recognition.

Recognition didn't mean trust.

He scanned the forecourt while Drogan and Ines maneuvered Brendt's stretcher through the service door. The chain-link fence remained closed. The old municipal warnings still rattled in the wind. No visible surveillance lights. No obvious second ambush. No fresh boot prints beyond Iris's path toward the east alley and two older impressions near the loading ramp, partly washed away.

Pellan emerged last and locked the door with hands that shook around the keycard override. "I am going to file for medical leave."

"No," Roan said.

Pellan stared. Rain ran down his face. "I beg your pardon?"

"Medical leave creates a record cluster. Greel audits record clusters. You are going to report a maintenance fault, accept emergency reassignment from Headmaster Cole, and complain loudly about budget neglect to anyone who asks."

"I am?"

Drogan looked over from the stretcher. "He's right."

Pellan looked betrayed by the universe. "I had hoped the terrifying student was exaggerating."

"He does," Drogan said. "Not there."

Ines pulled a folded tarp from the abandoned guard booth, shook three dead leaves out of it, and draped it over Brendt's frame. "We need transport. Not school-branded. Not hospital-branded. Not anything that logs medical priority until we are inside campus wards."

"I have a van," Pellan said weakly.

Everyone looked at him.

He raised both hands. "It's a municipal inspection van. It smells like rust and old lunches. It has no medical equipment and the rear suspension is questionable."

"Perfect," Roan said.

"That's the second time today someone has found my professional inadequacy useful. I dislike the trend."

They took the van.

It waited under the loading awning, squat and beige, plus aggressively forgettable. Pellan drove because the van's ignition required a municipal biometric strip and, according to him, "a particular emotional disappointment." Drogan sat beside him with a blade hidden under his coat. Ines and Roan rode in the back with Brendt.

The rear smelled exactly as advertised.

Brendt woke once when the van hit a pothole. His eyes opened, unfocused.

"Are we dead?" he whispered.

"Municipal transport," Roan said.

"Worse."

Then he passed out again.

Ines checked the graft patch and didn't smile. That told Roan more than any reassuring sentence would have. The patch was holding, but holding wasn't healing. It bought time in the same way a locked door bought time against a fire.

"How long?" he asked.

"If traffic is kind, he reaches Spire surgery alive. If traffic is ordinary, we improvise. If someone hits us before the medical wing, I will be very upset."

"Can you keep him stable through impact?"

"Define impact."

"No."

"Then no."

Roan looked through the rear window. Ironholm blurred past in wet lines: warehouses, rail pylons, shuttered shops, the distant black spear of the Spire district. People went about their evening beneath umbrellas and cheap ward-slickers, unaware that an old war had just exhaled under their city. He had once considered ignorance wasteful. Now he revised the category. Ignorance was infrastructure. It allowed shops to open, trams to run, mothers to send daughters to school, and children to complain about shoes without knowing which founders had built their comfort on erased worlds.

Useful. Fragile. Often deliberately maintained.

Mira couldn't know.

Lyra absolutely couldn't know.

He took out his notebook despite the van's sway and wrote with his left hand because the right still bled under the glove.

Immediate priorities:

1. Brendt surgery.

2. Evidence duplicated under null conditions.

3. Iris channel monitored, not trusted.

4. Cole contacted before Bureau.

5. Drogan debriefed while tired.

6. Ines paid enough to remain offended but cooperative.

7. Mother and Lyra cover story.

He paused, then added:

8. Third Refusal review before reuse.

Then, after a longer pause:

9. Don't tell Brendt item 1 included him as expenditure.

Ines read upside down because of course she did. "Your list is missing sleep."

"Sleep doesn't need ink."

"It does with you."

"Noted."

"You didn't write it."

"I noted it internally."

"That means you ignored it."

Roan closed the notebook. "Your professional concern is moving."

"It isn't concern. If you collapse before paying me, collecting becomes complicated."

"Better."

"I thought you'd appreciate that framing."

The route to the Spire district should have taken twelve minutes.

It took nineteen because Ironholm traffic had the moral imagination of a tax office. Twice, the van stopped behind delivery rigs whose drivers seemed convinced hazard lights converted inconvenience into law. Once, a public tram stalled across an intersection and forced Pellan to reverse through an alley lined with restaurant vents and surveillance cameras. Roan memorized each camera position, each reflective window, each pedestrian who looked at the van longer than boredom required.

Three possible tails appeared in the first ten minutes.

The first was a courier bike with a cracked blue fender. It followed through two turns, then cut toward the river after Pellan deliberately missed a light and swore at a bus. Coincidence or bad tradecraft.

The second was a black sedan that kept two vehicles back and never changed lanes. Drogan noticed it at the same time Roan did. He lowered the passenger window, let one hand rest outside with a throwing knife visible between two fingers, and the sedan discovered another destination.

The third was a woman under a yellow umbrella standing at a tram stop. She didn't follow. She didn't move. She watched the municipal van pass and touched two fingers to her ear.

Roan wrote her down.

"Friend of yours?" Ines asked.

"No."

"Enemy?"

"Not yet classified."

"You have categories for people before enemy?"

"Several."

"Do I want to know mine?"

"No."

"Rude."

"Accurate."

Brendt's monitor gave three quick warning chirps as the van hit another rough patch. Ines braced the frame with one knee and adjusted the graft regulator. Her face remained calm, but two fingers had gone white around the valve.

"Pellan," she called, "if you find another pothole, I will install it in you."

Pellan's voice floated back, strained. "Municipal road quality is outside my department."

"So is being alive if you keep driving like this."

The van became smoother.

Roan approved of clear incentives.

When the Spire towers finally rose between the warehouse roofs, black and cold against the rain, Roan felt the Vance Pendant cool again. Not Rift-cold. Recognition-cold. The campus wards were tasting the things he carried: Shard, pendant, Sidewound, Black Rite residue, Greel seal, memory solvent, Iris's crystal, Valech's glove, and one newly invented technique still trying to become a medical incident.

Too much evidence on one body.

He redistributed two pouches to Ines before the gate.

She took them without comment. That, from her, was practically a speech.

The van passed through Black Spire's outer service gate eleven minutes later. The guard opened his mouth to ask a question, saw Drogan in the passenger seat, saw Roan through the rear window, saw the covered stretcher, and decided paperwork had a survival instinct.

The gate rose.

No questions.

A second guard inside the service arch glanced at the tarp-covered stretcher, then at the rainwater and blood dripping from the van's rear bumper. His mouth moved around a question that didn't make it to air. Good. Either Cole had chosen staff with survival instincts, or Drogan's reputation was finally producing dividends.

Roan made a note to determine which.

The van stopped behind the Spire's medical wing, under an awning designed for deliveries of a less complicated kind. Pellan killed the engine and sat for a moment with both hands on the wheel, breathing.

"I will never speak of this," he said.

"Correct," Roan said.

"If asked, I was recalibrating drainage sensors."

"Flood risk in section seven."

Pellan turned. "You know the municipal flood code?"

"I read municipal garbage schedules."

"... why?"

"Dead drops in discarded correspondence. Also, it's useful when assessing operational tempo in low-income districts. The garbage trucks stop running before holidays. The surveillance patterns shift."

Pellan stared. "You are a very strange student."

"I am a very strange client. There is a difference."

Drogan opened the rear doors. The medical wing's service entrance lit automatically, a soft yellow glow against the wet dark. A nurse was already waiting—not a random night-shift picket. Marleen Voss, the Spire's senior surgical lead, someone who had seen enough Rift injuries to stop being impressed by volumes of blood.

She looked at Brendt on the stretcher.

She looked at Roan.

She said, "The one who can't afford to die or the one who refuses to?"

"Both," Ines said.

"Helpful." Voss gestured to the doors. "Bring the big one first. The student who looks like a failed assassination attempt can wait until I have clean hands."

Roan followed the stretcher into the wing.

The inside smelled of antiseptic, battery charge residue, and the particular sterile pressure that meant the surgical armor was already cycling. Voss walked beside the stretcher, peeling layers of field dressing with quick, critical fingers.

"You stabilized this underground?" she asked Ines.

"Field graft patch. Temporary."

"Good. Ugly. But good. How long's he been compromised?"

"Four hours from initial trauma to graft."

"Rift environment for all of it?"

"Most."

Voss made a sound that was not quite approval and not quite disappointment. "He'll live. It's going to cost you the armor's regeneration cycle for the month and a favor from the surgical wing's maintenance budget."

Roan matched her pace. "That's not a cost you discuss in front of the patient."

"He's unconscious."

"He's stubborn."

Brendt's hand twitched. "I heard that." His voice was barely a thread.

"Good," Roan said. "Stay mad. It helps."

Voss looked at Roan with something that might have been professional recognition. "The head wound?"

"Ines?"

"Concussive with minor spatial bleed. I drained the pressure but the source is sealed behind a rib brace I don't have the tools to cut."

"I have the tools." Voss pushed open the double doors to the surgery suite. "You—Roan. Twelve hours. No movement, no magic, no clever plans. That technique you used left structural debt. I can see it in your gate. Literally see it. Your left leg is landing differently."

"Injured."

"Inaccurate. Injured is honest. That landing is a compensation pattern for something deeper. Don't make me operate on you because you ignored a debt schedule."

"I'll consider it."

"I'll tie you to the bed."

"I'll consider it seriously."

She didn't smile, but the corner of her mouth moved.

The doors swung shut behind the stretcher.

Roan stood in the corridor. The lights flickered. The machine behind the wall made a slow hydraulic sound as the surgical armor prepared for a long cycle.

Ines appeared beside him, towelled off the rain, and pulled a sealed packet of crackers from her pocket.

"Voss—" she started.

"Already preparing a speech."

"Ignore it?"

"Cataloguing useful parts."

Ines almost smiled. "How long?"

"Seven to nine hours."

"How long until Cole contacts you?"

Roan checked his pocket watch. The face was cracked but the hands still moved. He had bought it from Old Pell for the equivalent of two meal vouchers and a promise he wouldn't ask about the engraving on the back.

"Cole already knows we're here," he said. "The service gate logs his office. The surgical wing's resource allocation board notifies him. He'll give us enough time to stabilize Brendt and clean the evidence trail before he summons me to a room without recording devices."

"You're describing your headmaster like an intelligence asset."

"Cole is a man who has run this Spire through three administrations without changing his office furniture. That level of stability is not accidental."

"A politician."

"An institutionalist. Worse."

Ines chewed her cracker. "And what are you?"

"A liability with a growing asset base."

The next nine hours passed in segments.

The first hour: Roan sat in the waiting alcove while Ines made calls from a secured line. She spoke to three people whose voices he didn't recognize, one person who clearly thought she was someone else, and a logistics coordinator who quoted prices that made Ines laugh and hang up. The laughter was a bad sign for the person who quoted.

The second hour: Roan reviewed the evidence. The payment ledger had twenty-three entries he couldn't trace without a deeper network. The memory solvent was genuine—he broke the seal on the test strip and watched the analytical dye confirm the compound signature. The data crystal from Iris remained dark in his hand. He didn't insert it. Information that arrived before it was requested was either leverage or bait. He marked it as undetermined.

The third hour: Voss sent a junior nurse to report that Brendt's graft had taken and the internal bleeding pattern was reversing. The nurse also mentioned that Roan's own chart needed updating.

Roan updated it. He listed the three wounds that would heal on surface examination, omitted Third Refusal's structural debt, and rated his pain at a five. The nurse looked skeptical but didn't argue.

The fourth hour: Drogan arrived from wherever he had gone. He sat opposite Roan and didn't speak.

The fifth hour: Roan began the review of Third Refusal.

The technique was not an Inversion. He had created it without the Shard's assistance, working from first principles that shouldn't have been accessible to a Tempered practitioner. It used the wound structure of the Sevenfold pathways as a delivery mechanism, converting temporary damage into a force that exceeded the caster's normal output. The cost was structural debt—not a system penalty, but a physical one. Micro-wounds in the channels that would heal slowly and leave him vulnerable to future damage in the same lines.

Uncatalogued technique.

Aldric's tone in the Shard had called it that. Uncatalogued, not forbidden. The distinction mattered. It meant the Shard recognized the method but had no record of it. Either Aldric had discovered something and never logged it, or the technique was new to the system itself.

Roan wrote his analysis in the margin of the ledger.

*Third Refusal: non-inversion channel weapon. Cost: physical, local. Not systemic. Repeatable with healing cycles. Risk: predictable. Utility: high.*

He circled the notation and added a question mark.

The sixth hour: Ines dozed in the chair beside him. Her head tilted against the wall, but her hand stayed on the pouch containing the evidence. Roan didn't wake her. She had earned the rest, and he didn't trust anyone else with the bag.

The seventh hour: Voss emerged from surgery. Her gloves were off, her sleeves rolled up, her face carrying the particular hollow satisfaction of someone who had just confirmed a diagnosis that matched her skill.

"He's stable," she said. "He'll wake in a few hours. The graft will need monitoring, but the structural damage is fully repaired. He will not be combat-ready for six weeks."

"Last time he was combat-ready for approximately six hours before needing this surgery."

"That's not my problem."

"It's ours."

Voss looked at him. "Your turn."

Roan stood. "Mine is not urgent."

"I know. That's why I'm scheduling it. If I wait until you think it's urgent, I'm operating in a field tent with inadequate light. Come."

Roan followed her into the secondary suite.

The armor bed was smaller than the main surgical frame, designed for diagnostics and minor interventions. Roan sat on the edge. Voss pulled a scanner from the wall mount and ran it over his torso without asking permission.

The scanner chirped.

Voss looked at the readout.

She looked at Roan.

She looked at the readout again.

"Your deep pathways have architecture I don't recognize."

"That's the third time someone has told me that."

"It's not a compliment. This isn't a normal cultivate pattern. It's a braided structure, layered under the standard channels. It's—" She turned the scanner, recalibrated, and ran it again. "It's inactive. Vestigial. Like a foundation built before the house, then buried."

Roan considered his answer. "I inherited it."

"From who?"

"My father."

Voss put the scanner down. Her face had lost the professional distance. "I've seen this once before. In an archived surgical record from twenty years ago. The wounds were classified, but the pathway structure matched. It came in from a Spire operative who never woke up."

"Aldric Vance."

"I didn't say that."

"You didn't have to."

Voss was quiet for a long moment. Then she said: "I'm going to modify the healing sequence. Your pathways need a different calibration. The standard modules will try to flatten the braided structure. They see it as an anomaly. If I let them, they'll remove what they can't read."

"And if you modify it?"

"Then I create an architecture that only you can use. Hostile to standard systems, friendly to yours."

"That sounds like a weapon."

"It's a foundation. What you build on it is your choice."

Roan lay back on the armor bed. "Do it."

The procedure took less than two hours. Voss worked with a precision that made Roan grateful for her reputation. She didn't ask questions. She didn't make conversation. She modified the healing sequence, recalibrated the scanning depth, and let the armor run a custom cycle that Roan felt as a vibration in bones he didn't know he had.

When it finished, the structural debt from Third Refusal remained—controlled. The pain was lower. The risk of the micro-wounds widening during exertion had been reduced.

Voss pulled off her gloves. "You'll feel the architecture settle over the next few days. Don't push it until it does."

"And after?"

She looked at him. "Then you own something the Spire wasn't designed to handle."

He understood.

When Roan woke in the private medical bay, the clock on the wall showed seventy-two hours had passed. His left arm was strapped down—standard protocol for channel-adjacent surgery—and a voice recorder sat on the table beside him, red light blinking.

He didn't remember turning it on.

He didn't turn it off.

Drogan was sitting in the corner chair, untouched coffee in his hand, watching Roan with an expression that belonged on someone who had just discovered their pension was a lie.

Roan sat up slowly. The architecture moved with him.

"Brendt?"

"Stable. Awake. Bored. Asking about you every four hours."

"And Ines?"

"Processing evidence. Complaining about your payment schedule. Standard Ines."

"Campus?"

"Quiet. Cole issued a statement about a training accident in the Deep Rift. No names. No details. The Bureau accepted it." Drogan paused. "The Greel woman—Iris—she's back in their network. Her story held. She's reporting that Valech's team was lost to a Rift surge. No mention of you."

Roan absorbed that. "And the Shard's prompt about the architecture?"

Waiting.

"Your father left more than a system, Roan. The Vance Architecture isn't just a channel mutation. It's a key. I didn't know until Voss described what she found."

"A key to what?"

Drogan set down the untouched coffee. "The Sevenths bleed, but the Sceptre waits. That's what he told me. Did he tell you?"

"No."

"Then you don't know yet. The biggest lie."

"The biggest lie."

The words settled. Drogan had been holding this for years—holding it so tightly that Roan had confused silence with ignorance. The distinction mattered. He filed it under debts he hadn't known he owed.

Roan looked at the ceiling panels. "Drogan."

"Yes."

"When did you start asking the right questions?"

Drogan was quiet for a long moment. "When you started giving answers that didn't match the questions."

Roan let the statement stand.

The architecture in his deep channels hummed at a frequency only he could feel. Brendt was alive. The evidence was secure. Iris was repaying a debt she hadn't been asked to owe. And Drogan was speaking like a man who had opened a door and found a room he'd forgotten existed.

The Shard remained silent.

The Vance Pendant stayed cold.

Outside the medical bay window, the Spire rose against a dark sky that held stars Roan didn't trust but had learned to read.

Seventy-two hours.

He had seventy-two hours of recovery left before Cole called him in. Seventy-two hours to review Third Refusal, understand the architecture, and decide how much of Drogan's sudden honesty was gift and how much was warning.

The biggest lie, Aldric had written.

Roan intended to find it.
