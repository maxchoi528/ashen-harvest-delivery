Chapter 92: False Weakness

The training yard smelled of iron and sweat at six in the morning. Roan arrived before the supervisors, which was the point.

Brendt was already there.

"Cyrus is coming," Brendt said.

Roan paused his stretch. "Source?"

"Kestrel watch. He left Ashford House thirty minutes ago with two of his usual anchors. Moving toward the admin block."

New information. Brendt had been active. Roan filed it away. "He's not going to the admin block. He'll come here first. He wants to see my face when I hear the news."

"Then we have time?"

"We have minutes. Let's make them count."

Cyrus arrived in six, not the predicted five. His boots echoed against the cobbles. The morning light caught the silver threading in his collar. He looked like he'd never worked a day in his life.

"Vance." The word was polished, sharp. "Internal affairs inquiry. The drug shipment last month. The inventory logs. Your name came up."

"Which office?"

"Administrator Hall. Third floor."

"Tell them I'll be there in twenty minutes."

Cyrus's smile flickered. "They said now."

"They can say what they like." The arithmetic was simple. Twenty minutes gave him a narrow window. Narrow was enough. "I have a form to complete. Interrupting a training sequence wastes academy resources. I'm sure the inquiry appreciates efficiency."

The absence of panic threw Cyrus off balance. He'd come expecting a reaction, a crack in the facade. He got geometry instead.

"Twenty minutes." He turned, his entourage following. "Don't make it thirty."

Brendt exhaled. "That was bait. Pure intimidation."

"Yes."

"And you're definitely walking into an ambush."

"Probably."

"Then why give him any time? Why not go immediately and try to control the narrative?"

"Because going immediately means facing their ground without mine. I need a weapon." Roan checked the clock. "I have nineteen minutes. I'm going to the armory."

Brendt didn't argue. He'd learned to trust the math.

The armory back entrance was unlocked, per routine. Old Pell was cataloguing blades, his precise script contradicting the tremor in his left hand.

"Morning, young Vance. You're here early. Usually you wait until you've actually broken something."

"I need the original receipt for the Greaves shipment. Six weeks ago."

Pell's pen stopped. He set it down with deliberate care. "That's a deep pull. Information that deep has a price, Vance."

"Name it."

"The Ashford family has been sniffing around my inventory for weeks. One of their quartermasters, a man named Sutter, offered to 'update' my filing system. I told him to file a formal request. I haven't seen the request. I might need to disappear for a few weeks if this gets hot."

"Done. If the evidence traces to this room, I'll fund your absence. Cover and expenses."

Pell studied him. "You're buying an exit strategy before you've seen the evidence. That's confidence."

"It's arithmetic. The exit is cheap if I never need it. The evidence is worthless if I can't get it."

Pell nodded. "Fair." He moved to the cabinet, pulled a ledger. "This is the original. Look at the ink on your copy, then look at this. The editing page is a different batch. Darker, less absorption. The signature on my entry matches the original supplier's stamp. The file on Yew's desk doesn't."

Roan copied the data onto a scrap of paper. "Proof of replacement."

"Proof that someone replaced the page. It's not a direct accusation against Ashford."

"It doesn't have to be. I just need to make the file heavy. Too heavy for Yew to hold without dropping it."

Pell returned the ledger to its place. "You have fifteen minutes. The admin building takes four at a normal pace. Don't let them see you running."

Administrator Yew's office smelled like coffee and toner. Yew himself was thin, bespectacled, with a desk buried in file folders. He looked up, made a note on his checklist, and didn't offer a seat.

"Roan Vance. You are being investigated for discrepancies in the Greaves shipment. You received thirty-two units. The invoice shows twenty-eight. The academy wants to know where the difference went."

"I received thirty-two. I invoiced for thirty-two. The file on your desk has been replaced."

Yew's pen tapped twice. "That is a serious accusation."

"I have proof." Roan slid the armory data across the desk. "Compare the ink lot number on the original armory receipt with the lot number on the page in your file. They don't match. The signatures don't match either."

Yew examined the documents. His expression remained flat, but his hand stilled. "This implies tampering. You are accusing an academy administrator of falsifying records."

"I'm not accusing an administrator. I'm accusing a file. The file is wrong. Who put it there is secondary to the fact that the investigation is based on a forgery."

"The forensic validity of ink lot numbers is debatable."

"The signature is not." Roan leaned forward. "The signature on the replacement page matches the handwriting style of the file clerk assigned to the Ashford family accounts. It's standard academy procedure to assign clerks to major families. If the inquiry proceeds, the committee will compare handwriting samples. They will find the match. And they will ask why the Ashford clerk altered the Vance file."

Yew's hand moved toward the phone.

"If you call security," Roan continued, "I will file a grievance with the Oversight Committee regarding the suppression of evidence. I will present the armory original. I will present my analysis of the handwriting. And I will explain how a routine file clerk had the authority to replace an invoicing document without a supervisor's signature. The committee will tear apart your chain of custody. You will lose the inquiry. You will lose your job. The only question is whether you want to be the person who tried to bury this, or the person who closed the file and walked away."

The silence stretched. Yew's hand hovered over the keypad.

"You're threatening my career on a handwriting match."

"I'm not threatening anything. I'm offering a peaceful resolution. You close the inquiry citing insufficient evidence. You flag the discrepancy as a clerical error. My name is cleared. The file is closed. Everyone moves on."

Yew's fingers stayed motionless. He was calculating. The specific references to the clerk, the ink, the armory. This wasn't fishing. It was a fully prepared attack vector.

The phone was put down.

"Insufficient evidence," Yew said, his voice flat. "The file is closed pending further review of the supply chain. You are free to return to your training."

"Thank you, Administrator."

Brendt was waiting outside the yard. He looked relieved. "Well?"

"Closed."

"And?"

"And I burned a favor with Pell and threatened an administrator's pension. The arithmetic held."

Brendt fell into step beside him. "I tracked Sutter's movements during your meeting. He was at the merchant circuit this morning planting the theft rumor. I had the Kestrels counter-spread that an Ashford clerk was under investigation for document forgery. The circuit is frozen. They don't know which rumor is real."

Roan stopped. "You coordinated a counter-narrative without my input."

"You told me to prepare. I prepared." Brendt shrugged. "It was the logical next step. If the inquiry was the primary attack, the rumor was the secondary. I closed the flank."

Roan measured him. Brendt wasn't just a friend or a question box anymore. He'd become a functional asset. "Good. Track Sutter. If he tries to restart the rumor, we feed him a different target."

"Already planned."

They reached the training yard. Other students were arriving, no one looking at Roan with the suspicion that would have followed a successful frame-up.

Roan dropped into his stance.

"Cyrus will try again," Brendt said.

"Of course. He's impulsive. He commits his pieces before he understands the board."

"And the cost?"

"The cost is on him. I spent a favor and an hour. He spent the credibility of his family's file clerk. Next time he attacks, he has less capital. I have the same."

Roan began the form. The morning sun climbed higher across the yard. The Codex Shard rested against his spine, a quiet weight, a promise of energy if the fight ever turned physical.

"They thought my clean file was a weakness," Roan said, more to himself than to Brendt. "An anomaly in a system full of errors. But in a system that tolerates corruption, the one clean file is the most dangerous data point. It's an accusation waiting to be read."

"Is that the lesson?"

"That's the formula. Weakness is contextual. Cyrus identified a target, but he didn't understand the terrain." He completed the rotation, felt the burn in his obliques. "I do."

The forms continued. Rotation after rotation.

The arithmetic favored the patient.

And Roan could wait.
