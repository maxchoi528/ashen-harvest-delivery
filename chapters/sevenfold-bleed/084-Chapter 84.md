Chapter 84: Old Debts

The morning after the Hollow Marrow Elixir, Roan’s skeleton was denser. Micro-fractures traced his long bones—closed, calcified, already mending. The reinforcement had taken. The residual ache was just data: two more days before full integration.

He checked the Codex Shard before dressing. A notification sat in the protocol log, stamped with the black market contract system’s flat script:

*Contract 00-Ashworth-G-Vance: Phase Two initiation. Debtor Roan Vance acknowledges outstanding obligation. Creditor Garrick Ashworth reserves right to specify terms within thirty days. Stand by.*

Roan read it twice. The Bloodscourge purchase had been eight weeks ago. The clause had always been there: Garrick could call in a favour of proportional value. The timing was inconvenient—three weeks out from the Spire tournament—but the obligation was legal. He logged it as an active variable and set the shard aside.

He found Brendt in the training yard, running forms with a driven rhythm. Theon Mercer was beside him, matching the movements despite the bruising that still mottled his ribs. His grimace said each repetition cost him.

“You’ll crack those open again,” Roan said, leaning on the fence.

Brendt finished his sequence before answering. “He’s cleared for light movement.”

“By who?”

“The medical team.”

Roan filed that. Medical teams cleared for walking, maybe gentle stretching, not forms. Either Brendt was bending the truth, or the medical team had been leaned on. Both possibilities favoured House Ashford.

“Cyrus Ashford,” Brendt said, shifting stances mid-form without losing pace. “He requested formal observation of my training schedule. Asked the Junior Hunters’ Hall for my activity logs.” His next strike came heavier. “I declined.”

“He’ll get a judge to compel it.”

“He can try.”

Roan considered the geometry. House Ashford had influence in the Sentinel Bureau, but the Junior Hunters’ Hall fell under Black Spire’s jurisdiction. Compelling disclosure of student records required documented cause and time. Cyrus didn’t have much time if the evidence against him was meant to land before the tournament.

That balance held. Unless something shifted between now and then.

“The Elixir,” Brendt said, stopping. “You used it.”

“Some of it.”

“The inversion—”

“Worked.” Roan pushed off the fence. “I’m not optimized. But I’m closer.”

Brendt studied him, reading between lines. It was one of the reasons Roan trusted him as much as he trusted anyone.

“How much closer?”

“Enough to test the parameters.” Roan checked the sky. Late morning. Too public here, but the alternatives were worse: the Veyrn Sanctum was still locked down; the combat chambers required booking and left records. “There’s a surface rift two kilometres northeast. Tier One. The one they use for introductory certifications.”

Brendt’s posture shifted. “Wallowdeep.”

“You’ve been?”

“Everyone’s been. Safest rift in the region. Families go on weekends.” Brendt wiped sweat from his forehead. “Why there?”

“Because safe is relative, and I need to calibrate against something that won’t kill me if I miscalculate.”

Theon had stopped his forms. His bruised face was unreadable.

“You’re not serious,” Theon said.

“I’m always serious.” Roan met his gaze. “The question is whether you’re useful.”

Theon’s jaw tightened. The tell meant anger, or calculation, or both.

“Useful for what?”

“Observation. Documentation.” Roan tilted his head. “You write for the Academy Gazette.”

“I *write about* the Academy Gazette. I’m not a staffer.”

“You will be. After this week you’ll have enough material for a series.” Roan started walking toward the gate. “The question is whether you want to be the reporter who covered the story from his dormitory, or the one who was there.”

Theon stood frozen, then fell in behind them.

Brendt caught up on the path. “You’re using him.”

“I’m giving him an opportunity.”

“You’re using him.”

Roan didn’t argue. Theon had made his choice when he decided exposure was worth more than safety.

---

The Wallowdeep Rift was an unremarkable crack in the earth. Three metres across, ten deep, walls coated in bioluminescent moss that cast everything in green. A wooden railing surrounded the rim and a sign warned visitors not to feed the husks.

Husks were the first layer of any rift ecosystem: mindless, slow, dangerous only in numbers. A Tier One rift like this might hold twenty or thirty. Barely worth Vitalis. Brendt descended the ladder first, Theon second, wincing as his ribs hit the rungs. Roan came last, pulling the gate closed.

The bottom was muddy. The moss gave enough light to see by, not enough to be comfortable. In the northeast corner, something dragged against stone.

“Husks,” Brendt said, reading the sound. “Three, maybe four.”

“Close them down before they aggro.” Roan was already moving. “Theon. Start recording. Time, location, participants.”

Theon had his slate out, stylus moving. “Participant names. Roan Vance and Brendt Holloway?”

“For now.”

Roan reached into his coat and pulled out a dark-glass vial. Wraithmilk Tincture. The same compound he’d taken two months ago, before the tournament, before he’d fully understood what Inversion could do. He’d purchased three more since, paying premium black market prices.

Most people couldn’t use Wraithmilk safely. It rewired threat-response centres, induced three days of mood swings and paranoia. Roan treated that as a feature. Paranoia was pattern recognition with better motivation.

He uncorked the vial and drank.

The world sharpened. The moss resolved into individual spores, each pulsing with its own rhythm. The mud beneath his feet became distinct layers: clay, organic sediment, pressure-compacted decades. The darkness in the northeast corner became a space with four shapes, each with a different weight and gait.

Brendt engaged the first husk. His blade was precise, cut to the carapace joint. The second and third were converging on him.

Roan moved.

The Wraithmilk sang in his blood. Every shadow carried potential vectors, and his body translated them into anticipation. The husk lunged. He stepped left, drove two fingers into the joint where foreleg met thorax. Carapace gave way. Green fluid splattered his sleeve.

The third husk came from the wrong angle. Its claw caught his shoulder, raking through coat and skin.

*Sidewound detected. Conversion initiated: +47 Vitalis. Daily capacity: 3/5 → 2/5.*

Pain crested and broke. The wound pulled closed under the Inversion—muscle fibre knitting, blood vessels sealing. By the time his counter-strike reached the creature’s throat joint, the bleeding had stopped.

He finished it with a strike to the skull, then pivoted. The fourth husk was pressing Brendt’s guard. Brendt was holding, but his breathing was uneven.

“Move right,” Roan said.

Brendt moved right. The husk adjusted, exposing a gap. Roan drove his blade through it.

The creature fell.

Silence. Only their breathing and the drip of moisture.

“Theon.” Roan’s voice was steady. The Wraithmilk still sharpened his thoughts. “How did that look?”

Theon stared. His slate was still in his hands, stylus frozen. “You killed four husks in under a minute.”

“Forty-seven seconds,” Brendt said, wiping his blade. “I was counting.”

“Forty-seven seconds.” Theon’s voice hitched. “Roan, you’re still Initiate.”

“Tempered.”

“What?”

“He broke through three weeks ago.” Brendt’s tone was flat. “Records are sealed for privacy.”

“Privacy—” Theon looked between them. “People notice rank breakthroughs.”

“People notice what they’re looking for.” Roan pressed a hand to his shoulder. The skin was warm, unbroken. “The question is whether they bother to look.”

Theon was writing. “Theon Mercer, Academy Gazette. Wallowdeep Rift, northeast quadrant. Observation of unsanctioned combat training. Participants engaged four Tier One husks, eliminated threats in under one minute. Vance exhibited combat speed and coordination inconsistent with reported Initiate classification.”

“Add a note about the wound,” Roan said. “Describe it as superficial.”

“Superficial.” Theon’s stylus paused. “Roan, that claw went through your coat and undershirt. You should be bleeding.”

“It was superficial enough.” Roan met his eyes. “Make it sound like something anyone could have walked away from.”

Theon’s stylus moved. His jaw was tight, but he wrote.

Brendt stepped closer. “The Elixir. You used it during the fight.”

“Some of it.”

“But it worked. You’re different now.” Brendt was reading between lines again. “Roan, what did it do?”

Roan considered. Brendt had earned a version of the truth.

“It made my bones harder. More impact-resistant.” He flexed his shoulder. “Before the Elixir, that hit would have cracked my collarbone.”

“You’re not hurt at all now.”

“I’m not hurt enough for it to matter.” He let the weight of the statement sit. “That’s the difference.”

Brendt processed. He built understandings brick by brick, which was why the partnership worked.

“Okay,” Brendt said. “What are you building toward?”

Roan thought about the question. The Wraithmilk was still active, edges of the world still too sharp. He could see where the moss-light was fading as the spores consumed each other, hear the slight change in Brendt’s breathing as it approached normal, see Theon watching them both like a man who had stumbled into an armoury and wasn’t sure which weapon to pick up.

“The tournament,” Roan said. “Cyrus Ashford.”

“What about it?”

“I need to win in a way House Ashford can’t bury.” He checked his shoulder again. Clean. “Cyrus thinks he knows what I’m capable of. He has three weeks to prepare.”

“And you want to show him something else.”

“I want to show him his strategy is based on incomplete information.” Roan’s mouth twitched. Not a smile. “Let him build on sand, then let the tide come in.”

Brendt nodded. “You want me to keep training. Stay visible. Be the target.”

“You’re a misdirection.”

“I know.”

“Cyrus thinks you’re the threat. That’s useful.”

“Because it means he’ll prepare for the wrong opponent.”

Roan started climbing the ladder. “Theon. Send your first report tonight. Three paragraphs. No inflammatory language. Just the facts.”

“Which facts?”

“All of them.”

---

The walk back took an hour. By the time they reached campus, the sun was past noon and the Wraithmilk was descending. The sharp clarity faded into a familiar baseline. Exhaustion settled into his bones—not the clean tired of productive work, but the chemical aftermath of pushing his system to its edges.

Brendt walked beside him. Theon had peeled off at the east dormitory, his slate clutched like something precious.

“Three weeks,” Brendt said.

“Yes.”

“You’re going to do something bigger than husks and Gazette articles.”

“Probably.”

“Can you tell me what?”

Roan considered. The tournament was one battle in a larger war. The evidence against Cyrus was solid, but solid evidence could be buried. Somewhere behind it all, House Greel was still watching. And now Garrick had activated the debt clause.

“The tournament is a test,” Roan said. “Not of Cyrus. Of the system.”

“The system?”

“The Conclave. The Academy. The way they handle evidence and reputation.” He looked toward the Black Spire silhouette. “Cyrus is a symptom. The disease is the assumption that power means rightness.”

Brendt absorbed that. “You think the tournament will expose it.”

“I think it will force a choice.” Roan started walking again. “Either the system works the way it’s supposed to, or it doesn’t. The tournament just provides the arena.”

“And you want to make sure the choice goes the right direction.”

“I want to make sure there *is* a choice.” He paused. “What I can control is whether the relevant factors are visible when the decision is made.”

Brendt nodded slowly. “Theon.”

“Theon. The Gazette. Public pressure.” Roan chose the path toward the dormitories. “Cyrus assumes he can control the narrative because no one is watching closely enough.”

“And you want people watching.”

“I want the ground frozen solid when he tries to bury it.”

---

The evening found Roan in his room. He changed the compress—more out of habit than necessity; the wound was already sealed. On the desk sat a fresh vial of Wraithmilk, next to a small ledger sheet listing his available credit.

Eight hundred per vial. Twelve vials for the full tolerance protocol. Ninety-six hundred total. He had roughly two thousand.

The arithmetic was simple. The gap was significant.

He could sell information. He had enough of it, and the market would pay. But information was leverage, and leverage only worked while you held it.

Alternative: ask Drogan. Drogan had resources and would probably help. But Drogan would also ask questions Roan wasn’t ready to answer.

The Codex Shard pulsed against his spine. A reminder.

*Contract 00-Ashworth-G-Vance: Phase Two initiated.*

Garrick had thirty days. Roan had three weeks until the tournament. Both deadlines converged, and he still didn’t have the full picture.

“The tournament is the first move,” he said to the empty room. “The evidence is the second. The Gazette is the third.”

The shard pulsed again. Agreement, maybe. Or the ghost of Aldric waiting.

*Fourth move?*

Roan set the vial down. “That’s the question.”

He was still turning it over when the window rattled. Not wind—a signal. Someone throwing pebbles, the old way, the way that left no logs.

He crossed to the window and looked down.

Mira stood in the courtyard. His mother. Travel clothes, hair tied back, a worn leather bag over her shoulder. She looked tired. She looked like she’d been crying.

“Roan.” Her voice carried up, thin and urgent. “Roan, please. I need to talk to you.”

He was at the door before he caught himself. The calculations ran automatically: how she got past Drogan’s wards, what she needed, what this meant. The figures said something was wrong—either a trap or a crisis. He needed more information before he committed.

But she was his mother. And she was crying.

The columns didn’t lie. But sometimes the arithmetic was incomplete.

He opened the door.
