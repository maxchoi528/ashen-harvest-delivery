Chapter 43: Mira in the Hospital

The arena beneath the Black Spire's eastern bleachers smelled of sweat and old blood. Third-tier sparring circles, the kind where instructors turned a blind eye to broken ribs and the occasional collapsed lung. Good place to test limits.

Brendt Holloway landed three meters away, settling into a low guard that Drogan had drilled into him until his stance became instinct. His jaw was set. His Vitalis pulsed at the edges of his skin, readable now, after three weeks of close observation. Roughly 280. Tempered, early sub-stage. Fast-twitch muscle fibers developed beyond his frame suggested a family history of combat training, likely father or older sibling.

The figures said Roan could take him in under ninety seconds. The question was how to do it without revealing the gap between "adequate sparring partner" and "statistical anomaly."

"You're thinking again," Brendt said. "I can see it. The way your eyes go flat."

Flat. Interesting word choice. Most people said "cold." Brendt saw the answer underneath.

"Observing," Roan corrected. "There's a difference."

"Four seconds, then you hit me. That's how long it takes before you move." Brendt shifted his weight forward, one inch without more. A tell. He was trying to bait an early strike. "You telegraph when you're about to—"

Roan closed the distance.

Not fast. Deliberate. Speed that let an opponent see everything and process nothing. Brendt's guard came up, correct response, textbook form, and Roan was already inside it, palm striking the floating rib at an angle that would compress without cracking. Brendt exhaled. Swung a counter. Roan stepped back, let the fist pass his ear by three centimeters, and delivered a second palm strike to the solar plexus. Not full force. Enough to fold Brendt forward without dropping him.

Clean. Educational. Controlled violence that instructors praised.

Brendt straightened, breathing hard. No anger, that was the interesting thing about him. Most people Roan's speed humiliated either got angry or got scared. Brendt got quiet.

"Four seconds," he said. "You let me talk for four seconds."

"Information extraction."

"You learned nothing. I baited you with nothing."

"Confirmation is also information." Roan reset his stance, weight balanced on the balls of his feet. "Again."

Brendt's expression shifted, a flicker of something harder underneath the exhaustion. He reset. They went again. And again. Seven rounds total before Drogan's voice cut across the circle like a cleaver.

"Enough."

The Warden stood at the eastern archway, arms crossed. His Iron Hand gleamed under the sparring circle's lamplight, polished steel, no scrimshaw without ornamentation. Prosthetic that said *this was a tool, not a trophy.* Behind him, two junior Disciples lingered. They saw Brendt's performance. They saw Roan's.

They filed those numbers away.

"Roan. My office. Now."

No elaboration. Drogan didn't do elaboration.

Brendt caught Roan's eye as he walked past. *What's happening?* It wasn't spoken. It didn't need to be.

Roan gave a minute shake of his head. *Unknown. Stay alert.*

The corridors beneath the Spire were older than the training halls above, stonework from the original structure, before the Institute's seven expansions. Drogan's office occupied a converted storage room in sub-level three. Shelves lined with incident reports, requisition forms, and a locked cabinet that held confiscated enhancement materials. The desk was a single slab of treated oak, scarred with fourteen years of Warden discipline.

Drogan closed the door. The latch clicked with a finality that made the room's dimensions feel smaller.

"Sit."

Roan sat. The wooden chair creaked under him, uneven legs, decades of use. Drogan remained standing, which put him in a position of advantage. Deliberate. The Warden was teaching something, or testing something, or both.

"Your Vitalis readings from last week came back," Drogan said. "Curious numbers."

Curious. A careful word. Not *impossible.* Not *alarming.* Curious.

"Curious how?"

"You know the ranges. Initiate threshold is fifty. Tempered begins at two-fifty. By standard progression curves, a student who awakened eight weeks ago should be reading between sixty and ninety. Depending on talent and diet, plus sleep quality." Drogan pulled a folded paper from his jacket. Unfolded it without looking at it, he'd already memorized the columns. "You registered eight hundred and twelve at yesterday's morning assessment."

Roan said nothing.

"The official report puts you at eighty-one. The assessment machine logged a calibration error. I'm the only one who saw the real number." Drogan set the paper on the desk. "So. Curious."

The room went quiet. Five seconds. Ten. Drogan didn't fill it, another test, or perhaps a courtesy. He was giving Roan room to decide what version of the truth to offer.

"I train differently," Roan said finally. "I've always trained differently."

"You're not wrong." Drogan moved to the window, a narrow slit in the stone wall, barely wide enough to see the courtyard below. "Your father trained differently too. Brought numbers that made the Assessors call him a myth for three years before anyone believed the readings were real."

The name landed. Drogan watched Roan's face for a reaction. Found none, presumably. Reactions were expensive.

"I'm not my father."

"No. You're younger, and you're quieter, and you fight like someone who's spent a decade learning to survive without backup." Drogan turned from the window. "That last part concerns me most."

*Concerned.* Another careful word. Not *suspicious.* Not *threatening.* Concerned, the framing of someone who wanted to be convinced, rather than someone who needed to be convinced otherwise.

"The Black Spire has operated under scrutiny since before I was born," Roan said. "The Assessors check everything. The Conclave checks the Assessors. If my numbers were a genuine concern, I'd have been pulled for formal review already."

"You were. Three days ago. Headmaster Cole signed off on a quiet exemption. *Quiet.*" Drogan let the word sit. "That means someone with authority decided your anomalous progression was less interesting than the problems that would come from making it public."

That was new information. And it changed the balance.

Cole. Edran Cole, Headmaster of Black Spire, a man whose public reputation was built on *transparency and institutional integrity.* He had personally signed an exemption that concealed Roan's numbers. That meant either Cole was protecting Roan, which raised the question of *why*, or Cole was protecting the Spire from the questions that would follow if Roan's actual readings became known. Neither answer was comfortable.

"Who asked for the exemption?" Roan said.

"You're asking the right questions in the wrong order." Drogan sat. The chair on the other side of the desk, his own chair, deliberately chosen, groaned under his weight. "Someone's been making inquiries about House Vance. Not the Conclave. Not the Sentinel Bureau. Private channels. Someone with enough resources to run background traces on a family that formally dissolved six years ago."

The room felt colder.

"What kind of inquiries?"

"Business records. Property transfers. The circumstances of your parents' deaths." Drogan's voice was level, but his flesh hand rested on the desk with a tension that suggested the words cost him something. "And your sister's current enrollment status at Greycross Secondary."

Lyra. Twelve years old, two months from her own awakening testing. Someone was looking at Lyra.

"What resources," Roan said. "Be specific."

"Traced through three intermediary firms before the trail went cold. Original source was a shell company registered in Eastreach. Cross-references to—" Drogan stopped. His jaw worked, once, hard. "Cross-references to House Greel."

Greel. The white-bearded man. The Northern Crown, patriarch of the old houses, the man whose family's influence extended from the Arctic Circle to the southern trade routes. And one of the names in Aldric's final journal entry, underlined twice.

Roan's hand moved to his collar. To the place beneath the shirt where the black iron anchor rested against his chest, warm, inert, carrying its dead weight of questions he couldn't yet answer.

*the white-bearded man has been hunting Inversion cultivators for thirty years. If he finds you, he won't investigate. He'll eliminate.*

His father's words, from the journal. Roan had read that passage fourteen times. He read it again now, behind his eyes, while his face stayed exactly where it was.

"The timing," Roan said. "When did the inquiries start?"

"Seven weeks ago. Two weeks before you arrived at Black Spire."

Two weeks after the night Mira had burned Aldric's last research notes. Two weeks after Roan had found the pendant in Aldric's box, still warm, still humming with a frequency he'd learned to recognize as *alive.*

Someone had known. Someone had talked. Or someone had been watching long before that night and had decided seven weeks ago was the right moment to start pulling threads.

"Does my mother know?"

"She was contacted directly. Three days ago. She told them she'd sold all Vance holdings years ago and had nothing left to discuss. They sent a follow-up letter offering compensation for 'historical consultation services.'" Drogan's lip curled. "She sent it back with a note in the margin that I'm told was creative enough to make Sentinel Bureau archivists blush."

Right. Mira Vance, née Corvus, whose family name carried its own weight in certain circles. She could handle a letter. But letters were reconnaissance. The next message might not be on paper.

"I need to contact her."

"You can't. The Sentinel Bureau has Greycross under low-level observation, standard protocol when an active inquiry involves a civilian residence. Any outgoing call from your family's residence is being logged and reviewed." Drogan's mechanical hand drummed once against the desk. A sharp, percussive sound. "She knows you're aware. She sent a message through unofficial channels. I received it this morning."

He held up a scrap of paper. Three words, in Mira's precise handwriting:

*Keep him learning.*

Not *protect him.* Not *hide him.* Learning. Mira Vance had looked at the situation, the white-bearded man, a dead husband's legacy, a teenage son with impossible Vitalis readings, and decided the right move was to keep Roan inside the Black Spire's training pipeline rather than pulling him out.

Smart. Mira was always smart.

The Codex Shard warmed slightly against his chest. Not activation-warm. Acknowledgment-warm. The distinction mattered, though only Roan could feel it.

"Your mother's advice is sound," Drogan said. "The Black Spire is the safest place for you right now. Inside the Institute, the Conclave's charter protects all enrolled students from external investigation without formal tribunal approval. The white-bearded man can ask questions through proxies. He can't touch you directly while you're enrolled."

"Unless he finds a reason to accelerate."

"Unless he finds a reason to accelerate." Drogan met his eyes. No softening. No reassurance. Just the flat truth delivered by a man who'd survived three decades of exactly this kind of pressure. "Your father was better than you are. Faster, stronger, more experienced. He still died in a situation he didn't see coming. The difference between you and him is that Aldric thought he could outthink the people hunting him. You know you can't. So work the angles he missed."

"What angles?"

"The ones that require time. Greel's people are looking for Inversion. They don't know what it looks like in an active user, they've never caught one alive long enough to study the symptoms. Your Vitalis readings are anomalous, but they're not *identifiable.* Not yet. Keep them that way. Train the parts of your ability that don't show up on Assessor machines. Learn the people around you well enough that you can tell when one of them starts asking questions they shouldn't know to ask."

"Brendt?"

"Brendt's clean. His family's too small and too poor to be a Greel asset. But clean doesn't mean safe, clean just means no one bought him yet. The world is full of people who don't know they're being used."

The corridor noise filtered through the stone walls. Distant footsteps. Someone laughing, two floors up. The ordinary sounds of an Institute that had no idea what was moving through its sub-levels.

"One more thing." Drogan reached into the locked cabinet behind him. His hand came back with a small glass vial, ten centimeters, dark liquid, sealed with a wax cap. "Wraithmilk Tincture. First-tier catalyst. Side effects include temporal perception distortion, mild hallucinations, and a thirty percent chance of acute mental confusion for up to seventy-two hours post-ingestion."

Roan looked at the vial. Looked at Drogan.

"I'm offering this because your current training regimen is burning through your body faster than you're recovering," Drogan said. "The Wraithmilk Tincture accelerates neural pathway development. For most people, the cost is seven days of hallucinations. For you—" He paused. His eyes were sharp and unblinking. "For you, I suspect the cost will be different."

The vial sat on the desk between them. Drogan wasn't offering it as a gift. He was offering it as a test, another data point in whatever model he was building around Roan Vance.

*He knows about the Inversion. Not the specifics. But he's seen enough of Aldric's patterns to recognize the shape of the ability.*

That was the most dangerous thing anyone had done since Roan arrived at Black Spire. And the most useful.

"Two questions," Roan said.

"One."

"The inquiries about my family. Were they made before or after my enrollment was finalized?"

Drogan considered. "The timing was simultaneous. Application submitted, inquiries began. Whoever's asking knew you were coming before the paperwork cleared."

Which meant either Greel's network was inside Black Spire's admissions office, or someone inside Black Spire was feeding information out. Neither option was acceptable. Both were tractable.

"My second question," Roan said. "Why are you helping me?"

The Warden was quiet. His mechanical hand opened and closed once, a grinding sound, gears catching.

"Because your father saved my life twice, and I never got to pay either debt back. And because—" He stopped. Started again. "Because the world has too many people who kill for power and not enough who think for it. I don't know what you are, Roan Vance. But I know what you'll become if someone gives you enough room to grow."

He pushed the vial across the desk.

"Take it or don't. But if you take it, do it somewhere private. And when the hallucinations start—" His mouth twisted. "Remember that not everything you see is a lie. Some of it's just information wearing the wrong face."

Roan picked up the vial. The glass was cool against his fingers, the liquid dark and still. He didn't thank Drogan. Gratitude created expectations. Instead he tucked it into an interior pocket and stood.

*Drogan sees too much, too fast—that's either a rare asset or a future liability.*

He filed the thought alongside the other data points. Then he left.

- break -

The call came at 3:47 AM.

Roan was in his storage room on the fourth sub-level, reviewing Drogan's data on the Greel inquiry network, when his personal tablet lit with an emergency channel ping. The number was registered to Greycross General Hospital.

He answered on the first vibration.

"Roan Vance?" A woman's voice. Professional. The flattened affect of someone who delivered bad news as a job function. "Your mother was brought in forty minutes ago. Respiratory collapse at her workplace. She's stable, but we need someone to authorize the admission paperwork and provide a deposit for the acute care unit."

He was on his feet before she finished the sentence.

"Deposit amount?"

"One thousand two hundred credits. We can set up a payment plan if—"

"I'll bring it."

He ended the call and stood in the dark with the tablet still warm in his hand. The numbers rearranged themselves in his head. He had one thousand four hundred credits saved from the protection payments, Greycross margins, careful budget management. He had been saving for a Wraithmilk Tincture, Drogan's offer, the next phase of training.

Now the money had another function.

He grabbed his jacket.

- break -

Greycross General occupied a four-story building at the edge of the industrial district, its facade stained by decades of exhaust and weather. The waiting room smelled of antiseptic and boiled coffee. A receptionist behind glass directed him to the third floor without looking up from her screen.

Mira Vance lay in a bed near the window, connected to monitors that beeped in intervals Roan didn't need to count to understand. Her face was the color of unbleached paper. Her breathing was shallow but steady. An oxygen cannula ran beneath her nose, taped to her cheek with the casual brutality of medical necessity.

He stood in the doorway for twelve seconds, cataloging.

*Pulse: sixty-two, low but stable. Respiration: fourteen per minute, supported. Oxygen saturation: ninety-four percent, acceptable. Pupils: reactive. No visible signs of stroke. The chemical plant's safety log would have recorded the exposure incident. If there was one.*

He crossed to the chair beside her bed and sat down.

The deposit slip was in his pocket, stamped PAID. One thousand two hundred credits in cash, delivered to the billing office without negotiation. The clerk had tried to offer him a payment plan. He had declined. Payment plans left paper trails. Paper trails were how people like Vorath Greel found leverage.

Mira's hand lay on the blanket, palm up, fingers slightly curled. The hand of someone who had spent thirty years working with chemicals and machinery, scarred in a dozen places, nails cut short, no rings. He didn't take it. He sat beside it, close enough that she would know he was there when she woke.

She woke at 6:12 AM.

Her eyes opened the way they always did, without grogginess, without transition. One moment closed, the next open and aware. She turned her head. Saw him. Her expression didn't change, but something in the set of her jaw relaxed a fraction.

"You're supposed to be at the Spire," she said. Her voice was rough from the oxygen tube.

"Classes don't start until eight."

"You're a terrible liar. Always were." She shifted, wincing. "The bill—"

"Paid."

Her eyes moved to his face. Stayed there. Reading the lines around his mouth, the set of his shoulders, the way he sat without leaning back. The maternal assessment he had never learned to evade.

"With what money?"

"Doesn't matter."

"Roan—"

"I said it doesn't matter." He held her gaze. Not challenging. Firm. The way you spoke to someone who needed to stop asking questions and start conserving strength.

Mira looked at him for a long moment. Then her gaze dropped to his collar, where the edge of the pendant scar was visible above his shirt. The blackened silhouette of the brand.

She had seen it.

"I was hoping you wouldn't find that," she said.

"The box in the basement?"

"Finding it. Wearing it. Becoming what it makes you." She closed her eyes. "Your father left that thing behind for a reason. He said someone would need it someday. I didn't think it would be you."

"Who else would it be?"

She didn't answer. The monitors beeped. The oxygen line hissed.

"Save it," Mira said finally. "The money. Save it for what's coming. That man with the white beard—" She opened her eyes. "He isn't sending letters anymore. He's sending people. I've seen them in the neighborhood. Watching. Waiting."

Roan's hand moved to the pendant beneath his shirt.

"How long?"

"Two weeks. Maybe three." She turned her head toward the window. The morning light was grey, industrial, filtered through layers of smog and overcast. "Your father used to say the best time to prepare for a storm is when the sky is still clear. The second best time is when you hear thunder."

"Mom—"

"I'm not dying, Roan. I'm tired. There's a difference." She met his eyes again. "But I need you to understand something. The Spire is the only place where you're safe. The campus has protections that Greycross doesn't. If you leave to check on me every time I cough, you'll be on the road instead of in the training hall, and the man with the white beard will find a gap in your armor that wouldn't exist if you'd stayed."

The logic was sound. He hated it.

"I'll visit when I can."

"Visit when you're ready. Not when you're worried." Her hand moved on the blanket. A fraction toward him, then back. The closest Mira Vance came to reaching out. "Now go. You have classes."

He stood. Paused at the door.

"Mom."

"What?"

"I paid the bill in cash. No records. If anyone asks—"

"I fell at work. The plant covered it." She almost smiled. "I've been lying to bureaucrats longer than you've been alive."

He nodded once and left.

The corridor was the same. The antiseptic smell was the same. The world outside was grey and industrial and unchanged. But something had shifted inside him. A piece of the calculation had been rewritten.

His mother had seen the pendant scar. She had known what it meant. And she had chosen not to ask him to stop.

That was permission. It was also a warning.

He walked to the shuttle stop with his hands in his pockets and the Codex Shard warm against his spine, counting the hours—and the alternatives.
