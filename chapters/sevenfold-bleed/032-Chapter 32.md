Chapter 32: Lyra's Pre-Screening

The examination hall of Black Spire smelled like iron and sweat and ambition. Roan Vance stood in the third row of the Tempered-ranked applicants' formation, counting the seconds until examiner Marrick's attention drifted toward his morning ledger. The man's focus lasted about twelve minutes. After that, Roan had approximately four minutes of relative quiet before the next trial phase began.

Twelve minutes. Four minutes. The variables pointed in one direction.

The problem was that Brendt kept elbowing him.

"You're in my light," Brendt muttered without moving his lips.

"It's your light only because you positioned yourself between me and it." Roan's gaze stayed forward. "Move left."

"You moved right."

"I'm aware."

Brendt shifted half a step. The morning sun cut across the examination hall at an angle that made the metal walkways blaze. Around them, two hundred Tempered-ranked applicants stood in rigid formation, their Vitalis readings hovering between two hundred and eight hundred, the visible public records, anyway. The rankings board fed off mandatory disclosure seals. Everyone here knew those numbers were incomplete.

Roan's public record read four hundred and sixteen. His actual reading sat at six hundred and twelve, a fact he intended to keep buried until circumstances demanded otherwise.

He'd used the Inversion once yesterday, converting the side effects of a cheap Bone-Fortifying Tincture into permanent tensile density in his fast-twitch fibers. The gain was structural—he wouldn't lose it by morning. The process had cost him eight hours of nausea but netted him a gain equivalent to three weeks of standard training. The arithmetic remained favorable. The discomfort was the price.

Marrick finished his ledger and snapped it shut. "Pairs. Candidates, you're partnering with whoever's standing next to you. No swapping."

Perfect. The universe had a sense of humor.

Cyrus Ashford was standing next to him.

The Ashford heir didn't need to look sideways to convey his displeasure. His posture did it for him, spine locked at an angle that communicated disdain before a single word was spoken. Theon Mercer, Cyrus's permanent shadow, shifted on Cyrus's other flank, blocking any last-second lane change Brendt might have attempted.

"Well," Cyrus said. "This should be educational."

"Undoubtedly." Roan kept his voice flat. "What's the exercise?"

"Controlled sparring. First-to-three touches. Vitalis suppression at sixty percent—the lattice will clamp your active projection to sixty percent of your true reserve, an artificial floor to force technique over raw power." Cyrus cracked his knuckles. The sound carried twenty feet. "I assume you know what suppression means. It's the part where you hold back what little you have."

Brendt muttered something Roan chose not to hear.

The exercise was announced over the hall's speaker: controlled contact, vital points only, suppression enforced by the examination hall's ambient field. A green shimmer settled over the arena as the suppression lattice activated. Roan's Vitalis dropped to a nominal hum. His body didn't care. The Inversion system ran independent of ambient fields—it drew from damage absorbed, not ambient energy.

That was the edge. Small and quiet, and lethal in ways the suppression lattice couldn't touch.

They stepped onto the mat. Cyrus moved with the loose confidence of someone who'd been training under private tutors since childhood. His form was textbook Ashford-style pressure fighting: heavy footwork, deliberate advancement, forcing opponents backward until they ran out of room.

Roan had watched three of Cyrus's recorded matches. The pattern was consistent. Advance, test, advance, test, then a decisive strike once he identified the opponent's weak angle.

The first exchange confirmed the pattern. Cyrus threw a probing jab that Roan parried without committing. The contact was light enough to satisfy the lattice. Cyrus frowned—the jab had been meant to provoke a flinch, not a clean deflection.

"Keeping it textbook?" Cyrus circled right. "How diligent."

"I'm conserving energy." Roan tracked the circle without matching it. "It's a long morning."

"You'll have energy for the walk to the infirmary."

Second exchange. Cyrus changed levels, dropped his center, and launched a low kick aimed at Roan's knee. Roan stepped over it—not a block, a redirect—and let Cyrus's momentum carry him off-balance for half a step. Not a touch. But a message.

Roan wasn't flinching.

Cyrus's jaw tightened. That was the tell. The Ashford heir liked fights that ended quickly, opponents who folded under pressure. Roan wasn't folding.

Third exchange. Cyrus committed. A three-strike combination aimed at Roan's torso, fast, precise, textbook—a sequence that scored on anyone who tried to match speed.

Roan didn't match speed. He shortened his own arc and let two of the strikes land—controlled contact, suppression lattice enforced—while his parry arrived one beat early. The timing was deliberate. It looked like clumsiness. It felt like surrender.

It also positioned his palm against Cyrus's incoming third strike's follow-through, redirecting the energy into an off-angle that sent Cyrus stumbling half a step off the mat's edge.

Not a touch. But Cyrus was off-balance now.

"What are you—" Cyrus caught himself, straightened. His voice had lost its edge. "That was nothing."

"It was a redirection," Roan said. "Friction and geometry."

"Stop talking."

Fourth exchange. Cyrus opened with something new—a feinted strike that transitioned into a grapnel grab. Ashford specialty. Close-range control designed to trap opponents before they could reestablish distance.

Roan allowed the grab. His left arm went into Cyrus's grip. The lattice registered it as contact, sustained grappling, within exercise parameters. Cyrus locked the hold, applied pressure, and prepared to torque Roan off-center.

The problem was that Cyrus was applying the hold based on strength differential. He was reading Roan's visible ranking, four hundred and sixteen, and assuming the leverage advantage was overwhelming. He was wrong. Roan's actual strength sat at three hundred and five even under suppression, the permanent tensile density from yesterday's conversion still integrated into his fast-twitch fibers.

The leverage didn't break. Cyrus's expression shifted from triumph to confusion in half a second.

Roan twisted out of the hold without rushing. Smooth, efficient without drama. His body came free with a rotation that turned Cyrus's own grip force against him, and by the time the Ashford heir re-established his stance, Roan was already three feet back and squared.

"One-one," Brendt announced from the sideline. His voice carried the faint amusement of someone who'd wagered nothing but still enjoyed watching the odds shift.

Cyrus's face had gone pale under the examination hall's lights. Not fear. Anger—the specific kind that came from discovering an equation didn't balance the way you expected.

"You're sandbagging," Cyrus said.

"I'm not."

"Your ranking is wrong."

"My ranking is what's on the board." Roan kept his hands loose. "You should take it up with the board."

The fifth exchange was the one that mattered.

Cyrus came in hard. The restraint he'd maintained in exchanges one through four vanished. His technique remained precise, but the intent shifted from scoring points to ending the match. A rising elbow aimed at Roan's chin, following a step-in strike that forced Roan backward toward the mat's edge.

This was the trap. Push Roan to the edge, force a desperate response, score the clincher from a position of advantage. It was the Ashford strategy—manufactured pressure, controlled geometry, forcing the opponent to make the mistake.

Roan didn't panic. He read the geometry, calculated the four possible exit vectors, discarded three because they led to walls, and chose the fourth: forward.

He stepped into the strike.

The elbow missed his chin by three inches because he'd already committed his body past the arc. Cyrus's forward momentum worked against him. Roan's palm struck the Ashford heir's sternum—not hard, not fast, just precisely placed at the moment when balance was maximally compromised.

Cyrus staggered. Roan stepped back.

"Two-one."

The formation had gone quiet. Two hundred applicants weren't supposed to watch a pair match with held breath, but they were.

Cyrus reset. His breathing had changed—shorter, sharper. The next exchange would be decisive. Either he'd find an angle that neutralized whatever Roan's edge was, or he'd escalate to something that crossed the exercise's parameters.

Roan ran the calculations. He had three options. One: maintain the current pace, let Cyrus overcommit again, and secure the third touch. Two: deliberately lose the next exchange, let the match extend to whatever ceiling Marrick imposed, and exit with a two-one win that was already outside expected parameters. Three: push past suppression limits, show something real, and deal with the consequences.

The math on option one was clean. But Cyrus was tilting. The next exchange would either be a desperate gambit or an outright violation of exercise parameters. If Roan took the win cleanly, he'd spend the next month being the Ashford family's new obsession. If Roan lost on purpose, he'd spend the next month being Cyrus's personal project.

Neither outcome was optimal.

A door opened at the back of the examination hall. Roan registered the movement peripherally, the ambient Vitalis shift that preceded someone's entrance. Examiner Marrick turned, which meant it was someone whose entrance warranted acknowledgment. The gatehouse must have called ahead—only authorized visitors could enter during a trial.

Mira Vance stood in the doorway.

His mother looked exactly like she'd looked when she packed his bags for Black Spire three months ago: composed, alert, dressed in the grey coveralls of someone who'd come straight from her shift at the Greycross processing plant. She wasn't supposed to be here. She worked double shifts. She never left the plant during weekdays.

Her eyes found him across the hall in under two seconds.

Cyrus was already resetting for the sixth exchange. His attention hadn't registered the interruption. Around them, the formation remained at attention but visibly distracted—candidates exchanging glances, curiosity replacing discipline.

Marrick crossed toward the door. "Ma'am, this is an active examination—"

"I can see that." Mira's voice carried without effort. "I was cleared at the gate. My son is in the middle of something."

She meant the match. She also meant something else. Roan didn't know which.

Cyrus charged.

The seventh exchange blurred. Roan sidestepped, absorbed a glancing shoulder strike, and countered with an open-hand tap to Cyrus's shoulder—touch that barely registered through the lattice, touch that looked like nothing.

It was also a scoring touch.

"Three-one."

Cyrus stopped.

His Vitalis spiked audibly, the suppression lattice flickered green around him, before Marrick's voice cut through. "Ashford. Reset your state. That's a warning."

"I wasn't—"

"Reset."

The formation was not watching the board anymore. Two hundred applicants were watching Mira Vance, who had positioned herself against the back wall with the posture of someone prepared to wait.

Cyrus reset. His face had gone red. Not exertion. Humiliation—the specific kind that came from losing to someone he'd already decided was beneath him. His Vitalis suppression wavered again.

Marrick noticed. "Mercer, take Ashford out. Cool-down lap."

Theon guided Cyrus toward the door with the practiced efficiency of someone who'd managed the Ashford heir's mood swings before. Cyrus didn't look at Roan on the way out. He didn't look at anyone.

Roan stepped off the mat.

Brendt caught his eye. A small nod—acknowledgment without spectacle. That was Brendt. Efficient.

Marrick crossed the mat toward Roan. "Vance. Your mother's here. Take ten, then report to the lower-ranked candidates for supplementary evaluation."

Roan didn't move. "Supplementary evaluation is for candidates below the four hundred mark."

Marrick's expression didn't change. "Consider it remedial work."

"That would require my actual ranking to be below four hundred."

The silence that followed lasted three seconds. Marrick's eyes narrowed—not anger, recalculation. The examiner was smart enough to understand what Roan was implying. He was also bound by the board's mandatory disclosure rules, which meant he couldn't act on the implication without evidence.

"Your mother's waiting," Marrick said finally. "Go."

Roan walked across the examination hall. Every step felt heavier than the last, not because of fatigue, but because he could feel the weight of two hundred gazes tracking him. He'd won. He'd also drawn exactly the attention he'd calculated he could avoid.

Mira watched him approach without smiling. "You won."

"He got sloppy."

"You let him get sloppy."

Roan stopped three feet from her. The hall's ambient noise filled the gap. "You shouldn't be here."

"I got a call from someone at the school. And Lyra's pre-screening result came today—she's in the second round." Mira paused, her jaw tight. "They said your Vitalis readings were flagged as inconsistent with your disclosed ranking."

Someone had reported the discrepancy already. Marrick? The lattice's monitoring system? It didn't matter which—the result was the same. The Ashford family had contacts inside Black Spire's administrative body. Of course they did.

"The readings fluctuate," Roan said. "The trial rations affect calibration."

"You just beat Cyrus Ashford in a controlled match." Mira's jaw was tight. "Three-to-one. I've been watching you for ninety seconds. You weren't fighting at four hundred and sixteen."

"Four hundred and thirty-seven."

"That's not the point."

"It entirely is the point." Roan's voice stayed flat. "The point is that my ranking fluctuates within normal parameters and the disclosure board rounds to the nearest five. Cyrus expected me to be weaker than my baseline. I wasn't. The tally of the match was straightforward."

Mira studied him the way she studied the readouts at the processing plant—looking for the data underneath the surface. "You're hiding something."

"I'm managing disclosure."

"You're thirteen steps ahead of everyone and you still look miserable about it."

Roan didn't answer. Brendt was crossing the hall toward them, his own session evidently complete. He stopped at a respectful distance and nodded to Mira.

"Ma'am," he said. "Roan's told me a lot about you."

"Brendt." Mira said. "The Holloway boy. Your father runs the insurance firm in Greycross."

"Yes, ma'am."

"Better than the plant work." Mira's gaze returned to Roan. "You have a friend. That's something your father didn't have at your age."

Roan's hands stayed loose at his sides. "What's the actual reason you're here?"

"The official reason was the call from administration." Mira pulled a folded paper from her coverall pocket. "The actual reason is this. It came in yesterday's post."

The envelope was Greycross postal markings. The return address was a name Roan didn't recognize—a long-dead Greycross merchant whose name had appeared in the police reports six years ago when they'd investigated Aldric's disappearance. The envelope contained a single sheet of paper. Three words, handwritten, in ink that had faded to brown.

*He's not dead.*

Roan read the words twice. His mind processed the information in the time it took his heartbeat to complete one cycle. Someone was either lying to his mother for leverage, telling the truth about something the police had buried, or trying to manipulate a grieving widow for reasons he couldn't yet calculate.

"Ma'am." Brendt's voice was careful. "That's not—"

"I know what it is." Mira's eyes hadn't left Roan's face. "I came because I need to know if my son is building something real or just playing games with numbers. The second option was acceptable when you were eleven and stealing equipment to take apart. It's not acceptable now."

The examination hall continued around them. Candidates shifting positions, examiners marking boards, the ambient hum of a hundred suppressed Vitalis readings cycling through the lattice field. Normal morning activity. Normal world.

Roan folded the paper and slid it into his pocket.

"When did this post arrive?"

"Friday. I was at the night shift."

"And you waited until Monday to come."

"I needed to be sure I wasn't coming for the wrong reason." Mira stepped closer. Her voice dropped. "Now I'm here for the right one. What are you doing, Roan?"

He looked at her. The Codex Shard hummed against his sternum—a faint, warm pulse. The system was quiet, its potential untapped since yesterday's conversion. His actual ranking sat at six hundred and twelve. His father's name hadn't surfaced in any official record since the investigation closed six years ago.

And now someone had mailed his mother three words.

"I'm doing the ledger," Roan said. "And right now the columns don't add up."

Mira studied him. Whatever she was looking for, she either found it or decided it wasn't here. She nodded once.

"Then do the balance faster," she said. "And call me when you know."

She turned and left the way she'd come. Marrick watched her go with an expression that suggested he was updating his own mental file on the Vance family. Brendt exhaled.

"She's terrifying," he said.

"She's thorough." Roan pulled the paper from his pocket and reread the three words. "Someone wants our attention. The question is whether they want us to look for my father or look away from whatever else is happening."

"Is there a difference?"

"There's always a difference." Roan pocketed the paper again. "The difference is whether we find it before or after it finds us."

He looked across the examination hall. Marrick was back at his ledger. The lower-ranked candidates were regrouping near the eastern wall. Cyrus had vanished through the exit, Theon presumably guiding him somewhere private where the Ashford heir could recalculate whatever had just gone wrong in his worldview.

And somewhere in Greycross, someone had sent a dead man's wife a note that said the man wasn't dead.

Arithmetic had no incentive to flatter him. But sometimes the math was incomplete.

He turned toward the supplementary group.

"Roan." Brendt fell into step beside him. "That note. Do you think it's real?"

"I think someone's betting we'll act on it." Roan's voice stayed level. "The question is what they're betting on."

"And if we don't act?"

"They bet again."

The supplementary group was all lower-ranked candidates, candidates who'd placed below the four hundred mark in their last evaluation. Roan would be the highest-ranked candidate in the group by a margin of nearly four hundred points. Marrick had assigned him here as punishment, or because he wanted to see what Roan would do with an obvious disadvantage.

Roan intended to find out which.
