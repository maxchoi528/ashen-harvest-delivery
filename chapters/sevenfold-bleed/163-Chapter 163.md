Chapter 163: He Stays

Brendt woke nine hours later and immediately tried to negotiate.

This wasn't, strictly speaking, unexpected.

Roan had known Brendt Holloway long enough to understand that the man had three dominant survival instincts: complain and bargain, plus ask questions that more cautious people left buried. The order varied by injury level. After a near-fatal Rift wound, emergency vascular collapse, exposure to an alien civilization's inheritance, and enough sedative to make a small ox reconsider its career, Brendt opened his eyes, stared at the ceiling of the Black Spire infirmary, and said, "I would like to renegotiate my life choices."

His voice sounded like gravel wrapped in cloth.

Dr. Seth looked up from the tablet at the foot of the bed. "Denied. You are on bed rest."

"I didn't specify terms."

"You negotiated before anesthesia. You lost."

Brendt's eyes moved, slowly, until they found Roan in the chair beside the window.

Roan had not moved much during the night. He had slept badly, which was still sleep, and had spent the remaining hours sorting the contents of Aldric's letter into categories that could hurt them now, hurt them later, or hurt other people if positioned correctly. A useful division. Not comforting. Comfort had never been the point of documents written by dead men.

The infirmary room smelled of antiseptic, warmed metal, and the faint acidic bite of medical Vitalis cycling through wall-mounted purification arrays. Morning light cut across the floor in pale bars. Brendt had three monitors attached to his chest, one sealed wound line down his ribs, and a nutrient drip entering his left arm. The machines gave soft, orderly beeps, which Roan disliked on principle. Orderly beeps encouraged people to believe a situation was controlled.

It wasn't.

Brendt had survived because Roan had spent the last full inversion quota of that day correcting damage that should have killed him. That decision had cost them a clean kill in the Rift and nearly cost them a clean exit. It had revealed more of Roan's capabilities to Drogan than planned, to Ines than preferred, and to himself than convenient.

The most irritating part was that he would make the same decision again.

This suggested either a strategic exception or a personal flaw.

Roan was still classifying it.

Brendt blinked twice. "You look terrible."

"You look billed."

"How much?"

"Insurance covered the first intervention. The second was coded as expedition trauma. The third is disputed because Dr. Seth wrote 'patient stupidity' in the preliminary cause field."

Dr. Seth didn't look up. "Accurate charting saves lives."

Brendt closed his eyes. "I'm suing all of you when I can sit up."

"You can't afford a lawyer," Roan said.

"I'll represent myself."

"Then you'll lose twice."

Brendt made a weak noise that might've been laughter if his ribs had allowed ambition. The monitors objected with a faster rhythm. Dr. Seth looked at him over the rim of her glasses.

"Do that again," she said, "and I will sedate you for recreational disrespect."

Brendt went still.

Good. He could learn.

Roan waited until Dr. Seth finished adjusting the drip and left the room with a warning about visitors, stress, laughter, unauthorized movement, and something she called "that Vance stare," which apparently counted as a clinical irritant.

The door sealed behind her.

The room became quieter.

Brendt looked at the ceiling for a while.

Roan didn't fill the silence. People who filled silences usually gave away more than they received. Besides, Brendt deserved time to assemble his thoughts. Not because Roan had suddenly developed sentimental patience, but because the next few minutes would determine whether Brendt remained an asset, liability, hostage risk, or something more complicated.

Friend, a less useful part of his mind supplied.

Roan ignored it with the dignity it deserved.

At last, Brendt said, "I remember most of it."

"Define most."

"The letter. The photographs. The part where your father was apparently from a civilization that got murdered by the people running our civilization. The part where your spine has a family heirloom with opinions. The part where I made an extremely clever remark about you being strange."

"You remember enough."

"I also remember you telling me about evacuation packets."

"Good."

"How many do you have?"

"Enough."

Brendt turned his head slightly. The motion cost him. He hid most of it badly. "Roan."

"No."

"I didn't ask."

"You were going to ask whether there's a packet for your family."

Brendt's mouth closed.

There it was. The same predictable flaw that had made him jump between Roan and danger in the Rift. Brendt thought in networks of obligation. Family, friends, debts, favors, who needed help, who would be left holding the door when everyone else ran. It made him inefficient in ways that saved lives and dangerous in ways that invited leverage.

Roan reached into his coat and withdrew a sealed envelope.

He placed it on the bedside table.

Brendt stared at it.

"That isn't the main packet," Roan said. "It's a contingency note. If House Greel or House Vell moves against your mother or brothers, this goes to Headmaster Cole and Drogan, plus three journalists who hate each other enough to verify before printing. It contains no Codex information. Only financial irregularities, coercion indicators, and enough names to make the attack publicly expensive."

Brendt looked from the envelope to Roan.

"When did you make that?"

"Before the Rift."

"You made an emergency protection packet for my family before I knew your father was an alien refugee."

"Alien is imprecise."

"Roan."

"Yes."

"That isn't the part of the sentence under review."

Roan considered the ceiling, because ceilings rarely asked follow-up questions. "You were already connected to me. That made you a pressure point. Securing pressure points is basic risk management."

"You are allergic to saying anything normal, aren't you?"

"I said you were a pressure point. That was accurate."

"I noticed."

Brendt was quiet again.

Outside the window, Black Spire's central courtyard stirred with late-morning movement. Students crossed the stone paths in clusters, laughing, arguing, carrying training bags, complaining about assignments. Life had resumed its usual posture of not noticing the cliff under its feet. The tournament banners had been removed from the inner towers, but Roan still saw his own face occasionally on student tablets and advertisement boards. The school had discovered that a poor Greycross student beating House heirs was an excellent recruitment image, provided no one looked too closely at the murder attempts following him.

Roan would have billed them for unauthorized use of likeness if it didn't draw more attention.

He might still bill them.

Brendt swallowed. "I thought about quitting."

Roan looked back at him.

"For about twelve seconds," Brendt said. "Maybe fifteen. I was very injured, so my thoughts were moving slowly."

"Reasonable."

"It was a good twelve seconds. Very peaceful. In that version of my life, I transferred to logistics, stopped entering murder holes, gained weight, found a nice job auditing procurement fraud, and lived long enough to complain about my knees."

"Procurement fraud would bore you."

"Yes," Brendt said. "But in a dignified way."

Roan waited.

Brendt's fingers twitched against the sheet. "Then I realized something."

"Unfortunate."

"Usually." He breathed carefully, measuring the pain. "If I leave, you continue anyway. Drogan continues. Ines sells information to herself and calls it market research. The people who killed your father keep moving. And I sit somewhere safe, knowing I chose safety after you used your last impossible miracle to keep me breathing."

"That's a poor reason to stay."

"I know."

"Debt gets people killed."

"So does ignorance. So does loyalty. So does being in the wrong cafeteria at the wrong time." Brendt looked at him. "People keep dying for worse reasons."

Roan didn't answer immediately.

That was inconveniently true.

He had expected fear. Anger. Denial. A request for selective memory suppression, if Brendt had done enough research to know such services existed at terrible prices and worse reliability. He had prepared arguments for all of those. He hadn't prepared as much for calm acceptance, because calm acceptance from Brendt usually arrived wrapped around something reckless.

"You aren't useful if you stay out of guilt," Roan said.

"Good thing it isn't only guilt."

"Then list the reasons."

Brendt stared at him. "This isn't an interview."

"It's now."

"You're impossible."

"Reason one."

Brendt exhaled through his nose, then winced. "Fine. One: you need someone who can look at your plans and say the words 'that's insane' without trying to control you."

"Drogan does that."

"Drogan says it like a weather report. You ignore him."

"I ignore you too."

"Yes, but you at least hear me."

Roan didn't dispute it.

"Two," Brendt continued, "I'm better at people than you."

"Most furniture is better at people than me."

"Exactly. Three: I'm not useless in a fight anymore, despite recent medical evidence. Four: I know enough of your operations to spot inconsistencies if someone tries to impersonate, blackmail, or bait you through me. Five: if you push everyone away because the truth is dangerous, you will end up with only dangerous people left."

Roan looked at the monitors. Their beeping continued, irritatingly calm.

"That was almost intelligent," he said.

"Near-death expands perspective."

"No. Painkillers lower standards."

Brendt smiled faintly. "Six: I want to know how this ends."

"Curiosity is a worse reason than debt."

"Curiosity got civilization here."

"Civilization is currently run by murderers."

"Point withdrawn."

Roan leaned back in the chair.

The rational course was to reduce Brendt's exposure. He could keep Brendt on logistics, feed him limited information, use him as a protected communications node. That preserved the asset and minimized risk. It would also insult Brendt enough to make him circumvent the restriction in some stupidly honorable way, likely involving inadequate preparation and handwritten notes.

Better to include him deliberately, with boundaries.

The fact that Roan had already reached that conclusion before the conversation was irrelevant.

Mostly.

"Conditions," Roan said.

Brendt's expression sharpened.

"You don't enter another Rift until Dr. Seth clears your circulation and Drogan clears your shield work. You tell me immediately if symptoms return. No hiding pain because it's inconvenient. No acting as bait unless I approve the plan. No solo research into Founding Seven names. No contacting journalists. No telling your family anything beyond generic expedition danger. No jokes about alien refugees in public."

"That last one hurts."

"Good. It will be memorable."

"Do I get conditions?"

"You can try."

"You sleep at least five hours in every twenty-four for the next week."

"No."

"Four."

"No."

"Three, and you don't get to count unconsciousness from injury."

Roan considered.

"Accepted."

"You tell me when a plan has a death probability above, say, forty percent."

"Whose death?"

"Mine."

"Accepted."

"Yours."

"No."

"Roan."

"If I disclose every plan with a personal death probability above forty percent, conversation efficiency collapses."

Brendt stared.

"That was a joke," Roan said.

"Was it?"

"Partially."

"I hate that answer."

"Then improve your questions."

Brendt's mouth twitched. The monitor beeped faster. He controlled the laugh before Dr. Seth could materialize from the corridor like a punishment spirit.

Then his expression sobered.

"One real condition," he said. "No more deciding alone who gets to know enough to choose. Not about me. Not about anyone you call a pressure point."

Roan looked at him.

That was a dangerous request. Not morally. Morality was the least of it. Operationally. Information wasn't kindness. It was exposure. Every person who knew the truth became a node enemies could interrogate, bribe, threaten, misread, or kill. Roan didn't conceal information because he enjoyed mystery. He concealed it because secrets behaved like poison gas once released.

But Brendt wasn't asking for all secrets.

He was asking not to be managed like cargo.

Less efficient. Necessary.

"Limited agreement," Roan said. "You receive enough information to assess risks that directly involve you. Not more."

"Who decides enough?"

"I do."

"Bad answer."

"Reality often is."

Brendt closed his eyes. For a moment Roan thought he had fallen asleep again. Then Brendt said, "Fine. For now. But I reserve the right to call you a controlling bastard."

"Accurate charting saves lives."

Brendt opened one eye. "Did you just make a callback?"

"No."

"You did."

"Sedation."

"You made a joke."

Roan looked toward the window.

Brendt's face changed slowly. Not dramatically. The medication made drama difficult. But the corners of his eyes narrowed, and the crooked smile returned, smaller and more dangerous.

"You did," Brendt said.

"File a complaint."

"I would, but I can't sit up."

Roan's mouth moved before he could stop it.

Not much. Not a grin. Not smile people photographed, analyzed, or used as evidence that someone had softened. Just a brief crack in the usual arrangement of his face.

Brendt saw it.

Of course he did.

The bastard had survived a Tier Three Rift and apparently used the experience to become observant at inconvenient times.

Brendt's own smile widened, then immediately collapsed into a grimace as his ribs protested.

"Worth it," he wheezed.

"Idiot."

"Loyal idiot."

"That isn't better."

"It's for me."

Roan looked at him.

There were people one kept because they were useful. People one kept because removing them would cost too much. People one kept because they had information, leverage, resources, or violence positioned in favorable directions.

Then there was Brendt, who had just looked at an inheritance of murdered civilizations, political assassins, alien rites, and Roan Vance's entire catalog of bad decisions, and decided the correct response was to stay close enough to complain.

Irrational.

Reliable. The same thing, if repeated under pressure often enough.

Roan stood.

"Rest," he said.

"That's it?"

"You are medically restricted from dramatic speeches."

"I gave a good one."

"Six out of ten."

"Cruel."

"Generous."

He picked up the envelope from the table, slid it beneath the false bottom of the small personal effects drawer beside Brendt's bed, and sealed the panel with a thumbprint lock keyed to Brendt and Dr. Seth, but not himself.

Brendt noticed.

"You removed your access."

"It concerns your family."

"And?"

"Pressure point security."

"That sounded almost respectful."

"Sedation."

Brendt closed his eyes, still smiling faintly. "Coward."

Roan walked to the door.

As it opened, Dr. Seth appeared in the corridor with a cup of coffee and the expression of a woman prepared to remove visitors surgically.

"Is he stressed?" she asked.

"No," Roan said.

From the bed, Brendt murmured, "He's lying. But productively."

Dr. Seth looked between them.

Roan stepped out before she could decide which one to punish.

In the corridor, Drogan leaned against the opposite wall with his arms folded. He had been there long enough to hear some of the conversation and old enough not to pretend otherwise.

"He staying?" Drogan asked.

"Yes."

"You tried to stop him?"

"No."

Drogan's eyebrows lifted.

Roan adjusted his coat. "Stopping him would have encouraged unsupervised stupidity."

"That's the only reason?"

"The only relevant one."

Drogan grunted.

For several seconds, neither moved.

Then the old man said, "Your father had one of those too."

"A loyal idiot?"

"Several."

Roan glanced at him.

Drogan looked down the corridor toward the sealed infirmary door.

"They died badly," he said.

There was no warning in it. No lecture. Just fact, placed carefully.

Roan considered several answers.

Then he said, "Then we improve the process."

Drogan stared at him for a beat, then huffed something that might've been a laugh if it had survived training.

"You're a cold little bastard."

"Dr. Seth values accurate charting."

This time Drogan did laugh.

Once.

It sounded rusty and surprised both of them.

Roan kept that too.

Dr. Seth caught Roan again before he left the infirmary wing entirely.

She did it by standing in the middle of the corridor with a folder in one hand and the expression of someone who considered hallway ambushes part of medical practice. Roan stopped at a safe distance. Not because he feared her physically. Because doctors operated through institutional permissions, and institutional permissions were harder to stab.

"You," she said. "Scan room."

"No."

"That wasn't a request."

"I am not the patient."

"You are the reason half my ward has new insurance language. Scan room."

Drogan, traitor that he was, stepped aside and folded his arms.

Roan looked at him.

Drogan's expression suggested he had discovered neutrality and intended to abuse it.

"Three minutes," Roan said.

"Ten," Seth said.

"Four."

"Eight, and I don't record the part where your spinal readings make my equipment consider religion."

Roan entered the scan room.

The bed was cold, the overhead array too bright, and the wall monitor displayed a polite blue standby screen that would become rude the moment it noticed the Codex Shard. Dr. Seth attached two diagnostic tabs to his wrist, one to his throat, and one to the center of his back. The last made the Shard pulse in irritation.

[External diagnostic contact detected.]

[Recommendation: do not let her keep samples.]

Roan stared at the ceiling.

The machine hummed.

Seth watched the readings for exactly eight seconds before muttering, "Obnoxious."

"Medical term?"

"It's now. Your organ reinforcement is uneven. Not dangerous yet, but uneven. Whoever taught you to break through under battlefield conditions should be dragged behind a slow cart."

"The battlefield was poorly scheduled."

"Fields usually are." She flicked to another screen. "Your left lung took stress. Your sternum shows dispersed impact trauma. Your vascular response has adaptations. I don't like adaptations I can't bill."

"I will recover."

"Everyone says that until their liver files a resignation letter."

Roan turned his head slightly. "Can livers do that?"

"Yours might. It seems creative."

She adjusted the scanner to a lower frequency. The Vance Pendant warmed against his chest, dampening something before the machine could taste it. The screen blurred for a second and stabilized around a deliberately boring set of results. Roan kept his face blank.

Seth noticed anyway.

"You are hiding something," she said.

"Everyone is."

"Medically relevant?"

"Not to Brendt."

"That wasn't what I asked."

"It was the answer you can use."

For a moment, Seth looked like she might argue. Then she pulled the tabs off his wrist one by one.

"Fine. Medically useful answer: no peak training for five days. No self-inflicted blood cycling. No Rift exposure for two weeks. If your spine heats, your vision doubles, or you lose time, you come here before doing whatever stupid thing you were planning to do after."

"Define lose time."

Her eyes sharpened.

Roan regretted the question immediately.

"Has that happened?"

"Historically. Briefly."

"How briefly?"

"Three seconds. Months ago. No recurrence under normal load."

Seth wrote something down.

"That isn't a note I enjoy," Roan said.

"Then have less interesting symptoms."

She handed him a discharge slip that wasn't a discharge slip so much as a leash written in clinical language. Roan read it, crossed out one phrase, amended another, and handed it back.

Seth looked at the edits. "Did you just negotiate medical restrictions?"

"Your wording allowed interpretation."

"My wording allowed trust."

"Common drafting error."

She stared at him for three full seconds, then signed the amended version. "Get out of my scan room. And send Holloway's mother a message that doesn't sound like a hostage note. He keeps trying to write one and falling asleep."

Roan paused at the door.

"She knows he's injured?"

"She knows he's alive and stable, plus lying badly about how much pain he's in. Mothers are difficult to deceive."

Roan knew.

Mira Vance had raised him in a Greycross apartment with thin walls, bad heating, and silence that taught children which questions adults couldn't afford to answer. She had known when he lied before he learned the word for lying. Lyra had inherited the talent and added cruelty to it.

"I'll draft something," he said.

Seth blinked, apparently surprised by the lack of argument.

Roan left before she could examine that too.

A message to Brendt's mother would need enough truth to calm, enough vagueness to protect, and no sentence Brendt would later call emotionally constipated.

Difficult.

He would make Brendt approve it before sending.

When conscious.

That wasn't sentiment.

That was stakeholder review.

Useful day.

He had acquired a confirmed loyal liability, a medical leash, a revised recovery schedule, and one more adult willing to laugh at him for surviving incorrectly. Most people would have called that emotionally complicated. Roan wrote it down later as resource consolidation under adverse conditions. The phrasing was uglier. It was also safer.

No one important had died today, either.

For Roan, that remained the cleanest metric available. Everything else could be repaired, priced, delayed, or weaponized, provided nobody mistook survival for safety.
