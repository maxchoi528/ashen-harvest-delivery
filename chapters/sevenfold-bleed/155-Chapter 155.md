Chapter 155: The Deep Pulse

The containment chamber hit Roan like bad weather.

Sound returned first: alarms, ward pylons whining, someone shouting above, Brendt coughing wetly, Ines swearing, Drogan's boots scraping across concrete, the Rift gate hissing behind them like an animal offended by escape.

Air came second.

It tasted stale and metallic, plus blessedly human.

Roan landed on one knee on the Ironholm side of the breach. His hand struck the containment floor. Concrete. Real concrete, not ash-fused stone pretending to have civic purpose. The difference mattered more than he expected. His body recognized the surface before his mind finished inventory.

The Codex Shard pulsed weakly.

[Transition complete.]

[Quota status: 0/5.]

[Injury status: moderate.]

[Exposure risk logged.]

[Recommendation: do not re-enter immediately.]

Roan almost answered aloud. The Shard was developing a gift for insulting the obvious.

He stood.

The containment chamber wasn't empty.

That was the next problem.

Two municipal technicians in gray hazard suits stood near the western console with their hands raised. A Sentinel Bureau observer, young and pale, had backed against the far wall and looked as if he had just discovered several new categories of regret. Three automated turrets tracked the Rift gate, their barrels glowing but uncertain. The old blast door beyond the chamber remained open.

No Greel mercenaries yet.

Good.

Or bad. If they weren't here, they were outside.

Brendt lay on the floor beside the pylon bank, half-propped against Ines's knee while she cut away the ruined shoulder armor. His face had gone the color of old paper. The wound remained sealed, but the seal was ugly and dark veins of locked contamination traced the skin beneath the collarbone.

Alive.

Roan checked again because optimism killed more quietly than monsters.

Breathing: shallow but regular. Pulse: fast. Consciousness: intermittent. Bleeding: controlled. Rift contamination: locked but not purged. Mobility: terrible.

"Medical kit," Ines snapped at one of the technicians.

The man stared.

Ines pointed her sidearm at the floor near his foot and fired.

The gunshot cracked through the chamber.

The technician moved.

"Effective," Roan said.

"I'm billing for ammunition."

"Denied."

"He's alive because I had a sidearm."

"Unlicensed sidearms don't qualify for reimbursement."

"You are impossible."

"Later."

Drogan staggered once and caught himself on a console. Blood ran down his cheek and along his left forearm. He looked worse than he would admit and better than the champion preferred.

Roan crossed to him. "Knee?"

"Functional."

"That wasn't the question."

"Then stop asking bad questions."

Fair.

The Sentinel observer found his voice. "You need to remain in the chamber for decontamination protocol. No one leaves until the Bureau clears—"

Roan looked at him.

The observer stopped.

Not because Roan used pressure. He didn't have spare Vitalis to waste on theater. He looked at the man while covered in Rift ash and blood, plus sovereign residue, with Sidewound still in his hand and zero patience left for bureaucratic obstruction.

The observer swallowed.

Drogan spoke without turning. "Name."

"Observer Pellan, Sentinel Bureau field office."

"Pellan, if you try to enforce standard protocol while a student bleeds out under locked Rift contamination, I will sign the incident report after breaking your jaw. Choose."

Pellan looked between Drogan and Roan.

"Medical evacuation," he said quickly. "Immediate."

"Good choice," Roan said.

Brendt coughed again.

This time blood came with it.

Ines pressed a patch to his sternum. "Need purge support. He won't survive normal transport if the lock slips."

Roan knelt beside Brendt.

The Shard remained hollow and empty around the quota count. No inversion. No clean reversal. No convenient cheat. He had spent it. Correctly, but spent was spent.

Other options.

Black Rite could refuse mental intrusion, not purge contamination. Sidewound could cut seams, not infections. Vance Pendant could sink signal, perhaps lower recognition, perhaps stabilize some command trace. Drogan knew field medicine but not Codex-linked contamination. Ines knew salvage toxins and emergency patches. Municipal kits were built for Tier Two exposure at best.

Need a hospital.

Need to reach it.

Need not to get intercepted.

The last item made the hairline calculation worse.

"Drogan," Roan said.

"I know."

Of course he did.

The Greel mercenaries hadn't attacked inside the chamber because the chamber had cameras, municipal logs, Sentinel presence, and too many wards. They would attack in the transfer gap: between sealed Rift facility and official evacuation route, where jurisdiction blurred and chaos provided witness contamination.

Iris Vell wouldn't be stupid enough to walk in personally.

Unless she was angry.

Anger made smart people punctual and visible.

Roan hoped she wasn't that angry.

He expected disappointment.

- break -

The containment technicians kept staring at the Rift gate.

Roan understood the impulse. The gate was doing several things a gate shouldn't do after a standard expedition return. The gray light inside the arch had thickened, threaded with black strands that curled toward the chamber floor before retreating. The tether line hummed like a plucked wire. One of the ward pylons displayed a diagnostic symbol Roan couldn't read, then corrected itself to a municipal error code that meant *please ask a better engineer*.

The Hollowed City hadn't followed them through.

Not physically.

That didn't mean nothing had crossed.

Roan stepped close enough to the gate for the pendant to cool sharply against his sternum. The Shard produced no new prompt. It had exhausted its immediate warnings or decided Roan had collected enough bad news for one day. Either possibility annoyed him.

A smear of black residue clung to the tether line where it entered the breach. Not random. Spiral pattern. Command residue, perhaps from the sovereign's final pressure. If left in place, it might let the creature read the exit, mark the route, or make the next poor municipal team unlucky in a more interesting way.

Roan pointed to it. "Burn that section."

One technician looked at him as if students weren't authorized to issue containment instructions.

Drogan turned his head.

The technician burned the section.

Good. Hierarchy retained some uses when attached to people with reputations for violence.

"Record it first," Roan said.

The technician froze.

"Before you burn it," Roan clarified. "Evidence becomes less useful as smoke."

The man took three photographs with shaking hands, then applied a blue flame capsule to the tether. The residue recoiled from the heat and made a thin sound. Everyone in the chamber heard it. No one commented. Some truths entered a room and didn't require conversation to make themselves unpleasant.

Pellan watched Roan instead of the gate.

That was new.

The young observer had started the day with bureaucratic confidence. The Rift had removed most of it. The ambulance bay would finish the work, though he didn't know that yet. At the moment he was still assembling categories: student, survivor, possible security threat, possible asset, probable paperwork disaster.

Roan had been called worse by more important people.

They left through the emergency service corridor eleven minutes later.

Too slow.

Brendt was strapped to a collapsible medical frame stolen from the municipal station. Ines had attached three improvised stabilizers and one device she refused to identify beyond "not explosive unless mishandled." Drogan walked beside the frame with his blade hidden under his coat. Roan walked behind, close enough to see Brendt's breathing and far enough to watch the corridor intersections.

Pellan followed because Drogan had ordered him to and because fear had temporarily made him sensible.

The two technicians remained behind to manage the unstable Rift gate.

That was acceptable. They were less useful outside and more likely to panic under gunfire.

The eleven minutes weren't idle.

Roan disliked idle minutes more than dangerous ones. Dangerous minutes at least declared their shape. Idle minutes tempted injured people to sit still and mistake stillness for recovery. He used them.

First, he stripped the broken fragments of Brendt's shoulder armor and kept the claw-scarred plate. The angle of the tear confirmed the attack pattern and might later identify the specific Husk strain. Also, Brendt would complain about losing the armor, and having the fragment would shorten the argument.

Second, he made Pellan sign a preliminary incident receipt stating that Brendt Holloway required immediate evacuation due to Tier Three contamination stabilized by unknown means. Pellan protested the wording *unknown means*. Roan offered *means currently above your clearance and below my patience*. Pellan signed.

Third, he photographed Drogan's wounds before the old man noticed.

Drogan noticed on the fourth photograph.

"Delete that."

"No."

"Roan."

"Evidence of injury supports unauthorized evacuation. Also future blackmail."

"You are a disease."

"Your limp is worse when you lie."

Drogan glared. Roan took a fifth photograph.

Pellan's signature mattered for another reason.

The Sentinel Bureau liked clean narratives. Unauthorized student expedition, unexpected Rift complication, heroic rescue, tragic injury: clean enough. Greel mercenaries inside a municipal evacuation corridor, dead-zone canisters deployed under a sealed station, access revoked between containment and ambulance bay: less clean. With Pellan's signature on the medical emergency, anyone trying to bury the incident would need to explain why an official observer authorized evacuation and then somehow failed to notice armed interference.

Bureaucracies were slow predators. Roan didn't respect them, but he had learned to feed them properly.

Give a bureaucracy a vague injustice and it produced meetings.

Give it a signed contradiction and it produced teeth.

Maybe small teeth. Maybe late teeth. Still useful.

"You are making me complicit," Pellan said when the receipt transmitted.

"No," Roan replied. "You were present. Complicity depends on what you do next."

"That's not reassuring."

"It wasn't designed to be."

Pellan looked at Brendt, then at the blood on the floor, then at Drogan calmly applying a sealant strip to his own arm with his teeth. Something in the observer's face shifted from fear into the duller expression of a man realizing the paperwork might be the easiest part of his day.

"They'll ask why I listened to you," he said.

"Tell them you didn't. Tell them you listened to Drogan."

Drogan grunted. "Leave me out of your lies."

"It's not a lie. You threatened his jaw."

"That was a promise."

"Even better."

Ines tightened Brendt's stabilizer. "This conversation is lowering the quality of care."

"His pulse?" Roan asked.

"Bad but ambitious."

"Contamination?"

"Locked. I don't know for how long. Your system bought time, not a cure."

Roan looked at her.

She looked back, one eyebrow raised. "Please. You think I watched that and filed it under normal?"

"I prefer people filing things incorrectly."

"Then be less obvious when rewriting injuries."

Fair.

He would consider that after surviving the next room.

The comment also confirmed something useful: Ines had seen enough to be dangerous but not enough to be certain. Dangerous uncertainty could be managed with contracts. Certainty required graves or trust, and Roan disliked both as first options.

Fourth, he checked the pendant.

The stain on it bothered him more than the cracked chain.

A crack he understood. Breakage could be repaired, reinforced, or replaced. A stain meant contact. Something in the Rift had touched the anchor deeply enough to leave residue on metal Eckhart had built to refuse attention. Roan had no way to know whether the stain was tracking, corrosion, or the pendant expressing a cost in a form visible to people without dead civilizations in their spines.

He wrapped it in a clean cloth for three seconds, then unwrapped it. The cloth bore the same gray smear. So not surface residue.

Excellent. Another problem pretending to be jewelry.

He put it back under his shirt anyway. Removing a compromised defense while Greel mercenaries waited outside would be like throwing away a cracked helmet because it offended him. The cracked helmet still stopped some rocks. It also kept enemies guessing which defenses remained intact, and uncertainty had more immediate value than neat equipment. In a day full of bad exchanges, paying comfort for ambiguity was one of the cheaper trades. Not good. Cheap. There was a difference, and he had learned it early in Greycross, where good choices were luxuries and cheap ones kept people breathing longer than pride ever did.

The disk itself felt warm to the touch from the gate transition. No visible crack. No surface distortion. But the gray stain had spread to the back of the chain where it touched his neck. He rubbed it once with his thumb. It didn't come off.

He would take the whole assembly to Eckhart. If Eckhart was still alive, willing, and not being watched by people with questions Roan didn't want asked. That required surveillance checking. Ines had the shop under watch. Assuming she hadn't subcontracted. He added verification to his mental list.

Fifth, he gave Ines the smallest of the sealed temple fragments they had recovered.

She stared at it. "Payment?"

"Collateral."

"For what?"

"Keeping Brendt alive through transport."

"You trust me more with collateral?"

"I trust your accounting."

Her expression shifted. Not soft. Ines didn't do soft under fluorescent lights and blood pressure alarms. But she slipped the fragment into an inner case with the care of someone receiving a knife handle-first.

"Accepted," she said.

Good.

People fought harder for debts than for gratitude. Gratitude decayed. Debts accumulated interest.

The corridor ran beneath the old transit station, a maintenance route connecting the containment chamber to an ambulance bay on the eastern side of the depot. Fluorescent lights flickered overhead. Warning signs peeled from the walls. A ventilation fan clicked every nine seconds.

At the third intersection, Roan stopped.

Everyone else stopped because they had learned.

"What?" Pellan whispered.

Roan held up one hand.

The ventilation fan clicked.

Nine seconds.

Clicked again.

Eight.

The timing had changed.

Roan drew Sidewound.

Drogan's knife appeared in his hand.

Ines lowered one hand toward the medical frame's underside.

"Left corridor," Roan said.

Pellan stared at the empty passage. "There's nothing—"

The first dead-zone canister also answered a question Roan had not asked aloud.

The technology hadn't been copied perfectly from the Hollowed City. The silence field bloomed too broadly, collapsed too quickly, and produced violet static instead of the absolute absence inside the Rift. That meant Greel had either salvaged principles without understanding the whole mechanism or received partial instruction from something that preferred them ignorant.

Both possibilities were ugly.

Partial understanding made humans inventive in the worst way. A complete dead zone suppressed Shard access. A crude one might shear Vitalis channels, scramble wards, or rupture a stabilizer if fired too close to Brendt. If they used another canister near the frame, the contamination lock might fail.

Roan's mind reordered the field.

Brendt couldn't touch another silence field. Drogan had to stay mobile. One Greel soldier needed to survive for questioning. And Iris — she needed to watch her own disposal.

The last item wasn't essential for survival. But it had long-term value, and Roan could afford the positioning.

Maybe.

A fifth instinct tried to add itself: punish Iris immediately. Roan rejected it. Punishment felt clean only to people who confused sequence with satisfaction. Iris was more useful frightened and compromised, then alive until her information stopped appreciating.

A metal canister rolled around the corner.

Roan kicked it back.

The canister bounced once, disappeared into the left corridor, and detonated in a burst of white foam and violet sparks. The blast didn't roar. It swallowed sound. For half a second the corridor became silent.

Dead-zone technology.

Human-made or at least human-deployed.

Greel, then.

Drogan moved first.

He threw his knife through the fading foam. Someone gasped on the other side. Not dead. Injured.

"Ambush," Ines said.

"Yes," Roan replied.

"Professional?"

"They used the dead-zone principle badly. Semi-professional."

"That's comforting only to you."
