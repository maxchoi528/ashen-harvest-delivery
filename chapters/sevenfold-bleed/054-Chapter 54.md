Chapter 54: Iris Vell

The morning sparring session ended three minutes before the official announcement, which meant Warden Drogan had planned it that way.

Roan landed on the practice yard's packed earth, rolled his shoulders, and walked toward the notice board without breaking stride. Seventeen names on parchment. His in the lower third. Deliberate.

He scanned the tournament bracket with the same detachment he'd use to read a market ledger. Third-round opponent: Theon Mercer. Class Two, Black Spire West Campus. Tempered rank, Vitalis reading estimated eight hundred. Specialization: defensive counter-strikes with a reach advantage.

His jaw tightened. The variables pointed in one direction. Mercer fought like a landmine, patient, still, dangerous only when you committed. A style that punished impatience and rewarded precision. One that forced an opponent to either overextend or accept a grinding attrition match.

Roan had neither the time nor the temperament for grinding.

In the upper stands, a girl with dark hair and a neutral expression lowered her rented Vitalis lens. Iris Vell had arrived late enough to avoid attention, early enough to catch the bracket posting. She watched Roan Vance walk away from the board, cataloguing his gait, his lack of reaction, the way Brendt fell into step beside him as if choreographed.

She'd read the file on Aldric Vance's son three times. The data said moderate talent, no remarkable lineage, a statistical outlier in the first two rounds. The lens said something else. His recovery curve between matches was too steep. His output spikes didn't align with his input baselines. The numbers didn't add up, and Iris had been trained to notice when numbers lied.

She pocketed the lens and made a note in her palm-codex: *Anomaly confirmed. Approach timeline: today.*

"You're up against the wall," Brendt said, appearing at Roan's shoulder. "Mercer's the real deal. Took out a Hardened-class cadet in last year's finals, nearly broke the kid's arm before the match was called."

"Defensive specialist."

"Near-perfect counter rate. Most people who fight him either panic and overcommit, or they freeze up and lose on points." Brendt studied the bracket. "Neither option works for you."

Because Roan couldn't afford to look weak. Couldn't afford to draw out matches and reveal patterns. Couldn't afford to let Mercer's style dictate the tempo.

He already knew what he was going to do. The question was whether the cost was worth it.

The match was scheduled for the afternoon session, third ring. Roan spent the intervening hours in the training hall, running Crimson Wake drills against wooden posts. The technique demanded blood loss as catalyst, each strike hemorrhaging a measurable quantity of Vitalis-infused fluid. In exchange, the subsequent surge multiplied his cutting power by a factor that made brute-force defense irrelevant.

The arithmetic was elegant. But Mercer wouldn't give him the opening.

He switched to Sevenfold Bleed conditioning. The first strike form was catalogued in his muscle memory now, seven Vitalis channels burning simultaneously, the momentary agony transmuted into structural reinforcement. He'd used it twice in the tournament already. The pattern was becoming predictable.

The second strike form was faster, messier. Designed for close quarters where the reach disadvantage mattered most. He'd never tested it in actual combat. The risk of backlash was higher because the timing window was narrower — a heartbeat of genuine damage, exactly like the system required, but compressed into a movement that lasted less than a heartbeat.

The margin for error was a hair's width.

He ran through the sequence anyway. Again. Again. The burning in his Vitalis channels settled into a familiar hum as his body adapted, rebuilt, strengthened in ways that violated every natural law of recovery time.

By noon, he'd consumed his post-breakfast Inversion slot on a training-induced strain. The Codex Shard hummed approval, converting the minor damage into a quiet increment of Vitalis that brought his total to 1,487, still below his theoretical maximum for Tempered, but climbing.

[Daily capacity: 2/4.]

[Two more before reset. Your father called this "banking daylight." — A.V.]

He ignored the commentary and went to find food. The tournament grounds were busier than the previous rounds. Spectators packed the upper tiers, many of them scanning the combat rings with specialized lenses that displayed Vitalis readings in real time. The technology was illegal in formal matches, privacy legislation protecting combatants from public Vitalis profiling, but enforcement was spotty at best. Anyone with two hundred credits to spare could rent a lens from the vendors clustered outside the arena.

Roan didn't bother. He already knew what his public reading would show: somewhere between six hundred and seven hundred, depending on how much he'd recovered since morning. Deliberately suppressed, deliberately unremarkable. Let them think he was a middling talent scraping through on technique.

The third ring was smaller than the central arena, designed for closer-quarters work where audiences could see the strikes land. Mercer was already there, running through a warm-up sequence that stressed hip rotation and weight transfer. Tall. Lean. A build that looked underfed until you noticed the layers of muscle packed onto every limb.

Mercer's eyes tracked Roan as he entered. No hostility. No dismissiveness. Just the flat, professional assessment of someone who'd already calculated the distance, the angles, the optimal strike windows.

Roan settled into his stance on the opposite side of the ring. The referee, a tired-looking Paragon-class instructor, raised her hand.

"Standard tournament rules. First to three clean strikes or incapacitation. Vitalis output capped at tournament maximum. Match begins on my mark."

The cap was meaningless for Roan. His Inversion bypassed the standard output ceiling by converting damage into power rather than generating it from his own reserves. But he'd learned not to abuse that advantage openly. Last round's opponent had noticed something off about the way Roan's strikes landed — he'd kept his mouth shut, but the way he'd looked at Roan afterward suggested the kid was filing that observation somewhere.

Roan couldn't afford too many of those.

"Begin."

Mercer didn't move.

The opening sequence of a defensive counter-strike specialist was always the same: nothing. They waited. They watched. They let you fill the silence with impatience, let you waste energy on feints that revealed nothing. The counter came when you committed to something real, when the pattern of your frustration became readable.

Roan stepped forward.

Not a feint. A real step, closing distance by half a meter before stopping. His weight shifted onto his lead foot, then his back foot — a subtle redistribution that mirrored the preparation for a Sevenfold Bleed strike.

Mercer's eyes tracked the shift. His body tensed, ready to pivot and deflect.

Roan stepped back.

The sequence repeated. Step, shift, pause, retreat. Again. He was painting a picture of indecision, of a fighter too cautious to commit and too proud to accept a stalemate. The audience would read it as weakness. The Vitalis-lens spectators would see his readings fluctuating without purpose, burning energy for nothing.

Mercer's expression didn't change, but his weight shifted slightly forward. Not enough to be readable. Just a suggestion that he was beginning to form hypotheses about Roan's strategy.

The third time through, Roan stepped forward and didn't stop.

The movement was fast, faster than his earlier pacing had suggested. His lead foot hit the deck hard, his body coiling into the Sevenfold Bleed preparation, Vitalis channels already igniting with the characteristic burn of imminent strike.

Mercer pivoted, expecting the counter.

Roan wasn't there.

He'd seen it in the man's hip rotation. Mercer's defensive style was mechanically predictable at the point of commitment, because perfection required consistency. When Mercer committed to a counter, he moved in a specific arc designed to intercept and redirect. That arc had a blind spot: the thirty-degree wedge directly behind the initial pivot point, if you were fast enough to arrive there before the redirection completed.

Roan was fast enough.

He slid past Mercer's extended guard, his blade arm cocked for a strike that couldn't land from that angle. The Sevenfold Bleed windup required a full extension. He didn't have it. The Vitalis channels burn was already peaking, the energy already gathered for release that couldn't happen.

He switched.

The Sevenfold Bleed second strike form was uglier than the first, designed for exactly this situation — for the moment when you'd committed too far and needed to salvage something from the approach. It burned fewer Vitalis channels at once, but it burned them harder, and the margin between successful conversion and backlash was razor-thin.

He triggered it anyway.

The sensation was immediate — not pain, but a hollow wrench through his left side, centering on the fourth and fifth Vitalis channels. The Codex Shard registered the disturbance, processed it, converted it.

[Sidewound absorbed.]

[Conversion successful: +67 Vitalis.]

[Second strike form: damage pattern logged. — A.V.]

The surge hit his muscles at exactly the moment Mercer was completing his pivot, trying to reset his guard. Roan's body was already moving, already inside the man's reach, already driving an elbow strike toward the gap beneath Mercer's ribs.

The impact was solid. Not clean enough for a full point — the angle was wrong, the force distribution suboptimal — but hard enough to shove Mercer back three steps and break his rhythm entirely.

"Point one, Vance," the referee called.

The audience noise shifted. Not excitement exactly — more surprise. The Vitalis-lens crowd had watched Roan's reading spike and crash in the space of a single exchange, a pattern that didn't match any known combat methodology.

Mercer's eyes found Roan's across the ring. The flat professional assessment was gone. In its place was something sharper. Something that recognized he'd just been hit by a technique that shouldn't exist in a Tempered-class fighter's repertoire.

In the stands, Iris Vell lowered her lens again. The spike-and-crash was clean. Too clean. It looked like a burst technique, but the restorative curve was wrong — he'd recovered from the channel burn in under two seconds. Normal Tempered fighters took at least forty-five. She updated her palm-codex: *Confirmed. Technique pattern matches restricted archive 7-Alpha. Approach urgency: high.*

The second exchange came faster.

Mercer was done waiting. His defensive style shifted to aggressive containment, driving forward with strikes designed to force Roan's guard open rather than waiting for an overcommit. It was an adaptation — the correct adaptation — but it was also a concession. He'd recognized that his patience strategy had failed, that Roan would wait him out until the time ran down.

Roan retreated two steps, then a third. His guard stayed high, his movement economical. The math of defensive fighting: every step backward was a step closer to the ring's edge. Too much retreating meant losing on position even if you never took a clean hit.

Mercer pressed harder.

The counter-strike came at the end of a combination, three jabs that Roan blocked, a hook that caught his forearm, a straight punch that he ducked under. The fourth strike of the sequence was the trap: a low kick that Roan had to check or take, which meant his weight had to shift onto his lead leg, which meant —

The straight punch was already traveling toward his face when he triggered the Inversion.

Not a full conversion. Just a micro-adjustment, a taste of damage accepted and redirected. The punch clipped his jaw anyway — glancing, not clean — but the impact registered as a Sidewound and processed, then fed back into his muscle timing.

He was already moving when the punch landed, his head clearing the worst of the impact. His body twisted, his hip rotating into a strike that Mercer couldn't see coming because the angle was wrong, because Roan's guard had been too high, because none of this matched any pattern Mercer had catalogued.

The elbow caught Mercer in the throat.

"Point two, Vance."

The crowd noise was louder now. The Vitalis-lens readings were going haywire — a fighter who took damage and somehow converted it into counterattack timing was either a freak of nature or a cheater. Neither possibility was good for Roan's long term anonymity.

Mercer was breathing hard. The throat impact had compressed his windpipe temporarily, leaving him with a wheeze that he was trying to hide. His eyes were flat again, but there was something else underneath — something that looked like the cold fury of a man who'd just realized he was outmatched.

One point to go.

The third exchange lasted eleven seconds.

Mercer came in with everything. The full offensive sequence of a defensive specialist who'd abandoned his style's core advantage: relentless, technically perfect, absolutely committed. He wasn't trying to counter anymore. He was trying to overwhelm.

Roan retreated until he couldn't. His back hit the ring's edge. The crowd noise reached a peak.

Mercer drove a combination toward his midsection, three strikes that had to be blocked or deflected because taking any of them would mean the match. Roan's forearms rang with the impacts. His guard held. The fourth strike was a head kick — telegraphed, easy to avoid. Except that avoiding it meant leaning back, which meant his weight shifted onto his heels, which meant he couldn't generate power for a counterstrike. Mercer knew this. Mercer was counting on it.

The kick was a feint. The real threat was the knee strike that came underneath it, targeting Roan's thigh to collapse his standing leg.

He saw it coming. A layered feint — the head kick forced a specific response, the response made a specific counter impossible, the knee strike exploited the resulting position. It was multi-layered offense that separated good fighters from great ones.

The Codex Shard processed the incoming damage too close. The knee was going to hit. The angle was going to hyperextend something. The Sidewound would be real enough to convert.

He let it land.

The impact was grinding — not screaming pain, but a deep, wrong sensation that shot up his leg and made his vision strobe at the edges. The Codex Shard converted it instantly, the damage registering and processing before the physiological shock could slow him down.

[Mortal Sidewound absorbed.] [Fatal-tier conversion registered.]

[That was stupid. Effective. But stupid. — A.V.]

The capacity warning flashed through his mind even as his body was already moving, already turning the impact into pivot, already using the force of Mercer's strike to spin himself into a position that the knee attack had made impossible a half-second earlier.

His heel caught Mercer in the temple as the man was still following through on his strike.

"Match to Vance. Three points to two."

The crowd erupted. Half of them were cheering because they'd bet on the upset. The other half were staring at their Vitalis lenses with expressions that suggested they'd just watched something impossible.

Roan straightened. His leg felt strange — a hollow charge where the Vitalis had been pulled and pushed, not pain but an absence of familiar feedback. The margin had been a hair's width. The real cost wasn't his leg. It was his cover.

He found Brendt in the corridor outside the medical station, where Roan had gone to get his leg wrapped. Brendt's face was carefully neutral, the expression of someone who'd just watched a friend do something brilliant and stupid in equal measure.

"Seven days," Brendt said.

"Minimum cost for maximum effect."

"That's not what I —" Brendt stopped, started again. "The referee flagged something. She didn't call it, but she flagged it. Your damage intake patterns don't match your output."

Roan didn't answer. The medical attendant was wrapping his thigh with practiced efficiency, her expression suggesting she'd seen worse and wasn't impressed by any of it.

"The Vitalis readings are going to get analyzed," Brendt continued. "After the tournament. Someone's going to run the tally and realize that you took more damage than your body should've been able to sustain, and then —"

"Then they'll have questions I can't answer."

"Roan."

"I know." He accepted the wrapped leg, tested his weight on it. The feeling was strange but functional — no lingering ache, just a quiet hum of converted energy. He'd had worse. "The ledger stopped working the moment I decided to compete publicly. Every match is a data point. Every data point narrows the possibilities. Eventually they'll figure out that I'm not fighting like a normal Tempered, and then —"

He didn't finish the sentence.

The medical attendant finished her work and left without comment. The corridor was quieter now, the post-match crowd having dispersed toward the next bout or the betting halls or the food vendors.

"Then what?" Brendt asked.

Roan stood, tested his weight again. The leg held. Not comfortable, but functional.

"Then I find out what happens when people know." He met Brendt's eyes. "I'm not sure I want to know. But I'm out of options that don't involve knowing."

He walked toward the exit. Behind him, he could feel Brendt's gaze tracking his movement — worried, calculating, already running scenarios that Roan didn't have the bandwidth to consider.

The tournament bracket would be posted tomorrow. Two more rounds until the finals. Two more chances for his patterns to be analyzed, for his techniques to be recognized, for the wrong person to start asking the right questions.

He reached the training yard entrance and stopped.

Warden Drogan was waiting.

The old man's face was unreadable, but his posture suggested he'd been there for a while. Watching. Calculating. Running his own version of the arithmetic that Roan had been ignoring.

"Office," Drogan said. "Now."

Roan's jaw tightened. He nodded.

The Warden turned and walked. Roan followed, his stride even, his mind already running through the possibilities of what Drogan had seen, what Drogan had concluded, what Drogan was going to do about it.

The corridor stretched ahead of them, quiet except for the distant sounds of the tournament continuing without him. Somewhere in the stands, a crowd was cheering for the next match, the next upset, the next data point in a competition that didn't know it was being used as cover for something older and more dangerous than any of them could imagine.

Drogan's office was in the Warden's wing, a cramped space cluttered with requisition forms and training schedules. The door closed behind them with a sound that felt more final than it should have.

"Sit," Drogan said.

Roan sat. His leg sent a quiet pulse of converted energy through his system, but no pain.

The Warden settled into his own chair, the wood groaning under his weight. Neither of them spoke. Drogan's eyes were fixed on Roan with an intensity that suggested he was reading something written underneath Roan's skin.

"That was your father's technique," Drogan said finally. "The conversion. The timing. The way you turn damage into advantage." He paused. "I've seen it before. A long time ago."

Roan went still. Not fear. Something colder. Something that felt like the ground shifting beneath his feet.

"You weren't supposed to see that," Roan said.

"No." Drogan's expression didn't change. "I wasn't. Which means we need to have a conversation that I was hoping to delay until after the tournament." He reached into his desk, pulled out a slim folder, set it on the desk between them. "Someone else saw it too. This morning's Vitalis data flagged an anomaly during your second match. It's been routed to the archives."

The archives. Where the Conclave kept records that were supposed to stay buried.

Roan looked at the folder. His name was written on the tab in official script, along with a date that was three days in the future.

"Disciplinary review," Drogan said. "Standard procedure for anomalous combat patterns. They'll pull your match recordings, run them through analysis, and start asking questions you can't answer." He leaned back. "Unless we do something about it first."

The room went quiet. Outside, the tournament continued — another match starting, another set of Vitalis readings taken, another pattern catalogued and filed away.

"What do you suggest?" Roan asked.

Drogan's eyes met his. For the first time since Roan had known the man, the Warden's expression held something that looked like uncertainty.

"That depends," Drogan said, "on how much you're willing to tell me about what you've become."

Roan didn't answer immediately. The silence stretched. Drogan waited.

"I'll tell you enough," Roan said finally, "to decide whether you want to help me or bury me."

He stood, the motion smooth. The leg had settled; the hollow charge had faded into a baseline hum.

"I need to think about how to frame it. Give me until tomorrow morning."

Drogan studied him for a long moment, then nodded once. "Tomorrow morning. My office. Before the quarterfinals."

Roan turned and walked out.

The corridor was dimmer now, the afternoon light slanted through high windows. He was halfway to the stairwell when he noticed the girl leaning against the wall near the exit, arms crossed, expression neutral.

She was average height, dark hair pulled back, student robes that marked her as Spire campus — not Black Spire, he noted; the insignia was Sundermere. She didn't move as he approached, but her eyes tracked him with an attentiveness that felt deliberate.

"You're Roan Vance," she said. Not a question.

He stopped. Measured the distance to the exit. Measured the girl's stance — relaxed but balanced, a fighter's posture dressed in civilian clothes.

"Who's asking?"

She smiled, a small, practiced expression that didn't reach her eyes. "Iris Vell. I watched your match. The footwork in the third exchange was interesting. You used a recovery curve that doesn't match any registered technique in the Spire combat archives."

Roan's pulse didn't change. "Plenty of techniques aren't registered."

"True." She pushed off the wall, closing the distance by a step. "But most of them don't leave a Vitalis residue that matches restricted archive patterns."

His mind snapped through possibilities. Denial. Deflection. Threat assessment. She was Tempered, likely, based on gait and breath control. No visible weapons. The fact that she was approaching him directly, not reporting to Conclave, meant she wanted something.

"What do you want?" he asked.

Iris tilted her head, studying him the way he'd studied Mercer's hip rotation — looking for the tell, the pattern, the opening.

"Conversation. Coffee. Tomorrow, after the tournament." She held up a hand before he could refuse. "I'm not here to expose you. If I were, I'd have already filed a report. I'm here because I think we have something interesting to discuss."

She let that settle, then turned and walked toward the stairwell, her footsteps light on the stone.

"Glassline Café," she said over her shoulder. "Six bells. Don't be late."

Roan watched her disappear into the stairwell's shadows. The corridor was quiet again.

He had a Warden to brief in the morning, a quarterfinal to fight in the afternoon, and now a daughter of a family he didn't fully trust wanted to buy him coffee.

The columns were stacking in ways he hadn't anticipated. The arithmetic was still incomplete.

But it was getting harder to call it coincidence.
