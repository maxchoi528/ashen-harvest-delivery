Chapter 9: The Cost of Knowing

Three days after the pendant fused to his spine, Roan learned that power without leverage was just another kind of poverty.

He discovered this in an alley off Greycross Avenue, eleven minutes past midnight, when three men stepped out of the shadows and blocked both ends of the narrow passage. The one in front was broad-shouldered, early twenties, with a tattoo curling up the side of his neck, a snake eating something that might have been a smaller snake. Initiate tier, maybe 200 on a generous day. The other two flanked, younger, less certain, holding pipes that they clearly hoped they wouldn't have to use.

Roan stopped walking.

He had known this was coming. Not the specifics, the alley, the time, the tattoo, but the shape of it. He had been wearing Aldric's pendant openly since the morning after the Bloodscourge. Word traveled in Greycross. People noticed things, a poor kid wearing something that caught light wrong, iron that moved like it was heavier than physics allowed. They talked. And talk attracted the kind of people who believed that other people's inheritances were just unclaimed gifts.

He had prepared for this conversation.

The leader stepped forward. "That around your neck. Give it."

Roan considered his options.

He could run. The alley had a fire escape at the far end, rusted but climbable. He could reach it in under four seconds. The thugs would follow, but they were heavier, slower, less practiced with vertical surfaces. Escape probability, high.

He could talk. Negotiation required leverage he didn't have yet. He knew their gang, the Ironmarsh Reclaimers, a minor player in Greycross's informal economy, mostly protection and resale. He knew their territory, five blocks of industrial zoning that produced nothing worth protecting. He knew their leader's name, Harver, from a police blotter Brendt had shown him. None of that helped here.

He could fight.

The Codex Shard pulsed against his spine—a neutral thrum, no different from a heartbeat or a steady breath. He could feel the Vitalis in his core, a reservoir that had been growing for three days, accumulating through sleep and stillness as his body adjusted to the new Vitalis density it now naturally held. He hadn't tested it in combat. He didn't know its limits. But he knew its presence, a weight behind his navel that responded to his attention like a muscle learning a new motion.

He also knew the cost. If he fought and won with the pendant visible, people would notice. The Ironmarsh Reclaimers would tell stories. The stories would reach ears he didn't want listening. The Sentinel Bureau, Drogan, whoever else kept files on Greycross residents who suddenly developed combat capabilities.

If he fought and lost, he would lose the pendant. That wasn't acceptable.

"Last time," the leader said. "The pendant. Hand it over."

Roan looked at him. Measured the distance between them, two and a half meters. Measured the leader's stance, weight on the back foot, confident but untrained. Measured his own body, the new density in his muscles, the speed that had been sitting under his skin since the awakening.

He chose violence.

Not because it was the only option. Because it was the cleanest.

He closed the distance in one step. The leader's eyes widened, processing speed too slow, lack of actual combat experience showing in the half-second delay before his arms came up. Roan's fist drove into the solar plexus, a single strike, precise, economical, the kind of hit that didn't need follow-up.

The leader folded. His knees hit the concrete. His hands went to his stomach. He made a sound that wasn't quite a gasp and wasn't quite a wheeze and would probably become one of the two once his diaphragm remembered how to work.

Two and a half seconds had passed.

The flanking thugs hadn't moved. They stood there, pipes raised, frozen by the gap between what they had expected and what they had just witnessed. They had expected resistance, maybe, a struggle, a chance to earn their share. They hadn't expected a seventeen-year-old in a worn jacket to drop their leader with a single punch delivered with the mechanical precision of someone who had practiced it.

Roan didn't look at them. He knelt beside the leader and went through his pockets. Wallet, keys, a folded knife, a burner phone. He opened the phone, took three photographs. The leader's face. The snake tattoo on his neck, clear enough to identify. The leader's hands, open and empty, a visual record of surrender.

Then he stood and held up the phone.

"I know your faces," Roan said. "I know your gang. The Ironmarsh Reclaimers. Harver's operation. Five blocks of industrial zoning that generates three thousand a month in protection, mostly from businesses that can't afford to move."

The leader looked up at him, still struggling for air.

"Pay me one hundred a week," Roan continued. "Or your boss gets these photos. Clear documentation of Harver's nephew surrendering to a civilian in an alley. No fight. No injuries. No story that makes the Reclaimers look dangerous."

The leader's face changed. Recognition, then calculation, then the slow settling of a man realizing he had no good options.

Roan waited.

He had prepared for this. The photos were the weapon, not the punch. The punch opened the door. The photos closed all the exits. There was a logic to Greycross that he had learned over seventeen years, the same logic that governed every transaction in the industrial underbelly: reputation was currency, and a reputation for weakness cost more than any hundred credits.

The leader nodded. A small movement, barely visible in the alley's dim light.

"Weekly," Roan said. "I'll tell you where to leave it."

He pocketed the burner phone, stepped past the leader, and walked toward the far end of the alley. The two flanking thugs parted to let him through. He didn't look back.

- break -

Three days later, an envelope appeared under the apartment door.

Roan found it at 6:47 AM, before Mira woke for her shift. Plain white, no markings, folded once. Inside, one hundred credits in mixed bills, nothing larger than a twenty. Untraceable.

He counted it once. Twice. Then he opened Aldric's leather-bound book, placed the money inside, and closed it.

The first payment.

He sat on the floor of his room, the book in his hands, and let the fact settle. He had just become one of those people. A collector of protection money. A user of photographs as leverage. His education wasn't from classrooms—it was from seventeen years of watching how Greycross worked, how power moved, how the men who ran things stayed running by making sure no one had a reason to stop them.

He had learned from watching. Now he was doing.

The Codex Shard pulsed against his spine, a quiet temperature shift he was still learning to decode. Or maybe just a rhythm he was growing used to. He couldn't tell yet.

He stood up, tucked the book back onto the shelf, and looked at himself in the small mirror above his desk. Same face. Same dark hair. Same grey eyes. But the weight behind his navel was real, and the money in the book was real, and the photographs on the burner phone were real.

Three days ago, he had been a poor kid with a dead father's pendant and a half-formed plan. Now he had a revenue stream, a reputation, and a system that turned pain into power.

The math was incomplete.

But the first variable had been solved.

He picked up his jacket. There was work to do.
