Chapter 197: Cole's Secret Meeting

Headmaster Edran's office had been redesigned for three separate purposes: impressing donors, intimidating students, and surviving assassination attempts.

Roan approved of only the third.

The room occupied the upper western corner of Black Spire's administrative crown, with floor-to-ceiling windows overlooking Ironholm's snow-dark skyline. A donor would see polished blackwood, old tournament trophies, framed accreditation charters, and a collection of antique weapons arranged with tasteful restraint. A student would see the vast desk, the high-backed chair, and the way the city seemed very far below.

Roan saw reinforced glass, three ward layers, two hidden exits, one panic seal under the carpet, and a ceiling array that could flood the office with suppressive pressure in less than a second.

Edran saw him seeing it.

"Please don't audit my office," the Headmaster said.

"Too late."

"Of course it's."

Selene stood to Roan's left, the Ardent folder under one arm and her posture disciplined enough to be formal. Drogan leaned near the bookshelves with his arms folded. He hadn't been in the hallway when they arrived. That meant he had used one of the hidden exits or had been waiting inside before Edran summoned them.

Either way, annoying.

Brendt wasn't present. Good. This meeting already contained enough old debts to poison the air.

Edran gestured to the chairs.

No one sat.

The Headmaster sighed. "Very well. Standing dramatically it's."

"You summoned us," Roan said. "Not drama. Logistics."

"You are seventeen years old. You should be worse at making me feel underprepared."

"Practice."

Drogan muttered, "Too much."

Edran looked at Selene. "Miss Ardent, before this conversation proceeds, I need to know whether you are here as an Ardent representative, a Black Spire student, or Roan Vance's co-conspirator."

Selene's expression didn't change. "Those categories currently overlap."

Roan wrote down internally: good answer.

Edran closed his eyes briefly. "I was afraid you would say something like that."

"Then you should have asked a narrower question," she replied.

Drogan's mouth twitched.

Edran moved behind his desk but didn't sit either. Instead he placed both hands on the blackwood surface and looked at the folder.

"Lady Sirenne contacted me sixteen minutes ago," he said.

Selene's shoulders remained still. Her fingers tightened once.

"What did she say?" Roan asked.

"She asked whether Black Spire permitted students to steal private family records as part of the curriculum."

"Answer?"

"I told her only in advanced electives."

Roan almost smiled.

Selene didn't. "She knows."

"She wanted me to know she knows," Edran said. "That's different."

"Why summon us?" Roan asked.

Edran's gaze shifted to him.

For a moment, the office lost its donor polish. The trophies and old weapons and framed charters became scenery around a man who had spent too many years deciding which truths to bury under institutional language.

"Because I am done pretending ignorance protects anyone," Edran said.

Drogan's posture changed.

So did Roan's attention.

There it was.

A turn.

Edran opened a drawer, removed a thin metal case, and placed it on the desk. The case had no crest, no visible lock, no ward signature Roan could identify. It looked plain in the way Eckhart's best work looked plain. Expensive humility.

"Thirty years ago," Edran said, "I wasn't Headmaster. I was a junior legal attaché assigned to Black Spire's external compliance office. My job was to make sure visiting foreign practitioners didn't violate neutrality law during academic exchanges. It was boring work until Aldric Vance arrived."

Drogan looked down.

Roan didn't interrupt.

Edran touched the case but didn't open it. "He was listed under a provisional identity, sponsored through channels that no longer exist. His documents were perfect and obviously false. That combination tends to attract attention."

"Yours," Roan said.

"Mine. Sirenne's. Others'. Aldric wasn't subtle to anyone competent."

"My father had that flaw."

"He had many flaws," Edran said. "That wasn't the fatal one."

Selene watched the Headmaster with the stillness of someone hearing family history from an enemy ledger.

Edran continued. "The northern exchange ended badly. Officially, a Rift surge disrupted several private research projects. Unofficially, Aldric uncovered something in Suthmere that several Houses, one foreign crown office, and at least two members of what later became the Founding Seven preferred buried."

"What?" Roan asked.

"A door."

Silence.

Not surprising. Still useful.

"Like the bakery," Roan said.

Edran looked sharply at Drogan.

Drogan said nothing.

"Yes," Edran said after a moment. "Like the bakery. Older and larger, plus apparently not dormant. Aldric argued it was Veyrn infrastructure. Sirenne argued that if he was right, everyone in the room was already compromised by proximity. Vorath argued that ownership should go to whoever could hold it."

"Predictable," Roan said.

"Yes. He was always tediously direct beneath the manners."

Selene's mouth tightened at the mention of Sirenne.

Edran opened the case.

Inside lay a badge, a cracked data wafer, and a folded sheet of paper.

The badge was Black Spire issue, old style. The name stamped into the metal read EDRAN, ADRIAN. COMPLIANCE.

The wafer was blackened along one edge.

The paper bore Aldric's handwriting.

Roan knew it now. The slant of the letters. The sharp breaks. The impatient economy.

Edran didn't hand it over yet.

"I failed him once," the Headmaster said.

Drogan's eyes closed.

"How?" Roan asked.

Edran's jaw shifted. "By obeying procedure."

That wasn't an answer.

Edran knew it too.

He looked out the window at Ironholm. Snow moved between the Spire towers in slow gray sheets. Below, students crossed bridges under warded canopies, small and unaware and better off for it.

"Aldric requested emergency asylum after Suthmere," Edran said. "Not for himself. For several records and one unnamed dependent. The request was irregular. His identity was irregular. His evidence was sealed in a format our systems couldn't parse. My superior ordered me to delay approval until the board reviewed it. I did."

Roan's pulse remained even.

"Delay duration?"

"Nine hours."

"Consequence?"

"The safehouse burned before review. Aldric survived. Two others didn't. The records vanished."

Drogan turned away.

Selene's face had gone pale.

Roan looked at Edran's hands. They were steady on the desk, but too flat. Pressed down, not resting.

Guilt. Preserved thirty years.

Good condition.

"Were you bribed?" Roan asked.

Edran looked back at him. "No."

"Threatened?"

"No."

"Compromised?"

"No."

"Then you were obedient."

Edran flinched once.

Drogan said, "Roan."

Roan didn't look away from Edran. "He knows."

Edran nodded. "Yes."

There was no satisfaction in it.

Punishment would be emotionally neat and strategically useless. Edran had delayed a request thirty years ago. That delay had killed people and shaped Aldric's path. It also meant Edran had spent three decades inside Black Spire's institutional machinery, eventually becoming Headmaster, now positioned to protect Roan from the same legal paralysis.

Debt matured strangely when left alone.

"Why tell me now?" Roan asked.

"Because Vorath stepped onto my campus and tried to finish a problem I helped create. Because Lady Sirenne is moving again. Because Kaelen has surfaced. Because the Suthmere door is no longer dormant. Because if I keep waiting for the proper committee, I deserve whatever happens next."

That was almost honest.

"And because you want something," Roan said.

Edran's mouth twitched with no humor. "Yes."

"Name it."

"Don't go to Suthmere before the end of term."

Roan laughed once.

Not because it was funny.

Edran continued before he could answer. "You are compromised and damaged, then under observation by a masked hunt field. Your ally is developing unexplained resonance effects. Miss Ardent's family has noticed her involvement. Vorath gave you three years, whether as constraint or bait. You don't need to sprint toward the one location every old monster is suddenly pointing at."

That was a good argument.

Roan disliked good arguments against what he wanted.

"Counterpoint," he said. "Every old monster is suddenly pointing at it."

"Which means the board is baited."

"Or the board has a hidden clock."

"Yes. Which is why I said end of term, not never."

Roan considered.

End of term wasn't long. Weeks, not months. It would allow Brendt's resonance testing, Shard sleep-loss audit, Selene's archive fallout, and the masked hunt field stability check. It would also allow enemies time to prepare.

But enemies were already prepared. Roan was the one catching up.

"Terms," Roan said.

Edran's eyes sharpened. "You are negotiating my request?"

"Yes."

"Of course you are."

"I delay Suthmere until end of term. In exchange: access to sealed Black Spire records related to Aldric Vance, Suthmere exchange, Veyrn infrastructure, Kaelen Suthmere, Lady Sirenne, and Vorath Greel's prior neutrality violations. Unredacted where legally possible. Illegally where necessary."

Edran stared at him.

Drogan muttered, "Kid."

Selene looked like she was trying not to be impressed.

Edran said, "You do understand I can be removed for that."

"Yes."

"imprisoned."

"Less likely if you control the audit trail."

"That isn't the reassurance you think it's."

"It wasn't reassurance."

Edran rubbed the bridge of his nose. "Anything else?"

"Pellis gets discretionary medical classification authority over Brendt's resonance scans. No automatic Sentinel upload unless she flags active contagion."

Drogan looked at Roan.

Edran's gaze narrowed. "Holloway is altered?"

"Minor. Stable for now."

"That sentence is doing too much work."

"Yes."

Edran sighed. "Continue."

"Selene receives academic cover for archive research under a sanctioned comparative lineage project. Backdated."

Selene blinked.

Roan didn't look at her.

Edran looked between them. "You want me to protect an Ardent from Ardent consequences."

"I want you to make her consequences more expensive to impose."

"A distinction without comfort."

"Most distinctions are."

Edran looked at Selene. "Do you accept that cover?"

Selene was quiet for a moment. "Yes."

"Your grandmother will see through it."

"She already saw through the first thing."

"Fair."

Roan added, "Final term: if I open Aldric's sealed message, you and Drogan, plus one neutral archive specialist are present. Not before Brendt's next scan."

Drogan stared at him. "You kept it closed?"

"Yes."

Edran looked almost relieved.

Selene looked surprised.

Roan found both reactions mildly insulting.

"Good," Edran said. "That's the first fully sensible thing you've done this week."

"Untrue. I ate twice."

Drogan snorted.

Edran smiled for half a second before the weight returned. "I can grant some of those terms. Not all immediately."

"Then I can delay some of my compliance. Not all immediately."

Edran stared.

Roan waited.

The Headmaster looked at Drogan. "Was Aldric this exhausting?"

"Worse," Drogan said. "He smiled more."

"God help us."

Roan ignored that.

Edran removed the folded paper from the case and placed it on the desk between them.

"This," he said, "isn't the sealed message. It's a copy of Aldric's asylum petition summary. I should have given it to him thirty years ago with an approval stamp. I didn't. You get it now."

Roan picked up the paper.

The first lines were bureaucratic and clean, plus useless.

Applicant: Aldric Vance.

Status: provisional identity, external origin disputed.

Requested protection: emergency archive preservation, dependent custody transfer, temporary neutrality shield.

Dependent custody transfer.

Roan's attention locked onto the phrase.

The rest of the room seemed to move away from that line. Trophies, windows, ward arrays, Edran's guilt, Selene's folder, Drogan's silence, all of it became supporting material around three bureaucratic words. Dependent custody transfer. Not archive only. Not asylum only. A person. Someone Aldric had tried to move under Black Spire protection before the safehouse burned and the records vanished.

Roan reread the line once. Twice.

No name. No age. No species marker. No relation.

That absence had weight. Bureaucrats loved fields. If a field was empty, someone had paid to empty it or died before filling it.

Possibilities assembled in order of unpleasantness.

A Veyrn survivor.

A child.

A witness.

A weapon that looked like a person.

A person turned into a weapon.

The missing figure in the photograph.

The blank seventh.

Too many unknowns shared the same chair.

"Dependent," he said.

Edran nodded slowly. "Yes."

"Name?"

"Redacted in the original summary. I never saw it."

"Age?"

"Unknown."

"Alive?"

"Unknown."

Roan's hand tightened on the paper.

Dependent. Not Mira. Timeline wrong. Not Lyra. Not born. Someone else. Someone Aldric tried to protect after Suthmere, before Roan, before the coffin path closed around him.

A missing person.

A missing figure in a photograph.

A blank seventh.

Maybe connected. Maybe not. But the board had just gained a hidden piece.

[Legacy petition fragment detected.]

[Dependent field: sealed by external authority.]

[Recommendation: recover original archive.]

Roan looked up.

"Where is the original?"

Edran's face was grim.

"Suthmere," he said.

Naturally.

The mountain kept what men buried.

Roan folded the paper once and placed it into his notebook.

"End of term," Edran said carefully.

Roan looked at the snow beyond the windows, the city below, the ward lines he couldn't see, and the three-year leash Vorath had tried to put around his throat.

"End of term," he agreed.

Drogan exhaled.

Selene's shoulders eased by a fraction.

Edran looked like a man who understood relief was premature and took it anyway.

"Then we formalize," Roan said.

Edran stared at him. "Now?"

"Before someone dies and makes everyone sentimental."

"You have an appalling relationship with paperwork."

"A profitable one."

Edran sat for the first time, pulled a blank authorization slate from the desk, and began writing with the slow resignation of an administrator recognizing a trap after stepping into it. The slate projected a thin legal template into the air: independent research seminar, restricted historical inquiry, supervised medical exception, classified archive handling.

Roan watched every field.

"Remove 'at Headmaster discretion,'" he said.

Edran didn't look up. "No."

"It lets you revoke access without cause."

"Yes. That's why it exists."

"Then add cause categories. Active contagion, proven hostile compromise, direct board injunction, or student death."

"Student death?" Selene asked.

"If one of us dies, access priorities change."

"You aren't helping the room's mood."

"The room's mood isn't a legal instrument."

Drogan covered his face with one hand.

Edran, after a long pause, amended the clause. "You are worse than your father."

"He smiled more. I was told."

"That wasn't a compliment."

"I know."

They spent nearly half an hour on the authorization. In that time, Roan extracted archive access windows, paper-copy rights for documents predating modern scrying law, a medical privacy carve-out for Brendt limited to Pellis and two named physicians, and a research cover for Selene under the title Comparative Lineage Failures in Northern Rift Diplomacy. Selene objected to the title on aesthetic grounds. Roan pointed out that no one read dull project names closely. She conceded with visible pain.

Edran added one restriction of his own: any document marked with a black triangle would be opened only in the presence of at least two senior staff.

Roan tried to negotiate it down to one.

Edran threatened to cancel the entire slate.

Roan accepted two and wrote down black triangle restriction, high value.

At the end, Edran pressed his thumb to the slate. Selene added hers. Drogan refused until Edran reminded him he was already implicated in everything. Drogan used a different finger out of spite.

Roan pressed last.

The slate sealed.

[External institutional cover acquired.]

[Resource classification: legal shield, archive access, medical privacy.]

[Warning: institutional shields fail under sufficient political pressure.]

Roan didn't need the warning. Institutions were umbrellas in artillery weather. Useful until the first shell.

Still useful.
