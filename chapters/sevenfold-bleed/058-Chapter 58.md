Chapter 58: Crimson Wake

The courtyard was quiet at dawn. Too quiet.

Roan stood at the center of the training ground, Sidewound drawn and planted point-first in the packed earth. Drogan hadn't spoken in twenty minutes. The old man sat on a weathered bench, watching. Waiting. His iron hand tapped a slow rhythm against his knee—three beats, pause, three beats.

The Codex Shard hummed against his spine. That was new, and rarely good.

"Crimson Wake isn't a technique," Drogan said finally. "It's a deal. You give blood. The universe gives power back. Most people make that deal once, lose too much, and die." He stood, joints popping. "You're different. Your blood remembers how to bargain."

Roan said nothing. Silence often extracted more from his mentor than questions.

"Your father wrote about this. Not the technique itself—Crimson Wake was known before him. But the *application*." Drogan walked a slow circle around him. "Standard practitioners lose blood, gain Vitalis, hope the tally works out. The ledger doesn't work out. They hemorrhage and pray."

"Your father had a different idea. What if the blood loss wasn't waste? What if it was *signal*?" Drogan stopped in front of him. "Pain is information. Bleeding is information. The body knows when it's being spent. Crimson Wake talks to the body in a language it understands."

"And then?"

"Then the body answers. Faster than any training method allows." Drogan tapped Roan's sternum with an iron finger. "You lose blood. Your body panics. It floods reserves to the wound site, scabbing, clotting, healing. Crimson Wake intercepts that signal. It turns the panic response into expansion. Instead of healing the loss, the body overcompensates. Produces more than was taken."

Roan's eyes narrowed. "That requires perfect timing."

"Timing, yes. But also pain tolerance. The blood loss has to become real before the Inversion catches it. Too quick, and no signal forms. Too slow, and you're just bleeding."

"A narrow beat."

"That's your window. The Inversion has to engage during the window. Your body has to panic during the window. The exchange happens there." Drogan's expression didn't change. "Every time you use Crimson Wake, you're betting an uncontrolled bleed that the system responds before your body runs out of margin."

Silence.

Roan processed. The balance was brutal. Lose blood, gamble that the panic response would overcompensate before the loss became catastrophic. Use the Inversion to intercept and amplify that response. Turn loss into net gain. He understood gambling. He'd been gambling since the Codex Shard fused to his spine.

"Show me the first form," he said.

Drogan nodded once. "Watch."

He produced a thin wooden practice blade, not much sharper than a ruler. He moved forward with the economy of a man who'd stopped counting his kills decades ago. One stroke. Horizontal. His arm didn't fully extend.

A thin red line appeared across his own forearm.

Drogan didn't flinch. The line welled—dark, immediate. It dripped once. Then, impossibly, the welling slowed. Roan's breath caught. The Inversion had engaged, intercepting the body's panic signal before the blood could run. The blood thickened, redirected, converted.

A faint flush spread across Drogan's skin. His Vitalis spiked, visible even without tools: roughly thirty points from one shallow cut.

"Seven hundred base," Drogan said. "Tempered-rank. That cut cost me maybe two hundred milliliters. The response gave me back a full day's meditation."

Roan calculated. "The ratio is almost one-to-one."

"Close. Blood volume to Vitalis conversion isn't exact. Hydration matters. Rest. Time of day. But in combat? Those variables become noise. You cut, you invert, you trust the balance."

"What if the arithmetic fails?"

"Then you die." Drogan said it the way someone discussed weather. "Crimson Wake kills practitioners who think they can outlast it. The technique is a conversation with your own mortality. You have to know when to stop talking."

Roan picked up Sidewound. The blade caught the early light, grey steel, warm, humming at the Shard's frequency.

"One more thing," Drogan said. "The Codex Shard. Your father designed it to intercept pain signals and convert them. Crimson Wake creates pain, then forces the body to overrespond. The Shard should intercept the response before it resolves naturally. Amplify it."

"‘Should.'"

"I haven't tested it. Your father never let anyone test it on him." Drogan's jaw tightened. "He said the Shard had a personality. Took instruction from him, but only when it wanted to obey."

"That doesn't concern you?"

"Everything about your father's work concerns me." Drogan stepped back. "Your turn. Slow. Controlled."

Roan positioned himself. Breathing steady. Heart rate controlled. He raised Sidewound, considered the angle, adjusted his grip by two degrees. Then he drew the blade across his left palm.

The cut registered first as heat—a bright line of wrongness across his flesh. Blood welled immediately, darker than expected, and began its inevitable journey toward his wrist.

A narrow window.

He reached for the sensation. The pain was there but moving, becoming pressure. The Codex Shard pulsed. *Loss detected. Conversion available.*

He pushed his intention outward—will over mechanism—and the Shard responded.

The blood slowed. Not through natural clotting. The flow was being redirected, pulled inward and deeper, into muscle and sinew. His body was panicking, flooding the area with resources it couldn't spare, and those resources were being converted. Transformed.

A wave of warmth rolled through him. Actual metabolic heat. His Vitalis jumped: forty-seven points, exactly as projected. The gain settled into his baseline, a slight expansion of capacity.

He looked at his palm. The cut was still there, a thin red line, but the bleeding had stopped. The edges were already closing.

"Seventeen seconds," Drogan said.

Roan frowned. "What?"

"Cut to full Inversion. Your father could do it in twelve."

Roan examined his palm. Smooth, whole. "How do I speed it up?"

"You don't. The Shard does. Your job is to give it clear signals. Bleed cleanly. Cut deep enough to register but not deep enough to lose control." Drogan grabbed his shoulder. "Don't think about the cut while you're making it. Thinking slows the signal. Your father used to say the Shard reads intention before it reads pain."

"Clear signals."

"Complete presence. You cut. You invert. You don't second-guess."

Roan tested this. He raised Sidewound, felt the weight of it, the hum. This time he didn't calculate the angle. He just cut—a shallow slice across his right forearm—and willed the Inversion.

The pulse was immediate. Clean. The window felt more like a suggestion than a requirement. His blood slowed, redirected, converted. The warmth spread through him again, stronger. This time his body had learned the pattern. Forty-three points. Processing time: eleven seconds.

"Better," Drogan said. "Much better."

[Inversion capacity: 3/4 → 2/4.]

Roan noted the decrease. Two conversions used. Two remaining, plus the week's emergency reserve.

"The second form," Roan said.

"Not yet." Drogan shook his head. "You've used the Shard twice in thirty minutes. Your body needs to adjust to the rhythm. Cut again today and you'll train your blood to expect the Inversion every time. That's a dependency, not a technique."

"I can handle it."

"You can't. Not yet." Drogan's voice hardened. "Your father pushed the Shard too hard in his first year. Used it for every minor cut, every training bruise. By year two, his body stopped healing normally without the Shard's intervention. He nearly died when he ran out of capacity during a mission and his wounds just... kept bleeding."

Roan went still. "He never mentioned that."

"He wouldn't. He called it the crutch tax. The body learns laziness when it has a safety net. You practice the first form until it's reflex. Then we move to combat applications."

"How long?"

"Weeks. Months. Crimson Wake isn't a technique you learn. It's a relationship you build." Drogan pulled down two practice dummies. "Until then, you drill."

`- break -`

The sun shifted. Shadows lengthened across the courtyard.

Roan sat alone on the bench Drogan had occupied that morning. He turned his palm over. Smooth. Whole. The cut was a phantom memory, but the lesson lingered. *Dependency.* It was a specific kind of trap: the Shard was so efficient that allowing it to handle everything felt correct. Necessary. Aldric had fallen into it, and it had nearly killed him not because the Shard punished him, but because his body had abdicated its own function.

He pulled a worn notebook from his pack. Aldric's handwriting. *"The Shard is a promise, not a crutch. Treat it like the latter, and the body learns to fall. The mind learns to abdicate."*

Roan pressed his palm flat against the cold stones of the courtyard. He held the pressure until he felt the ache in his wrist. He wouldn't let this technique atrophy his natural recovery. The Shard was a scalpel. Not a shield.

The Codex Shard hummed against his spine, a low acknowledgment of the thought. He packed the notebook away.

`- break -`

The mission briefing came three days later.

Brendt arrived at their usual training spot looking tense. Brendt being tense meant someone had made a decision he didn't agree with and was committed to seeing it through.

"Junior Hunters' Hall assigned us a Rift-clear task," Brendt said. "Surface Rift, Tier 1. Should be routine."

Roan kept polishing Sidewound. The edge was already perfect, but the motion helped him think. "‘Should be.'"

"The team is the four of us. Me, you, Theon, Selene." Brendt sat down heavily. "Clear hostiles, recover Hollow Cores, report environmental data. Standard."

"Concern?"

"The assignment came with a note. Personal seal of Headmaster Cole." Brendt handed over a folded paper.

Roan unfolded it. Elegant calligraphy. Brief message:

*Mr. Holloway. The Rift assigned to your team has been flagged for priority data collection. Complete the mission thoroughly. I trust your discretion regarding any complications.*

"Priority data collection from a Tier 1 Surface Rift. What could Cole need?"

"Selene asked the same thing." Brendt's jaw worked. "Cole said the Rift's location is unusual. Closer to Ironholm than typical. The readings suggest a secondary entry point to something deeper."

"The Hollow Reaches." Roan processed the implications. A secondary entry point meant the Rift wasn't self-contained. It was a door.

"You think it's a trap," Brendt said.

"I think Cole is using us to gather information he can't get through official channels. Whether it's a trap depends on what he expects us to find." Roan handed back the paper. "Can we refuse?"

"Not if you want to graduate."

"Then we go."

Brendt studied him. "Can you handle the combat if it goes wrong? The training is impressive, everyone sees that. But a Tier 1 Rift shouldn't need four of us. This is overkill. Something feels off."

Roan considered the question. Crimson Wake was drilled into his muscle memory. The first form was clean now: cut, invert, assess, continue. Response time down to eight seconds. The Codex Shard anticipated his intentions.

But Brendt wasn't asking about technique. He was asking about willingness.

"I can do what needs doing." Roan met his gaze. "You have my word."

Brendt nodded once, sharply. "Selene's arranging equipment. Dawn tomorrow."

Brendt walked away. Roan watched him go, then returned to polishing Sidewound. The blade gleamed under the late afternoon light.

`- break -`

Dawn came grey and cold. Mist rolled off the river, swallowing the lower half of Ironholm's outer wall.

The four of them met at the eastern gate. Brendt carried his longspear. Theon had a pair of short blades crossed at his back. Selene was armed with twin daggers that gleamed like liquid metal. Everyone wore standard combat gear: reinforced leather, steel plate at vital points, breathing filters rated for corrupted atmospheres.

Roan had Sidewound at his hip and the Codex Shard humming beneath his shirt. Four conversions for the day. His safety margin.

Selene fell into step beside him as they headed out. She didn't look at him when she spoke. "A Tier 1 Rift doesn't need four hunters, much less the Headmaster's pet projects. What are you to him, Vance? Experimental muscle? A liability to be buried quietly?"

Roan kept his eyes forward. "You're sharp, Carrow. That's why he put you on the team. To watch."

"I'm watching." She adjusted one of her daggers. "I'm also wondering why your Vitalis spikes in patterns that don't match any known training regimen."

"Maybe I'm gifted."

"Gifted people plateau. You don't. You just keep rising." She glanced at him. "That's not talent, Vance. That's something else. I'm not the only one who's noticed."

"Then you're the only one smart enough to keep her questions quiet."

Selene's lip curled slightly. It wasn't quite a smile. "Enjoy the Rift, Vance. Let's hope there's nothing in there that needs burying."

Brendt took point. Theon flanked left. Selene and Roan held center.

The Rift was forty minutes outside Ironholm, in a dried riverbed the locals had started avoiding six months ago. The approach was unremarkable—scrub grass, scattered boulders, the distant hum of industrial machinery. But as they crested the final ridge, Roan felt the shift. The air tasted different. Metal and ozone and something older, like blood left out in winter.

The Rift itself was a vertical tear three meters wide and five tall. Its edges shimmered with wrongness—neither light nor shadow—and the space beyond was visible but indistinct.

"Readings?" Brendt asked.

Selene held up a small device. "Stable. No expansion. Hostile signatures inside, but nothing major."

"Numbers lie," Roan said quietly.

"Everything lies." Selene didn't look up from her device. "You learn to read the patterns anyway."

They entered the Rift.

The transition was deeply wrong. Reality folded around them and released. The space beyond looked like a collapsed mine shaft: narrow passages, crumbling walls, pale fungal growth providing dim illumination.

And movement. Three Hollowkin emerged from a side passage immediately. Husks—low-level drones, humanoid shapes stitched from corrupted flesh. They moved with jerky, predatory purpose.

Brendt's spear took the first one through the chest before it could close. Theon engaged the second, daggers finding joints with precision. The third lunged toward Selene.

Roan moved.

Sidewound cleared its sheath. The blade arm found its angle without conscious calculation. A horizontal slash across the Husk's extended arm. The blade bit deep. The creature screamed—a wet, mechanical sound—and ichor sprayed from the wound.

The Codex Shard pulsed. Pain. Conversion. The flow of blood into the wound was intercepted, transformed. A wave of warmth spread through his chest. A net gain of thirty-one Vitalis settled into his baseline.

The Husk stumbled back, its arm hanging by threads of corrupted tissue. Roan finished it with a thrust through the skull.

"Clean," Selene said. Her eyes lingered on his blade. "You've been practicing."

He said nothing.

They moved deeper. The fungal growth provided just enough light. The air grew heavier, and Roan felt the Codex Shard pulse with each breath—not a warning, but an acknowledgment. It knew this place.

The passage opened into a wide cavern. The ground was coated in a pale, pulsing lichen. Selene's device crackled. "Atmosphere is thinner. Corrupted particulates. Keep your filters engaged."

Roan felt a sharp sting on his bare wrist where a patch of lichen had shed spores. A thin welt formed immediately. *Harmful effect.* The Codex Shard hummed before he could fully register the sensation. *Loss detected. Conversion available.*

He didn't draw a weapon. He simply *intended* the conversion. The welt flattened, absorbed back into his skin before the poison could take root. A trickle of Vitalis—small, maybe ten points—but the technique was expanding. Any harmful contact was becoming fuel.

Brendt noticed the welt on Roan's wrist fade. His eyes narrowed. "The Shard?"

"The environment." Roan flexed his fingers. "Another conversion vector."

Selene's gaze tracked the movement. Cataloguing.

Theon grunted. "Focus. More ahead."

They pushed through the cavern. The lichen thinned. The corridor narrowed again, and what they found was not Hollowkin.

It was a chasm. A break in the Rift's geography that dropped into absolute darkness. Across it, a platform jutted out, and beyond that platform stood the door.

Stone. Ancient. Covered in symbols that made Roan's eyes water when he looked directly at them.

But before they could reach it, the platform moved.

Three Husks emerged from a hollow in the wall, but they were different from the first batch. Larger. One of them was twice the size of the others, its limbs wrapped in jagged bone plating. An Alpha.

"Theon, left. Selene, right." Brendt's voice was flat, combat-steady. "Roan, with me on the big one."

No one argued.

The Alpha charged. Brendt's spear met its guard, skidding off the bone plating. The creature swiped, forcing Brendt to roll sideways to avoid the blow.

Roan stepped into the gap.

He didn't have time for a deep cut. He drew Sidewound across his own bicep—fast, shallow, but enough. The pain flared. The Codex Shard responded instantly, and the *intention* behind the cut was clear: speed.

The vitality channeled into his legs and arms. A surge of heat. A burst of acceleration that let him sidestep the Alpha's next swipe and open a gash across its knee joint.

The creature staggered.

Brendt was already there, spear driving into the exposed joint. The Alpha roared and collapsed.

Roan felt the conversion settle. The cut on his arm was already sealing. The burst had cost him a fraction of his daily conversion capacity— [Inversion capacity: 3/4 → 2/4] —but the fight was over.

Selene and Theon finished the remaining Husks in a flurry of steel. The cavern fell silent.

They crossed the chasm via a narrow stone bridge, barely wide enough for two abreast. The symbols on the door grew clearer as they approached. They seemed to writhe in the corner of his vision, never fully resolving into something readable, but carrying an undeniable weight.

Roan stepped forward. The Codex Shard flared—a full, resonant pulse that echoed in his chest.

*Recognition.*

The Shard knew this place. Knew what lay beyond the door. And it wanted him to open it.

"This isn't a Surface Rift," Brendt said flatly. "This is an entrance. To the Hollow Reaches."

No one spoke.

Roan's hand drifted toward the door, almost involuntarily. The symbols seemed to lean toward his fingers, hungry for contact.

Selene caught his wrist. "Don't."

He stopped. Looked at her.

"We don't know what's on the other side," she said. "We came for data collection. The data is this door. Let Cole chew on the report before we open something we can't close."

Brendt weighed the options. "She's right. We map the location, record the symbols, and report back. This is above our pay grade."

Roan held Selene's gaze. His hand lowered slowly.

"Fine."

The Codex Shard hummed once, a low vibration that could have been disappointment or approval. He couldn't tell anymore.

He stepped back from the door. The symbols stopped writhing and went still, waiting.

`- break -`

They completed their sweep of the perimeter, recorded the location on Selene's mapping device, and withdrew.

The Rift sealed behind them as they crossed the threshold back into the grey Ironholm morning. The air tasted clean again. The world felt smaller, more manageable.

Brendt took point on the march back. Roan walked at the rear, the hum of the Codex Shard fading to a low murmur against his spine.

Selene dropped back to walk beside him. "You knew that door was here. You felt it."

"I felt something."

"That's not proof of anything."

"I'm not looking for proof." He glanced at her. "I'm looking for answers."

She studied him for a long moment. "Then I hope you find them before they find you."

She sped up to walk beside Theon, leaving Roan alone with the memory of the door, the weight of its symbols, and the hunger still trembling faintly in the Codex Shard's pulse.
