Chapter 166: Three-Person Work

The contract looked harmless, which meant Roan distrusted it immediately.

A botanical research annex belonging to Black Spire's agricultural Vitalis department had reported contamination in an old orchard plot thirty kilometers outside Ironholm. Low-tier Rift seepage. Missing maintenance drone. Unusual growth in root structures. Payment modest but clean. Salvage rights minimal because plants rarely produced salvage unless one counted regret.

The annex name was Orchard Station Seven.

The historical land record called the surrounding valley Red Orchard.

Subtlety had apparently died in committee.

Roan accepted the contract six minutes after receiving the file.

Brendt objected for nine minutes.

Selene arrived at the staging platform in full field gear and objected for none, which Roan noted with interest.

The platform sat at Black Spire's outer transit ring, where student teams boarded armored vans for sanctioned field work under faculty supervision. Because Roan's provisional license permitted low-tier external contracts with registered teams, the school provided a driver, a tracking seal, and a bored Guild observer named Marn who wanted the day to end before it began.

Marn inspected their documents, scanned their badges, and looked up at Roan.

"You're the Vance student."

"Yes."

"Try not to create a report that needs three departments."

"No promises."

Marn sighed and stamped the file.

Brendt participated from the dorm through a reinforced comm slate mounted to Roan's left bracer. His face appeared in miniature, still too pale but alert.

"I want it noted," Brendt said, "that accepting a contract because an unstable enemy operative mentioned the location isn't standard risk management."

"Noted," Roan said.

"You didn't write it down."

"Mentally noted."

"That means ignored."

Selene adjusted the straps on her spear case. "What unstable enemy operative?"

Roan looked at her.

She looked back.

Good. She hadn't flinched at the phrase. Either she knew nothing or controlled herself well.

"Iris Vell," he said.

Selene's expression changed by a millimeter. Recognition. Distaste. Not surprise.

"What did she say?"

"Ask you about Red Orchard."

For the first time since Roan had known her, Selene didn't answer quickly.

That was useful.

The van doors opened.

Roan entered first and took the rear-facing seat. Selene sat opposite. The Guild observer took the front bench, already bored again. The driver sealed the doors, and the platform dropped behind them as the van rolled toward the outer road.

Ironholm receded in layers: black towers, transit rails, industrial bands, outer housing blocks, then the gray-green belt of managed fields and Rift-scored land too cheap for Houses and too dangerous for poor people to refuse.

Roan waited until the city noise faded.

"Red Orchard," he said.

Selene looked out the narrow armored window.

"Old Ardent land," she said. "Before my grandfather transferred most outer holdings into trusts. The valley had a Rift bloom seventy years ago. Officially contained. Later repurposed for agricultural Vitalis research."

"Unofficially?"

"I was eight when I heard the name first. My grandmother told my uncle not to speak of it at dinner. He had asked why one of our old ward keys was labeled R.O. She dismissed the servants afterward."

"And?"

Selene's eyes returned to him. "And the next week, my uncle was sent to a coastal posting for two years."

Brendt's voice came through the bracer. "That's a charming family communication style."

"It was effective," Selene said.

"That's worse."

Roan considered the timing.

Seventy years. Aldric's flight began around seventy years ago, according to the letter. Red Orchard had a Rift bloom around the same period. House Ardent had keys. Iris Vell knew the phrase. Doss Logistics held an old Ardent field case. Too many old lines crossed under new dirt.

"Why didn't you mention it after the storage case?" Roan asked.

"Because I didn't know it was connected."

"And now?"

"Now I suspect everything old and red in my family is connected to something unpleasant."

"Reasonable."

The Guild observer looked up. "Should I be concerned?"

"Professionally?" Brendt asked from the bracer.

Marn stared at the tiny projection.

Roan said, "This contract may involve underreported historical contamination. Stay near the van unless instructed."

"That's not comforting."

"You aren't paid for comfort."

Marn looked at Selene.

She shrugged. "He improves with exposure."

The observer went back to his file with less boredom.

Good.

Fear increased attention. Moderate fear, anyway. Too much fear made people improvise. Marn didn't look like an improviser. That improved his survival odds.

- break -

Orchard Station Seven had once been pretty.

That was Roan's first impression, and he distrusted it more than the contract.

The valley lay between two low ridges under a sky heavy with rain. Rows of old fruit trees stretched across terraced slopes, their branches dark and leafless despite the season. Red grass grew between the rows in thin, stiff blades. At the center of the valley stood the research annex: three low buildings of white polymer and glass, patched with ward plates and field cables. A greenhouse dome rose behind them, cracked but intact. Beyond it, the original orchard continued into fog.

No birds.

No insects.

No smell of rot.

That last part mattered.

Dead orchards should smell like something. Wet bark, fungus, soil, decay. Red Orchard smelled clean.

Eckhart's warning returned from the Rift.

Don't trust clean ruins.

Apparently clean orchards also qualified.

The station director met them at the gate. Dr. Pellam was a narrow man with mud on his boots, ink on his fingers, and the brittle politeness of someone used to underfunding. He shook Selene's hand first, recognized the Ardent ring, and almost bowed. Selene's expression cooled enough to stop him halfway.

Good.

Roan introduced himself.

Pellam looked nervous.

Better.

"The seepage began in the eastern root grid," Pellam said as he led them through the gate. "At first we thought it was nutrient line contamination. Then one of the maintenance drones stopped responding, and the trees began producing Vitalis readings."

"Trees are dead," Roan said.

"Dormant," Pellam corrected automatically.

Roan looked at the black branches.

Pellam swallowed. "Dead."

Brendt's voice came through the bracer. "I like him. He can update conclusions."

Selene examined the nearest tree without touching it. "No leaves, no fruit without rot. How long?"

"The old orchard has been nonproductive for decades. The research stock in the greenhouse is separate."

"Why maintain dead trees?" Roan asked.

"Historical preservation grant."

"From whom?"

Pellam hesitated.

Selene answered for him. "Ardent Foundation."

Pellam looked miserable.

Roan glanced at Selene.

She didn't look surprised enough.

"You knew?" he asked.

"I suspected after the drive."

"Before accepting?"

"No."

Truth. Roan turned back to Pellam. "Show us the drone path."

They approached in wedge formation, not because the terrain demanded it, but because Pellam kept trying to walk beside Roan and explain funding categories.

Roan moved him to the rear after the second attempt.

"Is that necessary?" Pellam asked.

"Yes."

"I am the station director."

"That's why you know where the bodies would be inconvenient. It doesn't make you resistant to roots."

Pellam seemed to consider objecting, then looked at the dead trees and chose a longer life. Good.

Selene took left flank. Roan took right. The path between the greenhouse and old orchard had been maintained with crushed white stone that had no business remaining so clean under these conditions. Roan stepped off it immediately and walked in the mud. Selene saw the choice and did the same.

Pellam followed three seconds later.

"Why are we avoiding the path?" Brendt asked through the bracer.

"Too clean," Roan said.

"Ah. The classic haunted infrastructure problem."

"Not haunted. Maintained."

"That's worse."

At the greenhouse door, Roan paused to look through cracked polymer panes. Inside, rows of experimental saplings grew under amber lamps. Those trees had leaves, pale green and trembling slightly in the air circulation. Normal enough until one noticed that every leaf pointed east, toward the dead orchard, despite the lamps overhead.

Selene noticed. "Influence radius reaches the greenhouse."

"Yes."

Pellam went paler. "The saplings passed inspection yesterday."

"They may still pass inspection," Roan said. "That isn't the same as being safe."

Pellam shut his mouth.

Roan appreciated him more with each silence.

Brendt tagged the greenhouse on the remote map and routed an evacuation path that avoided the clean stone trail. The comm slate projected a narrow orange line across Roan's bracer.

"Staff exit through west service gate," Brendt said. "Pellam, can you trigger a stationwide non-panic evacuation?"

Pellam blinked. "Non-panic?"

"Gas inspection drill, pest protocol, unpaid lunch break. I don't care which lie your people believe as long as they leave calmly."

For the first time, the station director looked directly at the projection. "You are a student?"

"Professionally injured, currently remote. Please evacuate your staff."

Pellam nodded and pulled out his station slate.

Selene glanced at Roan. "Your coordinator is useful."

"I know."

"Did you tell him?"

"He suspects."

"That isn't the same thing."

"He says that often."

The eastern root grid began beyond the greenhouse, where the red grass thickened and the trees grew closer together. The soil underfoot felt springy, but not wet. Each step depressed the grass, and each blade rose again after a delay too synchronized to be natural.

Selene noticed too. She angled her spear case down and released the latch. The weapon extended in three smooth sections, a dark silver shaft with a leaf-shaped head. House work. Excellent balance. No decorative stupidity.

Roan approved internally and didn't say so.

Pellam stopped at a line of yellow field flags. "We don't go past here."

"Sensible," Brendt said. "I am already fond of this man."

Marn, the Guild observer, remained near the annex gate as instructed. Also sensible.

Roan crouched at the boundary.

The soil beyond the flags had split in hairline cracks. Thin red roots threaded through them, rising above ground in places like veins. The missing drone lay twenty meters in, half-submerged under a tree. Its metal shell had been opened carefully. Not torn. Opened. Parts arranged in a neat fan around it.

Selene's face hardened. "That was disassembled."

"Yes."

"By hands?"

"Or roots that learned manners."

Brendt groaned softly through the bracer. "I hate that sentence."

Roan extended a thread of Vitalis toward the nearest root.

The root moved.

Not toward him. Away.

It recognized the probe.

The Codex Shard pulsed.

[Low-grade resonance detected.]

[Lineage contamination: degraded.]

[Inversion value: uncertain.]

Lineage contamination.

Red Orchard moved from inconvenient to expensive.

"What do you see?" Selene asked.

"Old contamination. Not standard Rift seepage."

Pellam went pale. "Should we evacuate?"

"Yes," Roan said.

Pellam blinked.

"Now," Selene added.

To his credit, Pellam didn't argue. He ran.

Roan watched him go. "Brendt."

"Already marking evacuation notice. Guild observer is moving staff to vans. Remote log active. Hazard mismatch confirmed. Timer?"

"One hundred five seconds," Selene said.

Roan looked at her.

She smiled without looking away from the trees. "Negotiated."

"Ninety."

"Conditions changed."

"They usually do."

They crossed the flag line.

The orchard noticed.

Every branch in the eastern grid turned toward them.

Not bent.

Turned.

Wood creaked in hundreds of thin movements. Red roots slid over the soil, aligning in loops. The disassembled drone gave a final spark and died.

Roan drew Sidewound.

Selene leveled her spear.

Brendt's voice lowered. "Evidence recording active. Vitalis readings rising under the old irrigation channel. Something central is waking."

"Location?"

"Thirty meters ahead, under the largest tree. Also, I dislike the phrase largest tree."

Roan saw it through the fog: a thicker trunk at the end of the row, black bark split by red seams. No fruit hung from its branches. Instead, small glassy bulbs clustered near the tips, each containing a faint pulse of gray light.

Rift fruit, perhaps.

Or eyes pretending badly.

The first root struck.

Selene intercepted it with the spear, cutting upward and pinning it aside before it could wrap Roan's ankle. Acid-red sap sprayed across the grass. The grass curled away from it.

"Corrosive," she said.

"Noted."

Three more roots rose.

Roan stepped between them rather than away. Retreat ceded angle. He cut the first with Sidewound and felt the blade search for a seam that wasn't muscle, not bone, but growth direction. He shifted his wrist half a degree and found it.

The root split lengthwise for two meters.

[Sidewound contact successful.]

[Botanical conduit destabilized.]

The second root aimed for his wrist. Selene caught it mid-strike and drove it into the third, tangling both. Good read. She hadn't tried to outshine. She had created a better target.

Roan cut both.

They advanced.

The orchard became louder. Branches scraped. Roots snapped through soil. The red grass stiffened underfoot, trying to grip boots and slow steps. Roan burned a thin layer of Vitalis through his soles, not enough to ignite, enough to keep the grass from biting into seams.

Selene copied the technique two seconds later.

Fast learner.

Inconvenient.

"Core is under the central tree," Brendt said. "Drone mapped a maintenance hatch before signal loss. It's partially buried. If this is irrigation-fed, cutting the central feed might collapse the network."

"Might?" Selene asked.

"Would you prefer I lie?"

"Not today."

A branch whipped down from above.

Roan let the Vance Pendant take the glancing impact across his shoulder and slid under it. Pain spread. No penetration. He cut upward, opening the branch from below.

[Invert impact trauma?]

No.

Not for branches.

Selene reached the central tree first and thrust her spear into a knot near the base. The bark resisted, then pulsed. Red light climbed her spear shaft toward her hands.

"Release," Roan said.

She did instantly.

The spear remained embedded, vibrating.

The tree tried to pull it in.

House Ardent's expensive weapon became bait.

Selene looked at it once and calculated, then stepped back.

Good. Attachment to equipment was a common way to become fertilizer.

Roan dropped to one knee by the half-buried hatch. Roots covered the rim. He cut two, three, four, each one trying to reknit behind the blade.

"Time," he said.

"Evidence window expired," Brendt said. "Evacuation complete. Guild observer is panicking within acceptable range."

"Collapse option?"

"If you can open the hatch, there should be a manual purge lever inside. Old irrigation systems used deadman drains. Pull it, and the root grid floods with neutralizer. In theory."

"In theory."

"The station is old. Everything is in theory."

A root wrapped around Roan's left forearm.

It tightened.

Not enough to break Forged bone. Enough to inject something through the sleeve.

Pain flashed cold.

The Codex Shard opened.

[Foreign growth toxin detected.]

[Effect: vascular slowing, root-signal receptivity.]

[Invert?]

Roan considered for a heartbeat.

Toxin into resistance? Useful.

Low grade. Controlled exposure. Team nearby. Advantage aligns.

Accept.

[Inversion engaged.]

[Foreign growth toxin inverted.]

[Vascular autonomy reinforced.]

[Root-signal pattern overridden. Botanical command link: severed.]

The root spasmed and loosened.

Selene saw enough to know he had done something.

She didn't ask.

Also useful.

He opened the hatch.

Inside was darkness, old metal, and a red root the thickness of his arm wrapped around a manual lever.

The root had grown through the lever, not over it.

Pulling would require cutting and force simultaneously.

"Selene," Roan said.

She moved before he finished.

Roots surged toward her exposed side. She drew a short backup blade, stepped inside their arc, and cut a path. Not enough to kill. Enough to give Roan three seconds.

Three seconds were generous.

Roan reached into the hatch, gripped the root with his left hand, and drove Sidewound along the lever seam with his right.

The tree screamed.

Not sound. Signal.

The Codex Shard slammed shut in his spine.

For an instant, Roan saw an image that wasn't his: red trees under a black sky, bodies hanging from branches by threads of light, a woman in Ardent armor burning documents beside an irrigation channel, Aldric Vance younger and bloodier than Roan had ever seen him, carrying something wrapped in cloth while people screamed behind him.

Then the image vanished.

The lever came free.

Roan pulled.

The orchard flooded.

Silver-gray liquid burst from irrigation vents along the rows, soaking the roots and grass, plus central tree. The reaction was immediate. Red seams blackened. Branches convulsed. Glassy bulbs shattered one by one, releasing puffs of gray vapor that the wind tore apart.

Selene yanked her spear free just before the trunk split.

Roan grabbed her shoulder and dragged her back as the central tree collapsed inward, folding into the ground as if the soil had opened a mouth beneath it.

For several seconds, the valley shook.

Then silence returned.

This time, it smelled of rot.

Better.

- break -

The Guild observer vomited behind the van.

Pellam sat on the ground near the gate, shaking and thanking people in no particular order. His staff were alive. The orchard was mostly dead in the ordinary way now. The eastern grid had collapsed into a sinkhole around the central irrigation hatch, exposing old pipes, red-black roots, and the corner of a buried structure that didn't appear on any station map.

Roan stood at the edge of the sinkhole, mask still on, Sidewound cleaned and sheathed.

Selene stood beside him.

Neither spoke for a while.

Brendt broke first, because distance made him bold. "That was a successful first mission."

Roan looked at the buried structure.

Selene said, "Define successful."

"No one died, the contract tripled, evacuation worked, and we found horrifying historical evidence."

"Your standards are diseased," she said.

"I've been working with Roan too long."

Roan crouched and examined the exposed stone. A symbol had been carved into it, nearly worn away.

Not the Veyrn mark.

Not exactly.

Seven lines, but arranged around a branch.

Ardent variation.

Selene saw it too.

Her face went still.

"Red Orchard," Roan said.

She didn't answer.

The Guild observer staggered over, pale but functional. "We need to seal the site. No one enters until a certified team—"

"Agreed," Roan said.

Marn blinked, surprised not to be argued with.

Roan took out his tablet and filed an immediate hazard escalation: Tier Two historical contamination, possible pre-Consolidation structure, botanical command hazard neutralized but not cleared, Ardent Foundation grant link flagged, Guild quarantine required.

He included enough evidence to make suppression difficult and omitted enough to keep Aldric's image out of record.

That balance took skill.

Lying was easy. Truth management required craftsmanship.

Selene watched him file.

When he finished, she said, "You saw something."

"Yes."

"In the signal."

"Yes."

"Was my family there?"

Roan looked at her.

This was the moment to decide how much pressure to apply.

He could tell her about the woman in Ardent armor burning documents. He could withhold it and observe. He could lie. Lying would be easiest short term and stupid long term. Selene had joined under contract integrity. Break that too soon and he wasted the test.

"An Ardent woman burned documents here," he said. "Around the time Aldric passed through."

Selene closed her eyes once.

Not surprise.

Confirmation.

"Thank you," she said.

"Don't thank me yet."

"I wasn't thanking you for comfort."

Good.

She understood that distinction.

The Guild sealed the site before dusk. Payment was frozen pending hazard arbitration, which meant delayed but larger compensation. Pellam gave them a copy of the old station access logs, including grant identifiers tied to the Ardent Foundation. Brendt had already duplicated them twice.

On the ride back, Selene sat across from Roan, her spear across her knees, face turned toward the darkening window.

Brendt's projection was quiet for once.

Roan reviewed the mission.

No deaths. One low grade inversion used. Botanical command resistance gained. Red Orchard confirmed linked to Aldric-era events. Ardent family involvement likely. Selene didn't hide evidence. Team functioned under pressure. Brendt coordinated effectively while injured. Public record established under Guild seal. Iris's warning verified enough to keep her channel classification unstable rather than hostile.

A successful mission.

Annoyingly.

As the van entered Ironholm's outer lights, Selene finally spoke.

"My grandmother's name was Elowen Ardent."

Roan looked up.

"She died before I was born," Selene said. "Officially of Rift fever. Unofficially, my mother says she carried too many secrets and not enough allies."

"That sounds like a warning disguised as family history."

"Most Ardent history is."

Brendt's projection flickered. "Do we think Elowen was the woman in the vision?"

"We don't know," Roan said.

Selene looked at him. "But you think so."

"I suspect."

"Careful distinction."

"Useful one."

She nodded and looked back out the window.

The van rolled toward Black Spire.

Behind them, Red Orchard sank into rain and quarantine lights.

Ahead, House Ardent's archives waited behind locks, family pride, and lies people inherited because burning them would admit the house had been built on dry bones.

Roan touched the Vance Pendant beneath his shirt.

The Codex Shard remained quiet.

Quiet, but not still.

Calculating.

So was he.
