Chapter 42: Aldermere

The proctor's voice cut the morning air. "Rotation groups, report to your assigned rings. Any student failing to complete the assessment will receive a formal reprimand on their permanent record."

Roan Vance didn't move.

He stood in the third row of the Eastern Assessment Yard, forty-two other Initiates breathing the dust through the morning light. Twelve chalk rings dotted the yard, each one littered with training dummies.

Standard quarterly evaluation. It decided class rankings, resource allocation, and which students got quietly removed from the rolls before they became an embarrassment. Seventeen Initiates had disappeared that way last year. The official line was academic withdrawal.

Roan had spent eighteen months making sure the administration placed him in the expendable column. Bottom quartile on every metric. Consistent. Forgettable.

Anonymity was armor.

"You heard the man." Brendt Holloway appeared at his shoulder, jaw tight. "Fourth ring. You?"

"Third."

Roan's eyes swept the station. Two Combat Faculty. One Administrative Oversight. By the eastern wall, a woman whose presence pulled the air taut. Selene Ardent.

House Ardent didn't cover routine assessments. They scouted talent. Which meant something had changed, or Roan was about to find out what it was.

Brendt followed his gaze. "She watching you?"

"She's watching everyone."

"That's not a no."

Roan didn't answer. Brendt knew the rhythm by now.

"Ring Three, form up! Section One begins in ninety seconds!"

Twelve dummies. Embedded Vitalis-sensitive crystals. Metrics on speed, output, technique. Grading designed to give the faculty a clear picture.

Roan had done this six times. Perfect mediocrity across the board. He was about to make it seven.

He stepped into the ring. The whistle blew. Students exploded into motion around him. Roan waited long enough to look ordinary, then moved.

His blade traced a clean arc. The first dummy's head snapped back, crystals flaring green. Second strike. Third. Deliberate. Footwork correct. Output in the lower-middle bracket.

This was where instructors looked away.

He felt it happen—the proctor's attention sliding toward the real prospects on the far side of the yard, where kinetic signatures bloomed hot with genuine ambition.

Eighth dummy. Ninth. Tenth.

*Three more. Safe territory.*

The tenth collapsed. The eleventh.

The air changed.

Pressure against his temples, slow as a rising tide. The proctor's voice went distant. The other students' movements blurred.

Someone was looking at him. Not at his performance. At *him*. Beneath the numbers.

*Selene.*

He couldn't see her from the ring. Didn't need to. The pressure was precise, patient. The assessment style of someone who evaluated *potential*.

She knew something.

The thought hit like cold water. Seventeen months of carefully constructed failure. Drogan had burned the records. The Codex Shard was invisible to standard diagnostics. The basement warded against passive probing.

House Ardent didn't rely on standard methods.

Twelfth dummy. He struck it down with mechanical precision. Crystals flared. The proctor called time. Roan stepped back to the mark, breathing steady.

On the surface, nothing had changed.

Section Two was technique evaluation. Six mandatory forms. No deviation. Roan performed each one as instructed. Proper form. Correct footwork. Output in the expected range.

The pressure from Section One had faded. Either Selene had found what she was looking for, or she'd catalogued the anomaly for later.

*This is what you built. Eighteen months of this.*

The thought sat cold.

*But ceilings work both ways. You built a box to hide in. You're still inside it.*

"Section Three begins in five minutes. Students failing to complete will receive an automatic failure for the entire assessment."

Standard pressure tactic. Roan took the water skin from the attendant, drank without tasting.

Section Three was applied combat. Paired engagement. Randomized opponents.

The pairings appeared on the central board.

Vance, Roan. Opponent: Envoy Greel.

The name clicked into place.

House Greel's heir apparent. Tempered threshold. Top five percent of the Initiate class. Nephew of the man who ordered the assassination of Roan's father.

The official channels called Aldric Vance's death a training accident. The unofficial channels said something different.

The envoy was a better fighter by every measurable metric. Better Vitalis. Better technique. Better equipment.

Exactly the opponent Roan was supposed to lose to.

*But that's not what this is about.*

Selene Ardent at a routine assessment. The envoy at his ring. The timing. The pressure.

Someone was testing him. The question was who, and how much they already knew.

The crowd parted. The envoy walked toward him.

"Vance."

The name carried the weight of fifteen years of neglect. The envoy was tall, fair-haired, posture forged by a lifetime of casual superiority. He didn't smile. He assessed.

"I've heard interesting things about your family line. The elders speak of it with respect."

*Respect that got people killed.*

"I wouldn't know." Roan's voice stayed flat. "The line didn't leave much behind."

The envoy's head tilted. "No. It didn't. Which makes your presence here a curiosity."

The words hung in the space between them. The envoy was fishing. But someone had told him where to cast the line.

The whistle blew. Section Three.

"Same ring as me, Vance." The envoy turned. "Try not to make it boring. The Ardent representative is watching."

Roan stood motionless as the other students filed past.

*Win openly and you confirm their suspicion. Lose too easily and you're worthless, which invites them to dig.*

*Too many variables. Not enough data.*

*The ledger doesn't change. Don't give them what they're looking for.*

Brendt appeared at his side. "Roan. That's the Greel heir. If he decides to hurt you—"

"I know."

"Then what's the plan?"

Roan stepped into the ring. The envoy stood at the center, rolling through warm-up forms with the casual confidence of someone who had never truly lost at anything that mattered.

"The plan is to make sure they don't get what they want."

The proctor raised the whistle.

The envoy's eyes locked onto his.

The whistle blew.

The envoy moved first. Fast. A strike aimed at the exposed line between Roan's guard and his hip.

Roan read the angle. Counter perfectly and Selene's notes would light up. Eat the hit clean and the envoy would know he'd fed it.

He chose the middle. His block caught the strike at an awkward angle. The force drove him back a step. The crystals on his training gear flickered yellow—partial damage.

The envoy's smile sharpened. "You're faster than your scores suggest."

"And you talk more than your reputation suggests."

The envoy's eyes narrowed.

Roan reset his stance. Enough to make the Greel work for the win, not enough to prove anything to the watcher on the wall.

The next exchange came harder. Roan caught the envoy's blade on the flat, turned the momentum, broke the grip. For one heartbeat, he had an open line to the envoy's ribs.

*Don't take it.*

He let the opening close. The envoy recovered, pressing the attack.

The crowd roared. The crystals flashed. The proctor's pen scratched notes that would find their way to House Ardent's files.

Roan's lungs burned. His arms ached from absorbing hits he could have dodged. Every movement required calculation. Too fast and the narrative changed. Too slow and the envoy would turn it into a slaughter, forcing faculty intervention.

Forty-four seconds remained on the clock when the envoy landed the decisive blow. Roan's training blade hit the dirt. The proctor called the match.

The envoy stood over him, breathing hard. "Better than I expected. Almost wasted."

Roan picked up his blade. "Almost."

He walked out of the ring. Brendt called his name. The crowd's noise faded. Selene Ardent was no longer at the wall.

She had what she came for. The question was whether she'd found what she was looking for, or just enough to keep looking.

Roan kept walking.

*Remember everything.*
