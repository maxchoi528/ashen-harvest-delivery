Chapter 99: The Coffee Shop

The coffee shop was called Glassline. It occupied the ground floor of a building that had been standing since before the Black Spire's first expansion, with windows that faced east and a menu that hadn't changed in forty years. Roan had chosen it for the geometry: three exits, a clear line of sight to the entrance, and a basement storage room that could be reached through the kitchen if the front door became unavailable.

He arrived at 2:47 PM, ten minutes early. Ordered black coffee. Sat at the table that gave him the best angle on both the street entrance and the kitchen door. He did not touch the coffee.

The folder sat on the table between his hands. Plain brown cardboard, unmarked, tied with string. Inside: thirty years of Maddox Vell's financial records, cross-referenced against known Conclave payments, flagged deposits, and shell company registrations that traced back to House Vell's northern operations. Also inside: a single sheet of paper with six lines of text, each one a date and a location where Aldric Vance had been seen alive before his disappearance. Each one corroborated by a separate source. Each one placing Aldric in the same cities as Maddox Vell's transfer agents.

The folder was a bomb. Roan had spent three weeks building it.

Iris Vell walked through the door at 2:53.

She was alone. That was the first thing he noted. No escort, no security detail that he could spot. She wore civilian clothes, a dark coat over a grey sweater, her hair down instead of pinned. She looked like someone who had told herself she was meeting a contact for routine intelligence exchange and had almost convinced herself it was true.

She spotted him. Crossed the room. Sat down without removing her coat.

"You're early," she said.

"You're early too." Roan pushed the folder to the center of the table. "That's not why you're here."

Iris's eyes dropped to the folder. Stayed there. "What is it?"

"A gift."

"I don't accept gifts from people who've spent the last month pretending to leak information to me."

Roan didn't react. He had known this conversation would start with the accusation. He had prepared for it.

"You wanted to know what House Vance was hiding," he said. "You thought you were fishing me for intelligence on my father's business network. You followed the trail I left, verified the sources I planted, and convinced yourself that the information you found was real."

Iris's jaw tightened. She didn't deny it.

"The information was real," Roan continued. "Every document you accessed was authentic. But what you found was not the extent of my father's network. It was the outer layer. The part I wanted you to see."

"To see what?"

"To see what happens when you dig deep enough to find something your grandfather didn't want you to know."

Iris's hand moved toward the folder. Stopped. "Explain."

Roan leaned back. The coffee sat between them, untouched, cooling.

"Your grandfather is Maddox Vell. One of the Founding Seven. One of the men who signed the order that killed my father."

The words landed in the space between them. Iris didn't flinch. She was too well-trained for that. But something shifted behind her eyes, a door closing, or opening.

"I know who my grandfather is," she said. "I also know he's been lying to me about what he did before the founding. That's why I started looking in the first place."

"I know."

"You knew?"

"I counted on it." Roan tapped the folder. "Thirty years of financial records. Cross-referenced against Conclave payment logs, Sentinel Bureau transfer authorizations, and personal account statements that your grandfather thought he'd erased. Every transaction that funded the operation against Aldric Vance is in there. Every shell company. Every false flag. Every payment to the contractor who pulled the trigger."

Iris stared at the folder. Her face was pale.

"Why are you giving this to me?"

"Because you deserve to know what your family built its fortune on. And because I want you to understand that when I say I'm not your enemy, I mean it." Roan's voice was flat. "Your grandfather made choices. You don't have to carry them. But you also don't get to pretend they didn't happen."

Iris reached out and pulled the folder toward her.

She untied the string.

She opened the cover.

She read.

For three minutes, she did nothing but turn pages. The coffee shop hummed around them, but Roan had already catalogued the ambient noise as irrelevant. The barista's call, the laughter from the counter—none of it held tactical value. The only sound that mattered was the rustle of paper. Her expression didn't change. Her breathing stayed steady. She was a trained intelligence operative, after all, and trained operatives didn't break down in public.

Then she reached the last page. The single sheet with the six dates and locations. She read it once. Twice.

When she looked up, her eyes were wet.

"He killed him," she said. It wasn't a question.

"Yes."

"My grandfather killed your father."

"With five others. But yes. He was one of the signatories."

Iris closed the folder. Her hands were steady, but her knuckles were white.

"You've had this for three weeks."

"Three weeks, four days."

"And you waited."

"I waited until you found enough on your own to believe it." Roan picked up his coffee. Finally. "If I had handed this to you the first time we met, you would have assumed it was fabricated. You would have run it through your own verification channels, found the connections I'd planted, and convinced yourself it was a disinformation package. You had to find the truth yourself before you could accept it."

Iris laughed. A short, sharp sound that held no humor.

"You manipulated me."

"Yes."

"From the beginning."

"From the moment you asked about my father's death." Roan set the coffee down. "I needed you to see the truth without me having to tell you. Because if I told you, you'd always wonder whether I'd altered it. This way, you know."

The silence stretched. Iris looked at the folder. At Roan. At the folder again.

"You're a monster," she said.

"Your grandfather is the one who signed the execution order."

"I know." She stood. The folder was in her hands. "I know."

She turned to leave. Roan stayed seated.

"Iris."

She stopped. Didn't turn around.

"If you need a place to go after you confront him," Roan said, "the offer stands. I don't make offers I don't mean."

She stood at the door for a long moment. Her shoulders moved once, a breath she hadn't meant to take.

Then she walked out.

The door swung shut behind her. The coffee shop continued its ordinary rhythm. Roan sat alone at the table with his cold coffee and the faint warmth of the Codex Shard against his spine.

He had just lost the best intelligence asset he'd ever cultivated. He had also just gained something more valuable: an ally who knew her grandfather's crimes were real because she had verified them herself.

The math was clean. Or clean enough.

He finished his coffee and left.

Outside, the streets of Ironholm were filling with evening traffic. Somewhere in the city, Iris Vell was walking home with a bomb in her hands, about to detonate everything she thought she knew about her family.

Roan walked the other direction.

The next piece would go out tomorrow. Twenty-four hours until Vell's network detected the leak, and he needed to be across the city when it did.
