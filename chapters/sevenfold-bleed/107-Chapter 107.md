Chapter 107: Round Two

The afternoon sun cut across the eastern training grounds at an angle that made every shadow sharp. Roan adjusted his stance at the edge of the quarter-circle, watching the crowd thicken along the observation platforms. Fifty meters to his left, the main bracket board displayed this round's matchups in stark white paint.

Match 17 of 32. Round Two: Roan Vance vs. Jorin Halftane

The name meant nothing. He ran the answer anyway.

Jorin Halftane. Twenty-one. Hardened tier, second sub-stage. Ironcross District representative. Primary technique: Centrifugal Break, a spinning palm strike that generated rotational force through the hips. Effective at close range. Weakness: the wind-up was visibly telegraphed.

Under a minute to study an opponent most of the crowd had never heard of.

Roan's gaze drifted to the other courts. Cyrus Ashford had finished his own match six minutes ago, clean first-round knockout, the way he always preferred. He stood now on the upper platform, the Ashford crest bright on his collar. His eyes found Roan across the distance.

Cyrus didn't look away.

*He's watching to see what I use*, Roan thought. *Classifying.*

Let him.

The whistle shrilled. Roan walked onto the sand. Jorin Halftane was exactly as advertised. Broad-shouldered, long arms, a build built for power generation. He centered his weight low, testing the sand with his leading foot. His instructor, some regional coach with Ironholm credentials, had drilled him well. No obvious openings. No wasted motion.

But his mental radius stopped at the edge of Roan's display range.

Roan let his own Vitalis reading sit at the lower end of Hardened. 1,100. Just enough to look threatening without confirming the actual number. He rolled his shoulders, letting the Codex Shard settle against his spine.

"Halftane," he said. "Your third cousin fought here two years ago. Mirin Halftane. She made the quarterfinals before losing to a kid from Vellmark."

Jorin's eyes flickered. A family legacy. Something to protect.

"That's not relevant," he said.

"It's not. Just observation." Roan widened his stance. "Your Centrifugal Break has a visible wind-up. I noticed it from the observation deck. Noticed you don't use it in warm-ups, only in actual matches. Means you can suppress it when you're not fighting. Means you're aware of the weakness."

The crowd's murmur shifted. That was more analysis than most sparring matches typically involved.

Jorin's jaw tightened. "You scouting me?"

"Calculating. There's a difference."

The referee signaled. Jorin lunged.

The first strike was a straight palm, probing, testing Roan's guard. Roan sidestepped, letting the strike pass his shoulder. His body knew Drogan's Hollowstrike form better than his own reflexes: redirect, not block. Jorin followed with a hook, then a cross. Three blows, all fast, all trying to establish pace.

Roan absorbed the rhythm.

*He's pushing too hard. Trying to close distance before I can set up a defense.*

Amateur mistake. Or rather, amateur confidence. Jorin had beaten six opponents in regional circuits by overwhelming them early. He expected the same here.

Roan slipped the fourth strike, a wide elbow, and drove his palm into Jorin's exposed ribs.

Jorin grunted. His momentum carried him forward, but he recovered quickly, spinning to create separation. The Centrifugal Break's wind-up began. His hips rotated. His leading shoulder dropped.

*Now.*

Roan moved inside the strike zone.

The spinning palm was fast, a good technique at this tier. But the rotation made the final position predictable. Roan's left hand caught the extended wrist. His right hand found the back of Jorin's neck. He used the spin against him, redirecting the force through Jorin's own momentum and into the sand.

Impact. Dust everywhere.

The referee held. No disqualification. Roan's grip had been controlling, not striking. Technically legal.

Jorin lay on his back for two full seconds. When he rose, his breathing was ragged, his expression shifting from anger to something closer to confusion.

*He doesn't understand how I knew*, Roan thought. *He's trying to calculate whether I cheated, found a match tape, received intelligence.*

Wrong questions. The right question—how did I read him so easily—wouldn't occur to him because he'd never encountered a mental radius that extended past his own by that margin.

"This isn't over," Jorin said.

Roan stepped back, resetting his stance. "Your wind-up is a visible wind-up. You telegraph with your leading shoulder. You rely on overwhelming speed at close range. Your stamina economy is optimized for sixty-second bursts." He tilted his head. "You have approximately forty seconds of competitive output before your form degrades. I'm being generous with the estimate."

Jorin's face went red.

The match resumed. This time, Jorin abandoned the Centrifugal Break, a smart adaptation. He tried a ground-and-pound approach, driving for Roan's legs to initiate a grappling exchange. On a sand court, against someone with less grappling experience, it might have worked.

It didn't work.

Roan slipped the first takedown attempt by rotating his hips and using Jorin's own force against him. The second takedown attempt, more desperate, less technical, he stepped over, landing on Jorin's back and controlling position without submitting.

Three minutes of this. Jorin trying techniques. Roan systematically dismantling each one by identifying the mechanical flaw, the timing window, the energy expenditure. He wasn't just winning. He was *cataloguing*.

The crowd had gone quiet. That happened sometimes, when the outcome became obvious before the end.

Roan let the match continue for another ninety seconds. He wanted Jorin to understand the full scope of his failure. Not brutality. *Education*.

Then he caught Jorin's overcommitted cross, torqued his body, and drove him into the sand with enough force to clear dust clouds from the boundary lines.

Referee stepped in. "Halftane is unable to continue. Vance wins by stoppage."

Right.

Roan wiped his hands on his training pants and walked to the edge of the court. Brendt was waiting there, arms crossed, expression unreadable.

"That was excessive," he said.

"That was efficient."

"Showboating. You didn't need to explain his technique to him. You didn't need to narrate his breakdown." Brendt fell into step beside him. "Half the people watching think you're an arrogant bastard. The other half think you're something else."

"What else?"

Brendt hesitated. "Scary."

Roan didn't respond.

- break -

The evening hours brought the bracket's second round to completion. By midnight, the field had narrowed from thirty-two to sixteen. Roan's name appeared in the upper bracket, paired now with a Tempered-tier fighter from Eastreach named Selden Vourne. A technical specialist. Solid fundamentals. No unusual techniques.

Cyrus Ashford had advanced through the opposite bracket. His second-round opponent had lasted forty seconds.

The bracket board put them on course to meet in the semifinals.

*If he doesn't lose to someone else first.*

Roan sat in his quarters, the Codex Shard cool against his spine. He ran the math on the remaining opponents. Fourteen fighters. Eleven Hardened or below. Two Tempered with exceptional showings. One other Hardened. Selden Vourne, who might present a challenge if his defensive form held.

But the tally didn't include unknowns.

The knock came at 22:47.

Not Brendt's pattern. Not Drogan's deliberate rhythm. Something else, hesitant, searching.

Roan crossed to the door, opened it to find a first-year student. Seventeen, maybe eighteen. Pale. Nervous.

"Mr. Vance?" The kid's voice cracked. "There's a message for you. Someone left it at the junior hall. Said you'd know what it meant."

He held out a folded paper. Roan took it.

The message contained four words, written in a hand he didn't recognize:

The Sanctum knows.

No signature. No context. No trace of the messenger beyond a description—short, dark hair, Eastern features—left before I could ask his name.

Roan closed the door.

The paper sat in his palm for ten seconds. Then another ten. He burned it in a small flame from his fingertips, letting the ash fall into the disposal bin.

The Sanctum knows.

The Veyrn Sanctum. Aldric's hidden vault. The place where the Codex Shard had first taken root. He hadn't told anyone about the Sanctum. Not Brendt. Not Drogan. Not even Lyra.

Someone else knew.

*— A message, not an attack. They're watching. Probing. Testing your response. — A.V.*

Roan sat on the edge of his bed.

The tournament would continue. The matches would proceed. Cyrus would advance. The bracket would resolve.

But the ledger had changed.

The Sanctum knew.

Now he needed to find out who else did.

- break -

The problem had moved from combat to information.

Roan opened a blank page in his notebook and wrote three columns: Sanctum, Messenger, Timing. The first column had the most danger and the least data. The second had a face too ordinary to be useful. The third had teeth.

The message arrived after round two, not before. Whoever sent it had waited until the field narrowed, until Cyrus had a reason to keep watching him, until the tournament's attention made every private movement more expensive.

That suggested one of three possibilities.

One: the sender wanted him distracted before the next bracket.

Two: the sender wanted him to know the Sanctum was compromised but lacked the means to contact him safely.

Three: the sender wanted to measure what Roan would do when a secret became a pressure point.

He disliked the third option most. It meant the message itself was a test.

Roan burned the ash again until nothing recognizable remained, then cleaned the disposal tray with water and a strip of cloth. Excessive, maybe. But paranoia was only wasteful when the cost exceeded the risk.

The Codex Shard stayed cool.

No warning. No commentary. No hint from Aldric's remnant. Either the Shard had nothing to add or it was withholding information because Roan had not asked the correct question.

"Fine," Roan said to the empty room.

The shard didn't answer.

He slept with a knife beneath the pillow, the bracket schedule beside the bed, and the words *The Sanctum knows* reconstructed from memory in the part of his mind that didn't sleep.

By morning, he had a plan.

Win the next match quickly.

Make Cyrus keep watching the wrong hand.

Then find the person who had learned a name he had never spoken aloud.
