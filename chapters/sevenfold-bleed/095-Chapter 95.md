Chapter 95: Pressure Accounting

The morning light hit Greycross from the east, casting long shadows across the training yard. Roan counted them as he walked, three days since he'd returned from Ironholm, four since the tournament's aftermath had stopped echoing in his skull.

Brendt was already at the practice dummies when Roan arrived. His friend had stopped asking questions about the night House Greel's gaze had nearly broken something in Roan's chest. That silence was its own kind of weight.

"You're limping," Brendt said without turning around.

"Training injury." Roan moved to the dummy beside him. "Nothing that matters."

"Theon mentioned you were in the infirmary twice this week."

Roan set his stance. The wooden blade felt heavier than it should have, the Codex Shard running calculations in the background, tracking the residual strain from three consecutive nights of Inversion-improved recovery. His capacity had reset that morning, but using it for something this mundane felt like spending currency on street performers.

"Let me guess," Brendt continued, still not looking. "You're going to say it doesn't matter. That you have it handled. That the ledger works out somehow."

"Numbers were harder to bribe than people."

"There it is."

Roan struck the dummy. The form was clean. Crimson Wake foundation, muscle memory drilled into his sinews. But the follow-through stuttered. A fraction of a second too long. A delay that wouldn't matter against Initiates or Tempered fighters, but against Hardened opponents—

"You've been hiding something for three years," Brendt said quietly. "I'm not asking what. I'm asking why you're pushing yourself like this when the semester's almost over. Like you're expecting something."

The dummy's head cracked under Roan's strike. He hadn't meant to hit it that hard.

"The semester's almost over," he agreed.

"That's not an answer."

"It's the answer you're getting."

Brendt finally turned. His expression wasn't angry, it was worse than that. It was patient. Patience that waited for you to fill the silence because it knew you eventually would.

"The exams," Roan said. "There's something I need to be ready for."

"Something specific."

"Something that won't wait for next semester."

Brendt studied him. The morning light caught the edge of his jaw, the set of his shoulders. He'd grown in the three months since the tournament. Not physically, or not physically alone, but in the way he carried himself. Like he'd started believing he belonged here.

"Is this about your father?" Brendt asked.

The question landed like a blade between ribs. Roan felt his breathing pause, the involuntary hitch that happened when something struck too close to a nerve he'd thought he'd numbed.

"What makes you think that?"

"You go quiet when people mention Greycross. When they talk about old families, or what happened during the Sundering. And three days ago, when we passed the memorial stones by the east gate, you stopped reading the names. Like you already knew them."

Roan picked up the wooden blade. Tested its balance. The weight was fine. The problem wasn't the blade.

"My father was investigated by the Sentinel Bureau," Roan said. "Twelve years ago. The case was closed. He was cleared. The official record says he died in a Rift incursion like everyone else in our family."

"And the unofficial record?"

Roan hit the dummy again. This time the strike was cleaner, the anger channeled into something useful.

"There's a file. I found it three months ago, buried in the old archives. Partial testimony. Witness statements that were struck from the official record because they 'lacked corroborating evidence.'" He let the word sit. "Witnesses who described my father fighting alongside someone they couldn't identify. Someone who wasn't from any known Conclave house."

"Fighting against or fighting with?"

Brendt knew him well enough to ask the right question. Most people would have assumed the confusion was about sides, about whether Aldric Vance had been collaborator or enemy. But the question was deeper than that.

"The witness descriptions contradicted each other. Same figure—tall, dark clothes, moved like a Forged or higher. Some said he was holding the Hollowkin back. Others said he was directing them." Roan paused. "One witness said she heard my father call him by name before everything went silent."

"What name?"

Roan shook his head. "The name was redacted. But the witness remembered it starting with a K."

Brendt was quiet for a moment. The training yard was filling with other students now, the usual morning crowd drifting toward their preferred stations. None of them were paying attention to the conversation in the corner.

"K," Brendt said slowly. "The founding families. House the old signatory, House the envoy—"

"House the envoy no longer exists. They were dissolved after the Sundering. Their estate was seized, their bloodline scattered." Roan watched Brendt's face for the recognition. "The official record says they were collaborators. That they helped open the path for the Hollow Reaches incursion."

"And the unofficial record?"

"Nowhere to be found. Someone made sure of that."

Brendt exhaled. "Roan. What are you asking me?"

The morning sun had climbed high enough to warm the back of his neck. Roan reset his stance, oriented himself toward the dummy, felt the geometry of the strike resolve in his muscles.

"I'm asking if you want to help me find out what really happened to my father. And whether the people who were supposed to protect us let him die."

The room went quiet. Brendt's breathing was steady, he was thinking, calculating the odds the same way Roan would have. The difference was that Brendt would say yes before he'd finished calculating.

"This is dangerous," Brendt said finally. "Worse than 'might get expelled' dangerous. 'Might get disappeared' dangerous."

"Yes."

Roan could see the arithmetic happening behind his friend's eyes—the weight of three years of trust against the gravity of what he was suggesting. Brendt had a healthy fear of powerful people. It was one of his smarter traits.

"If this blows up and I end up in a Rift with a hole in my chest, I'm going to haunt you."

"I'd expect nothing less."

Brendt let out a breath. "Then I'm in." He struck the dummy beside him. The form was rougher than Roan's, less refined, but there was power behind it. "But you're going to tell me what we're walking into. No vague 'something at the exams.'"

"The exams are the first access point. The old archive vault is sealed during the term, only faculty can enter during the semester. But during exams, the vault is opened for three days to process student records. Anyone with a student ID can get in."

"That's your in."

"That's the easy part." Roan hit the dummy again. "The hard part is that the archive index was updated six years ago. Someone went through the old Vance case file and removed the partial testimony. Pulled the original witness statements."

"You know who?"

"Not yet. But I know when. The index modification timestamp—I matched it against the administrative metadata from the records system—lines up perfectly with a Sentinel Bureau personnel transfer. Someone moved from Greycross to Ironholm right around that time."

Brendt's eyes narrowed. "You're talking about someone still active. Still close to the Black Spire."

"I'm talking about someone who might've been in the exhibition hall three days ago, watching me fight. Someone who would recognize the Vance name and connect it to an unsolved cold case."

The morning crowd had grown loud enough that their conversation was swallowed by the ambient noise. Good. Roan didn't need witnesses for this particular math.

"That's why you're limping," Brendt said quietly. "You're not training. You're checking something."

"Testing." Roan adjusted his grip on the wooden blade. "The Codian Reflexes, the technique I used against the Greel envoy. It came from somewhere. My father left notes. Fragments. Most of them don't make sense, but some of them activate when I—" He stopped. Too much information too fast. "When I push myself past certain thresholds."

"Activate how?"

"The body remembers what the mind forgot." That was as close to the truth as he could get. "When I use that technique, I see things. Flashes. Sometimes they're memories. Sometimes they're just shapes. But they tell me things I couldn't know any other way."

Brendt processed this. His friend had always been quick on the uptake, faster than most of their classmates gave him credit for.

"You're going to try to activate it during the exams," Brendt said. "Force whatever's in your head to show you something useful."

"I'm going to try to activate it when I access the archive. See if there's something in there that triggers a connection." Roan paused. "Or something in my head that connects to what's in there."

"That's insane."

"Probably."

"And if it doesn't work?"

"Then I have a backup plan."

Brendt laughed, a short, sharp sound that drew a few glances from nearby students. "Of course you do. Of course you have a backup plan for your backup plan."

Roan allowed himself a small smile. "The balance has to work. I just haven't figured out all the variables yet."

"What do you need from me?"

"Cover. Someone who looks like they're just helping a friend with exam prep. Someone who can watch the doors while I'm inside the vault." He met Brendt's eyes. "Someone who won't ask questions about why I'm spending three hours staring at case files from twelve years ago."

"I can do that." Brendt's stance shifted, his weight settling into something more comfortable. "What about Drogan? The Warden knows more than he's saying. He was here during the Sundering. He might—"

"He might also be exactly what I'm afraid of."

The words came out harder than Roan had intended. Brendt flinched, just slightly, just enough that Roan knew he'd struck something unexpected.

"You think Drogan was involved?" Brendt asked carefully.

"I think Drogan was in Black Spire during the investigation. I think he knew my father. I think he knows exactly why the case was closed the way it was, and he's been watching me since I arrived because he can't decide whether I'm going to be a problem or an asset." Roan's jaw tightened. "I don't know which answer is true. That's the problem."

"You could just ask him."

"And risk alerting him to what I'm looking for?" Roan shook his head. "No. The archive first. Then I know what questions to ask. Then I decide whether to trust him with the answers."

Brendt absorbed this. He was quiet, the wooden blade resting against his shoulder.

"There's something else," he said. "Something you haven't told me."

Roan didn't answer.

"The night of the tournament. After House Greel looked at you. You disappeared for two hours. I checked the dormitory, the training yards, the library, everywhere. And then you showed up at breakfast like nothing had happened, except you weren't limping anymore. You weren't even tired." Brendt's eyes were steady. "What happened in those two hours?"

The question hung in the air. The morning sun was bright enough now that Roan could feel the heat on his skin, the faint prickle of sweat beginning at his hairline. Around them, students cycled through their training forms, the rhythmic crack of wooden blades on dummies filling the yard.

Roan considered lying. The lie would be easy enough—a healing draught from the infirmary, a brief rest in an empty classroom, something mundane and forgettable. Brendt would accept it. His friend had a healthy sense of self-preservation and would prefer a comfortable fiction to an uncomfortable truth.

But Brendt had just agreed to help him break into an archive and dig through cold case files about their dead families. That kind of commitment earned a slightly higher truth threshold.

"I found something," Roan said quietly. "In my father's old things. Something that told me what he was really doing during the Sundering. And something that told me why someone might have wanted him dead."

"Something that explains the fighting stance you used against the Greel envoy?"

"Something that explains why the Greel envoy recognized that stance. And why his father looked at me like I was a ghost that shouldn't exist."

Brendt absorbed this. The morning noise continued around them—students laughing, instructors shouting corrections, the endless percussion of practice. But their corner of the yard had grown very quiet.

"You're saying your father used that technique before," Brendt said slowly. "That it's not something you learned from a book or a teacher. It's something you inherited."

"I'm saying there's a reason House Greel crossed an ocean to watch a tournament semifinal. And there's a reason he left after I used that technique." Roan adjusted his grip on the wooden blade. "I'm saying that whatever my father was doing twelve years ago, it connects to whatever's happening right now. And I need to find that connection before someone else finds it first."

Brendt nodded slowly. His expression had settled into something Roan recognized—the look of someone who'd done the arithmetic and decided to commit despite the odds.

"Three days until exams start," Brendt said. "What do we do until then?"

"Train. Act normal. Don't mention anything about archives or old cases or Vance family secrets to anyone." Roan struck the dummy one more time, feeling the impact travel up his arm. "And if anyone asks about my training injury, you don't know anything because I didn't tell you anything."

"Understood."

The morning bell rang across the yard, the signal for the end of free training and the start of formal instruction. Students began filtering toward the equipment racks, returning practice blades, moving into their assigned formations.

"Brendt." Roan caught his friend's arm as they walked. "Thank you."

"For what? I haven't done anything yet."

"For not asking if this is a good idea. Most people would have asked that."

Brendt smiled, the expression carrying an edge that reminded Roan why they'd become friends in the first place. "It's not a good idea. It's a terrible idea. But terrible ideas that work are better than good ideas that don't, and you've been making terrible ideas work since the day I met you."

"High praise."

"It's not praise. It's statistical observation." Brendt dropped his voice as other students passed. "Just promise me one thing."

"What?"

"If this goes sideways, if we find something that's worse than what we're expecting, you don't try to handle it alone. You come to me. You come to Drogan, to Mira, to anyone. You don't turn into another Vance ghost haunting Greycross corridors."

Roan didn't answer immediately. The promise was heavier than it sounded, heavier than Brendt realized. Because if they found what he expected to find, the answer wouldn't be something that could be handled by going to authority figures. It would be something that required a Vance to handle. And there was only one Vance left.

"I'll try," Roan said finally.

"That's not good enough."

"It's what you're getting."

Brendt shook his head, but he didn't press. They walked toward the equipment racks in silence, joining the flow of students moving into their morning formations. The sun was fully up now, burning off the last of the morning mist, casting the training yard in sharp relief.

Three days until exams. Three days to pretend that nothing was different, that he was just another student preparing for another test. Three days to let the Codex Shard run its calculations in the background, weighing variables he couldn't name, searching for patterns in data he didn't have.

The math would work. It always did. But sometimes the math was incomplete.

And incomplete math had a way of biting back.

- break -

The infirmary was quiet at noon. Roan waited until the lunch shift drew the nurses to the cafeteria, then slipped through the side entrance he'd mapped three weeks ago. The door's lock was mechanical, old construction, no electronic logging, no cameras pointed at the service corridor. Small mercies in an institution that had learned to watch its students.

The storage closet held what he needed: a bottle of Wraithmilk Tincture, mid-grade, the kind that sold for forty credits a dose and gave most students the shakes for three days afterward. The label warned of "temporary psychic sensitivity" and "possible auditory distortion." The more expensive versions filtered out those side effects. The cheaper ones didn't.

Roan didn't care about the side effects. He cared about what came after.

He uncorked the bottle. The liquid inside was pale blue, almost luminous in the dim light of the storage closet. The smell hit him immediately—sharp, astringent, like copper and ozone mixed together.

One swallow.

[Sidewound detected. Pattern: psychic hypersensitivity. Auditory distortion active.]
[Conversion queued. Capacity sufficient.]

The tincture burned a cold path down his throat. For a fraction of a second, the world swam, sounds bending into strange harmonics.

Then the Codex Shard activated.

[Conversion successful: +85 Vitalis. Side effects neutralized. Pattern logged.]

The distortion cut off mid-sound. The pressure eased not into a manageable ache, but into complete, absolute clarity. The world sharpened into perfect detail. Roan closed his eyes. The sense of being watched was still there—a thread of awareness stretched thin across the distance—but it was pure signal now. No noise. No distortion.

He breathed out slowly.

Somewhere in the Black Spire, someone was thinking about him. Not consciously, not yet, but the connection was there, thin as spider silk, cold as deep water. He couldn't see the source. Couldn't identify the shape. But the presence registered like a hand on his shoulder from across a crowded room.

*Not yet*, Roan thought. *But soon.*

He tucked the empty bottle back into the storage crate, adjusted his clothes, and slipped out the way he'd come. The hallway was empty. The lunch crowd was still in the cafeteria. No one had seen him.

The ledger was running. The threads were aligning. But the equation kept throwing an error he couldn't solve.

He reached the training yard. The presence he'd felt wasn't idle curiosity. It was observation. Protocol. Someone had been waiting for him to make a move.

The only variable he couldn't solve was whether they were waiting to help him, or waiting to bury him.

Three days. He had three days to find out before the archive opened.
