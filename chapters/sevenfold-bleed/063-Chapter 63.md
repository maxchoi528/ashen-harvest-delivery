Chapter 63: Six Hundredth Place

The corridor outside the seminar hall stretched longer than it should have.

Roan counted the doors as he walked, twelve on the left, eleven on the right, the same as last month. Maintenance hadn't updated the lighting arrays either, which meant someone had filed a complaint that went nowhere. He filed that observation away too. Information about what the administration ignored had its own value.

Three students passed going the opposite direction. Two gave him the standard acknowledgment: eyes forward, minimal acknowledgment without engagement. The third, younger, overeager, wearing a freshman's training token, let his gaze linger too long before snapping it away.

Roan noted the face. He didn't know it, which meant it wasn't someone with reason to study him. Which meant it was someone with orders. He filed that observation alongside his rank: six hundredth place on the last assessment. Low enough for breathing room, not low enough for comfort.

- break -

The courtyard beyond the east wing was busier than ideal. Roan cut through the western arc of the garden path, where the hedgerows were tall enough to provide cover without looking like he was hiding. Old Pell's bench sat empty at the far end, the old man was either in the infirmary again or tending to whatever project consumed his afternoons.

He settled onto the bench and pulled the crumpled paper from his jacket pocket.

Tarn Boyce—fourth-year, deep in gambling debt, sharp instincts buried under bad decisions. Roan had pulled him out of a spiral three months ago, not out of kindness, but because Tarn moved through Ironholm’s underbelly like a current. The price was information. Tarn paid on time.

His handwriting was cramped, efficient, the penmanship of someone who had learned to write under time pressure.

Three lines:

*the Greel envoy arrived at Ironholm yesterday. Staying at the Vell compound. Meeting with his sister.*

Roan read it twice. Then once more.

The Greel envoy. The Northern Crown's second son. The one who had publicly humiliated Brendt at the Aldermere Invitational eighteen months ago, not through superior technique, but by goading him into breaking rank formation to pursue a feigned retreat. The entire match had been a trap. Brendt had walked into it anyway, and the memory of that match still lived in the tendons of his left shoulder.

Roan folded the paper once, then again. He kept the second fold tight.

Iris Vell. The envoy's sister. The family connection between House Greel and House Vell ran deeper than most outsiders realized—closer than strategic alliance, closer than blood oath—a connection that coordinated movements across two continents without needing to state intentions.

Which meant the Greel envoy's presence at the Vell compound wasn't a courtesy call. And the timing couldn't be coincidental.

The tournament finals were eleven days out.

- break -

Brendt found him there, because Brendt always found him when it mattered.

"You're going to ask me about the Invitational." Brendt dropped onto the bench beside him, close enough that their shoulders nearly touched. Not accidental. Deliberate. He was signaling something, to Roan, or to anyone watching, or to himself.

"I'm going to ask you what you're going to do when he shows up at the tournament," Roan said.

Brendt's jaw worked once. Twice. A sharp exhale through the nose.

"That bad, then."

"He'll be in the stands for the qualifier rounds at minimum. Front row, probably. Watching."

"Watching you, or watching me?"

"Both. But you already know which one he'll be targeting."

Brendt was quiet for a moment. The garden was loud enough with distant footsteps and the irrigation system cycling through its afternoon rotation that the silence didn't read as empty. It read as processing.

"He'll do it again," Brendt said finally. "The same thing. I know he will. I know exactly what he's going to try. And I still—" He stopped. His right hand opened and closed against his thigh. "I've run the scenario in my head a hundred times. The counter for the feigned retreat is to hold position and let him overextend. I know the balance."

Roan studied his friend. Brendt's shoulders were set in the way they got when he was holding something down, a posture Roan's father had described once as *carrying weight you haven't earned yet*.

"The problem isn't the counter," Roan said. "The problem is that you care whether you look afraid. And he knows you care."

"I know he knows."

"So your counter isn't a counter. It's a counter to what you think he expects. But what he expects is for you to try to not-look-afraid while you're doing it. Which means you're already compromised before the first exchange."

Brendt exhaled sharply. "I know."

"You know, and knowing doesn't help."

"That's why I came to you."

Roan turned the paper over in his pocket. The Greel envoy. Iris Vell. The Vell compound. The tournament in eleven days.

"The question isn't how you beat him," Roan said. "The question is what you're willing to lose to beat him. Those are two very different questions, and most people can't tell the difference."

"Can you?"

Roan didn't answer immediately. He watched a groundskeeper a coil of tubing across the far lawn, the drag mark leaving a faint dark line in the grass. The man moved with the mechanical efficiency of someone who had done the same task so many times it no longer required thought.

"I can tell the difference," he said. "Whether I can do it's a different question."

"Roan."

"What."

"You're doing that thing where you answer a question I didn't ask."

Roan tilted his head. Fractional. Enough to acknowledge the observation without conceding the point. "The Greel envoy uses provocation as a primary tactic. It works because most people are emotionally reactive under pressure. You are—" He paused, selecting the word. "Predictably so. In that specific context."

"Thanks."

"I'm not insulting you. I'm stating a constraint. Constraints are solvable." He pulled the paper out, smoothed it against his thigh, and showed Brendt the message. Watched the color change in his friend's face, first confusion, then recognition, then something that sat in the chest cavity without a clean name.

"Iris," Brendt said.

"Iris."

"She and the envoy are close. Closer than most siblings. If he's here—"

"Whatever is happening at that compound involves planning that extends past the tournament. The qualifier rounds are eleven days out. He's not here for the qualifier rounds."

Brendt's hand closed over the paper. Not grabbing. Holding. "What are you not telling me?"

"I'm telling you what I know. The rest is math."

"You're doing it again."

Roan took the paper back. "Then stop asking questions you already know the answer to."

- break -

He went to the archive wing that evening, which was where he went when he needed to think without the noise.

The archive's third sub-level was technically restricted to senior students and faculty, but the access lock on the east stairwell had been malfunctioning for three weeks and no one had filed a repair request, or if they had, it had joined the same administrative void that swallowed the corridor lighting complaints. Roan appreciated the Black Spire's consistency in maintaining its blind spots.

He pulled the relevant files: House Greel. House Vell. The strategic relationship between the northern territories and the Vell industrial network. The Greel envoy's tournament history, four matches, four wins, all by psychological break rather than technical superiority. Iris Vell's profile, the tournament record was less extensive but showed the same pattern. Subtle manipulation. Controlled provocation. The appearance of fair play masking something that wasn't fair play at all.

Roan spread the documents across the reading table and began connecting the data points.

The envoy and Iris used the same trigger sequence. Not identical, the envoy favored the feigned retreat; Iris favored the provoked response, but the underlying architecture was the same. They identified the opponent's psychological pressure point, constructed a scenario that tested it directly, then capitalized on the resulting breakdown in form.

The solution, theoretically, was simple. Don't have pressure points.

Brendt had pressure points. Everyone had pressure points. The question was whether they could be removed, circumvented, or weaponized against the person deploying them.

He was three pages deep into Iris's match analysis when the archive's ambient hum shifted. A different frequency. The ventilation system had cycled, which happened at predictable intervals, unless someone had manually overridden the cycle.

Footsteps. Two sets. Neither heavy, neither attempting stealth in any meaningful way.

Roan gathered the papers, folded them into his jacket, and was half-standing when the footsteps rounded the corner.

Two figures. The lead one he recognized: Sentinel Halric, the Black Spire's on-campus security liaison, a Tempered-tier enforcer who had personally handled three Rift incursions inside city limits. The second was someone Roan didn't recognize, tall, angular, wearing civilian clothes that fit wrong in a way that suggested the cut prioritized concealment of movement over appearance.

Sentinel Halric's eyes tracked to the table. To the chair Roan had been sitting in. To the documents that were no longer visible.

"Vance."

"Sentinel."

"The archives close at nine."

Roan checked the phosphor-globe mounted on the far wall. 8:47. He had twelve minutes before the stated closing time. That wasn't why Halric was here. "I was just leaving."

Halric stepped to the side, not yielding the corridor, but adjusting his angle. The second figure mirrored the motion with mechanical precision. They were coordinating without signals. Trained.

"The gentleman with me has some questions about your recent travels," Halric said. "He'd like a word."

Roan's hand stayed in his jacket pocket. His fingers found the edge of Tarn's note. "I'm not aware of any reason I'd have questions to answer."

"That's not what I said."

"No. You said he'd like a word. I'd like to not have a word. I suspect we'll both be disappointed."

The second figure tilted his head. A small motion, maybe two degrees. Gesture that could be written off as curiosity, but Roan had seen it before in tournament footage. A readied stance disguised as casual attention.

"Who are you," Roan said. Directed at the second figure. Not at Halric.

"My name is Cassius. I'm with the Sentinel Bureau's External Affairs division." The voice was flat, modulated. Practiced at making statements sound like non-statements. "I'm investigating some anomalies in travel records connected to students affiliated with your academy."

"Travel records."

"You left Greycross eight weeks ago. Returned five days later. The declaration you filed listed standard family business."

Roan waited.

"Standard family business doesn't typically require a twenty-eight-hour window in Ironholm with no documented accommodation." Cassius paused. "Or a visit to the Old Quarter with a known unlicensed equipment dealer."

Eckhart. Cassius was talking about Eckhart.

Roan ran the arithmetic. The travel declaration was clean, the Academy required them for all off-campus travel, but the administrative review process was perfunctory. Someone had gone digging. Someone had found the gap between the declaration and the actual timeline. Someone had connected the gap to Eckhart's shop.

"If you're here to discuss the equipment dealer," Roan said, "I think you should know that the Sentinel Bureau's jurisdiction over student purchasing habits ended when the last guild reform passed. Six years ago. Unless you have evidence of a criminal transaction, of which I would note I have no knowledge, your questions are best directed elsewhere."

Cassius didn't blink. "We have evidence of a transaction."

"Then you have evidence of a transaction. I wasn't a party to any transaction. You'll need to take that up with whoever was."

Silence. The archive hummed around them.

"You seem calm for someone being questioned by External Affairs," Cassius said.

"I'm being asked questions by someone in civilian clothes who won't make eye contact while he speaks. Either he's embarrassed about something, or he's deliberately avoiding creating a documented connection between himself and this conversation. Given that External Affairs doesn't typically initiate contact in restricted archive spaces, I'd guess the latter." Roan looked at Halric. "Sentinel. Is there a formal complaint registered that I should be aware of?"

Halric's expression didn't change, but something in his posture shifted. A subtle rotation, not toward Cassius, but away from him. A slight increase in the distance between them.

Halric wasn't comfortable with this.

"There's no formal complaint on file," Halric said.

"Then I'm going to return to my dormitory." Roan stepped forward, directly toward the space between the two men. Not aggressive. Not hesitant. The posture of someone who had every right to walk through a corridor and expected the corridor to accommodate him.

Cassius didn't move. Halric did, a half-step sideways, creating the gap Roan had expected him to create.

Roan walked through it.

Behind him, he heard Cassius say something too low to parse. Halric's response was inaudible from the stairwell.

He climbed to the surface level and was halfway across the main courtyard before he allowed himself to think about what had just happened.

Cassius hadn't come to the archive by accident. He hadn't been monitoring travel declarations randomly. Someone had flagged Roan's Greycross trip specifically and directed External Affairs to investigate. The fact that Cassius had gone through Halric, a local security officer, rather than filing an official inquiry suggested he was operating outside standard channels. Which meant someone in the Bureau had authorized off-book surveillance.

The question was who. And whether it connected to the Greel envoy's presence in Ironholm.

- break -

Roan found a quiet corner of the training yards and sat on the equipment ledge with his back to the wall.

The evening was cool enough that most students had retreated indoors. The sparring circles were empty except for a pair of second-years running drills in the far corner, their movements coordinated and unremarkable. Routine practice. The kind that happened without anyone watching.

He pulled his phone and composed a message to Tarn Boyce. Short. Encrypted through the school's internal relay, which was less secure than a dedicated cipher but sufficient for his purposes, mostly because he was communicating with someone who understood the difference between security and paranoia.

*the Greel envoy. Connection to External Affairs. Need confirmation.*

He sent it. Then he sat with his hands on his knees and let the evening settle around him.

External Affairs investigating a student purchase from an unlicensed dealer wasn't normal. The Sentinel Bureau had jurisdictional authority over Rift-related security and public safety enforcement. Student purchasing habits fell under Academy administrative review. Cassius being in that archive tonight wasn't Bureau business. It was someone's private business, conducted using Bureau resources.

The Greel envoy had been in Ironholm for less than a full day.

Roan had been to Eckhart's shop yesterday.

The math wasn't difficult. Someone had put Cassius onto him within hours of the envoy's arrival. Which meant either the envoy had pre-arranged this investigation before he even left the northern territories, or he had contacts inside the Ironholm Bureau who were independently motivated to monitor anyone connected to House Vance.

Neither option was comfortable.

His phone buzzed. Tarn's response was three words: *Confirming. Will dig.*

Roan deleted both messages. He sat in the cooling air and listened to the second-years finish their drills, pack their gear, and leave without noticing him.

Eleven days. He counted them again.

The Codex Shard hummed faintly against his spine, not active, not triggering, just present.

He sat with the hum for a long moment. Then he thought about Brendt, sitting somewhere in the dorms, running the same impossible scenario through his head for the hundred-and-first time. He thought about the Greel envoy's face in the tournament footage, calm, patient, waiting for the moment when the opponent would break because they couldn't help it.

Roan stood. He rolled his shoulders once, twice.

Then he walked toward the eastern training hall, where the weighted dummies stood in rows and the floor was thick enough to absorb the sound of movement without echo.

He had eleven days. He needed to make them count.
