Chapter 184: Diplomatic Pressure

The Northern Compact delegation arrived in three vehicles and one implied army.

That was the proper way to do diplomacy, apparently. No armored convoy. No visible weapons beyond ceremonial sidearms. No practitioners radiating enough Vitalis to force a security response. Just three black cars passing through Black Spire's east gate under cold morning rain, each bearing a small white pennant with the northern crownflower stitched in silver thread.

Subtle. Expensive. Insufferable.

Roan watched from a second-floor preparation room beside the administrative hearing chamber, seated in a medical chair that had been chosen by committee. It supported his ribs, restricted sudden movement, and made him look like a victim if viewed from the correct angle. Ines had adjusted the chair twice and called it "optics with lumbar support."

She wasn't wrong.

His splinted left hand rested visibly on the arm. The bruising around his throat had been left uncovered. The healers had wanted to reduce swelling more aggressively. Roan had declined. Painkillers interfered with timing, and swelling was evidence.

Drogan had returned at dawn with the Vance Pendant.

It now rested beneath Roan's shirt again, repaired badly but honestly. Eckhart hadn't restored it to full function. The central crack remained as a hairline scar through the disk, filled with some dark solder that drank light. The signal sink felt uneven, as if one side of his chest stood in shadow and the other in weather.

But the Shard's first prompt after contact had been acceptable.

[External anchor restored: 61%.]

[Signal bleed reduced.]

[Warning: fracture memory retained.]

Fracture memory.

A concerning phrase. Also not today's most urgent fire.

Drogan stood behind Roan's chair, close enough to stop him standing and far enough to pretend that wasn't the purpose. Selene waited by the inner door in formal Ardent gray. Iris sat at the small side table with a stack of printed documents, ink pen in hand, expression sharpened into something more dangerous than guilt. Ines had a listening crystal nested inside a plain folder and wore the pleasant look of a woman currently committing three privacy violations for educational reasons.

Cole entered without ceremony.

The room changed around him. Not because of power. Cole didn't throw his Vitalis around like a House patriarch trying to impress old mirrors. He carried institutional authority like a blade no one could legally ask him to put down.

"Final terms," he said.

Iris slid one printed page forward. "Greel counsel will frame the request as cooperative inquiry, not custody. They will define Roan as a material actor in alleged oath interference. They will request temporary transfer to a Northern Compact neutral site. That site is controlled by Greel allies."

Cole nodded. "Expected."

Selene said, "They may introduce Kael's minority status under compact law."

"He's enrolled here as an adult combatant," Cole said.

"Yes. But they will use emotional weight, not legal force."

"Expected."

Ines tapped the folder. "One of the auditors is compromised."

Cole looked at her.

"Not bought outright," she said. "Overleveraged. Student debt, family medical bond, advisory contract with a Greel-aligned foundation. He will ask procedural questions that sound neutral and land north."

"Name."

"Marwick Hain."

Cole's mouth tightened. "I know him."

"That sounded like bad news," Iris said.

"He's very good at sounding reasonable."

"Bad news."

Roan looked through the glass wall into the hearing chamber.

The room had been arranged as a crescent. Cole's seat at center, raised by only half a step, enough to designate authority without theater. Delegation table opposite. Neutral observers to the left. Recorder station to the right. No audience, but every word logged under Spire record and mirrored to three sealed archives.

Rules mattered here. An arena without visible edges wasn't safe—it was a test. The edge of this one lay in jurisdiction. If the rule wasn't written, he had to find where the fight was actually allowed.

Greel would claim bloodline law.

Cole would claim institutional custody.

Roan would test where public confession intersected with attempted extradition.

Pressure ledger: Greel wanted transfer. Cole wanted refusal without rupture. Roan wanted the refusal on record and the demand made costly enough to force Vorath into personal action under witnesses.

Resource ledger potential: legal shield, public timing, names, Aldric case reopening.

Enemy ledger: Greel counsel, northern delegates, compromised auditor, absent Vorath.

Next debt: once Cole refused, Roan had to decide whether to speak publicly.

Cole looked at him. "You understand your role."

"Yes."

"State it."

"Sit. Bleed quietly. Don't speak unless addressed. Look difficult to justify kidnapping."

Selene closed her eyes for half a second.

Ines smiled.

Cole said, "Crude, but sufficient."

Drogan leaned down near Roan's ear. "If you try to use this room as a trap without telling me, I carry you out by the collar."

"I am injured."

"I'll use the back of the chair."

"Noted."

The inner door opened.

The hearing began.

Roan's chair rolled through the side door under medical escort, which had the useful effect of making every northern representative watch him enter slowly. Speed would have made him look combative. Assistance made him look controlled. The splint and bruising, plus careful breathing did the rest.

Maera Voss looked at him once and immediately looked away.

Not guilt. Calculation. She had expected injury. She hadn't expected it displayed without theatrics. That distinction mattered. A theatrical injury could be dismissed as manipulation. A practical one, presented as though everyone in the room already knew what it meant, forced opponents either to acknowledge it or look cowardly avoiding it.

Roan placed his right hand on the chair arm and let his left remain visible. The Vance Pendant sat beneath his shirt, its repaired crack cold against his sternum. The Codex Shard remained quiet, but not asleep. He could feel it the way one felt a knife on a table even when no hand touched it.

The silent security man at the Greel table glanced toward Roan's chest.

There.

Not enough for proof. Enough for attention.

Roan logged him again. Suppressed Vitalis, probable sensory training, awareness of signal shielding. Not counsel. More than a guard. A classifier, perhaps, sent to observe what Vorath couldn't yet handle directly.

The man looked away a fraction too late.

Useful.

The hearing began.

- break -

The Greel delegation had dressed for mourning.

That was Roan's first useful observation.

Not literal mourning. No black veils without public grief. But every northern representative wore white with muted silver trim, the palette of injured dignity under Greel custom. It made Kael's public defeat read as a family wound. It also made Roan's bruises look more dramatic by contrast, which suggested their stylist had prioritized internal symbolism over cross-room optics.

Mistake.

Small, but pleasant.

The lead counsel was a woman named Maera Voss, mid-forties, pale hair cut at the jaw, eyes calm enough to suggest either excellent discipline or no inner life. She placed a leather folio on the table and didn't open it immediately. Beside her sat two northern envoys, one compact clerk, and a silent man whose Vitalis had been suppressed too neatly.

Security, then. Not officially.

Roan filed him under possible problem. Not yet proven. Not yet dangerous enough to prioritize. But noted.

Neutral observers took their seats. Marwick Hain looked exactly like Ines had implied: soft-spoken, tidy, reasonable, a man who could suggest an execution be delayed until after lunch and sound compassionate.

Cole opened the session.

No speech. No welcome beyond legal minimum. He stated time, parties and matter, plus record preservation status. Then he allowed Greel to present.

Maera Voss stood.

"Headmaster Cole, Black Spire representatives, neutral observers. House Greel recognizes Black Spire's historic neutrality and its authority over internal student discipline. We also recognize that extraordinary circumstances require cooperative action between sovereign institutions."

Good start. Respect before knife.

"This matter concerns not a common duel injury, nor a House dispute improperly imported into academic grounds. It concerns the apparent forced destabilization of a lawful bloodline oath belonging to Kael Greel, minor heir under Northern Compact inheritance doctrine, during a duel in which Roan Vance employed unregistered techniques of unknown origin."

Roan kept his face still.

Unregistered techniques. Unknown origin. The phrase reached toward Codex without naming it.

"House Greel therefore requests that Mr. Vance be transferred temporarily to a neutral northern inquiry site for no more than seventy-two hours, under medical supervision, with Black Spire observers permitted, for the limited purpose of determining whether prohibited oath interference occurred."

Temporary. Neutral. Medical. Observers. Limited.

A box lined with velvet was still a box.

Cole asked, "Does House Greel deny that Kael Greel confessed to entering Black Spire to kill Roan Vance on Vorath Greel's order?"

The room cooled by several degrees.

Maera didn't blink. "House Greel contests the admissibility of that confession."

"That wasn't my question."

"No, Headmaster. It was your framing."

Good.

Roan approved despite himself. Maera was competent.

Cole leaned back. "Then answer directly."

"House Greel doesn't concede the validity of words spoken under oath destabilization."

"Does House Greel deny the words were spoken?"

"No."

The recorder crystal brightened slightly, marking the answer.

First cut: placed.

Maera continued before Cole could press. "But words produced by interference can't be treated as confession. A coerced mouth may produce any sentence."

Roan watched her hands. No tremor. No ring on the right index finger, which most House counsel used for memory prompts. Either she had memorized everything or the prompt was embedded elsewhere.

Hain raised one hand. "Headmaster, if I may clarify."

Cole's eyes moved. "You may ask."

"Was Mr. Vance's technique registered before the duel?"

Expected.

Cole said, "The weapon was registered. His internal circulation methods are protected student medical privacy unless deployed as prohibited art."

Hain nodded reasonably. "Of course. Yet if an internal method interacts with another student's oath structure, does institutional privacy override bloodline security?"

There it was.

A question shaped like balance. Weighted like a chain.

Cole said, "Only if House Greel establishes that the oath structure was lawful within Black Spire grounds and disclosed during student enrollment."

Maera's stillness sharpened.

Roan almost smiled.

Greel had a problem. If Kael's command oath was lawful, it should've been disclosed under high-risk technique registration. If it was undisclosed, Kael had entered Black Spire carrying hidden external compulsion. If it was unlawful, Greel couldn't claim protection. Every path had teeth.

Hain tried again. "But surely House students can't be expected to disclose every heritage imprint."

Cole looked at him. "A command oath tied to assassination intent isn't a heritage imprint."

"Intent is contested."

"Intent was spoken."

"Under interference."

"Because of interference with an undisclosed command oath."

The room went quiet.

Roan revised his estimate of Cole upward by one point.

Maera opened her folio at last. "Headmaster, no party benefits from trading accusations. House Greel proposes a narrow inquiry precisely to avoid politicizing the issue."

Iris, seated behind Roan as documentation aide, made a tiny notation.

Roan didn't need to see it to know what it said.

*They're losing direct law and shifting to stability appeal.*

Maera looked toward Roan for the first time.

Not at his face. At the splint. The bruises. The line of his posture in the chair.

"Mr. Vance appears injured. House Greel is prepared to guarantee medical standards during transfer."

Roan didn't answer.

Cole did. "Mr. Vance was injured by the heir you now describe as victim."

"During an accepted duel."

"Accepted after credible evidence of prior assault against his associate."

"Unproven."

"Under investigation."

"Then all the more reason for inquiry."

"Here."

Maera smiled faintly. "House Greel can't trust Black Spire to investigate itself."

Cole's expression didn't move. "House Greel sent an assassin into my school."

The word landed flat.

No drama. No raised voice.

That made it worse.

The northern envoys stiffened. Hain looked down. The silent security man shifted his weight by half an inch.

Roan noted the shift.

Maera's eyes chilled. "That's an inflammatory characterization."

"It's a preserved arena statement by the heir in question."

"Under interference."

"Then produce the lawful disclosure of the oath he carried."

Maera said nothing.

Second cut: opened.

Cole turned to the recorder. "Let the record show House Greel has declined to provide disclosure documentation when requested."

"We decline to provide it in this forum," Maera said quickly.

"Noted."

The damage remained.

Hain cleared his throat. "Perhaps a sealed review—"

"No," Cole said.

One syllable.

The room's legal rhythm broke.

Maera went still.

Cole stood.

"Black Spire refuses temporary transfer of Roan Vance to Northern Compact custody, inquiry site, neutral holding, medical review, or any equivalent construction. He remains an enrolled student under institutional protection. Black Spire will conduct its own investigation into Kael Greel's transfer, undisclosed oath structure, and confessed assassination intent. House Greel may submit evidence through recognized channels. House Greel may not remove a student from my grounds because its illegal instrument spoke on camera."

There was the refusal.

Clean. Public. Heavy.

Roan felt the shape of it settle into record.

Resource gained: institutional refusal.

Enemy pressure increased: substantial.

Maera's face didn't change, which meant she was very good or very angry.

"Then House Greel will consider Black Spire non-cooperative."

Cole nodded. "You are entitled to consider many things."

The compact clerk looked offended on behalf of paperwork.

Maera closed her folio. "Lord Vorath Greel will attend Ironholm personally to resolve this matter."

Cole didn't blink. "He announced as much."

"He requests a private meeting with Roan Vance."

Drogan's hand came down on the back of Roan's chair.

Selene inhaled through her nose.

Iris's pen stopped.

Roan looked at Maera.

There it was. The next door.

Cole said, "Denied."

Maera's faint smile returned. "The request was addressed to Mr. Vance."

Every gaze shifted.

Roan had agreed not to speak unless addressed.

He had now been addressed indirectly by formal counsel in a preserved hearing.

Cole knew it. His expression turned faintly lethal, but he didn't interrupt.

Roan sat forward carefully. Pain warned him not to. He ignored it.

"A private meeting," he said, "would be inefficient."

Maera studied him. "Is that a refusal?"

"No."

Drogan's fingers tightened on the chair.

Roan continued, "It's a condition."

Cole's eyes narrowed.

"What condition?" Maera asked.

"Public location. Neutral city jurisdiction. No House guards within ten meters. One beverage each. Full exterior witness perimeter. No audio recording inside the table field unless both parties consent."

The room stared.

Iris whispered, "Beverage?"

Roan ignored her.

Maera's composure cracked for the first time, not much, but enough. "You propose to meet Lord Greel for coffee?"

"Tea would imply trust."

Ines made a sound behind him that might've been pain from not laughing.

Maera recovered. "Lord Greel isn't a student adversary to be baited."

"No," Roan said. "He's the man Kael named."

Silence.

Cole didn't approve. Drogan certainly didn't approve. Selene looked like she was deciding whether murder would violate too many procedures. But no one stopped him.

Because they understood the same arithmetic.

Vorath wanted classification. Roan needed witnesses. A private meeting would be murder. A public challenge would be refused. A neutral coffee table was absurd enough to be difficult to attack without looking afraid.

Maera closed her folio fully.

"I will convey your condition."

"Good."

The hearing ended three minutes later.

As the northern delegation stood, the silent security man finally made his only mistake. His eyes moved not to Cole, not to Roan's splinted hand, not to Drogan, but to the repaired pendant beneath Roan's shirt as if he could see through fabric by habit rather than technique. The look lasted less than a breath.

Roan saw it.

Drogan saw Roan see it.

Selene saw both, because House training was an expensive disease.

The man turned away with the delegation. No challenge. No flare of Vitalis. No obvious signal. But when he passed within five meters, the Codex Shard pulsed once.

[External classifier contact: partial.]

[Recognition attempt deflected.]

[Anchor integrity strain: minor.]

Minor was better than major. Minor wasn't nothing.

Roan kept his gaze on Maera Voss until the door closed. Then he made a new note under enemy ledger.

Greel observer can sense anchor shadow. Assume Vorath receives report.

That made the coffee meeting more dangerous. It also made it more necessary. It meant Vorath would arrive with better questions, not simply more pressure, and better questions were often more dangerous than blades because they told the enemy where not to waste time. Roan disliked efficient enemies. They required cleaner traps, fewer assumptions, and better clocks, and colder patience, preferably borrowed.

- break -

Cole waited until the delegation left before speaking.

"Are you insane?"

The question was ordinary enough. The tone wasn't.

Roan remained in the chair while Drogan wheeled him back into the preparation room with unnecessary force.

"No," Roan said.

Drogan locked the chair brake harder than necessary. "You invited Vorath Greel to coffee."

"He invited me to custody."

"That isn't an improvement."

"It's a jurisdictional correction."

Cole entered behind them. "It's a suicidal escalation."

"Not if he accepts the terms."

"He won't honor them."

"No. But breaking them in public costs him."

Selene shut the door with deliberate care. "That assumes you survive the cost."

"Yes."

"I dislike when you agree with the worst part."

Iris placed her notes on the table. "For what it's worth, the condition was tactically elegant."

"It's worth very little," Drogan said.

"It forces Vorath to either refuse a wounded Forged student's public meeting or accept constraints that make immediate abduction harder."

"Harder isn't impossible."

"No one said impossible."

Ines leaned against the wall. "Also, coffee shops have exits."

Drogan turned on her. "Don't help."

"I am helping. Badly, according to tone, but still."

Cole looked at Roan. "You don't have the right to gamble institutional stability on your revenge."

That was an interesting sentence because it sounded moral and was legal.

"I didn't," Roan said. "Greel did. I am choosing table placement."

Cole's mouth thinned.

Roan continued, "If you forbid the meeting, he pressures you. If I refuse, he claims fear and pushes inquiry again. If I accept under controlled conditions, we define the next battlefield. He wants to classify me. I want him in witness range. Both sides get bait. Better than letting him choose all variables."

Drogan said, "The battlefield is a coffee shop."

"Yes."

"That sentence shouldn't exist."

"It does now."

Cole looked toward the sealed hearing room. "You will make no further statements without approval."

Roan considered.

"No."

The room chilled.

Selene said quietly, "Roan."

He looked at her, then at Cole. "Greel's demand is on record. Your refusal is on record. Vorath's personal attendance is on record. If we stop here, the narrative becomes institutional dispute. Black Spire versus House Greel. Student used as symbol. That helps Greel. It moves attention away from Aldric."

Speaking before the meeting forces Vorath to react to a public frame. Speaking after lets him control the opening position. The sequence is the leverage. His information advantage erodes by public record faster than by private threat.

Cole's eyes sharpened.

Drogan went still.

Roan said, "Tomorrow, I speak about my father."

No one answered at first.

The decision had been sitting under everything since the infirmary draft. Six men killed my father. I have their names. Simple. Dangerous. Not complete. Enough.

Selene's voice was careful. "Once you say that publicly, you can't unsay it."

"I know."

"You will reopen every sealed file, every rumor, every old enemy."

"Yes."

Iris said, "And every person who helped bury the case."

"Good."

Drogan looked at him for a long time.

Under the anger, there was fear. Not of Vorath alone. Of old doors opening from the wrong side.

"Your father spent years keeping you out of this," he said.

"He failed."

Drogan flinched as if struck.

Roan regretted the efficiency of the sentence almost immediately. It was accurate enough today. It was also poorly timed. Accuracy didn't excuse waste.

He added, quieter, "They brought it to me."

Drogan's jaw worked once.

Cole said, "A public statement must be controlled."

"Yes."

"Written."

"Yes."

"Reviewed."

"No."

"Vance."

"If you review it, they can claim institutional framing. It has to be mine."

Iris tapped the paper stack. "He's right."

Cole gave her a look.

She lifted both hands. "I dislike it too. But he's right."

Ines said, "We can help set distribution without touching text."

Selene nodded slowly. "Witness channels. Student press. External martial boards. Archived copies. No edits."

Drogan looked between them all. "You people are turning a revenge statement into a media operation."

Roan said, "No. Greel turned it into one. We are correcting distribution."

Cole closed his eyes for one second.

When he opened them, the decision had been made.

"Twenty-four hours," he said. "No earlier. You rest, repair what can be repaired, and write. Tomorrow afternoon, under controlled press conditions, you may make one statement."

Roan nodded.

The Codex Shard pulsed once.

[Witness record preparing.]

[Lineage exposure risk: high.]

[Proceed?]

Roan didn't answer the prompt.

Not yet.

But he already knew.
