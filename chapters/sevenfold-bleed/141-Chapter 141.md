Chapter 141: Sidewound in the Dark

Three weeks after the Greel estate's blood price settled, the dry canal didn't remain dry.

That was Roan's first objection to the morning route. The cracked black bed gathered mist low and wet until a ribbon of gray water formed, foul enough that no one tested its depth. By the ridge crossing, the ribbon had become a stream.

It flowed uphill.

Brendt stared at it for several seconds. "I am not qualified for this terrain."

"Water is rarely impressed by qualifications," Ines said.

"It's going uphill."

"Then it has ambition."

Roan crouched near the edge without touching it. The water didn't reflect the sky. It reflected walls that weren't above it.

[Hollow residue concentration: moderate.]
[No ingestion. No contact.]

Drogan pointed to a narrow maintenance bridge ahead. "We cross there."

The bridge was a strip of stone two feet wide, slick with moisture, spanning a canal that had decided to disobey gravity.

They crossed in order: Drogan, Ines, Brendt, Roan. Brendt's limp made the crossing slow. Over the wrong-water, anything that took more than five seconds became a scheduling issue.

Halfway across, the water spoke with Brendt's voice.

"I can't hold it."

Brendt froze.

Roan said, "Keep walking."

The water spoke again, this time with Ines's voice. "Roan, behind you."

Roan didn't look. He stepped forward instead, closing the distance.

"Brendt. Two steps. Left foot first."

"The water used my voice."

"Poorly. You complain with more structure."

"That shouldn't help. It helped."

"Move."

The water laughed with Drogan's voice. Drogan didn't react. Discipline, or long practice.

They reached the far side without falling. Ines marked the bridge as voice-contaminated on her route slate.

They continued along the ridge. The first hundred meters were worse for Brendt than he admitted and better than Roan expected. Brendt's stride shortened every seventh step. Roan moved one ration pack from Brendt to Ines without comment. Brendt noticed anyway.

"I can carry it."

"Yes."

"Then why take it?"

"Because can isn't should."

"That sounds like something an instructor would say."

Drogan, ahead, said, "No. Instructors say it after someone breaks. He said it before."

Ines accepted the extra pack with theatrical suffering. "This is damaging my profit margin."

"Charge it to medical support," Roan said.

"At my premium rate?"

"At your honest rate."

She considered. "Rude."

"Accurate."

The ridge path narrowed beside administrative buildings whose doors had been sealed from the outside with black resin. The resin bore fingerprints. Human-sized, mostly. Some smaller.

Roan didn't slow.

A sound followed them from behind the sealed doors.

Scratching.

Brendt's shoulders tightened.

"No," Roan said before anyone asked.

"I didn't."

"You were about to."

"There could be survivors."

"No."

The scratching became softer, almost deliberate.

Roan pointed at the resin. "Fingerprints are under the sealant. Doors were sealed after hands were on them. Years ago. Decades."

Brendt looked at the smallest fingerprints again. His jaw worked once. Then he nodded.

Drogan's pace adjusted slightly, giving Brendt an extra second to recover without making it obvious.

They moved on. The scratching faded like a conscience someone else had purchased.

The city north of the canal was older. The streets narrowed into processional paths lined with black pillars. Doorways stood taller than human proportions required. Several arches bore empty niches where statues had been removed. Removed, not destroyed. A dead city should contain rubble, not edited history.

The Husk pack didn't attack.

That worried everyone except perhaps the pack.

Roan saw signs of it three times: claw marks on a wall, ash displaced along a roofline, a pale shape withdrawing behind a pillar. No charges. No attempts to herd them.

Waiting again.

Or guiding.

At the first administrative square, they found the marker constructs.

Hundreds of bone-wire bodies lay scattered across the stone like dead insects, crushed, burned, or cut into pieces. Some still twitched. Brass route pins from other expeditions lay among them, chewed open and emptied. Not all pins were old.

Ines crouched beside one with a visible guild stamp. "This team came through within the last month."

Three packs lay against the far wall, neatly arranged. No bodies.

Neatness in the Hollowed City had become a threat signature.

Roan examined the constructs. He took one of the dead ones with tweezers and placed it into a sample tube.

Ines stared at him. "That sample is mine."

"You can invoice my manners again."

"That was one time."

"Efficient precedent."

Drogan moved to the edge of the square. "We don't stop."

They crossed. The square remained silent until Brendt reached the center.

Then all the dead constructs turned their heads toward him.

Hundreds of tiny bone faces rotated on wire necks.

Brendt stopped.

"No," Roan said.

Brendt resumed walking immediately.

Good.

The constructs didn't rise. They watched. When they left the square, the construct heads turned back to their original positions.

At the eastern edge, Ines found a route marker that hadn't been eaten.

Black ceramic instead of brass, too expensive for student teams and too subtle for licensed salvagers. A single silver thread coiled around its head.

Ines stopped breathing loudly.

Roan looked at her. "Recognize it."

"House the western signatory archival marker," she said. "Private issue."

Drogan turned so sharply his coat snapped.

Ines's voice stayed low. "They administer the archival courts below the Greel throne. Strictly neutral, strictly ambitious. They write history; they don't buy maps." Her jaw tightened. "I trained with one of their auditors. That's how I know their markers."

"Recent?" Roan asked.

She used the lens without touching. "Hard to tell. Ceramic doesn't weather normally here. But the silver thread is intact. Month at most."

Brendt looked from the marker to the surrounding dead constructs. "So the western signatory people came here recently."

"Or someone with their tools," Ines said.

Roan didn't like that answer because it was correct. They had been remote observers thirty years ago. Now their marker sat on the path toward Aldric's tower. Coincidence was possible in the same way falling through a roof and landing in a chair was possible.

He took a visual record, then left the marker in place.

Ines looked pained. "We aren't taking it?"

"No."

"It may tell its owner when removed."

"I said worth, not safe."

"Then admire it from a distance."

Drogan stared at the marker. "The western signatory shouldn't know this route."

"They knew the tower thirty years ago."

"Knowing and returning are different mistakes."

"People repeat profitable mistakes."

They left the marker untouched.

"I am adding that to the list of things I don't want explained," Brendt said.

"Coward," Ines said.

"Alive coward."

"Temporarily," Roan said.

"Roan."

"Everyone is temporarily alive."

"That's the least helpful true thing you have ever said."

The path climbed after the square. They found one corpse before the temple. Only one. That made it worse.

It lay in a side alcove, seated upright with its back against the wall and its hands folded neatly over its stomach. The armor was modern and high-grade, split open at the chest without blood around the wound. A House the western signatory archival charm hung from the throat, cracked through the center.

Ines stopped six feet away.

"I know him," she said. Her voice was flat. "Corvin. Archivist. I trained with his division. Competent. Expensive."

"When did you last see him?"

"Two months ago. Trying to buy a map I wanted."

Roan looked at the body, then the wall above the helmet. No writing. No visible trap.

"He was arranged for discovery."

"I know."

Her field deployment had been her own demand after the archive theft. My records, my liability. Now here she was, identifying the fallout.

Roan removed an empty sample tin from his pack and slid it across the stone to the charm. No reaction. He used Sidewound's sheath to flip the charm into the tin and sealed it.

Ines stared. "You said we could leave it."

"Could. Not should."

"That was almost kind."

"Controlled risk."

"Of course."

She took the tin when he handed it to her, and for once didn't negotiate custody terms.

The corpse's helmet turned after they walked away. Not far. Just enough to face the temple. Ines heard it. She stopped for half a step. Then she kept moving.

No one went back.

The path didn't rise steeply, but steadily, winding between halls whose facades had collapsed inward. On several walls, Roan saw fragments of the seven-line circle beneath scraped stone. Each time, the Codex Shard pulsed faintly. Recognition risk climbed without displaying a number.

His spine had begun to ache around the seventh vertebra. Not pain, exactly. Pressure. The Shard responding to something ahead. Sidewound also changed, its grip warming whenever they passed intact markings. The weapon and the Shard were having a conversation through him.

Drogan noticed. "Problems?"

"Several."

"Useful ones?"

"Potentially."

"Try not to die before converting them."

"That's the plan."

Ines snorted.

They reached the fountain at what would've been noon if the Rift respected clocks.

Roan knew it before Drogan said anything. The plaza opened suddenly between four black avenues. At its center stood a dry fountain shaped like a shallow bowl. Decapitated statues formed a broken ring, heads placed facing the fountain, each expression worn smooth by ash and time.

On the north side of the fountain, the stone was darker. The air there was older, heavier, as if the fountain remembered what the ash had failed to bury.

Drogan stopped at the edge of the plaza.

His eyes went to that dark patch.

Lysa died at the fountain.

Roan didn't say it. He marked the location, the reaction, and the way the Husk pack didn't enter the plaza. Predators avoided sacred ground, contaminated ground, or bait. All three.

"We cross straight," Drogan said. His voice had lost nothing.

They crossed.

Halfway past the fountain, the dark stone wet itself. Red seeped up through the cracks in a thin line, sketching a path toward Drogan's boots.

It stopped three inches from his boot and formed letters.

[Language fragment detected.]
[Hollowkin script: funerary challenge.]
[Approximate meaning: Debt stands where blood fell.]

Roan read it aloud.

Drogan closed his eyes for one second. Then he stepped around the letters and kept walking.

The blood-script dried behind him.

No one spoke until they reached the northern avenue.

Brendt finally said, "I have decided not to ask."

"Good," Drogan said.

"For now," Roan added.

Drogan gave him a look.

"Interest," Roan said.

"I remember."

Past the fountain, the city began using repetition as a weapon.

The same doorway appeared three times on three different buildings: tall, black, sealed by a bar of white stone. The first time, Roan marked it as notable. The second time, he considered whether space had looped. The third, he saw the differences. The left hinge scratch was longer. The ash drift sloped opposite. The white bar carried one additional notch.

Not loop. Sequence.

"These doors are counting something," he said.

Ines adjusted her lens. "Counting us?"

"Or counting how many thresholds we refuse."

Brendt limped around a broken pillar. "I would like credit for refusing several thresholds."

"You can redeem it if we survive."

"For what?"

"A boot."

"Too soon."

Drogan halted at the third door and studied the extra notch. "Black Squad saw these."

Roan looked at him. "Before the tower?"

"After the fountain. Mercer wanted to open one. Aldric told him if he touched it, he'd break his fingers."

"Did Mercer obey?"

"Yes."

"Then he had sense."

"He had fear."

"Useful substitute."

A whisper came from behind the door. The sound of paper being turned.

Ines went still.

Roan looked at her. "Archive bait."

"I know."

"You moved half a step."

"I said I know."

Roan stepped between her and the door.

She looked at him. "Do you think I'm that easy?"

"No. I think the door does."

That landed better. Her mouth tightened, and she looked away from the seam.

"Correct," she said.

The page-turning stopped. Something behind the door tapped once.

The fourth matching door never appeared. That was also a message.

They continued through a corridor of leaning pillars into a district where the ash gave way to black grit. At one point the Husk pack appeared across a parallel street. Twelve of them stood in a line, eyeless heads turned toward the group. None attacked. One raised a severed human hand and placed it palm-first against its own chest.

Brendt whispered, "What is it doing?"

[Gesture fragment detected.]
[Approximate meaning: unverified passage / false blood.]

"Calling us illegitimate," Roan said.

"Can monsters insult lineage?" Brendt asked.

"Apparently."

Ines watched the Husk lower the hand. "That wasn't random."

"No."

Drogan's face had gone bleak. "Hollowkin used blood challenges. Husks shouldn't copy that."

"Then either these aren't ordinary Husks," Roan said, "or something taught them theater."

"Both are bad," Brendt said.

"Yes."

The Husk line withdrew into mist. They didn't pursue.

Not the tower.

That was the first discrepancy.

Drogan stopped at the top of the rise, staring at the structure ahead with a face Roan had never seen on him before.

Confusion.

The old overlay had marked C-17 as a tower. It may have been one once. It wasn't one now.

A temple stood where the tower should have collapsed inward. It rose from the inner district like a black tooth, low and broad at the base, with seven slanted ribs of stone curving up around a central dome. No ash lay on its steps. No mist crossed its threshold. The walls were clean in the way Eckhart had meant: maintained without hands, preserved without care.

At the top of the steps, a stone door stood sealed. Across the door ran seven narrow lines meeting in a central wound-shaped mark.

The Codex Shard pulsed so hard Roan's vision flickered.

[Lineage marker confirmed.]
[Proximity: immediate.]
[Recommendation: approach.]
[Secondary recommendation: do not approach unprepared.]

Roan almost smiled. Even the Shard had begun hedging.

Drogan looked at the temple as if someone had rearranged a grave while he slept. "This wasn't here."

Ines lowered her route slate. "Buildings don't usually replace themselves."

"Here, they might," Brendt said.

"No," Roan said.

They all looked at him.

He kept his eyes on the seven lines. "The Codex Shard didn't react to a new building. It reacted to a surface placed over the truth. The tower was the projection the surveyors used to justify a blank spot on the map. Someone buried the temple under a lie."

Drogan's jaw tightened. "Aldric knew."

"Aldric buried it."

The old man didn't argue.

The temple door answered. A low sound like stone remembering a lock.
