Chapter 181: Reputation Kill

Kael Greel tried not to answer.

That mattered.

Not morally. Morality had very little to do with the next few seconds, despite what several thousand witnesses would later pretend. What mattered was that the resistance was visible. His jaw locked. His throat moved. The muscles around his exposed oath mark stood out under pale skin as if the command itself had become a wire pulled tight.

The arena saw the struggle.

The cameras saw it better.

Roan kept his broken fingers on Kael's wrist and did nothing else.

That was the whole point.

No blade. No grip strong enough to bruise. No hidden strike. No dramatic threat. If Kael spoke under a visible knife, Greel would claim coercion. If Kael screamed under pain, Greel would claim illegal mental assault. If Roan struck him again, Hale might intervene and turn the record muddy.

So Roan crouched in front of him, bleeding from the nose, chest aching around a cracked pendant, and waited for Kael's own oath to betray the House that had placed it in him.

Kael's eyes found the northern glass.

There.

A mistake.

Small and human, plus far more useful than another wound.

Roan didn't follow his gaze. He didn't need to. The warmer reflection behind the faculty box had brightened enough that the Vance Pendant, damaged as it was, felt like a coin of ice against his sternum. Vorath Greel was watching through glass and frost, illegal as a knife in a courtroom.

Kael knew it.

The oath knew it.

The crowd didn't, but they knew something was wrong.

Roan repeated the question.

"Why did you come to Black Spire?"

Kael's breath hitched.

"No," he said.

Not an answer. A refusal.

The oath mark flashed gold-red.

The Codex Shard pulsed.

[Foreign command structure destabilizing.]

[Do not increase pressure.]

That was new. The Shard giving tactical advice instead of offering to eat the problem. Roan filed the behavioral shift without dwelling; the change would need attention later, but not now.

Roan obeyed.

He removed his hand.

Kael blinked.

So did half the arena. Removing contact looked like mercy. It wasn't. Contact had opened the seam. Distance denied Greel the argument that Roan physically forced the answer. More importantly, it made the oath discharge against its own instability without an external anchor.

A trap didn't become kinder because one stepped away from the jaws.

Kael tried to stand again.

His left knee shook. Frost cracked under it. He got one foot beneath him, then another. The crowd began to make noise, uncertain, rising, hungry.

Hale stepped forward. "Combatant Greel, can you continue?"

Kael looked at her.

For one terrible second, Roan thought he might say yes and make the whole thing uglier.

Then Kael's gaze shifted again to the northern glass.

The reflection there moved.

Roan felt it without looking: pressure, cold and old, like a mountain deciding to lean.

Kael's oath flared.

His mouth opened.

"I came here," Kael said, each word dragged through gritted teeth, "to kill Roan Vance on Vorath Greel's order."

Silence hit the arena so hard the ward hum became audible.

Not metaphorical silence. Actual. Ten thousand people, several hundred cameras, dozens of officials, and one compromised House heir all froze around a sentence that couldn't be unsaid.

Kael looked as if someone had cut the tendons holding his world upright.

Then sound returned.

Not all at once. First a gasp from the lower student tiers. Then overlapping voices. Then the media partition erupting as reporters shouted into live feeds they weren't supposed to have. Then the forum boards crashing somewhere beyond the arena with all the dignity of wet paper.

Hale's face didn't change. That was impressive.

"Repeat," she said.

Kael stared at her.

The oath mark pulsed again, weaker.

"I was ordered," he said, voice hoarse, "by Vorath Greel to enter Black Spire under student transfer authority, cultivate proximity to Roan Vance, and kill him under plausible institutional cover."

The second silence was worse.

The first had been shock.

The second was arithmetic.

Everyone in the arena began calculating consequences.

Spire neutrality violation. Attempted assassination. House infiltration. Abuse of student transfer authority. Bloodline command oath on a student combatant. Possible illegal external observation. A Founding Seven patriarch named in public record.

Names had weight. Vorath Greel's name had enough to crush rooms. Kael had just dropped it in the middle of Black Spire's legal machinery and let the cameras watch the floor crack.

The Codex Shard registered the event in cooler grammar than the arena could manage.

[Foreign command structure: collapsed.]

[Public testimony recorded — nineteen feeds, three external.]

[Target ledger updated: Vorath Greel — named, confirmed.]

[Recognition risk: rising.]

[Inheritance refusal still binding.]

Roan logged the recognition warning and ignored the rest. The refusal still held—he hadn't touched what his father had hidden deeper. That was for later.

Then he stood.

Slowly.

His left hand didn't want to cooperate. His ribs objected. His nose was crooked. The cracked pendant sent a faint splintering sensation into every breath.

Fine.

A good victory should have an invoice. Free victories were usually bait.

Kael swayed.

For one moment, Roan saw the shape of the younger man beneath the House. But young, trained into a blade, sent into a neutral academy with an oath under his skin and an old monster watching through glass, not innocent or excused.

Roan didn't pity him.

Pity would be wasteful.

He did, however, adjust the final cut.

Hale turned toward Roan. "Combatant Vance. Your opponent remains conscious but impaired. Do you claim victory by incapacity?"

Roan looked at Kael.

Kael looked back with hatred and humiliation, then the hollow awareness of someone who had just realized surviving might be worse than dying.

Dead heirs became tragedies.

Living witnesses became evidence.

Roan turned to Hale. "No."

The arena noise faltered.

Hale's eyebrow moved by half a millimeter. "Clarify."

"He can stand."

"Combat may continue."

"Yes."

Drogan moved in the faculty box. Cole's hand tightened on the rail. Selene went very still. Iris, behind the partition, smiled like a banker watching a debt mature.

Kael's eyes narrowed.

"You want me to yield," he said.

Roan faced him again. "No."

"Then what?"

Roan drew Sidewound.

The arena inhaled.

He didn't raise it.

"Pick up your sword."

Kael stared.

Roan waited.

This was the dangerous part, and not because Kael could still kill him. He couldn't, barring a suicide burst and catastrophic ward failure. The dangerous part was perception. Too much pressure and Roan became cruel. Too little and Kael's confession became the entire victory, leaving Greel room to frame him as a broken youth coerced by strange Vance methods.

Roan needed one more exchange.

Clean. Short. Undeniable.

A duel ending, not an interrogation ending.

Kael bent slowly and picked up his pale sword.

His hand shook.

The crowd watched every inch.

Hale didn't intervene.

Good.

Kael raised the blade.

No domain formed. He didn't have the channels left. Frost gathered weakly around the edge, more habit than threat. His oath mark continued to pulse under torn glove, but the command had spent its first discharge. The next might damage him. Roan wouldn't push it.

"Ready?" Roan asked.

Kael laughed brokenly. "Do you care?"

"No."

Honest enough.

Kael attacked.

Three steps. That was all he had.

First step: the pale blade came high, too slow by his earlier standard.

Roan moved inside.

Second step: Kael tried to pivot, knee nearly failing.

Roan struck the side of the sword with Sidewound's spine, not edge. The pale blade slid out of line.

Third step: Kael raised his free hand, perhaps to grip, perhaps to form frost, perhaps because training hadn't yet accepted defeat.

Roan placed Sidewound's flat against his throat.

No cut.

No blood.

A cold black line resting where death would've been.

Kael froze.

The death-stop wards flared around them, not triggered but ready.

Roan held the position for one second, long enough for every camera to capture it, then lowered the knife.

"Yield," he said.

Kael's face twisted.

For a moment Roan thought pride might destroy the useful outcome. Then Kael looked again toward the northern glass.

Whatever he saw there didn't save him.

"I yield," Kael said.

Hale's hand came down.

"Duel concluded. Victory to Roan Vance by submission."

The arena detonated.

- break -

Roan didn't turn toward the crowd.

That was important.

Winners who turned toward applause invited interpretation. Winners who turned toward officials created record. Roan sheathed Sidewound, stepped back from Kael, and looked at Hale.

"I request preservation of full combat logs, audio, thermal recoil data, oath signature imaging, ward intervention telemetry, and all external interference readings from one hour before duel start through conclusion."

Hale stared at him for one second.

Then, to her credit, she smiled very faintly.

"Filed."

Kael's face went even paler.

Good. He understood.

The confession mattered. The telemetry would matter more. Words could be attacked. Data had to be buried, which required shovels and time, plus people willing to be seen digging.

Roan continued, "I request medical custody for Combatant Greel under Spire neutrality protection."

That caused a different kind of noise.

Kael looked at him sharply. "What?"

Roan didn't look at him. "His oath mark may constitute illegal coercive modification. He's evidence and a potential victim of House compulsion."

A lie by emphasis. Kael was perpetrator first, victim second. But putting him under Spire custody prevented Greel extraction, prevented convenient suicide, and made Roan look less like a boy who had just ruined a family and more like an aggrieved student preserving procedure.

Public morality loved a procedural hat.

Hale looked toward Cole.

Cole stood.

His voice carried through the arena amplification without strain. "Granted pending review."

The northern glass reflection flared gold-red.

For the first time, Roan looked.

Not directly into the eyes. At the reflection's edge.

Vorath Greel's face wasn't fully there. White beard. Severe mouth. The suggestion of a long weapon behind one shoulder. Gold-red eyes burning in glass that should have reflected only the faculty box.

The pressure hit him like altitude.

Not a full attack. A warning. A promise that the room remained small compared to the old man's reach.

The Codex Shard flared hot.

[Throne-tier attention confirmed.]

[Do not engage.]

Roan agreed.

Engagement came later.

He lowered his gaze and turned to Kael.

The young Greel stood between two medical arbiters now, shaking with cold recoil and something worse. His eyes had followed Roan's glance to the glass. He had seen enough to understand that his grandfather had watched the confession and done nothing.

That would do more damage than any cut.

Roan stopped two paces from him.

Kael spat blood onto the floor. "You should have killed me."

"No."

"Coward."

"Evidence."

Kael flinched.

Good. Precise.

Roan turned away.

The crowd noise surged again as reporters shouted questions from behind barriers. "Roan! Was this revenge for Brendt Holloway?" "Did you know about Vorath's involvement before the duel?" "Are you accusing House Greel of violating Spire neutrality?" "What did you do to his oath mark?" "Was that thirty thousand reading real?"

Roan walked to the arbitration dais.

Hale had a recorder hovering above her palm.

"Combatant statement?" she asked.

"Yes."

The arena quieted by degrees. Not fully. Enough.

Roan faced the official recorder, not the crowd.

"Kael Greel came here to kill me. He admitted it under his own House oath. He keeps his life."

He paused.

Blood dripped from his nose onto the white arena floor.

"That's the apology I want from his grandfather."

The sentence landed without ornament.

Then Roan walked out.

The medical arbiters reached Kael after Roan stepped away.

That, too, mattered. They reached him, not Greel attendants. Black Spire hands touched him first. Black Spire scanners documented the oath mark before any House physician could cover it. Black Spire sealant went over the torn glove area while three cameras recorded the angle. Hale had ordered it without looking at Roan, which confirmed she understood the same thing he did.

Custody began with the first bandage.

Kael tried to refuse assistance until his knees nearly failed. Then pride discovered gravity had better arguments. He allowed one medic to support his arm and glared at the floor while another scanned the gold-red mark. The scan display flashed restricted characters, failed to classify the structure, and copied the error to the official record.

Roan watched long enough to confirm the copy.

Only then did he turn away.

A reporter shouted from behind the barrier, "Did you plan the confession?"

Roan kept walking.

Another voice: "Was your recovery method illegal?"

Still walking.

A third, sharper: "Are you declaring war on House Greel?"

That one made him pause.

Not because he intended to answer. Because the phrasing would spread if left untouched. Declaring war was a grand phrase. Grand phrases made stupid people feel historic and dangerous people feel invited.

Roan looked toward the reporter without turning his body fully.

"No."

The crowd quieted in a small circle around the word.

He added, "I reported a crime."

Then he left.

It was a better sentence. Smaller. Harder to prosecute. Easier for cautious allies to repeat.

He didn't limp until he reached the tunnel.

The tunnel didn't fully protect him from the sound.

It followed through stone and warded glass, softened but not diminished. The arena had become a living market of reactions. Students shouting. Reporters reciting the confession into external feeds. House aides trying to contact superiors before superiors contacted them first. Medical staff calling for additional containment around Kael. Security teams arguing over who had authority to stand near a Greel heir who was now both accused assassin and protected witness.

Roan walked through it at a steady pace until the first corner hid him from public view.

Then his right knee tried to fold.

He caught the wall with his good hand and waited for the body to finish making its argument. Dizziness. Blood loss. Frost recoil. exposure risk pressure behind the eyes. Cracked pendant adding a sharp note to every breath. None of it surprising. None of it negotiable.

Behind him, two arena staff turned the corner, saw him, and stopped too quickly.

"Do you need medical assistance?" one asked.

"Yes," Roan said.

They both moved.

"In four minutes."

They stopped.

"That isn't how medical assistance works," the other said.

"It's today."

He pushed off the wall before they could decide whether procedure outranked fear of Drogan. The answer was no, but people sometimes made brave choices when paperwork was watching.

At the next junction, Cyrus Ashford stood half in shadow.

Of course.

His face was unreadable in the way faces became when the person behind them had too many reactions and no approved order for displaying them. He had watched Kael train. He had accepted correction from him. He had nearly become testimony or handle or collateral. Now he had heard Kael admit the entire proximity had been part of an assassination plan.

It was a lot to put on a person already built out of cracked pride.

Roan considered walking past.

Then Cyrus spoke.

"You knew."

"Yes."

"How much?"

"Enough."

Cyrus's hands curled at his sides. "You let me stand near him."

Roan looked at him. "You chose to."

That landed badly.

Good answers often did.

Cyrus took one step forward. "He could have used me."

"He did."

The words made Cyrus flinch harder than a punch would have. Roan didn't soften them. Softening a broken truth made people reach for the rounded edges and cut themselves later.

Cyrus swallowed. "Why warn me yesterday?"

"Because after Brendt, I had no patience left for collateral damage."

Cyrus looked away.

For a second he seemed younger than his House name, younger than his rivalry, younger than the forum mythologies that had made both of them into symbols before either had consented to being used as punctuation.

Then the moment passed.

"What happens to him?" Cyrus asked.

"Kael?"

"Yes."

"If Spire custody holds, he becomes evidence. If Greel extracts him, he becomes liability. If Vorath is practical, he becomes a lesson."

Cyrus's face tightened. "You say that like you're discussing equipment."

"No. Equipment is easier to repair."

Cyrus had no answer to that.

Roan resumed walking.

Behind him, Cyrus said, "Vance."

Roan stopped.

"I still hate you."

"Reasonable."

"But I read wording now."

Roan glanced back.

Cyrus didn't smile. Neither did Roan.

"Progress," Roan said.

He left him there.

- break -

Drogan caught him before the second bend.

Not literally. Roan would have objected. The old man appeared from a side passage, took one look at his face, another at the way his right shoulder sat, and opened a medical sealant pack with the expression of someone resisting profanity for institutional reasons.

"Sit," Drogan said.

"No."

Drogan pointed at the bench.

Roan evaluated the order.

The tunnel tilted slightly.

"Fine."

He sat.

Drogan pressed sealant against the shoulder cut hard enough to be rude.

Roan hissed through his teeth.

"Good," Drogan said. "Still alive."

"Diagnostic by irritation."

"Reliable with you."

Cole arrived thirty seconds later with Hale, two medical staff, and a security detail pretending not to stare at Roan's blood. Selene came behind them, carrying a data slate in one hand and her cracked witness tablet in the other. Iris wasn't present, which meant she was already stealing something useful.

Cole looked at Roan.

Roan looked back.

The headmaster sighed. "You turned my arena into an international tribunal."

"Temporary."

"That isn't better."

"It was already compromised."

"Yes," Cole said. "By you."

"By Greel first."

Hale cleared her throat. "The record supports that sequence."

Roan liked her even more.

Cole looked pained. "Hale."

"I am neutral, Headmaster. Not soothing."

Drogan snorted.

Selene stepped forward and handed Cole the cracked tablet. "Witness copy. Redundant feed stored offsite. Three offsite locations."

Cole closed his eyes briefly. "Of course."

Roan said, "Four."

Selene looked at him.

"Ines," Roan said.

"Right."

Cole opened his eyes. "I am surrounded by criminals."

"Students," Hale said.

"Somehow worse."

One of the medical staff tried to guide Roan's broken hand toward a brace. Roan let her because the fingers had begun to register pain in colors. She examined them and muttered something unprofessional.

Drogan leaned close. "Pendant?"

"Cracked."

Drogan's jaw tightened.

"Shard?"

"Loud."

"Quota?"

"Unclear."

"That means bad."

"Yes."

Roan made a mental note: the Shard's analytic bandwidth had boundaries, but those didn't constrain the Inversion itself. He would verify later.

Cole heard enough to decide not to ask. Wise man.

From beyond the tunnel, the arena noise changed again, lower and more chaotic. News spreading. Officials moving. House representatives calling people with titles. Somewhere, Kael was being placed under protective custody by people who would need protection themselves within the hour.

Roan stood.

The medical staff objected.

He ignored them.

Cole said, "You are going to the infirmary."

"Yes."

"Now."

"After Brendt."

Drogan's face changed by one degree.

Selene didn't speak.

Cole looked at him, then waved off the objection forming on the healer's face. "Five minutes."

Roan nodded.

Five minutes was enough.

Brendt wouldn't wake. That wasn't the point. The point was placement. Roan had told Brendt's mother the responsible party wouldn't get away with it. He disliked incomplete reporting.

On the way to the medical annex, his slate vibrated so many times he turned it off.

He didn't need to read the forum to know what it said.

This was how you ruined a family without a single permanent injury.

People would phrase it differently. Worse. Forums were where language went to develop infections.

But they would understand the shape.

Not mercy.

Accounting.

The shape arrived in chips before he turned the slate off.

*@BlackSpireStream*: KAEL JUST SAID THE NAME ON A LIVE WARD FEED I AM SCREAMING

*@ArenaTechWatch*: slow-mo at 0:43, oath mark discharge pattern consistent with bloodline command release, not coercion. Archiving full waveform before mods scrub. Someone mirror this.

*@HouseGreelPR* (verified, six-minute-old post, comments locked): The regrettable statement made under medical distress will be addressed through appropriate legal channels. We urge restraint and full trust in due process.

*@north_signal_news*: BREAKING. Founding Seven patriarch named in academy testimony. Spire neutrality status under emergency review. Three House delegations have requested travel clearance in the last eleven minutes.

*@VanceFanForum* (admin pinned): HE LET HIM PICK THE SWORD BACK UP. HE LET HIM. THIS ISN'T MERCY. STOP CALLING IT MERCY.

*@EckhartForge* (no avatar, twelve followers, posted at 03:14 local): boy did what i sold him the steel to do. Invoice paid.

Three thousand new comments per minute on the duel thread alone. Four cross-Spire trends. One House crest had already been edited off a public statue somewhere in the eastern wards before the night ended.

Roan turned the slate off.

The shape held without him.

When the elevator doors opened onto the medical level, Brendt's mother was waiting in the hall.

Roan stopped.

She wasn't supposed to be there. Visitor restrictions after high-profile student violence were strict, and Brendt's family lacked the status to cut through them. Which meant someone had escorted her. Selene. Or Cole, if guilt had temporarily defeated policy.

Mrs. Holloway was a small woman with gray at her temples and hands that looked as if they had spent a lifetime doing work no one photographed. She saw the blood on Roan's face, the brace forming around his fingers, the torn dueling weave, and didn't ask if he was all right.

Good.

Instead she said, "Is it done?"

Roan considered the accurate answer. No. Kael wasn't the source. Vorath hadn't paid. Brendt hadn't woken. The debt had only entered a public ledger.

But people didn't always ask the question their words carried.

"The first part," Roan said.

She nodded once.

Then she stepped forward and took a folded cloth from her coat pocket. Clean. Cheap cotton. She held it out.

Roan stared at it.

"For your face," she said.

He accepted it because refusing would be more theatrical than taking it. The cloth came away red when he pressed it under his nose.

Mrs. Holloway looked toward Brendt's room. "He always said you were trouble."

"He was correct."

"He also said you were his friend."

Roan said nothing.

His good hand closed around the cloth more tightly than he meant. Aldric had told him once, in a kitchen with bad light, that the test of a friend was whether their mother could look at you afterward.

She didn't ask him to. That was kinder than he deserved and more efficient than he expected.

At Brendt's bedside, Roan stood for exactly four minutes and thirty seconds.

Brendt breathed under the healing lattice, unaware that the first debt had been collected.

Roan placed Kael's torn glove clasp on the bedside table.

Not as a trophy.

Evidence.

Then he went to the infirmary before Drogan could carry him there, because some indignities were worth avoiding.

In the corridor beyond Brendt's room, a muted wall monitor cycled the same five-second clip on loop. Kael's mouth opening. The name leaving. Ten thousand throats catching at once. In the lower frame, a reporter was on her second cigarette. A House crest had paused the playback on a remote panel and was pointing at the northern glass with a finger that didn't look steady.

Two floors up, in a glass-walled office above the medical wing, a Spire administrator who hadn't slept in twenty hours unlocked an emergency directive she had hoped to retire without using.

Three districts away, in a market stall that closed at midnight, Esme stopped counting coins and listened to the city forget its own ambient noise for almost ten seconds.

Somewhere across the northern range, an old man at a stone cairn smiled with one half of his face.

The Codex Shard pulsed once against Roan's sternum.

[Public ledger: locked.]

[Vorath Greel — contract open.]

[Six remaining. Five named, one unwritten.]

[Recommended next action: do not be where he is expected to look.]

Roan adjusted the cracked pendant under his shirt and went to find the infirmary's quietest bed.
