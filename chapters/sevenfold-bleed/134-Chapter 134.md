Chapter 134: What the Crowd Remembers

The problem with a good weapon was that it encouraged stupid confidence.

Roan had seen it happen to House students for months. A new blade, a better armor weave, a custom breathing focus, some ancestral glove with a name longer than its warranty—give a teenager expensive equipment and within twenty-four hours they began standing differently. Shoulders looser. Chin higher. Eyes drifting toward reflective surfaces.

They mistook possession for competence.

The equipment didn't make them stronger. It made their mistakes more expensive.

Roan had no intention of becoming an expensive mistake.

At four in the morning, with the Black Spire training annex mostly empty and the maintenance staff still pretending not to notice him, he drew Sidewound for the fifty-third time and cut at nothing.

The blade passed through the air in silence.

Too much wrist.

He reset.

Draw. Step. Cut. Recover.

Still wrong.

The weapon didn't fight him, exactly. Fighting would've been easier. A resisting weapon could be overpowered, punished, or discarded. Sidewound did something more irritating: it revealed that several movements Roan had considered efficient were crude compromises made by someone used to compensating with Vitalis.

Not bad. Just not clean.

He drew again.

The training room around him was a long rectangular chamber beneath the auxiliary gymnasium, rented for three hours under an alias Ines had provided and Brendt had insisted was "legally dubious but emotionally supportive." Concrete walls. Reinforced floor. Four target posts. One pressure dummy. Two sensor arrays. No spectators.

Drogan had offered the main combat hall.

Roan had declined.

The main hall had students, cameras, instructors, and a forum culture that treated every visible movement as public property. After the tournament, someone had posted a slowed-down analysis of his three-strike exchange with Cyrus, complete with arrows, incorrect assumptions, and a twelve-minute section arguing that Roan's left foot placement revealed "hidden romantic frustration."

Roan had watched thirty seconds, identified four technical errors, and purchased a burner account solely to write: *No.*

The post had gained three thousand comments.

People were stupid in groups. On forums, they were stupid with timestamps.

He drew again.

Sidewound's tip angled half a degree off the intended line.

Roan stopped.

Half a degree wasn't much against soft targets. Against armor seams, core membranes, or Hollowkin joint plates, half a degree was the difference between opening a target and politely scratching it. The weapon's value lay in precision. Precision required removing all generous assumptions from his body.

He adjusted his thumb on the grip.

Draw. Step. Cut.

Better.

[Micro-alignment improved: marginal.]

Roan paused.

"That's insulting," he told the empty room.

The Codex didn't respond. Wise.

He continued.

By the end of the first hour, his right forearm ached in six places and his shoulder had developed a sharp complaint near the rotator cuff. Not an injury. A warning. The body's legal notice before litigation.

He switched hands.

The left-hand draw was uglier.

By the tenth repetition, it was merely bad.

By the thirtieth, he had identified the issue: the scabbard angle favored right-side concealment and cross-draw access, but in a grapple he would need left-hand retrieval if the dominant side was pinned. Against Husks, that mattered less. Against human enemies, especially trained ones who had watched the tournament footage, it mattered very much.

People would aim for his right hand now.

Cyrus had taught the world Roan's public style. Eckhart had given him a private correction.

Roan intended to keep it private for at least two weeks.

He drew again.

Pain flickered through the tendons in his left hand as Sidewound's channel tried to align. A heartbeat.

He didn't invert.

The pain stayed.

Good.

The temptation flickered in his ribs, too. He let it pass. Not every discomfort was a weakness. Some were information. The ache in his left hand told him where technique ended and compensation began. He could invert it and feel nothing—and learn nothing. Letting the weapon teach him was faster than letting the Shard cheat for him.

He cut harder.

The blade took a shallow line out of the pressure dummy's shoulder seam. The sensor core flashed yellow, indicating a simulated structural compromise—not deep, but useful.

Roan checked the readout.

Vitalis input: minimal.

Edge contact: narrow.

Damage coefficient: high.

Useful.

He reset.

The door opened exactly at the start of the second hour.

Drogan entered carrying a paper cup of coffee and a worse expression than usual.

Roan didn't stop training. "You're early."

"You rented a room under the name Cornelius Patch."

"Ines rented it."

"That doesn't make it better."

"It made it cheaper."

Drogan looked at the pressure dummy, then at the faint cut across its shoulder. His gaze dropped to Sidewound.

For a moment, the old instructor said nothing.

There were versions of silence. Roan had become fluent in several. Drogan's normal silence was blunt, like a wall refusing to explain itself. His dangerous silence narrowed around specific details. This was neither.

This was recognition trying to decide whether to become grief.

Roan sheathed the blade.

Drogan took a sip of coffee. "Eckhart made it."

"Yes."

"Idiot."

"The weapon or the man?"

"Yes."

Roan waited.

Drogan crossed the room and set the coffee on a bench. He looked older in the annex lighting. The tournament had turned him into a public figure again by proximity, which he hated. Reporters had rediscovered old footage of the Black Squad. Students had begun speculating about why a disgraced former operative had personally trained Roan Vance. One thread had suggested Drogan was Roan's secret father.

Drogan had read that one, gone very still, and broken his office mug.

Roan had replaced it with a cheaper mug the next day.

Drogan had noticed.

Neither of them had mentioned it.

"Draw it," Drogan said.

Roan did.

Drogan watched the line.

"Again."

Roan repeated.

"Again."

By the fifth draw, Drogan's eyes had shifted from the weapon to Roan's shoulder, elbow, wrist, hips, feet.

"Stop."

Roan stopped.

"You're treating it like a knife."

"It's a knife."

"No. It's an argument with an edge."

"That sounds like something a drunk weaponsmith would put on a sales plaque."

"It's true anyway." Drogan picked up the coffee again, decided against drinking it, and set it back down. "Sidewound doesn't reward force. It rewards timing. You don't cut where the seam is. You cut where the seam is going to be once the target reacts."

Roan considered that.

A normal blade attacked present structure. Sidewound attacked transitional failure. That made it less a weapon than an audit.

He disliked how much he liked that.

"Demonstrate," Roan said.

"No."

"Why?"

"Because Eckhart made the grip for you and I enjoy having fingers."

Drogan stepped to the pressure dummy and tapped three places: shoulder seam, floating rib, inner thigh.

"Most students see weak points as locations. They're not. They're events. A guard rises. A breath locks. A circulation pattern changes direction. That's the opening. Sidewound can widen it, but it won't create it from nothing."

"Unless I force Vitalis through it."

"Then it creates openings everywhere." Drogan looked at him. "Including you."

"Eckhart mentioned that."

"Eckhart mentioned the polite version."

Roan sheathed the blade and leaned against the training post. "How much did he tell you?"

"Nothing."

"You spoke to him."

"Yes."

"And he told you nothing."

"He told me you still have both hands."

"That isn't nothing."

Drogan grunted.

Roan studied him for a moment. "You knew he owed my father."

Drogan's face closed.

That was answer enough.

"You could have told me."

"I could tell you a lot of things."

"Try starting."

"No."

Roan didn't react. "Bad pedagogy."

"You're not my student because I'm good at talking."

"No. I assumed blackmail."

Drogan's mouth twitched despite himself. "Your father liked Eckhart."

"Why?"

"Because Eckhart hated everyone equally. Aldric found that restful."

That sounded plausible.

It also sounded like Drogan had given him the harmless answer because the useful one hurt.

Roan let it pass. For now.

He had learned over the past months that Drogan's secrets didn't respond well to direct force. They responded to necessity. Put the old man in a room where silence endangered someone he cared about and he would speak. Not before.

The Deep Rift would provide rooms like that. More than necessary.

"Training plan," Roan said.

Drogan stared at him for two seconds, then nodded once. Sentiment boxed and shelved. Good.

"For the next forty-eight hours, you stop chasing peak output," Drogan said. "No Sevenfold overload. No Pyrebrand peak cycle unless I authorize it. No Crimson Wake unless something is actively trying to kill you."

"That last condition tends to arrive without appointments."

"Then be surprised efficiently."

Roan waited.

Drogan walked to the whiteboard on the wall and wiped away someone else's footwork diagram with his sleeve. He drew four columns.

Body. Weapon. Rift. Team.

"Your body is at Hardened peak in bursts," Drogan said. "Not stable Forged. Don't confuse the two. Your bones can take impacts your organs still dislike. Tier Three environments specialize in exploiting that gap."

Roan nodded.

"Sidewound lets you threaten things you shouldn't threaten yet. That will tempt you to stand closer than you should."

"Noted."

"No. Not noted. Internalized. A Tier Three Husk doesn't need to beat your Vitalis. It needs to crack your ribs once and let Rift rot do the accounting."

Drogan moved to the second column.

"The weapon should be used for seam disruption, not killing blows unless the target is already compromised. First cut changes structure. Second cut kills. If you try to combine them, you waste the weapon's advantage."

That matched the dummy test.

Roan filed it under high confidence.

"Rift," Drogan continued. "No eating anything. No drinking anything. No touching clean water. No following voices. No entering intact buildings without me. No answering names spoken by people who aren't there."

Brendt had mentioned Rift briefings before. Most sounded like superstition wearing a uniform.

Roan disliked superstition. He disliked unknown rules more.

"What happens if a voice uses my name?"

"Depends what spoke."

"That isn't a comforting answer."

"It wasn't meant to be."

Drogan wrote under Team.

"Ines handles route mapping and salvage valuation. Brendt handles load and shields, plus camp anchors. I handle things above your pay grade. You handle observation, close threats, and not making me regret this."

"My role sounds smaller than expected."

"Good. Expectations kill."

Roan looked at the board. "And the real objective?"

Drogan's hand stopped.

There it was.

"The real objective," Roan said, "isn't training. It isn't salvage. It isn't exposure. You agreed too quickly after refusing for months. You selected Ines despite not trusting student contractors. You let Brendt come despite the risk because leaving him behind would make me refuse or make him follow stupidly. You chose a Tier Three Rift near old Hollowed territory, and Eckhart warned me not to trust clean ruins."

Drogan turned slowly.

Roan continued. "Either you found something connected to Aldric, or you think I will."

The room's ventilation hummed.

Drogan's coffee steamed on the bench, forgotten.

Finally, the old man said, "You always this irritating when given a knife?"

"No. The knife is unrelated."

Drogan rubbed a hand over his face. "There are survey records from that Rift. Old ones. Most are corrupted. One includes a symbol I saw once on your father's kit."

Roan's fingers tightened on the scabbard.

"What symbol?"

"I don't know what it means."

"That wasn't the question."

"No," Drogan said. "It wasn't."

He reached into his coat and removed a folded sheet of paper. Not a digital file. Paper. Either paranoia or respect. With Drogan, usually both.

Roan unfolded it.

The image had been copied poorly from a damaged survey scan: a stone wall half-buried in gray silt, marked by a circular glyph split through the center by seven narrow lines.

Roan didn't need the Codex Shard to react.

He had seen the pattern before in dreams that weren't dreams, in the moment Inversion first came online, and in the faint negative space behind his father's projection.

Seven lines.

One wound.

The Shard pulsed.

[Lineage marker detected.]

[Distance unknown.]

[Recommendation: proceed.]

[Secondary recommendation: do not proceed alone.]

Roan almost laughed.

That was new.

Drogan watched him. "Well?"

Roan folded the paper and handed it back. "We go."

"I didn't ask."

"You were going to."

"I was going to warn you that this may be bait."

"Of course it's bait."

Drogan's eyes narrowed.

Roan shrugged. "Everything worth investigating is either bait, a trap, or already looted. Bait has the advantage of being maintained."

The Shard went silent behind his ribs. No hum, no readout. The absence was worse than a warning.

Drogan stepped closer. "Your father said something like that once."

"He was right."

"He got killed."

"Then he missed a variable."

For a half second, the Shard pulsed once, low, out of sync with his heartbeat. Roan filed it beneath *new data* and kept his expression flat.

Drogan studied him, then exhaled through his nose. "Again."

Roan drew Sidewound.

Training resumed.

- break -

By noon, Brendt had arrived with three packs, two rented shield frames, and the expression of a man who had read too many safety documents.

"Did you know," he said without greeting, "that Tier Three Rift insurance excludes death by echo duplication?"

Roan adjusted the Vance Pendant beneath his shirt. "No."

"Do you know what echo duplication is?"

"No."

"Neither does the insurance company. I checked. They exclude it anyway."

"Efficient."

Brendt dropped the packs onto the bench. Each landed with a different metallic complaint. "I hate both of you."

Drogan inspected the shield frames. "These are decent."

Brendt brightened. "Really?"

"For rented equipment."

"Oh."

"They'll last one impact."

"Oh."

"Maybe two if the Husk is polite."

Brendt looked at Roan. "We aren't going."

"We are."

"I was afraid you'd say that."

Ines arrived ten minutes later, a compact rifle slung over one shoulder—illegal on campus, ignored by the staff who knew better—and dropped a rolled map case on the bench. The salvage contract followed before she said hello.

Roan read the contract twice.

"You changed clause seven."

Ines removed her gloves finger by finger. "I improved clause seven."

"You added discretionary valuation authority."

"I'm the only one qualified to value anomalous salvage."

"You're the only one qualified to overvalue your share."

"Both can be true."

Brendt leaned toward Drogan. "Is this how rich people flirt?"

"No," Drogan said. "This is how criminals invoice."

Roan crossed out three lines, amended two, initialed the change, and passed it back.

Ines read the edits and raised an eyebrow. "You removed my emergency premium."

"You defined emergency as 'any circumstance involving blood.'"

"It's a Rift."

"Exactly."

She considered, then nodded. "Fair."

Brendt stared. "No, wait. Why did that sound reasonable to me?"

"Exposure," Roan said. "You're developing resistance."

"I want a refund."

Roan opened the first pack. Brendt's packing was technically correct but optimistically distributed. Medical sealant was buried too deep—useless in an emergency. Flares were split into three pouches—smart. Spare socks—present. Most amateurs forgot socks, then their feet blistered, movement slowed, and the Rift killed them with terrain before any monster got the privilege. He didn't say that out loud. Brendt would make it weird.

He shifted sealant to Brendt's left hip. If Brendt's right arm was holding a shield, right-side storage was decorative.

Second pack came next: shield plates, collapsible camp frame, anchor rods, coil wire, moisture collector. Roan pulled out the coil wire and redistributed it to the third pack. "Moisture collector goes on top. If we need it, we need it fast." Brendt groaned.

Third pack held ammunition, Ines's survey tags, salvage bags, sample tubes, rope, cutting tools. Two vials of emergency stimulant were tucked at the bottom, one labeled in Ines's handwriting as *For Roan only, unless Brendt is about to die and Roan is being dramatic.* Roan held it up.

Ines smiled. "It induces controlled vascular stress. Terrible for most people. Useful for you."

"Probably."

"I had to guess. You're secretive."

"That isn't an argument for experimental drugging."

"It is if the experiment works."

Drogan took the vial from Roan, examined it, and pocketed it.

Ines frowned. "That was expensive."

"And now it's supervised."

Roan didn't object. He had no intention of taking unknown stimulants in a Deep Rift unless every other option had become worse.

He removed half of Ines's sample tubes from the third pack.

She objected.

He pointed out that dead people collected fewer samples.

She objected less.

They spent the next hour repacking. Drogan watched without interfering except to remove one ration bar from Roan's pack and replace it with two. Roan noticed. Drogan pretended not to.

Fine.

- break -

At dusk, Roan returned alone to his dorm room.

The room looked smaller than it had before the tournament. Or perhaps the problem was that too much had happened inside it. Aldric's projection had stood beside the bed. Roan had sat on the floor for three hours afterward. Brendt had once bled on the rug after a training mishap and tried to apologize to the rug. Theon Mercer, their reclusive roommate, had moved out two weeks ago after receiving a research placement, leaving behind one cracked mug and a note that read: *Try not to destabilize campus while I am gone.*

Roan had considered framing it.

He set Sidewound on the desk and removed the Vance Pendant.

The disk looked plain in his palm.

The Codex Shard had stayed quieter since he put it on. Four percent signal reduction, according to the prompt. Not much. But not nothing. In sensing terms, four percent could be the difference between a passing glance and recognition.

House Greel had recognized Aldric's energy at the tournament.

Roan had felt that recognition like cold wire sliding along his spine. He hadn't known the man's face under the hat, not with certainty, but his body had known predator pressure. The Shard had known more.

House Greel.

White beard. Gold-red eyes. Northern Glaive in a flash of old memory.

One of six.

Maybe the first one Roan would kill. Maybe not. Revenge required sequencing. Killing the most visible enemy first was emotionally satisfying and strategically childish.

He placed the pendant beside Sidewound.

Weapon. Anchor.

Both from his father's shadow.

Roan opened his notebook.

The first page still held the list he had made after Drogan told him six people had killed Aldric.

1. ?

2. ?

3. ?

4. ?

5. ?

6. ?

Underneath, added later:

House Greel, confirmed hostile. Founding Seven. Recognized Shard. Don't engage directly.

The Vell patriarch, indirect evidence. Financial network exposed. Iris unstable variable.

House Ardent, old transaction; Selene partial ally. Don't burn unless necessary.

Unknown Black Squad third. Kaelen? Status unclear.

Eckhart, old debt. Failed Aldric? Alive due wards. Asset, not safe.

He added another line.

Tier Three Rift, lineage marker. Possible bait. Proceed.

Then he stared at the words for a while.

Don't become me.

Aldric's last message, or the closest thing Roan had to one, hadn't been an instruction. It had been a warning shaped like a plea. That made it less useful. Pleas were emotionally weighted and operationally vague.

Become what, exactly?

Dead? Hunted? Guilty? Strong enough to frighten old weaponsmiths into silence? Weak enough to leave debts behind?

Roan didn't know.

He suspected the answer was inside the Rift, or under it, or waiting behind one of those clean ruins Eckhart had warned him not to trust.

Lyra's name sat in the corner of the page, unwritten. She was at Aldermere, outside immediate threat, but the Greel reach extended further than Spire walls. He had three days before departure. He'd leave a message with Old Pell—something retrievable, something deniable. It wasn't enough. It would have to be.

He picked up the pen and wrote one more line.

If becoming him is inefficient, identify alternative.

That was better.

He packed the notebook, secured Sidewound, put the pendant back on, and turned off the light.

Sleep would've been ideal.

He got three hours.

In the dark, the Shard pulsed once—not a message, not a readout, just a pulse. A reminder that it had advised against proceeding alone. He held that thought until unconsciousness pulled it under.

The Rift would remember a Vance. He intended to make sure that memory cost them.
