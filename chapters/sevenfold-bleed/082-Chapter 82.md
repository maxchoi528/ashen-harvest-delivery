Chapter 82: The Cleaner

Roan felt the tail three blocks from the Spire's east gate.

Not a visual. A pressure. The particular weight of someone's attention tracking his movement with the precision of a predator who had been given a description and a deadline. He didn't turn. He didn't slow. He adjusted his route, taking the longer path through the maintenance yards where the lighting was poor and the witnesses were few.

The tail followed.

Roan didn't bother with breakfast. The answer came fast: three weeks since he'd pushed through the Hardened barrier using Hollow Marrow Elixir, and his body still felt like a house that someone had moved all the furniture in wrong. Functional, but off-balance. The Vitalis coiled in his core read at a steady 4,200, well below his 7,500 peak, which meant he was still in the settling phase. Pushing now would be like trying to sprint on a sprained ankle.

He could do it anyway. That was the problem with Inversion. The option always existed, lurking in the back of every calculation like a dare.

He cataloged what he knew: time was 22:47, after evening drills, during the window between curfew enforcement and patrol rotations. The route he'd chosen passed through three zones of diminishing surveillance. Zone one: the public walkway with cameras every forty meters. Zone two: the maintenance yard with blind spots every twelve meters. Zone three: the old access tunnel where the cameras had been broken for six months and no one had bothered to file a repair order.

Brendt was already there, stretching against the wall. He caught Roan's eye and offered a small nod. No smile. Brendt had learned not to smile around Drogan.

Whoever was following him had either known his schedule or had been waiting for him to leave the Spire. Both possibilities suggested inside information. Both possibilities traced back to the same source.

Vorath Greel.

Roan entered Zone Two.

The maintenance yard was a graveyard of decommissioned equipment: cargo lifts with collapsed hydraulics, ventilation housings stripped for parts, a stack of steel beams left over from a renovation that had run out of budget. The emergency lights cast amber pools at irregular intervals, leaving most of the space in shadow.

He stopped next to a deactivated cargo lift. Let his posture shift to casual, leaning weight on one leg, hands in pockets, head tilted as if checking a message on a phone he didn't have. A pose that said *I'm waiting for someone who's late.* Not *I know you're there.*

The tail stopped too.

Twenty meters back, partially concealed behind a ventilation housing. Professional. Patient. Not in a hurry.

Roan ran the math. If the tail was Vorath's operative, they would be at least Hardened-tier. Possibly peak Hardened with assassination specialization. The Greel family had access to contractors who could end a target without leaving a trace. The fact that this one was visible at all meant either they wanted him to know he was being followed, or they were confident enough in their skills to let him see the threat.

Either way, the trap was already set.

The question was: who was the target?

He pushed off the cargo lift and walked toward Zone Three.

Letting the predator choose the ground meant giving up the advantage of preparation. But letting the predator think you hadn't noticed them was worth more than any prepared position.

- break -

The old access tunnel had been built to move equipment between the Black Spire's original construction site and the supply depot that predated the campus. It was narrow, concrete, wide enough for two people to walk side by side but not comfortably. The lighting was minimal. The cameras were dead.

Roan stopped at the tunnel's midpoint.

Turned.

"You've been following me for seven blocks," he said. "You're good, but you're carrying a blade with a distinctive weight shift. Left hip, cross-draw. That means you're right-handed and your primary training is close-quarters, not ranged."

A figure stepped out of the shadows at the tunnel entrance.

Tall. Lean. Dressed in dark tactical fabric that absorbed the low light. The face was unremarkable, the kind of features that could be forgotten thirty seconds after being seen. The eyes, visible even in the dim amber glow, were flat. Professional. The eyes of someone who had done this before.

"You're seventeen," the figure said. Voice calm, conversational. "The file said seventeen. You move like thirty."

"Your file was incomplete."

"Evidently." The figure took a step forward. "I'm here to deliver a message from my employer. He wanted me to tell you that the Vance name doesn't carry the weight it used to. He wanted me to demonstrate that personally."

Roan's hand found the edge of a steel support beam. The metal was cold, rough with rust. Grounding.

"Vorath Greel sent you."

The figure didn't confirm. Didn't deny. The lack of response was confirmation enough.

"He also told you not to kill me," Roan said. "Just to test me. Measure my response. Report back on what the son of Aldric Vance is capable of."

The figure's stillness shifted. The smallest movement, a reassessment. "You're as observant as the file claimed."

"The file also claimed I'd be easy."

A pause. "The file was wrong about several things."

"Yes," Roan said. "It was."

He moved.

Not toward the assassin. Toward the support beam. His palm struck the rusted metal at an angle calculated to produce maximum vibration, not damage. The beam sang. The sound echoed through the tunnel, a low frequency that bounced off the concrete walls and created a momentary distortion in the assassin's spatial awareness.

Half a second. That was all Roan needed.

He closed the distance in three steps. The assassin's hand went for the blade. Roan was already inside the draw arc, his forearm meeting the assassin's wrist before the weapon cleared the sheath. The impact was clean, precise, the kind of block that looked accidental and felt deliberate.

The assassin's eyes widened. Just slightly. The first crack in the professional facade.

"You've been watching me for three days," Roan said, holding the locked position. "You know my schedule. You know my training level. What you don't know is what happens when someone trained by the Black Spire's most dangerous instructor decides to stop holding back."

He released the lock and stepped back. Two meters. Open ground.

The assassin drew the blade.

It was a Greel-pattern combat knife, matte black, serrated along the spine, designed for penetration and tissue damage. Standard issue for House Greel's field operatives. The sight of it confirmed everything Roan needed to know.

"Your employer made a mistake," Roan said. "He sent a cleaner to do an investigator's job."

"Your father was killed by my employer."

Roan's expression didn't change. But behind his eyes, a calculation completed. The assassin knew about Aldric. Which meant this wasn't a simple test. This was a message: *We know who you are. We know what you're doing. And we're not afraid of you.*

"I'm not afraid of you either," Roan said.

Then he let the Codex Shard open.

The sensation was familiar now, the warmth spreading from his spine, the presence at the base of his skull, the system's recognition of a combat environment. The Inversion engine cycled, ready to convert.

[Sidewound pending. Combat engagement detected. Conversion eligible.]

The assassin lunged.

Roan sidestepped.

The blade passed centimeters from his ribs, close enough that he felt the displaced air. He didn't counter. He observed. The assassin's stance, the angle of the strike, the recovery speed, the way the blade's weight shifted the assassin's center of gravity during the follow-through. All data. All useful.

The assassin pivoted, blade coming around in a horizontal arc. Roan ducked under it, felt the edge graze his shoulder. Not deep. A scratch.

[Sidewound detected. Minor laceration, left shoulder. Conversion eligible. Yield: +12 Vitalis.]

The system processed the injury in under a second. Roan felt the pain convert, felt the Vitalis flow into his core. The scratch was already closing. Twelve Vitalis, not much, but confirmation that the system worked in live combat.

He let the next strike land.

A calculated risk. The assassin's blade caught his forearm, a shallow cut, three inches long, bleeding freely. The pain registered, and the Codex Shard converted it immediately.

[Sidewound detected. Superficial arm wound. Conversion eligible. Yield: +28 Vitalis.]

The assassin noticed. The slight hesitation in the follow-through, the way Roan wasn't bleeding as heavily as he should have been. The professional facade cracked further.

"What are you?"

Roan didn't answer. He stepped into the assassin's next strike, taking the blade's edge across his ribs in exchange for closing the distance to striking range. His palm connected with the assassin's solar plexus. Not a killing blow. A measured strike, hard enough to fold the assassin forward, not hard enough to end the fight.

The assassin's breath left in a controlled grunt. The blade dropped. Not dropped, fell, the assassin's hand opening involuntarily as the nervous system prioritized breathing over weapon retention.

Roan caught the blade before it hit the ground.

He held it up. The Greel pattern caught the amber light.

"Tell your employer," Roan said, "that his cleaner failed. Tell him the Vance son sends his regards."

He reversed the blade and drove it into the concrete at his feet, leaving it standing upright, a marker and a message.

The assassin straightened, hands empty, calculation running behind the flat eyes. The fight was over. The math was clear: a target who could not only survive but convert damage into power was not a target that could be tested with conventional methods.

"I'll deliver the message." The assassin's voice was level, professional, but something had changed. Respect, or fear, or the recognition that the job description had just expanded beyond the contract.

The assassin turned and walked back the way they had come, footsteps steady, posture controlled.

Roan watched until the figure disappeared into the dark.

Then he looked at the cuts on his arm, his ribs, his shoulder. The wounds were already sealing, the Codex Shard cycling the converted Vitalis through his system. He had gained forty Vitalis from the exchange. A small number, but the first time he had tested Inversion in true combat.

[Combat evaluation: successful. Threat classification: peak Hardened, assassination specialty. Recommend increased caution. Vorath Greel's interest is no longer theoretical. — A.V.]

Roan pulled his sleeve down over the healing cut.

The message was clear: Vorath Greel had sent a cleaner to test him. The cleaner had failed. But failure didn't mean the tests would stop. It meant the next one would be harder.

He walked back toward the Spire, the cold air drying the blood on his arm, the Codex Shard warm against his spine, the first true combat data logged and analyzed.

Vorath Greel had made his move.

Now it was Roan's turn.
