Chapter 185: Cole Refuses

Cole refused to surrender Roan twice.

The first refusal had been legal. It existed in transcript, sealed archive, and the northern delegation's collective expression as they left Black Spire through rain that had become too heavy for dignity. The second refusal happened in Cole's office four hours later, with fewer witnesses and more anger.

"No," Cole said.

Roan sat opposite him in the same medical chair because the healers had forbidden normal chairs and Drogan had taken the prohibition personally. The office had no windows, no trophies, no paintings with eyes, and no surface onto which Roan could conveniently project blame. It was a room designed to make excuses feel underdressed.

Drogan stood by the door. Selene stood near the bookshelves. Iris and Ines weren't present. Cole had barred them both on the grounds that one was politically explosive and the other had already tried to invoice the administration for "crisis analytics."

Ines had appealed the decision.

Cole had denied the appeal in four words.

The invoice remained pending.

Roan looked at the folder on Cole's desk. "No to which part?"

"All of it."

"That's imprecise."

"You invited a hostile Crowned-level patriarch to meet you in public, intend to make an unsupervised statement about a sealed murder case, and are refusing protective confinement after sustaining duel injuries. Precision would waste syllables."

"Vorath isn't Crowned."

Cole's eyes narrowed.

Roan continued, "His public profile says Crowned peak. His pressure through the remote observation exceeded that."

Drogan looked at him sharply. "How far exceeded?"

"Enough."

"That's my line."

"You underuse it."

Cole steepled his fingers. "Do you understand what happens if Vorath Greel steps onto Ironholm soil under diplomatic pretext?"

"Yes."

"State it."

"He gains movement rights under compact courtesy. Direct arrest becomes politically difficult unless he commits visible violation. Every House-aligned observer in the city starts measuring whether Black Spire can protect a student who challenged him. Sentinel Bureau increases surveillance but avoids first action. Media reframes the story from Greel assassination order to inevitable meeting between old bloodline and unknown student. If I hide, I become object. If I appear, I become risk."

Cole was silent for a moment.

Selene's eyes had lowered slightly, which meant she was listening harder.

Drogan said, "You left out the part where he kills you."

"That's included under risk."

"I hate your categories."

"They're more useful than panic."

Cole tapped the folder once. "Protective confinement exists for situations like this."

"No. It exists for simpler situations than this."

"Explain the distinction."

"If I were merely threatened, confinement would reduce attack surface. I am more than threatened. I am evidence. Moving evidence into a locked room may preserve it physically, but it reduces its effect. Greel wants the story abstract. Aldric Vance's son hidden behind institutional walls while adults argue jurisdiction. That lets Vorath talk over me."

Cole's expression remained flat. "You are seventeen."

"Yes."

"You say that as if it's irrelevant."

"It's relevant to their optics. Not to whether they killed my father."

Drogan's hand closed on the doorframe.

There it was again. The sentence that changed air.

Aldric Vance had been dead for six years officially, longer in some people's faces. His case had been labeled a failed Rift operation, then sealed under security classification, then buried beneath pity and institutional fatigue. The world had learned to leave it alone because leaving alone cost less than asking why a man like Aldric died with too few answers and too many powerful people relieved.

Roan had learned not to leave things alone in Greycross.

Things left alone grew teeth.

Cole opened the folder.

Inside were printouts. Old ones. Copies of copies. The top page showed Aldric's file image: younger than Roan remembered, because memory preferred fathers older than photographs did. Aldric looked tired and amused, then inconveniently alive.

Roan didn't reach for the file.

Cole noticed.

"I pulled what I could access after your first inquiry months ago," Cole said. "Most of it's sealed above my authority."

"Above the Headmaster of Black Spire."

"Yes."

"Convenient."

"Very."

Selene moved closer by one step. She stopped when Roan glanced at her. Not offended. Calibrating.

Cole turned the first page. "Official cause: catastrophic mission failure during Rift containment operation, northern theater. Witnesses unavailable or classified. Body unrecovered. Personal effects limited."

"False."

Cole looked up.

Roan's voice had been flat. Too flat, perhaps. He corrected breathing before anyone mistook it for emotion worth addressing.

"Which part?" Cole asked.

"Unavailable. Not dead. Not all."

Drogan said nothing.

Cole's eyes shifted to him. "You have names."

Roan didn't answer.

Drogan did.

"Some."

Cole's jaw tightened. "How long have you known?"

Drogan looked like a man considering several bad answers and selecting the least flammable.

"Long enough to know saying them wrong gets people killed."

Roan said, "Vorath Greel is one."

The office became smaller.

Cole didn't write it down. That was wise. He let the sentence enter memory.

"Confirmed?" he asked.

"Yes."

"By what standard?"

"Shard memory, Drogan's reactions, Vorath's recognition, Kael's order, and old battlefield fragments."

"Shard memory," Cole repeated.

Roan didn't elaborate.

Cole didn't ask.

That restraint raised him another point.

"You can't say Shard publicly," Cole said.

"I won't."

"You can't claim evidence you can't disclose."

"I won't."

"You can't accuse six unnamed powerful figures without proof structure."

"I can. It would be inefficient. I won't."

Cole leaned back. "Then what will you say?"

Roan removed one folded page from his coat and placed it on the desk.

Cole didn't touch it immediately.

Selene didn't move.

Drogan looked at the paper as if it might explode.

It might, socially.

Cole opened it.

The statement was short. Roan had cut it down four times. Long statements gave enemies more places to stand. Short statements forced them to attack the load-bearing beams.

Cole read.

His expression didn't change until the final line.

Then his mouth tightened.

"No."

"Second refusal," Roan said.

"Don't be clever."

"Then be specific."

Cole placed the page on the desk and turned it around.

Selene stepped close enough to read over his shoulder. Drogan didn't. He already knew the shape of it from Roan's face. The statement read:

Six years ago, the world was told my father, Aldric Vance, died in a failed Rift operation. That wasn't the whole truth. Six men killed my father. I have their names. One of them sent Kael Greel to Black Spire to kill me. Today, that attempt is on record. I am requesting the Aldric Vance case be reopened under neutral review. If the men who killed him believe I am wrong, they may say so under oath.

No flourish. No grief. No accusation beyond what the record could support.

Cole tapped the third sentence. "Six men killed my father."

"Yes."

"You can't prove all six."

"I can prove enough to make denial costly."

"Not the same."

"No. Better for now."

Selene said, "This implies you know all six."

"I don't."

"Then it's dangerous."

"It's bait."

Drogan finally moved from the door. "No."

Roan looked at him.

The old instructor's face wasn't angry now. That was worse. Anger was heat. This was old ash.

"No," Drogan repeated. "You don't use your father's murder as bait."

Roan held his gaze.

There was a line there. Not one of Roan's seven rules, but one of Drogan's. A dead man's name wasn't a lure. Grief wasn't a tool. Some things were supposed to remain heavier than strategy.

Roan understood the idea.

He also understood that the men who killed Aldric had used the weight of his death as cover for six years.

"If bait is what pulls them into light," Roan said, "then yes."

Drogan took one step forward.

Selene shifted, not between them exactly, but near enough to become a future complication.

Cole said, "Drogan."

The old man stopped.

Roan didn't look away. "They buried him with a false file. They sent Kael. Vorath is coming. They don't get solemnity because solemnity makes you comfortable."

Drogan's eyes flashed.

"Careful," he said.

Roan knew he should stop.

He continued anyway. Not from anger. From necessity, which sometimes looked similar from outside.

"You trained me to cut seams. This is a seam."

Drogan stared at him.

Then he turned away, stopping at the door. His hand found the handle. He didn't pull. After a long three seconds, he released it and moved to the wall.

That landed harder than anger.

Roan let it. Pain was measurement.

Cole picked up the page again. "Your wording also invites them to say you are destabilizing public order."

"They will anyway."

"It invites hostile defamation suits."

"Good. Discovery cuts both ways."

Selene's eyes flicked to him. "That's a legal tactic."

"Iris explained it."

"That explains why I dislike it."

Cole rubbed his brow with two fingers. "You are weaponizing civil procedure against men who can kill buildings."

"Yes."

"Why does that sound reasonable to me?" Selene murmured.

"Exposure," Roan said. "You're developing resistance."

She gave him a look sharp enough to qualify as a warning.

Cole set the page down. "I won't authorize this exact statement."

"Good."

"Good?"

"If you authorize it, it becomes institutional speech. You should object in writing. Then I say it as personal statement after being advised not to. That protects Black Spire from ownership and proves I wasn't scripted."

Cole looked at him.

For three seconds no one spoke. Then Cole said, very quietly, "You are exhausting."

"Yes."

Drogan laughed once from near the wall.

It wasn't a happy sound. But it wasn't the sound of a man walking away.

Cole opened a drawer and removed a formal advisory form. "I will state that Black Spire advises against unverified public allegations and recommends postponement pending internal review."

"Accepted."

"You will state that you speak personally."

"Yes."

"You won't name anyone except Kael, whose confession is preserved."

"And Vorath?"

Cole's eyes hardened.

Roan said, "Kael named him. That's record."

"It's contested."

"Everything useful is contested."

Selene said, "Name him once. As part of Kael's preserved statement. Don't add adjectives."

Roan considered. "Accepted."

Drogan looked back. "You are really doing this."

"Yes."

"He would hate it."

Aldric.

The unspoken name sat between them.

Roan looked at the old file photo on Cole's desk. His father smiled in the image like he had just noticed something everyone else missed and was deciding whether to tell them.

"Maybe," Roan said.

Drogan's face tightened.

Roan added, "He isn't here to argue."

No one answered that.

---

Cole required three additional safeguards before he allowed the press event to proceed.

First: a medical fail-safe embedded in Roan's chair, calibrated to trigger if his pulse dropped below a threshold or his Vitalis spiked above what healers considered "recklessly stupid." Roan objected to the wording. The healer showed him the chart. He withdrew the objection on technical grounds and maintained it on tone.

Second: a seven-second external feed delay controlled by the communications office and Cole, plus one neutral legal observer. Roan accepted because seven seconds wasn't enough to stop the statement if the first sentence landed properly, and because refusing would make Cole sedate him.

Third: no unscheduled questions from House representatives.

That last rule failed before it began.

"House representatives can register as legal correspondents," Selene said while they reviewed the attendance list in the secure conference room. "Several already have."

Cole looked at the list with institutional disgust. "Names."

Selene marked four. "Two Greel-aligned. One Vell-adjacent but not Iris's branch. One Ardent watcher who will report to my aunt before breathing twice."

Roan looked at her. "Will that be a problem?"

"Yes."

"Can you control it?"

"No."

"Then useful to know."

Selene's mouth tightened. "You are becoming too comfortable with hostile rooms."

"No. Comfortable people stop counting exits."

She didn't argue because the door behind Roan, the side service hatch, the ceiling maintenance panel, and the reinforced recorder booth had already been included in his notes. So had the press room's terrible table placement and the fact that the podium could conceal a short blade if a communications officer had poor hiring practices.

Drogan reviewed security with even less patience.

"No one approaches within three meters," he said. "No gifts. No paper handed directly. No floral arrangements."

Ines looked offended. "Who attacks with flowers?"

"People who know you will steal expensive vases."

"That's targeted slander."

"It hit."

Iris entered late with a sealed folder and the bright-eyed look of someone who hadn't slept because sleep was for people without leverage.

"I found precedent," she said.

Cole said, "For what?"

"Personal statements reopening sealed martial death cases."

"Why?"

"Because if anyone tries to suspend Roan for destabilizing public order, precedent matters." She placed the folder on the table. "Three cases in the last forty years. Two failed because the speaker made unsupported accusations against named officials. One succeeded because the speaker requested review rather than punishment."

Roan looked up. "Which is why we request reopening."

"Yes. Don't say justice."

"I wasn't going to."

"Good. Justice is a fog machine in legal contexts."

Cole looked at Iris for a moment. "You are unfortunately useful."

"I get that a lot now."

"No one means it as a compliment."

"I noticed."

Roan opened the folder. The successful precedent involved a border practitioner whose sister had died during a sanctioned extraction. He hadn't named the commander responsible. He had requested audit of the extraction order, then used discovery to expose falsified timestamps. The commander resigned before criminal charges could attach.

Useful structure.

Not enough.

Roan underlined one sentence: *The claimant avoided imputing motive and focused on procedural impossibility.*

Procedural impossibility was better than murder as an opening tool. Murder sounded emotional. Impossible records sounded expensive.

He adjusted one phrase in the statement.

*Six men killed my father* remained.

Everything after became more procedural.

Drogan watched the edit. "You are polishing a grenade."

"Yes."

"Does that make it safer?"

"No. Easier to aim."

The old man looked like he wanted to object and couldn't decide which part deserved it first.

Cole signed the advisory form at 18:40.

Black Spire formally advised postponement.

Roan formally declined.

The document required his signature. His left hand was splinted. He signed with his right and made the mark deliberately neat. Sloppiness suggested distress. Distress gave enemies room to call him unstable.

The advisory went into record.

A copy went to legal.

A copy went to communications.

A copy went to Greel counsel because Cole enjoyed cruelty when formatted correctly.

The response from Greel counsel wasn't immediate. That was its own answer. Immediate outrage would have meant surprise. Delayed silence meant the advisory had entered a room with lawyers, been read aloud, and caused at least one person to say something expensive.

Roan marked the time.

A narrow window later, Maera Voss acknowledged receipt with seven words: *House Greel reserves all rights and objections.*

Iris read it and laughed. "They hate it. Can't stop it before tomorrow."

"Useful."

"Very. Also, they used objections plural. That means they haven't chosen a primary attack."

Selene looked up. "Or they're reserving multiple."

"They always reserve multiple," Iris said. "The question is which one they lead with. They don't know yet."

Uncertainty in an enemy wasn't victory. It was a discount. A discount was enough.

He added the advisory response to the same folder as Kael's confession, Vorath's declaration, and the preserved arena logs. A case wasn't one large proof. It was a stack of smaller proofs arranged so that removing one made the remover visible. Preferably on camera, where denial had a timestamp.

Within nine minutes, a northern legal commentator posted that Black Spire was recklessly allowing an injured student to inflame an international matter.

Iris read the post and smiled. "They hate the advisory."

"Why?" Ines asked.

"Because it proves the school told him not to. If he speaks anyway, Greel has to attack him personally or admit the institution didn't script him."

Drogan muttered, "Everyone in this room needs a hobby."

Roan looked at him. "This is yours."

"My hobby was drinking."

"Was?" Selene asked.

Drogan glared at the wall.

For a brief moment, the room almost felt normal.

That was dangerous. Normality. Roan didn't trust it.

He returned to the attendance list.

The Vance Pendant pulsed cold against his chest.

[External attention density increasing.]

[Witness field forming.]

[Warning: witness fields attract hunters.]

Roan made a note.

Add hunters to security model.

By evening, the statement infrastructure existed.

That was Ines's phrase, and Roan disliked it only because it was accurate enough today.

Student press requested attendance. External martial outlets received formal notification of "personal statement regarding preserved duel confession and Aldric Vance case." Neutral archive mirrors were prepared. Black Spire legal office issued its advisory disavowing premature speculation while affirming student speech rights. The wording was dull enough to be protective and transparent enough to be read as: we told him not to, and he's doing it anyway.

Iris handled distribution paths. Selene handled House witness channels. Ines handled recordings and made three separate people angry by insisting camera angles were legal evidence rather than aesthetics. Drogan handled security by personally terrifying two hired event contractors into competence.

Roan wrote nothing new.

He sat in the medical annex with the statement page on his lap and watched Brendt eat soup poorly.

Brendt's hands still trembled when he lifted the spoon. The healers said nerve stability would return. Brendt said the soup had committed crimes and deserved instability. Both could be true.

"You look terrible," Brendt said.

"You look worse."

"I was stabbed by politics."

"You were stabbed by Greel."

"Same knife family."

Roan accepted that.

Brendt set the spoon down. "They told me about the statement."

"Which version of told?"

"The version where Selene explained the legal parts, Iris explained the political parts, Ines explained the profitable parts, and Drogan stood in the corner looking like he wanted to fight a concept."

"Comprehensive."

"Annoyingly."

Roan waited.

Brendt looked down at his soup. "You should do it."

That wasn't what Roan had expected.

He adjusted his estimate of Brendt's current mental state upward and his likelihood of saying something inconvenient likewise.

"Reason?" Roan asked.

"Because everyone keeps talking like this is dangerous because enemies will notice you." Brendt's mouth twisted. "They already noticed. They noticed when they sent Kael. They noticed when they sent people into a Rift. They noticed when your father died. The only people who haven't noticed are the ones who could make it expensive."

Roan looked at him.

Brendt shifted. "What?"

"That was coherent."

"I hate you."

"No, you don't."

"I hate that too."

Roan folded the statement once.

Brendt's gaze followed the paper. "Are you naming all six?"

"I don't know all six."

"Right. Stupid question."

"No. Common one."

"Do you know more than Vorath?"

"Yes."

"How many?"

Roan didn't answer immediately.

Trust had led them here. Not blind trust. Roan disliked blindness. But Brendt had followed him into Rifts, into illegal economies, into hospital beds, into the path of House politics. He had earned some numbers.

"Two confirmed," Roan said. "One probable. Three shadows."

Brendt nodded slowly. "And saying six tells the shadows you have them."

"It tells them I might."

"They'll move."

"Yes."

"And then you watch who moves."

Roan inclined his head.

Brendt stared at him. "That's horrible."

"Yes."

"Also smart."

"Yes."

"You could pretend to be conflicted occasionally."

"No."

Brendt smiled faintly, then winced because smiling pulled something. "Fine. Go ruin dead men's peace."

"Some are alive."

"Even better."

Roan stood carefully.

At the door, Brendt said, "Roan."

He looked back.

Brendt's expression had shifted. Less humor. More the thing under it.

"Don't let them make your father into a slogan."

Roan thought about that.

Then he nodded once.

"I won't."

---

The message from the Northern Compact arrived at 23:19.

Not through official channels.

Roan's slate chimed with an unknown sender and no metadata, which meant the sender was either skilled or had bribed someone who was. The message contained no greeting.

*Lord Greel accepts neutral meeting conditions in principle. Location and time to be proposed after review of security constraints.*

Below that, a second line.

*He looks forward to hearing what Aldric's boy has learned.*

Roan read it twice.

The wording was deliberate. Not Roan Vance. Aldric's boy. Possession by dead father. Diminishment by lineage. Bait aimed where Vorath assumed pain lived.

*But delay masked as protocol.* Vorath wasn't accepting. He was freezing the momentum. Every hour the meeting waited was an hour the statement lost edge. The trap wasn't in the conflict. It was in the calendar.

Roan saved the message.

Then he opened the statement file and added nothing.

He slept for ninety minutes.

At dawn, the Codex Shard woke him before the alarm.

[Witness record threshold approaching.]

[Lineage exposure risk: high.]

[Recommendation: do not name what cannot yet be killed.]

Roan sat up slowly, ribs protesting.

"That," he told the empty room, "was almost good advice."

The Shard didn't answer. It was learning.
