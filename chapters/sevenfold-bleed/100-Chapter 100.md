Chapter 100: The Ripple

The storage room on sub-level four smelled of dust and old paper. Roan sat on the cot with the burner phone in his hands, watching the screen glow in the dark. No messages. No flags. Iris Vell had walked out of Glassline two hours ago, and the world had not yet reacted.

That was normal. Explosions took time to propagate.

He had prepared for this moment the way he prepared for everything: by eliminating variables. The Maddox file contained no direct references to his sources. The financial records had been anonymized through three intermediary shells. The single sheet with the six dates could not be traced back to Drogan's copy of the execution order. He had burned every bridge behind him, salted every earth, closed every loop.

But Iris Vell was not a loop. Iris was a variable that he had deliberately introduced into a system that had been stable for six years. He could calculate her likely responses, but he could not control them.

That was the part of the plan that kept him awake.

- break -

Brendt found him at 6 AM, still sitting on the cot, the burner phone cold and dark in his hands.

"You look like shit," Brendt said.

"I've been calculating."

"Calculating what?"

"Whether I just made the best or worst decision of the last six months."

Brendt sat on the floor across from him. He didn't ask for context. He had learned, over months of proximity, that Roan would provide information when the information was ready.

"Iris has the file," Roan said.

Brendt's eyebrows rose. "The full file?"

"The full file. Thirty years of Maddox Vell's financial records. The execution order. The six names." He set the phone aside. "She knows her grandfather signed my father's death warrant."

Brendt let out a slow breath. "And you gave it to her because..."

"Because she needed to know. And because I needed her to choose a side before the tournament forced her hand."

"You're using her."

"I'm giving her information. What she does with it is her choice."

Brendt studied him for a long moment. "You actually believe that."

"I believe that she deserved the truth. I also believe that the truth, once known, cannot be unknown. Whatever she decides now, she decides with full information. That's more than most people get."

The ventilation system hummed. Somewhere above them, the Black Spire was waking up, students heading to morning drills, instructors preparing lesson plans, the ordinary machinery of institutional life continuing its relentless momentum.

"What happens if she confronts him?" Brendt asked.

"Then Maddox Vell learns that his granddaughter knows what he did. And he has to decide whether to silence her, discredit her, or try to bring her back into the fold. All three options create friction. Friction generates information."

"And if she doesn't confront him?"

"Then she sits on the information. And the knowledge corrodes her from the inside. Eventually, she'll make a choice." Roan stood. His joints protested, hours of stillness exacting their toll. "Either way, the system has been disrupted. Maddox Vell's control over his family's narrative is no longer absolute. That's worth the risk."

Brendt stood as well. "You're playing a long game."

"I've been playing a long game since I found my father's pendant." Roan picked up the burner phone, checked it one last time, then snapped it in half and dropped the pieces into the waste bin. "Iris is just the latest piece on the board."

"And if she decides you're the enemy?"

"Then I've gained information about her decision-making process." Roan paused, weighing the words. He had already run that branch to its terminus. If she went to Maddox, he lost the element of surprise. But the copies were buried where no single betrayal could reach them, and the knowledge in her head could not be erased—only denied. That cost he had accepted when he slipped the file across the table. "And I'll adjust accordingly."

He reached for his jacket. Paused.

"There's a second possibility," he said. "One I haven't fully calculated."

Brendt waited.

"She might decide to help."

The words hung in the air. Brendt said nothing. Roan opened the door and stepped into the corridor, leaving the question unanswered.

- break -

The morning drills passed without incident. Roan moved through the forms with mechanical precision, his mind running scenario models behind a face that gave nothing away. The instructors graded his movements as efficient, slightly tired. They did not catch the delay—a third of a second per transition—where his eyes tracked the exit routes, the window angles, the positions of every registered combatant in the hall. The calculation was not abstract; it was architectural.

At noon, he walked past the bulletin board outside the administration wing and noticed a new notice pinned to the cork. Official memorandum, Black Spire letterhead, signed by the Headmaster's office.

*Effective immediately: all student communications with off-campus parties are subject to randomized review. This measure is temporary and will be rescinded upon resolution of an active investigation.*

Roan read it once.

Kept walking.

Ahead, two second-years stood near the stairwell, voices low. "They pulled Elara Haven from third-period tactics. Routine check." Routine. He filed the name. Someone else's variable. The net was already wider than his alone.

The message was clear: someone had noticed the information flow. Someone was trying to contain it. The question was whether they were trying to protect Iris, protect Maddox, or protect the institution's reputation.

All three answers led to different responses. None of them changed the fundamental arithmetic.

He had lit a fuse. The only thing left to do was see which direction the explosion traveled.

The Codex Shard pulsed against his spine, warm and patient.

The math was incomplete. But for the first time in months, the variables were in motion.
