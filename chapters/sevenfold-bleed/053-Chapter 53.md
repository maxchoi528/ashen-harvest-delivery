Chapter 53: A Public Correction

The morning Vitalis readings showed 1,247. Down from yesterday's 1,251. Four points of regression, perfectly explained by skipping last night's meditation to finish the Wraithmilk batch.

Small losses accumulated. That was the tally.

Roan sat on the edge of his bunk, turning the empty vial between his fingers. Three Wraithmilk doses processed overnight, each one requiring forty minutes of careful temperature monitoring and spiritual pressure application. Three vials of what the market priced at eighteen credits each, transformed through Inversion into something worth considerably more.

The Codex Shard hummed faintly against his seventh vertebra. A low, consistent hum. Background signature of its presence.

[Capacity: 4/4. All conversions nominal. — A.V.]

His father's echoes had surfaced more frequently over the past weeks. Not actual speech—the Shard didn't hold enough of Aldric's consciousness for conversation—but impressions. The system messages occasionally carried a sardonic edge that felt less like code and more like opinion.

Roan noted the pattern. The Shard was a tool. Tools developed quirks. That didn't make them sentient.

The dormitory door slammed open.

Brendt stood in the corridor, breathing hard. His Vitalis reading was elevated, sustained physical exertion within the last fifteen minutes, based on the slight tremor in his hands.

"Warden's calling you."

"Warden Drogan?"

"Who else has that voice?" Brendt leaned against the doorframe, trying to look casual and failing. "Got back from morning sparring and found the summons on my bunk. Your name's on it too."

Roan stood, sliding the empty vial into his pocket. "Did he say why?"

"Didn't say anything. Just 'tell Vance to report immediately.' That's the whole message." Brendt's jaw tightened. "Could be anything. Could be good. Could be—"

"An interrogation about why my practical exam scores don't match my sparring performance?"

Brendt's expression flickered. "You noticed that too."

"Hard to miss." Roan grabbed his jacket from the bedpost. "My written scores put me in the top fifteen percent of our intake cohort. My sparring scores put me in the bottom third. The instructors have noticed the discrepancy."

"They think you're sandbagging."

"They think I'm hiding something." Roan checked the hallway. Empty. "Everyone at this institute is hiding something. The difference is whether they think mine is worth investigating."

They walked in silence through the dormitory corridors. Morning light slanted through the high windows, catching dust motes in golden suspension. Other students moved past them in clusters, conversations dying as Roan passed.

He'd gotten used to that. The social calculus at Black Spire operated on Vitalis readings, family connections, and demonstrated potential. Roan had none of the first publicly, limited knowledge of the second, and was actively suppressing the third.

The perfect profile for a nobody.

The Warden's office occupied a converted armory on the institute's eastern wing. Drogan had been a Warden for thirty years before taking the teaching position, a former Rift Hunter with more combat experience than anyone else on staff and a reputation for brutally practical instruction.

The iron-bound door swung open before Roan could knock.

"Son."

Drogan stood behind a desk cluttered with weapon maintenance logs and incident reports. Grey-streaked beard, scarred hands, eyes the color of old iron. He looked like he'd slept in his uniform.

"Close the door."

Roan complied. Brendt hovered in the corridor.

"You too, Holloway. Get in here."

Brendt stepped inside, confusion evident. The door closed with a heavy thud.

Drogan didn't sit. He stood with both hands flat on the desk, studying Roan with an intensity that felt like physical pressure.

"Your Wraithmilk batch from last night. The one you sold to the infirmary supply officer." A statement, not a question. "Where did you source it?"

The ledger recalculated instantly.

Drogan knew about the Wraithmilk. Drogan knew about the sale. Drogan knew enough to track individual batches back to their origin.

"I purchased it from a street vendor in the lower city," Roan said. "The label was mislabeled, deliberate. I corrected the error and resold it."

"At four hundred percent markup."

"Seventy-three percent profit margin, accounting for my processing time and materials."

Drogan's expression didn't change. "You've been running this operation for three months. Under my nose, under the instructors' noses, under the Sentinel Bureau's oversight protocols. No one caught it." A pause. "That's either very lucky or very deliberate."

"Can't it be both?"

"It can't." Drogan straightened. "Luck is for amateurs. You don't strike me as an amateur, Vance."

The room went quiet. Brendt looked between them, aware he was missing something necessary.

"The question I want to ask," Drogan said, "is why your batch of Wraithmilk showed a nineteen percent increase in spiritual conductivity compared to standard pharmaceutical grade. The infirmary submitted it for quality verification because the initial effects were stronger than expected. The lab is now very confused."

The Codex Shard registered the news without comment.

[Reagent anomaly: logged in external chain.]

[Cross-referencing requested by infirmary supply officer.]

[Bureau audit window: shortened by approximately 19%.]

Roan's pulse remained steady. "Maybe their equipment needs calibration."

"Maybe." Drogan moved around the desk. "Or maybe you're doing something to these reagents that no one else can do. Something that changes their fundamental properties." He stopped three feet from Roan. "I'm going to ask you a question, and I want you to answer honestly. Not because I'm your instructor, but because if I'm wrong about this, people are going to get hurt."

"What question?"

Drogan's eyes dropped to the space below Roan's collarbone, where the Vance Pendant hung beneath his shirt. A heavy, practical piece of black iron, designed to look like cheap ornamental metalwork.

The Shard pulsed once against Roan's spine.

"Is that what I think it is?"

Roan held the Warden's gaze. "What do you think it is?"

The question hung in the air. Drogan's scarred hands flexed at his sides.

"Your father had one. Identical weight, identical material profile, identical wear patterns on the chain." His voice dropped. "I watched him wear it every day for seven years. Then one night he took it off and put it in a box, and the next morning he was gone."

"You knew him."

"I served with him. Black Squad, before it fell apart." Drogan's jaw worked. "I told myself he died in that Rift engagement. That was the official story. His body was never recovered, but that happens sometimes—Rifts swallow things. People. Evidence." A hard breath. "But Aldric Vance didn't die in any Rift. I finally figured that out three years ago when I started digging into his disappearance. The evidence doesn't match. The timeline doesn't match. The official story is a cover-up for something else entirely."

"And now you think you've found proof."

"I think you've been walking around this institute with a weapon that doesn't officially exist, using it to transmute pharmaceutical reagents into something that defies laboratory analysis." Drogan stepped closer. "I think you've been operating in plain sight because no one knows what to look for. And I think either you're your father's son, which means you're exactly as dangerous as the people hunting him said you would be, or you're a plant from one of the other Houses, using Vance technology that was supposed to be destroyed."

"The Greels said my father was dangerous."

The words landed like a blade.

Drogan went still.

"The Greels certified the investigation," Roan said. He pulled the pendant free, letting the black iron catch the dim light. "They said he died in a Rift."

He let the silence stretch.

"They lied."

"You know what happened to him."

"I know they lied."

The office seemed to contract. Brendt stood frozen by the door, his Vitalis reading spiking with alarm.

Drogan's hand moved, not toward a weapon, but toward the window. He pulled the thin fabric curtain across the glass, blocking the view from the training yard below.

"The Black Squad was four people," he said quietly. "Aldric. Myself. Kaelen. And one more, someone I've never been able to identify—records were scrubbed after the Rift event. We were investigating something. I don't know what. Aldric handled that side of it. I just provided tactical support." His scarred fingers curled. "Three months after he disappeared, I got a message. Dead drop without sender identification. Just two words: 'Protect him.'"

Roan went still. A physical sensation, not emotional. The Shard was responding to something, a spike in his Vitalis circulation, perhaps, or an emotional signature it was designed to detect.

"You've been watching me."

"I've been waiting." Drogan's voice was rough. "Aldric knew someone would come for you eventually. He left instructions for your mother, but he also left instructions for me. In case she couldn't—couldn't keep you safe. In case they found you despite everything." He met Roan's eyes. "The Greels aren't the only ones who lied about what happened. Kaelen did too. I still don't know why."

"Kaelen the Unbroken."

"You've heard of him?"

"Heard of him." Roan turned the pendant in his fingers. "Fighter. Ascendant tier, last anyone checked. Operates out of Suthmere. No formal affiliation with any House or government. Takes contracts that interest him and ignores everything else." A pause. "He's supposed to have died seventeen years ago. Another convenient story."

"Convenient stories are the family business of every major power on this continent." Drogan exhaled. "The question is what you're going to do about it."

"Do about it?"

"The Greels are moving. I've had intel for three weeks—sources inside House Ashford, leaked through back channels. House Greel has people looking for something. Something his father supposedly destroyed forty years ago but might not have." His eyes dropped to the pendant again. "I don't know exactly what it is. But I know it rhymes with a certain piece of iron you're wearing."

Roan considered the information. The Greels. Looking for Vance technology. Had they always been looking, or had something recently reminded them to search?

The timeline was interesting. Three weeks. That matched the date of Selene Ardent's formal challenge.

"Someone challenged me to a public duel three weeks ago. Selene Ardent. The challenge was backed by House Ardent but sponsored through a third-party conduit I haven't fully traced."

Drogan's expression flickered. "That wasn't in my briefings."

"I didn't report it to your briefings."

"You should have." The Warden's voice sharpened. "Selene Ardent doesn't make moves without calculating seventeen steps ahead. If she's positioning herself around you, it's because someone told her to."

"Selene has her own agenda. She doesn't take orders from the Greels."

"Everyone takes orders from someone. The question is whether the orders fit her interests or just use them as camouflage." Drogan paced to the window, his back to them. "I'm telling you this because you need to understand the stakes. Whatever your father built into that device, whatever it does, it's useful enough that House Greel is personally overseeing the search. That means resources. That means patience. That means people willing to wait years for the right moment to strike."

"And you're telling me this because—"

"Because Aldric asked me to." Drogan turned. "He asked me to keep you safe until you were ready. Ready for what, I don't know. He didn't say, and I didn't ask. We weren't that kind of partners." A ghost of something—regret, maybe, or just old frustration—crossed his face. "But he said when the time came, I'd know. That you'd figure it out before I would."

The Shard pulsed again.

[Voice match: 87% Drogan Hale. File: Black Squad. Status: unclassified. — A.V.]

Dry identification, stripped of sentiment.

"I appreciate the warning," Roan said carefully. "What I need to understand is what you're offering. Protection I can get elsewhere. Information I can gather on my own. If you're positioning yourself as an ally, I need to know what you want in return."

Brendt made a small sound, half protest, half disbelief at the directness.

Drogan studied him. Then, unexpectedly, he smiled. A thin expression, more acknowledgment than warmth.

"There it is. Aldric's boy through and through." He sat down heavily in his chair. "What I want is very simple. I want to finish what we started. The Black Squad. Whatever Aldric uncovered forty years ago, whatever got him killed and Kaelen erased—I want to know. I've spent fifteen years running into dead ends. Fifteen years of watching the Greels grow stronger and the truth stay buried." He leaned forward. "Help me find the truth, Vance. Help me understand what your father died for. That's what I want."

"And if the truth implicates people you respect?"

"I've been implicated before. I'll survive it again."

The offer hung in the air. Outside, morning training sessions were concluding—the distant clash of practice weapons, shouts of instructors, the ordinary sounds of the institute carrying on without them.

Roan ran the balance.

Drogan had resources. Drogan had connections. Drogan had fifteen years of dead ends that contained, by definition, information about what didn't work. That was data too.

But Drogan also had history. Loyalty. A version of the past that might not fit what the Shard contained.

"I need to think about it," Roan said finally.

"You have until the end of the week." Drogan stood, opening the door. "After that, the Sentinel Bureau's quarterly audit comes through. If anyone's going to ask questions about unusual spiritual signatures in student housing, I'd rather it be me directing the conversation than some Bureau analyst pulling data logs."

That was a deadline. Not subtle, but not unexpected either.

"Brendt." Roan's voice was quiet. "You heard all of that."

"Yeah." Brendt looked like he wanted to be anywhere else. "Yeah, I heard it."

"You understand what happens if any of that leaks."

"I understand." His jaw was tight. "I also understand that I'm apparently the only person in this room who didn't know my best friend is carrying around classified Vance technology and being hunted by an aristocratic murder family." He shook his head. "I'm going to need a lot more than a warning before I process any of this."

"Walk with me." Roan headed for the corridor. "I need to check something."

They moved through the institute's eastern corridors, past the training yards, toward the administrative wing where student records were processed and filed. The halls grew quieter as they approached the data vault, physical archives maintained alongside the digital systems, in case of network failures or deliberate tampering.

"What are we checking?" Brendt kept his voice low.

"House Ardent's financial records. Specifically, any transfers made in the last thirty days that passed through third-party conduits before arriving at the academy."

"You're going to hack—"

"I'm going to look. There's a difference."

The data vault entrance was guarded by a junior administrator who looked bored and overworked. Roan approached with the carefully casual confidence of someone who belonged there.

"Warden's office sent me to verify a discrepancy in student fee allocations," he said. "Code 7-B, physical archives, subsection C."

The administrator barely glanced at him. "Third shelf from the left, green folders. Don't make a mess."

They passed through into the archive room, a narrow space lined with floor-to-ceiling shelves, organized by house affiliation and date. Green folders meant financial records. Subsection C meant transfer documentation.

Roan found the right section quickly. House Ardent. Last thirty days.

His fingers moved through the files with practiced efficiency, scanning transfer amounts, recipient accounts, routing numbers. Nothing obviously connected to his duel sponsorship.

Then he found it.

A transfer made eighteen days ago. Fourteen thousand Spire Credits, moved from a shell account registered to a textile import company into a general discretionary fund. From there, two thousand credits had been allocated to the Student Affairs Office as an "instructor development grant."

The remaining twelve thousand had vanished into a sub-account Roan couldn't access from this terminal.

But the routing information was complete. And that routing information pointed to a financial institution in Greycross.

The same institution that had processed the Vance family accounts before the fire.

"The trail is too clean," Roan said softly, studying the routing string on his wrist terminal. "This isn't a mistake. Someone routed the money through Greycross deliberately."

Brendt leaned in. "A warning?"

"A message. Or a test." Roan photographed the document. "Either Selene is pointing me toward something, or the House Ardent has a leak I hadn't accounted for."

"You think Selene's connected to whoever started the fire?"

"I think Selene knows exactly who started the fire."

He replaced the folder and guided Brendt back toward the exit. They emerged into the corridor. The morning had aged into midday—harsh light, increasing foot traffic, the institute settling into its daily rhythms.

"Roan." Brendt stopped him with a hand on his arm. "What Drogan said about the Greels hunting you. What he said about your father. Is any of this going to change what you're doing here?"

"Define 'change.'"

"Are you going to be more careful? More paranoid? Are you going to stop whatever you're doing with that pendant—"

"I'm going to keep doing what I'm doing." Roan's voice was flat. "The Greels are a long way from finding anything. Selene is a variable I haven't fully solved. Drogan is an unknown. None of that changes the fundamental math."

"The fundamental math." Brendt laughed, a short, humorless sound. "Right. Because the arithmetic always tells you to keep risking your life doing things that could get you killed."

"The math tells me I'm more likely to die doing nothing than doing something." Roan resumed walking. "The tally also tells me that if I stop now, everything I've built becomes worthless. The Inversion. The reputation. The network." He paused at a junction, checking both corridors for listeners. "The only way forward is through. So I'm going through."

"And if 'through' means the Greels find you?"

"Then I'll deal with the Greels."

He said it without inflection. Brendt's Vitalis spiked again—fear response, concern, something close to grief.

"Dude. You're not invincible. You're not your father. You're just—"

"A seventeen-year-old with an impossible advantage and a target on his back." Roan met his friend's eyes. "I know what I am. I know what the risks are. And I'm still here, because the alternative is watching everything burn and calling it survival."

The corridor emptied around them. Somewhere distant, a bell rang—midday meal service beginning.

"Whatever happens," Brendt said quietly, "I'm in."

The statement landed without preamble. No conditions. No calculations. Just a choice, made in full knowledge of the consequences.

Roan held his gaze.

"I know."

It was the only answer that mattered.

- break -

The afternoon passed in fragments.

A training session Roan skipped by citing a fabricated medical appointment. An hour in the lower city purchasing three more batches of mislabeled reagents from the same street vendor who never asked questions. A conversation with the infirmary supply officer that yielded new information about Bureau audit protocols.

The Shard remained quiet after Drogan's office. His father's echoes had apparently said what needed saying.

But the pendant felt heavier than usual against his chest. A physical weight that mirrored the strategic burden of new information.

The Greels were hunting. Selene was positioned. Drogan was an asset or a trap. Brendt was loyal beyond reason. And somewhere in Greycross, a financial trail led back to the night Roan Vance lost everything.

He sat in his dormitory as evening fell, turning the pendant in his fingers.

His thumb passed three times over the worn edge. Aldric's old coin-reading trick, taught when Roan was six, for a lesson that had never been about coins.

The Codex Shard pulsed once—acknowledgment, or warning, or just the hum of its own existence.

[Four days until the audit. — A.V.]

Four days. Four days to decide about Drogan. Four days to prepare for Selene's challenge. Four days before someone might notice that the Vance family's only surviving heir was doing things with spiritual technology that shouldn't exist.

Four days wasn't enough time to fix anything.

But it was enough time to make sure the ledger stayed in his favor.

[Public ledger: not yet open. Adjacent records: stirring.]

[Audit window: four days.]

Roan didn't need to see the infirmary basement to know the supply officer would have flagged the batch. Routine processing, a delay of a day or two before it reached the Bureau. Long enough.

He didn't need to check the training rosters to guess the sparring scores would be adjusted, smoothed over, denied.

And he didn't need to look into the eastern wing to picture Drogan sitting in the dark with a glass he wasn't drinking.

The arithmetic of the institution was predictable, even if the actors weren't.

He turned off the light and began his evening meditation, letting the Vitalis flow through channels he was slowly, carefully, permanently reshaping.

The columns tallied cleanly.

But the numbers were incomplete.
