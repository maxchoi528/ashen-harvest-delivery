Chapter 15: Lyra's Birthday

The training yard smelled of iron and sweat. Roan counted nineteen bodies between him and the equipment rack, eighteen students dragging through morning forms, one instructor pretending not to watch. Lyra's birthday was in three days. The book was wrapped and hidden beneath his bedboard—old botanical engravings, useless for anything except proof that he remembered. That was the point.

His fingers found the weight of a practice blade, and the ledger started immediately. Nineteen students. One instructor. Three exits if he didn't care about looking like he was fleeing.

He cared.

"Arms up. You're not waving goodbye to your grandmother."

The instructor's voice carried across the yard. Roan adjusted his stance by exactly two degrees, enough to look engaged, not enough to stand out. The blade felt wrong in his hand. Everything felt wrong lately. His body had spent fourteen days learning what real damage felt like, and now practice weapons registered as background noise.

Fourteen days since he'd emptied himself reversing the Wraithmilk Tincture's chaos. His capacity had reset to three. Restored. Unspent. The balance wasn't there yet.

Brendt's position showed empty. Third row from the left, second from the front, where a large man shouldn't be able to disappear. But disappear he had. No message. No note.

Something was wrong.

Roan weighed the variables for what felt like the dozenth time. Brendt showed up. Brendt trained. Brendt occasionally forgot to eat but never forgot to show. Three possibilities: injury, detention, or choice. Injury would have drawn attention. Detention would have been communicated. Choice meant Brendt had decided to miss something he never skipped.

Roan watched the instructor's patrol pattern. Thirty-second rotation. One corner blind spot, four meters wide, lasting exactly eleven seconds. If he moved now, he could reach the administrative wing's side entrance in under a minute. No one would notice unless they were looking.

He was always looking.

The blind spot arrived. Roan set the practice blade on the rack with deliberate casualness and walked toward the equipment return, not toward the exit. Three steps. Pause. Check the instructor's position. Turn left toward the water station. Another pause. Turn toward the side corridor.

A clean cut through the route. He'd saved enough.

The corridor stretched empty ahead, its stone walls absorbing the morning's pale light. He knew this route. Mapped every corridor in his first week. Every blind spot, every shortcut, every place an instructor might corner him.

The administrative wing would have records. Attendance logs. Detention lists. The information existed; the question was accessing it without drawing attention.

The main office door appeared at the corridor's end. Light leaked underneath. Two clerks, if the shift rotation held. Neither would be watching the door with any particular attention.

Roan pushed through.

"Help you?" The first clerk, young, bored, half-asleep over a stack of forms, barely looked up.

"Record request." Roan's voice came out flat. Practice. "Holloway, Brendt. Disciplinary review prep."

The clerk blinked. "You're not his sponsor."

"His sponsor is Drogan Hale." True. "I'm handling paperwork while he's in session." Also true, technically. Drogan was teaching. Drogan had never explicitly said Roan couldn't handle administrative details. "The review is this week. Paperwork doesn't wait."

The clerk's eyes moved to the clock. Back to Roan. Calculation, not suspicion. Administrative staff loved efficiency more than protocol.

"Third cabinet. Alphabetical. Don't make a mess."

The cabinet stood where memory placed it. Holloway. Brendt. The file was thinner than expected.

Roan opened it against his body, angling to block the clerk's line of sight. Attendance records. Standard. Medical notes. One disciplinary report. Dated yesterday. Signed by Headmaster Edran Cole.

*Student Holloway, B. Restricted training status pending psychological evaluation. Mandatory session with school counselor scheduled 0800, 14th day. Indefinite continuation until clearance obtained.*

Roan's jaw tightened. No cause listed. No incident described. Just a psychological hold on his best friend for no visible reason.

Or rather, for a reason the document didn't want recorded.

He photographed the page with his memory—every stroke of the signature, every spacing in the dates—and slid the file back. The cabinet closed with a soft click.

"Find what you needed?"

"Yes." Roan walked toward the door. "Thank you."

The corridor welcomed him back with the same empty silence. His mind ran the sequences faster now. Brendt had been pulled from training yesterday evening. The evaluation happened this morning. If the counselor decided what the Headmaster apparently already wanted, Brendt could be locked in "treatment" for weeks.

Weeks without training. Weeks without access. Weeks where the white-bearded man's people could make a problem disappear through proper channels.

Roan reached the training yard's edge. The students were still running forms. No one looked at him. No one cared that he'd been gone for seven minutes.

No one except the figure leaning against the wall twenty meters to his left.

Theon Mercer watched Roan with an expression that tried for casual and landed somewhere around intensely interested. Dark hair. Sharp eyes. One of the few students who'd matched Roan in the preliminary rankings. His usual group was gone. Standing alone now.

"Busy morning." Theon's voice carried just far enough.

Roan stopped. Calculated distance. Angle of approach. Theon's stance suggested curiosity, not aggression. Theon's type was smart enough to see patterns. Not always smart enough to see he was being read.

"You're alone." Roan said it without inflection.

"People move on." Theon pushed off the wall. "Interesting thing about the tournament. Reveals who was carrying whom."

Roan waited. Theon had information. The cost of admission was trust. The reward was the infirmary's layout. The variables weighed out cleanly in Roan's head. The sister created a motive. The loneliness created a need for leverage. It wasn't friendship. It was alignment.

"Your friend." Theon nodded toward the empty space in the third row. "Holloway. Ran into some paperwork trouble, didn't he?"

Interesting.

"You know about his evaluation?"

"I know they pulled him yesterday. I know the order came from above the instructor level. And I know that kind of paperwork usually follows someone who asks the wrong questions at the wrong time."

"What questions?"

"Something about the infirmary records. Checking on a student who didn't exist anymore." Theon's gaze sharpened. "Heard someone mention it three days ago. Thought it was just gossip. Turns out gossip has a way of becoming official."

The infirmary. Brendt had been asking about the infirmary. The place where students disappeared and came back different, if they came back at all.

Roan updated the ledger. Brendt hadn't been targeted for what he knew about Roan. He'd been targeted for what he'd found on his own.

"Why tell me?"

"Because you're calculating right now." Theon smiled without warmth. "I don't like people who make problems disappear through paperwork. My sister spent two weeks in that infirmary last year. Came out different. Eyes that didn't track right. Won't talk about it. Won't even look at the east wing."

Roan studied Theon. The anger was real. The risk profile said useful but volatile. Exactly the kind of asset that could shift a situation or blow it apart.

"You're proposing an alliance."

"I'm proposing information sharing. You want to know why they grabbed your friend. I want to know what they did to my sister. Our interests overlap." Theon shrugged. "Or they don't. Your call."

The instructor's voice cut across the yard, barking corrections at a student whose form had slipped. The world continued its rotation.

Trust was the wrong word. Alliance. Calculation.

"What do you know about the infirmary's record system?"

Theon's smile returned, sharper now. "Enough to get us in. Not enough to get us out clean."

"Probably." Roan acknowledged. "But better than nothing."

The morning light had shifted. The sun stood higher, burning off the early chill. Students completed their forms and waited for dismissal.

"Afternoon." Theon said. "The east wing. Third floor. There's a maintenance access that bypasses the main checkpoints."

Roan nodded. He walked back toward his position in the yard. The gap in the third row remained empty. Brendt's absence carved a hole in the formation that everyone noticed and no one mentioned.

The instructor called dismissal. Students scattered toward the morning's next obligation. Roan stood still, letting the calculations settle into place.

Brendt had found something. Something worth disappearing for. And now Roan had an ally. A duplicate variable. A potential failure point.

His capacity sat at three three. Fresh. Ready. The moment where he needed it was approaching.

---

The east wing smelled like antiseptic and secrets.

Roan counted the stairs as he climbed. Forty-seven steps to the third floor, matching the architectural records he'd memorized. The maintenance access waited at the corridor's end, a service door most students didn't know existed. Theon's information had proven accurate so far.

Theon's silhouette appeared beside the door, darker against the corridor's dim light. "You're early."

"You're here." Roan stopped three meters away. Safe distance mapped. "Ready?"

"No." Theon smiled despite the word. "But ready enough."

The door opened without resistance. The corridor beyond stretched into shadow, emergency lighting casting pools of yellow at irregular intervals. The infirmary occupied the building's far end. Forty meters. Two junctions. One security checkpoint that Theon assured him operated on a rotation.

The first junction arrived. Clear. The second—

"Stop." Roan's hand caught Theon's shoulder, pulling them both into an alcove. Footsteps echoed from the connecting corridor. Not the lazy rhythm of a patrol. The deliberate pace of someone searching.

The footsteps passed. Continued. Faded.

"That was close."

"That was routine." Roan's voice stayed low. "They search this corridor twice per shift. We have eleven minutes before the next pass."

"You know the patrol schedules?"

"I know everything I can access." He moved forward, counting steps. The checkpoint door stood ahead. The clerk inside was handling intake forms, attention momentarily elsewhere. "Now."

They crossed in four seconds. The infirmary's main floor opened ahead, rows of beds separated by privacy curtains, medical equipment humming along the walls. Empty at this hour.

"The back section." Theon pointed toward a reinforced door marked RESTRICTED. "That's where they keep the ones who need watching."

Roan nodded. The door required keycard access. He watched the corridor's flow, noting the positions of two nurses conducting rounds. Neither had reason to check the restricted door in the next five minutes.

He pulled the lockpick from his sleeve. Acquired from a third-year who'd owed him a favor. Expensive exchange. The mechanism was simple, designed for emergency access rather than security. The leads buzzed. Eight seconds. Seven. Six.

A guard's radio crackled in the distance. "Fire alarm false. Returning to posts."

Roan read the time. Forty seconds until the perimeter was restored.

The lock clicked on thirty-seven.

Beyond lay a corridor different from the main floor. Sterile white walls. Recessed lighting without windows. Environment designed to disorient, to strip away reference points.

The first door on the left showed a name plate: HOLLOWAY, B.

Roan's hand pressed flat against the door's surface. No vibration from within. No sound. Brendt was either unconscious, sedated, or separated from his ability to make noise.

"We've got maybe three minutes before someone comes through." Theon's voice was tight.

"Two minutes." Roan's eyes moved to the door's lock. Electronic. Different from the corridor entrance. "I can't open this without alerting the system."

"Then we need a different approach." Theon was thinking now, weighing options. "What if we made noise somewhere else? Triggered an alert at the north end of the wing?"

Roan ran the probabilities. Division of attention. Security pulled toward the noise. A window opening in the chaos. Risky, but possible.

"Do it." He pulled a small canister from his pocket, smoke compound, purchased for exactly this kind of contingency. "North corridor. Thirty seconds."

Theon took it. Moved.

Roan counted heartbeats, measuring the seconds until the smoke detonated.

The alarm hit like a wave. Red lights flashing. Klaxons screaming. Security personnel rushing toward the north end, away from Brendt's room.

Roan pressed the electronic lock. Still engaged. But the system was overloaded now, attention divided, operators focused on the false alarm.

He pulled the lockpick again. Different mechanism. More complex. The operators weren't watching.

Eleven seconds. Twelve. Thirteen.

The lock clicked.

The door opened onto a small room. White walls. Single bed. Brendt on the mattress, wrists restrained, eyes closed.

Unconscious. Not sedated. Roan checked the IV line, found it disconnected. Someone had wanted Brendt awake, then changed their mind.

"Roan?" Brendt's voice rasped, eyes opening. Confusion, then recognition. "What—"

"No time." Roan worked the restraints, fingers finding the releases. "Can you walk?"

"Define walk."

"Legs functional?"

"Mostly." Brendt sat up, swaying. "They asked questions. About you. About what you did in the tournament. I didn't tell them anything."

Roan updated the ledger. Brendt had been detained because of Roan's own actions, confirmed. The evaluation was interrogation, confirmed. The source was someone with access and authority, likely Edran Cole or someone above him.

"Move." Roan pulled Brendt toward the door. "We have sixty seconds before they realize the alarm was wrong."

They made it to the restricted corridor's junction. The klaxons continued their wail. Security hadn't returned yet, but the window was closing.

A guard rounded the corner ahead.

Roan pulled Brendt into a storage alcove, hand pressing his friend's mouth shut. The guard's light swept across the floor. Inches from their boots. Paused. The guard grunted and moved on.

Roan released Brendt. "Cut that closer than the math liked."

They crossed the main floor in the confusion. The checkpoint door stood closed but no longer monitored. They emerged into the public corridor. Kept walking. Normal pace. Nothing to suggest escape.

The east wing's exterior door waited. Beyond it, the afternoon light, the training yards, the world that continued its rotation while Roan pulled his best friend out of a hole.

"Roan." Brendt's voice caught. "There's something else. They flagged the ones who performed above threshold in the event. Anyone who tested the Greel envoy. But that wasn't the real reason. The excuse was a student who doesn't exist. A file in the infirmary that doesn't match the living body. They knew I was looking. The tournament just gave them the paperwork angle."

Roan's blood cooled.

The Greel envoy. The fighter Roan had faced in the tournament, faced and deliberately lost to, playing low exactly as planned. But "tested" meant something different. It meant his performance had been too good. Drawn too much attention. Showed exactly what he'd tried to hide.

The goal: stay low. The obstacle: his own success. The stakes: everything.

"Go." Roan pulled Brendt forward. "We have ninety days until the next evaluation cycle. Ninety days to find who is watching and what they want."

---

The Vance apartments were quiet when he arrived.

Lyra was at the table. A single candle burning on a small pastry. Mira watched from the doorway, arms folded, eyes saying everything her mouth didn't.

"You came." Lyra's voice was flat. Not accusing. Just stating a fact that had surprised her.

Roan placed the book on the table. Old botanical engravings. Flora no one had seen in a century. Useless for anything except saying he remembered. "Late."

She opened it. Her fingers traced the spine. She didn't say anything for a long moment.

Roan sat across from her. The clock in his head was still ticking. Brendt's restraints. Theon's sharp eyes. The infirmary's antiseptic scent clinging to his clothes.

The candle flickered.

"Thank you." Her voice was soft.

Roan nodded. The moment stretched.

Mira caught his eye. Her gaze was a question he couldn't answer. Not yet. Maybe not ever.

He stayed until the candle burned low. Lyra fell asleep on the couch, the book open on her chest. Mira covered her with a blanket.

Roan stood in the doorway. The arithmetic hummed beneath his skin. Ninety days. A surveillance system learning his shape. A sister sleeping with a book of useless plants pressed against her heart.

His capacity sat at three three. Fresh. Ready.

The hunt wouldn't wait. But it had let him choose his ground.
