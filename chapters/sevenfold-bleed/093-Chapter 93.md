Chapter 93: A Controlled Loss

The morning bell found Roan already awake, sitting on the edge of his bed with his eyes closed. Not meditation. He was counting.

Six days since the Ashford match. Five since he'd stopped seeing silver sparks at the edge of his vision when he opened his eyes too fast. Three since Brendt had stopped looking at him like he might shatter. Two since Warden Drogan had called him into the training hall and spent forty minutes watching him run through forms without saying a word.

The answer came fast: everyone was waiting for him to break. The question was whether they were waiting because they expected him to, or because they were afraid he would.

The black iron anchor hung cold against his sternum. He hadn't worn it openly since the Forged breakthrough, too many questions, but he'd kept it close. Tucked beneath his shirt, resting against skin that still felt too tight for his bones.

*Numbers were harder to bribe than people.*

He opened his eyes and reached for the small pouch on his nightstand. Two Wraithmilk Tinctures remained from the batch he'd purchased two weeks ago. Expired by two days. The vendor had marked them down to half-price, certain no one would buy them.

Roan had bought all twelve.

His fingers closed around one vial. The liquid inside had started to cloud at the edges, oxidation beginning. Normal users would drink these and experience the advertised surge: mental acuity jumping, sensory range expanding, the world becoming sharper and clearer. And then, three days later, the backlash would hit. The tremors. The rages. The moments where they couldn't tell their own thoughts from the voices they were hearing.

He'd already used eight of them. His body had converted each dose before the toxicity could establish itself, turning the poison into fuel and the backlash into build-up. His mental radius had expanded by nearly three meters in two weeks, sitting now at a comfortable nine meters of active scanning range, better than most Tempered practitioners, and he'd done it without anyone knowing he was training at all.

*Aldric would have called this cheating.*

The thought arrived without invitation. He'd gotten used to those, little fragments of his father's voice appearing in his mind at odd moments. They weren't the Codex Shard's formal prompts, just... Echoes. The way someone's handwriting becomes familiar after enough practice.

He wasn't sure if he was comfortable with that or not.

The vial was cold against his palm. He'd calculated the risk: expired Wraithmilk had a 50/50 chance of unpredictable side effects. The Inversion system could handle unknown inputs with a 50% failure rate. He was gambling that the upside, the continued expansion of his mental radius, would outweigh the downside of a failed conversion.

The gamble was acceptable.

He uncorked the vial and drank.

The taste was bitter, metallic, like swallowing a copper coin with a film of something organic coating it. His body recognized the pattern immediately: alkaloid compounds, trace metals, the protein chains that triggered the initial surge. His system began processing before the taste had even faded.

And then. Pain.

Not the sharp sting of a Sidewound, the clean signal his Codex Shard was designed to intercept. This was deeper. Duller. Spreading through his temples like someone had wrapped a hot cloth around his skull and left it there.

The Inversion system tried to engage and failed.

[Sidewound type unrecognized.]
[Inversion failed. Pain retained.]

Roan's jaw tightened. He kept his breathing even, four seconds in, four seconds out, the pattern he'd trained since childhood, while the pressure built behind his eyes. His mental radius contracted involuntarily, shrinking from nine meters to six, then to four.

The expired tincture wasn't just producing the normal side effects. It was producing mutations. Compounds that broke down differently in an expired solution, creating derivatives the Codex Shard couldn't recognize.

He could wait it out. Ride the wave, let his body process the toxins naturally, and lose a day of training in exchange for the certainty that nothing worse happened. That was the conservative choice. The safe choice.

The conservative choice was for people who could afford to be safe.

He reached for the second vial.

The logic was sound even as his vision started to blur. If one dose was unpredictable, two doses in rapid succession might create a combined effect the Codex Shard could recognize. The system had failed on the specific compounds, but the base alkaloids were still present in both. A second wave might establish a pattern.

Or it might accelerate the mutations and kill him.

The figures said the risk was acceptable. The math always said the risk was acceptable. That was the problem with math, it didn't account for the times when being wrong meant not existing anymore.

He drank.

The second wave hit harder.

Roan's hand spasmed, the empty vial clattering against the floor. His mental radius collapsed to nothing, his consciousness shrinking inward until he couldn't feel the walls of the room anymore, couldn't feel the presence of the other students two floors down, couldn't feel anything except the pressure and the heat and the growing certainty that he'd made a mistake.

The heat peaked. Loss of vision, complete sensory blackout, the sensation of falling through a space that had no floor. His body tried to convulse and found that it couldn't; something had locked his muscles in place, keeping him rigid and still while the world burned around him.

Then, just as suddenly, it stopped.

[Compound pattern recognized. Inversion successful. Vitalis +180. Mental radius expanded to 13 meters. All residual effects neutralized.]

The pain receded like a tide going out, leaving him shivering on the bed, his shirt soaked through with sweat. His mental radius snapped back to full strength, eleven meters now, wider than before, and kept expanding. Twelve. Thirteen.

The Codex Shard pulsed warm from his spine.

*That was stupid.*

His father's voice again, that particular tone of disappointed resignation that Roan had heard a thousand times as a child. When he'd jumped off the roof to see if he could fly. When he'd mixed household chemicals to see what would happen. When he'd walked into Black Spire at fifteen with nothing but a forged recommendation letter and a plan that should have gotten him killed.

Roan sat up slowly, testing his limbs. Everything responded. His vision was clear. His thoughts were sharp.

Thirteen meters. The expansion had been worth the risk.

He allowed himself thirty seconds of satisfaction before he started calculating the cost. The process had been clean, but the gain wouldn't go unnoticed. Purchase records, dosage patterns, the spread of his mental radius — each was a thread someone could pull. The exposure risk was real. But for now, he had thirteen meters of active range, which meant he could sense the three people approaching his door before they'd even knocked.

Two he recognized: Brendt's steady presence, and something familiar but harder to place, a weight that moved like a person but felt like a building.

The third was wrong.

Not a threat, exactly. Just wrong. A presence that didn't match anything in his memory, that felt like looking at a photograph taken slightly out of focus.

Three knocks.

"Roan?" Brendt's voice, carefully neutral. "You're wanted at the Administrator's Hall. They sent an escort."

They. Not who. An escort. Someone official.

He stood, ran a hand through his hair, and opened the door.

Brendt stood on the left, his face doing the thing it did when he was worried but didn't want to show it. Drogan stood on the right, his expression unreadable, the Iron Hand of Hale glinting dully in the corridor's artificial light.

And in the middle,

"You must be Roan Vance." The woman smiled like she was meeting an old friend. "I've heard so much about you."

She was tall, angular, her hair pulled back in a severe knot that made her face look like it had been carved from something harder than flesh. Her Vitalis presence was immaculate, not a single fluctuation, not the slightest leak. She'd trained her output so completely that she felt almost like empty space.

Almost. But Roan had thirteen meters now, and what he sensed at that range wasn't her Vitalis output but her Vitalis control. And her control was perfect because she'd built it that way on purpose, layer after layer of trained suppression creating a surface that looked like calm water but was a frozen lake with very deep water underneath.

Hardened tier. Higher. Someone who could kill him before he finished taking a breath.

"My name is Selene Ardent," she continued, apparently not requiring a response. "I'm here from the Conclave's Regional Oversight Division. We need to discuss your recent performance in the Trials."

*Regional Oversight Division.* The words landed in his mind and started a cascade of associations. ROD was the investigative arm of the seven great houses, the people who got sent in when something didn't add up. When resources went missing. When students died in circumstances that seemed accidental. When someone's progress didn't match their documented training.

She was here about the Inversion.

Not directly, nothing so crude. But somewhere in the mountain of paperwork that Black Spire generated, his name had appeared enough times that someone had decided to pull the thread. The expired Wraithmilk purchases. The unexplained recovery from the Ashford match. The way he'd jumped two sub-stages in a single week without any recorded dosage adjustments.

Selene Ardent was good at her job. She'd gotten this assignment because she was good at her job. And she was standing in front of him now, wearing a smile that people used when they already knew the answer and were just waiting for you to lie about it.

Roan looked at Brendt. Brendt's jaw was tight, the specific way it got tight when he was furious about something he couldn't say. Drogan hadn't moved at all, which was worse. When Drogan went still, it meant he was calculating whether violence was worth the consequences.

Neither of them had been given a choice about this escort.

"I'd be happy to answer any questions," Roan said. His voice was level. He was almost proud of that. "Though I'm not sure what there is to discuss. I trained hard. I performed adequately in the Trials. That's the extent of it."

Selene's smile didn't waver. "Adequately. Yes. That's one word for it." She gestured down the corridor. "Shall we? The Administrator's office is a bit more private than the hallway, and I expect this conversation will take some time."

Roan stepped out of his room and fell into step beside her. Brendt and Drogan followed at a careful distance, not close enough to interfere, not far enough to abandon.

"Tell me," Selene said, her voice conversational, "do you know why I requested Warden Hale and your friend Brendt as escorts?"

He didn't like the question. "Because you wanted witnesses."

"Because I wanted to make sure you understood that I know both of them. Drogan Hale and I have a history; he was the one who recommended I focus on investigative work rather than field operations. Said I had a mind for patterns." Her eyes stayed forward. "And Brendt Holloway's family has done business with my house for three generations. His father consulted on a case I worked two years ago involving counterfeit Vitalis distribution."

She was telling him she had context. She was telling him she knew things he didn't know she knew. She was telling him that this wasn't a random inspection, it was a targeted investigation built on connections he hadn't accounted for.

"Your father's name came up in my preliminary research," Selene continued. "Aldric Vance. Died under circumstances the Sentinel Bureau never fully explained. No body recovered without formal inquiry. Just a note in the files that said 'deceased, cause unclassified.'"

Roan's stride didn't falter. "My father was a complex man. I assume the Bureau found his death equally complex."

"I assume they found it convenient." She glanced at him sidelong. "The Vance family had certain resources that House Ardent always found interesting. Techniques. Approaches to the Discipline that didn't match the standard curriculum. When Aldric died, those resources disappeared along with him."

They'd reached the elevator. The doors slid open, revealing an empty car. Selene stepped in and turned to face him.

"I don't believe in coincidences, Mr. Vance. I believe in patterns. And in the last six months, your pattern has developed several irregularities that don't fit together." She held up a hand and began ticking points off her fingers. "Unrecorded recovery from injuries that should have required weeks of rest. Purchase history that suggests regular consumption of products you should have no interest in. Performance metrics that jump without corresponding increases in documented training time."

The elevator doors began to close. Roan stepped inside.

"And I'm supposed to believe," Selene finished, "that a seventeen-year-old student with no family support and limited resources somehow achieved all of this through merit alone?"

The doors closed fully. They began to rise.

"You're assuming I achieved it alone," Roan said. "That's the first mistake."

Selene's smile flickered, just for a moment, a crack in the perfect surface. "Explain."

"I'm not going to explain. I'm going to tell you what I think you already know." He met her eyes. "My father left me something before he disappeared. I don't fully understand it. I'm not sure anyone does. But it works, and I'm not going to apologize for using it."

Silence.

The elevator continued climbing. Seven floors. Eight. Nine.

"You're either very brave or very stupid," Selene said finally. "Admitting you have an inheritance you can't explain. Do you understand what the Conclave does with unlicensed techniques?"

"I understand what they do with unlicensed practitioners." He kept his voice even. "But here's the thing: you're here asking questions, not arresting me. Which means you don't have enough evidence to justify that conclusion. And if you did, you wouldn't be having this conversation in an elevator."

"I could have this conversation anywhere."

"Exactly. So why here?" He tilted his head. "Because you want to know what it is before you decide what to do with it. Because you're curious. Because you've spent years learning to see patterns, and my pattern is something you haven't seen before, and that bothers you."

The elevator slowed. Tenth floor. The Administrator's level.

"You want to understand," Roan said. "So do I. Maybe we can help each other."

Selene's expression had gone completely neutral now, no smile, no calculating assessment, just a flat surface that revealed nothing. "Help each other how?"

"I don't know yet. But I'm willing to find out."

The doors opened. The Administrator's hallway stretched before them, all polished stone and muted lighting.

Selene stepped out and turned to face him.

"My preliminary report is due in three days," she said. "In that time, I will verify every claim you've made and investigate every lead I can find. If I determine that you are operating outside the bounds of sanctioned practice, I will recommend your immediate expulsion and transfer to the Sentinel Bureau for formal inquiry."

"Understood."

"If I determine that you are operating within bounds but with an undocumented advantage, I will recommend your formal registration as a licensed practitioner with the Conclave, which comes with certain... Reporting requirements."

"Reasonable."

"And if I determine that you are operating with something genuinely new, something that doesn't fit any existing category—" She paused. "Then I will write a very interesting report that will get me promoted and transferred, then killed, because the Conclave doesn't like interesting."

"Also reasonable."

"I'm glad you think so." She extended a hand. "Three days, Mr. Vance. I suggest you spend them deciding how much of the truth you're willing to tell."

Roan took her hand. Her grip was firm, controlled, the grip of someone who knew exactly how much pressure they were applying.

"I look forward to your report," he said.

She released his hand and walked down the hallway, disappearing around a corner. A moment later, the soft sound of a door opening and closing.

Brendt appeared at his elbow. "What the hell was that?"

Roan watched the empty hallway. "That was someone I can't lie to and can't afford to fight." He exhaled slowly. "Also someone who could become an ally if I play this correctly."

"Play it correctly," Brendt repeated flatly. "Roan, she just spent the last ten minutes threatening your entire future."

"She spent the last ten minutes revealing that she knows about my father, has resources to investigate, and is professionally curious enough to delay formal action in favor of understanding the situation." He started walking. "That's leverage. I just need to figure out what she wants badly enough to look the other way."

Drogan fell into step on his other side. The Warden's face was still unreadable, but something in his posture had shifted. Not relaxed. Drogan didn't relax, but less rigid. Like he'd decided the immediate threat had passed.

"You handled that well," Drogan said quietly. "Better than I expected."

Roan glanced at him. "You expected me to panic."

"I expected you to lie. She would have known, and everything would've been worse from there." Drogan's eyes met his. "You told her the truth, or something close enough that she couldn't pin down the exact shape of it. That's... Not what most people would have done."

"Most people don't have a system that limits how much they can lie about their own capabilities." The words came out before he could stop them. He didn't usually acknowledge the Inversion so openly.

Drogan's eyebrow rose slightly. "No. They don't."

They'd reached the end of the hallway. The elevator waited, doors closed, the soft hum of machinery the only sound.

"Brendt," Roan said, "I need you to do something for me."

"When you say 'do something,' I need to know if it involves me breaking any rules, because I feel like we're at a point where I should start asking."

"It involves you talking to your father about Selene Ardent. Specifically, whether House Ardent has any outstanding interests in Aldric Vance's research."

Brendt went quiet for a moment. "My father's going to ask why."

"Tell him the truth. Partially. Say that an investigator from the Conclave is looking into my background and you want to know if there's anything your family knows that could help me prepare."

"That's also going to raise questions."

"I know. But I'd rather have questions from a friendly house than surprises from a hostile one." Roan pressed the elevator button. "Selene told me she investigated my father before. If House Ardent has information I don't have, I need to know what it is before I can use it."

The elevator arrived. They stepped in.

"Roan," Brendt said slowly, "you know what this sounds like, right? This sounds like you're building a case against someone who hasn't done anything to you yet."

"She investigated my father. She came to Black Spire with a specific purpose. She's not here by accident." The doors closed. "People like her don't waste time on accidents. Whatever brought her here, it has to do with something bigger than my exam scores."

"And if it does?"

"Then I need to understand it before it understands me."

The elevator descended.

His room felt smaller when he returned to it. The empty Wraithmilk vials were still on the floor where they'd fallen. He picked them up and tucked them into his wastebin, not disposed of, just hidden. Evidence of anything was evidence that could be used against him.

The Codex Shard pulsed warm from his spine.

[Mental Radius: 13 meters.]
[Conversion yield: +180 Vitalis.]
[Status: stable. No residual instability.]

He'd expected a warning, some token of the risk he'd taken. But the system registered the process as clean. No compound instability. No pending complications. The Inversion had handled everything.

That was almost more worrying. Clean conversions meant no latent markers, but they also meant no built-in defense against the next variable.

He sat on his bed and began running through scenarios.

Best case: She found nothing actionable, wrote a clean report, and he continued training without interference. Moderate case: She found enough to demand registration, adding reporting requirements to his already complicated situation. Worst case: She found enough to justify a full investigation, and suddenly every secret he'd been keeping became a point of evidence against him.

None of those scenarios included the possibility that she became an ally. That required her wanting something he could provide and being willing to overlook what he couldn't explain. That required him offering her something useful enough to justify the risk.

*Intelligence*, his father's voice whispered. *You can't buy what she's selling. But you can trade for it.*

Roan closed his eyes.

Too many variables were still missing. He was missing variables, and he didn't know which ones mattered most.

Three days to find out.
