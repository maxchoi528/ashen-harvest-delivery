Chapter 139: The First Hollow City

Brendt's injury didn't look dramatic enough for the amount of trouble it represented.

Three punctures above the ankle. Minor bleeding. Skin gray around the edges. No exposed bone, no shredded muscle, no satisfying wound one could point at and say, yes, that's the problem. Roan had seen the pattern before. Bodies didn't die where the wound looked worst. They died from small failures in inconvenient places.

Roan disliked that about bodies.

He pressed two fingers above Brendt's ankle and fed a narrow thread of Vitalis into the tissue.

The wound answered.

Not like ordinary infection. Ordinary infection had heat, swelling, direction. Husk contamination had intent. The gray tissue didn't spread randomly through blood. It moved along Vitalis channels first, looking for reinforcement patterns, attempting to convince the body that the foreign structure belonged there.

A bureaucratic poison.

That felt insulting.

Brendt watched him with forced patience. His hands gripped the edge of the cot hard enough to whiten his knuckles. The signal station's emergency lamps painted everyone in flat blue light. Outside, Husks scraped and clicked against the ward line but didn't attack directly. Not yet. Either they had learned the outer wards hurt, or something had told them to wait.

Waiting enemies were worse than charging ones.

"Scale?" Drogan asked.

Roan didn't look up. "Localized for now. Entered three superficial channels. One deeper contact near the tendon. No core access."

Brendt swallowed. "That sounded almost reassuring until the tendon part."

"Don't move."

"That part was reassuringly clear."

Ines knelt by the station's main control panel, one ear toward the conversation and both hands inside an access hatch. Her rifle lay beside her, reassembled. She had said nothing since Brendt removed the boot. That meant she either cared or was calculating how much a contaminated teammate reduced salvage odds.

Knowing Ines, both.

Drogan opened a field case and removed a sealed vial of amber fluid. "Cleanse wash."

"No," Roan said.

The old man's eyes narrowed. "It works on Husk rot."

"Low-tier Husk rot. This is copying his Vitalis structure. Wash will burn surface tissue and drive the deeper thread inward."

"You know that how?"

"Because the contamination moved away from my probe before contact. It recognizes hostile treatment."

Brendt stared at the wound. "My ankle is being politically evasive."

"Yes."

"I hate this place."

"Reasonable."

Drogan capped the vial slowly. "Options."

Roan liked that about him. Drogan objected, glared, withheld information, and occasionally treated mentorship as an endurance sport for both parties, but when Roan gave a technical assessment under pressure, he didn't waste time protecting his pride.

"Option one," Roan said. "Cut out contaminated tissue. Fast, simple, risks tendon damage. He walks badly for days, longer."

Brendt looked at Drogan. "Define badly."

"Don't ask questions you don't want answered," Drogan said.

"I specifically want that one answered."

"Then ask later."

Roan continued. "Option two: flood the area with controlled Vitalis and force contamination into a visible thread. Then remove. Lower structural damage. Higher pain, requires Brendt not to thrash."

Brendt breathed in. "I can do that."

"I know."

That wasn't comfort. It was assessment. Still, Brendt's grip loosened by half a fraction.

"Option three," Drogan said.

Roan paused.

The Codex Shard waited behind his spine like a loaded clause.

"Inversion," he said.

Drogan's face hardened. "No."

Brendt looked between them. "That sounded like a useful no."

"Uncertain outcome," Roan said. "The Shard could trigger adaptation, but the result might be useful resistance — or a lingering structural quirk I'd rather not risk."

Ines glanced over from the panel. "So option two?"

"Yes."

Brendt closed his eyes. "I withdraw my interest in option three."

"Good. It was mostly included for completeness."

"Your completeness needs bedside manner."

"My bedside manner is why you're informed before the cutting starts."

"Terrifyingly fair."

Drogan handed Roan a narrow clamp, sealant, and a roll of binding wire. "Option two."

Roan nodded.

"Brendt," he said. "Bite this."

Brendt accepted the folded leather strip. Then he removed it and asked, "Is this sterilized?"

Roan stared at him.

"Right," Brendt said, and put it back between his teeth.

The procedure took eleven minutes.

It would have taken seven if Brendt had been unconscious, but unconscious patients couldn't adjust their own Vitalis flow on command. Roan needed him awake. That was unfortunate for Brendt and efficient for the outcome.

Roan fed a thread of his own Vitalis near the wound, not into it, while Drogan stabilized the surrounding tissue. Brendt pushed from inside, following Roan's instructions through clenched teeth.

"Less force."

Brendt's breath hitched.

"Less. Not none."

A muffled sound came through the leather.

"I assume that was insulting. Correct, but insulting. Again."

The gray infection resisted.

It retreated from Roan's probe, burrowed toward Brendt's tendon, then met Drogan's pressure from the other side and folded back on itself. Ines, having apparently decided the control panel could survive without her for three minutes, held a lamp close enough for Roan to see the exact moment the contamination formed a visible thread.

It looked like a hair made of ash.

Roan caught it with the clamp and pulled.

Brendt went rigid.

Drogan pressed one hand on his shin. "Still."

The thread came out slowly, too long for the size of the wound. That was unpleasant. It kept coming, coiling around the clamp, gray-black and wet, until finally the last fiber slipped free with a faint sound like tearing silk.

Roan dropped it into an alcohol capsule.

The capsule hissed.

[Husk contamination removed.]

[Residual trace: 7%.]

[Inversion not triggered.]

Roan exhaled through his nose.

"Done," he said.

Brendt spat out the leather. "I hate you less than I hate the ankle."

"Progress."

"Don't get sentimental."

"Unlikely."

Roan sealed the punctures and bound the ankle. Brendt would walk. Poorly, but he would walk. The residual trace could be monitored. If it flared, they had bigger problems.

They already had bigger problems, technically, but one didn't need to invite hierarchy into every disaster.

Outside, the clicking stopped.

The station itself tried to be helpful in the manner of abandoned military infrastructure: grudgingly and incompletely, plus with several hazards disguised as safeguards. A wall cabinet held emergency blankets that had fused into a single silver brick. The water purifier lit green and produced one drop of black liquid before Ines disconnected it with the expression of someone terminating a bad employee. A medical printer near the cot accepted Drogan's command code, considered for twelve seconds, and dispensed a bandage roll labeled for equine use.

Brendt stared at it. "Do I want to know why this station stocks horse bandages?"

"No," Roan said.

"Is it because the Rift changed the label?"

"Possibly."

"Is it because Sentinel procurement is terrible?"

Drogan, from the control panel, said, "."

The answer seemed to comfort Brendt. Roan supposed familiar incompetence had value under extreme conditions.

Roan made him drink half a ration bottle and eat two measured bites of salt bar. Brendt objected to both, which meant his blood pressure hadn't collapsed and his personality remained intact.

"I'm not hungry."

"Irrelevant."

"That bar tastes like mineral dust and regret."

"Also irrelevant."

"You have a gift for care."

"Yes. It's mostly logistics."

Brendt chewed with visible resentment. Good. Resentment anchored attention outward. People in pain who turned inward missed symptoms.

Roan checked the wound again after the binding settled. The residual gray had drawn into three tiny dots around the punctures, each the size of a pinhead. Stable. Not gone. Stable was enough for the next hour and a lie for any longer period. He marked the skin around each dot with a charcoal ring so expansion would show immediately.

Brendt watched him do it. "That's not ominous at all."

"It's measurement."

"Measurement can be ominous."

"Only if the result is bad."

"And is it?"

Roan held his gaze for half a second too long to be comforting and not long enough to be cruel. "Not yet."

Brendt nodded once.

That was one of the reasons Roan trusted him. Brendt didn't enjoy ugly answers, but he could use them. Most people preferred false reassurance until reality charged interest.

Ines restored one bank of internal shutters. Steel plates dropped over two windows with a bang that made Brendt's shield twitch up. She looked back at him.

"Sorry."

"I nearly blocked architecture," Brendt said. "This is where I am as a person."

"Architecture has attacked us today."

"Thank you for validating me."

Roan moved to the largest window slit and checked the street through the blue ward shimmer. The Husks beyond it had changed spacing. He counted twelve visible silhouettes and enough roof movement to suggest twice that. None tested the door now. They crouched along lines that corresponded to the station's blind spots. If the wards failed, the first rush would come from left roof, right platform, and under the rail stairs.

He drew the layout in his notebook.

Drogan noticed. "Planning to defend the station?"

"Planning to leave it. Same map."

"Not the same."

"It's if departure fails."

Ines gave a quiet laugh from the panel.

Drogan didn't, but he didn't argue either.

Roan added likely breach points, then a note: pack hesitates at sustained ward contact; not suicidal; command node outside visual range. If the pack had a commander, killing random Husks would only reduce pressure temporarily. Killing or disrupting command would matter. Unfortunately, command hadn't presented itself politely. Sensible of it.

He disliked sensible enemies too.

A faint vibration passed through the floor.

Everyone stopped.

The station lights dimmed, recovered, then shifted from blue to a sickly white for one pulse. The dead speaker crackled, though Ines had cut the feed line.

She looked at the severed cable in her hand. "That's rude."

The speaker whispered something too distorted to parse.

Roan looked at Drogan.

The old man looked back.

This time he didn't step toward it.

Improvement.

The whisper became clearer for half a word: "...tower..."

Ines opened the panel and ripped out the entire audio crystal. The speaker kept whispering for two more seconds before dying.

Brendt exhaled. "I want it noted that destroying haunted electronics is an excellent policy."

"Noted," Roan said.

"You mean that one?"

"Yes."

"Progress."

Roan looked at the dead speaker. The voice had persisted without the crystal, which meant the system hadn't been transmitting sound. It had been providing a shape for something else to use. That distinction mattered. Destroying every speaker in the city wouldn't remove the thing behind them. It might, however, reduce its vocabulary.

That was still useful.

"Any more audio fixtures?" he asked.

Ines checked the ceiling. "Two external horns, one internal emergency chime."

"Disable them."

"Already doing it."

Brendt raised a hand from the cot. "Can we disable the city?"

"Working list," Roan said.

"That was almost reassuring."

The wound dots on Brendt's ankle didn't spread during the next fifteen minutes. Roan accepted that as permission to address the other problem in the room.

Everyone noticed.

The silence pressed against the warded walls.

Ines returned to the control panel. "That's either good or the opposite of good."

"Opposite," Drogan said.

A speaker near the ceiling crackled.

Brendt flinched.

The signal station's old public-address system had no reason to work. The panel was half-dead. The antenna tower had collapsed. The place had been sealed for emergency use, not conversation.

The speaker crackled again.

Then a voice came through.

"—ogan?"

Static swallowed the rest.

Drogan went very still.

Roan looked at him.

The old instructor's hand had closed around his blade. Not drawn. Just closed.

The speaker hissed.

"Drogan Vell."

Brendt's eyes widened.

Ines's hands froze above the controls.

Roan didn't look away from Drogan. Vell. Not a name Drogan used. Not publicly. Roan had never heard him use any surname beyond 'Hale.' The voice knew something else. The Vell patriarch used that name. Old House records used it. Drogan didn't.

The voice from the speaker was male, low, distorted by static and distance. It didn't sound like a Husk. That wasn't reassuring. Rule four: anything that speaks with a human voice in there's lying until proven otherwise.

Drogan lifted his head slowly. "Don't answer."

"Wasn't planning to," Brendt said.

The speaker crackled. "You came back."

Drogan's jaw tightened.

Roan added several things together.

The pack had tried to force them into the culvert. The signal station lock had rejected Drogan's access despite his history. The public-address system knew his old name. This wasn't random Rift mimicry. Someone or something had linked Drogan's identity to this place.

Possibilities: corrupted station memory, Hollowkin intelligence, human observer, Aldric-linked defense, Founding Seven bait, or Drogan's past coming due with poor timing.

Likely more than one.

The voice returned.

"You left him under the tower."

Drogan moved.

Not much. One step toward the speaker.

Roan stepped in front of him.

The old man's eyes snapped down to him.

"No," Roan said.

Drogan's voice came out flat. "Move."

"No."

"Boy."

"It wants you emotional. You're obliging."

For a second, Drogan looked like he might hit him. Roan had considered that. Drogan hitting him was acceptable. Drogan opening the door, breaking the panel, or answering a voice that used old guilt as bait wasn't.

Brendt, pale and sweating on the cot, said, "For the record, I also vote against listening to the murder speaker."

Ines found the audio feed line and cut it.

The speaker died mid-crackle.

Drogan stared at the dead speaker.

Roan watched him, not the ceiling.

"Under what tower?" Roan asked.

No answer.

"Drogan."

The old man turned away. "Not now."

"That phrase is accumulating interest."

"Then charge me later."

Roan almost did. There were ways to press. Mention Aldric. Mention Vell. Mention that the Rift had already used Drogan's old name and therefore ignorance had become a tactical liability.

He didn't, because Brendt's ankle had begun bleeding again through the sealant.

Priorities were irritating but useful.

Roan returned to the cot and reinforced the binding.

The ankle also became a diagnostic tool, which Brendt accepted with poor grace.

Roan made him cycle a thread of Vitalis through the injured foot every ten minutes while Ines watched the panel for external pressure changes. If the gray trace responded to Brendt's own energy, they would know before it reached anything central. If it responded to the station speaker, they would also know, and then Roan would cut something more important than a sock.

"What exactly am I looking for?" Brendt asked during the third cycle.

"Anything that feels like your ankle answering before you ask."

He stared. "That isn't a sensation I have a category for."

"Create one."

"I shall call it nightmare."

"Fine. Report nightmare above baseline."

Ines, from the panel, said, "That may be the most accurate field-medicine instruction I've heard."

Drogan didn't smile, but one corner of his mouth considered treason.

The fourth cycle produced a twitch in the smallest toe and no gray expansion. The fifth produced sweat along Brendt's hairline and a complaint that the wound felt cold from the inside. Roan checked it, found the trace stable, and decided not to mention that internal cold often preceded deeper contamination in lower-tier cases. The fact wouldn't change procedure. Unused fear was just weight.

He did tell Drogan with a look.

Drogan understood and moved one stimulant injector from his own kit to Roan's without comment.

Brendt saw that too. "I am choosing to believe that was routine."

"Good choice," Roan said.

"Was it?"

"No."

"I walked into that."

"Carefully, with the uninjured foot."

The joke was bad enough to make Brendt laugh once. The laugh cut off halfway when the ankle pulled. Still worth it. A person laughing at pain hadn't yet surrendered the room to it.

Ines also found a maintenance log buried in the station cache.

Most entries were corrupted into blocks of symbols, but three survived in pieces. The first listed ward calibration failures. The second noted missing salvage teams and recommended suspension of student permits, a recommendation someone had apparently ignored with administrative confidence. The third was only a timestamp and one sentence.

VOICE EVENT REPEATED NAME OF UNREGISTERED TEAM MEMBER. DON'T CONFIRM IDENTITY ALOUD.

Roan read it twice.

"Unregistered," he said.

Ines nodded. "Someone was inside a team manifest without being on it."

"Or something learned a name the station shouldn't have had."

Brendt lifted his head from the cot. "Can we adopt the policy where nobody says anyone's full name again?"

"Yes," Roan said.

Drogan glanced at him.

Roan added, "Given names only until exit. No House names. No old names. No corrections if something uses the wrong one."

"That's comforting," Brendt said.

"It isn't meant to be."

"I am stealing comfort from it anyway."

Drogan looked at the dead speaker. "Good rule."

Coming from Drogan, that was practically applause. Brendt looked absurdly pleased by the promotion of his fear into policy, then tried to hide it by checking the shield latch twice. Roan let him. Rituals weren't useless if they made the hands remember their work.

Ines spoke from the panel. "Station has partial power. Outer wards at sixty percent. Inner shutters functional. Emergency beacon dead. Map cache corrupted, but I can pull old route overlays if this thing doesn't electrocute me."

"Don't die," Brendt said.

"You're sentimental when injured."

"I'm sentimental when people are near exposed wiring."

Drogan stood at the window slit, watching the dark street beyond the ward shimmer. His shoulders had settled again, but not to their previous line. The voice had found an old wound and pressed. Roan recognized the mechanics. People with secrets developed scar tissue around them. The problem was that scar tissue still marked where to cut.

Roan cleaned the clamp, sealed the alcohol capsule, and tucked the ash-thread sample into a containment tube.

Ines noticed. "That sample is mine."

"You abandoned salvage priority by holding the lamp."

"That was medical assistance."

"Yes. Thank you."

She blinked.

Brendt looked suspiciously amused despite sweating through his collar.

Roan labeled the tube and placed it into his own pack.

"That wasn't an agreement," Ines said.

"No. It was gratitude. Different category."

"You are weaponizing manners."

"Efficient."

Outside, one Husk tapped the ward with a claw.

Once.

Then silence.

Drogan spoke without turning. "We remain here until Brendt can move."

"I can move," Brendt said.

"You can limp," Roan said. "Different verb."

"Useful distinction. Hate it."

Ines pulled a route overlay from the station cache. A shaky projection appeared above the panel, flickering between old survey geometry and current distortion. Several areas glowed red as unreliable.

"If we go back to the gate," she said, "we can reach it by dawn if the route hasn't shifted."

Brendt looked at Roan. "Back to the gate sounds lovely."

Roan studied the map.

Gate route: compromised by pack pursuit, marker-eating constructs, possible lockout at station. Return would preserve team but abandon objective. The anomaly remained north-northeast. Drogan's reaction to the voice proved the site connected to his past, and likely Aldric's. Brendt was injured but not incapacitated. Ines had retrieved route overlays. Roan had used one inversion and retained four. Drogan remained combat-effective, if emotionally contaminated.

The efficient answer wasn't retreat.

Not yet.

"Forward camp until first light," Roan said. "Then north by canal ridge, avoiding the market. Brendt moves center with reduced load. Ines carries markers and medical overflow. Drogan stops withholding route-critical history."

Drogan turned.

"That last part sounds negotiable," Ines said.

"It isn't."

Brendt raised one hand. "As the person with the haunted ankle, I support mandatory disclosure."

Drogan looked at each of them.

The old man seemed, for a moment, very tired of surviving.

Then he walked to the cot opposite Brendt and sat down.

"There was a tower," he said.

Roan remained still.

Drogan stared at the floor between his boots. "Thirty years ago, before the city folded into a Tier Three Rift completely, Black Squad entered through a northern breach. Aldric led. I followed. Three others came with us. We were told it was a salvage denial operation. Burn records. Confirm no civilians remained. Get out."

"It wasn't," Roan said.

Drogan gave a humorless breath. "No."

Brendt had stopped fidgeting.

Ines stood beside the panel, not moving.

Drogan continued. "There was a tower in the inner district. Clean stone. No ash. Aldric recognized markings inside. He didn't say how. We were attacked before he could explain. Hollowkin, not Husks. Organized. Armed. Speaking a language none of us knew."

The Codex Shard pulsed once.

Roan ignored it.

"Under the tower," Drogan said, "we found a chamber. Aldric went in alone. He came out with blood on his hands that wasn't his and told us the mission had changed. Then the orders changed too. From outside."

"Whose orders?" Roan asked.

Drogan looked at him.

"Founding Seven," he said.

The signal station's wards hummed.

Outside, the Husks waited.

Inside, Roan added another line to the list of people who had been lying to him by omission.

It was getting crowded.
