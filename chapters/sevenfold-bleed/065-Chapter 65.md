Chapter 65: The Call From Home

The morning bell at Black Spire rang at sixth bell. Not seventh, not eighth. Sixth.

Roan noted this as he crossed the courtyard from the dormitories, feet finding the worn path between the dormitory blocks and the training yards. Sixth bell meant something had changed in the schedule. The change would complicate his timeline—he’d planned to be off-campus before noon.

The letter from Lyra was folded in his inner pocket, edges sharp against his ribs. She’d written it in haste, the script cramped: *Mira collapsed last night. Old Pell came by. He left something about Father. She’s stable now, but I think you should come home.*

He’d read it three times before dawn. The arithmetic had shifted. The ranked sparring was an unwelcome variable, but he could work around it. His exit from campus required the appearance of normalcy. Missing an announced qualification round would draw more attention than participating and leaving afterward.

He reached the eastern training yard to find thirty-seven students already assembled. Theon Mercer stood near the central sparring circle, arms crossed, expression hovering somewhere between boredom and preemptive condescension. Brendt arrived two minutes later, jogging slightly, and took a position near Roan’s flank without comment. They’d developed that non-verbal coordination without discussing it.

“Good.” Edran Cole’s voice cut across the yard. The instructor strode to the front, his posture suggesting he’d rather be anywhere else. “The Dean has approved preliminary qualification rounds for the junior division. Ranked sparring matches. Results will factor into mid-term assessment.”

The murmur that rippled through the crowd was equal parts excitement and anxiety. Roan kept his expression neutral, but his mind was already running calculations. Ranked matches meant visible performance. Visible performance meant reputation management. He needed to win—needed to keep the path home clear—but he also needed to avoid drawing the kind of attention that would slow him down.

Theon raised a hand, his tone carefully casual. “Instructor, what about students below standard tier? Any accommodations for, say, students who haven’t reached baseline assessment expectations?”

Several heads turned. Theon’s gaze slid toward Roan with the barest flicker of a smile.

Roan didn’t rise to it. He had more immediate variables to process: the train schedule to Greycross, the hour he could slip out without raising alarms, the contents of the package Old Pell had left. Theon’s need for dominance was background noise.

Cole’s jaw tightened almost imperceptibly. “All students compete in their assigned bracket regardless of assessment status. The dampeners ensure safety. Any other questions?”

Theon lowered his hand. The faint smile remained.

Brendt shifted beside Roan, a low rumble in his chest. “He’s baiting you.”

“I know.”

“Don’t take the bait.”

“I don’t take bait,” Roan said. He meant it. Theon was a known variable. The letter in his pocket was not.

The assignments came. Names read from the scroll, students pairing off, the yard reorganizing into a patchwork of practice circles. Roan watched his own name appear on Cole’s list and waited.

“Vance, you’re facing Marchetti.”

Marchetti. Two syllables. Roan’s internal database pulled the file: Marcus Marchetti, seventeen, Vitalis reading 480, Tempered tier. Aggressive opener, average defense, above-average stamina. A match he could resolve without excess output.

Cole’s eyes met his for a fraction of a second. Not a warning. Something closer to assessment. Roan had seen that look before—instructors recalibrating their mental model of a student they hadn’t yet placed.

Roan moved to the assigned circle. Theon was watching from three circles over, positioned for a clear sightline.

He’d need to calibrate the output carefully. Enough to win, not enough to trigger the kind of review that would delay his departure. A decisive win was acceptable if it looked like technical edge rather than Vitalis anomaly. The Sentinel Bureau had ears in Spire; he couldn’t afford a flagged readout today.

The match began.

Marchetti closed distance in a standard Mountainbreak advance, weight forward, leading with a palm strike. Textbook. Roan sidestepped, letting the strike pass wide, and responded with a lateral parry that met minimal resistance. The dampeners reduced Vitalis output by roughly sixty percent. That worked in his favor—the limitation explained narrow margins.

Marchetti pressed the advantage. A right hook, a follow-up elbow, a knee drive aimed at the inner thigh. Roan absorbed the sequence, cataloguing the timing, the rhythm, the specific weaknesses in Marchetti’s recovery phase.

Fifteen seconds in, he had the pattern. Marchetti’s combination ended with a half-second pause.

Roan struck. A precise tap to the solar plexus—enough to disrupt breathing, not enough to incapacitate. Marchetti coughed, staggered, and Roan advanced with a controlled flurry that pushed him to the circle’s edge.

Theon was watching. Calculating.

Marchetti recovered, circled, reset his stance. He came again, faster, forcing error through aggression. Roan let him. The second exchange lasted eight seconds. Marchetti landed a glancing blow to Roan’s ribs—acceptable, within margin. Roan absorbed it, turned the momentum, and delivered a finishing strike to Marchetti’s outer knee. Not a joint-breaker. Just enough to buckle the leg.

Marchetti hit the ground. He tapped out.

“Solid work.” Cole made a note. “Vance, Marchetti. Next.”

Roan stepped back. Theon had turned away. The bait had failed to land, and now Theon was recalibrating.

Brendt materialized at his shoulder. “Clean.”

“Expected.”

“Everything alright? You seem—distracted.”

Roan considered the weight of the letter against his ribs. “I’m fine. Focus on your own match.”

Brendt studied him for a moment but didn’t push. That was one of the things Roan valued about him: he knew when to stop asking.

The next round came. Brendt drew a match against another Tempered student and handled it in just over a minute—an overwhelming pressure display that sent murmurs through the watching students. Roan noted Brendt’s efficiency and filed it. Good. He might need Brendt to cover his absence later.

Theon’s matches came next. He disposed of two opponents with practiced efficiency, his style fluid, performance designed to be watched. Roan recognized the social signaling. Theon was building a narrative of effortless competence.

The morning produced the bracket semifinals. Cole read the names.

“Vance. You’re facing Holloway.”

Brendt caught the motion and grinned, not a contestant’s grin, but a friend’s. “Didn’t think they’d match us.”

“Neither did I.” Cole’s expression was unreadable. “Circle four. Standard rules.”

They moved to the circle. Around them, the other matches continued, but there was a perceptible shift in attention—eyes turning, conversations pausing.

Brendt rolled his neck. “Friendly match?”

“Friendly,” Roan agreed. He had no intention of losing, but he also needed this over quickly. The train to Greycross left at midday. If he missed it, the next wasn’t until evening.

Brendt came in low, a grapple attempt designed to neutralize technical advantage through sheer physical contact. Roan twisted away, used the momentum to counter, struck at the floating ribs. Brendt absorbed the blow and drove forward.

They separated. Reset. Exchanged.

Within twenty seconds, the pattern was clear. Brendt was testing Roan’s upper limits—each exchange probed a different vector: speed, power, precision. He was mapping Roan’s responses deliberately, scientifically.

Roan adjusted. He began setting traps: a telegraphed opening, followed by the actual strike from an unexpected angle. Brendt caught the first two. The third landed—contact, palm striking Brendt’s forearm, disrupting his grip.

Brendt’s eyes narrowed. He changed tactics, abandoning measured probing for aggressive pressure. A shoulder check, an elbow strike, a sweeping kick. Roan jumped the kick and landed a counterstrike to Brendt’s exposed back.

Neither was holding back. The dampeners hummed with strain.

Thirty seconds. One minute. The exchange intensified.

Brendt feinted left, struck right. The blow caught Roan’s shoulder and spun him half around. Roan’s counter came automatically—a heel strike to Brendt’s knee that buckled the leg. Brendt went down on one knee.

Roan stepped in for the finish.

He stopped.

Brendt looked up at him, chest heaving, an odd expression on his face. Not anger. Not defeat. Recognition.

“Go ahead,” Brendt said. His voice was rough. “Take the point.”

Roan hesitated. Brendt was yielding—for reasons that weren’t immediately clear. Their eyes met. Brendt gave a slight nod toward the edge of the yard, toward the path that led to the gates. *Go.*

Brendt knew. He’d read Roan’s distraction, put the pieces together.

Cole’s voice cut across the yard. “Holloway has yielded. Vance wins.”

Theon was watching now, his expression unreadable.

Brendt rose, testing his knee. “You held back.”

“You probed.”

“Probed harder. You’ve got somewhere to be.”

Roan didn’t confirm. He didn’t need to. “Thanks.”

“Don’t thank me. Win the final so no one questions the bracket.”

The final. Right. Theon.

Cole’s voice carried over. “Vance, you’ll face Mercer in the final bracket. Report to Circle One in fifteen minutes.”

Theon met Roan’s gaze across the yard. The practiced smile was gone. In its place was something harder, more focused—a predator recalculating its approach.

Roan let the look pass. He needed those fifteen minutes to resync his plan. One match. Then the train.

Brendt fell in beside him as they moved toward the bench. “How bad is it?”

“Lyra says Mira collapsed. Old Pell left something about my father.”

“Shit. You’re going after this?”

“Before the Dean’s office starts asking questions I can’t answer.”

“The Dean will want to see you after the finals. Cole said tomorrow, but tonight you’ll be gone.”

“I’ll handle Cole.” Roan sat on the bench, running the numbers. “The finals are the last variable. I win, I leave, I’m back before dawn—or I send word. The penalty for missing a Dean’s call is minor if the reason holds.”

Brendt sat beside him. “And if Theon gives you trouble?”

“Theon’s predictable. That’s his weakness.”

The horn sounded. Five minutes.

Roan rose. The letter pressed against his ribs, a constant reminder of what waited beyond the Spire walls. He crossed the yard to Circle One. Theon was already there, waiting, his posture carrying the satisfaction of a predator who’d cornered prey.

“No hard feelings, Vance,” Theon said. “This is just business.”

Roan didn’t respond. The signal came.

Theon moved.

Fast. The Ashford Spire style in its purest form: pressure through overwhelming offense, each strike flowing into the next with mechanical precision. Roan backed, parried, absorbed. The dampeners hummed with strain.

Theon wasn’t holding back. He wanted a decisive win.

Roan let him—for twelve seconds. He catalogued the rhythm, the distance management, the predictable reset after each five-strike combination. Theon was good. He was also formulaic.

The next combination came: feint high, hook body, knee, elbow, sweep. Roan blocked the feint, absorbed the body hook, sidestepped the knee, and caught Theon’s elbow on his forearm. The sweep missed.

Roan struck.

Not a probing blow. A calculated counter that landed square on Theon’s solar plexus, hard enough to fold him forward. Theon’s breath left in a gasp. Roan followed with a knee to the ribs, then a palm strike to Theon’s temple—controlled, not enough to stun, enough to make him see stars.

Theon staggered. Roan stepped inside his guard and delivered a final strike to the jaw. The kind of blow that ended matches.

Theon hit the ground.

Silence.

Roan stepped back. His lungs burned, but his breathing was steady. He’d shown more than he’d intended for a low-profile play—but the low-profile play had been overridden by the need to finish quickly. Theon wouldn’t trouble him again today. That was the only variable that mattered.

Cole’s voice cut through the silence. “Mercer has yielded. Vance wins the finals.”

The crowd was still processing. Theon’s supporters were silent. Theon himself was rising, jaw swelling, eyes dark with shock and something colder.

Roan met the look. “You’re good, Mercer. But you telegraph your resets.”

Theon’s jaw tightened, but he said nothing.

Brendt appeared at Roan’s shoulder. “That was—a statement.”

“That was necessary. I don’t have time for a rematch.”

Cole approached, scroll in hand. “Clean match, Vance. Report to the Dean’s office at second bell tomorrow. We’ll need to discuss your assessment records.”

“Understood, Instructor.” Roan kept his voice neutral. “May I request a delay until third bell? I have a family matter to attend this evening.”

Cole’s eyes narrowed, but he didn’t push. “Approved. Third bell. Don’t miss it.”

“I won’t.”

The crowd began to disperse. Roan headed for the edge of the yard, Brendt at his side. Theon’s gaze followed until they were out of sight.

At the dormitory path, Brendt stopped. “You want me to come?”

“No. Stay, cover for me. If anyone asks, I’m ill or I’m in the library.”

“And if they check?”

“They won’t. Not before morning.” Roan pulled the letter from his pocket, read Lyra’s words one more time. “I’ll be back before the Dean’s call.”

“And if you’re not?”

“Then I’ll be in worse trouble than skipping a meeting.” Roan folded the letter and started toward the gates. “Keep an eye on Theon. He won’t let today go.”

“He’s not the one I’m worried about.”

Roan paused. “I know.”

He walked. The Spire gates loomed ahead, the path to Greycross stretching beyond. The letter was warm against his chest—a tether pulling him home.

The Inversion had given him power. The calculation had given him leverage. But the call from home was a variable no arithmetic could fully absorb.

He would get there. He would find out what Old Pell had left. He would make sure Mira was safe.

Then he would come back and finish what he’d started.

The gate closed behind him. The morning bell faded into the rhythm of his footsteps on the iron road. Six to go, he reminded himself. But first, the ones already waiting.
