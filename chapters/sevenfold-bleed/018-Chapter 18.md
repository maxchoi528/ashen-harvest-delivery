Chapter 18: The Rival

The dining hall's east wing had seventeen tables arranged by cohort and rank. Roan had mapped every one of them within the first three days.

Theon Mercer sat at table nine, center seat, surrounded by a semicircle of fellow Initiates who laughed at something he'd said. The laugh carried, intentionally. So did the way Theon's eyes tracked Roan the moment he entered through the north doors.

Roan selected a seat at table fourteen, far enough to avoid the implication of proximity, close enough to observe. He set his tray down without ceremony. Grit porridge. Hard bread. Cold beans. The Spire's kitchen staff had perfected the art of making food that sustained life without offering any of its pleasures.

Brendt dropped into the seat across from him, already chewing. "You're staring again."

"I'm eating."

"You're staring while eating. Different thing." Brendt took a long drink of water, then leaned forward. "Mercer's been asking around about you. Third person this week."

Third person. Roan's spoon paused halfway to his mouth. The math was simpler than he'd expected. "What does he want to know?"

"Where you're really from. Who you're connected to. Whether the Vance name means anything." Brendt shrugged one shoulder. "Standard digging. Everyone does it."

"Not everyone does it this persistently."

"You're more interesting than most." Brendt's grin was unrepentant. "Lower-middle family, decent but not exceptional showing at the Qualifier, no connections in the student registry, no sponsor on record. You're a blank space. Blank spaces make people nervous."

Roan returned to his porridge. Gaps got filled. Never with the truth. Theon Mercer was constructing a story right now, something about hidden lineage or secret sponsors or connections to one of the old houses. None of them would guess the truth, that Aldric Vance had left his son exactly one thing, and it was already fused to his spine.

"Theon Mercer," Roan said, tasting the name. "Family?"

"House Mercer. Minor gentry, Ironholm's western district. They run logistics contracts for the Sentinel Bureau. Shipping, warehousing, supply chain management." Brendt lowered his voice. "Solid money, middling influence. Theon's the first Mercer to make it into Black Spire in three generations. He's... sensitive about establishing himself."

Sensitive about establishing himself. In other words, Theon Mercer had something to prove and was looking for someone to prove it against. A blank space with no sponsors and a common name was the perfect target.

"What's his Vitalis?"

"Routine reads are around four hundred. Peak combat stress tests show six-twenty." Brendt wiped his mouth. "He's Tempered, early sub-tier. About where you should be, rank-wise."

Roan set that aside for later. Four hundred base, six-twenty peak. His own readings sat at roughly the same baseline, but the Inversion system let him push higher under pressure. The gap was narrow enough to close and wide enough to matter. That was the only math he needed.

"He approached you directly?"

"Twice. Asked if I knew anything about your family background. I told him you were a cousin from Greycross and that we didn't talk much. Kept it boring." Brendt grinned again. "You're welcome."

The porridge had gone cold. Roan pushed the bowl aside. "You didn't have to lie for me."

"I didn't do it for you. I did it because Mercer's a shark and I don't like sharks." Brendt stood, collecting his tray. "Drogan wants to see you after morning drill. He asked me to pass the message."

"Warden Drogan?"

"Who else?" Brendt was already walking backward toward the tray return. "Don't be late."

- break -

Drogan's message changed the shape of the morning.

Roan finished the rest of his food because wasted calories were a form of stupidity, then carried his tray to the return station and took the longer route out of the hall. The longer route passed close enough to table nine for Theon Mercer to notice him without giving Theon the satisfaction of being approached.

Theon noticed.

So did the two students beside him. One of them leaned in and whispered something. Theon didn't smile. He had the controlled face of a young man rehearsing dignity in public because dignity was cheaper than confidence and easier to fake.

Roan let his gaze pass over the table once. No challenge. No invitation. Just enough acknowledgment to confirm he knew Mercer had been looking.

That was the second useful result of the meal.

By the time he reached the corridor, Brendt had caught up with him again.

"You know," Brendt said, "most people respond to social pressure by making friends."

"Most people respond to smoke by coughing. That doesn't make it strategy."

"I'm going to pretend that made sense." Brendt adjusted the strap of his practice bag. "Drogan sounded serious. Warden-serious, not old-man-yells-at-door serious."

"Those are different categories?"

"One gets you bruised. The other gets you buried under paperwork before you get bruised."

Roan kept that with the rest. Drogan didn't send informal summons because he was bored. If Mercer had been asking questions and Drogan wanted a meeting on the same morning, either the two facts were connected or the day had become inefficient in two separate directions.

Both options were bad.

He checked his public Vitalis bracelet before entering the drill yard. Four hundred and twelve. Safe. Boring. The number of a competent student who would graduate if he kept his head down and accepted the rank assigned to him.

The Codex Shard remained silent against his spine.

Good.

Silence meant no capacity spent, no automatic warning, no sign that the morning had already become an emergency. It also meant he was walking into Drogan's office with no extra information.

Roan preferred extra information.

At the far end of the yard, Theon Mercer stepped out beneath the awning and watched him cross the stones. This time, he didn't just watch. He walked forward, angling his path to intersect Roan's before the main drill lines.

Roan stopped. Not because he had to, but because stopping on his own terms set the tempo.

"Vance." Theon's voice was measured, pleasant in the way that cost nothing. "We haven't been introduced properly. I'm Theon Mercer."

"I know who you are." Roan let the pause hold. "You've been asking about me."

Theon's smile didn't waver. "A new face always draws curiosity. Black Spire's a small world for those of us who take it seriously. I like to know who I'm sharing it with."

"You know my file, then."

"Files are incomplete conversations." Theon tilted his head. "I prefer the real thing. But if you're the type who keeps to himself, I can respect that too."

Roan met his eyes. "Respect is cheap. Understanding costs more."

Theon's smile tightened at the edges. "I'll keep that in mind." He stepped aside. "Good luck with Warden Drogan. I hear he doesn't waste time on students who don't matter."

The implication hung in the air. Roan didn't address it. He continued toward the administration wing, letting Theon watch his back.

There it was. Third useful result. Theon wasn't merely asking around. He was willing to show his angle—a little too soon, a little too polished. The real shape of the threat would take a direct exchange to verify. Roan filed that away.

- break -

The message from Greycross arrived folded into a newspaper Brendt had left on the bench: classified ads page, third column, a listing circled in pencil. *Wraithmilk bulk — 30% below market. Ironmarsh Reclaimers, new supply.* Roan read it once and understood.

Dren was moving.

Dren was the other Greycross dealer, a year older, same background, same desperation, different math. Roan had mapped his operation during the first week of the Spire intake: three suppliers, two safe houses, a distribution network that ran through the Ironmarsh Reclaimers' old territory. Limited reach. Limited ambition. But Dren had noticed Roan's absence from the Greycross market and interpreted it as retreat.

Not an unreasonable assumption. A new Spire student had limited hours for street business. Class schedules, mandatory drills, curfews, and the constant low-grade attention from instructors who noticed when a first-year's finances didn't match his background. Dren saw an opening. Dren was filling it.

Roan set the newspaper aside.

He could respond directly. Visit Dren's operation, have a conversation, the kind that established boundaries without requiring enforcement. But direct response cost time and attention. It also cost information, because Dren would know Roan had noticed, would adjust his defenses, would start watching for retaliation.

Better to observe first.

- break -

Three days later, Roan stood at the edge of Greycross's eastern market district, watching a basement door that opened twice an hour.

Dren's operation was clean. Roan had to give him that. The basement had a rotating lookout, a coded knock sequence, and a delivery schedule that never used the same route twice. The product was Wraithmilk, diluted to sixty percent, sold in single-dose vials wrapped in brown paper. Not elegant. Functional.

Roan counted the foot traffic. Seventeen customers in two hours. At twenty credits per vial, that was three hundred forty credits in revenue, minus supplier cost, minus labor, minus the Reclaimers' cut. Dren was clearing maybe a hundred and fifty a day. Respectable. Not a threat.

But the pattern worried him. Dren wasn't selling to new customers. He was selling to Roan's customers.

That changed the math.

Roan stepped back from the observation point and walked a slow circuit around the block. The Reclaimers' territory markers were still up, still fresh. Meaning Dren had permission. Meaning someone in the Reclaimers had decided that Roan's absence from the market was permanent enough to reassign his customer base.

He stopped at a corner stall selling fried dough and cheap coffee. Bought a cup he didn't intend to drink and stood there, watching the basement door through the steam.

The question wasn't whether Dren was a rival. Dren was too small to be a rival. The question was whether Dren was useful.

A dealer who ran a clean operation with consistent traffic and no visible enforcer presence was either very careful or very well-protected. Either quality could be directed. If Dren could be made to understand that Roan's absence was temporary and that cooperation was more profitable than competition, the Greycross supply line would stabilize without further intervention.

If Dren couldn't understand that, removal would be necessary. Clean removal. No traces. The kind that looked like a business decision because it was one.

Roan finished the coffee he hadn't wanted and left the cup on the counter.

He would wait one more week. Let Dren build a little more. Let the customer list grow long enough that Dren felt secure. A week of lost revenue was a price he could absorb, but a rushed move would cost more.

Then he would visit.

- break -

The morning drills passed without incident. Brendt kept glancing at him with the particular look of someone who knew something had changed but didn't know what. Roan didn't explain.

At the end of the second drill, Instructor Vance called him aside. "Drogan's office. Now."

Not a request.

Roan walked to the administration wing, past the bulletin boards covered in tournament schedules and housing notices, past the door with the Warden's nameplate that someone had scratched a small line beneath, as if marking territory. He knocked once.

"Come in."

Drogan sat behind a desk that was too clean to be functional. No papers. No folders. A single cup of tea, cooling. His eyes tracked Roan from the door to the chair without moving his head.

"Close it."

Roan closed the door.

"Sit."

Roan sat.

Drogan studied him for a long moment. The kind of silence that was itself a question, testing whether the person on the other side would fill it.

Roan didn't fill it.

"Theon Mercer," Drogan said finally. "You've met."

It wasn't a question.

"Yes."

"What do you make of him?"

Roan considered the question. Drogan wasn't asking for gossip. He was asking for an assessment, the kind one professional gave another about a third party's utility.

"Competent," Roan said. "Ambitious. Used to being heard. Not used to being wrong."

"Can you work with him?"

"Can I, or will I?"

Drogan's mouth moved a fraction. Not a smile. The acknowledgment of a distinction that mattered. "Both."

"Work with him, yes, if the objective requires it. Trust him, no. He's looking for a foothold and he'll use whatever angle presents itself."

"He wants to know who you are," Drogan said.

"I know."

"Do you know why?"

Roan met his eyes. "Because he's smart enough to recognize that I'm not what my file says. The question is whether you want him to find out what I actually am, or whether you want me to remain uninteresting."

Drogan picked up his tea. Drank. Set it down. The tea had gone cold; he didn't seem to notice.

"That's the question," he said. "Keep yourself uninteresting. It's the only safe way to survive a place like this."

The message was clear. Drogan wasn't warning him about Mercer. Drogan was telling him that the Spire had its own eyes, its own calculations, its own reasons for letting certain students remain blank spaces.

Roan stored that with the rest.

"I can do uninteresting."

Drogan nodded once. The meeting was over.

Roan stood, crossed to the door, and paused with his hand on the handle.

"One more thing," Drogan said. "Your supply line in Greycross. The boy running it now. His name is Dren."

Roan's hand stayed on the handle.

"A Reclaimer shipment manifest crossed the Ironholm weigh station two days ago," Drogan continued. "The pattern caught my eye. Dren's operation has territorial approval, but he's being watched by people who aren't me. If you're planning to do something about him, do it quietly. Or don't do it at all."

Roan opened the door.

"Understood."
