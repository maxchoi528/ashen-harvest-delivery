Chapter 74: Tempered Peak

The first alarm ward shimmered through the expedition pavilion at dawn. Roan was already awake.

Not because he'd been waiting for it. The Codex Shard pulsed twice during the night, a preemptive calibration routine that Aldric's margin notes might have called obsessive if they could speak. Four daily inversions at his current threshold, or one serious-tier if the tally demanded it. The ledger rarely required the serious tier. He preferred to keep that option in the red zone of his calculations, available, but costly in ways that couldn't be easily reconciled.

Brendt was still snoring in the adjacent bunk. Third day of the expedition, and his friend had developed a troubling attachment to sleeping past dawn. Not a character flaw, exactly. Just an inefficiency that Roan filed away under the mental column labeled "things that will bite him later."

He dressed in expedition gear, strapped the Codex Shard beneath his collar, still disguised as the cheap pendant it appeared to be, and left the tent without waking Brendt.

The camp was quieter at this hour. The real traffic came later, when the full expedition team mustered for the day's push into the deeper tiers. At dawn, the only people moving were serious ones. Drogan, reviewing the perimeter reports at the command tent. Ines Carrow, organizing the supply manifest with her characteristic silence. And Roan.

He took the eastern path, less monitored, and emerged onto a ridge overlooking the Hollowed City. Grey mist curled through broken avenues and skeletal archways, the remnants of a civilization that had fallen before the Founding Seven's compact. The Spire's expedition camp sat at the edge of it, a small bastion of order against the consuming silence. Seventy years ago, Aldric had walked these same streets. The bones of the city were the same.

Roan let himself think that thought for exactly three seconds before cataloguing it as irrelevant. His father's footsteps didn't matter. The city's history didn't matter. What mattered was the deep descent deadline in three days, the Sentinel Bureau meeting in sixteen hours, and the fact that his Vitalis readings had plateaued at 1,247, a full three hundred points below his peak during the Hollow Marrow Elixir trial.

His body had absorbed the marrow. His channels had expanded. But the raw energy accumulation had stalled because he'd burned through his inversion quotas during that trial. The balance was clear: he needed more resources, or he needed to be more efficient with what he had.

Or both. Usually both.

The training grounds within the defensive perimeter were empty at this hour. Roan preferred it that way. He found his usual spot, a flat section of broken flagstone that made good footing for pivot drills, and began his warm-up sequence.

First: shadow forms. No weapons, no Vitalis expenditure. Just the body moving through patterns that Drogan had drilled into him over the past months. Seven minutes of this. The blood came, the joints loosened, the mind slipped into the focus that was neither meditation nor aggression but something between, the readiness that every fighter talked about but few achieved.

Second: the Sevenfold Bleed forms. He didn't activate the technique fully. That would be stupid with the Bureau watching. But he traced the energy pathways, felt the potential coiled in his channels like a serpent waiting for the command to strike.

Third: nothing. He stood in the center of the broken square and breathed. Let the grey light touch his face. Let the distant sounds of the Hollowed City fade into static.

Then he began the countermeasure drill. The principle was simple: wait for the opponent to commit, read their weight distribution, their energy signature, the micro-tension in their shoulders and hips. Then move, not to block, not to dodge, but to redirect. Use their momentum. Let their strength become your leverage.

The problem was that perfect stillness was the hardest thing to maintain when someone was trying to kill you.

He was three repetitions into his fourth set when he caught the shift in the ambient sound. Not the crumbling of ancient stone. Not the distant skitter of Hollowkin. These were footsteps. Light, deliberate, controlled. The walking pattern of someone who wanted to be heard approaching but didn't want it to seem that way.

Roan completed his current form, then turned.

A figure stood at the edge of the training ground, wearing the heavy grey cloak of the Ashen Watch. The man wore no identifying badge of rank, but his bearing marked him as someone accustomed to being listened to. He was alone, which was interesting. The Watch typically traveled in pairs.

"Roan Vance." The voice carried across the broken stone, polished and warm and utterly false. "Training early, I see. How diligent."

Roan said nothing. He reset his stance and began his warm-up sequence again.

The man didn't take the hint. He walked forward, unhurried, his footsteps making precise sounds against the rubble. "I wanted to speak with you. Privately. About the deep descent."

"There's nothing to discuss."

"Oh, I think there is." The man stopped ten feet away, close enough to talk, far enough to maintain plausible deniability if this went badly. "You've been making an impression, Vance. The whispers about you reached us before we left Ironholm. The preliminary assessments. The display against the Husk scouts last night." A pause, carefully weighted. "People are noticing."

"Let them."

"That's the problem." The man tilted his head slightly, and for a moment, the mask slipped, not to reveal something worse, just something more honest. Boredom, maybe. Or genuine curiosity. "I'm not accustomed to being surprised. My house taught me that surprises are failures of information gathering. So I investigated."

"And?"

"And you're strange, Vance. Your Vitalis readings fluctuate in ways that don't match any standard training profile. Your technique choices make no tactical sense until you make them, and then they're brilliant. You should be nobody, a scholarship case with good fundamentals and average potential." The man's eyes narrowed slightly. "But you're not nobody. Which means you're hiding something."

Roan felt the cold clarity settling over his thoughts, the calculation state that had served him through every confrontation since he'd discovered what he could do. "If I were hiding something, would I announce it to an agent of the Ashen Watch in an empty ruin at dawn?"

"That depends on what you're hiding." The man smiled, and it didn't reach his eyes. "But I suppose you're right. A cornered animal shows its teeth. A confident one doesn't need to."

"I don't need to do anything except survive the descent. Same as you."

"Are we the same?" The man took a step forward, and this time the distance closed to something that felt more intimate, more dangerous. "Because I looked at your records, Vance. Your family's records. The Vance line was expelled from the Registry thirty years ago. Their training lineage was declared terminated. And yet here you are, with techniques that don't match your profile and a body that breaks every rule I've been taught about recovery rates."

He knew. Not everything. Roan could read that in the man's expression. The Watch had assembled pieces of a puzzle, but they hadn't found the picture on the box yet. What they had were suspicions. And suspicions were dangerous precisely because they could be wrong and right simultaneously.

"What do you want?"

"I want to make you an offer." The words came smoothly, rehearsed but not badly so. "The deep descent pairs teams by preliminary assessment. If my information is correct, your expedition is slotted in the mid-tier. I can ensure that your first two layers are favorable. You get priority access to the core sectors. Walkovers." A pause.
