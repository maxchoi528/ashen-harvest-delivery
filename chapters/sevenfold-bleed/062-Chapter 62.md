Chapter 62: The Hand That Trembled

The morning after the tournament's final day, Roan woke to find Brendt already sitting up in his bed.

That was unusual. Brendt Holloway ran his body's recovery like a schedule. Eight hours of sleep, mandatory. Anything less than six and he descended into irritable silence that made the dormitory feel smaller. The fact that he was upright, pale as bone-dry clay and held together by nothing but determination and the thin sheet he'd bunched around his midsection, meant one of two things.

Either the world had caught fire and he was too stubborn to acknowledge it, or he'd slept even less than Roan had.

Brendt got there before Roan could ask. “Three hours,” he said. “Maybe four. Close enough.”

“You fought Cyrus Ashford yesterday. Your abdomen looks like someone tried to turn it inside out.”

“Warden's Heal does the heavy lifting overnight. It's the next two days that are the problem.” Brendt shifted, wincing at the movement. “He came to see me. The Headmaster.”

That landed differently than it should have. Edran Cole didn't visit injured students. He summoned them to his office in the Black Spire's Crown Tier and delivered verdicts from behind a desk older than the Academy itself. The man had better things to do than walk across campus to check on a Tempered student who'd taken a bad hit in a championship match.

Roan sat on the edge of his own bed. The dormitory was empty. First bell was twenty minutes out. “What did he say?”

“He asked if I wanted to withdraw from the program.”

The calculation lined up. Brendt's injury was severe enough that a full recovery would take weeks, maybe longer. The Junior Hunters' Hall qualifying window was closing. If he dropped now, he kept his academy credits and his family kept the tuition payments they'd already made. If he stayed and re-injured himself before the qualifiers, he lost both, and the chance to ever re-enroll.

“He give you a timeline?”

“Decision by next week.” Brendt looked at his hands. The knuckles were still scabbed from punching through Cyrus's stance three times yesterday. “If I drop now, I keep the credits and the window stays open. If I withdraw from the qualifiers instead of resigning, I lose both. The Junior Hunters' Hall qualifying window is closing anyway.”

“That's tight.”

“It's what I have.” Brendt's jaw worked for a second. “He also said something about you.”

Roan waited.

“He said he watched the match. Both matches. Yours and mine.” Brendt's jaw worked for a second, choosing words. “He said, and I quote: 'Your friend has been playing low for a very long time.' Then he left.”

Playing low. Cole had used those words with deliberate weight. Playing low meant the gap between what Roan showed publicly and what he was had finally attracted the wrong kind of attention, or the right kind, depending on how he looked at it.

“Did he say anything else?”

“No. Just looked at me like he was trying to decide whether to be impressed or concerned.” Brendt pulled the blanket tighter. “You know what the worst part is? I couldn't tell which one he'd settled on.”

Roan considered the ceiling. Cole was a Forged. Third-tier, late in the stage, with Vitalis readout that made most of the student body feel like they were standing at the bottom of a well. If he was watching Roan closely enough to notice the act, that changed the risk profile considerably.

But Cole wasn't the only one who'd been watching.

Garrick had been on the far side of the arena, exactly where he'd been every match. Selene Ardent had sat three rows behind the judges' panel, her posture suggesting boredom but her eyes suggesting anything but. And somewhere in the crowd. Roan hadn't caught sight of him, which was worse than seeing him directly. House Greel or one of his people had almost certainly been present.

Cyrus had made noise in front of six hundred witnesses. Loud enough to reach ears in Ironholm, Greycross, maybe further.

The conclusion didn't change. But the audience had grown.

“You going to make a decision?” Roan asked.

“About withdrawing?”

“About what you're going to do when you're healed. Assuming you heal.”

Brendt exhaled. It was as close to a laugh as he could manage right now. “I'm going to kill every Ashford descendant in that tournament. Starting with Cyrus, working outward.”

“That's not a plan. That's a feeling.”

“Feelings are a start.”

He wasn't wrong. But feelings didn't stop infections from setting into torn muscle tissue, and feelings didn't pay for the second round of Heal Draughts that Brendt's recovery was going to require. That was going to be two hundred credits, minimum. Brendt's family had maybe a third of that left after this semester's tuition.

Roan kept the number. Not in the active calculation, but in the category of problems that would surface within the month whether he wanted them to or not.

“Forget Cole,” Roan said. “What did the medic say this morning?”

“Full tissue regeneration in eight days if I rest. Fourteen if I'm stupid. Thirty if I try to train before the twelfth day.” Brendt met his eyes. “I'm not resting for eight days.”

“Of course you're not.”

“Three days. I'll push through the worst of it and be back at seventy percent by the time the qualifying brackets are announced.”

“You'll collapse the first time someone checks your left flank.”

“Then I won't let anyone check my left flank.”

This was the version of Brendt that people who didn't know him well called reckless. The version that people who did know him called something else, stubborn, yes, but rooted in a specific kind of arithmetic. Brendt Holloway calculated the cost of quitting against the cost of suffering, and suffering always came out lower in his ledger. It was an ugly way to think. It was also how he'd made it into Black Spire at all, competing against legacy admits and hereditary Bloodlines on a scholarship that barely existed.

Roan understood the logic. He just didn't agree with the conclusion.

“You need credits,” he said. “Real ones. Not the four hundred you've got left.”

“I'm aware.”

“The Hollow Marrow Elixir you wanted to buy last month. You still thinking about it?”

Brendt went quiet for a moment. The Elixir was a Tier 2 catalyst, mid-grade, expensive, effective for bone and marrow conditioning. It was also something that Black Spire's standard formulary didn't carry, which meant buying it on the open market through the campus exchange. Last month's listed price had been six hundred credits. It would be higher now.

“Thinking about it and being able to afford it are different verbs,” Brendt said.

“I know a supplier.”

This was a lie, technically. What Roan knew was the Black Market channel that Old Pell used for moving materials that didn't appear on official ledgers. The exchange rate was different, credits converted to Tokens at roughly three to one, and Token purchases didn't leave a paper trail. It was also, technically, a violation of the student code of conduct.

“Define 'supplier,'” Brendt said carefully.

“Someone who sells things that don't have a price tag in the official catalogue. Things that might help with deep tissue and bone conditioning.”

“You're talking about the grey market.”

“I'm talking about efficiency.”

Brendt stared at him. The room felt smaller than it was. Morning light cut through the narrow window in a pale stripe across the floor, illuminating dust motes and the edge of a training shoe someone had left by the door.

“How do you know about this?” Brendt asked.

“I know about a lot of things.”

“That answer is the opposite of reassuring.”

Roan stood. First bell was close enough to start moving. “You want credits or you want me to explain my sources. Pick one.”

Brendt's mouth twisted. He was doing the balance on how badly he needed what Roan was offering versus how much he didn't want to owe a debt he couldn't quantify.

“Tell me it won't blow back on us.”

“Everything blows back. The question is whether you see it coming.”

Silence. Then Brendt nodded once, sharp.

- break -

The campus exchange was a long hallway lined with glass-fronted booths, each one displaying inventory manifests on scrolling boards that scrolled too slowly to be useful. Roan had learned to read the margins, the prices that didn't match demand, the items that appeared on Tuesday mornings and vanished before Thursday.

He found the listing at the fourth booth from the eastern entrance. Wraithmilk Tincture, Tier 1. Fifty credits for a standard vial, which was already thirty percent above market rate. The booth was staffed by a second-year who looked like she'd been awake for two days straight and was selling the tincture out of personal inventory rather than a warehouse manifest.

Roan had no interest in the tincture itself. He needed the booth operator.

“The Elixir,” he said, sliding a Token across the counter, black polymer, unmarked, worth fifty credits in the grey economy. “I'm told you can get things that don't appear in the catalogue.”

The second-year's eyes dropped to the Token. Her expression didn't change, but her hand moved under the counter. When it came back up, it was holding a small card, cream-colored, printed with a phone number that looked like it had been run through a copier six times too many.

“Tell them Petra sent you. The price is Token-denominated.”

“Understood.”

She took the Token and the card disappeared into his jacket pocket in the same motion. He'd learned that trick from Drogan, who had a hundred small movements like that, ways of making things appear and disappear that were less about concealment and more about speed. The eye processed what it expected to see. If nothing stayed in the hand long enough to register as an object, the hand itself became invisible.

He walked out of the exchange without looking back.

The phone number belonged to a voice that sounded like it lived in a basement. Flat, unspecific, uninterested in pleasantries. The price for a single vial of Hollow Marrow Elixir through the grey channel was eight Tokens, which converted to roughly two hundred and sixty credits, significantly better than the six hundred on the official market.

But there was a catch, and the voice delivered it with the same monotone it used for everything.

“Delivery takes three days. Items are staged at the Greycross depot. Someone will leave instructions at the locker. You pick up, you don't ask where it came from, and you don't ask what happened to the last person who asked.”

“That's steep.”

“That's the market. You want official, pay official prices.”

Three days put the delivery past the window Brendt needed. Not by much, six days total from today, versus the eight days of mandatory rest that the medic had prescribed. Close enough for Brendt to argue his way onto the training floor.

Close enough if nothing went wrong.

Roan considered the variables. The depot was in the commercial district, four blocks from the market square. He'd been there before with Old Pell, picking up shipments that Pell kept off his personal ledger. The security was minimal, a keypad, a guard who checked IDs and didn't care what was in the packages, and the lockers were the combination type. No cameras pointed at the individual units.

It was as clean as grey market got.

He confirmed the order. Eight Tokens, which he withdrew from the account he'd been building since last semester, earnings from minor contracts, arbitrage on low grade catalysts, and the occasional payout from Drogan for jobs that Drogan didn't want to file with the Academy.

His balance dropped to ninety-three Tokens. Enough for two more orders like this, or one major purchase, or a week of food if the campus dining hall stopped accepting standard currency.

The arithmetic was the math. He knew where every unit of resource sat in his ledger, and he knew what it would cost to move any of it. Right now, the most expensive thing in his ledger wasn't a number. It was Brendt Holloway refusing to rest, bleeding credits he didn't have, and betting his entire future on the assumption that his body would hold together longer than his resolve.

Roan had seen that bet made before. He'd watched it work exactly once, and fail eleven times out of twelve. He found Brendt in the training yard, which meant Brendt had already broken three of the medic's seven explicit instructions before the sun was fully over the eastern wall.

He was moving through slow forms, basic stances, weight shifting, nothing that required torque on the core. Even that was enough to pull the color out of his face. Drogan stood three meters away, arms crossed, watching with an expression that Roan had learned to read as professional concern. He wasn't going to stop Brendt. He was going to watch him do it anyway and then file a report that said he'd advised against it.

That was the game. Students made choices. Wardens documented. The paperwork absolved everyone.

Roan hung back near the stone pillar at the yard's edge. Drogan glanced at him, nodded once. The nod contained a question.

Roan shook his head. Not yet.

Brendt finished the sequence, six minutes of careful, pain-filtered movement that would have taken most students ninety seconds. He stood in final stance for a moment, breathing carefully through his nose, and then let his arms drop.

“Four more sets,” he said.

“Your core temperature is elevated,” Drogan said. It wasn't a question.

“Slightly.”

“Significantly. The medic's report was very specific about core temperature.”

“The medic hasn't fought Cyrus Ashford.”

The corner of Drogan's mouth twitched. Not amusement. Something closer to recognition. He'd heard that argument before, or one exactly like it. Warden Drogan Hale had been an active combat instructor for sixteen years. He'd watched a hundred students make exactly this choice, and he'd developed professional detachment that let him document it without intervening unless the risk crossed a threshold he couldn't sign off on.

Roan understood the position. He also understood that Brendt's core temperature was rising, his breathing was getting shallower, and the next set would push his heart rate into territory that the tissue regen couldn't compensate for. Not today. Maybe not for two or three more days.

“Drogan,” Roan called.

The Warden turned.

“Word on the Ashford kid?”

Drogan's expression shifted subtly. He wasn't going to discuss a student in front of another student. Not here, not now, not without knowing what Roan was asking. But the pause was long enough to be an answer.

“Healing faster than expected,” Drogan said. “The Ashford family sent their personal physician. He's back in a private suite on the western wing by now.”

Private suite. Personal physician. The Ashfords had money that Brendt's family couldn't imagine, and Cyrus was going to spend the next two weeks recovering in comfort while Brendt bled credits in a shared dormitory room.

The asymmetry was structural. It wasn't personal. But Brendt treated everything personal, and that was both his greatest strength and the thing that was going to get him killed in a real Rift encounter.

“Drogan,” Roan said again. “Could I speak with you? Privately.”

The Warden's eyes moved to Brendt, then back. “Finish your sets. Slowly. When you're done, come find me.”

Brendt nodded. He'd already begun the second set.

Drogan walked to the yard's edge and met Roan behind the stone pillar, out of earshot. This close, Roan could smell the old leather and the faint mineral trace of bone-tinged sweat that clung to every combat instructor who spent his days near the training dummies.

“Talk,” Drogan said.

“The Hollow Marrow Elixir. Do you know it?”

Drogan's expression didn't change. But his weight shifted almost imperceptibly onto his back foot, a micro-adjustment that most people would miss. Roan caught it. Drogan caught him catching it.

“Why do you ask?”

“Brendt needs it. Bone and marrow conditioning. The official market is six hundred credits. I found a grey supplier who can do it for less.”

“Less,” Drogan repeated. The word had a flat, heavy quality.

“Eight Tokens.”

Drogan didn't answer immediately. He turned the key over in his palm twice, the metal catching the low light. Then he looked at Roan with an expression that was hard to read.

“You're playing a game I don't fully see,” Drogan said. It wasn't accusation. It was assessment. “And you're playing it with your friend's health as the wager.”

“The wager is already placed. Cyrus Ashford placed it yesterday. The Elixir changes the odds.”

“The Elixir also takes three days to deliver and at least two days to cycle. If Brendt takes it before his tissue is stable, he risks rejection.”

“He won't take it until day six. The tissue will be ready.”

Drogan studied him. This part where most people, students, peers, strangers, would have filled the silence with explanation or justification. Roan didn't. He stood there, hands loose at his sides, waiting.

Drogan had known Aldric Vance. Not well, they'd been in the same program for two years before Aldric's disappearance, but well enough to recognize the way his son held silence. It wasn't empty. It was full, and deliberate, and packed tight with calculations that weren't being shared.

“You're going to do it anyway,” Drogan said. “The purchase. The whole plan. Whether I advise for or against.”

“Yes.”

“Because you've already run the tally.”

“Because the columns are the part that works.”

Drogan exhaled. It was a long, slow breath, and Roan could almost see the mental document being filed, concerns noted, objections overruled, decision delegated to someone who had more information than he'd started with this morning.

“If I find out that grey channel is connected to something that endangers this Academy or the students in it,” Drogan said, “I will end your time here personally. Do you understand that?”

“Yes.”

“I don't think you do. But I believe you think you do, and that's going to have to be enough.” Drogan stepped back. “Get your friend the Elixir. Make sure he waits until day six. And when he breaks protocol and starts training on day four, call me before he tears something he can't regrow.”

Roan nodded.

Drogan held the look for one more second. Then he turned and walked back into the yard.

- break -

That afternoon, Roan went to the library.

He didn't go to the public stacks. He went to the restricted section on the third floor, the one that required a faculty reference to access and a second key that Drogan had given him eight months ago without explanation. The key was aluminium, old, slightly bent at the edge. It fit a door that looked like it had been added to the building sometime in the last century and not integrated particularly well with the surrounding architecture.

The restricted section smelled like compressed air and old glue. The shelves were wooden, handcrafted, sized for volumes that hadn't been printed in decades. Most of the titles were in languages Roan couldn't read, the Founding Seven's original texts, predating the modern standardization of martial manuscripts. But the catalogue at the entrance was indexed in modern script, cross-referenced by topic.

He was looking for something specific.

The Black Rite of Veyrn had been mentioned in Aldric's notes. Not explained, never explained, but referenced in the context of the Veyrn Sanctum and a phrase that Roan had been turning over for weeks: *“When the mind begins to crack, the Rite holds the edges together.”*

It wasn't a healing technique. The language suggested something defensive, something structural, a framework that braced the mind against damage that couldn't be fixed with Elixirs or Heal Draughts. Mental damage. The kind that came from overexposure to Inversion, from pushing the Shard's conversion rate past thresholds that the Codex wasn't designed to handle.

Roan had been pushing. The match against Cyrus had required three reversals in under four minutes. The subsequent match against Selene had required two more. Five reversals in a single day—and the Vitalis influx from each one had flooded his system like a river breaching its banks. His body was still processing yesterday's overflow, converting raw energy into structure faster than his tissues could accommodate.

The conversion was still running clean. But the margin between stable processing and overload was narrowing. He could feel it in the way his thoughts moved—a slight hesitation between impulse and action, a half-second lag between intention and execution that hadn't been there three months ago.

The Codex Shard was converting side effects faster than his body could integrate the result. The excess Vitalis was bleeding into his Mental Radius, expanding his perception, his reaction speed, his ability to read intent before it became action. But expansion wasn't the same as stability. A balloon could expand until it didn't.

He found the entry on the third shelf, printed on a single loose sheet that had been folded and refolded so many times the creases had gone soft. The title was in old script, but someone had added a modern translation in pencil along the margin.

The Black Rite of Veyrn: Mental Foundation Protocol

Classification: Restricted. Tier 4+ recommended access

Brief: A framework for stabilizing Mental Radius during rapid expansion. Developed for practitioners of the Veyrn Discipline who required resilience against High-Order Inversion interference. Not a combat technique. Not a healing method. A load-bearing structure for the mind.

Roan read the first page twice. The introduction described a series of mental exercises, visualizations, self-referential anchoring, identity reinforcement protocols, designed to create what the text called “cognitive redundancies.” Redundant pathways. Backup versions of thought patterns. Ways to keep the mind coherent when the primary architecture was compromised.

The second page was a warning.

The Black Rite isn't without cost. Practitioners report emotional numbness that can persist through the stabilization period—but the original commentary noted dissipative counter-patterns for those who sought to restore access. This is a trade-off, not a permanent alteration. The mind can only hold so much structure. If you choose to reinforce your cognitive walls, you must accept that some windows will be shuttered in the process—shuttered, not sealed. With the right practice, the shutters can be opened again.

Roan stared at the paragraph.

Emotional numbness. Shuttered windows. The language was clinical, detached, written by someone who'd made the calculation and found it acceptable—but had also left a door open.

He thought about Brendt, standing in the training yard this morning, pale and shaking and refusing to sit down. He thought about the way anger moved through Brendt's body, visible, kinetic, fuel for everything the man did. He thought about what would happen to Brendt if someone built walls around that anger, framed it in cognitive structure, turned it into something manageable instead of something that burned.

Then he thought about himself. The half-second lag. The hesitation. The growing sense that his own mind was a structure under pressure, beams bending in a wind that kept picking up.

The Black Rite would fix part of the problem. The cost was defined, measurable, and the text suggested it didn't have to be permanent.

Roan took the sheet and folded it into his jacket. He didn't need to decide today. But he needed to have the option, and the option was something he couldn't afford to leave sitting on a shelf in a room that required a key he didn't technically have the right to use.

He left the restricted section. He returned the key to his pocket. He walked back through the library stacks in the late afternoon light, his boots quiet on the stone floor, the folded sheet pressing against his ribs like a second heartbeat.

Inversion gave. But the giving had a cost, and the cost was always structural, bone, blood, time, or mind. Every trade was on the ledger. Every exchange left a scar somewhere.

The question was which scars he could afford to carry.

Brendt needed the Elixir in three days. The tournament qualifiers were in two weeks. Cole was watching. The Ashfords were watching. Somewhere in the background, House Greel was running calculations of his own, and those calculations almost certainly included a boy who'd just made Cyrus Ashford look like an idiot in front of the entire Western Hemisphere's martial elite.

Roan walked out of the library and into the late sun. The sky over Ironholm was the color of old iron, streaked with the amber haze that gave the city its name. He had three days to make sure Brendt's delivery arrived without incident, six days until the Elixir could safely cycle, and fourteen days until the qualifiers, when he would have to fight at full capacity while pretending to be something significantly less.

The ledger was still working.

But math was a language, and like any language, it had grammar, and grammar had limits, and every system eventually confronted the sentence it couldn't complete.

He was going to need a plan for that sentence.

He just didn't have one yet.
