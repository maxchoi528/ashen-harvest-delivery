Chapter 178: Kael Accepts

The official answer arrived at 6:12 in the morning, which was discourteous.

Not because Roan was asleep. He hadn't done anything so optimistic. Discourtesy was a matter of form, and sending a duel acceptance before dawn told the recipient one of three things: the sender hadn't slept, the sender wanted people to think he hadn't slept, or the sender employed someone who confused theatrical timing with strategy.

Kael Greel's reply managed all three.

Roan read it once on the cracked slate beside his bed, then again because the first reading had irritated him enough to risk missing details. The message was written in formal challenge language, all polished nouns and inherited arrogance. Kael accepted the public arena. Kael accepted Black Spire neutral arbitration. Kael accepted the death-stop wards, the medical constraints, and the waiver of House complaint regarding injuries sustained within legal bounds.

He added one condition.

No outside equipment except registered personal weapons.

Roan looked at that line for a while.

Then he smiled slightly.

Not enough to be called amusement by anyone with healthy survival instincts. More the expression of a man finding a typo in a contract written by an enemy who had hired expensive lawyers.

Kael didn't understand what counted as equipment.

That wasn't entirely his fault. House students treated equipment as objects: swords, armor, pills, ward stones, focus bands, custom boots with irritatingly efficient grip patterns. They grew up in families where wealth had texture and weight. A Greel heir heard "outside equipment" and thought of hidden blades, alchemical patches, illegal stimulants, maybe a concealed rifle if the duel code had been written by someone pessimistic.

Roan heard "outside equipment" and thought of broadcast rights, medical procedure, phrasing of oath language, audience composition, public inference, and whether a sedative could be classified as a medical stabilizer if administered by arena staff before the duel under the pretense of preventing ice-pathway seizure.

Different childhoods taught different inventories.

He saved the acceptance, copied it to three separate archives, and sent one to Ines.

Her reply came nine seconds later.

*Already awake. Obviously.*

A second message followed.

*Clause 9 is sloppy. He thinks he trapped your knife. He trapped his own bloodline doctrine instead.*

Roan typed: *Explain.*

*Greel ice arts require open thermal rejection. Death-stop wards will read his recoil as hostile output unless he registers it as self-harm profile. If he does, arena logs record every backlash. If he doesn't, ward interference ruins his domain.*

Useful.

Roan replied: *Get the registration file.*

*Already bribing someone tasteful.*

*Tasteful bribery is usually overpriced.*

*That's why I bill you.*

He set the slate down.

Across the room, Brendt's empty bed had been made by hospital staff when they came to collect his spare clothing. They had folded the blanket too neatly. Brendt never made his bed like that. He slept like a man losing a wrestling match to furniture and woke as if he blamed the furniture for winning. The neatness looked less like order and more like absence pretending to help.

Roan considered throwing the blanket onto the floor.

Pointless.

He did it anyway.

The room improved.

A knock came at the door.

Roan checked the time. 6:17. Too early for Selene if she had slept. Too precise for Cyrus. Too polite for Drogan.

He opened it.

Iris Vell stood outside with a cup carrier, two coffees, and the expression of someone who had decided that apology was inefficient but still owed a substitute.

Roan looked at the coffees.

"I didn't poison them," she said.

"You brought two."

"I know your objection pattern."

"You think I won't drink mine, so yours is the proof sample."

"Yes."

"You could have poisoned yours with something you're immune to."

"I could have. Did I?"

"not."

"Progress."

He took the carrier and stepped aside.

Iris entered without comment. That was new. Earlier in the year, she had moved as if every room were an invitation to control the lighting. Now she moved like someone measuring exits. Truth had done that to her. Truth, and the discovery that her grandfather had been feeding her an inheritance built out of lies and dead students.

Roan had not forgiven her. Forgiveness was for people with spare emotional storage. But she had become useful, and more importantly, she had become angry in the correct direction.

Anger was a crude fuel. Directed properly, it moved heavy doors.

She looked at Brendt's bed. The blanket on the floor. She didn't ask.

Good.

"Kael accepted," she said.

"Everyone will know in ten minutes."

"Everyone knows now. The forum crashed for ninety seconds."

"That isn't a structural measure."

"It's at Black Spire."

Roan took one coffee, waited until Iris drank from hers, then took a small sip. Bitter. Cheap. Strong enough to qualify as an assault.

"You bribed the west arena feed team," Iris said.

Roan didn't answer.

"You also filed a preservation motion with the neutral arbitration office. That means the duel record can't be sealed by House petition for seventy-two hours after conclusion."

Still no answer.

"Selene Ardent submitted a procedural witness request at three in the morning. Cole approved it at four. Ines is trying to purchase thermal recoil logs from the medical ward, which means she already has a buyer for whatever comes out of this."

Roan drank more coffee.

Iris sat on Theon's abandoned desk chair. "You realize this isn't a duel anymore."

"It was never a duel."

"Yes. But Kael doesn't know that."

"He knows enough."

"No," Iris said. "He knows fighting. He knows House politics. He knows what Vorath taught him to know. He doesn't know poverty litigation."

Roan looked at her.

She lifted the coffee cup in a small salute. "I learned the phrase from your notes."

"Stop reading my notes."

"Stop leaving copies where people you blackmailed can find them."

"You found a decoy."

"Yes. It was insulting."

"It worked."

"Only because I wanted it to."

"That's what people usually say after it works."

For a moment, Iris almost smiled. Then her face settled again.

"Kael will try to kill you," she said.

"Yes."

"In the ring."

"Yes."

"The death-stop wards won't trigger fast enough if he uses Northern Stillness correctly."

That was information.

Roan set the coffee down. "You know the technique?"

"I know of it. My grandfather wanted Greel favor badly enough to collect things he shouldn't have been able to collect." Her fingers tightened around the cup. "Northern Stillness creates an absolute thermal suppression shell for less than two seconds. It isn't absolute zero. House propaganda lies. But for a Forged practitioner, it might as well be. Blood slows. Nerves misfire. Vitalis paths contract. Most duelists panic and overcycle. That makes the frost bite deeper."

"And the recoil?"

"Severe. It burns the user's channels cold. Greel children train with staged frostbite before they're old enough to sign waivers."

"Efficient parenting."

"Vorath calls it inheritance."

Roan considered that.

Northern Stillness: short duration, high dominance, self-damaging recoil, proud family doctrine. Kael would use it because not using it would look like fear. He would register the recoil as self-harm because House pride required formal acknowledgment of mastery. The arena would log every backlash if Ines obtained access. The Shard could invert Roan's borrowed exposure if he allowed the frost into his own channels, but only within the registration-window acceptance window. Too early and the effect would fail. Too late and his blood would lose enough speed to make reaction academic.

A clean rule. A sharp risk.

RoyalRoad readers would approve, if Roan had been person to think in platform terms. He wasn't. He thought in failure modes.

"What does Kael think I will do?" he asked.

"Use Sidewound to cut through the domain boundary."

"Can I?"

"once."

"Then he has a counter."

"Yes."

"Which means I should do it once visibly and fail in a controlled way."

Iris stared at him.

Roan waited.

"That," she said slowly, "is a deranged way to use lethal information."

"No. A deranged way would be doing it twice."

She looked down at her coffee. "I used to think you were confident because you had a secret."

"I have several."

"I know."

"No, you don't."

"Fair." She leaned back. "But that isn't what I mean. You don't act like someone certain he'll survive. You act like someone who has already priced not surviving and found it inconvenient."

Roan thought about that.

It wasn't wrong. He disliked inaccurate criticism, but accurate criticism from compromised assets was merely unpleasant.

"I need Kael alive," he said.

"I know."

"No permanent crippling."

"That's generous."

"No. It's prosecutable."

Iris laughed once, short and sharp. "There he's."

Roan picked up the slate. "What else?"

She hesitated.

That was rare enough to matter.

"Vorath will be watching."

"Yes."

"No. Not politically. Not through agents. Watching."

Roan went still.

Iris's grandfather's files had contained references to Greel long-distance observation rites. Most were expensive and limited, then illegal under Spire neutrality agreements if directed at a student combat event. Which meant Vorath would use one if provoked and trust no one could prove it.

"What signature?" Roan asked.

"Gold-red thermal bleed in glass and ice. Cameras may distort. Frost mirrors may form along ward pylons. If you see a reflection that's warmer than the room, don't meet its eyes."

"Helpful."

"I considered telling you after the duel."

"Why didn't you?"

She smiled without humor. "Because I would like Vorath Greel to lose something, and you are the most unpleasant available instrument."

That was honest enough.

Roan lifted the coffee. "Accepted."

- break -

By noon, Black Spire had divided itself into factions with the speed and stupidity of a student body given official permission to speculate.

The duel announcement appeared on the forum first as a dry notice from the arbitration office. Within twelve minutes, it had generated six thousand comments, three unauthorized tactical breakdowns, two petitions to move the duel to a larger arena, and one thread arguing that Roan had challenged Kael because of Selene Ardent.

Roan reported that thread for poor reasoning.

The moderators rejected the report.

Naturally.

He spent the afternoon in the medical annex, not because Brendt would wake, he wouldn't, according to the healers, but because several people expected Roan to avoid the room for fear of emotional instability. It was important to disappoint expectations when they belonged to enemies.

Brendt lay under a layered healing lattice, his chest rising with machine-assisted steadiness. The wound had closed, but the healers had left faint silver lines across the skin where Greel cold had tried to stop the tissue from remembering warmth. His face looked wrong without expression. Brendt was built out of reactions. Remove them and he looked like a sketch of himself.

Roan sat beside the bed and reviewed Kael's public combat record.

Selene arrived at 2:08.

She wore the Ardent uniform without ornament, silver hair tied back, face composed in the way House children learned before they learned multiplication. She stopped at the foot of Brendt's bed and was quiet for a moment.

"His mother called again," she said.

"I know."

"You answered?"

"Yes."

Selene looked at him. "What did you tell her?"

"The truth allowed by medical privacy."

"That's a terrible phrase."

"Yes."

"What did you say?"

"That he was alive, stable and unconscious, plus that the person responsible had accepted a legal duel tomorrow."

Selene closed her eyes briefly. "Roan."

"She asked."

"She asked whether her son was going to be okay."

"No. She asked whether the people who did this would get away with it."

"And you said?"

"No."

The room hummed with healing equipment.

Selene's gaze moved from Brendt to Roan's slate. "Kael's reply was too clean."

"Yes."

"Vorath approved it."

"Likely."

"Then this isn't only Kael being young and proud."

"No. It's Kael being young and proud inside a plan designed by someone older."

She nodded once, accepting the correction. "House Ardent files confirm Greel doctrine requires public dominance. If Kael refuses after the challenge, he loses standing. If he accepts and loses cleanly, he loses standing. His only viable outcome is to kill you or force you into illegal overreach."

"Good."

Selene stared. "That isn't good."

"It narrows his options."

"It also narrows yours."

"I need fewer options. Too many encourage waste."

She was silent for a moment.

Then she said, "You are going to let him almost win."

Roan didn't answer.

Selene's jaw tightened. "That isn't a guess."

"It's a correct inference."

"Why?"

"Because if I beat him without showing danger, Vorath claims Kael was incompetent. If Kael nearly kills me using Greel doctrine, then confesses under public record, Vorath has to choose between disowning his heir's competence and admitting the order. Both cost him."

"And the cost to you?"

"Variable."

"Roan."

He looked up.

Selene's expression hadn't broken, but the room around her seemed to have. Small details gave it away. Her left hand had curled against her sleeve. Her breathing was measured too carefully. House discipline wrapped around actual concern until both became difficult to separate.

This was one of the reasons trust was inconvenient. People began objecting to efficient risks because they cared who paid them.

Roan considered lying.

He decided against it. Not out of tenderness. Selene had supplied files and witnesses, plus House risk. She had earned accurate information within the agreed circle.

"If Northern Stillness touches my channels," he said, "I can turn the recoil into reinforcement. But I need contact. Not surface frost. Internal contraction."

Selene's face went very still.

"You are going to let a Greel ice art reach your meridians."

"Briefly."

"That's insane."

"No. Insane would be doing it without the sedative variable."

"What sedative variable?"

Roan returned to the slate. "You don't need that part."

"I absolutely do."

"No. You need plausible denial."

Selene stared at him, then looked at Brendt.

"Is this because of him?"

"Yes."

The answer came too fast.

Roan disliked that. Speed revealed load-bearing truths.

Selene heard it too.

She didn't smile. She didn't soften. She nodded, as if recording a line in a ledger.

"Then win," she said.

"That's the plan."

"No," Selene said. "Win and come back able to explain the parts you refused to explain."

Roan thought about that.

"That's a larger plan."

"Consider it an amended contract."

"Terms?"

"You survive. I keep helping."

"That's coercive."

"Yes."

He looked at her for another second.

"Accepted."

- break -

Kael Greel held his acceptance briefing at five in the afternoon.

Not officially. Officially, it was a student interview outside the north practice hall, arranged by a Spire media club and watched by several hundred students who had accidentally chosen the same corridor at the same time. Unofficially, it was a House Greel position statement wearing a student face.

Roan watched from Ines's workroom through three different feeds, none legal.

Kael looked excellent on camera. That was annoying but predictable. He stood tall in the black-and-silver training uniform, pale hair tied back, eyes clear, posture relaxed enough to imply superiority without visibly trying. He had the Greel coldness, but not Vorath's old predator weight. Kael was a blade still proud of being sharp.

A student reporter asked, "Do you regret accepting a duel with Roan Vance so soon after the off-campus incident involving Brendt Holloway?"

Good. Someone had taught the media club to carry knives.

Kael's expression shifted by exactly nothing. "I regret that private conflicts have been distorted by rumor."

Ines, seated beside Roan with one boot on the table, snorted. "Private conflicts. Charming."

On screen, the reporter pressed. "Are you denying involvement?"

"I am saying the arena exists because young practitioners require lawful places to resolve disputes. Vance challenged me. I accepted. That's all tomorrow needs to be."

Not bad.

Kael wasn't stupid. He refused to deny, because denial could be disproved. He reframed, because reframing absorbed pressure. He used "lawful" to make Roan's challenge sound emotional and archaic.

Competent.

Better.

Roan preferred ruining competent enemies. It produced clearer records.

Another reporter asked whether Kael feared Roan's tournament record.

Kael smiled faintly. "Vance is talented."

The hallway murmured.

"He's also," Kael continued, "new to the level of conflict he has chosen."

That would play well with House students and badly with everyone who remembered Roan breaking Cyrus. It implied Roan had won school fights and was stepping into bloodline war. Accurate enough to be useful.

Roan made a note.

Ines leaned closer. "He's baiting you toward proving you belong."

"Yes."

"Will you?"

"No."

"Good. Belonging is expensive."

The final question came from someone near the back. The camera angle shifted, catching only a sleeve.

"Kael, did House Greel authorize your transfer?"

The hallway quieted.

Kael looked toward the unseen questioner.

"Every House child carries their House," he said.

A polished answer. Too polished.

Roan rewound the feed ten seconds and watched again. Not Kael's face. His right hand.

There.

When asked about authorization, Kael's thumb touched the inside of his wrist. Not nervous. Reflexive. Checking a pulse point? No. A seal mark, perhaps hidden under the cuff. A communications oath? A bloodline command brand?

Roan zoomed.

The resolution was poor, but a faint gold-red thread pulsed beneath the skin.

Ines sat up.

"Well," she said. "That seems illegal."

"Can you prove it?"

"Not from this."

"Get me a better angle."

"Already trying."

The feed ended with Kael saying he trusted Black Spire's arbitration and looked forward to a clean exchange.

Roan closed the screen.

Clean exchange.

Of course.

The cleaner a noble wanted something to look, the more likely someone had been paid to mop blood before guests arrived.

He stood.

Ines looked up. "Where are you going?"

"Medical annex."

"You were just there."

"I need Brendt's admission timestamp."

"For what?"

Roan paused at the door.

"Kael's wrist seal is likely keyed to the order. If the order predates Brendt's injury, it proves intent before duel challenge. If it postdates, it proves cover authorization."

Ines stared.

Then she grinned. "You are very unpleasant."

"Yes."

"Never change."

"I'll bill you for advice."

He left before she could answer.

The corridor outside was crowded with students pretending not to watch him. They looked away too late. Whispers followed. Some sounded excited. Some afraid. Some hungry for spectacle in the way safe people enjoyed danger happening to someone else.

Roan ignored them.

At the stairwell, Cyrus Ashford waited.

That was inconvenient.

Cyrus looked thinner than he had before Kael's arrival, or perhaps sharper. His uniform was immaculate. His eyes weren't. They carried the sleepless brightness of someone who had been given a method and mistaken it for purpose.

"Vance," Cyrus said.

Roan stopped two steps away. "No."

Cyrus blinked. "I haven't said anything."

"You were about to."

"Kael will kill you."

"Better. You saved time."

Cyrus's jaw worked. "You think this is another game."

"No. Games usually have clearer rules."

"You don't understand what he's."

"I understand what he's using you for."

That hit.

Cyrus stepped forward. "You don't know anything about me."

"I know you were broken by losing publicly, not by being wrong. I know Kael offered you a doctrine that lets you rename humiliation as training. I know he gives correction instead of pity, which makes you think he respects you. He may even respect parts of you. That doesn't make you less useful to him."

Cyrus's face went pale.

Several nearby students stopped pretending not to listen.

Roan should have walked away. Publicly dissecting Cyrus served no immediate purpose, and humiliation without strategic need violated efficiency if not his code. But there was a strategic need, unfortunately. Cyrus was a handle Kael had picked up. Handles could be greased, loosened, or broken.

Roan chose loosened.

"If he asks you to testify tomorrow," Roan said, quieter, "read the wording before you agree."

Cyrus stared.

"He won't lie to you. He'll give you a sentence where every word is true and the whole thing makes you owned."

"You expect me to believe you're warning me?"

"No. I expect you to dislike being used slightly more than you dislike me."

Cyrus didn't answer.

Roan stepped around him.

At the stairwell door, Cyrus spoke again.

"Why not just kill him?"

Roan looked back.

Cyrus's expression was raw enough to be honest. He wanted the answer, not for Kael. For himself.

"Because dead heirs become tragedies," Roan said. "Living witnesses become evidence."

He left Cyrus standing there.

- break -

At 11:43 that night, Roan finally received Ines's better angle.

The image showed Kael's wrist during the interview, captured by a student's bracelet camera reflected in a trophy case. Grainy. Oblique. Good enough.

A gold-red mark coiled beneath the skin in the shape of a narrow crown split by a vertical line.

Greel command oath.

More than House affiliation. More than family blessing.

Command.

Roan stared at the image until the Codex Shard pulsed once against his spine.

[Foreign oath structure detected.]

[Lineage hostility: confirmed.]

[Inversion vector: indirect.]

Indirect.

Interesting.

He opened his notebook and adjusted the plan.

Not because the old plan had failed. Because a better knife had been found in the drawer.

The sedative variable wouldn't loosen Kael's control under frost recoil. It would interact with the command oath. Kael's own technique would freeze his channels. Roan would invert the self-harm contact and feed the backlash into the oath seam. Not enough to break it. Breaking it might damage Kael permanently or alert Vorath too early. Enough to make the oath stutter at the moment of defeat.

A stuttering command oath did what damaged oaths often did.

It tried to discharge truth.

Risk: high. If mistimed, Kael retained control and Roan got frozen from inside out. If overdone, Kael's mind could fracture, violating the duel's medical limits and giving Greel grounds to claim illegal mental attack. If underdone without confession. Reward: public record of authorization.

Cost: one or more Inversion quotas, possible exposure risk spike, exposure of an interaction pattern Vorath might recognize.

Roan wrote the sequence in small, neat script.

Then he crossed out half of it.

Too complicated.

Plans should be complex before battle and simple during it. The body couldn't execute paragraphs while freezing.

He reduced it to three lines.

Let frost in.

Cut oath seam.

Ask one question.

Better.

The Codex Shard pulsed again.

[Warning: oath-linked inversion may increase recognition risk.]

Roan wrote beneath the warning:

Vorath already watching.

The Shard didn't respond.

He placed the notebook on the desk and looked at Brendt's blanket on the floor.

Tomorrow, Kael would try to kill him. Tomorrow, Vorath would watch. Tomorrow, Black Spire would become a courtroom with a combat ring in the middle and a student body serving unwillingly as jury.

Good.

Witnesses made crimes heavier.

Roan turned off the light and lay down without getting under the blanket.

He slept for two hours and forty minutes.

That would have to do.
