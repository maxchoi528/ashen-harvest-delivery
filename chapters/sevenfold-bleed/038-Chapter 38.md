Chapter 38: The Score He Tried to Hide

The ranking board update was thirty seconds late.

Roan noticed. The other forty-seven students in the combat pavilion didn't.

He stood in the third row from the back, Brendt two paces to his left, and watched the digital display mounted above the instructor's platform cycle through the weekly Vitalis rankings. His name sat at forty-three. Up three positions from last week. A display of 847 units, a modest showing, deliberately modest. The air in the pavilion carried the faint metallic tang of discharged Vitalis, a static charge that clung to the training stone.

The figures were plain. The Black Spire ranked students by monthly gains, not absolute totals. A student who started at eight hundred and climbed to nine hundred looked more impressive than one who started at twelve hundred and climbed to twelve-fifty. Roan had spent the last two months suppressing his actual output, taking only the assignments that required minimum participation, letting his displayed Vitalis creep upward at the pace of an average Initiate.

He needed another four weeks. Maybe five. Enough time to finish building the buffer between his real capacity and his public number, so that when he finally let the mask slip, the gap would be attributed to hidden talent rather than hidden power.

"The rankings will reset at the end of the month," Instructor Vane announced from the platform. The man stood with his hands clasped behind his back, his Hardened-tier Vitalis radiating a faint pressure that made the younger students shift uncomfortably. "Those of you in the bottom quartile will face remedial evaluation. Those in the top ten will receive priority access to the Tier-2 training chambers."

Roan's expression didn't change. Tier-2 chambers were useful for students who needed external stimulation to break through. He didn't.

"What about the mid-range students?" someone asked from the second row. Cyrus Ashford. The name carried weight at Black Spire. House Ashford had funded three of the combat pavilion's seven training halls. "Do they just rot?"

Instructor Vane's jaw tightened. "Mid-range students are encouraged to seek supplemental training. Office hours are posted on the board."

"Encouraged." Cyrus's tone made the word sound like a death sentence. He stood with his shoulders squared, his Vitalis reading displayed at 1,247 on the public board, the eighteenth highest in their cohort. Not the highest. But close enough to matter.

Roan calculated the curve without shifting his gaze. Cyrus had gained a hundred and forty Vitalis in the past week. At that rate, Cyrus would hit two thousand by the end of the semester. Top five. Not a threat yet. Worth monitoring.

"The rest of you, dismissed to your assigned training blocks." Instructor Vane turned away from the platform. "Pair assignments have been posted. I expect improvement reports by Friday."

The crowd dispersed. Roan stayed where he was, watching the board cycle through the names. Brendt shifted beside him.

"You're ranked lower than me again," Brendt said. He didn't sound smug. He sounded concerned. "That's not right. I've seen you in the lower halls. You move like someone who's hit Tempered."

Roan didn't look at him. *Brendt read the movement because he knew the baseline. The Envoy read the number on the board. The gap between those two lenses was a vulnerability he hadn't fully closed.* "I've been pacing myself."

"Pacing yourself into remedial evaluation territory?" Brendt's voice dropped. "Theon Mercer is ranked fifty-one. Below you. If you drop any further—"

"Then I'll stop pacing."

Brendt stared at him. "What are you doing?"

The honest answer was: building a foundation so deep that when it finally became visible, it would look like a mountain rising from flat ground. The dishonest answer was easier.

"Protecting something." Roan finally turned from the board. "Come on. We're going to be late for the lower hall."

The lower training hall smelled of sweat and old blood. The Black Spire maintained three tiers of training facilities: the upper halls for Tempered and above, the middle halls for Initiates approaching breakthrough, and the lower halls for students still building their baseline. The lower halls were functional. They weren't comfortable.

Roan preferred them for that reason.

He and Brendt stepped through the reinforced door and immediately split. Brendt moved toward the free-weight stations on the eastern wall, his 1,020 Vitalis reading making him a mid-tier presence in this space. Roan moved toward the catalyst dispensary near the back, where the approved training enhancers were distributed to students who had completed the liability waivers.

He didn't need the catalysts. But he needed the appearance of needing them.

"Roan Vance." The voice came from the weapon racks along the north wall. "Didn't expect to see you down here."

The Greel envoy leaned against the rack, his arms crossed, his Vitalis reading displayed at 1,089. Not impressive by Greel family standards — the House had three sons, and the envoy was the middle child, neither the heir nor the spare. But he was Hardened-tier, which meant he could crush most of the students in this room without shifting his weight.

Roan kept walking. "Lost?"

"I heard you requested assignment to the lowest-tier combat simulations." The envoy pushed off from the rack and fell into step beside him. "For the third month running. That's either humility or stupidity."

"Could be efficiency." Roan reached the dispensary and tapped his student ID against the reader. The machine hummed. "The lower simulations are less crowded. Shorter wait times."

"And easier to cheat." The envoy's smile didn't reach his eyes. "I wonder what you're doing down here. What you're hiding."

The dispensary clicked open. Inside, three vials of standard-tier Vitalis stimulant waited. Roan took them without looking at the envoy.

"I'm hiding nothing." He turned the vials over in his hands, examining them with the careful attention of someone who cared about their quality. "I'm not showing."

"That's the same thing."

"No." Roan met his gaze for the first time. "Hiding implies fear. Not showing implies strategy. They're different."

The envoy's expression flickered. For a moment, something cold moved behind his eyes — not anger, but calculation. The Greel family trained their children to think before they acted. The envoy wasn't the family's best student, but he wasn't the worst either.

"You're strange, Vance." He stepped back, giving Roan room. "Most students in your position would be scrambling for advantage. Using their family's name, their connections. You're out here pretending to be nothing."

"I'm not pretending." Roan pocketed the vials and turned toward the simulation pods. "I'm measuring."

"Measuring what?"

"The gap between potential and expectation." He didn't slow down. "And how wide it needs to be before it becomes useful."

\[break\]

The simulation pod was small and dark, the air faintly antiseptic. Roan sealed the door behind him and settled into the neural interface harness, letting the Black Spire's combat simulation system calibrate to his baseline.

"Simulation type?" the system asked in a neutral, synthesized voice.

"Adaptive response. Tier-one difficulty. No external assistance."

"Loading."

The world dissolved.

When it reformed, he stood in a flat circular arena surrounded by featureless grey walls. Across from him, a training construct materialized — humanoid, mid-height, carrying a standard-issue training blade. The construct's Vitalis reading flickered to life above its head: 750.

Roan didn't move.

The construct charged.

He watched it come. The footwork was mechanical, the blade angle slightly off-center, the weight distribution favoring the right side. In a real fight, these flaws would be exploitable. In a simulation designed to test baseline combat response, they were irrelevant — the construct's purpose wasn't to be challenging but to be predictable.

The blade swung toward his left shoulder.

Roan stepped forward, inside the arc, and struck the construct's wrist with the heel of his palm. The joint hyperextended. The blade clattered to the ground. The construct stumbled, off-balance, and Roan drove his elbow into its sternum.

Vitalis: 847.

Simulation difficulty: Tier one.

Result: Complete dominance.

He had known it would be this way. That wasn't arrogance. That was arithmetic.

"End simulation," he said.

The arena dissolved. The pod reopened. Roan sat in the harness for a moment, letting his breathing settle, feeling the weight of the Codex Shard against his chest. The system prompt flickered at the edge of his awareness.

\[Simulation complete. Adaptive response tier exceeded. Record masked.\]
\[Current Vitalis variance: stable.\]
\[Concealed capacity: retained.\]

He unstrapped from the harness and stepped out of the pod.

Brendt was waiting for him, leaning against the wall with his arms crossed. His expression was unreadable.

"You're done already?"

"The simulation was short."

"Short." Brendt pushed off the wall. "You haven't run a short simulation since you got here. The shortest one I've ever seen you do was forty minutes."

Roan moved past him toward the exit. "I'm adjusting my training regimen."

"You're hiding something."

"I'm managing a process."

"They're the same thing."

Roan stopped at the door. He didn't turn around. "Brendt. How long have we known each other?"

"Since orientation. Four months."

"In four months, have I ever lied to you?"

Brendt was quiet for a moment. "No."

"Have I ever betrayed a trust?"

"No."

"Have I ever put you in danger without warning you first?"

"...No."

"Then whatever you think I'm hiding—" Roan finally turned, meeting Brendt's gaze. "—it's not a betrayal. It's a protection. The less you know, the less you can be compelled to give up. That's not distrust. That's math."

Brendt's jaw worked. He wasn't angry. He was frustrated — which was worse, because it meant he understood the logic and still didn't like it.

"The less I know, the less I can help you," Brendt said. "That's not protection. That's isolation."

"Sometimes those are the same thing too."

Roan pushed through the door before Brendt could respond.

The corridor outside the lower training hall was empty. Most students had migrated to the dining hall for the midday meal, or to their assigned training blocks in the middle-tier facilities. Roan walked alone, his footsteps echoing against the polished concrete. The lanterns here didn't burn flame. They cycled surplus Vitalis in a steady pulse — bright, dim, bright — leaving a constant low thrum that moved through the stone like a second heartbeat.

He could feel the attention as he walked. Not physical, not visual, but something deeper. The weight of observation that came from places he couldn't identify. The Black Spire had eyes everywhere: instructors, proctors, administrative staff, students embedded as informants for various Houses. Any one of them could be reporting his movements to someone with an interest in House Vance's surviving heir.

He turned a corner and found her waiting.

Selene Ardent stood directly in his path, arms crossed, no pretense of coincidence. Her silver hair caught the cycling light. Ranked seventh in their cohort with a displayed Vitalis of 1,156. One of the three students at Black Spire most likely to be working as an intelligence asset for one of the old houses.

"Drogan ran full-background sweeps on our cohort last month," she said without preamble. "Deep-level audits. Financial records. Family service histories. The Warden doesn't move like that unless someone above him is applying pressure."

Roan kept his voice flat. "You're reading sealed Warden reports."

"I have people in administration who process the deep-audit queue. The exception list is what's interesting. Drogan flagged three profiles. He only has cause to flag one."

"Mine."

"The Vance estate is a closed book, Vance. The Greels picked it clean twelve years ago. But you're still breathing, you're still enrolled, and the Warden is still digging. The Houses think you're a footnote. I think you're a catalyst for whatever comes next between them."

"And if I am?"

"Then your perimeter is weak. The Greels have an envoy in the building. You need better intelligence than what the lower halls can provide. An information-sharing pact. Your blind spots for mine."

Roan filed the offer. "Your blind spots are hard to quantify. You have a network in administration, but you're missing the core piece. What did Drogan actually flag? Bring me the findings. The raw audit exception list. The cross-references he ran. Then I'll calculate the terms of the exchange."

"How do I know you'll reciprocate?"

"You don't. But you know my position isn't tied to any House. The Ardent name has no stake in the Vance massacre. I'm an independent variable. That's the closest thing to safe you're going to find in Black Spire."

He stepped past her.

"Three days," Selene said. "I'll have the list in three days."

"You know where to find me."

The training yards were crowded by the time Roan arrived. The afternoon session drew students from all tiers — the open-air arenas filled with sparring matches, technique demonstrations, and the occasional brawl that the instructors pretended not to see. The stone underfoot was worn into wide, shallow arcs. The accumulated scoring of decades of Tempered-grade blades dragged across the same motion. The light came from a single mage-luminary hung so high in the rafters it turned every shadow into a long, depthless grey.

Roan moved through the crowd toward the eastern edge, where a row of meditation alcoves offered partial privacy. He chose one near the back and settled into a cross-legged position, pulling the three vials from his pocket. Standard Vitalis stimulants. Legal, common, unremarkable. Things a struggling student would buy to maintain their training pace. Things a student who didn't need them would buy to maintain their cover.

He uncapped the first vial and drank it in one swallow.

The effect was immediate. Heat moved through his ribs, his Vitalis reading ticking upward on the display he had activated on his wrist. 847. 851. 855. The stimulants worked by accelerating the body's natural Vitalis circulation, pushing more energy through the meridians in a shorter time. The side effect was a slight fever and a drying of the mouth. Nothing serious.

Nothing the Codex Shard would bother converting.

He wasn't here to train. He was here to be seen training.

The second vial went down. The third. His Vitalis held steady at 863, the stimulants pushing him toward the upper limit of his displayed range. Around him, the training yards churned with activity. Students sparred. Instructors critiqued. The afternoon sun beat down on the open stone.

And at the edge of his perception, the observation continued.

Roan closed his eyes. He didn't need to react to it. He only needed to wait. The variance between the ranking board and his real capacity was still too narrow. Let it widen.

A shadow fell across the alcove. Roan opened his eyes.

Theon Mercer stood at the entrance, his Vitalis reading displayed at 721 — the forty-eighth ranking, one step above the remedial cutoff. His expression was strained. His hands were clenched at his sides.

"Vance." Theon's voice was low, urgent. "I need to talk to you."

Roan didn't move. "About what?"

"About what the Greel envoy asked me to do last night."

The words hung in the air. Roan didn't react. He ran the scenario like a problem paper. Theon was an unlisted expenditure. The envoy recruiting a student was standard procedure — predictable. Theon coming to him first was a deviation. A variable that could be turned. If Theon fed the envoy fabricated data, the envoy's confidence would outpace his accuracy. The margin for error in the trap would tighten, but the probability of the envoy committing a fatal misstep would rise.

He chose the tighter margin.

"I'm listening."

Theon glanced over his shoulder. The training yards were still busy, but they had drifted toward the edge, away from the main clusters. No one was close enough to hear without effort.

"He asked me to report on your training patterns. Who you talk to. What you do in the simulation pods. How long you spend in the lower halls." Theon's jaw tightened. "He said House Greel would reward me. Priority access to the Tier-2 chambers. Maybe a recommendation for the junior rift-hunting program."

"And?"

"And I said yes."

Roan waited.

"But I haven't reported anything yet." Theon's hands unclenched, then clenched again. "Because I don't understand you, Vance. I've been watching you for three months. You're not what they think you are. You're not what anyone thinks you are. And the Greel envoy is dangerous. If I start feeding him information—"

"You'll be complicit in whatever happens next."

"Yes."

Roan stood slowly, unfolding from the meditation pose with deliberate grace. He was taller than Theon by three inches. Not enough to matter. But enough to be noticed.

"Why are you telling me this?"

Theon hesitated. "Because I don't want to be on the wrong side of whatever you're planning. And because—" He stopped. Started again. "Because you beat me in the third-week sparring assessment. I had thirty pounds on you. Better technique. Higher Vitalis. And you dismantled me in under a minute without ever looking like you were trying."

Roan remembered the match. He had kept it close, let Theon think he was winning until the final exchange, when he had stripped away the pretense and demonstrated exactly how far the gap was. It was a mistake — showing that kind of ability, even to a low-ranking student, created risk.

But it had also created an opportunity.

"The Greel envoy doesn't know what I am," Roan said quietly. "Neither do you. But you're going to tell him exactly what he wants to hear."

Theon's brow furrowed. "What?"

"You'll report that I've been hiding in the lower halls. That my Vitalis is barely above remedial threshold. That I seem nervous, distracted, unstable after the anniversary of my family's death." Roan's voice was flat, precise. "Give him enough truth to seem credible. Give him enough weakness to seem safe."

"And then?"

"And then you watch what happens when he believes it." Roan's voice carried no threat. It carried a statement of fact. "Do we have an understanding?"

Theon swallowed. "Yes."

"Good." Roan stepped past him, moving back toward the training yards. "Next time the envoy asks, tell him I'm getting worse. Tell him my simulation times are slipping. Tell him you saw me coughing in the corridor yesterday."

"You weren't coughing."

"I will be tomorrow."

He left Theon standing in the alcove and walked back into the crowd, his display holding at 863, the scored stone arcs of the training yard stretching beneath his feet. The feed had been compromised before the enemy had finished wiring it. The envoy wanted to know what Roan was hiding.

He would soon believe he had found the answer.
