Chapter 41: The Black Spire Board

The morning air carried the smell of forged iron and mineral oil. Greycross was waking in stages, shutters creaking open, cart wheels on cobblestone, a dog barking somewhere near the tanner's district. Roan Vance sat on the edge of his bed with his boots already laced and his coat buttoned to the throat.

Two days since the Thornfield contract. Two days since he'd inverted the Bloodscourge backlash and walked away with the Greel envoy's knife at his belt. The Sentinel Bureau hadn't come knocking. The Guild hadn't filed a formal inquiry. Either the body had been cleaned up before anyone noticed, or the white-bearded man was choosing patience over noise.

Neither option was comforting.

Brendt had been by twice. The second time he'd brought soup from the refectory and a list of things he thought Roan should be worried about. Roan had listened to all of it, nodded at appropriate intervals, and filed none of it. Brendt's concerns were valid. They were also problems for future Roan. Present Roan had different priorities.

The black iron anchor rested against his chest beneath his shirt, a familiar weight. The Shard was hungry, waiting for a trigger. He was Tempered now, officially. Drogan had filed the paperwork three days ago. The Spire's records showed a standard progression from Initiate to Tempered: eighteen months of training, average Vitalis readout of 890, standard aptitude markers.

The records didn't show the part where that progression had taken forty-six days and involved three near-deaths.

Roan stood, crossed to the window, and looked out at Greycross. The morning markets were setting up in the square below. Merchants calling prices. Children running between the stalls. Ordinary people living ordinary lives. The Spire rose in the distance, a black needle against the pale sky.

He had two months until the Ironholm qualifying tournament. Two months to decide whether he was going to attend as a Tempered from a minor house, or find a way to close the gap on the Ashfords and Vells and Greels.

The figures said he couldn't catch them through normal training. His family's account was depleted. The Black Spire stipend covered tuition and basic materials, nothing more. His fellow disciples were pulling in sponsorships, family artifacts, expensive catalysts. He was working with the standard issue equipment and whatever he could scrounge from the training yards.

The ledger also said he had something nobody else in his cohort had: a system that turned poison into medicine and waste into gain.

He'd been collecting rejected catalysts for three weeks. The ones nobody else wanted, contaminated stock, off-brand brews, anything with a warning label. The other disciples dumped the failures into the discard bins. Roan bought those bins for pennies on the credit.

The discard bin near the Tempered laboratory held the real treasures. Second-rate catalysts that didn't meet quality standards but weren't dangerous enough to warrant destruction. Most of them would give a standard cultivator modest gains at best and uncomfortable side effects at worst.

Most cultivators didn't have a Shard embedded in their spine.

Right.

He checked the small pouch at his belt, eight contaminated vials, purchased for a total of twelve credits. The merchant had practically given them away. The merchant also hadn't asked questions, which Roan appreciated.

He pulled on his coat and left the room.

- break -

The laboratory annex sat behind the main spire hall, a squat stone building with ventilation grates along the roof. Morning sunlight fell across the courtyard in long rectangles. A handful of disciples stood near the entrance, chatting in low voices. None of them looked at Roan as he approached.

He walked past them into the building.

The interior smelled of sulfur and something faintly sweet. Workbenches lined the walls, most of them occupied by students grinding, mixing, or pouring. A few senior disciples occupied the back stations with their privacy curtains drawn. Roan found his assigned bench near the window and set down his materials.

Eight vials. Eight potential gains. Eight opportunities to push his numbers higher without spending credits he didn't have.

He pulled the first vial from the pouch and examined it. The liquid inside was murky green, with sediment settled at the bottom. A standard Catalyst Identification scroll would tell him exactly what this was. Roan didn't have a scroll, but he had something better.

He uncorked the vial and took a small sip, holding it on his tongue.

The taste was bitter, almost metallic. His stomach clenched. The first wave of side effects hit within seconds, a hot prickling sensation along his meridians, a spike of nausea, a dull pressure behind his eyes.

Standard stuff. Liver toxicity. Most cultivators would stop here and wait for the discomfort to pass. They'd lose maybe thirty minutes of training time and feel vaguely unwell for the rest of the day.

Roan let the sensation build. Counted to three.

The Codex Shard activated.

[Sidewound detected.]

[Converting.]

[Toxic accumulation reduced. Vitalis +8.]

The nausea faded. The pressure behind his eyes became a sharp clarity. His meridians stopped prickling and started humming.

Roan set the vial aside and reached for the second.

Different compound. Different side effects. He tasted it, catalogued the sensations, let the Shard do its work. This one hit his bloodstream faster. His heart rate climbed. His palms flushed hot.

[Sidewound detected.]

[Conversion: cardiac overstrain inverted.]

[Minor Vitalis surge: +14.]

Two down. His hands were steady, his mind clear. The third vial was a thick amber liquid, looked like old honey, smelled like burnt resin. He uncorked it.

The taste was worse than the first two combined. His teeth ached. His jaw locked for half a second before releasing. A grinding sensation traveled down his spine, settling somewhere in his lower back.

That one was new.

He waited for the Shard to process. The sensation persisted, not painful, exactly, but unfamiliar. A grinding that didn't resolve into anything recognizable.

[Sidewound type partially unrecognized.]

[Partial conversion: +3 Vitalis.]

[Warning: residual sediment detected in meridians.]

[Clear within 48 hours. Probably. — A.V.]

Roan stared at the amber liquid. The Shard's warning was ambiguous—*probably* harmless meant a non-zero chance of harm. But the alternative was wasting it, and he didn't have credits to burn. Two months until Ironholm. He couldn't afford to leave potential gains on the table.

He drank the rest of it.

The grinding sensation intensified for a moment, then faded to a dull background presence. Like having a pebble in his boot, noticeable, but not debilitating.

Two more vials. He worked through them methodically, one after another. The fourth was a clear liquid that tasted like salt water and left a ringing in his ears. The ringing became silence became a strange sharpening of his hearing.

[Sidewound: auditory strain.]

[Conversion: enhanced sonic processing.]

[Bonus: +11 Vitalis.]

He set the fourth vial down. Four vials processed, four held in reserve. The equation remained honest. It also didn't brag.

He capped the remaining vials and tucked them into his pouch. The grinding sensation in his lower back hadn't faded. The Codex Shard had flagged it as *probably* harmless, which meant there was a non-zero chance it was harmful. Aldric's sense of humor in the system messages had always run toward the dark.

Roan stood and stretched, then walked to the water basin at the corner of the room. He splashed cold water on his face and studied his reflection in the copper basin.

Seventeen years old. Tempered rank. Vitalis readout somewhere between 1,200 and 1,400 depending on how you measured. Looking like a slightly tired version of every other disciple in the room.

Nobody would look at him and see a threat. That was the point.

He walked back to his bench, capped the remaining vials, and tucked them into his pouch. The grinding sensation pulsed once, a slow beat like a second heartbeat somewhere in his spine.

Right.

Drogan would want a report on the Thornfield contract. He'd been putting off that conversation for two days, but the Warden didn't appreciate delays. If Roan was going to explain the disappearance of the Greel envoy's scout team, he needed a story. The truth wasn't an option. A partial truth might work.

He gathered his things and left the laboratory.

- break -

The Warden's office occupied the third floor of the training wing, above the equipment stores. The stairs creaked under Roan's boots. A single guard sat at a desk outside the door, one of Drogan's old associates, a hard-faced woman named Harric who had been with him since the Black Squad days.

"Holloway," Harric said. "Warden's expecting you."

Roan nodded and pushed through the door.

Drogan Hale sat behind a heavy oak desk covered in documents. The Iron Hand of Hale rested on the desk surface, polished to a mirror shine. The Warden looked up as Roan entered. His eyes, grey, evaluating, didn't leave Roan's face.

"Close the door."

Roan closed it.

"Sit."

He sat in the chair across from the desk. It was harder than it looked. Drogan's offices always had furniture designed to make visitors uncomfortable. The Warden believed in controlling the physical environment.

"The Thornfield contract," Drogan said. "Report."

Roan had prepared for this. He started with the approach, the standard route without deviations. Then the initial contact with the client, the assessment of the problem, the discovery that the situation was more complex than advertised. He described the tunnel network, the structural instability, the three-hostage scenario.

He didn't describe the Greel envoy's arrival.

He didn't describe the knife.

He described a collapse. A cave-in that sealed the tunnel behind the extraction team. Howled winds from the Rift. The desperate scramble for the secondary exit. How they'd emerged two miles east with two of the three hostages alive and one dead from injuries sustained during the collapse.

It was a good story. It had the advantage of being mostly true.

Drogan listened without interrupting. When Roan finished, the Warden leaned back in his chair and steepled his fingers.

"The Sentinel Bureau received a report from the site," Drogan said. "Structural collapse caused by pre-existing Rift instability. Two survivors extracted by independent contractor. One casualty attributed to accident. Case closed."

Roan kept his expression neutral.

"The Bureau didn't mention any complications on site," Drogan continued. "No other contractors were reported in the area. No hostile encounters. No bodies."

"I'm not aware of any bodies, sir."

"You were contracted to retrieve three hostages from a collapsed tunnel system during an active Rift event." Drogan's voice was flat. "You succeeded in retrieving two living hostages. The third died from injuries. The Bureau is satisfied with this outcome."

"Yes, sir."

"The Bureau," Drogan said, "doesn't know what happened in that tunnel. I don't know what happened in that tunnel. You don't know what happened in that tunnel."

The grinding sensation in Roan's back pulsed again. He breathed through it.

"Is there something you'd like to tell me, Holloway?"

The question hung in the air. Drogan's expression didn't change, but something in the quality of his attention shifted. The Warden was many things, cruel when necessary, calculating when useful, brutal when other options failed, but he wasn't stupid. He knew when he was being fed a partial truth.

Roan calculated the angles.

Telling Drogan the full truth meant explaining the Greels' involvement. The contract within a contract. The envoy's attempt to eliminate competition and frame the client for the deaths of her own hostages. The three men Roan had killed in the dark.

Drogan had history with House Greel. The white-bearded man had been an ally once, before the Black Squad fell apart. If Drogan learned that the envoy had survived, he might feel obligated to deal with that personally. If Drogan learned that Roan had dealt with it instead, the balance became unpredictable.

The Warden's loyalty was to the Spire first, the Discipline second, and individual students a distant third. If Roan became more liability than asset, that third category would evaporate.

"I'm not aware of anything that would change the Bureau's assessment, sir."

Drogan studied him for three more seconds. Then he nodded.

"Fine. Dismissed."

Roan stood and walked to the door. He had his hand on the handle when Drogan spoke again.

"Holloway."

He stopped.

"If there's something you're not telling me, it will come out eventually. The Spire has eyes everywhere. The Guild has eyes everywhere. The Sentinel Bureau has eyes everywhere." A pause. "I prefer to hear about problems from my own students before they're reported by someone else."

"Understood, sir."

He left.

Drogan letting it slide didn't feel like mercy. It felt like the Warden was saving the leash for something bigger—or waiting for him to hang himself with a longer rope. Roan assumed the latter.

- break -

The walk back to his quarters took him through the eastern courtyard, past the weapon racks and the sparring circles. A group of senior disciples were running drills. Crimson Wake forms, from the look of it. The air hummed with displaced Vitalis.

Roan paused to watch. Crimson Wake was a high-risk technique: each strike consumed blood volume in exchange for explosive power. The practitioners looked pale, focused, precise. Their movements were efficient and brutal.

He was still too low-ranked to attempt it. The entry requirements alone would kill a standard Initiate.

But not a standard Initiate with a Codex Shard turning the blood loss into gains.

He filed that thought for later. The Ironholm qualifier was two months away, and he was running out of time to close the gap. The grinding in his back hadn't stopped. It had, if anything, intensified during the conversation with Drogan. Something was wrong. The Shard had flagged the third vial as *probably* harmless, and Aldric's sarcasm usually preceded something worth paying attention to.

He walked faster.

His room was empty when he arrived. He locked the door, stripped off his coat, and sat on the bed with his back to the wall. The Shard was warm against his skin. The grinding sensation pulsed in time with his heartbeat.

He closed his eyes and reached inward, feeling for the Shard's presence.

The response came faster than usual. A flicker of something that might've been concern.

[You've got something stuck in you.]

*What is it?*

[Your guess is as good as mine. The compound didn't match any of my stored patterns. Partial conversion, partial retention. It's sitting in your lower meridian like a splinter in a boot.]

*How do I get it out?*

[You wait. It'll either dissolve or expel itself in the next two days. Or it won't, and you'll find out what happens when unknown compounds accumulate in your spine. Good times.]

The sarcasm was unmistakable. Aldric's ghost had opinions about stupid risks.

*You could have warned me.*

[I did. The word *probably* was doing a lot of work in that message. Also, you drank the rest of the vial after I flagged it. That was a choice you made.]

Fair.

Roan opened his eyes and stared at the ceiling. His hands were steady. His breathing was even. The grinding sensation was uncomfortable but not debilitating. He could function. He could train. He could attend classes.

He couldn't compete in the Ironholm qualifier if something was lodged in his spine.

The tournament was in two months. His qualification slot was secure. Drogan had confirmed that after Thornfield. But qualification meant nothing if he couldn't perform. And something was wrong with his meridians.

He needed information. The laboratory's reference materials were too basic for compound analysis. The library might have something, but the texts there were sanitized for public consumption. Real knowledge about catalyst interactions was kept in restricted archives.

Archives that required faculty authorization to access.

Or archives that could be accessed by anyone willing to climb through a window on the fourth floor of the Spire's east wing at two in the morning.

Roan was good at climbing.

He stood, crossed to his small desk, and pulled out a sheet of paper. The Ironholm qualifier had seven event categories. Four were combat trials, duels, team scenarios, endurance runs. Three were technical demonstrations, technique precision, catalyst knowledge, theoretical application.

The catalyst knowledge trial was the easiest to fake. You could talk your way through most of it if you knew the right jargon. The theoretical application was harder, but still manageable.

The combat trials were where he'd be judged against people with twice his training time and five times his resources.

Unless he found a way to close that gap.

The grinding pulsed again. Roan set down the paper and pressed his palm against his lower back. The sensation wasn't painful, exactly. It was like a gear that didn't mesh, a mechanical friction in organic tissue.

He needed to know what he'd swallowed. He needed to know if it would kill him, help him, or do nothing.

And he needed to know before the tournament, or the qualifier would expose every weakness he was trying to hide.

The window was dark. He had six hours until midnight.

He could wait and see if the compound dissolved on its own. That was the conservative option. Safe. Patient. The choice that Brendt would recommend.

Or he could take the risk now, get the information, and make a decision based on actual data instead of hope.

The figures said the conservative option lost time he couldn't afford to waste.

The arithmetic also said unknown compounds in the spine were dangerous.

Both things could be true.

Roan picked up his coat.

The Spire's east wing had seventeen windows on the fourth floor. One of them was always left unlatched for maintenance access. He'd noticed it during his third week at the academy and kept it for later use.

Later had arrived.

He checked the pouch at his belt, four vials remaining, his emergency reserves. The grinding in his back pulsed in what might've been response. Then he left the room, locked the door behind him, and started walking toward the archives.

The tournament was in two months.

He didn't have time to wait.
