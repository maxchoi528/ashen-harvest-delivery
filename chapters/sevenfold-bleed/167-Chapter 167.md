Chapter 167: Limited Trust

Selene didn't ask her real question until three days after Red Orchard.

That was to her credit.

Most people, given a buried family secret, a partial vision of an ancestor burning documents, and a teammate who knew more than he admitted, would have pushed immediately. They would have demanded answers in the van, in the decontamination bay, in the Guild arbitration room, or through late-night messages phrased as concern and sharpened into accusation by the second paragraph.

Selene did none of that.

She filed her mission notes.

She confirmed the Guild quarantine.

She made sure Pellam's staff received medical screening and that Doss Logistics' legal team didn't attempt to muddy Red Orchard into an unrelated agricultural incident. She also sent Roan a corrected copy of the salvage valuation with two lines showed: one identifying Ardent Foundation grant routing through a shell trust, the other showing that the shell trust had been dissolved twenty-nine years ago by order of House Greel's legal office.

That wasn't subtle.

It was also not a question.

Questions came later.

Roan spent those three days preparing.

The file wasn't only about Selene.

Roan also built one on himself from her perspective.

This wasn't vanity. Vanity produced mirrors. Threat modeling produced better lies and fewer surprises. If Selene was competent, and she was, then she had already built a working model of Roan Vance. Not complete. Not accurate in the places that mattered most. But useful enough to predict which door he would avoid, which insult he would ignore, which risk he would take for Brendt while pretending the decision had no emotional component.

So Roan wrote what she likely knew.

Poor origin. Greycross. Father dead under unclear circumstances. Mother and sister alive, protected but not visibly. Sudden advancement pattern masked by tournament performance and private training. Known associations: Brendt Holloway, Drogan, Ines Carrow, limited contact with Headmaster Cole, antagonistic history with Iris Vell, public rivalry with Cyrus Ashford, emerging association with Selene Ardent. Combat style: knife, close-range seam targeting, abnormal pain tolerance, hidden peak output, new Forged baseline. Personality: transactional, guarded, retaliatory, reliable under contract, emotionally avoidant to the point of tactical comedy according to Brendt, who hadn't been invited to annotate the file but somehow did.

Roan found the line later and left it.

It wasn't wrong.

The exercise clarified the central problem: Selene didn't need the full truth to become useful. She needed enough truth that her model stopped compensating in dangerous directions.

That part of trust people preferred not to discuss. Trust wasn't a feeling. It was permission for someone else's model of reality to influence your survival. If their model was wrong, trust became a delayed execution method.

Roan had no intention of dying because someone had guessed kindly.

Preparation, in this case, meant building seven different disclosure models and rejecting five.

Model one: tell Selene nothing. Clean, safe, insulting. Likely to push her toward independent investigation through House channels, where her family could detect, redirect, or silence her. Poor outcome.

Model two: tell Selene everything. Idiotic. Full truth included Aldric's origin, the Inversion civilization, Codex Shard functions, Founding Seven culpability, and Roan's revenge vector. Selene had shown discipline and integrity under contract pressure. That didn't entitle her to civilization-ending secrets after one orchard and a good clause negotiation.

Model three: lie. Efficient short term, fragile long term. Selene wasn't Iris. Iris had been trained to expect manipulation and respond with counter-manipulation. Selene expected structure. If she found a deliberate lie, she wouldn't feel betrayed; she would revise the entire team relationship into hostile audit. Unwise.

Model four: tell her House Ardent may have interacted with Aldric before his death, without stating why Aldric mattered beyond being Roan's father. Partial truth. Useful. Low Codex exposure. High chance of follow-up questions.

Model five: frame the issue as a contract risk: old House records are connected to recent attempts on Roan's life. This allowed operational disclosure without emotional framing. Accurate, but incomplete enough to irritate her.

Model six: tell her he was investigating his father's death. Not the civilization truth. Not the Shard. Not the full Founding Seven structure. Just the human-shaped part of the story: Aldric Vance was killed by powerful people, and Roan was following records that led through old House archives.

Model seven: combine four and five, plus six, then see whether Selene pushed for more than she needed.

Roan selected seven.

Brendt, when consulted, said, "That's the most Roan sentence I have ever heard."

"It's a plan."

"It's a trap wearing a trust exercise."

"Trust exercises are traps with worse liability."

Brendt rubbed at the bridge of his nose. He had been upgraded from couch exile to chair-based operations, which made him insufferably pleased with himself despite the fact that he still walked like his ribs were negotiating independence. "You could try saying, 'Selene, I don't trust easily, but I need to know whether you can keep a dangerous secret.'"

"That's terrible."

"It's normal."

"Worse."

"Roan."

"It reveals need and uncertainty, then secret weight before establishing her ask. Bad sequencing."

"It also treats her like a person."

"She's a person. That's why sequencing matters. Objects don't change behavior when informed."

Brendt stared at him. "You know, sometimes you take the scenic route to decency."

"As long as I arrive."

"Do you?"

Roan looked up from the board.

Brendt held his gaze for three seconds, then looked away first.

Not out of fear. Fatigue, perhaps. Or tactical mercy.

"She joined knowing you were dangerous," Brendt said more quietly. "She stayed after Red Orchard. She's going to ask because she needs to know whether helping you means betraying her family, saving it, or both. If you answer like you're cross-examining a hostile witness, she'll learn something too."

"Good."

"Not necessarily what you want her to learn."

That was annoying because it was true.

Roan added an eighth note.

Avoid unnecessary hostility.

Then, after considering, he underlined unnecessary.

- break -

Selene chose neutral ground.

Not the Ardent study hall. Not Roan's dorm. Not a cafeteria, training room, Guild office, or any place with obvious House listening privileges. She requested a meeting at the old observatory balcony above Black Spire's western lecture wing, a rarely used platform where astronomy students occasionally pretended Ironholm's light pollution allowed serious work.

Good choice.

Open air reduced hidden audio risks. Height limited approach paths. Wind disrupted some passive listening arrays. The balcony had two exits, both visible. It also overlooked half the campus, which made direct violence unlikely unless someone wanted a spectacle.

Roan arrived early and found Selene already there.

Also good.

She stood at the railing in a dark coat, hair tied back, no armor visible beyond the subtle line of a reinforced collar. The city spread beyond her in black glass and cold light. Rift monitoring beams rose from distant towers into low clouds, blue-white pillars holding the sky in place by institutional insistence.

"You picked a dramatic location," Roan said.

"It has clean sight lines."

"Dramatic ones."

She glanced back. "You object to drama?"

"Only when it invoices poorly."

"Then you'll survive the balcony."

Roan moved to the opposite side of the railing, leaving several feet between them. Not distance enough to imply fear. Enough to avoid theater.

For a while, they watched the city.

Selene spoke first.

"Red Orchard wasn't a research accident."

"No."

"My family funded the station to watch something buried there."

"Likely."

"House Greel helped erase records."

"Evidence suggests legal involvement."

"Legal involvement," she repeated. "That's a careful phrase."

"Accurate phrases survive longer."

She looked at him. "What did your father have to do with Red Orchard?"

There it was.

Not what did you see. Not what do you know. Not what are you hiding.

Your father.

Selene had chosen the personal hinge because she understood that all the operational lines led back there. Better than expected.

Roan kept his hands in his coat pockets. The Vance Pendant rested cold beneath his shirt. Sidewound wasn't visible. The Codex Shard remained quiet.

"I don't know the full answer," he said.

"But you know enough to suspect."

"Yes."

"Then I need to know what kind of history I am stepping into."

Need. Not want.

Roan considered the word. People used need to inflate desire. Selene didn't sound like she was inflating anything. She sounded like someone standing at the edge of a family grave and deciding whether to climb down.

"Before I answer," Roan said, "state your objective."

Her mouth tightened. "This isn't a contract interview."

"It's exactly that."

"I'm asking whether my family's old crimes are tied to your father's death."

"And I'm asking what you will do if the answer is yes."

Wind moved across the balcony, cold enough to sting the eyes.

Selene turned back toward the city.

"I don't know," she said.

Good answer.

Most people lied at that point. They said they would pursue justice, protect the innocent, expose the truth, or other attractive sentences with no logistics attached. Selene didn't know. That made her more reliable, not less.

"Better," Roan said.

She gave him a sharp look.

"Don't patronize me."

"Then don't answer badly."

Her eyes narrowed.

There was pride there. House pride, personal pride, trained reflex. Roan watched it rise, hit discipline, and stop.

Excellent control.

"My objective," Selene said carefully, "is to determine whether House Ardent's current leadership is protecting old crimes that continue to endanger people. If yes, I need enough evidence to decide whether internal correction is possible. If not, external leverage. If neither, containment."

"Containment includes what?"

"Removal from authority. Financial seizure. Public exposure. In extreme cases, sanctioned violence."

"Against family?"

Her jaw shifted once. "Against threats."

Roan nodded.

That was answer that cost something.

"My father was murdered," he said.

Selene went still.

He continued before she could speak. "Not in a street killing. Not by a rogue Rift creature. Not by illness, expedition failure, or whatever sanitized version exists in polite databases. He was hunted. Powerful people arranged it, benefited from it, and buried pieces afterward. I am identifying them."

Selene's face had no color now, but her voice remained even. "Founding Seven?"

Roan looked at her.

She breathed out once. "Iris Vell. Greel mercenaries. House records disappearing. Red Orchard. Your reaction to old symbols. I am not an idiot."

"No."

"Is House Ardent one of them?"

"I don't know."

"Do you suspect us?"

"I suspect everyone with old archives, old money, and old silence."

"That includes my family."

"Yes."

She absorbed that without flinching.

The city lights flickered below as a tram line passed between towers. Somewhere far off, a Rift siren gave a low warning pulse and stopped. Routine. The world continuing because it hadn't been informed of the conversation.

"Why tell me this much?" Selene asked.

"Because you are already investigating. Uninformed investigation will get you killed or used. Informed investigation may still get you killed, but with better odds."

"Charming."

"Accurate."

"And because?"

Roan looked at her.

She had learned from him. Irritating.

Because Brendt was right. Because treating her only as a potential liability would make her one. Because House Ardent's archives might hold names Roan needed. Because Selene hadn't hidden Red Orchard. Because trust, if it had to exist, should be limited and explicit, then attached to consequences.

He chose the sentence least likely to rot.

"Because you kept contract when it cost you."

Selene's expression changed.

Barely.

Enough.

"That's all?" she asked.

"For now."

"Limited trust."

"Yes."

"You make it sound like an equipment grade."

"Equipment grades are useful."

"People aren't equipment."

"No. They fail less predictably."

She stared at him.

Then, despite everything, she laughed once.

Not loud. Not soft exactly. A short, unwilling sound pulled out by the absurdity of standing above Black Spire discussing murder, family treason, and trust classifications with Roan Vance.

"You are impossible," she said.

"Brendt filed prior claim on that phrase."

"He would."

The laugh faded. The serious expression returned, but not as cold as before.

"What do you need from me?" she asked.

"Nothing yet."

"Wrong answer."

Roan's mouth almost moved.

She noticed.

"I can learn too," Selene said.

"Apparently."

He looked out over the city.

The cautious answer was to say he needed general access to Ardent public records. The useful answer was sharper.

"Old archive references," he said. "Not files. Not theft. References. Names of sealed collections, destroyed caches, field operations involving Red Orchard, Greel legal offices, Vell finance, or unexplained Rift blooms between sixty and eighty years ago."

Selene listened without interrupting.

"If you find anything marked with seven lines, inversion, Veyrn, Aldric, Vance, Hollow, Codex, or Orchard, you don't copy it through House systems. You memorize location and metadata only. Then you tell me in person."

"That's a long list."

"History is inconsiderate."

"And what do I tell my family if they ask why I'm looking?"

"You are investigating Red Orchard because a Guild-sealed mission exposed a family liability. That's true."

"And if they ask about you?"

"Tell them I am annoying. That's also true."

"They know."

"Then tell them I am useful."

"That will alarm them more."

"Good."

Selene folded her arms against the cold. "What won't you tell me?"

"A lot."

"Specific categories."

"My father's full origin. My current inheritance mechanisms. Names I can't yet verify. Anything that would make you a target without improving your decisions."

"You decide that last category."

"Yes."

"I dislike it."

"Expected."

"But I understand it."

"Better."

She looked down at the campus paths below. Students crossed between buildings under rain shields, small figures moving through institutional light. Roan wondered how many of them belonged to Houses with old blood on their archives. Most. Guilt scaled well with inheritance.

"If I find that my family helped kill your father," Selene said, "what happens?"

Roan didn't answer immediately.

That question deserved precision.

"Depends on who and why, plus whether the people responsible are alive and powerful, plus still dangerous."

"If they're?"

"Then I remove them."

"Kill?"

"If efficient."

Her eyes returned to his.

There was no flinch this time. Only measurement.

"And if I stand in the way?"

"Don't."

"That isn't an answer."

"It's advice."

"Roan."

He turned fully toward her.

"If you stand in the way to protect murderers, you become part of the obstruction. I will handle you accordingly. If you stand in the way because I am wrong, prove it quickly."

The wind moved between them.

Selene held his gaze.

A lesser person would have called that monstrous. A more dramatic one would have declared opposition or loyalty. Selene did neither.

She nodded once.

"Fair."

Good.

Not warm.

Fair.

That was stronger.

- break -

The second part of the conversation happened after Roan thought the first had ended.

He had turned toward the stairs when Selene said, "One more thing."

Of course.

Roan stopped.

"Iris warned you about Red Orchard," Selene said.

"Yes."

"Why?"

"Unclear."

"Guess."

Roan considered not answering. Then decided the cost of a controlled guess was low.

"She wants distance from Greel but lacks a clean exit. Warning me damages them indirectly while preserving deniability. It also tests whether I act on her information. If I die, she loses a problem. If I survive, she gains proof her information has value."

"And emotionally?"

"Irrelevant."

Selene gave him a look.

"Fine," Roan said. "Unstable."

"She isn't stupid."

"No."

"But she's cornered."

"Yes."

Selene tapped one finger against the railing. "Cornered House heirs burn things."

"Personal experience?"

"Family history."

Roan waited.

Selene looked toward the north, where the richest House districts hid behind rain and elevation.

"If Iris warns you again," she said, "tell me. Not because I trust her. Because I know what it looks like when someone is deciding whether to betray the cage or decorate it."

That was a better sentence than Roan expected.

"Accepted," he said.

"Limited?"

"Obviously."

This time her smile was faint and real enough to be dangerous.

"Good night, Roan."

"Selene."

She left by the eastern stair.

Roan waited until her footsteps faded, then remained on the balcony for another four minutes.

Not for reflection.

For surveillance.

No one followed her. No watcher moved from the roofline. No ward pulse indicated a recording array. Either she had chosen the balcony well, or the people watching were better than his current detection.

Both possibilities mattered.

The Codex Shard pulsed once.

[Trust vector established: Selene Ardent.]

[Classification: limited / unstable / potentially high yield.]

[Warning: inherited guilt does not equal inherited choice.]

Roan stared at the prompt.

"That was almost philosophical," he murmured.

The Shard didn't answer.

Good. He disliked being lectured by spinal artifacts.

He left the balcony.

When Roan told Brendt about the balcony meeting afterward, Brendt listened without interrupting for almost seven minutes.

This was alarming.

Roan stopped halfway through the summary. "Are you in pain?"

"No."

"You are quiet."

"I am demonstrating growth."

"Stop. It's unsettling."

Brendt ignored that. "You told her enough."

"I told her a version."

"A true version."

"Partial truth can mislead more effectively than lies."

"Yes," Brendt said. "But it can also be the amount a person can carry without dropping it on their foot."

Roan considered.

"That metaphor needs work."

"I am injured. My metaphors are on light duty."

Brendt shifted carefully in the operations chair. He wasn't supposed to sit there for more than two hours. He had been there for three and a half. Roan had noticed. Brendt had noticed Roan noticing and pretended to be absorbed in the monitoring feed. Their friendship had developed several layers of mutually acknowledged noncompliance.

"She asked the right question," Brendt said. "What happens if her family is involved."

"Yes."

"And you gave the honest answer."

"Yes."

"That answer might scare her off."

"Then better now."

"I know." Brendt rubbed his eyes. "I hate that I know."

Roan checked the time and closed one of Brendt's tablets. "Medical limit."

"Don't use my health to avoid emotional follow-up."

"I am using your health to avoid preventable collapse. Emotional follow-up is a secondary benefit."

"At least you're honest."

"Limitedly."

Brendt smiled. "You told Selene limited trust."

"Yes."

"What am I classified as?"

Roan didn't answer quickly enough.

Brendt's smile widened. "You have classifications."

"Everyone has classifications."

"What is mine?"

"Annoying."

"Primary or secondary?"

"Structural."

Brendt laughed, then immediately pressed a hand to his ribs and regretted it.

"Worth it," he said again.

"You keep saying that before medical consequences."

"Pattern recognition."

Roan stood and took the remaining tablet from him. "Sleep."

"Is that an order from field lead?"

"From the person who will tell Dr. Seth you exceeded chair time."

"Tyrant."

"Alive tyrant."

Brendt let himself be bullied toward the couch, which proved either exhaustion or unprecedented wisdom.

As he lowered himself down, he said, "For what it's worth, I think Selene stays."

"Based on?"

"She asked what happens if she stands in the way. People planning to run don't ask where the road breaks."

Roan looked at him.

Brendt pulled the blanket over himself with excessive dignity. "Also, she likes clause amendments. That's your version of courtship."

Roan threw a folded pair of socks at him.

Brendt caught them badly and looked delighted.

"Physical aggression," he said. "I win."

"Sleep."

"Limited trust with socks," Brendt murmured, already closing his eyes. "Progress."

By the time he returned to the dorm, Brendt had fallen asleep in the operations chair with three tablets open, a blanket crooked around his shoulders, and a half-finished note reading: *Ask Roan if limited trust comes with warranty.*

Roan looked at the note.

Then he took the pen and wrote beneath it:

No refunds.

He turned off two of the tablets, left the active monitoring feed running, and adjusted the blanket before going to his desk.

There, he opened his investigation board and added Selene's name to the section he had previously labeled Assets.

After a moment, he crossed out the label.

Not because it was wrong.

Because Brendt would complain.

He replaced it with: People Who Know Enough To Be Dangerous.

Then, after another pause, he added three subcolumns.

Protected.

Useful.

Watched.

Selene went under all three.

Brendt was already there.

Roan stared at the board for a while.

Trust, apparently, wasn't a door one opened.

It was a ledger one updated while hoping the entries didn't start bleeding.

Outside, Ironholm rain tapped against the glass.

Somewhere north of the city, Red Orchard sat under Guild quarantine, roots rotting above buried stone. Somewhere inside House Ardent, old archives waited with names wrapped in dust and pride. Somewhere in whatever cold room House Greel used for family strategy, the silence after their failed ambush was turning into a plan.

Roan picked up his pen.

On the next page, he wrote:

The envoy? Greel younger generation. Check transfer records.

He didn't know yet why the name mattered.

That was fine.

Unknown knives usually announced themselves first as blank spaces.

Roan had become very good at reading blanks.
