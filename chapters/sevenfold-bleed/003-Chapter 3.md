Chapter 3: First of Chariot

The numbers didn’t work.

Roan Vance had been running them since he left the examination hall. Spire tuition. Travel to Ironholm. Equipment. An Awakening Draught if he wanted to compete in the physical trials. The sum was a wall he couldn’t climb with the hours he had left.

He ran them one more time on the walk home, just to be certain. The deficit was still there. Large enough to swallow a year of dock wages. Small enough to feel like a personal failure.

A contact from the waterfront had mentioned a man named Garrick. He sold failed batches of stimulants, the kind of cheap product that cleared inspection for everything except safety. The side effects were brutal. The price was almost criminal. Almost.

Roan turned down Morrow Street, heading south into the industrial quarter where the streets narrowed and the Rift monitors cast long shadows. Greycross didn’t have a nice part of town, but it definitely had a less-nice part, and that was where Garrick did business.

The shop was a hole-in-the-wall wedged between a scrap yard and a boarded-up tenement. The sign above the door read *Garrick’s Pharmaceuticals* in letters faded by rain and neglect. The windows were barred.

Roan pushed open the door.

The air inside was thick with disinfectant and something metallic. Rows of shelves held bottles with labels printed on cheap paper. A counter ran the length of the back wall, protected by a screen of reinforced mesh. Behind it sat Garrick, a man in his fifties who looked like he’d been carved from old leather and bad intentions.

“Help you?” Garrick’s voice was flat. He didn’t look up from the newspaper he was reading.

“Bloodscourge.”

The newspaper lowered an inch. Garrick’s eyes, small and sharp, tracked over Roan like he was reading a balance sheet. “You know what that is?”

“Tempered-rank refining agent. Accelerates Vitalis circulation by a factor of three for a short window. Side effects include systemic shock, organ inflammation, and cardiac arrest in high doses.”

Garrick folded the newspaper. “And you want it because—”

“It’s cheap.”

“It’s cheap because it kills people, kid.”

“It’s cheap because the market rate for failure is low.” Roan stepped closer to the counter. “I’m not planning on failing.”

A long pause. Garrick studied him. Roan let him. There was nothing to hide from a man who sold poison for a living. The only thing that mattered was the price.

“I’ve got a batch from last month,” Garrick said finally. “Failed the purity test. The base formulation is sound, but the stabilizer degraded. It’ll hit harder and burn out faster. Most people can’t handle the ramp.”

“How much?”

“Eight hundred.”

“Half that.”

“Six hundred.”

“Four fifty, and I sign a liability waiver.”

Garrick laughed, a dry sound like paper tearing. “You don’t even know the terms yet.”

Roan pulled a folded sheet from his pocket. “I drafted them. You provide the product, I waive recourse for known side effects. You guarantee delivery within ten days or the contract voids. I pay half on signature, half on delivery.”

Garrick’s laughter stopped. He took the sheet, read it slowly. His expression didn’t change.

“You wrote this yourself?”

“Yes.”

“You’re seventeen.”

“Does that change the math?”

Garrick set the paper down. “The liability waiver is too narrow. You’re only covering known side effects. If the batch has an unknown contaminant, I’m still on the hook.”

“You test your batches. If there’s an unknown contaminant, that’s your negligence, not my risk.”

“The contract says—”

“The contract says you guarantee the product is as described. If it isn’t, you owe me double.”

Garrick stared at him. The air in the shop felt like it had thickened. Then, slowly, a grin spread across his face.

“You’re a difficult customer.”

“I pay my bills.”

“Four fifty, signature today, delivery in seven days. And if you die, I keep the deposit.”

“Seven days. Delivery at my address. If you’re late, the contract voids and I get a full refund.”

“Fine.”

Roan wrote the address on the contract. His hand was steady. He signed, counted out four hundred and fifty credits from the envelope he’d been saving for six months, and slid them through the mesh.

Garrick counted the money, tucked it into his pocket, and handed over a receipt. “You’re either very smart or very stupid.”

“I’ll find out in seven days.”

Roan left the shop. The air outside was cold and damp, carrying the familiar tang of the chemical plants. He walked north, toward the apartment, his hand resting in his pocket where the receipt sat next to his last few credits.

Steps in the plan:
1. Garrick delivers the Bloodscourge.
2. He tests it.
3. He finds a way to pass the Spire exam.
4. He never looks back.

The plan assumed the drug wouldn’t kill him. That was the weak point. But Roan had been working with weak points his entire life. He knew how to brace them, reinforce them, turn them into something that held.

The apartment was quiet when he climbed the stairs to the third floor. His mother was at the chemical plant, wouldn’t be home until midnight. From the back room, he heard the soft rustle of pages turning. Lyra was studying.

He didn’t disturb her.

He went to his room, closed the door, and sat on the edge of his bed. The box was in the corner, where it had been for six years. Aldric Vance’s legacy, packed into cardboard and dust.

Roan opened it.

He’d been through the contents a dozen times. Notebooks full of diagrams he couldn’t parse. Photographs of people he didn’t recognize. Objects that had meant nothing to a seventeen-year-old who had never been taught their significance.

But tonight, after a contract with a man who sold poison, after a calculation that kept coming up short, he looked at the box differently.

He pulled out the notebook. The pages fell open to a familiar passage, a paragraph half-circled with notes, and one sentence underlined so hard the paper had torn:

*The cure is on the wrong shelf.*

Roan read it again. He’d read it a hundred times. It never made sense.

He set the notebook aside and reached for the pouch. The cheap one, the one that held the iron pendant he’d dismissed a hundred times as garbage.

He emptied it into his palm.

The pendant was dark iron, etched with patterns that might have been symbols or might have been decoration. The leather cord was frayed. It looked like something you could buy at a market stall for five credits.

But the weight was wrong.

He’d known it for years. He’d felt it every time he picked it up. But he’d never stopped to ask why a piece of cheap iron weighed three times what it should.

He held it up to the light. The patterns caught the glow, and for a moment, something shifted in the depth of the metal. Not a reflection. A pull.

Roan lowered the pendant. The metal was warm, dense, wrong.

*The cure is on the wrong shelf.*

He didn’t know what it meant. But it was a variable he hadn’t accounted for. And his father had taught him that the math wasn’t finished until every variable had been found.

He put the cord around his neck. The pendant settled against his chest.

Outside the window, a red light began to pulse. Tier 1 Rift alert. Routine. The patrols would handle it.

Roan looked at it, then back at the notebook.

He had a contract. He had a pendant. He had ninety days until the Spire exam.

The numbers still didn’t work. But he was starting to think he’d missed a variable.

He opened the textbook and started reading. The red light continued to pulse, ignored, familiar, a heartbeat for a city that had learned to live with wounds.

His hand went to the pendant, touched the metal.

The weight was still wrong.

He’d find out why.
