Chapter 177: The Public Challenge

Black Spire's sanctioned duel law was older than most of its buildings and more honest than its admissions brochures.

The modern academy preferred to speak of structured conflict resolution, supervised martial assessment, and the preservation of institutional neutrality through controlled channels. The old statutes used fewer words. Two qualified practitioners could settle a dispute on academy ground under warded supervision, with declared stakes, recorded consent, and liability waiver. The duel ended by concession, incapacitation, ring breach, instructor stoppage, or death.

Death wasn't encouraged.

It was priced.

Roan read the statute three times while standing beside Brendt's medical bed.

Brendt didn't wake.

The healers had moved him from emergency surgery to a private recovery room under Cole's authority. Tubes ran into his arm. A breathing charm pulsed against his chest in slow blue intervals. His injured shoulder remained wrapped from earlier graft work, now joined by fresh bandages across his ribs and a stabilization brace along his spine. He looked smaller under medical sheets, which irritated Roan because Brendt had spent months becoming harder to move.

The room smelled of antiseptic, healing light, and expensive failure.

Roan stood by the window with the duel statute open on his slate.

"Clause nineteen," Selene said from the chair near the door. "Mutual consent under public witness prevents later House retaliation through academy grievance channels."

"Prevents formal retaliation," Roan said.

"Yes."

"Informal retaliation continues."

"Obviously."

"Good law."

She looked at him. "That's your standard?"

"It describes reality."

Ines sat cross-legged on the windowsill despite two nurses telling her not to. She had spent the morning reconstructing the recorder crystal and appeared personally offended by its encryption quality. "Clause twenty-two is more useful. If one party introduces prohibited external interference, all duel records release automatically to the Oversight Board and neutral Spire network."

Roan nodded. "Global broadcast?"

"Not by default."

"Can be attached?"

Selene hesitated. "For rank challenges, yes. For personal disputes, only with consent or institutional interest."

"Cole can declare institutional interest."

"Cole will ask why."

"Then he's predictable."

Drogan stood at the foot of Brendt's bed, arms folded. He had said little since the freight yard. That wasn't peace. It was weather before impact.

"You challenge Kael," Drogan said, "you give him what he wants."

"Yes."

"You understand that?"

"Yes."

"And you still plan to do it."

"Yes."

Drogan's eyes narrowed. "Explain without sounding like your father on his worst day."

Roan lowered the slate.

That was fair.

He looked at Brendt first. Still unconscious. Still breathing because Roan had accepted poison into his own veins and torn something behind his eyes to buy time. Still alive because enough people had moved in the same direction quickly enough. Alone, Roan would have saved him later or not at all.

Alone was inefficient.

Drogan had been right.

Annoying.

"Kael wants clean ground," Roan said. "If I refuse, he continues using dirty ground. Markets. Corridors. Cyrus. Vell remnants. Brendt. If I attack him privately, Greel controls the legal frame. If I accuse him publicly without proof, Cyrus hardens around him and campus reads it as paranoia. If I wait, he chooses time and terrain."

Selene listened without interrupting.

Ines stopped working on the crystal.

Roan continued. "A sanctioned duel gives him enough of what he wants to accept. It gives me terrain, witnesses, recording, liability structure, and a channel for forced evidence release. The duel isn't the punishment. It's the container."

Drogan grunted. "Container for what?"

"For his mistake."

"And if he doesn't make one?"

"Then I make it for him."

Ines smiled slightly. "That sounds illegal."

"Not if written properly."

Selene closed her eyes for a second. "I am a bad influence on you."

"No," Roan said. "A precise one."

Drogan didn't look reassured. "Kael is Greel blood. You saw the genealogy. Vessel-candidate isn't decorative language. He may have techniques you haven't seen."

"He will."

"He uses ice."

"Yes."

"You have fought cold before?"

"Not Greel cold."

"Then this is stupid."

Roan looked at the slate again. "It's risky."

"Same smell."

"No. Stupid ignores cost. Risk counts it and proceeds when alternatives are worse."

Drogan's jaw worked.

The condensation on the inside of the breathing mask thickened, then thinned.

Brendt's breathing charm pulsed.

No one spoke for ten seconds.

Then Brendt's fingers twitched.

Roan turned before the others noticed.

The movement was small. Barely more than a flex against the sheet. But the body had answered itself, not waking or recovery in the dramatic sense.

The breathing charm steadied.

Selene stood. Ines slipped from the windowsill. Drogan moved closer to the bed.

Roan stayed where he was.

If he stepped closer, he would have to decide what expression belonged on his face. Waste of resources.

Brendt's lips moved.

No sound.

Roan leaned just enough to hear.

A breath. A broken whisper.

"...noodles?"

Ines covered her mouth.

Selene looked down.

Drogan turned away, shoulders shaking once, with anger at the universe's poor taste.

Roan closed his eyes.

Only for a second.

When he opened them, Brendt was unconscious again.

But the room had changed.

Alive was no longer a line on a chart.

It had become a complaint about dinner.

Good.

Roan picked up the slate. "I'm challenging Kael today."

Drogan sighed. "Of course that's what you took from this."

"He'll want to watch Brendt's condition before deciding whether pressure succeeded. We move before his next adjustment."

Selene took the slate from him and began editing the petition. "Then we need wording that forces him to accept without making you look unstable."

"That excludes several accurate drafts," Ines said.

Roan ignored her. "Public rank challenge under dispute of hostile interference."

Selene shook her head. "Too accusatory. He can decline as defamation."

"Martial courtesy challenge?"

"Too weak."

Ines raised a finger. "What about Cyrus?"

Roan looked at her.

She shrugged. "Kael has invested in him. Challenge Kael in a way that makes declining look like admitting his lessons are ornamental. Tie it to Cyrus's recovery narrative."

Selene's eyes narrowed thoughtfully. "A technical challenge."

"Public demonstration of northern counter-methods," Roan said.

Drogan looked disgusted. "You are laundering a revenge trap through pedagogy."

"Yes."

"Your father would be proud and horrified."

"Efficient range."

Selene began writing.

- break -

Cole objected to the petition for seven minutes.

This was longer than Roan expected and shorter than Selene had predicted. They met in Cole's office with Drogan by the window, Selene at the side table, Ines officially excluded from the meeting. Cole had refused her entry, so she had left an open slate and a recording array on the table outside. Roan didn't look up. Neither did Cole. Both of them knew it was there. Decorum survived by mutual fraud.

"You are asking me to approve a public duel against a politically protected transfer student less than twenty-four hours after an off-campus assassination attempt," Cole said.

"Yes."

"That sentence should embarrass you."

"It describes the utility."

"It describes an administrative disaster."

"Those overlap often."

Cole looked at Selene. "And you drafted this?"

"I corrected it," Selene said. "His first version contained three clauses that could be read as threats and one that was a threat."

"Only one?" Drogan muttered.

Roan ignored him.

Cole read the petition again. His eyes paused at the external interference release clause. "You want automatic release to Oversight if Kael receives outside aid."

"Or if I do."

"You won't."

"No."

"That confidence isn't comforting."

"It's accurate."

Cole set the petition down. "Kael may be baiting you toward exactly this."

"Yes."

"And you are proceeding because?"

"Because bait points toward the fisherman."

Cole closed his eyes. "I am approving recording, not spectacle."

"Recording without audience can be buried."

"Public audience risks panic."

"Public audience makes later denial harder."

"Denial by whom?"

Roan met his gaze.

Cole's face changed by a degree. He knew. Perhaps not all of it. Enough. Old Spire men understood Houses better than students did. They knew that a family like Greel could turn private violence into paperwork and paperwork into fog.

"You won't kill him for vengeance," Cole said.

"No."

Drogan looked at him sharply.

Roan added, "If he dies, it will be because the alternative became worse."

Cole didn't like that. He signed anyway.

The challenge went live at 1:00 p.m.

By 1:03, the student forums crashed.

That wasn't entirely Roan's fault. Ines had arranged simultaneous distribution through the official martial board, the rank challenge feed, three student commentary mirrors, and an anonymous analysis account that had spent the past month criticizing Roan's footwork with enough detail to be trusted by people who disliked him. Selene had ensured the language met academy law. Cole, after a meeting lasting four minutes and containing what Drogan later described as "dangerous silence," declared the matter of sufficient institutional interest to permit public recording.

The petition title was simple.

*Technical Challenge: Roan Vance requests sanctioned duel with Kael Greel for comparative evaluation of northern field methods against adaptive close-pressure systems.*

Brendt, if awake, would have called it the least romantic duel challenge ever written.

That was why it worked.

It didn't accuse Kael. It praised him by implication. It framed the duel as technical and educational, plus relevant to student combat development after Cyrus Ashford's visible retraining under Kael's guidance. Declining would make Kael look either afraid of demonstrating his methods or unwilling to stand behind what he had taught Cyrus. Accepting gave him legal ground and public access to Roan.

Both sides received bait.

Roan's part included barbs subtle enough for adults to miss and students to taste.

*Given Mr. Greel's recent informal contributions to campus combat recovery, a direct controlled exchange would benefit the student body more than corridor speculation.*

Translation: you have been teaching Cyrus in public. Prove your work.

*As both parties are Forged-class registered practitioners, standard injury liability provisions apply.*

Translation: no protective category. No excuses.

*External interference release clause requested.*

Translation: cheat and the school bleeds evidence.

The comments began immediately.

*Vance challenging the transfer student already?*

*This is about Cyrus, right?*

*No way Greel declines if he's northern line.*

*Did Vance just weaponize curriculum?*

*Comparative evaluation my ass. Someone buy popcorn.*

*Holloway still in medical. This isn't about technique.*

That last comment lasted thirty seconds before disappearing.

Roan noticed.

So did Ines.

"Moderation pulled it fast," she said from the war room office, fingers moving across two slates. "Not Cole's pattern. Student-side suppression."

"Kael?" Selene asked.

"Maybe. Or someone protecting the narrative."

Roan watched the thread update.

Cyrus hadn't commented.

Good or bad. Hard to tell.

Kael responded at 1:17 p.m.

One line through the official board.

*Accepted. I welcome a clean exchange.*

Clean.

Roan looked at the word for a while.

Then he sent his reply.

*Tomorrow. Main arena. Noon.*

Kael accepted in under a minute.

Drogan stood behind Roan, reading over his shoulder. "Too fast."

"Yes."

"He was waiting."

"Yes."

"You still think you control terrain?"

"No."

Drogan looked at him.

Roan closed the slate. "I control which terrain he thinks I chose."

Ines laughed softly. "That sentence needs either a drink or a lawyer."

Selene rubbed her temples. "It already has both."

- break -

Cyrus found Roan at dusk.

Not alone. Roan had made being alone difficult since the market strike. Brendt unconscious removed one reliable shadow, but Selene and Ines had adapted, and Drogan had become a mobile wall whenever he sensed stupidity developing. Still, Cyrus managed to intercept Roan in the western cloister between the library and the old dueling hall, during the eight-minute window between Selene's archive meeting and Ines's return from whatever illegal errand she had named "insurance."

Drogan was twenty paces behind, close enough to kill, far enough to let conversation happen.

Cyrus stepped out from behind a stone pillar.

He looked better than he had in weeks. That was the problem. His uniform was clean. His posture steadier. His eyes held anger, but it no longer spilled everywhere. Kael had done good work.

Roan stopped.

"Vance," Cyrus said.

"Vale."

Cyrus flinched very slightly at the shortened name. Once, Roan had used full names like weapons. Now the omission did work of its own.

"You challenged Kael."

"Yes."

"Why?"

"The petition is public."

"Don't do that."

Roan studied him. "Do what?"

"Answer like I'm one of your traps."

"You walked into my path at dusk to discuss a man training you to hate me more effectively."

Cyrus's mouth tightened. "He isn't training me to hate you."

"No. He's training you so the hate becomes useful."

Silence.

Cyrus looked away first.

Progress, perhaps. Or damage.

"He helped me," Cyrus said.

"Yes."

"After everyone else decided I was finished."

"Yes."

"You don't get to decide that makes him poison."

Roan considered several answers.

The cruel one: poison also changes symptoms.

The strategic one: ask who benefits from your recovery.

The honest one: he's Vorath Greel's blood and came here under a knife-shaped shadow.

The useful one was harder.

"Do you want to beat me?" Roan asked.

Cyrus looked back. "Yes."

"Good."

The answer unsettled him.

Roan continued. "Then do it with clean hands. Train. Improve. Challenge me when your goal is victory instead of repair. If you use Kael's hand to hold the blade, whatever you win belongs to him."

Cyrus's expression twisted. "You think I'm that weak."

"I think weakness isn't the problem. Hunger is."

"Hunger."

"You're hungry for the version of yourself that existed before the final. Kael is feeding it to you in pieces."

Cyrus stepped closer. "And you? What are you hungry for?"

Roan's hand didn't move.

Drogan's presence sharpened behind him.

For one moment, Roan saw Brendt on the market stones, blood in his mouth, asking about noodles.

"Murder," Roan said.

Cyrus went still.

Roan looked at him evenly. "That's why I am using law."

The words landed harder than anger would have.

Cyrus searched his face, perhaps expecting mockery, perhaps hoping for it. Mockery would be easier to dismiss. He found none.

"You think Kael ordered the attack on Holloway," Cyrus said.

"I think Kael knew enough to record a message before the broker died."

Cyrus's face paled.

Good. Not satisfaction. Data.

"He said Holloway wasn't the intended kill," Roan added.

Cyrus swallowed.

The sentence did what Roan needed. It didn't ask Cyrus to abandon Kael. It introduced a crack where certainty had been standing. Cracks mattered. Sidewound had taught him that.

Cyrus looked toward the arena towers visible beyond the cloister arch. "If he wins?"

"Then I was wrong about several variables."

"And if you win?"

"Then watch what he says when losing costs him more than pride."

Cyrus said nothing.

Roan stepped past him.

At the archway, Cyrus spoke again.

"Don't kill him."

Roan stopped.

There were many reasons not to promise that. Kael might force lethal response. The duel statute allowed death. Greel had put Brendt in a coma. Roan had no obligation to soothe Cyrus's conscience or protect Kael from consequences he had invited.

But Cyrus wasn't asking as Kael's ally.

He was asking as someone who had just realized he might be standing too close to a machine that ate people.

Roan said, "If he keeps the fight clean, he leaves breathing."

Cyrus closed his eyes.

It wasn't forgiveness.

It wasn't trust.

It was a line.

Lines were useful.

Cyrus remained in the cloister after Roan left, and that mattered too. He didn't run to Kael immediately. Roan watched his reflection in the dark library glass as he paused under the arch, head bowed, hands clenched. A simple man would've been easier. A villain would've been cleaner. Cyrus was neither. He was proud, injured, vain and talented, then currently being offered dignity by someone who had placed a knife near Brendt's heart.

That didn't make Cyrus innocent.

It made him salvageable under poor conditions.

Roan disliked salvage work. It required patience, and the materials often resented being handled.

Drogan fell into step beside him once they were clear of the cloister. "You told him the truth."

"Part of it."

"Why?"

"Because a lie would push him toward Kael if exposed. Full truth would overload him. Partial truth creates friction."

"Also because you don't want him dead."

Roan said nothing.

Drogan snorted. "Terrible secret. Nearly invisible under all the tactical language."

"Cyrus alive is useful."

"So are chairs. You don't warn chairs before storms."

Roan looked at him.

Drogan's expression was almost mild. Dangerous.

"Don't become sentimental because I chose not to kill one broken aristocrat," Roan said.

"I'll try to contain myself."

They walked in silence for twenty paces.

Then Drogan said, "Brendt would approve."

Roan's jaw tightened. "Brendt approves of bad plans if they involve people becoming less stupid."

"Yes."

"That isn't a standard."

"It's a start."

Roan hated that answer enough to remember it.

- break -

The main arena lights came on at midnight for maintenance checks.

Roan stood alone in the upper gallery and watched the ward crews prepare the field.

Not entirely alone. Drogan waited near the stairwell, but he had the decency to loom quietly. Below, technicians walked the ring perimeter, testing barrier pylons, liability recorders, impact gauges, medical extraction routes, and the automatic external interference release array Selene had insisted be written into the challenge terms.

The arena looked different without a crowd.

During tournaments, noise softened the violence. Applause, commentary, banners, announcements, rival chants, public spectacle wrapped impact in meaning people could purchase. Empty, the arena was just a legal pit with excellent lighting.

Roan preferred it empty.

Tomorrow it would be full.

He reviewed the plan again.

Objective one: survive Kael's opening without revealing Black Rite full depth.

Objective two: force cryo technique escalation.

Objective three: confirm whether Greel ice carried self-damaging circulation patterns suitable for inversion.

Objective four: seed public record with Kael's operational knowledge of the market attack.

Objective five: avoid killing him unless necessary.

Objective six: make necessary flexible.

The Codex Shard pulsed.

[Hostile lineage architecture likely.]

[External anchor recommended.]

[Sidewound compatibility stable.]

[Exposure risk remains elevated.]

[Sleep recommended.]

Roan ignored the last line.

Drogan approached finally.

"You need rest."

"I know."

"You keep saying that before not doing it."

"Yes."

Drogan leaned on the gallery rail beside him. For a while, both men looked down at the arena.

Then Drogan said, "Aldric would have challenged him too."

Roan didn't answer.

"He would have done it angrier."

"That isn't comforting."

"Wasn't meant to be."

Roan watched a technician test the death-stop threshold on the west pylon. It flared red, then green.

Drogan continued, "The difference is whether you know what you're protecting."

"Brendt."

"That's who. Not what."

Roan's jaw tightened.

Drogan looked at him. "What are you protecting?"

Roan almost said the Shard. Father's truth. Lyra. Mira. The team. The evidence. The future revenge path. All true. None complete.

Below, the arena lights reflected on the polished floor like cold water.

"I don't know yet," Roan said.

Drogan nodded slowly, as if that answer was better than certainty.

"Good."

Roan looked at him.

"Men who know too early usually pick something small and call it the world." Drogan pushed away from the rail. "Sleep for four hours. That's an order."

"I don't take orders."

"No. You evaluate them. Evaluate this one faster."

Annoying.

Correct.

Roan took one last look at the arena.

Legal ground. Clean exchange. Public record. Hidden knives.

Tomorrow, Kael Greel would step into a ring believing he had obtained the stage he wanted.

Roan intended to let him believe that until the first seam opened.

Before leaving, he walked down from the gallery to the arena floor. The maintenance crew had gone. The ring wards slept in their pylons, dim and patient. His boots made no sound on the polished surface. At the center, he stopped and turned slowly, measuring distance to each boundary, each extraction gate, each camera aperture.

The floor remembered impacts. Not literally, unless Cole had worse secrets than expected, but every arena carried habits. Students drifted toward the east side under pressure because the benches there rose higher and made space feel larger. Duelists with public nerves overplayed openings facing the faculty box. The western pylon hummed half a tone lower than the others, likely older, maybe slower to react under interference.

Roan marked all of it.

Then he drew Sidewound and made one cut through empty air.

Not for practice. For calibration.

The blade found nothing, because there was nothing to find yet.

Tomorrow, Kael would bring cold into this ring. Cold had seams. Pride had seams. Bloodlines had seams.

Roan sheathed the knife.

Above him, the public cameras remained dark.

For now.

He didn't know what he would do if Brendt died. That was the real problem.

He left the arena by the west tunnel instead of the main stair. Halfway through, he stopped at the medical notification on his slate. No change in Brendt's status. Stable. Unconscious. The words were insufficient and therefore useful; they didn't pretend to comfort him.

Roan closed the message and kept walking.

Tomorrow required sleep, murder discipline, legal precision, and a face the crowd could misunderstand.

He could supply three of those easily.

The fourth would be negotiable.
