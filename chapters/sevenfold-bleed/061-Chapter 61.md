Chapter 61: Hollowstrike

Theon Mercer's fist stopped six inches from Roan's jaw.

Not because Theon chose to stop it. Because something in Roan's stance, the way he'd planted his feet, angled his guard, invited the punch in the first place, made the follow-through feel like stepping off a cliff.

"That's Hollowstrike," Theon said, recalibrating. His knuckles hovered near Roan's face. "You're using the doctrine."

"Observant."

"You're supposed to be a sword guy."

"I'm supposed to be a lot of things." Roan disengaged, rolling his shoulder. Theon's strike had been clean. Tempered-tier speed, nothing fancy, but the absorption cost him a half-second of positioning. A half-second was a problem. "Drogan says the doctrine applies to any strike you want to absorb and redirect."

"Drogan says a lot of things." Theon reset his stance, testing. "Most of them involve hitting things until they break."

"Also accurate."

They'd been at this for forty minutes. The training hall's north wall was scored with impact marks, and the afternoon light slanted low through the high windows, painting everything amber. Roan was drenched in sweat. Theon was breathing hard but controlled, the difference between someone who trained for rankings and someone who trained because he couldn't afford not to. Roan catalogued both states. The arithmetic mattered.

Theon's next combination came faster. Three-strike burst: solar plexus, jaw, floating rib. Standard tournament sequence, designed to overwhelm guard recovery between impacts. Roan took the first on his forearm guard, let the second glance off his shoulder, and set for the third. The Hollowstrike doctrine fired.

He felt it in his oblique, the way the impact didn't stop at his ribs but pulled inward, got absorbed into the surrounding tissue, converted. The doctrine said to redirect the stored force into the next action. Most practitioners would channel it into a counterpunch. Roan channeled his into a pivot that put his hip behind Theon's guard and his knee into Theon's thigh—not a damaging strike, but one that compromised the next stance.

Theon staggered. His combination broke.

"Again," Roan said.

Theon grinned, reset. Theon had a good grin, wide, uncomplicated, the kind that made people underestimate him. Which was exactly the point.

Forty minutes became an hour. Theon stopped resetting after the first thirty seconds of each exchange. Roan stopped offering commentary after the first twenty. They sparred in silence, two bodies learning each other's reach and timing, the hollow echo of fists on flesh filling the training hall.

Roan took damage. Absorbed it. Redirected it. Took more damage. The doctrine was demanding in ways Crimson Wake wasn't—no dramatic energy surge without visible result. Just the internal sensation of impact becoming momentum, of being hit and using that hit to position for the next exchange.

His Inversion capacity sat at 1/4. He hadn't used it once.

That wasn't an accident. Drogan found them as the light shifted from amber to grey.

"Tolerable," he said from the doorway, arms crossed. He was wearing a compression sleeve on his left arm—the Iron Hand of Hale was off for maintenance, which meant he'd been in the forge district since dawn. "Mercer, your third combination has a two-inch gap between the jaw strike and the floating rib. Vance, you're closing it, but you're closing it late."

"He hit me before I could close it," Roan said.

"He hit you because you let him hit you."

Roan's jaw tightened. Theon made a sound that might've been a suppressed laugh.

"Hollowstrike isn't a counter-doctrine," Drogan continued, stepping into the hall. His footsteps were heavier than usual—without the Iron Hand's counterweight, his gait had a slight asymmetry that Roan filed away. "It's a pre-emption doctrine. You're not supposed to absorb a strike and then redirect it. You're supposed to absorb a strike and then redirect *the next one*. The timing is everything."

"The timing requires you to eat a hit first," Roan said.

"Yes."

"That seems suboptimal."

"Most useful things are." Drogan dropped onto an equipment crate with the careful motion of a man who'd learned exactly how much his body could take. "The doctrine is built on an old principle: the best defense against force isn't blocking it. It's using your opponent's commitment against them. But that requires you to let them commit first."

Roan considered this. Theon had drifted to the water station, drinking with the methodical focus of someone who'd been working hard.

"You're talking about psychology," Roan said.

"I'm talking about geometry. When someone commits to a strike, they commit to a direction, a force, a moment of balance. The strike is a vector. If you absorb it, you're taking that vector into your body. But you're also taking it *out of theirs*. They've emptied their stance for that moment. That's your window."

"And if the next strike comes faster than I can redirect?"

"Then you ate a hit for nothing, and you're on the ground."

Roan ran the math. Hollowstrike in the tournament context meant eating one attack—the opener, the probing strike—then redirecting the follow-up. But tournament fighters didn't fight in predictable sequences. They varied tempo, feinted, reset. If the opponent chose not to follow up, the doctrine's advantage collapsed.

Unless you made them follow up.

"The doctrine assumes your opponent will commit to a second strike," Roan said.

"The doctrine assumes your opponent is human," Drogan replied. "Humans don't like unanswered aggression. You hit them once, they'll hit you back. That's not theory. That's twenty years of watching people try to be rational in a fight." He tilted his head. "Why do you think I'm teaching you this now?"

Roan had a theory. He had several, sorted by probability. One: Drogan wanted him to have a doctrine that worked without Vitalis expenditure, since his Inversion capacity was limited. Two: Drogan was preparing him for a specific opponent in the tournament, someone with overwhelming offense. Three: something was coming that Drogan knew about and Roan didn't.

He didn't voice any of these. "Because Ashford's going to fight like he has something to prove."

Drogan's expression didn't change, which meant Roan had said something relevant.

"Cyrus Ashford favors aggression," Drogan said. "He hits hard, hits fast, and he doesn't stop until you either go down or make him stop. His doctrine is called the Ashford Spire—a sequence technique designed to keep pressure on until the opponent breaks. He's been using it since he was fourteen."

"And you think Hollowstrike is the counter."

"I think you need to learn how to eat a hit without flinching." Drogan leaned forward. "I'm not sending you into the tournament to dodge and defend. That's not your style. And it's not how you beat someone like Ashford. You beat someone like Ashford by taking his best shots, staying standing, and hitting him back when he can't."

Theon's water break had turned into eavesdropping. He was standing by the station, pretending to refill his bottle while listening.

Roan absorbed the logic. It was sound—brutally, obviously sound—the kind that only became visible when you ran the scenarios. Cyrus Ashford's entire fighting style was a hammer. The counter to a hammer wasn't a bigger hammer. It was an anvil.

"How long until the tournament rounds are announced?" Roan asked.

"Five days."

Five days. Seventeen days since he'd beaten the Greycross qualifier field without using the Sevenfold Bleed, without breaking a sweat, without anyone seeing what he could do. Five days until the bracket placed him across from someone like Cyrus Ashford—someone with a name, a reputation, a Conclave family watching from the stands.

Five days to learn a doctrine that required him to get hit on purpose.

"Show me again," Roan said.

Drogan stood.

The next three days collapsed into repetition.

- break -

Morning: Hollowstrike drills until Roan's ribs felt like they'd been tenderized. Drogan would throw combinations at him—open-palm strikes, forearm checks, occasional kicks—and Roan would absorb each one and attempt to redirect the next. The timing was brutal. Too early and he redirected nothing. Too late and he'd already taken the second hit.

"You're thinking," Drogan said on the second morning.

"I always think."

"Stop. The doctrine doesn't work if you analyze the timing. You have to *feel* it."

"That doesn't make sense."

"Lots of useful things don't."

- break -

Afternoon: Theon, who had apparently decided that Roan's training schedule was his training schedule, would come to the hall and throw three-strike combinations until his knuckles went red. Theon was good at combinations. Theon was also good at varying them—adding a fourth strike, swapping the sequence, throwing feints that looked like commits and commits that looked like feints.

"Ashford's going to have better combinations than me," Theon said on the second afternoon. He'd developed a bruise on his forearm from blocking Roan's redirect attempts. "I'm Tempered-tier. He's Tempered-tier. But he's been tournament-fighting since he was fifteen."

"I know."

"So why am I the one throwing combinations at you?"

"Because you're here and he's not."

Theon considered this. "That's not really an answer."

"It's the only answer that matters."

- break -

Evening: Roan alone in the training hall, running Crimson Wake forms with his off-hand while his primary side recovered. The Sevenfold Bleed First Strike was off the table—he'd used it once in the qualifier, and the memory of what it felt like was enough. His Vitalis had settled around 1,200 since the tournament circuit began, drifting upward with the steady pressure of training. He was still Tempered. He'd be Tempered for another few weeks, maybe longer.

The Inversion system tracked his capacity. 3/4 remaining. He hadn't used it since Ch 58, when he'd burned through two conversions in a single fight.

Three days without using Inversion felt strange. Like a tool sitting in a drawer, unused. He could feel the Codex Shard's attention at the base of his skull—a faint hum, a readiness. Aldric's echo had been quiet since the qualifier. Not absent, just… waiting.

"You're thinking about it again," Aldric's voice said from nowhere, which was how Aldric communicated when he felt like communicating.

"I'm thinking about the tournament."

"You're thinking about how much you'd like to hit Cyrus Ashford in the face."

Roan's expression didn't change. "That thought has occurred."

"It occurred in Ch 42 when you watched him beat that kid from Aldermere. It occurred again in Ch 45 when he said your name during the bracket preview. It's occurring right now." A pause that felt deliberate. "The tally doesn't lie, kid. You want to hurt him."

"I want to win."

"There's no difference, in your case. You just haven't decided which one to admit."

Roan returned to his Crimson Wake forms. Aldric faded back into silence—a presence without words, the way he'd been since the Codex Shard had embedded itself in Roan's spine.

Theon found him at sunset on the third day.

"Announcement came," Theon said. He'd been checking the bracket boards in the main hall. "Your quarterfinal opponent."

Roan didn't break his form. "Who."

"Me."

Theon stood in the doorway, silhouetted against the orange light, his breathing controlled, his expression carefully neutral. Not a feint. Not a joke. The bracket boards didn't lie.

"First round," Theon continued, "you draw Mira Holloway. She pulled out with a stress fracture. Next bracket slot fell to you, then to me." He stepped into the hall. "I didn't rig it."

"I know."

"If you want to throw the match—"

"No."

"—I understand. You need to conserve for Ashford. I know that too."

Roan completed his Crimson Wake form, sheathed his off-hand blade, and turned to face Theon. Theon was wearing his training clothes, already sweating from whatever drill he'd run before coming here. His Vitalis reading would be around 600, maybe 650 if he'd been pushing hard. Standard Tempered. Nothing special.

"I'm not throwing the match," Roan said.

Theon's expression tightened. "You know this is my bracket too. I'm not just a warm-up drill, Vance."

"I know."

"Good. Don't make me regret this."

He let the silence sit for a beat before continuing. "If we fight, I'm going to try to beat you."

"Okay."

"You're okay with that?"

Theon shrugged. It wasn't the easy grin he wore in public. This one had edges. "I was never going to beat Ashford. I got placed in his bracket half. But you and me?" He smiled, and it was quieter now. "I've been waiting to see what happens when you stop holding back."

"You've been throwing combinations at me for three days."

"I've been throwing combinations at you for three days, and you've been using them for Hollowstrike practice, and I've been watching, and I think you've been holding back." Theon's eyes were sharp in the low light. "The qualifier. I was in the stands. You beat four Tempered-tier fighters and I didn't see you break a sweat."

"I was measured."

"You were *performing*. There's a difference." Theon stepped closer. "I don't know what you are, Vance. Nobody does. But I know what you're not doing, which is fighting like you mean it. So yeah." He dropped his hands to his sides. "I'm okay with you beating me. Because I want to see what happens after."

Roan studied him. Theon's Vitalis, his stance, the way he held his shoulders. Theon's sincerity—genuine, uncomplicated, the kind that didn't calculate returns.

"You're an idiot," Roan said.

"I've been told."

"Your combinations have a gap. The one Drogan mentioned—the two-inch space between the jaw strike and the floating rib. It's exploitable."

"I know."

"If I fight you in the bracket, I'm going to exploit it."

"Expected."

"I'm not going to feel bad about it."

Theon laughed. "Okay, I'm going to feel a little bad about it." His grin returned, but it was quieter now. "But I'll get over it. Because you'll beat Ashford, and I'll have been part of the reason you were ready."

Roan held his gaze. Then he picked up his primary blade.

"Five-minute break," he said. "Then you throw combinations. I practice Hollowstrike. And tomorrow, we see what the bracket decides."

"Sounds like a plan."

It wasn't a plan. It was a countdown. Three days until the quarterfinals. Three days until the bracket put him across from Theon Mercer or anyone else the tournament assigned. Three days until Cyrus Ashford watched whoever survived that exchange and started preparing his Ashford Spire.

Three days until Roan found out whether Hollowstrike was enough. The hall was dark when he left. Theon's combinations had given him three bruises, a cracked knuckle from a redirect gone wrong, and a solid understanding of how the doctrine felt when it worked.

The Hollowstrike doctrine drained him in a way Crimson Wake didn't—a kinetic awareness tax, the constant mental load of tracking vectors in real time. It was a different kind of burnout entirely, one that had nothing to do with his Inversion capacity. The resource the Codex Shard tracked, the conversion potential, sat untouched at a full 3/4.

The Codex Shard hummed at the base of his skull.

[Capacity: 3/4. — A.V.]

Aldric was keeping score again.

Roan walked back to his quarters through the Greycross night, the tournament lights glowing on the eastern horizon like a second sun. In three days, he'd be in that arena. In three days, someone was going to try to hurt him, and he was going to let them, and he was going to use what they gave him to hurt them back.

Arithmetic had no incentive to flatter him.

But the ledger didn't tell him whether Theon was right—whether he was holding back, whether the qualifier had been a performance, whether the thing underneath the performance was something he wanted anyone to see.

He didn't think about it. He calculated.

One day at a time. One hit at a time. One redirect at a time.

The tournament lights pulsed on the horizon, patient and absolute.

He had three days.
