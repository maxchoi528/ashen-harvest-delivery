Chapter 4: First Blood

"Where did you find that?"

Mira Vance stood in the doorway, dish towel slung over one shoulder, hands still wet from the evening wash. The kitchen behind her was clean, the same obsessive cleanliness that filled every corner of their Greycross apartment.

Roan looked up from his desk. The pendant hung against his chest, its leather cord threaded through the clasp he'd fashioned from a split keyring. He'd expected comments. He'd prepared deflections. "School project" was already queued. "Found it in the closet" was the backup.

He hadn't prepared for her voice.

Not the tone, the tone was normal. Mild curiosity, the kind you'd use if your son brought home a stray cat. But underneath that, something else. Something he'd heard only once before, when Lyra had swallowed a battery and Mira had found it missing from the remote.

"Father's things," Roan said. "Box in the basement."

Mira's hands stopped moving. The dish towel hung there, damp and forgotten. Her eyes stayed on the pendant, on the matte black iron, the scratched surface, the symbols that looked decorative but weren't.

"That's his," she said. It wasn't a question. The words came out flat, like she'd said them before and knew exactly how they sounded. She stepped into the room. One step. Two. She crossed the threshold and stood there, close enough that Roan could see the fine lines at the corners of her eyes, the ones that hadn't been there when he was twelve.

"When did you find it?" she asked.

"Today. Sorting through."

"Today." She repeated it like she was testing the word. "Before or after you put it on?"

"After."

She nodded. Once. The gesture looked mechanical, like she'd learned it from watching other people nod and hadn't internalized the reasoning.

Roan waited. He had about forty responses ready, explanations, deflections, the standard vocabulary of a seventeen-year-old trying to avoid unnecessary conflict. But he also had something else, a skill he'd developed over three years of living with a mother who spoke in silences: the ability to read what wasn't being said.

Mira was afraid.

Not of the pendant. Not of him. Of something connected to both, or to the space where both overlapped. Her jaw wasn't tight—that would've been anger, and this wasn't anger. Her shoulders weren't hunched. This was the posture of someone standing very still because they knew that moving would break something.

"You should put it back," she said. "The box. It belongs with the rest."

"I know what it is."

Those four words landed in the room and stayed there. Mira's hands, which had been hanging at her sides, shifted. Left hand over right. Fingers interlaced. The posture of someone preparing to hold something heavy.

"I know what it is too," she said. "That's why I'm telling you to put it back."

"And I'm telling you I won't."

The room went quiet. Five seconds. Ten. Fifteen.

Roan watched his mother count the same seconds. He could see the answer happening behind her eyes, not the emotional kind, the arithmetic kind. She was weighing options, measuring consequences, running scenarios like he would have done. Except her math was different. Her math included things like "how much will pushing this cost me" and "what happens if he resents me" and "is that better or worse than what happens if he keeps it on."

Twenty seconds.

Lyra's music leaked through the wall from the next room. Something with fast percussion and guitars that sounded like they were arguing with each other. Normal life, continuing its normal rhythms.

Twenty-five.

Mira uncrossed her fingers. Recrossed them the other way. Roan recognized the tell—when someone's trying to reverse a decision they just made in their head. She wanted to say something. She was stopping herself.

Thirty.

She sat down on the edge of his bed.

This was new. Mira never sat on his bed. She hovered in doorways. She stopped at the edge of rooms. She existed in threshold states, always ready to exit. But now she was sitting, and the weight of it registered in Roan's chest like a stone dropped into still water.

"The Spire testing is tomorrow," she said.

Testing was generous language. Tomorrow was the Greycross pre-screening and subsidy registration, the last local intake before the standard ninety-day preparation track locked without him. The words came out wrong—too formal, too prepared. She was reading from a script she'd written in her head during those thirty seconds. Emergency pivot. Talk about something else. Don't let him see you scared.

"I know," Roan said.

"You need sleep. Early start. There's a shuttle from the community center at six."

"I know."

"Eat breakfast. The protein bars, not the sugar ones. You always forget to—"

"Mom."

She stopped.

"I know," he said. "I've done this before. Twice."

She looked at him. Her eyes were the same grey as his father's had been, or so Lyra said. Roan had never been able to see it. To him, Aldric's eyes had been a different color entirely, something he'd lost the ability to recall correctly the way you lost the exact sound of someone's voice after they were gone.

"Three times," she said. "You went three times."

"Four. You stopped counting after I was fourteen."

"I counted." She stood up. The moment of sitting had been an anomaly, and now it was over. "I just stopped mentioning it."

She moved toward the door. Roan let her. He understood what was happening. She'd given him the pendant conversation, acknowledged it, made her position clear, and retreated to defensible ground. She wasn't going to fight him about this. She was going to let him make his own choices and bear the consequences herself, the way she'd done since Aldric died.

But she was going to do it scared. That part he couldn't fix.

"Brush your teeth," she said from the doorway. "And take that off before you sleep. Cords leave marks."

The door closed. Her footsteps retreated down the hall. The kitchen faucet turned on, he could hear the pipes shudder two rooms away, the sound of a house settling into its nighttime routine.

Roan looked down at the pendant.

- break -

He waited until her light went out.

Ninety minutes. He'd counted. Mira kept a schedule that would've impressed a military unit: kitchen cleanup complete by nine-fifteen, bathroom by nine-thirty, bedroom by nine-forty-five, lights out by ten sharp. She'd been doing it since the funeral, or maybe before. He didn't remember a time when the house had run on any other rhythm.

At ten-thirty, he got up from his desk.

The apartment was dark except for the streetlight bleeding through the blinds, painting everything in stripes of yellow and black. Lyra's room was silent now—she'd either fallen asleep to her music or turned it off to conserve battery. Either way, she wasn't a factor.

Roan sat on the edge of his bed and held the pendant in both hands.

The weight was the first thing he'd noticed that afternoon, before Mira had come in. It was wrong. The pendant was maybe three centimeters across, half a centimeter thick, made of iron that looked like it had been cast somewhere cheap. Standard mass for that size would've put it at thirty grams, maybe forty if the iron was pure.

This weighed more like a hundred and twenty.

He'd tested it against other objects. A wrench from the toolbox. A stack of quarters. His bathroom scale was analog and not precise enough to give him an exact number, but the comparison was obvious. The thing was dense in a way that didn't match its volume.

He'd also noticed something else. The chain.

The chain was steel, the cheap kind, the kind you got at a hardware store for a credit and a half per meter. Standard issue. But when he'd tried to pull it apart to examine the links, he couldn't. The steel resisted. His fingers weren't strong enough, but neither were pliers. He'd squeezed until his hand cramped, and the chain links hadn't deformed so much as a millimeter.

Now, in the dark, he examined it again.

The pendant's surface was covered in etched symbols. Not decorative—they were too precise for that. Each line was exactly the same depth, the same width, cut into the iron with a tool that left no variation. He'd tried to trace one of the symbols with his thumbnail and felt resistance. The etches were harder than the surrounding metal.

He reached into his desk drawer and pulled out a craft knife. The blade was disposable, designed for cutting paper and foam board. Way too soft for anything real. But he needed to know.

He pressed the edge against the pendant's surface.

The blade bent. Not broke, bent, like a piece of soft aluminum foil. The metal curled away from the iron without leaving a mark.

Roan stared at the bent knife.

Then he laughed.

It wasn't a real laugh. He'd heard the difference on recordings, the distinction between a laugh that started in your chest and one that started in your throat and went nowhere. This was the second kind. The kind that happened when you were alone and something was absurd enough that your body insisted on marking it, even if your brain wanted no part of it.

He put the knife back in the drawer.

So the pendant was heavy and hard. Those were properties. Properties had explanations. Even weird properties had explanations—unusual alloys, manufacturing defects, deliberate modifications. None of these things were supernatural. None of them required him to assume anything beyond "this object isn't what it appears to be."

The pendant was a pendant. It was his father's, which meant it had belonged to a man who had vanished under circumstances that Roan wasn't supposed to ask about. The weight was a mystery. The hardness was a mystery. The symbols were mysteries.

Mysteries could be solved. That was all they were: problems without visible solutions.

He hung the pendant back around his neck. The weight settled against his chest, and he felt it there—not uncomfortable, but present. A fact he had to carry.

Something knocked on his door.

Not a knock, exactly. More like a tap, three quick hits with a knuckle, followed immediately by the handle turning. Lyra never waited for answers. She had the patience of a natural disaster.

"You still up?"

He wasn't going to answer that. "What do you want?"

She came in anyway, padding across the carpet in socks that had cartoon cats on them. She'd been wearing those socks since she was twelve. The cats were faded now, their expressions worn down to a permanent grimace.

"I saw Mom's light go out. Figured you'd still be brooding."

"I'm not brooding."

"You have your brooding face on. The one where your eyebrows do that thing."

Roan touched his eyebrows. He hadn't known they were doing a thing. "What thing?"

"I don't know. The thing." She sat on the end of his bed, the same spot where Mira had sat forty minutes earlier. Different energy, though. Lyra sat like she belonged there. Mira had sat like she was visiting a foreign country.

Lyra's eyes went to the pendant.

Something shifted in her expression. Not surprise, not the "oh that's interesting" reaction he'd expected. Something older. Something that looked like recognition.

"That's Dad's," she said.

"Yeah."

"Where'd you find it?"

"Basement. The box."

"The locked one?"

Roan raised an eyebrow. "It wasn't locked."

"It was when I tried. Last year. I wanted to see what was in there." She shrugged, a gesture that carried no weight of guilt. Lyra didn't do guilt—she'd mastered the art of treating her own boundary-testing as totally reasonable. "Couldn't get it open. Seemed like it wasn't supposed to be opened."

"You got into it today?"

"No. You did, apparently." She leaned forward, studying the pendant from a distance.
