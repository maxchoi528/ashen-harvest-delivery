Chapter 14: The Partnership

The shop smelled like sulfur and older things. Dried herbs, maybe, or the residue of things that had been alive and were now just ash. Old Pell’s hand stopped on the ledger when Roan spoke.

“Left cabinet, third shelf, behind the Ironbile cases. Stamped with last month’s batch code.”

Pell didn’t look up. “Didn’t think anyone was checking.”

“I’m checking.”

The old man set down his pen. When he finally raised his head, there was something careful in his expression. Not fear, exactly, but the wariness of someone who had just learned they were being observed more closely than they’d assumed.

“Wraithmilk’s not cheap,” Pell said. “And it’s not popular.”

“Explain.”

“Mental power, right? That’s what the textbooks say. But that’s not what happens to most people who drink it.” He leaned back. The chair creaked. “They get the boost. Then they get the crash. Three days a month where their thoughts turn to static. Some call it the haze. Others call it the fracture. Point is, it’s not a supplement. It’s a gamble. Smart kids don’t buy it.”

Roan kept his face neutral. “What if the gamble had better odds?”

“There are no better odds. The haze is baked into the tincture. Always has been. Every alchemist who’s tried to remove it ends up removing the boost along with it.”

Pell studied Roan for a moment, then shook his head. “You’re not buying this for the boost, are you.”

No response.

“Alright.” Pell stood. Joints popped. “Three bottles. Sixty credits each. Total of one hundred and eighty.”

Roan placed the stones on the counter. Pell swept them into a drawer without counting.

“Back cabinet. Third shelf. I didn’t see anything.”

“Appreciated.”

He collected the bottles from the back. Dark glass. Sealed with wax. Warm to the touch even though the storage room was cold.

---

Back in his room, Roan set the bottles on the desk and sat down.

He didn’t open one immediately.

Bloodscourge had been about the body. Raw power. Faster recovery. The ability to push past limits that would cripple normal Initiates. Wraithmilk was about something else entirely. Mental fortitude. Resistance to interference. The ability to function when everything around him was trying to tear his thoughts apart.

Black Spire was full of people who would try to do exactly that. Seniors who tested newcomers. Instructors who pushed students to breaking points. The constant pressure of ranking, of comparison, of being measured against people who had been training since they were children while Roan had spent six years stealing food and dodging security patrols.

He needed to be able to think when everyone else was falling apart.

But three days a month of static thoughts, fractured cognition, lost time, no control over his own mind — that was a liability he couldn’t afford. Not here. Not now.

He picked up the first bottle.

The seal broke with a soft crack. The smell hit him immediately: something acrid, almost metallic, with an undertone of something floral that didn’t belong. His stomach tightened.

*Small dose. Test the conversion rate.*

He drank.

The liquid was cold going down. Then warmth — not the burning of Bloodscourge, but something gentler, spreading through his skull like oil on water. His thoughts began to move faster. Not clearer, exactly, but quicker. The background noise of a normal mind became sharper, more distinct.

And then the haze started.

It came in slowly, a softening at the edges of perception, like someone had turned down the resolution on everything he was seeing. Colors bled into each other. Sounds became indistinct. His hands, resting on the desk, looked like they belonged to someone else.

He felt his breathing slow.

Felt his grip on his own cognition start to slip.

*Right. So this was the fracture.*

The Codex Shard pressed against his spine. Warm. Quiet. Waiting.

Roan focused. The sensation was familiar now: the feeling of something foreign taking root in his body, trying to establish itself as a new normal. He knew how to handle this. He’d done it once already.

He held the sensation for exactly two seconds, counting the way he’d learned. One. Two. Three.

On three, he pushed.

The sensation inverted. The haze didn’t disappear — it reversed. Instead of fracturing his thoughts, it began to reinforce them. The edges of his perception sharpened. The background noise didn’t fade; it became organized, structured, a kind of pattern-recognition he’d never experienced before.

His mind felt like a room that had just been swept clean. Empty, but in a useful way. Ready to receive new furniture.

[Sidewound absorbed. Conversion successful. Status: Mental Fortitude (Partial). Duration: 6h. Do not sleep.]

Roan exhaled slowly. His hands looked normal again. His thoughts were his own.

*Okay.*

He looked at the remaining two bottles. If he could convert one successfully, he could convert all three. But the system had warned him about something — the warning at the end, about not sleeping during the active period. That implied there were conditions he didn’t fully understand yet.

He needed more information.

He needed to test the boundaries.

Before he could reach for the second bottle, there was a knock at his door.

Three sharp raps. A pause. Two more.

Roan went still.

That wasn’t a random visitor. That was a pattern. Someone who knew the schedule, who knew when Roan would be back, who had marked his door and was now announcing their presence with a specific rhythm.

He stood. Crossed to the door. Opened it exactly six inches — enough to see, not enough to be vulnerable.

Brendt stood in the hallway.

The other boy looked like he hadn’t slept. Shadows under his eyes. Uniform wrinkled in a way that suggested he’d been wearing it for more than a standard training day. He was holding a folded piece of paper.

“Spire command sent these out an hour ago,” Brendt said. “You’re going to want to see this.”

He held the paper through the gap.

Roan took it. Unfolded it.

The header was stamped with the Black Spire seal. Below that, a list of names. And below the names, a single line of text:

GROUP ASSIGNMENT: TRIAL SECTORS 7-9. DURATION: 72 HOURS. REPORT TO COMBAT INSTRUCTOR VANCE NO. 4 BY 0600 TOMORROW.

Roan read it twice.

The name at the top of the list was his.

“Sector 7?” Brendt’s voice was low. “That’s the deep-level testing grounds. They don’t assign first-years to Sector 7. They don’t assign anyone to Sector 7 unless they’ve—”

“Unless they’ve done something to get noticed.” Roan’s jaw tightened. He folded the paper carefully. “Or unless someone wants to put them there for a reason.”

“What reason? You’ve been here three days. You haven’t done anything except—”

“Exactly.” He looked at Brendt over the top of the paper. “That’s the problem, isn’t it.”

Brendt didn’t respond.

Roan closed the door. Locked it.

He stood in the center of his room. Wraithmilk bottles on the desk. Assignment in his hand. The faint hum of the Codex Shard at the base of his skull.

Someone had flagged him. Someone had looked at a nobody from Greycross and decided he was worth watching. Worth testing. Worth putting into a situation where three days in deep-level sectors might turn into three days in a shallow grave.

The math was incomplete.

He didn’t know who. Didn’t know why. Didn’t know what they were expecting to happen.

But he knew one thing: the assignment was real. The clock was running. And if he didn’t report at 0600 tomorrow, he’d be marked as a deserter.

Which meant he was going.

He looked at the Wraithmilk bottles. Six hours of improved mental fortitude. Three bottles remaining. Seventy-two hours in a deep-level sector with unknown threats and unknown enemies.

The shape of it wasn’t kind. But it was what he had.

He reached for the second bottle. Stopped. Six hours. The briefing was at 0600. The timing didn’t work.

He let his hand fall. Pocketed the remaining two bottles instead. Wraithmilk was a resource, not a habit. He’d need it in the dark, not before.

---

The corridors at 0530 were nearly empty. The pre-dawn silence of Black Spire felt different from the silence of his room — thicker, more deliberate, like the building itself was holding its breath.

Roan walked toward the briefing hall. His mind was clear. The Wraithmilk conversion was still active, and the effect was stronger than he’d expected: his perception felt sharper, his reaction time noticeably faster, and the ambient stress of the situation felt manageable in a way it wouldn’t have without the boost.

But underneath that clarity, he was calculating.

The assignment was legitimate. Brendt had confirmed that much. Spire command issued group trials. Sector assignments were part of the normal curriculum. Nothing about the paperwork had been falsified.

Which meant the trap, if it was a trap, wasn’t in the paperwork.

It was in the sector itself.

“Roan Vance.”

The voice came from ahead. Roan stopped.

Combat Instructor Vance No. 4 was a woman in her forties. Tall. Posture that suggested she’d spent her entire life being watched. She stood at the entrance to the briefing hall with her arms crossed. Behind her, the lights inside were already on.

“You’re early,” she said.

“Habit.”

She studied him. “Vance. That name’s not common.”

“Family name.”

“Mm.” She didn’t elaborate. Instead, she stepped aside and gestured toward the hall. “Inside. The rest of your group is waiting.”

Roan walked past her.

The briefing hall was a wide room with a central holotable and seating arranged in a semicircle. There were five other students already present, all of them older than Roan, all wearing the gray training uniforms of Initiates who had been at Black Spire longer than three days.

None of them looked surprised to see him.

That was interesting.

He found an empty seat at the end of the semicircle and sat down. The others glanced at him, then away. The silence in the room had a quality to it — not hostile, exactly, but watchful. Evaluating.

Instructor Vance entered behind him and walked to the holotable.

“Sector 7 is the deepest level currently accessible to Initiate-tier students,” she said without preamble. The display behind her activated, showing a three-dimensional map of underground tunnels, environmental readouts, and threat classifications. “It was opened for testing four months ago. Since then, we’ve lost two students and had four serious injuries.”

She paused.

“We don’t know exactly what’s down there. The initial surveys were incomplete. What we do know is that there are Hollowkin presence indicators in the midlevels, and we suspect there’s a Hollow Core somewhere in the lower sections. Your job is to map the primary corridors, mark any structural instabilities, and retrieve any artifacts of potential research value.”

She looked at the group.

“You will be operating in pairs. Pairings are assigned based on your application files.” She tapped the table. Two names lit up on the display. “Holloway and Mercer. Tanner and Westbrook. Vance and Cole.”

Silence.

Roan looked at the name that had just appeared beside his own.

Eamon Cole.

The boy across the room — slight build, unremarkable face that disappeared in crowds — raised his hand in a small wave. He didn’t smile.

Roan watched him. Labeling Cole wasn’t worth the mental ink yet. He just watched.

Application files meant someone had chosen this pairing deliberately. Either the system had auto-assigned based on compatibility scores, or someone had manually inserted two names into the same group. The difference mattered.

“Deployment begins at 0700,” Instructor Vance continued. “You have ninety minutes to prepare your equipment and review the sector briefing materials. Dismissed.”

The group stood. The others filed out — some in pairs, some alone.

Roan stayed seated.

Cole approached.

“Roan Vance.” The boy’s voice was quiet. Uninflected. “Eamon Cole. I assume you’ve already calculated the odds of this pairing being random.”

“High enough to warrant observation.”

“Agreed.” Cole tilted his head slightly. “I looked up your file. Greycross. No lineage. Test scores that don’t match your housing block assessment. You’re a utility-maximizer playing catch-up. I’m the same, just from a different ditch.”

Roan considered this. “You’re suggesting this assignment is designed to eliminate us.”

“I’m suggesting it’s designed to test whether we’re worth the investment or just an empty seat. The ones they want to eliminate get exactly this kind of assignment. If we come back with useful data, we’ll have proven ourselves. If we don’t come back, we’ll have proven we weren’t worth the trouble.”

Roan stood.

“Then we make sure we come back with something useful.”

Cole extended a hand. “Partnership? My odds improve if you’re alive. I trust yours do too.”

Roan looked at the hand. Considered the risks. Calculated the potential value of an ally who understood institutional manipulation from the inside.

He shook.

“Partnership.”

---

The elevator to Sector 7 descended for too long. Roan stood in the center of the small platform, the Wraithmilk conversion still humming in his system, the Codex Shard a faint pressure at the base of his skull. Beside him, Cole was silent, reviewing the sector maps on a handheld display.

The numbers on the depth gauge ticked upward.

Sector 1 was the training level. Basic combat scenarios. Controlled environments. Nothing that could kill a student. Sector 4 was where things started to get interesting: real Rift-adjacent conditions, limited visibility, occasional Hollowkin contact.

Sector 7 was something else entirely.

The elevator slowed.

The doors opened onto darkness.

The air that rushed in was cold and heavy, carrying the unmistakable scent of somewhere the boundary between worlds had worn thin: ozone and decay.

Roan stepped out.

Cole followed.

Behind them, the elevator doors closed. The lights inside the platform flickered once, twice, then went dark entirely.

They were alone.

“Seventy-two hours,” Cole said quietly. “Starting now.”

Roan looked at the tunnel ahead. The darkness was absolute. Somewhere in the distance, something moved — a sound that wasn’t a footstep, wasn’t a whisper.

The tally was incomplete.

But the clock was running.

He took the first step forward.
