Chapter 37: Will Trial

The fifth trial of the Spire's entrance examination was called the Mask Breaker. It did not test how hard you could strike. It tested how well you could hold the shape of yourself when the world began to warp around you.

Roan Vance crossed the threshold into the arena and felt the construct fasten onto him like a second skin. It was not a physical space. It was a pressure chamber of the mind—a system designed to find the edges of your mask and pry them loose.

The Proctors sat in a gallery above. One of them was Selene Ardent. She was not a student here. She was an observer, her presence a signal from the old houses: *We are watching how the new assets break.*

The simulation opened.

He was standing in a dormitory. Grey light under the door. Brendt's breathing in the bunk beside him. The morning of an evaluation day.

*It's going to use the familiar against you,* the Codex Shard hummed against his chest. *Don't forget what's real.*

Roan already knew the rule. The Will Trial constructed a believable reality and watched how the subject navigated it. The better you performed, the more pressure it applied. The deeper it probed.

To pass, he had to be good enough to survive, but flawed enough to be dismissed.

He played along. The combat circuit activated. Wooden strikes extruded from the walls. Selene's projection watched from the edge of the grid.

The first strike came from his left. He ducked. Clean.
The second came from his right. He rolled. Textbook.
The third came from above.

He could have avoided it. His body knew the angle. The Inversion was already standing by, waiting to convert the force into energy. But he let his shoulder intercept the weighted swing instead of his head. Pain lanced through the joint—real enough in the simulation to register.

*Sidewound detected. Inversion standing by.*

*No,* he told the Shard. *Not here.*

He took the hit. Let it roll off. Finished the circuit in adequate time.

Not fast. Not flashy. Just enough to clear the bar.

In the gallery, Selene was writing.

The construct held. The simulation advanced. The corridor materialized around him in the transition between phases. Emergency lighting. Empty halls. Footsteps behind him.

A figure stepped from the shadow.

"Roan Vance."

He turned. The silhouette carried the weight of a name he hadn't wanted to hear inside the trial. *Theon Mercer.*

"Theon Mercer sends his regards," the figure said. "And a warning. He's been watching you. Since the tournament. He's noticed things. Inconsistencies. The way you move. The way you calculate. The way you took that swing this morning instead of avoiding it."

Roan's hand dropped to his belt. The knife wasn't real here. The threat was.

"Theon thinks you're hiding something. House Greel is very interested in dangerous people who hide things."

*They're reading the manual,* the Codex Shard whispered.

The trial had built this from intake data. From the profiles the Spire had already collected. Theon's surveillance wasn't a guess. It was a documented pattern that the construct could access.

Roan didn't flinch. The trial wanted a reaction. It wanted the mask to slip.

He fed it a surface truth. "Everyone hides something in a place like this."

The figure held for a long moment. Then it dissolved.

The simulation shifted again. A second-year named Voss materialized on the combat platform—a projection built from the academy's behavioral records. Hardened tier. Aggressive. Designed to pressure the subject into revealing more than they intended.

"Heard Ardent was asking about you," Voss said. "Let me give her something to write about."

Voss charged.

Roan sidestepped. Let the momentum carry past. Then he reached out and tapped the older student's shoulder blade, once, precisely, with just enough force to register.

"Tap," Roan said. "Match point. My favor."

Voss turned. "That doesn't count—"

"Section twelve. Paragraph three. 'Any strike that lands prior to defensive response constitutes valid contact.' You were still falling when I struck. I was already standing."

The construct flickered. Accepted.

In the gallery, Selene Ardent was smiling. Not the polite smile of an evaluator. Something sharper. Something that said *I see you.*

Then the corridor was back. The figure was back.

"You're running out of room to move," it said. "Theon's data is already logged. Selene's report is already half-written. The question isn't *if* they find the edges of your mask. The question is whether you survive the extraction."

Roan said nothing. The trial was a loop. It would keep applying pressure until he broke or until the time expired.

He held.

Three hours. Four. Five.

The construct began to thin at the edges, its power budget exhausted by his refusal to crack. The dormitory dissolved. The corridor dissolved. The arena walls reformed around him, grey and solid and real.

The Proctors' gallery was silent. Selene was scrolling through her tablet.

Roan walked off the floor with a steady pulse and a quiet alarm ringing in the back of his skull.

The trial was over. The real problem was just beginning.

He found the recovery ward. Sat on a bench. Brendt collapsed beside him, sweat-soaked.

"You held," Brendt said. "Six hours. Longest trial of the cycle."

"The trial built Theon's threat from the academy's intake data," Roan said. "Which means the surveillance is documented. Which means it's going to surface in the Proctors' cross-referencing logs."

"Surface when?"

"Three days. That's the evaluation window before the next administrative review."

"So what do we do?"

Roan looked at his hands. The Codex Shard pulsed beneath his ribs.

"We give them nothing to find. I become exactly what the trial log says I am. A cautious, slightly below-average candidate with good instincts and bad reaction times."

"Can you do that?"

"I have to."

Brendt didn't argue. He just nodded slowly and stared at the floor.

The recovery ward hummed with the quiet machinery of the Spire's medical systems. Other examinees were filing in, pale and shaken. Two of them were being carried.

The Codex Shard pulsed again.

*They're reading the manual. The window's closing. — A.V.*

Roan opened his eyes. Looked at the grey ceiling.

"Yeah," he said. "I know."

He didn't sleep.
