Chapter 128: House Pressure

The cell beneath the Ironworks foundry smelled of copper and rust. Roan had counted the stones in the walls during the first hour—forty-seven on the left, forty-three on the right, twelve in the ceiling that he could see. His eyes had adjusted to the darkness. A drain in the corner gurgled intermittently. Someone had scratched tally marks into the wall near the door. Forty-seven days' worth, if the tally was right.

They'd taken his weapons. Of course they had. Sidewound and Tertiary both, stripped in the back of a wagon that smelled of furnace soot. They'd taken the Vance Pendant too, but that didn't matter—the Codex Shard was embedded in his spine, and no amount of searching hands had found it. Small mercies.

The door opened. Torchlight spilled across the stone floor.

"You look less impressive without the sword."

The Greel envoy stepped into the cell, followed by two House guards. He wasn't wearing armor either, but somehow that made him look more dangerous, not less. His posture said he didn't need it.

Roan remained seated against the wall. "You look less impressive with a hundred guards and a locked door."

The envoy's jaw tightened—irritation, not anger. The distinction mattered. Irritation meant Roan had surprised him. Anger meant he'd struck a nerve.

"The Warden submitted your records before the Guild Tribunal," the envoy said. "Tempered tier, top of your class, three years of consistent advancement. They almost awarded you full credits for the Ashwood Rift clearances."

Almost. The word hung in the air.

"We amended that decision." The envoy crouched, bringing himself to Roan's eye level. "House Greel has petitioned for academic review. Your achievements are frozen pending investigation."

Investigation. The polite word for suffocation. They'd freeze his credits, block his advancement, lock his access to the practice halls and the supply office. They'd strangle him slowly and call it due process.

"The Tribunal meets in three days," the envoy continued. "If you want to attend, you may. But I think you'll find the outcome… predictable."

He produced a folded document. Roan recognized the seal: House Greel's crest, a northern bear wrapped around a broken crown. Official. Legal. Completely airtight.

"Surrender your Codex Shard and stop the Inversion research, and we'll drop the petition. Your credits unfreeze. Your record clears. You graduate on time."

Roan took the document but didn't unfold it. He held the envoy's gaze. "You're not here because House Greel cares about academic integrity. You're here because House Greel wants what my father left behind."

The envoy's smile thinned. "Your father was a traitor who—"

"My father was an Inversion researcher who died for knowledge your family tried to destroy." Roan tapped the document. "And you're trying to bury it with paperwork. But that's not the real target, is it? The Codex Shard is just a piece. There are others. You're collecting them."

The envoy's expression flickered—something between surprise and wariness. Roan had been testing, but the micro-reaction confirmed it. *Seven shards* wasn't a guess anymore; it was a probability with evidence.

"I don't know what you're talking about," the envoy said flatly.

"You're a bad liar." Roan stood slowly, keeping his movements unhurried. "You came here with a script. *Sign the document, stop the research, go back to your life.* But the script cracked when I said 'others.' Which means House Greel is hunting more than just my father's work."

The envoy's face went still. "You're grasping at shadows."

"Maybe." Roan folded the document and tucked it into his coat. "But I've got three days until the Tribunal. Let's see what I find in the meantime."

The envoy stared at him for a long moment, then turned and left. The guards followed. The door slammed shut, but Roan had already begun counting the seconds until he could move.

---

Brendt found him two hours later in a safe house near the Foundry District's eastern edge—a crumbling tenancy that Roan had purchased months ago under a false name. Brendt's shoulder was bandaged, and his face carried the grey pallor of someone who'd been running for hours.

"Found you. Took me an hour to track the right wagon routes."

Roan didn't turn from the window. "You shouldn't be here."

"Neither should you." Brendt dropped onto a crate, exhaling hard. "The Warden submitted your defense package. The Guild ruled it inadmissible."

"Inadmissible." Roan repeated the word as if tasting it. "On what grounds?"

"They cited Suthmere precedent—anything submitted by an interested party in the student's defense is automatically excluded when the petitioning house has prior academic jurisdiction."

Roan's fingers whitened against the windowsill. Prior academic jurisdiction. Another legal fiction. House Greel had been petitioning for authority over Black Spire's disciplinary proceedings for decades; the Tribunal had granted it three years ago in exchange for the family's Rift-hunting contributions.

"The Warden tried to argue conflict of interest," Brendt continued. "They voted eight to two against him."

Eight to two. Drogan had spent political capital Roan didn't know he possessed, and he'd lost by a margin that suggested the outcome had been decided before the hearing started.

"There's more." Brendt hesitated, then pressed on. "Cyrus Ashford submitted testimony."

Roan turned. "What kind of testimony?"

"Character evidence. In your favor." Brendt's voice dropped. "He argued that House Greel's petition was politically motivated and that your academic record demonstrated consistent excellence regardless of whether you were using prohibited techniques."

Cyrus Ashford—his tournament rival, the Ashford heir who'd been positioned across from him at every public evaluation—had just made himself an enemy of House Greel.

"Why?" Roan asked.

"I don't know. He looked… tired." Brendt paused. "But there's something else. After the session, I overheard one of the clerks mention that Cyrus's grandfather kept a private archive. Old research notes, predating the Founding Seven's consolidation."

Roan's mind clicked. "The Ashfords have a history with my father."

"Apparently. The clerk said Cyrus's grandfather and Aldric Vance were close. Shared theories, traded data. The archive is sealed now, but Cyrus might have accessed it."

The ledger rearranged itself. Cyrus's public defense wasn't altruism—it was a signal. A message that he knew something about House Greel's real motives, and that he was willing to burn bridges to act on it.

"Where is he now?" Roan asked.

"Ashford residence in the Upper Ward. Security's tight since the Ashwood Rift disaster—twice the normal complement."

Roan was already moving toward the door. "Then we don't go through the front."

---

The Ashford residence occupied a corner lot near the Guild Tribunal—close enough that its proximity to power was a statement of intent. Security patrols moved in predictable arcs: eight minutes between sweeps, based on the gap between torchlight cycles Roan had tracked from the adjacent rooftop.

Brendt crouched beside him on the tiles. "This is insane. If they catch us—"

"They won't catch us."

"You don't know that."

Roan did know. He'd spent three hours in the safe house calculating angles, timing guard rotations, identifying pressure points in the estate's defense. He'd mapped every patrol route from memory, cross-referenced them with the Tribunal building's posted guard schedules, and identified the seventeen-minute window when the eastern perimeter would be undermanned.

Seventeen minutes was enough.

The eastern wall was three stories up. Brendt paled when Roan showed him the route. "You're not serious."

"I never joke about infiltration."

"That's not what I—" Brendt sighed. "Fine. How?"

Roan produced a thin cord from his pack. "The furnace exhaust runs along the eastern wall. We follow it to the third floor, bypass the guard station, and enter through the servant's quarters."

"The servant's quarters have their own guards."

"Not at the hour when the kitchen staff changes shifts." Roan checked the angle of the moon. "They'll be moving between the scullery and the main house—four minutes of gap. We drop down, cross through the laundry yard, and we're inside."

"That assumes everything goes perfectly."

"I assume it won't, and I plan for the deviations." Roan slung the cord over his shoulder. "But we'll only know what happens if we try."

Brendt muttered something under his breath that might've been a complaint or might've been a prayer. Either way, he followed when Roan dropped off the roof.

The exhaust pipe was narrow and hot, sweating rust-scented condensation. Roan moved first—hand over hand, feet braced against rivets he'd identified from the rooftop. Brendt followed, his breathing loud in the confined space.

The third-floor entry point was a maintenance hatch used by furnace technicians. It took Roan thirty seconds to bypass the latch—twice as long as he'd planned, because the lock had been reinforced since the last patrol rotation. A variable. He'd adjust.

They dropped into a corridor that smelled of soap and lavender. Servants' quarters. The sounds of the kitchen filled the background: clattering pots, shouted orders, the rhythmic chopping that suggested prep work for the evening meal. They moved through the laundry yard in single file, Roan leading, Brendt covering the rear.

Cyrus Ashford's study was on the second floor, east wing. Roan had memorized the layout from public records and architectural guides—the Ashfords were old money, prominent enough that their family history appeared in social registers. The study had two entrances: the main door from the hallway, and a private entrance through the bedchamber that Cyrus used for sensitive meetings.

The bedchamber door was locked. Roan was working on the mechanism when footsteps echoed in the hallway.

He pulled Brendt into an alcove. They held their breath as a guard passed, torchlight sweeping across the wall three inches from Roan's face. The footsteps faded. Roan returned to the lock.

The mechanism clicked. The door opened.

Cyrus Ashford sat at his desk, writing by candlelight. He looked up as they entered, and his expression showed neither surprise nor alarm. He set down his pen with deliberate care.

"I wondered when you'd come."

Roan didn't sit. "You left a trail. Public testimony, a clerk who 'accidentally' mentioned your grandfather's archive—you wanted me here."

Cyrus's lips curved—not quite a smile. "You're sharper than I gave you credit for."

"I'm also running out of time. The Tribunal convenes in three days. House Greel has frozen my record and wants my Codex Shard." Roan stepped closer. "You went public against them. That means you know something that makes Greel a bigger threat than the cost of opposing them. I need to know what that is."

Cyrus leaned back in his chair. In the candlelight, he looked older than his years—lines around his eyes, grey threading through hair that should've been fully black at twenty-three. The exhaustion Brendt had described was visible now, carved into his face.

"House Greel isn't trying to stop Inversion research," Cyrus said. "They're trying to monopolize it. They've been hunting the original archive—the civilization that created the Codex Shard—for three generations. My grandfather helped your father hide the research, and he paid for it with his reputation."

Roan filed that away. "And now?"

"Now my family is compromised. Greel has agents inside Ashford—my uncle, two cousins, half the household staff." Cyrus's voice hardened. "I've been watching them pick apart my grandfather's legacy piece by piece. When I saw the petition against you, I knew they'd found a way to accelerate their hunt through the Tribunal system. If they get your Shard, they'll have a working key to decode the original archive."

"Key?" Roan caught the word. "The Shard isn't the prize. It's a tool to find something else."

Cyrus opened a desk drawer and produced a folder—worn at the edges, handled often. He slid it across the desk. "My grandfather left this. It's a fragment of the original Inversion research—notes on the structure of the Codex system. There were seven shards originally, not one. The civilization that built them scattered them before the Founding Seven destroyed everything."

Roan took the folder but didn't open it. "Why are you giving this to me? You could have burned it. Stayed neutral."

"Because I want to burn Greel's influence out of my house." Cyrus met his eyes. "You're the only one with a working Shard. You're the only one who can find the others before Greel does. And when you do, you'll need allies inside the old families who don't answer to the Northern Crown."

The ledger aligned with cold precision. Cyrus wasn't offering charity—he was offering a trade. Knowledge for a future favor. Access for leverage.

"What do you want?" Roan asked.

"Proof that my uncle is on Greel's payroll. The documents are in the archive vault beneath the Tribunal building, but I can't get to them without triggering a purge." Cyrus's jaw tightened. "You're already a target. If you're caught breaking into the vault, it looks like you're trying to suppress evidence. Greel won't connect it to me."

"And if I succeed?"

"Then I have the leverage to clean my house. And you have a friend on the inside of a family that's been watching Greel's movements for forty years." Cyrus extended his hand. "Do we have a deal?"

Roan calculated. The risk was high—breaking into the Tribunal vault was a capital offense if he was caught. But the reward was information flow from a source embedded in the old aristocracy, and a direct line to the Greel family's internal operations.

He took Cyrus's hand. "We have a deal."

"Good." Cyrus released his grip. "The vault access is through the Tribunal's basement maintenance corridor—the same one the cleaning staff uses. There's a six-minute window at midnight when the guards rotate. I'll leave the corridor door unlocked."

"And the attack on your study? The guards?"

Cyrus's expression darkened. "That's the price of the meeting. By morning, my uncle will know I spoke to you privately. I'll need to be seen as hostile to you to maintain cover. When the guards come—and they will come—you need to make it look like I tried to stop you."

Roan nodded. "Understood."

He tucked the folder into his coat. Brendt tensed by the door, ready to move.

Cyrus spoke one last time, his voice low. "The seven shards aren't power sources. They're keys. And when all seven are united—"

The door exploded inward.

Cyrus's face shifted into a mask of fury. "Guards! They're here!"

Roan caught Brendt's arm and dove sideways. The guards surged into the room—four of them, exactly as predicted. Roan took a hit to the forearm, rolled, and came up with a chair between himself and the nearest attacker. Brendt slammed a guard into the wall.

Cyrus threw a powder that hissed and spread a choking cloud. "Get out! I'll hold them!"

Roan didn't hesitate. He crashed through the window, glass exploding around him. The garden path was three stories below. He hit, rolled, and came up running, Brendt half a step behind.

They cleared the estate's outer wall by the time the alarm bells started ringing.

---

The Veyrn Sanctum was quiet when they arrived, the old stone walls absorbing sound like a held breath. The Warden sat on the bench where Aldric had once written his notes, a single lantern casting shadows across his scarred face.

"I heard about the Ashford residence," Drogan said. "You're getting a reputation."

"Desperate times." Roan dropped into the chair across from him. He pulled out the folder Cyrus had given him and laid it on the table. "Cyrus showed me this. Fragment of the original Inversion research. Seven Codex Shards. Keys to rewrite Vitalis itself."

Drogan's expression didn't change, but his shoulders tightened. "The Ashford archive. Cyrus's grandfather was a friend of your father's. They shared notes."

"Shared notes that House Greel has been hunting for forty years." Roan met the old man's eyes. "You knew."

"I knew fragments." Drogan's voice was heavy. "Your father left information with me, but he made me promise not to release it until you'd found the first major thread on your own. He said the knowledge would be worthless if you weren't ready to follow it."

"And I've found the thread. So what else is there?"

Drogan was silent for a long moment. Then he reached into his coat and produced a key—iron, unremarkable, heavy in the lantern light.

"The Sanctum has a chamber your father built. Beneath the seventh stone from the left. Warded, hidden, protected by things that aren't easily broken." He held out the key. "What he left inside isn't just notes and theories. It's the location of three more shards, including the one House Greel is hunting. And it contains instructions for what happens when they all come together."

Roan took the key. It was cold in his palm, heavier than it looked. "Why now? Why not before?"

"Because before, you were surviving. Now you're hunting." Drogan's voice was low. "The difference matters."

Roan turned the key over, feeling the weight of it. Three days until the Tribunal. One chamber beneath the Sanctum. Three shard coordinates. A vault to break into, a deal with Cyrus Ashford, and a war with House Greel that had just escalated from academic politics to something far deeper.

Too many variables were still missing. But incomplete math was still math.

He tucked the key into his coat, next to the folder.

"I'll need a plan—the kind that accounts for everything Greel can throw at us."

Drogan nodded. "Then we start now."
