Chapter 35: Combat Trial

The charm rested in Roan Vance’s palm, its weight a familiar counterbalance against his ribs. He had taken it off the nail exactly once since the dormitory assignment—the morning of orientation—and had spent three hours mapping the inner surface with a magnifying loupe borrowed from the supply cart on the third floor. The lotus embossing was stamped, not etched. The blank side was hollow by a margin of approximately three millimeters.

He set it on the desk beside the data slate Selene had left an hour ago. The slate displayed the Combat Fundamentals exam rubric, annotated with red markings around the Vitalis variance metric. Variance. Not peak output. The degree of fluctuation between stable-state and activated-state Vitalis. His Inversion cycle didn’t produce stable curves. It produced spikes, troughs, and reversals that matched nothing in the Academy’s diagnostic database.

*A perfect flag.*

Selene had pulled the engineering specs from the diagnostic suite upgrade. Calibrated to catch exactly the Inversion signature. His father’s system. His problem.

Roan picked up the worn business card from beside the slate. *Ines Carrow, Supply Services, East Wing, Room 114.* The six-digit requisition code was familiar. Selene had given him the card six weeks ago, during their second formal contact. He’d used it twice since then for catalyst requisitions that the standard channels couldn’t fulfill without generating oversight flags. The system worked because Ines Carrow didn’t ask questions, and Roan Vance didn’t leave traces.

He typed the code into the Spire’s internal supply portal. The catalog loaded.

*Ironbile Resin. Classification: Tier 2. Side effect profile: temporary hepatic dysfunction with associated Vitalis suppression. Suppression duration: 4–6 hours post-consumption. Stock: 14 units. Requisition requires: faculty authorization code or existing emergency standing.*

He had the standing. Brendt’s partnership had generated a shadow requisition account four months ago, seeded with a faculty authorization code that Selene had flagged as “clean for low-volume purchases.” This was low volume. One unit. The risk was worth the cost.

Temporary Vitalis suppression. A catalyst whose side effect was the exact opposite of everything his Inversion cycle was designed to harvest—except that the suppression would hold long enough to flatten his variance curve on the exam. The Inversion would detect the side effect and attempt to convert it, but the conversion lag would keep his output stable during the four-minute assessment window. His peak would be lower. His variance would be unremarkable.

He stared at the screen. The logic was clean. The execution window was forty-four hours.

He placed the order. *Ironbile Resin. Quantity: 1. Priority: Emergency.*

The system accepted the requisition. A pickup window appeared: East Wing, Room 114, within six hours.

Roan closed the portal. The charm sat beside the slate, silent and patient. Selene’s words from her visit were still present in the room’s atmosphere, compressed into the space between the desk and the doorframe.

*“There’s a second layer.”*

*“What’s in it?”*

*“The reason your father died. The Vellmark Initiative wasn’t a project. It was a purge.”*

He had known the Codex Shard was incomplete. The first layer had given him activation, the system, the initial message. But the weight of the housing unit was wrong for a single layer. He’d measured the cavity. There was space for a secondary insert beneath the primary plate.

He picked up the charm and held it flat in both palms. The blank side faced him. He pressed his thumb against the center of the metal, the exact point where the weight distribution shifted. Nothing. He applied pressure to the edge, rotating counterclockwise.

The housing unit emitted a faint click. The blank surface split along a seam he hadn’t seen, revealing a thin compartment containing a folded sheet of translucent polymer. The material was archival grade, the kind used for long-term data storage in Rift-adjacent environments. The only symbol on the visible fold was a hand-drawn circle bisected by a vertical line, with a notation in Aldric Vance’s handwriting:

*Vellmark Survey 9. The circle is not the whole.*

Roan read the line three times. The circle is not the whole. He didn’t unfold the polymer sheet. He didn’t have the time to decode it before the exam, and his odds of interpreting a fragment of his father’s research without context were catastrophically low. He refolded the compartment, clicked the seam back into place, and placed the charm in his left pocket.

Forty-four hours. One variance test. One Ironbile pickup. One polymer sheet to decrypt after the exam window closed.

He unlocked the reinforced storage drawer beside the bed. Seven standardized catalyst vials from the Wraithmilk batch, triple-filtered. Two requisition packets from the Brendt partnership, pending distribution. One sealed package from Old Pell, delivered the previous week, containing a data slate with annotated evacuation routes through the Academy’s maintenance sub-levels. And the photograph. Mira and Lyra, taken the previous spring.

He folded the photograph and placed it in his right pocket. He didn’t look at it. He knew the weight of it by heart.

He stood, crossed to the door, and paused with his hand on the knob.

The arithmetic was complete. The variables were no longer incomplete.

The charm was against his ribs. The polymer sheet was inside the charm. The Ironbile was waiting in East Wing. And the people who were watching the Vance name had already activated their first diagnostic sweep.

Roan Vance opened the door and walked out into the corridor.
