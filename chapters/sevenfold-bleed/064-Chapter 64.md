Chapter 64: Private Study

The library annex reeked of dust and old binding glue. Roan set his tea on the table—proper ceramic, not the student cafeteria's chipped porcelain—and cracked open the ledger.

Three columns. Item, quantity, vendor. Simple math. Except the tally had stopped adding up three weeks ago, and Selene Ardent was asking questions in the morning that required answers he didn't have.

The Hollow Marrow Elixir purchase order showed forty-seven units pulled from Black Spire's regional warehouse. The consumption manifest, stamped and signed by the Infirmary Dispensary, showed nineteen doses administered to students with documented injuries. The discrepancy: twenty-eight vials. Gone. Vanished between warehouse shelf and patient record.

Roan traced his finger down the ledger. The numbers were clean, no obvious falsification, no altered figures. Whoever had been skimming knew accounting well enough to leave trails that looked legitimate until you started cross-referencing against the dispensary's injury log.

He pulled the log. Sorted by date. Crossed each administration against the student roster.

The first missing dose landed on a Thursday. He noted it.

The second missing dose: another Thursday.

The third: Tuesday. A coincidence.

The fourth: Thursday again.

He isolated the Thursdays. Scanned back six months. Twelve discrepancies. Nine fell on a specific weekday pattern. The arithmetic resolved cleanly. Someone had a fixed day for their operation, a day when the right shift was on the counter.

He studied the dispensing records more closely. The patient names were real. The injuries were documented. But someone had been siphoning the authentic doses during the dispensing step, switching the vials before they left the counter. The ledger recorded distribution. The patient logged treatment. The physical evidence had been swapped.

Twenty-eight doses. A consistent extraction rate. Long enough to avoid triggering a financial audit.

The equation remained honest. The ledger just didn't say who was doing it.

Roan checked the requisition seals. Every signature matched protocol. The warehouse log was stamped with the correct dates. The only break in the pattern was the absence of physical product. This wasn't casual theft. It was metered. The thief understood the margin of error for a facility this size. Twenty-eight doses in six months meant someone was taking exactly what they could hide. Exactly enough to avoid a reflexive audit.

Whoever designed this knew the threshold. They were operating at the edge of what the system could absorb before it flagged.

Roan closed the ledger. He knew the feeling. He'd been operating at the edge of the system's tolerance his whole life.

---

The second ledger was harder.

This one belonged to the Combat Forms registry, not the public log, but the shadow record that Wardens kept for tracking student technique mastery. He'd sweet-talked Brendt into borrowing it for the weekend, citing research purposes for his upcoming examination prep.

The Combat Forms registry tracked more than technique acquisition. It tracked progression speed, breakthrough patterns, and something the administration called anomalous development curves. Students whose Vitalis readings spiked faster than their training hours justified got flagged. Students whose technique acquisition outpaced their physical maturation got investigated.

Roan flipped to his own file. The tally matched what he expected: Tempered-tier, slightly above average progression, nothing that would trigger a deep audit. Standard documentation for a standard student.

He flipped to the Greel envoy's file.

The progression data was clean on the surface. Tempered-tier breakthrough at sixteen, standard timeline for a Greel scion with family resources and personal motivation. Technique acquisition records showed solid mastery of the expected curriculum, nothing exceptional, nothing concerning.

But the anomaly flags.

Someone had annotated the envoy's file with three separate flags over the past year. Each flag corresponded to a sudden Vitalis spike that didn't match any recorded training session or breakthrough event. The annotations noted the spikes, questioned their source, and then nothing. No proper investigation had been initiated. But someone had already prepared the resolution. The closure records were there, timestamps aligned weeks before the flags had allegedly been entered. The file looked reviewed. It looked closed. It was a paper coffin for an inquiry that never happened.

Roan studied the dates. The first spike: fourteen months ago, shortly after the envoy's younger sister Iris had enrolled at Black Spire. The second: nine months ago, when House Greel's annual tribute shipment had arrived early. The third: six weeks ago, right after the Spire had received notice that House Greel's patriarch would be visiting for the Founder's Day ceremonies.

Coincidence. Maybe. But three unexplained spikes in fourteen months, all coinciding with events that might require a Greel heir to perform above expectations? That was preparation. Or it was a frame. Someone wanted the file to tell a story before the event even happened.

He looked at the handwriting. The first flag was dark, deliberate, pressed hard into the paper. The second was similar, but the angle was slightly off. The third was noticeably different—lighter, faster, a hand that had practiced the letterforms until they emerged without thought.

Someone was training to replace the reviewer. The frame wasn't just a frame. It was a qualification program.

Roan set down the file. The tea had gone cold.

---

He found Brendt in the training yard, running Theon Mercer through a partnered drill that required both students to maintain synchronized breathing patterns while executing complementary strikes. Theon kept breaking rhythm at the transition point between defensive form and counterattack.

"Your hips are rotating too early," Brendt said. "You're committing to the counter before your partner has finished the setup. Wait for the signal."

"The signal is when she shifts her weight—"

"The signal is when she shifts her weight *and* her breathing changes. You're reading the body, not the intent." Brendt caught Roan's eye over Theon's shoulder. A small nod. *Give me a minute.*

Roan leaned against the equipment rack and waited. Brendt finished the drill correction in three more attempts, dismissed Theon to solo practice, and crossed the yard.

"You look like someone owes you money," Brendt said.

"Someone's been siphoning Hollow Marrow Elixir through a Thursday swap scheme for six months." Roan handed him the Infirmary ledger. "Twenty-eight doses missing."

Brendt took the ledger. Didn't open it. "Thursday. That's the Gerrish shift. The senior tech was 23rd Garrison. Northern skirmishes. Honorable discharge. He knows how to run a supply bleed without tripping an audit."

Roan absorbed the information. "A military background changes the profile. They're not just thieves—they're systematic."

"Systematic is generous. That's a protocol." Brendt's expression tightened as Roan laid out the Greel file findings. The three flags. The identical closure dates. The unexplained Vitalis spikes.

"These flags aren't a cover story for a power boost," Brendt said quietly. "This is Second Pattern denial tradecraft. You seed false contradictions in the record so the investigation suffocates in its own paperwork. I've seen it used in border intelligence. If the spikes are planted, the envoy isn't the one benefiting from whatever this is. He's the target of the frame. Someone wants the file to be evidence that he *was* cheating."

"Second Pattern is expensive," Brendt continued. "It's not a student vendetta. It's an intelligence play. Someone spent real money buying this methodology."

"Or they inherited it. Gerrish was 23rd Garrison."

"Gerrish is a tech. This file work has an officer's layout."

Roan absorbed the correction. "So the theft and the frame share a brain, but not necessarily a hand."

"The hand is whoever is consolidating the elixirs. The brain is whoever built the stage."

"Founder's Day is the stage."

Brendt's jaw tightened. "If the suppression hits during the demonstration, the frame catches the Greel envoy. The real operation becomes a power statement. 'We can turn off the Spire.' The price of silence goes up."

"Unless the point isn't the price," Roan said. "Unless the point is to make a rival pay."

"The envoy's father is Vorath Greel. If the frame sticks, it damages House Greel."

"Who gains?"

Brendt was quiet. "That's the question you should be asking the paper trail. Not me."

The wind carried the smell of the forge district across the yard. "The elixir theft provides the mechanism. The Greel file provides the cover story. The Founder's Day demonstration is the stage."

"Or the weapon." Brendt's gaze was hard. "Founder's Day is six weeks out. The entire faculty, the visiting delegations, the full-student Vitalis demonstration—everyone's attention will be on the spectacle. That's a perfect blind spot for a strike."

"It bypasses suspicion entirely," Roan said. "If the suppression hits during the demonstration, the investigation points at the envoy's planted file. The real operation stays invisible."

"Whoever's running this knows the system from the inside. They're not just stealing resources—they're constructing a reality."

"Then we need to know what reality they're building towards."

"You need to flag the Infirmary audit for the Warden. Off the record," Brendt said. "If you do it, you look like a Greel patsy. If I submit a standard facility complaint about supply chain integrity, it buys you a public record without painting a target on your back."

Roan considered the angle. "You'd do that?"

"I'm already in it. You handed me the ledger. If the roof comes down, they'll look at who had the books last." Brendt clapped him on the shoulder. "Don't get dead before Founder's Day. I'm not writing the note to your mother."

---

Dinner service had ended by the time Roan reached the refectory. The kitchen staff was cleaning up, and the serving counters had been cleared and covered. He grabbed a wrapped bundle from his locker, leftover bread and preserved fruit he'd been saving for late study sessions, and headed for the library annex.

The desk was occupied when he arrived.

Selene Ardent sat in his chair, turning the Hollow Marrow Elixir ledger in her hands. Her posture was relaxed, her expression pleasant, but her eyes had the quality of a trap that had been waiting patiently for its prey to arrive.

"You're a hard student to find," she said. "When you're not in class, not in the training yard, and not in your dormitory."

Roan set his bread on the table. Took the chair across from her instead of beside her. Kept his hands visible on the surface.

"The library annex is technically public space," he said. "Anyone can reserve the desk."

"Anyone can. Most people don't reserve the same desk every evening for three months straight while working through acquisition records that are two levels above their access clearance." Selene set the ledger down. "You're good. The cross-referencing methodology is textbook, but the execution is faster than most third-year administrators. You've either been trained by someone with practical audit experience, or you picked it up yourself from observation."

"Is there a question in there?"

"The question is whether you're investigating the elixir discrepancy because you're worried about resource waste, or because you're building a case." Selene leaned back. "The answer matters, because I'm trying to decide whether to help you or report you."

The room went quiet. "You have questions," Roan finally said. "I have answers. But the exchange rate matters."

"The exchange rate being—"

"You tell me why you're sitting in my chair instead of sitting on the other side of the table asking these questions through proper channels." He kept his voice even. "The Infirmary ledger isn't restricted material, but it's not casual reading either. You knew what I was working on because you were watching me work on it. Which means you either already suspected the discrepancy, or someone told you to watch for it."

Selene's expression didn't change, but something shifted behind her eyes. Assessment, recalculation, adjustment.

"House Carrow has been tracking regional resource flows for eighteen months," she said. "Not officially. Unofficially, as part of a private arrangement between my family and the Sentinel Bureau. The arrangement allows us to monitor inventory discrepancies at educational facilities in exchange for early warning on potential black market activity."

"The Sentinel Bureau is using House Carrow as a deniable auditing proxy."

"There's a difference between an audit and a surveillance mandate." Selene's mouth curved slightly. "We've identified seventeen discrepancies across six facilities in the past year. Most of them are small-scale. But the Black Spire pattern changed six weeks ago. The volume started increasing."

"Six weeks ago." Roan recalculated. "Right after the Founder's Day notice went out."

"The timing is interesting. The volume increase is more interesting." Selene slid a folded paper across the table. "Seventeen vials in the past six weeks. The Thursday pattern is still intact, but the extraction method changed. New demand on the buyer's end."

Roan unfolded the paper. A handwritten list, dates, quantities, pricing estimates based on current black market rates. The columns were clean, organized, exactly what he'd expect from a professional surveillance operation.

"You've been tracking this for six weeks," he said. "Why hasn't anyone shut it down?"

"Because we're not sure what we're shutting down." Selene folded her hands. "The elixirs are being siphoned and sold to an intermediary. The intermediary is consolidating the doses. We know there's a buyer on the other end. We don't know who, and we don't know what they're planning."

"Twenty-eight doses isn't enough to do much with. Unless—"

"Unless they're not planning to use it on students." Selene leaned forward. "Hollow Marrow Elixir has one property that's useful for non-medical applications. In high concentrations, it can be processed."

She stopped. Deliberately.

Roan waited. She didn't continue.

"Processed into what?"

"I'm a political asset, Mr. Vance, not a chemist. I don't have the license to speculate on the finished product."

He read the evasion. She knew exactly what it became. She just wasn't going to say it aloud in an unsecured room. The silence was her offering. She was giving him the space to arrive at the conclusion himself.

"Founder's Day," he said. "The full-student demonstration. Several hundred Vitalis signatures in one place."

"I couldn't possibly comment on the operational consequences of that correlation." Selene's smile was thin.

"Then why give me the list?"

"Because you're already in the water, and I'm curious to see if you can swim to shore or if you'll attract the sharks. If you live, you might be useful. If you die, you were never my problem."

"Comforting."

"I'm not a comforter. I'm a bet-hedger." She stood. "The handwriting on the Greel file changed three times. That means the forger was training. It means the frame was prepared in advance. A timeline that long usually means the buyer isn't local. They're waiting for the product to arrive before they move on the stage."

"Waiting for what?"

"A catalyst. A delivery window. A ship from the Northern Crown." She let the name hang in the air. "Good luck, Mr. Vance. You're going to need it."

She paused at the door. "The 23rd Garrison protocols vanished from the official archives six years ago, but they survived in private collections. If you want to understand the operating rhythm, look at who wrote the handbooks before the purge. The same people who are now administration here."

She left. The door clicked shut.

---

Roan sat in the hollow silence of the annex. Twenty-eight doses. A suppressant field. A frame. Northern trade. The web was wider than he thought.

He wasn't the patsy for a campus scandal. He was an inconvenience to a supply chain that connected Black Spire to the Greel holdings. If the buyer was waiting for a Northern Crown shipment, the logistics window was tight. The sea routes closed in the winter. The last ship from the North anchors in the harbor within the month.

He had weeks, not months.

He pulled the Infirmary roster. Gerrish's shift was tomorrow. Roan didn't need to catch the thief. He needed to follow the delivery. He needed to know where the dead drops terminated.

The military signature worried him more than the frame. It meant discipline. It meant a chain of command. It was the kind of setup that wouldn't hesitate to silence a student investigator's family. He'd have to tighten Mira's access. He'd have to write a careful letter to Lyra. The game was no longer just about power. It was about survival.

Selene's data was clean, but her motives were opaque. Brendt's support was tactical. He was the only variable he could fully control.

The best way to survive a frame was to build a better frame. He needed a target for the conspiracy. A decoy.

He smiled drily. *No one expects the patsy to build a mirror.*

He checked the Founder's Day schedule. The student demonstration was slated for the third hour. Right after the gifts of state.

Someone wanted to poison the gift exchange.

Roan stood up. The chair scraped against the stone floor. He had six weeks to invert their stage into a trap of his own. The arithmetic was simple. The execution was a razor.

He knew exactly how much time he had.

He grabbed his coat and headed for the forge district. If he was going to track a military extraction, he needed gear that didn't look like it belonged to a student. And he needed a reason to be on the wrong side of the stage when the curtain rose.

Six weeks.

He counted them, one by one, in the rhythm of his footsteps on the cold cobblestones.
