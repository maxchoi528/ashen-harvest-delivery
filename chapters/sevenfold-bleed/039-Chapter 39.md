Chapter 39: Drogan Hale

The corridor outside the Armory Annex ran thirty meters before turning twice and opening into the Junior Hunters' Hall common area. Roan counted the steps as he walked. Twenty-three. His left arm ached where the training dummy had caught him three hours ago, the bruise already yellowing at the edges.

Beneath his shirt, pressed against his sternum, the black iron anchor hummed at a frequency he had learned to recognize as *idle*. Not hungry. Not processing. Just there, like a second heartbeat he couldn't silence.

He adjusted the strap of his bag. Inside: three vials of Hollow Marrow Elixir he'd purchased at forty percent below market rate from a graduating student who needed cash more than he needed to think. The man had practically thrown them at Roan, desperate to clear his locker before departure.

*Three vials. At current Inversion efficiency, that's approximately six hundred Vitalis if I can absorb the marrow degradation without triggering the paralysis threshold.*

He checked it a second time and found the same answer. He always did.

The common area opened ahead of him. Evening light slanted through the high windows, casting long rectangles of amber across the stone floor. A cluster of Tempered-year students occupied the central table, their voices carrying the cadence of people who believed they were funnier than they were.

Roan scanned the room as he entered. Automatic. Necessary.

Brendt sat in his usual corner, back to the wall, tray already set with the week's ration of protein and grain. He looked up when Roan approached, and something in his expression shifted, a micro-adjustment of the eyebrows that Roan had learned to read as *we need to talk*.

Not urgent. Not panicked. Just *talk*.

Roan took the seat across from him. Set his bag beneath the table where he wouldn't kick it.

"You're late," Brendt said.

"Six minutes."

"Thirty-seven."

*Closer. The wall clock runs fast.*

"Your definition of 'late' needs calibration."

"My definition of 'late' accounts for the Warden calling your name three times during third bell." Brendt picked up his fork. "Drogan wanted to see you. Tomorrow morning. His office."

The information landed in Roan's mind like a stone into water. He let it sink, then reached for the implications beneath.

Drogan didn't summon students to his office. The Warden held his meetings in the training yards, the armory, the sparring circles, anywhere with enough space to demonstrate what he meant. Calling someone to his private office meant documentation. Meant records. Meant something had triggered a formal review.

"The tournament bracket," Roan said.

"Released an hour ago." Brendt pushed a bread roll across the table without looking at it. "You're seeded sixteenth. Your first match is against Theon Mercer."

*Mercer.* Roan pulled the name from the tournament registry he'd memorized two weeks ago. Third-year. Tempered rank, approximately eight hundred Vitalis. Known for aggressive opening sequences and a preference for overwhelming force over tactical adaptation.

Not a problem. The arithmetic was straightforward.

"The bracket also put Cyrus Ashford in your quarter," Brendt added. "If you beat Mercer."

*Ah.*

"There it is," Brendt said, watching Roan's face. "The thing you've been pretending not to calculate since the brackets dropped."

"I'm not pretending anything." Roan picked up the bread roll. Tore it in half. "Cyrus Ashford is a significant tactical challenge. He's been Tempered for eighteen months. His Vitalis readings hover around twelve hundred, with peaks that suggest he's close to Hardened breakthrough."

"You've been watching him."

"I've been watching everyone in my bracket." The bread was dry. He chewed anyway. "Ashford included. His weakness is patience. He commits too early in sequence, relies on his family's technique library to cover gaps that should be addressed through fundamentals. Against an opponent who can survive his initial assault, his win rate drops by approximately thirty percent."

"By the time you reach him, you'll have already fought Mercer. Mercer's not weak."

"No. But Mercer is predictable." Roan swallowed the bread. "His signature sequence, the Iron Tide Cascade, requires a three-second charge. Anyone who's seen him fight knows to use that window. He hasn't adapted because he's never needed to—the student tournaments attract opponents who panic under pressure rather than exploit openings."

Brendt set down his fork. "You're treating this like a puzzle."

"It's a puzzle."

"It's also your first public match in three months. People have been asking questions."

*Questions.* Roan felt the pressure behind his eyes, the one that meant someone had noticed something they shouldn't have. "What kind of questions."

"The kind where they say things like 'Vance has been suspiciously quiet since the Incident' and 'I heard he's been spending all his time in the library, trying to figure out how to compensate for the fact that his Vitalis readings are barely above Initiate.'"

The words landed softly, but their weight was considerable. Brendt wasn't warning him. Brendt was *reporting*. Someone had been talking. Someone had been watching Roan close enough to notice the discrepancy between his public Vitalis reading and his actual output.

The Inversion worked best in shadows. Every time he used it publicly, every time his performance exceeded expectations, the margin between his real capacity and his displayed capacity narrowed. Eventually, someone would notice the pattern. Eventually, someone would run the columns.

Math didn't lie. But math also didn't hide.

"The Incident," Roan repeated. He kept his voice neutral. "Is that what they're calling it?"

"That's what *I'm* calling it. Officially, there's no incident. Unofficially, half the school knows something happened in the eastern training yard two months ago. Something that left a Forged-year student in the medical wing for a week with injuries that didn't match his own technique profile."

*Garrick.* The name surfaced like a knife in water. The collarbone had been bad arithmetic—but the Inversion had done what it always did. Roan hadn't planned the spike. The medical wing had called it luck. He'd called it a leak he couldn't plug fast enough.

But the incident remained. And people had been watching.

"You need to win your first matches cleanly," Brendt said. "Without anything that makes people ask questions. Mercer is safe. He's standard curriculum. Beat him with standard techniques and people will stop looking."

"And if I can't?"

Brendt's expression didn't change. "Then you explain to Drogan tomorrow why you're performing at Tempered peak when your last three official readings put you at Initiate threshold. And you hope he's more interested in your explanation than in reporting the discrepancy to the Conclave."

The temperature in the room did not change. Roan's calculation did. He finished chewing the bread. Swallowed.

"I'll fight Mercer the way everyone expects me to fight."

"Good."

"That means it will take longer. Mercer will interpret caution as fear. He'll push harder. I'll need to let him believe he's winning for at least ninety seconds before I can safely close the distance."

"Letting someone believe they're winning isn't the same as letting them win."

"No. But it has the same effect on an audience." Roan pushed the remaining half of his bread across the table. "People remember the comeback. They forget the patient part."

Brendt picked up the bread. Bit into it. Chewed thoughtfully.

"You know," he said, around a mouthful, "most people in your position would just be worried about winning."

"Most people in my position don't have a system that rewards controlled damage."

*There it was. The thing they didn't talk about directly, the fact that Brendt had known, had *seen*—something impossible in the training yard two months ago. He'd arrived thirty seconds after Garrick hit the ground, long enough to see Roan standing over the fallen student, long enough to notice the way Roan's Vitalis reading had spiked and then settled back to baseline like a wave retreating from shore.*

*He'd never asked the question directly. Neither had Roan offered an explanation. That was the nature of their arrangement: trust without comprehension, alliance without complete information.*

*It worked. For now.*

"Mercer's Iron Tide Cascade," Brendt said, shifting topics with the ease of someone who'd learned that some doors opened better from the side. "What are you planning for the charge window?"

"The standard counter is a lateral dodge followed by a hamstring strike. But that requires speed I can't afford to demonstrate without raising questions."

"So?"

"So I'm going to take the hit."

Brendt stopped chewing. Swallowed slowly. "You're going to let him charge a three-second full-power technique."

"I'm going to let him *believe* he's going to hit me. There's a difference." Roan leaned back in his chair. "The Cascade is a linear attack. It requires a specific stance, a specific breath pattern, a specific angle of entry. If I position myself at thirty-seven degrees off his center line, the strike will clip my shoulder instead of my chest. The impact will look catastrophic. The damage will be minimal."

"And then?"
