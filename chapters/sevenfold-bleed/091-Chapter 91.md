Chapter 91: The Ranking House

The morning after the failed extraction at the warehouse district, Greycross woke under a sky the color of old dishwater.

Roan stood at the window of his rented room in the Ironveil Flats, watching a Sentinel patrol pass three buildings down. Two officers. One scanning a handheld device while the other kept his hand near his sidearm. Standard harassment routine, they were looking for the commotion reported in the warehouse sector the night before. The Bureau was already casting a net.

The balance hadn't changed overnight. Brendt was still in the medical wing, three floors below his current position. The extraction team was scattered, half to the safe house in the eastern quarter, the rest laying low per Roan's terse message sent at oh-three-hundred. The Hollow Marrow Elixir was still somewhere inside House Greel's processing facility on the north side of the city. And Selene Ardent was still an unknown variable in an equation that already had too many of those.

He turned from the window. Lyra sat on the edge of the narrow bed, her hands folded in her lap with a stillness that looked practiced rather than natural. She'd been quiet the whole walk back from the warehouse. Quieter than him, which was a barometer he didn't like using.

"You're thinking about going back for Drogan," she said. Not a question.

"I'm thinking about the medical wing's visiting hours." He pulled his jacket off the chair. "They open at nine."

"Drogan isn't in the medical wing."

"He's three floors up from it. Which makes visiting hours a secondary concern."

Lyra's jaw tightened, the gesture that meant she wanted to argue and had already run the calculations on how that argument would go and discarded it. She was getting better at that. It still looked uncomfortable on her face.

"The Sentinel patrols increased overnight," she said instead. "Three times normal density on the north corridor. Whatever happened at the warehouse set something off."

"Good." He shrugged into the jacket. "Means they're looking south. Opens the north routes."

"Roan."

"One minute."

"The Greel processing facility is a Tier 3 site. You walked in once on a student credentials pass that was already halfway through its audit review. You aren't walking in again."

"Tier 3 sites have supply rotations. Supply trucks don't get searched. Bureau protocol, because searching commercial traffic costs time and political capital. Three trucks a week, scheduled for Wednesday mornings. Today's Wednesday."

"You knew that before we left the warehouse."

"I gathered data. The warehouse gave me one useful piece of information: Garrick talks when he's scared. He mentioned a processing facility supply run during the verbal diarrhea phase. Cross-referenced with publicly available transport manifests, yes, those exist, they just cost Spire Credits to access, and you get a three-hour window starting at six AM where the facility's northern intake gate operates on a skeleton crew."

Lyra stared at him. "You planned the extraction to fail."

He stopped with his hand on the door handle. "No. I planned for the possibility that it would fail. There's a difference between accepting risk and engineering failure. I gathered data because I knew we might lose. I didn't make it happen. And I'm going back for Drogan right now. Does that settle your theory?"

"You still sent us in without a real extraction plan."

"I sent you in with enough to extract if the situation held. It didn't. That's war, not betrayal." He turned the handle. "I need you to watch the perimeter. Text Brendt's burner if anything changes with the Sentinel movement patterns. And don't get caught, because if you get caught before I do, it compromises my window, and if you get caught after I do, it triggers a facility-wide lockdown and I'm stuck inside a Tier 3 security grid with no exit strategy."

She held his gaze. "Understood."

"I need you to mean it."

"I'm a Vance. I always mean it."

- break -

Pell and Sons Chemical occupied a squat concrete building on the industrial fringe, a structure that existed purely to look unimportant. Which was the entire point, Roan supposed. Unimportant-looking buildings attracted fewer questions. He approached from the loading bay side, carrying a catalyst crate branded with the company logo, acquired from a Pell delivery van that had been parked and unlocked behind the Greeley district cafeteria twenty minutes prior. Small crimes feeding larger ones. The crate contained nothing of value, just packing material weighted to feel like it held bottles.

The driver of the delivery van hadn't noticed the crate's absence. The driver of the delivery van had been asleep with the window cracked, listening to something on a personal player that Roan had identified as a syndicated drama about municipal court reform. The man would notice eventually. Not before nine.

The north intake gate was exactly where the manifest had indicated. Two guards. One checkpoint booth with the window rolled down. No scanning equipment visible past the gate line, the facility saved its heavy screening for the interior. The supply truck was a twelve-ton freight hauler already halfway through the gate when Roan arrived, and he fell into step behind it with the crate held chest-high, posture slack, face arranged into the specific vacancy of a day laborer who had done this run a hundred times and would do it a hundred more.

The checkpoint guard glanced at the truck, then at the manifest tablet the truck driver was holding up. Didn't glance at Roan. Didn't glance at the guy in the Spire jacket walking twenty meters behind the truck toward the interior loading dock. Why would he? The manifest was clean, the truck was scheduled, and nobody who worked at a Tier 3 processing facility had any particular interest in the guy carrying the crate. That was the whole value of being unremarkable.

The interior was a maze of conveyor belts and sorting tables. Roan mapped it in the first sixty seconds, three loading bays, one central processing corridor, a sealed stairwell on the east wall that led up. The medical wing was on the third floor. The processing floor was on the second. The enforcer he'd identified in the security manifests would be circulating on a predictable route based on the facility's standard patrol architecture.

He set the crate on a sorting table near Bay Two and moved toward the stairwell. A worker in a grease-stained coverall looked up and squinted. "Hey. You're the Pell kid, right? Tarn? Thought you got reassigned."

Roan didn't break stride. "I did. They called me back for a special run. Something about a manifest error."

The worker grunted. "Figures. Well, if you see the floor supervisor, tell him the Bay Two sorter's down."

"Noted."

The worker turned away, satisfied. Roan's pulse didn't change. The name check had been unexpected, but the lie had held. He filed the encounter for later analysis and continued toward the stairwell.

The stairwell was locked with a commercial-grade cipher, but the access code was printed on the delivery schedule he'd memorized. The lock clicked open. Second floor. The processing corridor smelled like iron and something astringent, catalyst base compounds in their raw form had a bite to them that went straight through the sinuses. He'd smelled worse during the Black Spire's introductory alchemy modules. He'd chosen not to inhale during those modules, which had puzzled the instructor and annoyed Theon Mercer, who had interpreted the stillness as judgment. Theon Mercer could go to hell.

The third-floor corridor was quieter. Climate-controlled. The air tasted different up here, recycled, filtered, carrying the faint ozone undertone of active security systems. He could hear the hum of the frequency-jamming grid before he saw it. A low, almost subliminal vibration that would deaden any wireless communication in a two-hundred-meter radius. Which meant his comm device was dead, Lyra's texts were unread, and Brendt's burner was a paperweight.

Which he'd already known, and planned around.

The medical wing entrance was ahead on the left. Double doors without guard. He pushed through without breaking stride and found himself in a corridor lined with examination rooms, each door numbered. He checked the room manifest posted by the elevator bank. Room 311. Drogan Hale, admitted under the name of *T. Marsh*, ward observation, third door on the right.

The door was closed. The handle was cold under his palm.

He opened it.

Drogan was sitting upright in a medical bed, his left arm in a pneumatic splint, his face the color of old wax. Two monitors beeped quietly beside him. An IV line ran into his right hand. His eyes were open, and they tracked to the doorway with the flat recognition of someone who had been expecting this.

"Took you long enough," Drogan said. His voice was a ruin.

"Supply truck window." Roan closed the door behind him. "How mobile are you?"

"I can walk if the building isn't on fire."

"Not a no. That's progress." He crossed to the bed, scanned the medical displays. Pulse elevated but stable. Vitalis readouts, the monitors were displaying the patient's Vitalis index, and Roan noted the number with a flicker of something he didn't examine. Drogan was running at about twelve hundred, down from a baseline Roan had always estimated in the mid-four-thousands. A Forged-tier combatant running at Initiate-level readings. Whatever the Greel facility had done to him, it had been thorough.

"The inhibitor collar," Drogan said, following Roan's gaze. "On the neck. It's cycling my own Vitalis back through my system at a controlled rate, more than suppressing output — it's burning it from the inside. Like a slow fever that won't break."

Roan saw it now. A band of dull grey metal around Drogan's throat, flush with the skin without visible clasps. He'd mistaken it for a medical collar at first glance. It wasn't. It was a leash.

"The collar can be removed?"

"Not by anyone in this room. The release mechanism is keyed to a biometric seal in the facility command center. Fourth floor."

"Of course it is."

"The kid they brought in here, the one they were testing the new inhibitor formula on, they took him to the processing floor an hour ago. Subject number seven. I heard them talking before they sedated me."

Roan absorbed this. "Seven test subjects."

"At minimum. I counted six before they put me in the collar. And it's not just Greel running this. I saw crests from House Aldermere and two others on the equipment inventory. Someone's spreading the investment across multiple families. This is a Ranking House project, Roan. The whole damned council has its hands in it."

The implications arranged themselves in his mind with the cold clarity of arithmetic. A Ranking House project. Multiple families investing in inhibitor weapons. A technology the Sentinel Bureau had officially classified as decommissioned, humming away in a commercial chemical plant in Greycross, thirty kilometers from the Black Spire. He wasn't just pulling on a Greel thread—he was pulling on a knot that tied half the aristocracy together.

"You're not leaving without the kid," Drogan said. Not a question. Not a judgment either.

"I'm not leaving without you."

"You might have to."

"That's a problem for the version of me that hasn't figured out the solution yet." He pulled a small toolkit from his jacket, flathead driver, tension wrench, a compressed-air canister the size of a lipstick tube. "I'm going to neutralize the collar's power supply first. That buys us maybe forty minutes before the backup cycle kicks in and the jammer reactivates. We get the release code from the command center, get the collar off you, and walk out through the supply bay the same way I came in."

Drogan studied him. "You've got a face I'm not seeing."

"Ideally." He opened the toolkit, selected the tension wrench. "Sit still."

"It was your sister who taught you the mechanical stuff? Mira?"

"Lyra. And no, that was Old Pell. The chemical supplier. He ran a repair shop out of his garage and I watched him take apart a Sentinel-issue comm unit when I was twelve. Seemed useful."

Drogan's mouth twitched. Not a smile. The ghost of one, maybe. "You were a weird kid."

"I was an observant kid. There's a difference." He positioned the wrench against the collar's lower rim, found the seam. "This is going to hurt."

"It already hurts."

"Less philosophically. More literally."

He applied pressure. The collar resisted, the metal was warm, almost body-temperature, and it pulsed faintly under his fingers. A locking mechanism rather than a simple band. He eased the tension wrench into the seam and felt for the catch. The collar was well-made. Eastron-grade engineering, the Greel family had ties to military contractors in the eastern provinces, and Eastron-grade biomechanical hardware was their specialty. The seams were flush, the tolerances were tight, and the security micro-filament running just under the surface would register any attempt to cut through.

But someone had designed this collar to be maintained, not to be impenetrable. Every system had an access point.

He found the maintenance port at the third attempt, eased the wrench in, and felt the micro-latch release with a click that was more tactile than audible. The collar's hum changed, dropped half a tone, became less insistent. The Vitalis readout on the monitor spiked upward by twenty points, then settled.

"First stage," Roan said. "The jammer's offline. You should be able to feel your output returning."

Drogan closed his eyes. The monitors registered a slow climb. Twelve-forty. Twelve-seventy. Thirteen-hundred and moving.

"Not fast," Drogan said quietly. "But it's there."

"The collar's cycling through your stored Vitalis to keep itself running. Now that the jammer's down, it'll try to pull more. You need to starve it, keep your output suppressed internally for the next thirty minutes so it doesn't have reserves to burn. When we get to the command center and shut down the collar's power feed entirely, it'll release."

"That's a lot of 'when.'"

"Three more steps. Command center. Biometric release. Supply bay." He packed the toolkit. "Tell me about the facility layout on the fourth floor."

Drogan told him. Two guards on the command center entry. Biometric seal requiring Greel family credentials, but the system had a secondary override protocol for emergency maintenance scenarios. The maintenance team used it when the grid needed recalibration. The codes rotated on a six-hour cycle, and Drogan had seen one of the maintenance technicians enter the sequence eight hours ago.

Eight hours. The codes had rotated once since then. The new codes would be the current maintenance team's knowledge, which meant they were with the team. Which meant they were in the building.

Roan checked his watch. Forty minutes until the supply truck window closed. Twenty minutes until the collar's backup cycle reactivated. The command center was two floors up and across the central atrium. And above it all, the Ranking House's shadow stretched further than he'd anticipated. This wasn't a single-family dirty project—it was a coalition. If he pulled the wrong thread, he'd bring down more than he could carry.

He pushed the thought aside. Threads were meant to be pulled.

"I'm going up," he said. "Don't move until you hear from me. If you can walk by the time I get back, we're leaving. If you can't walk, I'm carrying you, and it's going to be uncomfortable for both of us."

"You're a weird kid," Drogan repeated, but his voice was steadier now.

"I'm an adult who acts like a kid so people underestimate me. There's a difference."

He left the room. Behind him, the monitors beeped their quiet arithmetic, and Drogan's Vitalis reading continued its slow, stubborn climb. Ahead, the fourth floor waited with its secrets, its codes, and its leash on a man who had already decided he would not wear one.
