Chapter 70: Twelve Thousand Credits

The notification arrived mid-morning, a single line from the Spire accounting system scrolling across the exam booking terminal in the common hall:

*Credit Transfer: 12,074 Marks — Source: Black Spire Financial — Beneficiary: Vance, R. — Status: Cleared.*

Roan read it twice, then scanned the hall. A few students milled near the notice board. No one was watching the terminal. He saved the screen capture to his personal interface and left the common room at an even pace.

Twelve thousand credits. The number had landed exactly where he'd calculated. The full payment from Garrick's chain for the first batch of revised analgesics had arrived, routed through the Spire's medical supply vendor to keep the transaction within official channels. The money was clean, taxable, and logged against his student account.

And it was large enough to trip every profit flag the academy's vault-keepers maintained.

He had thirty-six hours before the system compiled its monthly anomaly report. By then, he needed a narrative.

---

The Vault-Keeper's office was located in the sub-ground floor of the administration tower, behind a door that opened onto a cramped waiting room with no windows. The woman behind the counter had a face that suggested she'd spent fifty years watching auditors walk past her desk.

"Master Vance." She didn't look up from her ledger. "Guardian Marn's office. Second door on the right. He's expecting you."

Roan nodded and moved through.

The office was wider than the waiting room but equally bare: a steel desk, two chairs, a filing cabinet, and a man in his late fifties with silver-streaked hair and the heavy shoulders of someone who had once been a field operative before settling into bureaucratic power. Guardian Marn was not a vault-keeper in the clerical sense. He was the head of the Spire's internal financial oversight division, a position that answered directly to the Director's office.

"Vance." Marn gestured to the chair. "Sit."

Roan sat.

Marn folded his hands on the desk. "You received a credit transfer this morning totaling twelve thousand and seventy-four marks. That exceeds the monthly threshold for unflagged student transactions by a factor of six. I'm required to ask where it came from."

"Medical supply sale," Roan said. "A batch of refined analgesics produced by a contracted manufacturer and sold through the Spire's preferred vendor list. The funds were routed through your own billing system. The transaction ID is CIV-7-4412-08."

Marn tapped on his terminal. His eyes moved as he read. "CIV prefix. Civil goods. Who manufactured the batch?"

"I did."

The silence stretched. Marn's fingers stopped moving.

"You produced a scheduled analgesic compound and sold it through Garrick's Pharmaceutical Distributors, who then invoiced the Spire medical division at a markup of forty-two percent, passed the payment to you after deducting their handling fee." Marn's voice didn't change. "You're a second-year Rift student, not a licensed alchemist."

"The compound's formulation is a variant of standard Wraithmilk tincture with a modified stabilizer. No scheduled ingredients. No controlled catalysts. It falls under personal invention exemptions clause 9-03 of the Spire's student entrepreneurship policy." Roan had memorized the text. "I filed the formula amendment with the Office of Academic Compliance sixty days ago. It was approved."

Marn sat back. The chair groaned. "You're telling me that you personally manufactured a pharmaceutical batch that the Spire's medical division deemed worth twelve thousand credits, and you filed the paperwork on time."

"Yes."

"You're not being investigated for fraud."

"That's correct."

"You're being investigated because you followed the rules and made too much money too fast."

Marn studied him for a long moment. Then he laughed. A short, dry sound that didn't reach his eyes.

"Follow the rules hard enough, they'll investigate you for that too, Vance." He tapped the terminal again. "I'm flagging your account for quarterly review. Standard procedure for high-volume student incomes. It means someone will check your filings every three months and confirm you haven't crossed into restricted commerce. You'll receive a notification each time. If you stay clean, the flag resolves itself after a year."

"Understood."

"One more thing." Marn's voice dropped half a tone. "The amount you deposited triggered a notification to the Spire's external liaison office. Not my doing. The system does that automatically for any student transfer above ten thousand marks. The report was logged this morning and forwarded to the Conclave observation desk."

Roan kept his face still. The Conclave observation desk was a title that meant little to most students. But he knew the list of families that staffed it. Greel was one of them.

"Thank you for the information."

Marn waved a hand. "Don't thank me. I'm not doing you a favor. I'm telling you that your life just got more complicated. The question is whether you knew it would before you made the transaction."

Roan didn't answer. He stood, nodded once, and left.

---

He found Brendt in the south training yard, running cooling sets after a sparring session. His friend's movements were slower than usual, the left shoulder still favoring protection after the Hollow Reach trip. When he spotted Roan approaching, he dropped his stance and grabbed a towel.

"You look like someone just told you the Academy's out of coffee."

"Worse." Roan sat on the bench and let the morning sun hit his face. "The vault-keeper just told me my account is flagged and the Conclave observation desk has my name."

Brendt's expression shifted. "The twelve thousand?"

"It triggered an automatic notification."

"That's not automatic. That's a rule someone wrote specifically to catch students doing what you're doing."

"I know." Roan watched a group of first-years drill under a shouting instructor. "The report went out this morning. It's already in someone's inbox."

"Whose?"

"Doesn't matter. Anyone on that desk who sees a Vance name next to a twelve-thousand-credit transfer is going to dig deeper." He paused. "I need to change how I move money through the Spire system."

Brendt draped the towel over his shoulder. "There's a merchant in the mid-market who runs off-ledger clearing. He takes a fifteen percent cut and asks no questions. But he's known for feeding information to the Northern trading consortiums if you give him enough volume."

"Not the Consortium."

"Then the Ironholm Exchange's night ledger. House Ardent keeps a private clearing agent there. Costs more, but they burn their records every seventy-two hours."

Roan considered it. "Ardent's too visible now. I need something that doesn't tie back to a Conclave family."

"You need a ghost money, basically. Someone with no political ties in Ironholm."

"Exactly."

Brendt was quiet for a moment. Then he said, "I might know someone. Old contact from my uncle's circle. He runs a salvage firm in the southern docks. Legitimate front, but he handles off-book exchange for Rift crews who don't want their earnings tracked. Lower cut, ten percent. But you'd need face-to-face."

"Give me the name."

"I'll send it tonight." Brendt's voice turned serious. "Roan. Twelve thousand credits from a student's personal account means people will start asking who you are. You ready for that?"

Roan looked out at the training yard. The first-years had moved on to paired drills, sparring with padded weapons under the indifferent eye of the assistant instructor. In six months, some of them would wash out. In two years, most of them would be dead or reassigned to non-combat roles. The Spire was a machine that processed ambition into something more manageable.

He was the part of the input that refused to be processed.

"It's not a question of being ready," he said. "It's a question of whether I can stay ahead long enough."

---

The walk back to his dormitory was quiet. The afternoon chime had rung, pulling students to elective lectures and lab sessions. Roan took the rear staircase, three flights up, and moved down the corridor to his door.

The lock was intact. He turned the key, stepped inside.

The room was exactly as he'd left it—bed made, footlocker closed, desk organized. But the window at the far wall was open two centimeters more than he'd left it. He always left a precise gap for air circulation. This was shifted.

He didn't move. His eyes swept the room. Everything looked correct. But the air felt wrong. A faint trace of something, cinder dust and cold iron, that didn't belong to his space.

He crossed to the desk and checked the hidden compartment behind his bed frame. Intact. Nothing missing.

But the letter he'd written to Brendt's contact—the sealed envelope he'd left on the desk, ready to send—was gone.

In its place was a single sheet of paper, blank except for a line of script in a tight, unfamiliar hand:

*Your analgesic formula has interesting properties. We should discuss them.*

No signature. No mark.

Roan read it twice, then read it a third time, committing the handwriting to memory. He didn't know who had left it. But they'd known exactly where to leave it, and they'd known about the formula.

He turned the paper over. Nothing.

Outside, the corridor remained empty. The window gap was still two centimeters open.

He set the paper down carefully, locked the door, and began recalculating the variables. The vault-keeper's warning had been immediate. The Conclave observation desk had been notified. And now someone had sent a message that meant they were close enough to touch.

The arithmetic resolved cleanly. The twelve thousand credits had bought him more than money.

It had bought him attention.

He pulled out a fresh sheet and began writing a new letter, this time to the salvage firm contact. And this time, he'd find a more secure way to send it.
