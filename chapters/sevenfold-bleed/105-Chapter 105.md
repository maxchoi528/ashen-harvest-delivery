Chapter 105: The Warden Speaks

The suppressor cuff clicked shut on Roan's wrist, and the first thing he measured was the lag.

Not the weight—the alloy was light, matte-black, stamped with the Sentinel Bureau's seven-point seal—but the micro-stutter between his Vitalis pulse and the cuff's suppression matrix. Most candidates would register nothing beyond the cold click of magnetic locks. Roan felt the gap in arterial time. A sliver of arterial time. A lifetime, if you knew how to spend it.

"Fifty-unit ceiling," Brendt muttered beside him, rolling his shoulder under an identical band. "They're turning us into civilians."

"They're turning us into variables," Roan said. "Controlled ones."

"You love variables."

"I love measurable ones."

Thirty candidates filled the prep box beneath Black Spire's east armory. Recycled air, metallic with anticipation. Roan didn't survey the room. He indexed it. Vitalis signatures were masked by the cuffs, but bodies betrayed themselves. A girl from House Carrow stood with her weight on her right heel, left hip cocked—old fracture, poorly healed, a sparring accident she'd never had reset. Three Ashford boys shared the same smirk, genetic or trained, chins elevated by the certainty that rules existed to bend around their names. Near the far wall, a lean boy in slate-gray livery watched Roan with the patience of a creditor. Vell. Not the Vell patriarch—too young, too immaculate—but close enough to the patriarch's blood to carry the same math in his posture.

The Rift Klaxon sounded. Not an alert. The simulation tone.

The gray walls dissolved into static, then reformed into the broken geometry of a Hollowed City. Sky the color of bruised iron. Wind that tasted like copper and old electricity under a dome that wasn't sky. The floor tilted—rubble and shattered ferrocrete—a main avenue choked with the calcified wombs of long-dead husks.

Roan's cuff flared. A number blinked across its face. Fifty. Hard ceiling.

"Welcome to the Culling Maze," the overhead speaker crackled. "Survival format. Last four standing receive Global Tournament seeding. Bleed Field active. Drain proportional to exertion. Begin."

The field hit like a toothache in every vein.

Roan felt it immediately—not the gentle siphon of training equipment, but the aggressive pull of a Hollow Core fragment buried somewhere in the architecture. It tugged at his reserves with a rhythm. Pull. Pause. Pull. The pause lasted long enough for a heartbeat. Not enough for a strike.

Around him, students swore and dropped into stances, reaching for techniques that wouldn't come. A boy from the eastern districts sprinted for cover, burning his capped fifty units on speed, and collapsed behind a toppled pillar as the field stripped him to thirty, then twenty, then nothing. His cuff blinked red. Unconscious in four seconds.

Brendt grimaced, leaning against a ferrocrete slab. "That's not a cap. That's theft."

"It's a Sidewound," Roan said.

The Vell boy straightened, his composure brittle but intact. "Poetic. Everything here bleeds."

"Not everything."

Roan turned his attention to the avenue. The field had a frequency. It wasn't ambient. It was directed, pulsing from a source somewhere ahead, beyond the tilt of the second spire. The buildings were hollow shells—some collapsed, some rigged to fall. A perfect environment for enemies who wanted you tired before they killed you.

He started walking.

Brendt fell in at his left, three meters clear, trusting the gap. "You're not going to run?"

"Running burns Vitalis. The drain takes proportional to exertion."

"So we stroll to victory."

"We stroll while others sprint themselves unconscious."

The Carrow girl limped after them, favoring her good hip. Smart. She'd seen Roan's pace and calculated the same angles. The Vell boy followed last, his stride deliberate, his eyes on Roan's back.

They passed two more bodies in the first hundred meters. Not dead. Curled, fetal, cuffs screaming red. The maze was doing the culling. The candidates were just bleeding into it.

Twenty minutes in, the maze had narrowed to a drainage channel between two collapsed spires. The air was colder here, sharp with ozone. The Bleed Field had intensified, syncing with their movement—the faster they burned Vitalis, the harder the siphon bit.

Roan's reserves were untouched. Not because the cuff spared him, but because he hadn't spent any.

He'd walked. He'd let others sprint past, burning their capped fifty units on panic, collapsing behind cover while the field stripped them dry. Brendt had followed his pace, trusting the balance even when instinct screamed to run. The Carrow girl had kept up, her breathing ragged but controlled. The Vell boy brought up the rear, his hair still perfect, his smile finally gone.

"We're being herded," Vell said. "The exits seal behind us."

"They sealed eleven minutes ago," Roan said. "You're only noticing now?"

Vell's jaw tightened. Not anger. Recalculation. "Then what's the exit strategy, Vance?"

Roan didn't answer. He was listening to the field.

The siphon had a waveform. It pulled in cycles, each surge carrying a harmonic undertone—a familiar inversion of pressure, a vacuum that pulled before it pushed. His father had written about fields like this. Fragment four of the Vance Codex, smuggled out of Greycross in a dead man's luggage. *A Sidewound by any other name still breaks the skin. The question is who collects the blood.*

The drone swept overhead, a Sentinel observation unit, matte and silent, its lens array cycling through thermal and Vitalis spectra. Roan tracked its arc without looking up. Twelve seconds per pass. Two-second blind spot where the spires' magnetic interference scrambled the feed. He'd mapped the blind spot eight minutes ago by the static tick in his cuff.

The field pulled again. Harder.

Roan let it.

For one ugly beat, the drain bit deep enough to hurt. Real damage. Cellular level. Agony that preceded permanent depletion if you let it linger. His nerves screamed. His channels spasmed.

Then,

Pressure. Controlled expansion. The converted Vitalis flooded his hidden reserves, swelling his true output beneath the cuff's public-facing lie. He'd entered the maze at a stable Tempered baseline, his real readings compressed and masked. Now he had more.

But the drone was turning.

Roan shifted his weight, kicked a loose stone, and let his shoulder brush the ferrocrete wall. The motion looked like a stagger. On thermal, it would read as a boy losing balance under the drain. On Vitalis scan, the spike of his Inversion would blend with the ambient fluctuation of the field itself.

The drone passed.

Brendt watched him. Not with suspicion. With recognition. They'd shared enough training sessions in Drogan's hell-pits. He knew Roan's stillness meant computation, not exhaustion.

"You found a current," Brendt said. It wasn't a question.

"I found the direction," Roan corrected. "Swim with it."

The Vell boy stared from three meters back, his eyes narrowed. He hadn't seen the Inversion—no one could, not without Aldric's math—but he'd seen the stagger, and he'd seen the recovery. Too fast. Too complete.

"Lucky physiology," Vell said.

"Lucky," Roan agreed.

The columns didn't lie. But sometimes the math was incomplete.

- break -

The final chamber was a cathedral of rust.

They'd climbed through the second spire's corpse into an open space—a domed intersection where six avenues met, the ceiling vaulted with iron ribs that might once have been a transit hub. The Bleed Field was a roar here. Students who'd arrived ahead were on their knees. One, a broad boy from the lower districts, was vomiting blood into a crack in the floor. The cuff couldn't protect what the field stripped beyond zero.

Eight survivors, including Roan's group. Minus the two unconscious behind them. Minus the boy vomiting.

Four slots.

Braddock Vell stepped into the center of the dome, his cuff glowing amber. Not red. Amber. Some modification Roan hadn't calculated—a bypass that let him retain more than the regulation fifty. House resources. Cheating, if the Sentinels cared to look.

"Endgame," Vell announced. "Simple arithmetic. Four of us. Four slots. But why share?"

The Carrow girl raised her rebar. "You want to thin the herd here? The drones see everything."

"The drones see what they're told to see," Vell said.

He moved. Not fast—fast was impossible with the cap—but efficiently. A palm strike to the Carrow girl's sternum. She folded, rebar clattering. Brendt stepped in, guard up, but Vell had already pivoted, a low sweep taking Brendt's ankle. Brendt hit the ground, skull smacking against ferrocrete.

Roan measured the sound. Dull. Non-penetrating. Stun, not rupture. He'd feel it tomorrow, but the structural threat was shallow.

Not dead.

Vell straightened, turning to Roan. Seven meters. His output was still technically capped, but his technique was Hardened-grade, economical, brutal. He'd been fighting at Initiate levels to hide his true rank, same as Roan. The difference was that Vell had just shown his hand.

"Vance," Vell said. "You don't belong in this tournament. You don't belong in this Spire. Your father was a heretic, your house is ash, and your survival is an accounting error."

Roan didn't speak. His jaw tightened.

— Vell knew more than an Ashford puppet should. Vorath was sharing files. Drogan's warning about the logs made sense; Vell had been reading them. The field's algorithm logs were leaking, and Vell had the access to interpret them.

The field pulled. Roan let it bite again, deeper this time. The pain was genuine. Nerve-deep. One ugly beat of real, permanent-threat damage.

Too much. The conversion spiked his internal reserves hard enough to strain the cuff's suppression matrix. The band on his wrist heated, threatening to log an anomaly.

Roan bled the excess into his left hand, letting it pool in dead channels so the sensors read stability. A trick from Pyrebrand Cycling—redirect overflow into tissue that wouldn't flare on scan.

Vell closed the distance. A straight punch, textbook, aimed at Roan's throat. Kill strikes only. No sport.

Roan didn't dodge. He shifted fourteen centimeters left, enough for the fist to graze his collar, and struck the floor.

Not with Vitalis. With physics.

Sevenfold Bleed: First Strike.

The technique wasn't designed for suppression cuffs. It was designed to rupture enemy channels. But Roan had inverted the application. Instead of channeling outward, he drove the kinetic echo into the ferrocrete beneath them, exploiting the fracture lines he'd mapped during the approach. Compression, torsion, collapse.

The floor imploded.

Not dramatically. Precisely. A two-meter circle dropped six inches—just enough to unbalance Vell, to send his second strike wide, to put his chin at the exact height Roan needed.

Hollowstrike Doctrine: Reflected Blow.

Roan's palm met Vell's solar plexus. No Vitalis flare. Just air pressure, bone leverage, and the stored momentum of the collapsing stone. Vell's eyes widened. The amber on his cuff flickered out as his diaphragm spasmed. He hit the rubble and didn't rise.

Roan stood still.

The drone was overhead. Twelve seconds. Eleven. He breathed out, bleeding the last of the conversion spike into the ambient drain, letting the field swallow the evidence.

Ten seconds.

The drone passed.

Brendt groaned, pushing himself up. He swayed slightly, blinked twice, and pressed a hand to his temple. "You could've warned me about the floor."

"You would've tensed," Roan said. "Tensed bodies fall harder."

The remaining candidates—two boys huddled near the iron ribs—stared at Roan with something that wasn't fear. They were too drained for fear.

Four survivors. Four slots.

Right.

The corridor outside the medical bay smelled of antiseptic and recycled victory. Brendt had an ice pack pressed to the back of his head, his face still pale, his pupils slow to track. Roan sat on the bench beside him, his hands still cold from the drain field.

Other candidates limped past—some weeping, some raging. Braddock Vell had been carried out on a stretcher, conscious, his eyes finding Roan's with a promise that didn't need words. Not a grudge. A data point.

Drogan Hale found them there.

The Warden moved like a man who'd never learned to walk quietly. His prosthetic leg—the Iron Hand of Hale—clicked against the tile in a rhythm Roan had learned to read like Vitalis. He didn't ask if they were alright. He assessed. His gaze lingered on Roan's wrist. The cuff was gone, but the ring of pale skin remained.

"Sentinel Bureau pulled the maze logs," Drogan said. "They're running anomaly detection."

Roan kept his eyes forward. "The field was unstable."

"The field was perfect," Drogan corrected. "That's the problem. It was a Greel design. Supposed to identify students with irregular resilience."

Brendt lowered his ice pack and winced, a wave of nausea briefly crossing his face. "They were fishing?"

"They were trawling," Drogan said. He held out a data chip. Small. Black. "Eckhart pulled the schematic overlay. The drain algorithm. It's Aldric's work, Roan. Your father built the original framework for that field. House Greel reverse-engineered it. They don't know who they're looking for yet. But they know the math."

Roan took the chip. It was lighter than it should've been.

He turned it in his fingers. If House Greel had the drain field's data, he had a template for Inversion. He had a map of what to look for. And the next tournament stage—the Global Qualifier—was hosted in Greycross.

Greycross. Roan's birthplace. House Greel's northern territory.

"They're waiting," Roan said.

Drogan's jaw tightened. "They're hunting."

"Then I'll need better cuffs."

"You'll need more than cuffs." Drogan's iron hand clicked once, a sound like a gun cocking. "Bracket releases tonight. Ashford pulled strings. Or Greel did. Doesn't matter which."

Brendt looked up, his hand still pressed against his skull. "What?"

"Your first-round opponent isn't a student." Drogan's voice dropped. "Crowned. Registered Rift Hunter. Sentinel Bureau's 'test candidate' for the junior bracket."

Roan's fingers stilled around the chip.

A Crowned. Tier 4. Against a publicly capped Initiate.

The tally on that was simple. Brutal. And entirely incorrect, because they were measuring the wrong boy.

But House Greel wouldn't have made that mistake unless he wanted to see what broke the rules.

Roan stood. The chip went into his pocket.

"Good," he said.
