Chapter 59: Five Thousand Credits

The first task payment cleared three days after the Rift expedition. Five thousand Spire Credits, deposited directly into his account with a terse note from the Bursar's office: *Outstanding performance. Continue accordingly.*

Five thousand. Enough to buy forty bottles of low grade Wraithmilk Tincture on the open market. Enough to fuel a month of aggressive Inversion training if he played the angles correctly.

Roan sat in his dormitory room, cross-legged on the bare floor, staring at the number on his handheld display. The fluorescent light overhead buzzed. Somewhere down the hall, a group of second-years argued about technique rankings. Normal sounds. Normal life.

Except nothing felt normal anymore.

He closed his eyes. Felt the weight of the Codex Shard set at the base of his skull, permanently fused now, a fact that still caught him off-guard when he reached up to touch the spot and found only smooth skin. The pendant had melted into him three weeks ago. The first time he'd drunk Bloodscourge, the backlash had been so severe that the Shard had no choice but to anchor itself to survive.

That was the official explanation, anyway. The one the Shard had offered in its clipped, sardonic notifications.

*Sidewound absorbed. Fusing for stability. You're welcome.*

He ran his fingers along the edge of the bed. The metal was cold. Real. His Vitalis sat at approximately 1,480, well within Tempered range, comfortably past the threshold where most Initiates maxed out. He'd gained nearly two hundred points since the expedition, most of it from the Hollow Marrow Elixir's Inversion yield.

The equation left little room for comfort. The more toxins he processed, the faster he grew.

The problem was the math was also incomplete.

He opened his eyes and pulled up his Inversion log. The interface was spartan, another quirk of the Codex Shard's design. No fancy graphics without animated progress bars. Just text.

[Daily Capacity: 4/4 — Tempered]

[Mortal Capacity: 1/1]

[Conversion Rate: 100%]

[Active Inversions This Session: 0]

[Exposure risk: negligible]

One inversion left for the day. After using Crimson Wake's First Cut twice in the same fight, once to open the Hollowed, once to finish it, his capacity had dropped from four to one. He could push harder, use his single mortal slot, but that would cost him seven days of recovery.

The Shard had made the implications clear when he'd asked.

*One mortal inversion per week at your current tier. Use it when losing means dying. Using it to look impressive in training would be monumentally stupid. I'm technically dead and I can see that's monumentally stupid.*

Roan had not asked what "technically dead" meant. He suspected he wouldn't like the answer.

His handheld chimed. A message from Brendt.

*Training hall. Now. Drogan's teaching Hollowstrike today.*

He was on his feet before the message finished loading.

- break -

The combat training hall smelled of sweat and iron. Drogan stood at the center of the raised platform, his mechanical left arm catching the overhead lights. The fingers flexed and unflexed with a soft whir, audible only because Roan had learned to listen for such things.

Mira had insisted on staying in Ironholm for the week after the expedition. She claimed she wanted to see what the Spire's teaching looked like, so Brendt had arranged a seat for her in the front row. She stood beside him now, arms folded, her expression caught between curiosity and skepticism. Mira's interests ran toward theoretical combat analysis, not practical application, but Roan suspected she was really here to assess Drogan.

Brendt waved.

Theon Mercer wasn't present. Interesting.

Roan took his position near the back. Not hiding, exactly. Just... Observing.

"Fifteen students," Drogan said, scanning the assembled crowd. His voice was gravel and smoke, the result of a throat scarred by decades of Rift exposure. "Fifteen students who think they know what Hollowstrike means."

He raised his mechanical hand. The fingers extended into sharp points, retracted blade extensions, another feature Roan hadn't known about.

"Hollowstrike Doctrine was developed by a woman named Yara Coldwell. Tempered rank. No special bloodline, no family legacy, no connections to any of the great houses. Just a soldier who fought in the Third Rift War and lived long enough to think about what she'd learned."

Drogan brought the blade-hand down in a controlled strike. The wooden training post split cleanly.

"The philosophy is simple: your body is a vessel. Your enemy's body is also a vessel. Most fighters try to overpower vessels. Coldwell asked a different question."

He stepped back, gesturing for a volunteer. Silence. No one wanted to volunteer for whatever Drogan had planned.

"Third row. The one pretending to analyze me while he thinks I'm not paying attention."

Every head turned toward Roan.

Right.

He moved forward, acutely aware of the eyes tracking him. Mira's gaze was calculating. Brendt's was concerned. The others, curious, dismissive, faintly pitying. He'd been the Rift expedition's surprise performer, but that had been three weeks ago. People forgot. People always forgot.

Drogan studied him with the unblinking assessment of a predator evaluating prey. "You've done Hollowstrike before. I can see it in your stance."

"Once." The lie came easily. He'd done it twice, both times against enemies who couldn't be allowed to live.

"One time is enough to develop bad habits." Drogan circled him slowly. "Show me your guard."

Roan shifted into position. Crimson Wake's foundation stance, modified with elements of the basic Hollowstrike setup. The blade of his palm angled down, weight distributed for rapid transition between defensive and offensive postures.

Drogan's lip twitched. Almost a smile. "Interesting. You've been mixing styles."

"Making it up as I go."

"Most students say that as an excuse for being sloppy." Drogan stopped in front of him. "You say it like it's a strategy."

"Everything's a strategy."

"Fair." The old man raised his blade-hand. "Defend."

Roan moved.

Drogan struck first, fast, precise, economical. No wasted energy without dramatic windups. Just a controlled thrust aimed at Roan's center mass.

He blocked. Felt the impact travel up his arm, not painful, but present. Real. The mechanical fingers had stopped centimeters from his chest.

"Good. Again."

This time Drogan struck from a different angle, compensating for the block. Roan pivoted, deflecting rather than absorbing. His blade-hand caught the mechanical forearm and pushed, redirecting the force.

Contact. Brief. The Hollowstrike principle in microcosm, use the enemy's momentum against them.

But something was wrong. The redirect worked, yet the energy transfer felt... Incomplete. Like trying to pour water through a sieve.

"You're thinking too much," Drogan said, stepping back. "Hollowstrike isn't about calculation. It's about sensing the gaps."

"Gaps in what?"

"In everything. Stance. Balance. Vitalis flow." Drogan's eyes narrowed. "When I struck, my weight was on my back foot. My shoulder was slightly forward. My blade was perfectly aligned for a thrust, but that alignment created a weakness in my elbow joint. You could have hyperextended it if you'd pressed harder instead of just deflecting."

Roan replayed the moment in his memory. The analysis was correct. He'd seen the gap, calculated the angle, and chosen the safer option.

"That's your problem," Drogan continued, as if reading his thoughts. "You're good at seeing the gaps. You're terrible at using them."

The criticism stung more than it should have. Or rather, it didn't sting at all, and that absence was what bothered him. He'd grown used to understanding his weaknesses intellectually. Having someone else name them aloud felt different.

"Again," Drogan said.

They went again. And again. Twenty repetitions in ten minutes, each cycle slightly faster than the last. Drogan's attacks grew more complex, feints, combination strikes, Vitalis-improved bursts that left afterimages in the air.

Roan blocked. Redirected. Absorbed impacts he couldn't avoid. Felt his muscles protest, his Vitalis circulation adjusting to the strain.

The pattern was the same every time: see the gap, calculate the response, execute the safer option.

"Stop," Drogan said finally.

Roan stepped back, breathing harder than he wanted to admit. Sweat dampened his collar. His forearm ached from repeated deflections.

"You blocked every attack," Drogan said. "Impressive. But you won nothing."

The words landed like stones in still water.

"What do you mean?" Brendt called from the sidelines. "He defended perfectly."

"He defended safely," Drogan corrected. "There's a difference. Perfect defense means your opponent can't touch you. Safe defense means you're still standing when they stop."

The distinction clicked into place with uncomfortable clarity. He'd been optimizing for survival, not victory. The same pattern he'd fallen into with the Hollowed, the calculated retreat, the preserved ammunition, the tactical advantage preserved at the cost of decisive action.

"The doctrine is called Hollowstrike because you're striking the hollows," Drogan continued. "Not the obvious targets. Not the strong points. The gaps, the weaknesses, the places where force has nowhere to go. But you can't find those gaps if you're too busy protecting yourself."

He turned to address the full class. "The first principle of Hollowstrike is accepting that you will take damage. Not recklessly, never recklessly, but inevitably. Every fight leaves marks. The question is whether those marks buys you something worth having."

Roan stood motionless, processing.

"Class dismissed," Drogan said. "Except you, Vance. Stay."

The other students filtered out. Brendt shot him a questioning look; Roan gave a slight shake of his head. Mira departed with visible relief, already typing something on her handheld.

When the hall was empty, Drogan sat down on the edge of the training platform. His mechanical arm retracted its blades, folding back into a more casual configuration.

"You understand the theory," he said. "You don't understand the practice."

"No."

"Most students at this level are afraid of getting hurt. You've seen combat. You know pain is survivable." Drogan's eyes met his. "So what's holding you back?"

The question deserved a real answer. Drogan had earned that much.

"I don't know what the damage is worth."

It was the closest Roan had come to admitting the truth. The Inversion system converted Sidewounds into Vitalis gains, but only if the damage registered as a recognized side effect. Random injury during training wouldn't trigger the mechanism. Only deliberate exposure to known toxins and techniques, plus conditions would produce the yield.

If he let Drogan hit him, really hit him, the damage might be worthless. Or it might be exactly what he needed.

The model was incomplete.

"Find out," Drogan said.

He raised his blade-hand.

Roan didn't move. Didn't block. Didn't prepare to defend.

Drogan struck.

The impact lifted him off his feet. He hit the platform hard, tasting copper, seeing spots. Pain exploded across his ribs, a clean break, maybe two, injury that would take weeks to heal naturally.

Under the pain, something else started up.

[Sidewound detected: Blunt trauma, ribs.]

[Pattern recognized: Controlled impact training.]

[Conversion yield: +38 Vitalis.]

[Capacity: 1/4 → 0/4.]

Thirty-eight points. Modest. But the break itself would heal in three days with proper treatment, and the Inversion had already begun processing the damage. By morning, the fractures would be hairline. By tomorrow evening, they'd be memory.

"Interesting," Drogan said, lowering his hand. "You took that without bracing."

"You told me to find out."

"Good." Drogan's expression was unreadable. "You're still optimizing. You decided the information was worth the cost."

Roan pushed himself upright. Breathing hurt. Speaking hurt more. "Is that wrong?"

"It's incomplete." Drogan extended his mechanical hand, pulling Roan to his feet. "The gap isn't always in the enemy. Sometimes it's in yourself. You have to know which weaknesses to expose and which will kill you if the opponent notices."

The lesson settled into place alongside everything else. Seeing gaps was the easy part. Seeing which gaps mattered was the lesson. Which risks were acceptable. Which calculations were worth completing.

"Thank you, Warden."

"Drogan." The older man's jaw tightened. "We're not in class anymore. And I owe your father more than a title."

The name hung in the air. Aldric Vance. Dead seven years, or so everyone believed. The truth was more complicated, buried in Codex Shard notifications and fragments of encrypted memory.

"I'll work on the gaps," Roan said.

"You do that." Drogan turned toward the exit. Paused. "The mission debrief mentioned something interesting. You killed a Hollowed Sovereign. Hardened rank. Alone."

The statement required no response. The report had already been filed, reviewed, filed away. Official record: Roan Vance had faced a tier-4 threat and survived through tactical excellence and.

No one mentioned the Crimson Wake. No one mentioned the second Inversion of the fight. The examiners had assumed the kill was clean, efficient, the product of a student operating above his apparent level.

Which, technically, was accurate.

"Hollowed Sovereigns are rare," Drogan continued. "The one you killed was tagged as a secondary objective. Someone wanted it dead before it matured further."

"Greel?"

"Possibly. Someone else." Drogan didn't turn around. "The Black Spire doesn't like loose ends. Neither do the families. Watch your back, Vance."

He departed, leaving Roan alone in the empty hall.

- break -

The walk back to his dormitory took twenty minutes. The pain in his ribs had settled into a persistent ache, easier to manage now that he understood its parameters. Hairline fractures. Healing accelerated by Inversion conversion. Three days to full recovery if he didn't do anything stupid.

He did something stupid anyway.

The underground market operated out of a converted storage complex three blocks from the dormitories. No signs, no advertisements without official record. You got in by knowing someone who knew someone, and you bought what you could afford without asking where it came from.

Roan had gotten in through an upperclassman who owed him a favor. The details weren't important.

The market smelled of damp concrete and desperation. Dealers displayed their wares on folding tables: low grade pills of uncertain provenance, combat techniques printed on cheap paper, used equipment that might or might not function as advertised. The customers were a mix, students who spent their stipend on quick gains, soldiers between deployments, a few professional types who moved with the practiced efficiency of people who'd done this many times.

Roan navigated the aisles with his hood up, eyes scanning. Not looking for anything specific. Just... Assessing.

A table caught his attention. The vendor was old, weathered, missing two fingers on his left hand. The display was modest: three unmarked bottles, a stack of faded documents, a single combat blade that had seen better days.

The bottles.

Roan stopped. Extended his senses the way Drogan had described, not calculating, just... Feeling.

The first bottle contained Ironbile Resin. Common toxin, used in low-tier body hardening techniques. Unremarkable.

The second contained Sablefang Venom. Fifty percent chance of Vitalis boost, fifty percent chance of cardiac arrest. High risk, high reward. Dangerous.

The third was different. The liquid inside was nearly black, with a faint iridescent sheen that shifted as the light changed. No label. No documentation.

"What is this?" Roan asked, pointing.

The old man's eyes were sharp despite his weathered appearance. "You can tell it's something."

"I can tell it's not nothing."

A pause. The vendor leaned forward. "Pyrebrand Tincture. High-grade. The kind they don't sell in official channels anymore."

Pyrebrand. The same family of techniques as Pyrebrand Cycling, the method he'd studied in his father's notes. The tincture would amplify any fire-based technique, increasing potency at the cost of severe thermal stress.

In standard use, it would burn his hands. Maybe his forearms. Depending on dosage, worse.

In Inversion use...

He didn't know. The Codex Shard would have to evaluate the specific compounds before he could calculate the yield. But the potential was there, significant potential, given Pyrebrand's history of powerful side effects.

"Price?"

"For the whole set, including the standard tinctures? Three thousand credits."

Three thousand for three bottles. He'd paid five thousand for the mission. The tally worked, barely. Enough left over for food and basic supplies, nothing more.

But if the Pyrebrand worked, if the Inversion converted the thermal damage into sustained enhancement, he'd gain more than three thousand credits worth of training value.

Or he could end up with burns that took weeks to heal, while his enemies pressed their advantage.

Too many variables were still missing. Again.

"Two thousand," Roan said.

"Twenty-six hundred."

"Twenty-two."

The old man studied him. Whatever he saw seemed to satisfy some internal calculation. "Twenty-two hundred. Cash transfer without questions."

Roan transferred the credits. The vendor pushed the three bottles across the table, along with a small card reading *Ember's Rest, Room 7*, presumably a contact method if the product proved defective.

Not that he planned to complain. The Black Market didn't offer refunds.

He tucked the bottles into his pack and continued through the market, ignoring the other potential purchases. The money was spent. The decision was made. What mattered now was the execution.

The Pyrebrand Tincture sat against his back, a weight both literal and figurative. If it worked, he'd have a new tool for increasing his Inversion yield. If it didn't, if the Codex Shard couldn't process the specific compound, he'd have paid twenty-two hundred credits for a severe injury and a lesson in overconfidence.

Drogan's words echoed: *The question is whether those marks buys you something worth having.*

Time to find out.

- break -

His dormitory room again. The same bare walls, the same fluorescent buzz, the same narrow bed. But the context had shifted. Everything had shifted.

Roan sat at his desk, the Pyrebrand Tincture in front of him. The black liquid caught the light, iridescent threads swirling within. Beautiful, in a dangerous way.

He pulled up the Codex Shard interface.

[Substance detected: Pyrebrand Tincture, grade 3.]

[Composition: Thermal accelerant, vitalis catalyst, unknown binding agent.]

[Sidewound prediction: Thermal burn, class 2, duration 4-6 minutes.]

[Conversion probability: 87%.]

[Estimated yield: +120 to +180 Vitalis, dependent on burn severity.]

[Warning: Binding agent non-standard. Inversion may require manual calibration.]

Eighty-seven percent. High, but not certain. And the manual calibration requirement meant he couldn't just drink it and wait for the results. He'd have to actively guide the conversion process, which required maintaining consciousness through several minutes of genuine thermal damage.

Not impossible. Not comfortable.

His ribs throbbed in counterpoint to his heartbeat. The hairline fractures were already knitting, but the residual pain served as reminder: he was still recovering from Drogan's lesson. His capacity was empty for the first time since he'd broken into Tempered.

Adding another inversion now would stress his system further. Not dangerous, exactly. But not optimal either.

The tactical choice was to wait. Let his capacity refill. Build back to four inversions before pushing into unknown compounds.

The strategic choice was to test now, while his body was already adapting to damage, while the Codex Shard was still processing the rib injuries.

Math versus instinct. Calculation versus action.

He uncorked the bottle.

The smell hit him first, ash and copper, underlaid with something sweeter. His sinuses burned. His eyes watered. The compound was more concentrated than he'd anticipated, the binding agent creating a persistent aerosol that he couldn't avoid inhaling.

*Manual calibration required*, the Shard had warned. Which meant he needed to initiate the Inversion deliberately, not passively.

He raised the bottle. Made the choice.

Drank.

The liquid was cold. That was the first surprise, he'd expected heat, fire, the obvious sensation of thermal damage. Instead, the tincture spread through his stomach like ice water, carrying the burn deeper into his system before the reaction even began.

Then the burning started.

It erupted in his chest, spreading outward along his arms, down his torso, climbing toward his face. Not the sharp immediate pain of an impact, something slower, more insidious, the sensation of being cooked from the inside.

Roan gritted his teeth. Maintained consciousness through force of will.

*Manual calibration*, he thought. *Guide the conversion. Don't let the damage progress randomly.*

He reached for the Codex Shard's interface, felt it respond to his attention. The notifications were already appearing:

[Sidewound detected: Pyrebrand thermal burn.]

[Burning duration: sustained exposure.]

[Inversion threshold not yet reached. Continue exposure.]

Under a minute. He had to keep the burn alive long enough for the Inversion to recognize it. But the Shard wanted more than recognition. It wanted the burn to progress to a point it could map and own, plus steal.

Why?

Because the binding agent was non-standard. Because the conversion yield depended on how far the damage spread before reversal. Because this tincture had been designed to burn deep, to leave permanent scars if used incorrectly, and the Inversion could only save what it understood.

The count kept climbing. The skin on his forearms blistered. He could smell his own flesh cooking.

Still not enough.

[Inversion threshold reached.]

[Manual calibration accepted.]

[Converting...]

[Conversion successful: +147 Vitalis.]

[Capacity: 0/4 → 1/4.]

[Note: Manual calibration bypassed standard consumption—binding agent quirk.]

[Binding agent neutralized. No permanent damage.]

[New Sidewound pattern logged: Pyrebrand thermal cycling.]

He collapsed forward onto the desk, gasping. Sweat poured down his face. His arms throbbed with residual heat, but when he looked down, the blisters were already receding, silver traces where the burns had been, skin regenerating at visible speed.

The yield was respectable, more than he'd gotten from the Hollow Marrow Elixir, less than the theoretical maximum. The manual calibration had worked, but imperfectly, he'd held the burn slightly longer than optimal, sacrificing a few points for margin of safety.

Not the most efficient outcome. But acceptable.

He sat back, breathing through the residual pain. The Codex Shard's interface refreshed:

[Capacity: 1/4 — Tempered]

[Pyrebrand thermal cycling: Available for rapid deployment.]

[Note: Extended burn duration improves yield but increases calibration difficulty. Precision improves with practice.]

Precision improves with practice. A new skill to develop. Another variable to optimize.

Outside his window, the Black Spire's lights blazed against the night sky. Somewhere in that building, exams were being graded, advancement rankings were being calculated, the machine of martial society continued its grinding assessment of who mattered and who didn't.

Roan stood. His arms still ached, but the pain was manageable. The gains were real.

He had two thousand eight hundred credits left. Four inversions available tomorrow. A new technique burning itself into his muscle memory.

And a growing awareness that his father's legacy had opened doors he hadn't known existed.

The Codex Shard pulsed once, soft and warm against his spine.

*You're doing well. Don't get cocky. A.V.*

Roan smiled. The first real smile in days.

"Working on it."

His handheld buzzed. A message from a blocked source: the Spire's security log had flagged an external query about his recent market transactions. The request had been denied, but the trail existed now.

He stared at the screen. The smile cooled.

Someone was watching. And the night was still young.
