Chapter 148: No Safe History

Roan made it twelve steps down the corridor before deciding the plan had already failed.

Not catastrophically. Catastrophic failure had a different texture. Screaming, blood loss, uncontrolled fire, Brendt making that strained little sound he made when trying to keep a joke alive through pain. This was merely the quieter kind of failure where all available routes remained technically open while every sensible reason for taking them had been removed.

The temple lights moved with them, one lamp brightening ahead as the last dimmed behind. The effect was too orderly to be comforting. It wasn't guiding them so much as limiting where uncertainty could stand.

Behind them lay Aldric's untouched room, a cup of tea that hadn't cooled in thirty years, five photographs of the people who had helped murder him, one blank paper that had called itself the seventh, and a folded manuscript the Codex Shard had described as unread in the same tone it used for active knives.

Ahead lay the Hollowed City.

The Hollowed City had learned they were inside.

Roan could feel it now that he knew what to look for. Not a sound. Not a tremor. A pressure against the sealed temple walls, like rain made of teeth. The exterior threat density prompt hadn't been exaggerating. The city outside had rearranged around the entrance while they read dead men's letters and discovered that civilization, as a term, depended heavily on who got to write the plaque afterward.

Drogan walked in front, blade loose in his hand. Brendt followed with his shield raised despite the corridor being empty. Ines moved in the center with the manuscript case under one arm and her eyes on everything reflective, which in this temple meant nearly nothing.

The guardian walked last.

That part Roan disliked most.

The Hollowkin remnant had no visible reason to guard their retreat if the temple wished them gone. Its shape still offended several categories at once: too tall for a human, too thin for a proper monster, wrapped in old black mail that seemed grown rather than forged. Its face remained hidden behind the smooth mask of bone-white ceramic, marked with seven hairline cuts down one side.

It had called Roan blood-marked.

It had accepted Brendt's apology.

It hadn't lied yet.

That made it more suspicious, not less.

Roan stopped.

Drogan stopped immediately. Good. No questions without wasted motion. His head tilted just enough to imply the question without spending breath.

"We don't leave yet," Roan said.

Brendt turned very slowly. "I would like to file a minority objection."

"Denied."

"You didn't let me file it."

"Efficiency."

Ines's gaze moved from Roan to the manuscript case under her arm. "Because of the Black Rite."

"Because the exterior threat density increased after archive contact," Roan said. "Because the temple warned us before we opened the door and didn't force us out. Because the guardian is walking behind us instead of ahead, which means it expects pressure from the entrance or wants us to think it does. Because Drogan's left hand has tightened three times since we started moving, and he only does that when he recognizes a tactical pattern he hasn't admitted recognizing."

Drogan's left hand opened.

Brendt looked at it, then back at Roan. "That's unsettlingly specific."

"It's also correct," Ines said.

Drogan said nothing.

Roan looked at the guardian. "If we open the front door now, what happens?"

The mask angled toward him. The voice that came from behind it had the flat resonance of something reciting court procedure from memory. "Hungry corrections enter. Blood-marked heir is tested in open ruin. Three companions reduce outcome variance. One falls if shield-line breaks."

Brendt lowered his shield half an inch. "I dislike being a probability category."

"Then stop being predictable," Roan said.

"Working on it."

Drogan finally spoke. "How long can the temple hold the door?"

"The door isn't holding," the guardian said. "The door is deciding."

Ines's mouth tightened. "That's worse."

"Yes," Roan said.

A clean answer from a remnant. Everyone appreciated professionalism eventually.

Roan turned back toward the dark corridor behind them. The lamps reacted at once, reversing the moving pocket of light. The temple had wanted them to notice that option. Or it had always been willing to permit it.

Either way, leaving without reading the Black Rite was strategically indefensible.

It was also emotionally obvious, which made Roan distrust it. His father's letter had been designed to push him toward something. Aldric Vance had been too careful, too guilty, and too much like Roan in several irritating ways to leave a forbidden technique under a desk by accident. If the Black Rite was here, it had a function beyond sentiment.

A dangerous function. Good.

Useful things usually bit.

"We go back to the training room," Roan said.

"We don't know there's a training room," Brendt said.

Roan pointed at the guardian.

The guardian said, "There's a training room."

Brendt stared. "Fine. That one was on me."

Drogan's expression had gone flat in the way it did when a past he disliked had put its hand on his shoulder. "Roan, that rite has a reputation."

"You know it."

"I know the rumors."

"You knew Aldric had it."

Drogan's jaw shifted.

There it was. Not full confirmation, but enough.

"Thirty years ago," Drogan said, "I watched him use a partial form once. Afterward, three men who had been trying to kill us stopped. They didn't die immediately. They just stood there and forgot why they had bones."

Brendt went pale. "That sounds like something we shouldn't learn."

"It sounds like something I should understand before something outside uses its opposite," Roan said.

Ines adjusted her grip on the case. "You assume the army outside is related."

"No. I assume the temple gave us a tool immediately before a pressure event. Either that's coincidence, manipulation, or a test. Coincidence is low probability. Manipulation and tests both require learning the tool."

"What about corruption?" Brendt asked.

Roan looked at him.

Brendt lifted one hand. "I am aware this may sound morally delicate by current group standards, but when a technique is literally called the Black Rite and everyone pauses before saying it, corruption seems like a reasonable agenda item."

It was.

That was annoying.

Roan took the manuscript case from Ines and opened it.

The Black Rite of Veyrn didn't look theatrical. No flayed leather cover. No bloodstained clasps. No screaming faces pressed into parchment. It consisted of thirty-one black pages bound with a dull gray cord, each page thin enough to flex in the air but too rigid to be paper. The symbols on the first page shifted once when exposed to light, then settled into English.

Not translated. Settled. As if English had been waiting under the original script, wearing a mask.

Brendt leaned closer, then immediately leaned away. "It feels like being judged by a tax document."

"That's specific," Ines said.

"My uncle works in compliance."

Roan read the opening line.

A wound doesn't weaken the vessel. Unnamed leakage weakens the vessel. Name the wound, invert the leakage, and let the scar become architecture.

The Codex Shard pulsed.

[Black Rite of Veyrn recognized.]

[Category: Inversion Depth discipline.]

[Primary hazard: identity erosion through wound-personification.]

[Secondary hazard: hostile empathy bleed.]

[Recommendation: do not practice without anchor.]

Roan touched the Vance Pendant through his shirt.

[External anchor present.]

[Companion anchors present: 3.]

[Commentary — A. Vance: Dead-parent emotional instability present.]

Roan blinked. That wasn't a standard Shard readout. It had his father's cadence—dry, evaluative, uncomfortable with its own honesty.

[Commentary — A. Vance: Correction: highly present.]

"Problem?" Ines asked.

Roan's fingers stayed on the pendant. "The Shard's echo layer activated. My father left annotations."

Drogan made a sound in the back of his throat. It might've been amusement if one lowered standards.

The guardian hadn't moved.

"Training room," Roan said.

The remnant turned and walked toward the corridor branching left from Aldric's study, where no corridor had been visible before.

Brendt stared at the new passage. "I hate when buildings participate."

"Then don't become an architect," Ines said.

"I wasn't planning to."

"Good."

The training room beyond the passage looked less like a gymnasium and more like a courtroom designed by someone who expected the defendant to explode.

Seven circular platforms occupied the floor in a wide ring. Each was carved with narrow channels that converged toward the center of the chamber, where a shallow basin sat dry and black. The walls bore no weapons, no racks, no diagrams. Only words, repeated in multiple scripts, some sharp enough to make Roan's eyes want to slide off them.

The air tasted of copper through the filter mask.

"Masks off?" Brendt asked.

Drogan checked a small sensor, then looked to the guardian.

"Air is clean," the remnant said. "Cleanliness isn't safety."

"New favorite sentence," Brendt muttered.

Roan removed his mask. The copper taste intensified.

He stepped onto the nearest platform.

The Codex Shard hummed against his spine.

[Training field dormant.]

[Blood-mark required.]

Of course.

Roan used his standard knife to reopen the shallow cut across his left palm. The wound had mostly sealed during the temple archive sequence, helped by his Hardened circulation and the small amount of controlled Vitalis he had allowed. He pressed blood to the platform.

The channels lit black.

Not absence-of-light black. Active black. The color of a closed eye behind skin.

The basin at the center filled with clear water.

No one moved.

"That wasn't there," Brendt said.

"Thank you," Ines said. "I was worried my entire profession had failed in one second."

Roan read the second page.

The first rite isn't power. The first rite is refusal.

He continued.

Most techniques began by telling the practitioner what to pull, where to circulate, how to shape Vitalis through muscle, bone, tendon, organ, breath. The Black Rite did none of that. It began with damage. Not physical damage, though physical wounds could serve. It asked the practitioner to identify a wound that hadn't closed because the self had built traffic around it.

Grief qualified.

Fear qualified.

Shame qualified.

Obsession qualified.

So did revenge.

Roan disliked the list because it was accurate.

The technique didn't ask him to cleanse the wound. That would've been suspicious. Cleansing was what amateurs promised before they sold you incense and a subscription. It didn't ask him to indulge it, either. That would've been worse. It asked him to name the wound as a structural force and then prevent it from leaking into unrelated decisions.

A spiritual tourniquet.

A mental ledger.

A way to turn damage into architecture without letting the building worship the crack.

No wonder people called it demonic. It didn't make suffering noble. It made suffering useful, which offended nearly everyone.

Roan sat cross-legged on the platform.

Before Roan sat, he made himself read three more paragraphs.

That wasn't caution in the heroic sense. Caution was a word people used afterward when the outcome favored them. This was basic fraud prevention. The universe had just presented him with a secret civilization's forbidden rite in a locked temple while an army politely waited outside. Any merchant in Greycross would have checked the stitching on the purse.

The third paragraph explained why the Black Rite had earned its reputation.

The technique didn't corrupt by whispering evil suggestions. That would've been crude and, frankly, easier to defend against. It corrupted by improving decision making around one wound until the practitioner began treating every other part of life as irrelevant data. A man who named betrayal as his wound became impossibly good at seeing treachery, then gradually unable to recognize loyalty except as treachery not yet complete. A woman who named fear became fearless, then careless, then dead. Someone who named grief could function with perfect calm until one day they could no longer remember why they had loved the person whose death had started the training.

It wasn't madness.

It was optimization without governance.

Roan understood that immediately, which made him trust the manuscript slightly more. Dangerous manuals that admitted their own failure states were either honest or extremely sophisticated traps. Sophisticated traps deserved respect. Honest tools deserved even more.

He turned the page.

Don't carve away pain. Pain is signal. Don't enthrone pain. Pain isn't sovereign. Establish court, not crown.

Brendt leaned over his shoulder despite not wanting to. "That sounds almost healthy."

"That's how they get you," Ines said.

Roan shook his head. "No. That's how they warn you what the knife is for before handing it over."

"You are unusually generous toward the cursed manuscript," Brendt said.

"It has better disclosure practices than most Spire contracts."

Ines's mouth twitched. "True."

Drogan didn't smile. He had gone quiet in a way Roan disliked. The old man's eyes were fixed not on the manuscript, but on the black channels in the floor, and his right thumb rubbed across his palm as if remembering a cut that had never closed properly.

"You saw Aldric use this," Roan said.

Drogan's gaze moved to him. "I said partial form."

"You saw enough to fear it."

"I saw enough to know it worked."

That was more useful. Fear of a technique could mean anything. Fear of its effectiveness narrowed the category.

"Against people or Hollowkin?"

Drogan hesitated.

The guardian answered for him. "Against oath-bound men who had accepted a command not born in their own minds."

Roan looked at Drogan.

The old man's face didn't change, which meant the statement had hit something vital.

"Mental slavery," Roan said.

The guardian's mask angled. "A crude term. Not inaccurate."

The room became colder in a way the air didn't explain. Roan's third code hadn't needed refreshing, but it received one anyway. He didn't accept mental slavery. Not in business, not in war, not as a weapon someone else used conveniently while claiming the target had signed a contract under pressure they couldn't remember.

If the Black Rite could cut command lines, then it wasn't merely a corruption risk. It was the counter-knife to one of the ways Aldric had been cornered.

That changed its priority.

"I practice first," Roan said.

Drogan's head snapped up. "No."

"Yes."

"I have seen it used."

"And avoided learning it for thirty years. That makes your prior exposure suspect."

Brendt put one hand over his eyes. "There are kinder ways to say everything you say."

"Usually less precise."

Ines glanced toward the corridor. The distant pressure outside rolled through the temple again, faint but growing. "The door's patience may have a limit."

"Then we stop wasting it."

Drogan stepped forward. "You don't have to do it now."

"We established that I do."

"You know what I mean."

"Yes. It's still wrong."

Brendt shifted. "Roan—"

"If the technique attempts control, you interrupt," Roan said. "If I attack, Drogan disables. If Drogan hesitates, Ines shoots my right knee. If Ines hesitates, Brendt uses the shield edge to break my wrist."

Brendt stared at him. "I object to several parts of that."

"Pick one."

"The wrist."

"Knee then."

"That wasn't the improvement I was hoping for."

Ines folded her arms. "If he attacks, I shoot center mass."

Roan looked at her.

"Fine," she said. "Right knee first. Center mass if the knee doesn't produce a useful conversation."

"Acceptable."

Drogan looked like he wanted to refuse all of this and knew he had no good reason. Eventually he nodded once.

Roan closed his eyes.

The rite opened under his skin.

Not violently. Not at first. It arrived as a question with teeth.

What wound names you?

Roan could have answered with his father's death. That would've been obvious, and obvious answers were usually bait. Aldric's death mattered, but the wound was older and more precise. The day Mira stopped saying Aldric would come home. The first time Lyra asked why other children had fathers at registration. The weight of eighty-seven credits in a jar. Garrick's contract. The black iron pendant heavier than it looked. Bloodscourge burning his veins for a heartbeat while the Shard welcomed him home in a voice no one living had authorized.

No.

Not one event.

The wound was inheritance without explanation.

A debt dropped into his spine.

A father who had left enemies, tools, warnings, and a letter that arrived thirty years late.

The rite accepted that.

Pain opened through Roan's sternum and spine. The Vance Pendant went cold, then colder, until it felt like a coin made from winter pressing into bone. The Codex Shard flared.

[Black Rite initiation detected.]

[Identity erosion vector forming.]

[Wound-personification attempt: INHERITANCE-DEBT.]

[Invert?]

Roan almost said yes.

That would've been wrong.

If he inverted the whole thing, he would turn the wound into strength without understanding its shape. Efficient in the short term. Catastrophic later. He had enough inherited machinery in his body already.

He refused the automatic inversion and held the wound open.

The rite punished him for it.

His father's letter replayed in fragments. Not as memory. As pressure.

I came from Veyrn.

They burned us from the sky.

Inversion wasn't a trick. It was how we refused the universe's accounting.

If you are reading this, I failed twice.

The words tried to become commands. Not through magic, exactly. Through emotional gravity. A dead father's explanation had a way of pretending to be law.

Roan named that too.

Paternal authority: unverified.

The pressure stuttered.

Somewhere outside his closed eyes, Brendt made a strangled sound that might've been laughter or panic.

The Shard flashed again.

[Partial inversion available: leakage stabilization only.]

[Risk: anchor-network exposure.]

[Accept?]

Roan evaluated.

Leakage stabilization meant the rite wouldn't erase the wound or amplify it. It would prevent it from contaminating unrelated decisions. Risk: anchor-network exposure. The companions might see fragments. Not ideal. But they already knew enough to be targeted, and the exterior threat density remained high. A stabilized mind before battle had immediate value.

He accepted. A heartbeat.

The pain didn't vanish.

It became orderly.

That was a disturbing improvement.

Roan felt the wound arrange itself inside him like a black line drawn through a map. Aldric's death. Veyrn's fall. House Greel's recognition. Lyra's safety. Brendt's loyalty. Drogan's guilt. Ines's ambition. Each connection moved to its own shelf. The grief remained, but it stopped leaning on everything. Revenge remained, but it no longer shouted over risk assessment. The unknowns remained unknowns.

Good.

He could work with that.

Around him, the training room darkened.

A voice that wasn't the guardian's whispered in a language Roan didn't know and understood anyway.

Blood-marked heirs aren't sons.

They're continuations.

Roan opened his eyes.

"No," he said.

The black light in the platform recoiled.

The Codex Shard pulsed hard enough to make his teeth click.

[Black Rite: First Refusal established.]

[Inversion Depth: shallow → deepening]

[Mental leakage reduced.]

[Exposure risk unchanged.]

[Warning: rite responds poorly to inherited obedience.]

Roan looked down at his hands.

They were steady.

That was the first obvious change.

Not more power. No surge in Vitalis. No convenient breakthrough. The same body sat on the same platform with the same bruised ribs and the same shallow cut across his palm.

But the room had become easier to think in.

Not safer. Easier.

He looked up.

Brendt had one hand braced against the wall, breathing hard.

Ines's face was pale, her eyes too sharp.

Drogan stood completely still, as if movement would break something he had spent thirty years avoiding.

Roan exhaled. "Memory bleed?"

"A little," Ines said.

Brendt swallowed. "Eighty-seven credits. A jar. Your mother pretending not to count twice."

Roan's fingers closed once.

Not anger. Exposure.

He filed it properly and let the Black Rite do what it had promised.

The wound stayed in its lane.

"Anything else?" Roan asked.

Brendt's jaw tightened, but he didn't speak. He just shook his head.

Ines answered. "A door. Burning from the other side. Someone telling a child not to answer when the sky speaks. Not your memory, I think."

Drogan's voice was rough. "Veyrn."

The guardian had bowed its head.

Roan stood slowly. His knees didn't shake. That was useful.

"How long did it take?"

"Nine minutes," Ines said. "Subjective?"

"Longer."

"Naturally."

Brendt pushed away from the wall. He didn't crack a joke. He just looked at Roan, waiting.

Roan considered the question Brendt hadn't asked.

He checked for intrusive commands. None. Emotional flattening beyond baseline? No significant change. Desire to obey father civilization? Negative. Desire to organize all inherited trauma into tactical categories? Present, but not new. Violent impulse toward companions? None. Irritation at Brendt? Normal.

"Sufficiently," Roan said.

Brendt let out a long breath. He didn't say anything. He looked at the platform, then at Drogan.

Drogan hadn't stopped looking at the platform.

The platform next to Roan's had lit faintly.

Not black.

Gray.

The guardian spoke. "Unfinished oath recognized."

Drogan's face changed.

Not much. Enough.

Roan stepped off his platform. "Yours."

Drogan said nothing.

The gray light waited.

Outside the temple, something struck the door hard enough to make dust fall from the ceiling.

The training room didn't tremble.

The door kept deciding.

Brendt stood still, his hand resting on his shield strap, his mouth closed.

Roan picked up the Black Rite manuscript and turned to the next page.

A wound inherited by one may be answered by another, if the other lived where the first died.

He looked at Drogan.

"Bad news," Roan said. "The temple has a sense of humor."
