Chapter 77: Drogan's Warning

The training yard at dawn was never quiet. Roan had learned that within his first week at Black Spire, some eager disciple always arrived before the sun crested the eastern wall, either chasing glory or fleeing sleep. Today's early bird happened to be Theon Mercer, already halfway through a drilling sequence that echoed off the limestone walls like a metronome.

*Click. Click. Click.*

Right. Theon had finally been cleared for combat drills again after his two-week suspension. He'd chosen to announce this with volume.

Roan leaned against the eastern colonnade, arms folded, watching. Theon moved through a spear form, *Ashford Spire* technique, third variation, with mechanical precision. His Vitalis reading pulsed at 680 on the display wall nearby. Solid for a Tempered, if unspectacular. The arrogance radiating from his stance suggested otherwise.

*680 Vitalis. Solid frame. Good reach on the Ashford Spire. He's been practicing the fourth variation, see the hip rotation on that thrust, but his core stability suggests he's still learning it. Another two weeks before that becomes instinctive.*

Brendt would have called Roan over to join the drills by now, would have dragged him into some competitive nonsense to pass the morning. Instead, Brendt was across the yard, speaking with Warden Drogan near the equipment rack. Their conversation carried fragments of something serious. Warden's jaw set, Brendt's shoulders tight.

Brendt caught Roan's eye across the yard. A slight shake of the head. *Later.*

Interesting. Warden Drogan didn't usually conduct yard-side debriefs with students. The serious expression wasn't his teaching face; it was his *something's wrong outside these walls* face.

Theon finished his sequence with a flourish that sent his spear-tip wavering, the tell of an overconfident finish. He reset his stance, spotted Roan against the colonnade, and his mouth curved into something unpleasant.

"Vance." Theon planted the butt of his spear against the packed earth. "Come to watch the real fighters train? I don't blame you. Not everyone has the natural talent to—"

"How long did they suspend you for?" Roan asked.

Theon's smile flickered. "Two weeks."

"And you spent it practicing the fourth variation instead of reflecting on your conduct." Roan pushed off the colonnade, walking toward the equipment rack at an unhurried pace. "Interesting choice. Most people who get suspended spend their time feeling sorry for themselves. You spent yours getting better at something you shouldn't have been doing in the first place." A pause. "Smart."

Theon blinked. Whatever response he'd prepared hadn't accounted for agreement.

Roan selected a practice blade from the rack, heavier than Sidewound, balanced for drilling rather than lethality. He turned it in his grip, testing the weight distribution. "The Warden will want to see your progress. He'll test you again in three days."

"How do you—"

"Circular reasoning, Mercer. You've been drilling the same sequence for forty minutes, but you keep restarting the fourth variation when you think no one's watching the display board." Roan shrugged. "The display board doesn't lie. Neither does repetition data. You're not practicing to improve, you're practicing to prove something to yourself."

Theon's knuckles whitened around the spear haft.

"And if I am?"

"Then you're wasting time you could be spending proving it to them." Roan gestured toward the watching students who'd begun to gather along the perimeter, drawn by the promise of confrontation, however small. "This audience doesn't care about your form. They care about the next story. You want a story? Give them something worth watching."

He dropped into a neutral guard stance, practice blade angled across his body. Not challenging, he wasn't that stupid, but not backing away either. Theon wanted to prove something. Fine. Let him prove it here, where the stakes were low and the audience was captive.

*He won't take the bait. Probably. He's not stupid enough to risk another suspension within thirty days of the first one ending. But if he does—*

Theon's eyes tracked the gathered spectators. His breathing was shallow. The spear trembled almost imperceptibly in his grip.

*He's going to do it anyway. Because he can't stand being analyzed. Being watched. Being reduced to data points by someone he considers beneath him.*

Right.

Roan shifted his weight forward half an inch, not an attack, just a subtle redistribution. A small predator's trick: make yourself look smaller right before you strike, so your target thinks they have the advantage.

Theon lunged.

It was a decent strike. Ashford Spire fifth variation, the feint-thrust combo that had put three first-years in the infirmary last semester. Fast, brutal, designed to overwhelm defensive reflexes. The crowd murmured appreciation.

Roan sidestepped. The practice blade turned the spear aside with minimal effort, just enough contact to redirect the trajectory without committing to a counter. He let the momentum carry him past Theon, resuming his guard position three feet away.

Theon's face reddened.

"That's your best?" Roan asked. "I've seen instructors move faster."

Theon attacked again, sixth variation this time, a sweeping arc meant to catch Roan mid-recovery. The theory was sound. The execution. Roan pivoted inside the arc, letting the spear pass over his shoulder close enough to feel the displaced air. His practice blade tapped Theon's exposed ribs. A scoring touch, clean and clinical.

"Sloppy. You're rushing the transition. Your core isn't set before you commit to the swing."

Theon spun, spear whipping toward Roan's head in a brutal overhand strike. This one had no feint, pure aggression, anger feeding the technique. It was also unbalanced, heavy at the end, vulnerable to. Roan caught the haft mid-swing, trapping it against his shoulder. Their faces were inches apart. Theon's breath came ragged, hot against Roan's cheek.

"Your heart's not in this," Roan said, low enough that only Theon could hear. "You're not angry at me. You're angry at whatever got you suspended in the first place. Taking it out on the wrong target won't fix that."

He released the spear and stepped back, returning to his guard stance.

The crowd had gone quiet. They were watching something other than a spar now, not the technique, not the outcome, but the strange interlude of two almost-enemies sharing a moment they hadn't expected.

*This is a mistake. Why am I doing this?*

The answer surfaced before he could suppress it. Brendt's expression across the yard. The serious face. Whatever news the Warden had, Roan wanted to know it before it became official. Which meant he needed Brendt focused on him later, not distracted by whatever damage Theon Mercer was about to do to his own career.

*Pragmatism. Not sympathy. I can acknowledge his technique without endorsing his choices. The arithmetic still works.*

Theon lowered his spear. His breathing was still ragged, but the blind anger had dimmed to something more manageable. Confusion, maybe.

"You're strange, Vance."

"I've been told."

"I was going to—" Theon stopped. Swallowed. "I had reasons for that suspension. Good ones."

"I don't doubt it."

"Then why—"

"Because reasons and results aren't the same thing." Roan reset his stance. "You have fifteen minutes before the Warden finishes his conversation with Brendt and starts his rounds. You can spend them being angry, or you can spend them fixing that hip rotation. Your choice."

Theon stared at him. Then something in his expression shifted, not friendly, not grateful, but something adjacent to both. Recognition, maybe. The acknowledgment of an equal.

He turned and walked back to his starting marks. Began the sequence again.

Roan watched for three more exchanges, enough to confirm the hip rotation was indeed improving, then sheathed his practice blade and walked toward Brendt. The conversation ended as he approached. Warden Drogan's expression had shifted to something Roan couldn't read, a careful blankness that meant the Warden was preparing to manage information rather than share it. Drogan nodded to Brendt and turned, then walked toward the administrative building without looking back.

Brendt watched him go. His jaw was tight.

"What's happening?" Roan asked.

Brendt started walking toward the colonnade, away from the training yard's listeners. Roan fell into step beside him. They didn't speak until they'd reached the sheltered alcove near the equipment storage, where the stone walls muffled sound and the angle kept them out of casual sightlines.

"There's been an incident," Brendt said. "A bad one."

"How bad?"

"The Hollow Reaches. Someone triggered a Deep Rift alarm yesterday, third one this month. Sentinel Bureau's already dispatched a response team, but—" Brendt paused. His hand moved to the strap of his training bag, tightening and loosening in a repetitive motion. "The team went in, and they haven't reported back. Standard check-in was two hours ago. Nothing."

Roan processed that. Deep Rift response teams were six to eight Sentinels at Tempered minimum, usually Hardened-led. Failed check-ins meant one of two things: communication blackout or no one left to communicate.

"When was the last successful report?"

"Fourteen hours. Klaxon triggered at oh-three-hundred yesterday morning. Response team deployed by oh-six-hundred. Last contact was a status ping at eleven-hundred saying they'd reached the initial breach point." Brendt's voice was steady, but his shoulders had climbed toward his ears. "Standard protocol is check-in every four hours. They missed the fifteen-hundred. Missed the nineteen-hundred. Missed the twenty-three-hundred. Nothing at oh-three-hundred this morning."

"Four missed windows."

"Four." Brendt turned to face him. "The Bureau's convening an emergency council. Warden Drogan's been summoned, along with every other active Warden and senior instructor. They're going to decide whether to classify this as a catastrophic loss or assume communication equipment failure and send a recovery probe."

"And you know what they're thinking."

Brendt's laugh was hollow. "Yeah. The last time a response team went dark in the Hollow Reaches, they found bodies. Not survivors. Not even enough to recover. Just—" He drew a finger across his throat.

The Hollow Reaches. Tier 5 and above. Rifts that turned experienced hunters into cautionary tales. Danger that didn't announce itself with dramatic flair, it just swallowed you between one breath and the next.

"The Spire's going to mobilize a student response," Roan said. It wasn't a question.

Brendt's expression confirmed it before he spoke. "The Junior Hunters' Hall is being put on standby. They'll need volunteers for perimeter security, supply runs, communication relays, everything non-combat."

"Volunteers." Roan tested the word.

"That's what they'll call it." Brendt's eyes met his. "You and I both know what it means. First real mission out of the training yards, and it might be watching the place where people died for signs of trouble."

*And if the trouble finds us first?*

Roan ran the calculations. The answer came fast and brutal: a student volunteer force deployed near a catastrophic Rift event, handling support roles while actual Sentinels dealt with whatever had swallowed the previous team. If everything went right, they'd come back with experience and credits. If everything went wrong...

"How long until the official announcement?"

"Two hours. Maybe less if the council finishes early." Brendt started walking again, heading toward the dormitory path. "I'm going to volunteer. Warden Drogan knows my Vitalis readings, knows I can handle myself in the field. He'll slot me into something useful."

"And me?"

"I don't know." Brendt stopped at the junction where their paths diverged, his toward the eastern dormitories, Roan's toward the archives and his solitary study corner. "You're Tempered. Solid enough for perimeter work. But you don't have the temperament for a support role, and you know it. You'd drive whoever you were assigned with up a wall inside the first day."

"That's not an answer."

"No." Brendt's jaw worked for a moment. "The answer is that I don't know what you're going to do, Roan. I never do. And normally that doesn't bother me, but right now—" He gestured vaguely at the training yard, the dissipating crowd, the administrative building where decisions were being made that would shape the next several weeks. "Right now I really wish I knew what you were thinking."

*What am I thinking?*

Roan considered the question seriously. A Deep Rift event. Missing response team. Student volunteers on standby. The probability matrices arranged themselves in his mind like the pieces of a puzzle he hadn't finished solving yet.

A Deep Rift meant dangerous work. Dangerous work meant risk. Risk meant potential Sidewounds. Sidewounds meant...

*Inversion Yield.*

The thought arrived cold and clean, stripped of everything except opportunity. A catastrophic Rift event, a missing response team, an institution scrambling to respond. Inversion had rules about unknown Sidewound types, fifty percent failure probability. But the Inversion system also logged new patterns automatically. Every failure was a data point. Every data point refined the next attempt.

The Hollow Reaches wouldn't just be dangerous. It would be *educational.*

"Two hours," Roan said. "I'll figure it out."

Brendt watched him for a moment. Whatever he saw in Roan's expression made him exhale slowly, resignation mixing with something else, something that might've been concern if either of them had been the type to name things like that.

"Just—don't do anything stupid, okay? I know that's like asking you to stop breathing, but—"

"I'll do the math," Roan said. "I'll figure out what makes sense."

He turned and walked toward the archives.

Behind him, he heard Brendt's footsteps heading east. The archive study corner was a small alcove on the third floor, tucked between rows of historical records that no one had consulted in years. Roan had claimed it during his second week at Black Spire, a space too inconvenient to attract competition, private enough to think, and close enough to the restricted section that a well-placed bribe to the junior archivist could get him access to materials he shouldn't technically be able to read.

He settled into his chair. The Codex Shard hummed faintly against his spine, not actively, just a background presence that had become as familiar as his own heartbeat. He reached up, pressing two fingers against the base of his neck where the Shard had embedded itself.

*Aldric. You dealt with Rifts. You dealt with the Hollow Reaches.*

No response. The echo was selective about when it chose to manifest, never when summoned, always when least expected. A father even in death.

Roan pulled his notebook from the concealed shelf space behind the historical records. Flipped to a blank page. Began writing.

HOLLOW REACHES INCIDENT. PRELIMINARY ANALYSIS

KNOWN FACTS:

- Deep Rift (Tier 5+) triggered at 03:00

- Response team: 6-8 Sentinels, Hardened-led

- Last contact: 11:00 (breach point reached)

- No contact since (14+ hours)

PROBABILITY ASSESSMENT:

- Communication failure: 15%

- Team delayed by environmental factors: 25%

- Team casualties (partial): 30%

- Team casualties (total): 20%

- Team captured/alive in Reaches: 10%

IMPLICATIONS IF TOTAL LOSS:

- Bureau will escalate response

- Student volunteers likely regardless

- Someone will need to confirm the site

- Risk assessment suggests perimeter role is safest

- BUT: perimeter role offers no Inversion Yield potential

INVERSION CONSIDERATIONS:

- Hollow Reaches Sidewounds: unknown

- Historical data: limited (Cael, the old signatory accounts)

- Failure probability: 50% for novel patterns

- Success probability: 100% for logged patterns

- LOGGING NEW PATTERNS = long term VALUE

NET ASSESSMENT:

- Perimeter = safe, low-yield

- Advance recon = dangerous, high-yield

- Hybrid approach: find middle ground

He sat back. Stared at the page.

The figures said something he didn't like. The tally was always honest, that was its nature. And right now, the ledger was telling him that a catastrophic Rift event was also an opportunity. A place where people died was also a place where Sidewounds accumulated in configurations no one had catalogued before.

*Is that what you're thinking? That dead Sentinels might leave behind useful data?*

Roan closed the notebook. Tapped his fingers against the worn leather cover.

*Numbers were harder to bribe than people. But the balance isn't complete yet.*

He needed more information. The restricted section. Cael's accounts of the Hollow Reaches. Lady Sirenne—the western signatory, The Veiled Empress, one of the Founding Seven. Her journals were in the sealed archives, technically accessible only to senior faculty. But the junior archivist owed him for three weeks of completed research tasks, and access to a study corner that no one else wanted.

*The question isn't whether I can get the files. The question is what I do with them once I have them.*

Brendt would volunteer. He'd be assigned to perimeter or supply, the safe roles, the ones that kept bodies alive without asking them to fight. Brendt would come back with stories and credits and nothing that couldn't be healed.

Roan would do something else. He was already running scenarios, calculating routes, weighing risk against yield. The junior archivist wouldn't be in until the afternoon. The Bureau council would announce their decision before then.

Two hours, he'd told Brendt.

He had time to think.

The announcement came ninety minutes later.

Roan heard it from the archive alcove, a distant rumble of voices from the courtyard below, then the sharp tone of the official bell that summoned all students to the main assembly hall. He closed his notebook, tucked it away, and descended through the archive stacks.

At the bottom of the stairs, a figure stood in the dim light. Warden Drogan.

Roan stopped. "Warden."

Drogan’s face was hard to read in the shadows. "I know what you're doing up there. Running numbers on a Rift that eats response teams." He stepped closer, lowering his voice. "Let me save you some calculation. The Hollow Reaches has patterns that don't show up in any archive. And the arithmetic that matters isn't always the kind you can write down."

He held Roan’s gaze for a moment.

"Sometimes the most dangerous Rift isn't the one in the ground. It's the one in your calculations."

Then he turned and walked away, his footsteps echoing down the corridor.

Roan watched him disappear into the grey morning light, then resumed his path to the assembly hall.

The assembly hall was packed by the time he arrived. Students pressed against walls, filled every aisle, clustered in year-groups that made the hierarchy visible at a glance. Up on the raised platform, Warden Drogan stood beside Headmaster Edran Cole and three senior instructors whose names Roan didn't know. Their expressions were carefully neutral, the masks that meant *we are deciding how much to tell you* rather than *we have already decided*.

Brendt found him near the back, pressed between a cluster of second-years who kept stealing glances at Roan's expression.

"They're going to announce it," Brendt said.

"I know."

"You still haven't told me what you're going to do."

Roan watched the platform. Warden Drogan was speaking now, something about duty and honor and the Black Spire's responsibility to the wider world. The speech was polished and professional, but completely useless for anyone who needed to know what was happening.

*He's buying time. The council's still debating what to call this.*

"I have an idea," Roan said.

Brendt's eyes narrowed. "An idea."

"A theory. It needs testing before I commit." Roan watched the Headmaster exchange a quiet word with the Warden. Something had shifted in the room's energy, a new tension rippling through the crowd. "Look."

Headmaster Cole stepped forward. The room fell silent with a speed that suggested everyone had been waiting for exactly this moment.

"Effective immediately," Cole said, "the Junior Hunters' Hall is being placed on active deployment status. We have received official notification from the Sentinel Bureau that a Deep Rift response operation has entered crisis protocol. Your role will be support and logistics, not direct engagement, but essential work that frees our professional Sentinels to handle the primary threat."

The crowd murmured. Some students looked relieved; others, Brendt included, looked like they'd just received confirmation of something they'd feared.

"Volunteers will be organized into teams of six, assigned to rotating shifts at the forward staging area. Assignments will be based on demonstrated Vitalis readings, field certification, and, in some cases, specific skill sets that the operational command has identified as necessary."

*Specific skill sets.*

Roan remembered that. Not all volunteers would be treated equally. Some would get safe assignments; others would get useful ones. The difference would depend on what skills the command structure needed, and who knew about those skills.

"You have one hour to register your intent with your year-coordinator. Deployment begins at fourteen-hundred hours."

Cole stepped back. The senior instructors began filing off the platform. Warden Drogan paused for a moment, scanning the crowd, his gaze passing over Brendt without stopping, then settling on Roan.

Holding for half a second.

Then moving on.

*He saw me. He knows I'll volunteer. He's deciding whether to be concerned or amused.*

Both. Drogan had known Roan long enough to expect the unexpected.

"Registration's in the main hall," Brendt said. "One hour."

"I know."

"Roan—"

"I'll be there." Roan turned to face his friend. "I have a theory. It involves risk, but calculated risk. The kind where the arithmetic works out if I'm right and I'm not dead if I'm wrong."

"That's not as reassuring as you think it is."

"It's not meant to be reassuring. It's meant to be accurate."

Brendt stared at him. The concern was naked in his expression now, no point hiding it, not with the announcement hanging over them both.

"One hour," Brendt said. "And you're going to tell me what this theory is before we register. Because if you're planning something stupid, I want to know about it in advance so I can try to stop you or—I don't know, have your back when it goes wrong."

Roan considered the demand. Considered Brendt, steady, predictable, loyal in ways that made the word feel inadequate.

"Meet me at the archive alcove," he said. "Twenty minutes. I'll explain."

He turned and pushed through the crowd before Brendt could respond.

*Twenty minutes. That's enough time to pull Cael's Hollow Reaches files and find the sections on Sidewound formation in Deep Rift environments.*

Enough time to calculate whether the risk was worth the yield.

Enough time to decide whether he was someone who calculated these things at all.
