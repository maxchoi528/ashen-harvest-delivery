Chapter 169: House Ardent Archives

Iris Vell sent the warning through a flower shop.

That was the first insult.

The second insult was that the flower shop existed.

Roan stood across the street from *Maribel's Seasonal Arrangements* at nine in the morning and considered the storefront with the careful hostility it deserved. The shop occupied the ground floor of a narrow brick building between a pastry cafe and a watch repair stall. Buckets of cut flowers stood beneath a striped awning. The windows were clean. A chalkboard offered sympathy bouquets at a discount, which Roan considered either excellent marketing or evidence of moral collapse. Both.

The message had arrived nearly half an hour earlier through a student delivery service: a receipt for one prepaid arrangement addressed to "R. Vance, Black Spire," with no sender name and a pickup code written in lilac ink. Lilac had been one of Iris's private markers in the Vell finance files, rare enough to be intentional, ridiculous enough to be hers.

Brendt, when informed, had said, "Maybe she's apologizing with flowers."

Roan had looked at him.

Brendt had sighed. "No, I don't believe that either."

Selene hadn't found it funny. That was likely why Roan had included her in the call.

Now Selene waited two doors down under the pastry cafe awning, looking like a House heir with a reason to be annoyed at breakfast quality. Ines occupied the watch repair stall, pretending to inspect a cracked chronometer while evaluating how much of the display could be fenced if the owner died suddenly. Brendt wasn't present because Dr. Seth had threatened to sedate him if he left the medical wing again before afternoon.

Drogan had offered to come.

Roan had declined.

A warning delivered through flowers was unlikely to require Drogan's blade. If it did, Roan had made several other mistakes first.

He crossed the street.

The bell above the door chimed with offensive cheer.

Inside, Maribel's smelled like damp stems, cold water, and sweetness that hid rot for two days before surrendering. An old woman behind the counter looked up from trimming white lilies.

"Mr. Vance?"

Either she recognized him from tournament footage or the code had included description. Her pulse didn't change enough for fear. Civilian. Maybe paid. Maybe unaware.

"Yes."

"Your arrangement is ready."

She reached beneath the counter and produced a black vase holding seven white flowers.

Not lilies. Not roses. White crownflowers, northern variety. Rare in Ironholm because they disliked warmth and pollution, plus people who bought discount sympathy bouquets.

Roan stared at them.

Subtle, Iris.

He accepted the vase without touching the flowers directly. "Who ordered it?"

"The card says anonymous."

"Who paid?"

"Cash."

"When?"

"Yesterday evening."

"Description?"

The old woman's expression tightened. "We respect customer privacy."

Roan placed five credits on the counter.

She looked at them.

"Tall young woman," she said. "Dark hair. Very pretty. Too sad for someone buying flowers she didn't look at."

Iris herself, then. Or a deliberate impersonation by someone who had studied her well enough to invite death.

"Anything else?"

"She asked whether crownflowers wilt faster indoors."

"What did you say?"

"That everything wilts faster indoors."

The old woman looked at him for a moment. "She laughed like she hadn't meant to."

Roan considered that.

Useful. Not operationally, perhaps. But people under pressure leaked through strange places.

He carried the vase outside.

Selene joined him at the corner. Her eyes went to the crownflowers, then to his face.

"Greel," she said.

"Yes."

"Message inside?"

"Likely."

"Trap?"

"Also likely."

Ines crossed from the watch stall. "If it explodes, I am billing your estate."

"My estate disputes vague invoices."

"Your estate will be ash."

"Then it will dispute quietly."

Selene removed a slim silver case from her coat and unfolded a portable screening ward over the vase. The ward's surface shimmered once, testing for poison, explosive pressure, listening glyphs, and three categories of curse Roan had not known House etiquette required.

"No active harm," Selene said.

"That detects active harm," Roan said.

"Yes."

"Not delayed."

"No."

"Convenient limitation."

"I didn't design it."

"Did your family?"

"Probably."

"Then I dislike it accurately."

She almost smiled and looked irritated about it.

Roan tipped the vase. Water sloshed. Beneath the stems lay a narrow black capsule, sealed with wax. No crest. No obvious rune.

Ines plucked it out with forceps, placed it on Selene's ward field, and stepped back.

"Your suspicious flower sludge," she said.

Roan cracked the capsule.

Inside was a strip of paper.

Three lines, written by hand.

*The old man is tired of failures.*

*He will send blood that can pass as student blood.*

*Don't let Cyrus be hungry near a knife.*

No signature.

None needed.

Selene read the lines once. "House Greel."

"Likely."

"Blood that can pass as student blood," Ines said. "Greel heir?"

"Or cadet. Or bastard. Or someone altered to appear younger."

Selene's mouth tightened. "Cyrus."

Roan folded the note. "Vulnerable target."

"That's a charitable phrase for him," Ines said.

"No. It's clinical."

Cyrus Ashford had been many things before the tournament: arrogant, talented, politically sheltered, emotionally undisciplined, and useful to people who wanted a public champion without too many independent thoughts. After Roan broke him in front of the international audience, those traits hadn't vanished. They had curdled.

Roan had not spent much time thinking about Cyrus since.

That might've been an error.

Humiliation, if left untreated, became a market. Someone always arrived with a product.

Selene looked down the street, face distant. "Iris says not to let him be hungry near a knife. She thinks Greel will feed him revenge."

"Yes."

"Can we warn him?"

Ines gave her a look.

Selene's own expression acknowledged the problem before Roan had to. Cyrus wouldn't accept a warning from Roan. He might not accept it from Selene either, not after House gossip had framed her association with Roan as opportunism, betrayal, or romantic nonsense depending on which forum had needed engagement that morning. Drogan could warn him, but Cyrus had learned to hear adult correction as pity. Headmaster Cole could restrict him, but restriction would make him hungrier.

"Warning him directly accelerates recruitment," Roan said.

Selene nodded. "Because then the envoy, whoever it is, can say you are afraid of Cyrus becoming strong."

"Not bad."

"Don't sound proud. It's strange."

"I didn't."

"You did, for you."

Ines leaned against the wall. "We could remove Cyrus from campus."

Selene looked at her. "Kidnapping?"

"Temporary logistical correction."

"No."

Roan said, "Bad risk profile."

"Thank you," Selene said.

"Not moral objection."

"I noticed."

Ines sighed. "You are both less fun after forming a limited trust vector."

Roan looked at her.

She smiled. "Brendt told me."

"Of course he did."

The flower shop bell chimed behind them as a customer entered. Life continued. People bought bouquets while old murderers sent heirs through school systems.

The world's ability to multitask remained offensive.

Roan tucked the note into his inner coat pocket. "We need transfer records."

Selene said, "Student transfers are sealed until approved."

"Ines?"

Ines held up a hand. "Before you ask, yes, I can access rumor channels, procurement changes, dorm assignment anomalies, training hall reservations, and uniform orders. No, I can't hack central admissions without creating enough noise to wake Cole's office."

"Do the quiet version."

"I was going to do the loud version for free."

"You dislike free."

"I dislike boredom."

Selene's gaze remained on the crownflowers. "I can check House introduction schedules. If Greel sends someone with status, even disguised, other Houses will prepare reception."

"Disguised as ordinary student," Roan said.

"Then the absence of reception becomes data."

Good.

Iris's warning had three parts: House Greel dissatisfied, student-cover assassin, Cyrus vulnerability. It didn't include a name. Either she didn't know, or she withheld it because withholding preserved value. Both.

Roan lifted the vase and threw it into a public waste bin.

Selene looked at him. "Was that necessary?"

"No."

"Then why?"

"I disliked it."

Ines laughed.

- break -

They returned to the operations room without the flowers.

Brendt objected to this when he heard about it, which proved his recovery had advanced from medically fragile to socially irritating.

"You threw away evidence," he said through the wall-mounted speaker.

Roan placed Iris's note under a glass weight on the table. "I kept the evidence."

"You threw away thematic evidence."

"In a public bin."

"That doesn't make it better."

"It makes it traceable if someone cares enough to retrieve it."

Brendt went quiet for half a second. "You baited the trash."

"I disliked the vase and used the dislike efficiently."

Selene sat across the table, still wearing her coat. Rain had darkened the shoulders. She hadn't commented on the discarded flowers after the first attempt, but she had watched Roan throw them away with the expression of someone adding another private rule to a growing and regrettable list.

Ines opened her portable kit and laid out three slips of paper. "No tail from the shop. One municipal cleaner noticed the vase. No retrieval yet. I left a marker on the bin."

"Legal marker?" Selene asked.

Ines looked offended. "Quiet marker."

"That means illegal."

"That means quiet."

Roan copied Iris's three lines onto the board. Handwriting beside handwriting. The original remained under glass. Copies were useful until originals disappeared; originals were liabilities until proven inert.

*The old man is tired of failures.*

*He will send blood that can pass as student blood.*

*Don't let Cyrus be hungry near a knife.*

He circled *old man*, *blood*, *student*, *Cyrus*, and *knife*.

Brendt said, "Old man is House Greel. Blood means family. Student means transfer. Cyrus means obvious disaster. Knife means either literal weapon or metaphorical revenge vector."

Roan looked at the speaker.

"What?" Brendt said. "Being injured gave me time to develop ominous vocabulary."

"Stop developing."

"No."

Selene leaned forward. "Iris chose not to give a name. Why?"

"Options," Roan said. "She doesn't know. She knows but is testing whether we can confirm independently. She knows but fears the name itself is traceable. She knows and withheld it to preserve leverage."

"All four may be true," Selene said.

"Yes." He paused. "Her last contact ended with a drawn line. This is a crossing back. Cautious, partial, deniable. But crossing."

Selene considered that. "Which means something changed on her end."

"Good."

Ines tapped the phrase *blood that can pass as student blood*. "If it's a Greel heir, the transfer leaves money somewhere. Compact fees, housing retainer, uniform issue, medical deposit, travel clearance. Rich families can hide intent, but they still hate paying twice."

Roan nodded. "Follow payments that pretend not to be payments."

"I was going to."

"Quietly."

Ines smiled. "Less fun, but yes."

Selene's eyes remained on the board. "If House Greel sends an actual blood relative, it isn't merely assassination. It's a statement."

"To whom?" Brendt asked.

"To House Greel's own people," Selene said. "The previous attempts failed through proxies, mercenaries, Iris's unstable cooperation, Greel-linked contractors. Sending blood means the family is reclaiming the matter. Or House Greel is punishing the younger line by making them clean his embarrassment."

Roan considered that. "Both create pressure on the operative."

"Pressure makes mistakes," Ines said.

"Or speed."

Selene looked at Roan. "And if the operative wants to prove himself?"

"Then he may escalate before his cover is mature."

Brendt made a low sound. "That sounds good until I remember we are the people he escalates at."

Roan added *operative pride / family pressure* beneath the board.

Cyrus remained the problem. Not the largest threat. The least stable one. The envoy, if that was the incoming knife, would have training and objectives, plus constraints. Cyrus had injury disguised as pride and enough talent to make poor decisions matter.

Roan wrote one more line.

*Cyrus isn't the blade. He's the wound the envoy can grip.*

Brendt said nothing for several seconds.

Then, quieter, "That one is good. I hate it."

Roan capped the marker. "Then we confirm the hand."

The summons from Cole arrived not long after.

- break -

By evening, the name arrived.

Not through admissions. Not through House reception. Not through Ines's rumor channels, though those produced enough useless speculation to make Roan consider targeted arson against student forums.

The name came from Headmaster Cole.

A formal summons appeared on Roan's student slate at seventeen-thirty, requesting his presence in the administrative tower within the hour. It was phrased politely enough that refusal wasn't technically forbidden and precisely enough that refusing would be stupid.

Drogan walked with him.

"You knew?" Roan asked as they crossed the suspended bridge between Black Spire's main body and the administrative tower.

"Got notice ten minutes before you."

"Name?"

Drogan's jaw tightened. "The Greel envoy."

There it was.

The envoy.

The blank space Roan had written after Chapter 167, after Selene, after the investigation board, after the sense that unknown knives announced themselves as blank spaces, now had a handle.

Names were useful.

They gave paperwork something to bleed on.

"Relation?" Roan asked.

"House Greel's eldest grandson."

"Age?"

"Nineteen, officially."

"Rank?"

"Forged."

Roan looked at him.

Drogan's expression didn't improve.

"Officially," Roan said.

"Yes."

"?"

"Don't know."

"Guess."

"Forged peak controlled down, or Crowned suppressing hard enough to pass standardized scans."

That was worse.

A legitimate Forged peak was dangerous. A suppressed Crowned infiltrating as a student was an assassination dressed as paperwork. Roan's current baseline had stabilized around low Forged after the Deep Rift breakthrough. His combat peak could spike higher through Inversion and refined techniques, but peak wasn't baseline. Peak required cost. Cost accumulated.

The Greel envoy, if Crowned, could force Roan into bad accounting before the first real move.

"Why allow transfer?" Roan asked.

"Cole may not have a choice."

"Headmasters usually enjoy having choices."

"Founding Seven exchange provisions."

Roan made a mental note to find and burn whichever legal scholar had written those.

Drogan glanced at him. "Don't start with killing him."

"I hadn't reached that step."

"You looked peaceful. That worries me."

"Legal murder requires quiet planning."

"Roan."

"I am not starting with murder."

"Good."

"I am starting with admissions fraud."

Drogan pinched the bridge of his nose.

Cole's office occupied the top floor of the administrative tower, behind three security doors and a receptionist who looked at Drogan with the resigned familiarity of someone who had once been yelled at by him and survived. The office itself was wide and dark-paneled, then spare. No ornamental trophies. No walls of degrees. One large desk. Four chairs. A window overlooking campus.

Headmaster Cole stood at the window when they entered.

Selene was already there.

So was Ines, which meant either Cole's office had become careless or Ines had made herself inconvenient enough to be invited. The latter.

A file rested on the desk.

Cole turned. "Mr. Vance. Instructor Drogan."

"Headmaster," Roan said.

Drogan grunted.

Cole ignored the grunt with administrative discipline. "The Greel envoy's transfer request was filed under the Northern Crown academic exchange provision at fourteen-twelve today. It has been sponsored by House Greel, co-certified by two external Spire observers, and legally countersigned by the Founding Seven Education Compact."

Roan looked at the file. "And you want me not to kill him."

Selene's eyes closed briefly.

Ines smiled.

Cole's expression didn't change. "I want you to understand the restrictions before you choose which rule to break."

Better than expected.

Cole opened the file and slid a profile across the desk.

The Greel envoy looked ordinary in the way expensive assassins sometimes did when they wanted people to feel clever for not noticing them. Dark blond hair. Pale skin. Gray eyes without House Greel's gold-red signature. Lean build. No obvious scars. A face pleasant enough to vanish in a crowd of polished House students. His recorded achievements were modest: mid-tier tournament placements in northern regional circuits, strong theory scores, no public scandals, no dueling bans without elite command certifications. Too clean.

Roan read the profile twice.

"What is wrong?" Selene asked.

"Everything he chose not to be good at."

Cole's gaze sharpened. "Explain."

"Regional placements high enough to justify transfer, low enough not to invite study. Theory scores strong but not record-setting. No elite dueling certification despite House Greel combat culture. No disciplinary history. No close associates listed. No medical irregularities. No public rivalries."

Ines leaned forward. "A student designed by committee."

"Yes."

Selene looked at the image. "Or one kept off public records deliberately."

"Same result."

Drogan tapped the file. "Can you block it?"

Cole's mouth tightened. "Not without cause that survives review."

"House Greel tried to have Roan killed in a Rift," Drogan said.

"Prove it in a form admissible under the Compact."

Drogan went very still.

Cole didn't look away. "I am not disputing reality. I am describing the cage around it."

Roan liked Cole more when he sounded angry.

Not emotionally. Usefully.

"Then don't block," Roan said.

Everyone looked at him.

Drogan said, "No."

"You haven't heard the plan."

"I heard enough."

"Blocking alerts them and fails. Allowing transfer gives us line of sight."

"Line of sight to a Greel assassin in a school."

"Better than no line of sight to one outside it."

Selene's expression turned thoughtful despite herself. "If Cole delays, House Greel escalates through the Compact. They control the public frame. Black Spire appears to be retaliating against a student because Roan dislikes the family."

"I do dislike the family," Roan said.

"That doesn't help."

"It's still true."

Ines tapped the desk. "If he comes, we can watch dorm assignment, training partners, procurement, messaging, medical scans, and who approaches him first."

"Cyrus," Roan said.

Cole looked at him sharply.

Roan removed Iris's flower note and placed it beside the file.

Cole read it once.

His face hardened by increments.

"Source?"

"Iris Vell."

Selene said, "Likely authentic."

Cole looked at her.

"She warned Roan before Red Orchard," Selene said. "That warning proved material."

Cole sat behind the desk for the first time. That made him look less like a headmaster and more like a man deciding which institutional wall to break with his forehead.

"Mr. Ashford is currently under academic probation and psychological review after tournament conduct deterioration," Cole said.

Roan translated that into useful terms: Cyrus was angry, isolated, watched by adults he distrusted, and officially fragile enough that anyone offering strength would look like escape.

Bad.

"What restrictions can you place on the envoy?" Roan asked.

"Dorm location. Training access tiers. Faculty mentor assignment. Mandatory baseline scan."

"Assign Drogan."

"No," Drogan said.

Cole looked at him.

Drogan repeated, "No. If I mentor him, House Greel sees the threat posture immediately."

Roan agreed. "Assign someone weak enough to insult him or neutral enough to bore him."

Cole considered. "Professor Ilmere."

Ines made a face. "Theory department?"

"Political history," Selene said. "Low combat relevance. High attendance requirements. Publicly harmless."

"Also," Roan said, "she hates everyone with aristocratic posture."

Cole's eyes moved to him. "You know Professor Ilmere?"

"She threw chalk at Brendt for saying House unification was 'kind of messy.'"

"It was one time," Brendt would have said if present.

Drogan gave a low grunt that might've been approval.

"Dorm?" Cole asked.

"Not near Roan," Selene said.

"Too obvious," Ines said. "Near Cyrus."

Drogan glared at her.

Roan shook his head. "Near enough to approach, not enough to make it look arranged. If the envoy wants Cyrus, he should have to choose contact. We need intent, not opportunity granted by us."

Cole nodded slowly. "East wing second-year transfer block. Same dining hall rotation as Mr. Ashford, different floor."

"Baseline scan?" Drogan asked.

"Tomorrow on arrival."

"He can cheat it."

"Yes," Cole said. "But cheating leaves shape if we know what to look for."

Roan looked at the file again.

The Greel envoy's mild recorded face looked back.

But blood that could pass as student blood, not House Greel or yet.

"Let him in," Roan said.

Drogan swore under his breath.

Cole closed the file. "Transfer approved under improved internal watch."

It sounded official. It meant: the knife was coming inside.

Roan preferred knives where he could see them.

Usually.

- break -

That night, Roan updated the board.

*The Greel envoy, transfer approved. Official Forged. Possible Crowned suppression. Objective: Roan assassination? Secondary: recruit Cyrus as vector. Tertiary: observe Codex? Don't reveal recognition.*

Under Cyrus, he added:

*Humiliation unresolved. Vulnerable to revenge framing. Don't confront directly. Create non-Roan intervention path?*

He stared at the last line.

Non-Roan intervention path.

Who could reach Cyrus?

Not Roan. Not Drogan. Not Cole. Maybe an old teammate from his tournament circle, but most had abandoned him when his reputation became contagious. Iris? Dangerous. Selene? Politically charged. Brendt? Cyrus respected him poorly and had hurt him indirectly by siding with Roan.

No clean options.

Useful options rarely were.

The Codex Shard pulsed.

[Northern Crown vector confirmed.]

[Recommendation: observe before cutting.]

Roan leaned back.

"You are developing restraint," he told the Shard.

No answer.

Maybe it disliked being accused.

His student slate chimed.

A new message from an unknown account.

No text. One attached image.

Seven white crownflowers in a vase.

Underneath, a single line.

*He arrives tomorrow.*

Roan deleted the message after copying the timestamp.

Then he took out Sidewound, cleaned it, and sharpened a blade that didn't need sharpening.

Some rituals weren't about the weapon.
