Chapter 123: A Question of Names

The Greycross city gate rose against a pewter sky, its iron-bound timbers scarred by generations of siege and repair. Three Sentinel guards flanked the checkpoint, their armor dulled by the same rain that slicked the cobblestones beneath. One of them glanced up as Roan approached, noted the Black Spire student crest on his collar, and waved him through without breaking conversation with his companions.

Cheap. The man hadn't even looked at his face.

Roan kept that. Cheap guards meant cheap infrastructure, which meant cheap security on the roads beyond. A man could exploit that, or be exploited by it. The ledger was always the balance.

He passed through the archway and into the city proper, letting the press of the market crowd absorb him. Greycross smelled the way he remembered, wet stone, woodsmoke, cheap sausage from a vendor's cart somewhere to his left. The sounds layered over each other: merchants calling prices, a child shrieking somewhere in an alley, the rhythmic clatter of a smithy three streets over.

He was home.

Not home. His actual home was a blackened crater forty kilometers north. This was the city he'd grown up in before his family moved to the estate. He knew its rhythms the way he knew his own pulse. Knew which alleys connected, which taverns had back rooms for discreet transactions, which Sentinel patrols followed predictable routes.

Knew, most importantly, that Selene Ardent had been tracking his movements for nine days.

She thought she was subtle. Using a combination of local informants and Sentinel Bureau contacts to monitor the road from Ironholm. Her people had flagged his departure from Black Spire, noted his travel speed, estimated his route. Standard Conclave protocol for a Vance, especially one who'd just humiliated the Ashford heir at the Global Tournament.

What she didn't know was that Brendt had intercepted her operational codes two weeks ago. Roan had read the full intercept over dinner, passed it to Brendt with a simple "Interesting," and watched Brendt's face cycle through confusion and comprehension, plus something that resembled impressed horror.

Three people were tracking him with Selene's authorization. One in Ironholm, one in the border town of Kelvross, one here in Greycross. The one in Greycross was a young Sentinel lieutenant named Davin Merreth, temperament: ambitious, susceptible to social pressure, currently sitting in a tavern two streets north pretending to drink.

Roan could see him through the crowd. Average height, military posture, eyes that kept drifting to scan the street rather than his drink.

Two streets north. Watching the main thoroughfare.

Roan adjusted his path thirty degrees east, cutting through a narrow market lane that would put him on the secondary road toward the old district. If Merreth was any good at his job, and Selene didn't keep bad people, he'd have a secondary position. A man that focused, with eyes on both routes.

He'd bet on a man with eyes on both routes.

The rain picked up as he crossed into the old district. The buildings here sagged inward, their upper floors jutting to block the sky. The ground level was given over to storage cellars, night-soil collectors, a pawnshop whose sign had faded to illegibility. Nobody lived here anymore. It was a transition zone between the market and the industrial quarter, used by people who preferred not to be observed.

Selene wanted to observe him. Fine.

Roan stopped in front of a shuttered cooper's workshop, its windows dark, its door chained. He turned casually and leaned against the wall. Arms crossed. Waiting.

The rain drummed on his coat.

Ninety seconds later, Davin Merreth rounded the corner from the north, his Sentinel cloak pulled tight against the weather. He saw Roan. Stopped. Assessed. Decided.

He walked over with the careful neutrality of a man who knew he was expected. He was younger than Roan had guessed, mid-twenties, with rain running off a jaw that hadn't shaved in two days.

"Vance." He stopped at conversational distance. No threats, no posturing. Professional. "You're early."

"I'm always early. You're late." Roan's voice carried no heat. A statement of fact. "Selene sent you to make contact. I've decided to grant it. You have ninety seconds before I leave, and you won't find me again."

Merreth's jaw tightened. He glanced at the street, then back at Roan. "Lady Ardent extends her—"

"Lady Ardent wants to know why I came home." Roan tilted his head. "She thinks I'm visiting my mother's grave. Sentimental. She's wrong. She also thinks I don't know she's been tracking me since Ironholm. She should fire whoever gave her that operational security. Your lieutenant tells me you want a promotion within the Sentinel Bureau. Lady Ardent has influence there. I have information about the Ashford family's financial exposure in the eastern provinces. A trade can be arranged."

Merreth's throat worked. He wiped rain from his mouth. "You're offering to—"

"I'm offering you a choice. Report to Lady Ardent that I declined contact and that you've lost my trail in the old district, technically accurate; you'll find a route to the eastern gate that has three blind corners and no witnesses, or report that we had a productive conversation and that I'm cooperating with Conclave interests. One makes your career faster. The other maintains your cover with me. You can have both, but not if you stand here processing for another forty seconds."

The rain ran down the back of Merreth's neck. He didn't wipe it away.

"Ashford's eastern financial exposure," he said slowly.

"Three accounts. One in Kelvross, two in the eastern commercial district of Ironholm. Combined liability: approximately eighteen thousand Spire Credits, most of it leverage-based. Lady Ardent would find that useful. I would find Lady Ardent's silence useful. You would find your promotion useful. Everyone wins."

"And if I'm ordered to detain you?"

"You're a Sentinel lieutenant, not a combat specialist. I'm a tournament finalist with a Hardened-tier read-out and a Codex Shard embedded in my spine." Roan smiled, thin and brief. "You'd lose. Badly. And Selene would lose her asset in Greycross. She likes her assets."

Merreth studied him. The boy from the tournament footage. The Vance who'd killed a Greel enforcer in Ironholm. The same boy who was offering him a career-defining intelligence exchange with the subtlety of a merchant haggling over fish.

"Three days," Merreth said. "I'll report lost contact. But I want the Ashford intelligence within three days, or Lady Ardent gets a very different report."

"Two days. I want a meeting with Selene herself, in a neutral location, within the next week. She'll come. She wants to understand what I am more than she wants to control me." Roan pushed off the wall. "The Ashford accounts will reach you via dead-drop protocol. You know the one."

He walked east, toward the eastern gate, and didn't look back.

The rain swallowed his footsteps within twenty meters.

- break -

The Vance Cairn stood on a hilltop three kilometers north of Greycross, in a meadow that had once been part of a farmland. Now it was just meadow, wild grass, a few stubborn apple trees that had survived the blight, and the cairn itself: a man-high pile of river stones arranged in a spiral pattern around a central pillar of black granite.

Aldric Vance's name was carved into the granite. Below the name, a date. Below the date, nothing. No "beloved father," no "rest in peace," no epitaph.

Roan had designed the headstone himself, at age eleven, two years after the fire.

He stood in front of it now, in the rain, and said nothing. There was nothing to say. The Inversion system hummed faintly against his vertebrae, a low thrum he only noticed when he stood still for more than a few minutes. The Codex Shard had been quiet since the tournament. No Aldric-voice commentary, no system notifications, no sarcastic asides about his combat form.

The silence was louder than the voice had ever been.

[You're standing in the rain like an idiot. — A.V.]

Roan's breath caught. Then released. "You're back."

[Never left. Just conserving bandwidth. Tournament finals drained more from me than I expected. Give a dead man some time to recover. — A.V.]

"Three weeks."

[Three weeks. I watched you nearly die seven times and I couldn't do a single thing about it. You try being a passive observer to your own son almost getting killed. — A.V.]

The rain soaked through his coat. He didn't move.

He filed the bandwidth comment. The Inversion had never cost him anything—whatever limits Aldric faced were his own, the price of consciousness without a body. The distinction mattered. He filed that too.

"The tournament is over. I won."

[You won the tournament. House Greel won the war. He's positioning the Greel envoy as the face of the Conclave's next generation. You're a footnote on page twelve of the tournament report. He's playing a longer game, and he's better at it. — A.V.]

"I know."

[Do you? Because you're here at my grave instead of in Ironholm consolidating your position. — A.V.]

"I came to tell you something."

A pause. Not the lag of a system processing—it was attention itself, weighted and focused.

[I'm listening.]

Roan reached into his coat and pulled out a small object wrapped in oilcloth. He unwrapped it carefully. Inside was a fragment of black iron, no larger than his thumb, its surface etched with the same spiraling symbols that marked the Codex Shard. A shard of a shard.

"I found this in the tournament. It's from the Veyrn Sanctum. Same origin as the Codex Shard."

[Let me see it.]

Roan pressed the fragment against the granite beside the name. The symbols on its surface flickered briefly, then the low hum against his spine intensified for a moment. Then it stopped.

[That's a resonance fragment. Pre-Inversion archive material. Same metallurgical signature as my research. — A.V.]

"It reacted to the Codex Shard."

[It would. They're the same source material. This one's been separated from the main Codex for decades, maybe centuries. Long enough that it's degraded. But it still carries the base code. — A.V.]

Roan replaced the oilcloth wrapping and tucked the fragment back inside his coat. "After the finals, when they cleared the debris from the collapse, I found it lodged in a fragment of the envoy's gauntlet. The impact had already broken it free. No one noticed me pick it up in the chaos."

[You're thinking about going back.]

"I'm thinking the Veyrn Sanctum contains more Codex Shard fragments. More Inversion potential. More information about the civilization that created this system." He paused. "And I think House Greel is thinking the same thing."

[He would be. House Greel's father—the previous Northern Crown—spent thirty years trying to locate the Sanctum. House Greel inherited that obsession. He doesn't want the Sanctum for money or power. He wants it for answers. — A.V.]

"To what question?"

[That's the right question. I don't know. My memory of those decades is incomplete—the Shard doesn't store everything, and I've had to let go of pieces to hold the rest. But whatever House Greel is looking for, it's connected to the Founding Seven's decision to destroy the Inversion archive. There's a reason they didn't just defeat them. They tore it all down. I don't know why yet. — A.V.]

The rain kept falling. The cairn stood in silence.

Roan turned from the grave and looked north, toward the Black Spire mountains. Somewhere beyond them, House Greel was positioning his pieces on a game board that spanned continents. The Greel envoy was recovering from his tournament loss. Selene Ardent was trying to understand what Roan Vance was and why he kept defying her predictions.

And somewhere east of here, in a vault beneath a mountain that had once housed a thriving civilization, there was more of the technology that had made Aldric Vance everything he was.

"You're telling me to be careful," Roan said.

[I'm telling you that the Sanctum is not a place you walk into with a tournament trophy and expect to walk out with the prize. House Greel has been searching for it since before you were born. If you go there, you go prepared. You go with people you trust. And you understand that whatever you find might change the question you're asking. — A.V.]

"What question?"

[Why did they destroy us? What were we building that terrified seven of the most powerful people in history so much that they made sure nothing remained?] A pause. [The Inversion isn't just a system, Roan. It's a philosophy. A way of understanding how power works and who gets to have it. And I think—though the memory is fractured, and I'm piecing it together from slivers—it wasn't about good and evil. It was about something more fundamental. A vision of power that couldn't coexist with theirs. Incompatibility, maybe. But that's a guess. I don't have the full picture. — A.V.]

The wind shifted. Rain drove sideways across the meadow.

"I need to think about this."

[That's the most sensible thing you've said in three weeks. — A.V.]

Roan turned and walked back down the hill toward Greycross. The fragment sat heavy in his coat pocket. In the city below, Davin Merreth would be writing his false report. Selene would be waiting for intelligence that might never come. Brendt would be monitoring the channels in Ironholm. And somewhere in the north, House Greel was moving pieces that had been in position for years.

The model was incomplete. Too many variables. Not enough data.

He needed more inputs before he could calculate the outcome.

The rain covered his tracks, and the cairn watched him go.
