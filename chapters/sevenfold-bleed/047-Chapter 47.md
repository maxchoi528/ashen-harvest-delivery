Chapter 47: D-Tier Dormitory

The corridor outside the assessment wing hummed with the low murmur of students who'd already completed their evaluations. Some leaned against the walls, expressions ranging from relief to bitter disappointment. Others clustered in tight circles, comparing notes about what they'd been asked.

Roan stood apart from both groups.

He'd finished his debrief thirty minutes ago and spent the subsequent time cataloguing the crowd's composition. Twenty-six students remained for the second phase of evaluations. The distribution told him something: the process wasn't filtering by competence but by risk tolerance. Students with more to hide were taking longer to clear.

"Roan."

Brendt appeared at his elbow, slightly breathless. His uniform showed the disarray that came from rapid movement followed by forced composure. "They called me in. Oral review."

"Already?"

"First wave." Brendt's jaw worked. "Ines Carrow is conducting it. She's asking specific questions about the Hollow Reaches incident. The Deep Rift descent three weeks ago where we—"

"Went in without authorization."

"Yeah." Brendt glanced toward the sealed door. "She knows. I can tell. She's been running questions like she already has the answers and she's just confirming the details."

Roan absorbed this. Ines Carrow was third-year, Carrow family, moderate reputation, no particular axe to grind with either of them as far as his data suggested. That she was asking about the unauthorized expedition meant someone had talked. Or she'd seen something in the reports. Or—

"Which questions specifically?"

"She asked why we went in at all. What we were looking for. Then she asked—" Brendt lowered his voice. "She asked if I'd seen anything unusual. Anything that didn't match standard Hollow Reaches phenomena."

The corridor's noise receded. Roan recalculated.

"What did you tell her?"

"I said no. Because I didn't. We found what we found. It's not like we ran into some secret—" Brendt stopped. "Wait. Did we?"

"Define 'secret.'"

"Roan."

"The Hollow Core we recovered from the third-tier chamber. The one that felt wrong when we touched it. That was unusual." He watched Brendt's face cycle through several expressions. "Yes, we brought it out. Yes, it's in my room. No, it hasn't done anything yet. And yes, you should be very careful about what you say in that evaluation."

Brendt closed his eyes briefly. "You're going to get us expelled."

"I'm going to get us graduated. There's a difference."

"Expelled from a dead-end life versus entering a career with actual prospects. Some people might call that a reasonable trade."

"Those people haven't met Warden Drogan."

"Fair point."

The evaluation door opened. A second-year student emerged, face pale, movements mechanical. He walked past without acknowledging anyone, heading for the far end of the corridor with the focused determination of someone who needed to be alone.

"Ines Carrow will push," Roan said quietly. "She'll want to understand the context for why students were in a Deep Rift during restricted hours. The answer she wants is 'we were reckless.' The answer you give should be 'we were exploring academic curiosity within acceptable risk parameters.'"

"That sounds like something you wrote for me."

"I did."

"Ines Carrow isn't stupid."

"No. But she's also not Vell. She's not looking for ammunition against Black Spire students. She wants to understand." Roan paused. "Understanding isn't the same as condemnation. Give her enough to satisfy her question. Not enough to create new questions."

Brendt studied him for a moment. "You're weirdly good at this."

"I had a good teacher."

"Your father?"

"Various people." The black iron anchor sat heavy against his sternum, a weight he couldn't acknowledge in conversation. "Go in. Stay calm. Answer what's asked. If she pushes harder than expected, pause before responding. That pause signals you need to consider your answer, not that you're hiding something."

"Got it." Brendt squared his shoulders and walked toward the door.

Roan watched him enter. The door sealed. The murmur of the corridor resumed around him.

He needed to think about the Hollow Core.

The object sat in his desk drawer, wrapped in the cloth he'd used to handle it. He'd told Brendt it hadn't done anything, which was true. But 'nothing' was a spectrum. The Core wasn't inert. It pulsed at intervals, roughly every six hours, a faint thrumming that he felt more than heard when he was in proximity. Like a heartbeat. Like something waiting.

The Codex Shard hadn't responded to it. Not for recognition, not for warning, not for the low hum of resonance he'd felt when near other Inversion-related objects. That either meant the Hollow Core was outside the Codex's cataloguing system, or it was so deeply encoded that the Shard required specific activation to recognize it.

Or it meant the Core was exactly what it appeared to be: a Deep Rift artifact with unusual properties and no connection to the Inversion archive at all.

He didn't believe that. The feeling he'd gotten when his hand closed around the Core—something between recognition and dread—felt earned. Like his body knew something his mind hadn't processed yet.

But believing and acting were different problems.

He couldn't bring the Core to the evaluation. He couldn't dispose of it without understanding what it was. He couldn't ignore it if the pulsing was, in fact, a signal.

The corridor's noise shifted. Heads turned. A path cleared.

Kael Greel walked through the crowd like it wasn't there.

The envoy followed two steps behind, expression carefully neutral, posture broadcasting deference in a way that didn't match his eyes. The younger Greel watched everything with more attention than his posture suggested.

Kael stopped five meters from Roan.

The crowd had become very interested in their shoes.

"Vance."

"Greel."

"I heard you performed well on the practical assessment."

"You heard correctly."

"Third-highest score in the first-year cohort. Behind Mercer and Ashford." Kael's voice carried the flatness that meant he was calculating something. "Unusual for someone who supposedly focuses on practical application over theoretical knowledge."

"I find the two less contradictory than advertised."

"Do you." Not a question. "My family tracks unusual academic patterns. We're concerned about students who perform inconsistently. Patterns suggest hidden variables."

"Patterns do suggest hidden variables. That's how patterns work."

The envoy made a small sound—something between a cough and a suppressed laugh. Kael's eyes didn't leave Roan. "We prefer to understand hidden variables before they become problems."

"And I prefer to understand why someone asks questions about hidden variables when the question is about the asker's comfort with uncertainty."

A beat.

"Comfort with uncertainty," Kael repeated.

"Every question about my performance is really a question about whether I'm a threat. The answer is yes. Everyone is a threat to someone. The question is whether I'm a threat to you specifically, and the answer to that is only interesting if you're planning to make me relevant to your objectives."

"Planning?"

"Greel." Roan let the pause stretch. "You stopped to talk to me. That was a choice. You could have walked past. You chose to communicate. Communication implies intent. So yes, planning."

The corridor had gone very quiet.

Kael studied him. Then something shifted in his expression—not warmth, nothing that obvious, but a recalibration. Like he'd filed a new report and found it interesting.

"You're more direct than I expected."

"I find ambiguity expensive."

"Expensive."

"Time spent decoding subtext is time not spent achieving objectives." Roan kept his stance relaxed, his breathing even. Kael Greel's Vitalis presence was substantial—he could feel the pressure of it even from five meters away. Hardened tier at minimum, pushing toward the threshold of Forged given the way it saturated the air between them. "If you have a question, ask it. If you have an objective, state it. I'll tell you if it's relevant to my interests."

"And if it's not?"

"Then we'll both know we've wasted less time than a ninety-minute social dance."

The envoy's hand moved to his mouth. He covered it poorly.

Kael didn't smile. But something in his posture eased by a fraction of a degree. "I appreciate clarity. Most people here don't offer it."

"Most people here have different priorities than clarity."

"Do they." Kael tilted his head. "What are yours?"

"I'm not having this conversation in a corridor."

"No. You're not." Kael reached into his uniform and produced a small card—thick paper, embossed lettering, contact information for the Greel family administrative office. "My private communication channel. Not the public channels. This one goes directly to me."

Roan took the card. He didn't examine it yet. "You're recruiting."

"I'm identifying. There's a difference."

"What's the difference?"

"Recruitment implies I want you to work for House Greel. Identification means I want to know who you are before you become relevant to things I care about." Kael's eyes held his for a moment longer. "You're going to be relevant, Vance. The question is how."

He turned and walked away. The envoy followed, but as he passed Roan, he spoke quietly: "He doesn't give those out."

"Apparently."

"He gave one to Theon Mercer last year. Mercer used it once and then burned it."

"Interesting."

"Mercer said using it felt like signing a contract with terms you couldn't read." The envoy's eyes met Roan's for a fraction of a second—something calculating behind them—before he continued after his brother.

Roan watched them go. The card sat in his palm, heavy with implications.

He wasn't surprised House Greel had approached. The Greels were methodical. They collected information, catalogued threats, assessed potential. Roan had expected contact within the first month of the Deep Rift incident. The fact that it had taken until the post-expedition evaluation cycle suggested House Greel had been watching, waiting, confirming patterns before moving.

The card was a test. Taking it was an acknowledgment. What happened next would be observed.

"Roan." Brendt emerged from the evaluation room, face carefully neutral. He walked to Roan and stopped, waiting until they were both moving before speaking. "She asked about the Hollow Core."

Roan's stride didn't falter. "Define 'asked.'"

"Exactly that. She said 'I'm aware you retrieved an artifact from the Hollow Reaches three weeks ago. Where is it now?'"

"Directly."

"Very directly. I told her I didn't know. That you handled it."

"What did she do?"

"She wrote something down. Then she moved on to other questions." Brendt glanced at him. "She's going to ask you."

"I know."

"What do we tell her?"

The card from House Greel sat in Roan's pocket. Ines Carrow was asking about artifacts. The Hollow Core pulsed in his desk drawer. The Codex Shard sat silent against his spine, offering no guidance.

"We tell her the truth," Roan said. "We found something unusual. We brought it out. We don't know what it is."

"And if she wants to see it?"

"Then she can see it. Eventually."

"Eventually?"

"It's been three weeks. If it were dangerous, something would have happened. If it were significant, someone would have noticed. Right now it's an artifact we're studying. That's the story, and it's also mostly true."

"Mostly?"

"The studying part is minimal."

They walked in silence for another few meters. The corridor opened into the main academic wing. Students moved around them in the flow of post-evaluation traffic.

"She's going to request an interview with you," Brendt said. "Ines Carrow. She mentioned it while I was leaving. Said she wanted to speak with the primary retrieval team directly."

"I expected that."

"What do we do about the Core?"

"We wait." Roan found a bench near the academic wing's central atrium and sat. Brendt joined him. "We see what questions she asks. We calibrate our responses to what she already knows versus what she wants to know. And we don't volunteer anything we haven't been asked about directly."

"That's your strategy? Defensive positioning?"

"That's my strategy until I understand the battlefield." He pulled out the Greel card, turned it over in his fingers. "Things are changing."

"What things?"

"Attention. People who weren't looking are starting to look. That's either an opportunity or a threat depending on how we position ourselves."

"You're talking like we're entering some kind of conflict."

"We are. We have been since we went into the Hollow Reaches. The only difference is whether we acknowledge it."

Brendt was quiet for a moment. "The Core is important, isn't it?"

"Probably."

"And you're going to do something about it."

"I'm going to do several things. Starting with understanding what questions are being asked and why." Roan pocketed the card. "Ines Carrow isn't Greel. She doesn't want leverage. She wants to understand. That means either she's genuinely concerned about artifact safety, or she's looking for something specific."

"Or both."

"Or both. Yes."

The central atrium bustled with post-evaluation traffic. Students clustered around notice boards, checking posted results. The noise filled the space, providing cover for quieter conversations.

"I should study," Brendt said. "The second evaluation component is written theory. If Ines is already evaluating my pattern recognition—"

"Go."

Brendt stood, hesitated. "Roan. Whatever you're planning—"

"Stop."

"—just tell me you're not planning anything that gets us expelled. Or worse."

"I'm not planning anything that gets us expelled." Roan met his eyes. "Worse is a more complicated calculation."

"Right." Brendt's jaw worked. "That's not as reassuring as you think it is."

"I know."

He watched Brendt go, then stayed on the bench, watching the flow of students. The Greel card pressed against his leg. The Codex Shard pulsed faintly—his own circulation, not the Shard's response, but a reminder that his body wasn't entirely his own anymore. The Hollow Core sat in his room, six hours since the last pulse. Four hours until the next one.

Ines Carrow would want to interview him. House Greel had extended an unusual invitation. The evaluation results would post by evening. His standing would shift, up or down depending on how the assessors weighed his practical performance against his theoretical scores.

He had four hours before the Core pulsed again.

Four hours to prepare responses for questions he hadn't been asked yet.

Four hours to decide whether the Greel card was a door or a trap.

The numbers didn't care. But the tally also didn't tell him what other people wanted—only what they were willing to show him.

He stood and walked toward the academic wing exit. Behind him, the evaluation results posted. Ahead of him, the long corridor that led to the residential towers where his room waited with its hidden weight.

The Core pulsed. He felt it like a footnote—acknowledged but not yet relevant.

Four hours.

He could get a lot done in four hours.
