Chapter 120: The Public Lesson

The morning after the third trial, Roan found Brendt in the eastern courtyard.

The tall boy sat on a stone bench beneath a scrawny oak, one leg bouncing with restless energy that came from too much sitting and not enough answers. His notebook lay open on his lap, pages filled with cramped handwriting that Roan couldn't read from fifteen feet away.

Not that he needed to. The answer sat in front of him. Three days since the trial. Cyrus had been making moves—small ones, the kind that seemed like normal faction business on the surface. A few senior students redirected to “special training” in the western wing. A supply requisition for materials that matched the standard pre-examination upgrade kits. Nothing overtly hostile.

Nothing that could be proven.

“You’re thinking too loudly,” Brendt said without looking up. “I can hear you calculating from here.”

Roan crossed the courtyard in measured steps, choosing a bench across from his friend rather than beside him. The distance was deliberate. Comfortable. Two people having a conversation that looked casual to any passing observer. “What did she say?”

Brendt’s pen stopped moving. “The Headmaster confirmed the trial was legitimate. No procedural violations. She also confirmed that the ranking system has been—” he paused, searching for the right word. “Adjusted.”

“Adjusted.”

“To reflect recent performance metrics.” Brendt’s jaw tightened. “My score dropped by forty points in the last update.”

Roan processed that. Forty points was roughly two full tiers of assessment. The gap that moved a student from “promising” to “average.”

“He did it through his family connections.” Not a question.

“Allegedly.” Brendt finally looked up. “She didn’t say House Ashford. She didn’t need to. The pattern was clear. The timing was clear. The materials requisition matched exactly what was used in the previous trial scoring adjustments three years ago.” He closed the notebook. “She can’t act without proof, and I can’t produce proof because the system is designed to make proof impossible.”

The logic was clean. Elegant, in the way corruption often was when practiced by people who’d had generations to refine it.

Roan considered his options. The direct approach: confront Cyrus publicly, force a reaction, create a scene that might generate witnesses or documentation. But Cyrus was good at this—three years of practiced manipulation meant he knew every angle, every blind spot, every moment when an accuser would sound desperate rather than righteous.

The indirect approach: finding evidence, building a case, working through official channels. Exactly what Brendt was already doing, and it wasn’t working.

The creative approach: make the cost of the campaign higher than the benefit.

“I need to see the full requisition records,” Roan said. “Every material, every transfer, every authorization signature from the past month.”

Brendt raised an eyebrow. “That’s a lot of data to process. And access requires faculty approval.”

“Then I need faculty approval.”

“Roan.” The warning tone was familiar. “What are you planning?”

“Proof.”

“Proof that was designed to be unfindable.”

“Proof that someone thought was unfindable.” He stood, brushing off his training clothes. The morning session would start in twenty minutes. Warden Drogan had been running them harder since the trial. “There’s a difference.”

Brendt stood too, notebook vanishing into his bag. “I’m coming with you.”

“You should focus on your own situation.”

“My situation is your situation.” Brendt’s voice carried an edge Roan rarely heard. “Cyrus didn’t drop my score by forty points because he was bored. He’s building a case. A record of patterns. Next month it’ll be another forty. Then another. Until I’m not even in the top hundred, buried in enough procedural noise that no one will bother to question it.”

Roan recognized the logic. The same logic used against House Vance three years ago—not one dramatic act of destruction, but a thousand small ones. A contract here. A credit there. A recommendation letter that arrived three days late. By the time anyone noticed the pattern, the pieces were already gone.

“The requisition records,” Roan said. “I’m going to need someone with access to the administrative archive.”

“You mean Mira.”

The name landed between them. Mira Vance. Roan’s mother, who worked in the Spire’s records division. She had access that most faculty didn’t, and she’d made it clear, in the specific way that mothers who worked at academies did, that using her position to benefit her son’s social politics wasn’t acceptable.

“She’s not going to like it.”

“She’s not going to like a lot of things.” Brendt shouldered his bag. “But she’ll do it anyway, because you’re her son, and because she hates the Ashfords more than she hates rule-breaking.”

That second part was true. Mira’s documented opinions on House Ashford’s business practices were … colorful.

“I’ll talk to her tonight. After dinner.”

“And if she says no?”

“Then I’ll find another way.”

Brendt studied him, the same look he’d been giving Roan since they were twelve—trying to find the line between creative problem-solving and self-destructive escalation. Whatever calculation he did, he apparently decided not to voice it.

“Fine. But I’m coming with you to the archives. If we’re doing this, we’re doing it together.”

Roan nodded. The partnership was useful. Brendt’s analytical skills complemented his own—where Roan saw angles and leverage points, Brendt saw structures and weaknesses. Together they were harder to dismiss.

“Training,” Roan said, already turning toward the western wing.

“Right. Training.” Brendt fell into step beside him. “Any word on the next trial?”

“Not yet.”

“But you’re preparing anyway.”

“Always.”

The training hall was half-full when they arrived. Warden Drogan stood near the far wall, arms crossed, watching a pair of second-year students run drills with an expression that suggested he was mentally composing a critique. The Iron Hand of Hale gleamed dully in the morning light, every joint and hinge visible.

He didn’t look at Roan as the younger boy entered. Didn’t acknowledge him at all.

That was unusual. Drogan typically had some comment—sharp or encouraging—depending on his mood. The silence felt deliberate.

Roan chose a mat near the center of the room, beginning his warm-up routine. Stretching, joint rotation, a series of basic forms designed to wake up muscle memory. His body responded cleanly; the residual soreness from the trial two days ago had faded to a dull awareness.

He was three forms into his sequence when Drogan finally moved.

The older man crossed the room in silence, stopping three feet from Roan’s mat. Close enough for a quiet conversation. Far enough that it looked like a casual check-in.

“Theon Mercer requested a meeting with the Headmaster this morning,” Drogan said, voice pitched low. “He’s claiming that your performance in the third trial was the result of unauthorized enhancement substances.”

Roan’s hands continued their pattern. “He has evidence?”

“He has a theory. He’s presenting it as a concern for institutional integrity.” Drogan’s prosthetic hand flexed, metal sliding against metal. “The Headmaster hasn’t scheduled a hearing yet. She’s giving him time to ‘build his case.’”

“How long?”

“Weeks, potentially. Long enough to create noise. Long enough to make people wonder.” Drogan’s eyes met his. “He’s not trying to win the case. He’s trying to win the narrative.”

A familiar ledger. The Ashfords had used the same tactic against the Vances three years ago—not accusations that would hold up in a formal review, but rumors that would hang in the air long enough to stain.

“I need to see the requisition records,” Roan said quietly. “Everything connected to the trial scoring. Supplies, materials, authorization chains.”

Drogan was silent for a moment. Then: “That’s not going to be enough.”

“What will be enough?”

“Proof that you didn’t take anything.” The older man’s voice dropped another notch. “The system they used to adjust the trial—they built it specifically to avoid creating records. The adjustments happen in the grading algorithm itself, not in the supply chain. Even if you find the requisition orders, they won’t show anything.”

Roan’s forms continued, but his mind was racing. An algorithm. Adjustments built into the grading system. That meant someone with access to the scoring parameters had made changes.

“Could the algorithm be audited?”

“By who? The people running the audit are the same people who benefit from the current system.” Drogan’s jaw tightened. “This is why no one fights them directly. They don’t fight fair. They fight procedural.”

Roan finished his sequence, coming to a stop in the final stance. His breathing was even, his mind anything but. They weren’t dealing with a single corruption event, but with a system designed to be corrupted without leaving traces.

“Can the algorithm be compared against historical norms?” Brendt asked. He’d appeared at some point during the conversation, his own training bag over one shoulder. “If the adjustments are statistical anomalies, that might constitute evidence.”

Drogan looked at Brendt with something that might’ve been approval. “Potentially. But you’d need access to both the current algorithm parameters and the historical baseline data. Both are restricted.”

“Then we need restricted access.”

“From who?”

Roan considered. The Headmaster wasn’t an option—she was bound by institutional protocol. The senior faculty were compromised or complicit. The administrative staff answered to people who answered to the Ashfords.

Then he thought of the one person who had both access and motivation.

“Selene Ardent. She was on the examination committee. She would have seen the grading parameters.”

Drogan’s expression shifted. “House Ardent has its own arrangements with House Ashford.”

“Arrangements can be renegotiated.” Roan moved to his next form, the rhythm giving him space to think. “Selene doesn’t like being in debt. Right now, she owes me for the trial.”

“She owes you for not exposing what she was doing with the scoring data during the first trial.”

“Which means she’ll want to settle the debt before it becomes leverage.” Roan completed the form, pausing in the stance. “I’m not going to expose her. I’m going to ask for help. The difference is clear.”

“Is it?”

“To me.” Roan met Drogan’s gaze. “To her, it depends on how I frame the request.”

The older man studied him. Then he stepped back. “Training in two hours. Bring your sword.”

Roan nodded, watching Drogan move away. The request for training after the conversation wasn’t coincidental. Drogan was a man of patterns, and those patterns included using physical exertion to burn off mental tension.

Roan understood the impulse. He also understood that some decisions couldn’t wait.

He finished his warm-up, then moved to the equipment racks to begin his blade work. Sidewound’s weight was familiar now, a comfort more than a novelty. He ran through his basic forms, motion smooth, control precise. He’d learned the stances before he could read. Aldric, on kitchen tile, showing foot placement in a voice that pretended to be patient.

Beside him, Brendt had started his own routine with a pair of practice weights. They didn’t speak. Brendt adjusted his grip as Roan shifted stance. No words. No eye contact. A conversation the watchers couldn’t hear.

Thirty minutes in, Selene Ardent entered the training hall.

She was alone, which was unusual. The senior student typically traveled with a small entourage, but today she walked through the door with a specific destination, her eyes scanning until they found Roan.

She didn’t approach immediately. Instead, she chose a mat on the opposite side of the room, beginning her own warm-up with the mechanical efficiency of someone who had trained since childhood. The Twin Embers weren’t at her hip today—only the inferior practice blades, glinting as she moved.

Fifteen minutes passed. Then thirty.

Roan finished his current sequence and moved to the water station near the eastern wall. Selene was three feet away, filling her own container. Two students getting water at the same time. Nothing notable.

“Theon Mercer filed a complaint this morning,” Selene said without looking at him, her voice pitched for his ears only. “About your trial performance.”

“I heard.”

“The Headmaster hasn’t scheduled a hearing yet. She’s waiting.”

“For what?”

“For him to build momentum. For the rumors to start spreading.” Selene capped her container. “Same playbook they used against your father. The complaint isn’t the weapon. The timing is. By the time anyone investigates, the narrative is already established.”

Roan processed that. Selene was offering information—a warning, maybe, or a peace offering. The question was why.

“I need access to the examination algorithm used for trial scoring.”

Selene’s hand paused on her container. “That’s restricted.”

“I know.”

“Accessing it without authorization would be a disciplinary offense. Criminal, depending on how you acquired the data.”

“I know that too.”

Selene turned to look at him fully, her expression carefully neutral. “Then what exactly are you asking me for?”

The question was a test. A check on his intentions, his preparation, his willingness to accept risk.

“I’m asking you to consider that the algorithm was modified during my trial. I’m asking you to consider that you were on the examination committee, and you might have noticed something that didn’t feel right. I’m asking you to confirm or deny that suspicion.”

“And if I confirm it?”

“Then we have common ground. And maybe common interests.”

Selene studied him. Her eyes, dark, calculating, moved across his face like she was searching for something—a tell, a weakness, a lie.

Whatever she found seemed to satisfy her.

“The algorithm was modified six hours before the trial began. The modification affected the scoring weights for three specific categories: strategic adaptation, environmental response, and resource efficiency. The official justification cited ‘updated pedagogical priorities.’ The actual effect was to reduce the maximum possible score for students who relied on non-standard approaches.”

Roan’s pulse quickened, but his expression stayed level. “And you noticed this.”

“I noticed that a grading algorithm was changed at the last minute without proper review. I noticed that the change affected categories that would have heavily penalized a student who used unconventional combat strategies.” Selene’s voice dropped another notch. “I noticed that the person who requested the modification was Theon Mercer.”

The Codex Shard logged the name.

[Rule violation: confirmed.]

Theon Mercer. Cyrus Ashford’s faction second. The man currently filing complaints about Roan’s “unauthorized enhancements.” He’d had access to modify the algorithm before anyone else arrived.

“You have proof?” Roan asked.

“I have a timestamp on the modification request. I have the authorization signature. I have the original algorithm parameters stored in my personal files because I’m someone who keeps records.” Selene’s lips curved slightly, not a smile. “I didn’t keep them for you. I kept them because I don’t trust anyone who changes rules at the last minute.”

“But you’re willing to share them now.”

“I’m willing to offer a trade.” Selene stepped back, her expression settling into something more guarded. “I give you the algorithm modification records. You use them to expose what Theon did. And when the investigation happens—when they start looking for who benefited from the modification—”

“They find Cyrus Ashford.”

“Exactly.” Selene picked up her practice blades. “I’m not interested in your vendetta, Vance. I’m not interested in being your ally or your enemy. But I am interested in a world where people can’t change the rules in the middle of the game.”

She walked away before he could respond, returning to her mat with the unhurried grace of someone who had already said everything she intended to say.

Roan stood by the water station. Selene had handed him exactly what he needed—documentation of deliberate manipulation, a clear chain of causality, a path to proving the complaint against him was retaliatory rather than genuine. But she’d also handed him a choice.

Use the evidence to expose Theon. Let the investigation fall where it may. Accept that the fallout might not stop at Theon—that it might reach Cyrus, and from Cyrus to House Ashford, and from House Ashford to everyone who’d ever benefited from their protection.

Or use it more surgically. Negotiate. Trade. Leverage what he knew to extract concessions rather than destruction.

Brendt appeared at his elbow, a fresh container of water in hand. “She has records.”

“She has records.”

“Are you going to use them?”

Roan drank. The water was cool, the taste familiar. “I need to think.”

“You’ve been thinking for the past thirty seconds. That’s not thinking. That’s stalling.”

“Smart observation.”

“Someone has to be.” Brendt’s voice carried the mix of exasperation and affection that had characterized their friendship for years. “The evidence is good, Roan. Maybe not enough to take down House Ashford directly, but enough to discredit Theon’s complaint and create enough noise that the next time they try something, people will be watching.”

“Or enough to make them escalate. Skip the subtle manipulation and go straight for something physical.”

“Is that worse?”

Roan considered. The Ashfords had killed his father through procedural strangulation—a thousand cuts disguised as legitimate actions. If they switched to direct violence, the danger would be higher, but the evidence would also be clearer. A knife attack left traces that a grading algorithm didn’t.

“I need to talk to my mother. And I need to think about what happens after.”

“After what?”

“After I stop being reactive.” Roan set down his container, picking up Sidewound from where it rested against the wall. “Right now they’re always one step ahead. They change the rules, I adapt. They adjust the algorithm, I find out why. They’re always defining the battlefield, and I’m always responding.”

“So change the battlefield.”

“I’m working on it.”

He returned to his training, the rhythm of the blade forms steadying his mind. Around him, the training hall continued its morning routine—students drilling, equipment clanking, Warden Drogan occasionally shouting corrections. Normal sounds. A world that didn’t know yet that the rules were about to change.

The evidence would come. The meeting with his mother would happen. The decision about how to use what Selene had given him would come after that.

But somewhere in the sequence, Roan needed to stop playing defense.

He just hadn’t figured out how yet.

Sidewound sang through the air, the edge catching light. The thought surfaced and dissolved, replaced by the immediate calculation of angles, timing, the precise distance between his current position and his next stance.

Arithmetic had no incentive to flatter him.

But sometimes the math was incomplete.

He finished the form, reset, began again. The blade moved. His mind worked.

By midday, the rumor had three legs.

The student channels carried it cleanest: a story about a Vance who allegedly took something he shouldn’t have, sourced from nowhere, repeated without question. By the second period, two faction-aligned tutors were quietly recommending students avoid being seen in study halls with him.

*@HouseAshfordPR* (verified, posted in the cycle’s quiet window): The administration’s review of trial methodology is welcomed. Concerns regarding student supplementation are taken seriously regardless of rank or origin.

*@ArenaTechWatch*: requesting raw scoring-algorithm hash from the third-trial cycle. Anyone with archive access can confirm whether a six-hour pre-trial parameter change is consistent with normal pedagogical revision frequency. Spoiler: it isn’t.

In the records division, Mira Vance opened a maintenance log that didn’t need maintenance, kept her face still, and waited for the system to remember that some clerks watched longer than others.

In the Warden’s office, Drogan flagged the Mercer complaint with a single character—*?*—and routed it nowhere.

Roan tracked the pieces. Under forty-eight hours before the narrative locked. Twelve before it left his control. He had counter-evidence, but deploying it now meant full Ashford retaliation. The arithmetic was clean: delay cost him the story; action cost him the element of surprise.

Across the room, Selene Ardent watched him train with an expression he couldn’t read. Couldn’t read it. Shouldn’t try. Not yet.
