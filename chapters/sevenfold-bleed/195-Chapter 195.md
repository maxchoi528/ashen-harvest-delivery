Chapter 195: Brendt's Recovery

Brendt's first training session after nearly dying lasted twelve minutes before he broke the equipment.

Not dramatically. Brendt didn't explode the dummy, shatter a wall, or perform any of the other things first-years imagined when they heard the phrase abnormal recovery. He stepped into a shield brace, absorbed a standard impact from a calibrated pressure arm, and drove the training post three inches backward despite the floor clamps being rated for a low Hardened striker.

The post made a sad metallic sound.

Brendt froze.

The instructor on duty looked up from her tablet.

Roan, standing beside the wall with a notebook, wrote down the time.

Twelve minutes, under a minute.

Brendt slowly lowered his shield. "That wasn't me."

"It was mostly you," Roan said.

"That's not better."

"It's more accurate."

The instructor, a square-shouldered woman named Pellis who had never forgiven Roan for submitting a requisition audit that found three redundant supervision fees in the training annex budget, walked over and examined the displaced post.

"Holloway," she said. "What exactly did you do?"

"Blocked."

"Try a longer answer."

"Blocked with surprise."

Pellis stared.

Brendt glanced at Roan. "Help."

"He blocked," Roan said.

"That isn't help."

"It's corroboration."

Pellis turned her stare on Roan. "Vance. Why are you here?"

"Medical observation."

"You aren't medical staff."

"No."

"Then leave."

"No."

Pellis closed her eyes for one second. "I am too underpaid for whatever this is."

"You should start with Finance."

"With whom?"

"Someone who isn't me."

Brendt made a faint choking sound that was laughter pretending to be pain.

Pellis looked between them, then at the post. "No more impact drills until I recalibrate. Holloway, sit down. Vance, stop taking notes in my gym. It makes everyone nervous."

"That may be because they make mistakes."

"Out."

Roan didn't leave.

Pellis considered forcing the issue, decided she liked the structural integrity of her morning, and stalked away to fetch a maintenance wand.

Brendt sat on a bench, shield across his knees. His face had gone pale.

Not from exertion.

Fear, then.

Good. Fear meant he understood enough not to celebrate.

The training annex around them was busy but not crowded. Most students had been reassigned to remote coursework after Vorath's incident, as if studying threat response slides in dorm rooms would protect them from grandmaster politics. The few present kept looking over. Brendt Holloway had been stabbed, hospitalized, declared stable, then returned to training faster than the rumors had finished arguing whether Roan had sacrificed a goat in the infirmary.

Rumors were inaccurate. No goats had been available.

Roan sat beside Brendt.

"Pain?"

"No."

"Stiffness?"

"Less than yesterday."

"Dizziness?"

"No."

"Hunger?"

Brendt hesitated. "Constant."

Roan wrote it down.

"Stop looking pleased."

"I'm not pleased."

"You have your corpse-dissection face."

"I don't dissect corpses."

"You have a face for it anyway."

Roan looked at him.

Brendt sighed. "Fine. My appetite is insane. I ate two breakfasts, three ration bars, and half a loaf of bread before training. I was going to pretend that was normal because you already have enough to worry about, which was stupid, because you notice when people breathe wrong."

"Yes."

"That's not an apology."

"Correct."

"Terrible friend."

Roan wrote: Increased caloric demand. Possible accelerated tissue repair. Strength output above pre-injury baseline.

Then he stopped.

Brendt watched his pen. "What?"

"Your scar."

Brendt's hand went to his side.

The wound that had nearly killed him ran beneath his ribs, sealed by Spire medical work, Roan's emergency intervention, and whatever strange aftereffect the Codex Shard had spilled during the rescue. The infirmary had insisted on a support wrap and limited movement.

Brendt lifted his training shirt enough to expose the bandage.

The bandage was loose.

Roan carefully peeled back one edge.

Underneath, the skin was closed.

Not fully healed. A red line remained, tender and raised. But it looked like a wound weeks into recovery, not days. The tissue around it had no grayness, no Rift contamination bloom, no inflammation beyond ordinary strain. Roan's spine warmed faintly.

[Resonant exposure echo detected.]

[Secondary biological correction: unstable.]

[Range: limited by repeated proximity.]

[Warning: non-lineage subjects may develop incompatible dependencies.]

Roan wrote the prompt word for word.

Brendt leaned over. "That looks bad."

"It's ambiguous."

"Roan."

"Fine. It's bad."

"Potentially?"

"You are healing faster because the Shard's resonance altered your body's correction response. It may be temporary. It may require proximity. It may create dependency. It may mark you to people who can sense Veyrn effects."

Brendt was quiet for a moment. He touched the bandage at his side, pressing lightly, testing the seal. Then he said, "So I'm infected with friendship."

Roan looked at him.

Brendt held up his hands. "Bad joke. I know. Panic response."

"Keep the panic. Improve the joke."

"Working on both." Brendt paused. "Roan. If I become a problem. For you. For the plan. You'll tell me?"

"Yes."

"Before you start treating me like a target?"

"I treat everyone like a target. It's how I keep them alive."

Brendt met his eyes, then nodded once. "Good. That's good enough."

Roan replaced the bandage and secured it properly. "We need controlled tests."

"Of course we do."

"Blood panel. Vitalis resonance scan. Caloric tracking. Sleep tracking. Distance test. Stress test."

"I regret waking up."

"You almost didn't."

Brendt's smile faded.

That had been too direct.

Roan knew it before the silence fully formed. He could correct with softer language. He could ignore it. He could explain that near-death statistics were useful because they clarified risk. All three options had costs.

He chose none.

Instead, he reached into his coat and handed Brendt a wrapped sandwich.

Brendt blinked. "What is this?"

"Food."

"I can see that. Why do you have it?"

"You said you were hungry."

"I said that thirty seconds ago."

"You ate two breakfasts and three ration bars before training. Prediction was easy."

Brendt stared at the sandwich.

Then he took it without looking up.

"Thanks," he muttered.

Roan didn't say you're welcome. Brendt didn't need him to.

For several minutes, they sat on the bench while Pellis recalibrated the post and pretended not to glance at them every few seconds. Students whispered near the far wall. One took out a phone, saw Roan looking, and put it away with impressive survival instinct.

Social consequence: Brendt's recovery would spread.

Enemy consequence: anyone watching Roan's circle would notice abnormal ally resilience.

Rule question: Does resonance affect only those Roan actively inverts near, or those emotionally and operationally linked?

He disliked that second phrasing. Emotional links were imprecise. Operational links were better. Brendt had been near Roan during multiple inversions, including high-output emergency healing, Crimson Wake exposure, and Codex flares. That was enough without invoking sentiment.

.

"You're doing the face again," Brendt said around the sandwich.

"I have one face."

"No. You have several terrible ones. That one means you're turning me into a chart."

"You are already a chart."

"I hate that this is reassuring."

Roan clicked the pen once. "You can opt out."

Brendt stopped chewing.

"Of tests?"

"Of proximity. Of involvement. Of being on the board. Vorath has already targeted people near me. You were stabbed once. The Shard may have made you more visible. Leaving is rational."

Brendt looked at him for a long time.

The annex noise seemed to recede: metal strikes, instructor commands, the buzz of ward arrays. Snow pressed against the high windows in pale smears.

Finally, Brendt said, "Did you rehearse that?"

"No."

"Good. It was awful."

Roan waited.

Brendt set the sandwich down on his knee. "You think giving me the exit makes this cleaner. It doesn't. It just lets you write down that you offered."

That was uncomfortably close to correct.

"Choice matters," Roan said.

"Yes. Mine too." Brendt tapped the shield across his knees. "I know I'm weaker than you. I know Drogan could kill me with a spoon. I know Vorath wouldn't notice if I died unless the paperwork annoyed him. I am not confused about the board."

Roan said nothing.

"But I also know what happens when you decide you're the only acceptable casualty. You stop sleeping. You stop eating unless someone makes it tactically inconvenient. You start using words like manageable. Then eventually someone has to drag you out of a hole, and I'd rather be close enough to complain while doing it."

Sentimental. Worse, it was quantifiable.

Roan looked down at his notebook.

Sleep Compression Loss Audit: Brendt's empty bed still wrong.

Good. Still wrong.

"You may become dependent on the resonance," Roan said.

"Then we test it."

"It may make you a target."

"Already was."

"It may alter you."

Brendt shrugged. "Black Spire was doing that anyway."

Roan stared at him.

Brendt picked up the sandwich again. "What? That was decent."

"Adequate."

"High praise."

Pellis returned before Roan could answer. "Post recalibrated. Holloway, you aren't hitting anything until I run a scan."

"That's wise," Brendt said.

Pellis narrowed her eyes. "I hate when students become reasonable after making my job harder."

"I can be unreasonable if that helps."

"Don't."

Roan stood. "I'll assist with scan parameters."

"No, you won't."

"Your default scan will miss low-grade Veyrn resonance."

Pellis stopped.

She looked at Brendt.

Then at Roan.

Then very carefully at the cameras in the corners of the annex.

"My office," she said.

Good.

Pellis understood the implication without him finishing. Authority recognized the category. He logged the calibration. Useful. Dangerous.

- break -

Pellis's office had three chairs, two filing cabinets, and a window overlooking the annex floor. It also had privacy wards built into the doorframe, better than a midlevel instructor should've been able to afford.

Roan noted them.

Pellis noticed him noting them and scowled.

"Family gift," she said.

"I didn't ask."

"You were going to."

"Eventually."

She sat behind her desk and pulled up a diagnostic panel. "Start talking. Use small words only when necessary."

Roan gave her a controlled version.

Brendt suffered a contaminated wound. Emergency intervention involved nonstandard Vitalis inversion. Since recovery, he demonstrated accelerated tissue repair, elevated appetite, and output deviation. The phenomenon might be resonance-based and might interact with unknown lineage effects. They needed scans that didn't automatically classify all anomalies as Rift taint and trigger quarantine protocols.

He didn't mention Codex Shard.

He didn't mention Veyrn lineage beyond the word Pellis already reacted to. He didn't mention Vorath's hunt field.

Pellis listened without interrupting.

That improved Roan's opinion of her by a measurable amount.

When he finished, she leaned back.

"You understand that if I run a private scan and fail to report a dangerous contamination, I lose my license. My pension. My skin, depending on what this is."

"Yes."

"You also understand that if I run an official scan and the system flags him, Sentinel locks him in a white room for six months."

Brendt went pale.

"Yes," Roan said.

Pellis tapped one finger on the desk. "Why come to me?"

"You hate administrative incompetence more than student rumors."

"Flattery?"

"Classification."

"You are an unpleasant child."

"Yes."

She stared at him, then laughed once. "Fine. We do a manual scan. No network upload. If he's contagious, cursed, possessed, or growing secondary organs, I report it."

Brendt looked down at his side. "Secondary organs?"

"You'd be surprised," Pellis said.

"I would like not to be."

The scan took forty minutes.

Pellis used three devices and one old technique involving a silver thread, a shallow cut on Brendt's thumb, and a glass plate that fogged when placed over his pulse. Roan watched every step. Pellis allowed it after threatening to staple his hand to the desk if he corrected her twice.

He corrected her once.

The results were strange.

Not catastrophic. Strange.

Brendt's Vitalis circulation had developed a faint secondary rhythm around the injury site. It didn't match Roan's signature. It didn't match standard healing acceleration. It wasn't Rift taint. The closest analog in Pellis's database was adaptive marrow correction seen in rare high-tier body refiners after surviving repeated near-fatal trauma.

Brendt wasn't a rare high-tier body refiner.

He was Brendt.

"So," Brendt said, staring at the panel. "Am I dying?"

Pellis considered. "Not from this today."

"That's a terrible answer."

"Medicine is mostly terrible answers with invoices."

Roan studied the rhythm trace.

The secondary pattern pulsed every seven beats.

Roan's breast pocket went cold. The Codex Shard, reacting to something it hadn't shown before. Not a warning. A recognition. A number that matched something in its buried architecture. He kept his expression still and filed the observation for later analysis.

Pellis noticed his focus. "You know something."

"I know it isn't spreading actively."

"That's not the same as safe."

"No."

"Can you reverse it?"

Roan looked at Brendt.

Brendt looked back.

The question had too many unknowns. The Shard's cold presence was a new variable. Reversing a resonance he hadn't intentionally created might remove the healing benefit. It might destabilize Brendt's recovery. It might flare the Codex loud enough for Vorath's masked anchor to notice. It might reveal to the Shard that external subjects could be edited, which wasn't a category Roan wanted encouraged.

"Not now," he said.

Brendt nodded immediately.

Trust. Inconvenient. Useful. Heavy.

Pellis printed the results on paper, then deleted the local file. "I saw nothing dangerous enough to report. Yet. Holloway eats more, sleeps eight hours minimum, no high-impact drills without me present. Vance, you stop experimenting on your friends in ways that make me accessory to treason."

"I didn't experiment."

"You did something."

"I prevented death."

Pellis looked at Brendt's scar, then at Roan. Her expression shifted.

"Fine," she said. "Do less of that near my paperwork."

Roan took the paper scan results.

Resource gain: Brendt's survival improved, manual scan data acquired, Pellis partially recruited by necessity.

Risk gain: seven-beat resonance, possible dependence, potential external detection.

Brendt stood slowly and picked up his shield.

"Food?" Roan asked.

"Starving," Brendt said.

"Good."

"That's still weird to hear."

They left Pellis's office and returned to the annex floor.

The displaced post had been reclamped. Students pretended not to stare. One failed.

Brendt noticed.

For the first time since waking in the infirmary, he smiled with teeth.

Not Roan's kind of smile. Warmer. Dumber. More dangerous than people gave it credit for.

"Maybe," Brendt said quietly, "we test something light."

Roan looked at him.

"No impact drills," Brendt added. "Just footwork."

"Five minutes."

"Ten."

"Six."

"Eight."

"Seven."

"Deal."

Roan wrote down the negotiation result.

Seven minutes later, Brendt completed a shield transition pattern that had taken him half an hour before the stabbing.

He didn't notice at first.

Roan did.

So did Pellis from behind the office glass.

So did the training annex cameras.

By evening, the campus forums had a new thread.

BRANDT HOLLOWAY IS BACK AND MOVING WRONG.

The first comment read: Vance's team doesn't stay dead properly.

The second comment was a slowed clip of Brendt's shield transition, annotated by someone with too much time and a tolerable eye. The poster had circled Brendt's hip alignment, the timing of his brace, and the way the training post shifted before the strike fully landed. Half the replies accused the clip of being edited. The other half began arguing whether Roan had access to illegal recovery draughts.

By the thirty-seventh comment, someone mentioned Greel.

(Comment 38: "is that the guy from the fight video?"
Comment 39: "no that's the roommate. wait. is he dating vance?"
Comment 40: "irrelevant. look at the hip transition.")
By the forty-second, someone pointed out that every person who had tried to hurt Roan recently had either lost status, lost money, or been publicly humiliated.

(Comment 58: "this is getting weird."
Comment 59: "the lighting in the annex is terrible.")
By the sixty-first, a user with a blank profile wrote: People near Vance become abnormal. Track the roommate.

Roan copied the username.

There.

A signal, not proof of an enemy or even proof of competence. Ordinary students gossiped about duels and injuries, plus whether Brendt's recovery made him dateable, which was a category Roan didn't intend to examine. Watchers used verbs like track.

He sent the username to Ines with a single instruction: Price the curiosity.

Her response: Already did. Cheap account. Expensive route. Someone is laundering attention.

Good.

The rumor had become bait faster than expected. That was useful, though not free. Brendt was the bait whether they liked it or not. Better to control the hook.

Roan saved the thread.

Then he made Brendt eat in public.

Brendt objected for the first three minutes. He objected on tactical grounds, social grounds, nutritional grounds, and finally on the general principle that being watched while eating a third lunch made him feel like a livestock exhibit. Roan listened to all objections, assigned them low operational value, and bought him a tray large enough that the cashier looked personally invested in the outcome.

The Black Spire dining hall hadn't recovered from the past week. Tables that usually hosted House cliques, club recruiters, and students pretending not to revise for exams now held clusters of people whispering over phones. News clips of the café incident had been banned on campus networks, which meant everyone had seen them. Kael's public defeat still sat on the forums like a corpse nobody could move. Vorath's visit had upgraded gossip into geopolitical stress.

Brendt walking in alive and upright, hungry enough to threaten the kitchen supply chain, didn't help.

Heads turned.

Roan counted them.

Twenty-three immediate observers. Six recording attempts abandoned after he looked in their direction. Three House students from minor northern families. One medical-track second year who watched Brendt's gait instead of his face. Two people trying too hard not to look.

Those two mattered.

Ines slid into the seat across from them without asking. She placed a small paper envelope beside Roan's tray.

"Your laundering route," she said. "The forum account passed through a student proxy, then a commercial privacy relay, then a server owned by a shell that buys equipment from a Greel-friendly supplier. Very boring. Very expensive."

Brendt paused with a fork halfway to his mouth. "Can we have one meal where nobody says Greel?"

"No," Roan and Ines said together.

Brendt put the fork down. "That was disturbing."

Ines glanced at his tray. "Are you eating for four?"

"Apparently I'm a medical event."

"Congratulations. Those bill well."

Roan opened the envelope. Inside were three names, two probable aliases, and a map of attention routes on campus. Ines had marked one node in red: a logistics aide assigned to the visiting-student office. The same office that had processed Kael Greel's transfer papers.

Good.

The bait had already pulled a thread from the old net.

"We don't confront," Roan said.

Ines leaned back. "Obviously. We feed."

Brendt closed his eyes. "I dislike being the verb in that sentence."

"You aren't the verb," Roan said. "You are the hook."

"Worse."

"More accurate."

Ines took one of Brendt's rolls. He was too busy looking betrayed to stop her. "What do we feed them?"

Roan looked across the hall at the two students trying not to look. "Not truth. Not lies. A shape. Brendt is recovering because of an emergency Spire procedure. Dangerous, expensive, not repeatable, and under Pellis's authority. Anyone pursuing it must go through administrative channels."

Ines smiled. "Make them chase paperwork. Cruel."

"Efficient."

Brendt pointed his fork at Roan. "You are weaponizing bureaucracy around my spleen."

"Your spleen is intact."

"Not the point."

"It often is."

By the end of the meal, Brendt had eaten everything, three students had messaged medical contacts, and Ines had stolen enough bread to qualify as organized crime.

Roan left the dining hall with two new watchers identified and one rumor pointed toward a locked administrative door.

That was acceptable yield for lunch.

Pellis messaged twelve minutes later.

If you are using my authority as bait, I will break your fingers in alphabetical order.

Roan considered the logistics of alphabetical finger-breaking, decided Pellis would invent a system, and replied: The bait is bureaucracy. Your authority is the shield.

Her response arrived almost instantly.

I hate that distinction. Send me the thread.

Good. Pellis understood faster than most instructors and complained more honestly than most administrators. Both traits were useful. Roan forwarded the visible thread, not Ines's route map. Pellis didn't need that yet. Giving allies partial information wasn't distrust by default. It was compartmentalization. Full information made people feel respected right up until an enemy peeled it out of them.

Brendt read the exchange over his shoulder. "She likes us."

"No."

"She likes me."

"Possibly."

"I'll take it."

Roan looked at him. "You understand this means more tests."

"I assumed friendship infection came with follow-up appointments."

"Stop calling it that."

"Make me a better name."

Roan thought for a moment. "Secondary correction resonance."

Brendt stared. "Friendship infection it is."

"If you write that on any medical form, I let Pellis sedate you."

"She'd enjoy that too much."

"Probably."

Brendt leaned back with the expression of a man trying to enjoy being alive without thinking too hard about the mechanism. Roan let him have six seconds. Six seconds was generous. On the seventh, he slid a second ration bar across the table.

Brendt looked at it, then at him. "You had that ready."

"Yes."

"Planning for my hunger isn't the same as emotional availability, but it's close enough today."

Roan considered disputing that. Then he decided the return would be poor.

He wrote one final note instead: Humor response intact. That mattered more than Brendt would appreciate and less than Roan preferred. Another external marker to track if the resonance began changing personality rather than tissue.

Reputation consequence: visible.

Enemy consequence: imminent.

He fed Brendt another sandwich. Brendt took it. His sleeve pulled back. For half a second, against the pale skin of his wrist, a vein shimmered silver.

Roan froze.

"What?" Brendt asked.

"Nothing."

A new note in the mental file. Unstable resonance manifestation. The echo was deepening.

His phone buzzed. Ines. Query on Pellis's offline log. Four hours before someone shadows your next move.

He fed Brendt a third sandwich and began adjusting the timeline.
