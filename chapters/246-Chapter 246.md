The letter crumbled in Kael's fist. He stood in the shadow of a steamwork foundry, smoke billowing from pipes that jutted like mechanical bones against the gray sky, and read the words again.
Lyra's hand. The seal of the Arcane Spire. But the message was wrong.
*Meet me at the Hollows. Midnight. Come alone. What I'm about to tell you changes everything.*
He'd known Lyra for three years. She wrote like a soldier, clipped and precise. This message read like something else—calculated, the kind of language a handler used when briefing an asset.
Kael dropped the letter into the coal chute beside him and watched it disappear into the furnace below. The foundry's roar swallowed the paper's final protest.
"You're going to meet her anyway."
Sera emerged from the alley mouth, her Mortician's Guild coat buttoned to the throat despite the summer heat. She carried a leather satchel that clinked with glass vials—soul-essence, probably, though Kael didn't ask. The Mortician's Guild had been unusually generous with their recent payments.
"You've been reading my correspondence now?" Kael kept his voice flat.
"You left it on the table at the boarding house." Sera's dark eyes never wavered. "I'm not criticizing. I'm warning you. Lyra hasn't been herself for weeks. Ask yourself why she's so eager to meet in the Hollows—a place the city pretends doesn't exist."
The Hollows ran beneath Thornwick's old quarter, a network of tunnels and flooded basements where the Awakened conducted business they didn't want witnessed. Kael had harvested there before. The corpses were fresh, at least.
"You know something."
"I know the cult has people everywhere." Sera moved closer, lowering her voice. "I know Zarek's network extends into the Arcane Spire. And I know Lyra trained in the Shadow Court's methods before she joined the Orders. Those methods don't disappear just because someone changes employers."
Kael's hand drifted to the Soul Reaver's mark on his chest—hidden beneath his shirt, but always present. The cold awareness of it pulsed like a second heartbeat.
"You're suggesting she's been compromised."
"I'm suggesting you be careful." Sera opened her satchel, withdrew a small vial of amber liquid. "Take this. If things go wrong—if you feel your spiritual reserves draining—drink it. It won't save you from a stage-five strike, but it might buy you time to run."
Stage five. Ascendant. Lyra was barely stage three.
"You think she's working with someone else."
"I think secrets are the currency of this city, and everyone has a price." Sera pressed the vial into his palm. "Even people we trust. Especially them."
---
The Hollows swallowed sound. Kael descended the iron ladder, each rung slick with condensation, until the street-level noise faded to nothing. Below, the tunnel stretched in both directions, lit by phosphorescent moss that clung to the walls in sickly patches.
No footsteps. No voices. Just the drip of water from somewhere ahead.
Kael moved forward, keeping his footsteps light. The Soul Reaver's mark warmed against his chest—not a warning, but an anticipation. The dead congregated in places like this, their residual energy pooling in the darkness. He could feel them now, a faint pressure at the edges of his perception. Former merchants. Whores. Awakened who had died on business they shouldn't have been conducting.
The tunnel opened into a vaulted chamber, its ceiling lost in shadow. Crates lined the walls—smuggler's stock, probably, though Kael didn't stop to examine them. At the chamber's center, a single lantern burned on a makeshift table.
Lyra sat beside it. Her Order of Dawn coat was missing, replaced by a simple gray shirt and trousers. No visible weapons, though Kael knew better than to assume that meant she was unarmed.
"You came." Lyra rose, her face half-lit by the lantern's glow. "I wasn't sure you would."
"Your letter was strange." Kael stopped ten paces away, outside easy striking distance. "You don't write like that."
"I've been learning to write like other people. It's useful." Lyra gestured to a second chair across from her. "Sit. Please. I didn't ask you here to fight."
"Then why?"
"Because I found something. Something about the Soul Reaver."
Kael's hand twitched toward his chest, toward the mark. "What kind of something?"
"Sit down and I'll show you."
He sat. The chair was cold, damp wood that seeped through his trousers. Lyra reached into her shirt and withdrew a folded paper—old, the edges brown with age. She unfolded it on the table between them.
It was a sketch. A man, naked, his body covered in lines that could have been tattoos or scars. The lines converged on his chest, forming a pattern Kael recognized instantly.
The Soul Reaver's mark.
"Where did you get this?" His voice came out hoarse.
"The Shadow Court's archives. Before I left." Lyra traced a finger along the lines. "This is the first recorded Soul Reaver. The original. We don't know his name—everything about him was erased from the Orders' records after the Abyssal Wars. But the Shadow Court kept copies. They always keep copies."
"Why?"
"Because they want to understand what he was." Lyra looked up, meeting his eyes. "And what he became. Kael, the first Soul Reaver didn't just harvest abilities from the dead. He harvested something else. Something that let him walk between the Material and the Abyss without being destroyed."
The lantern flickered. Kael became aware of the weight of the earth above him, the miles of stone and pipe and foundation separating this chamber from the sky.
"You're saying I could do that."
"I'm saying you might already be doing it. Without knowing." Lyra leaned forward. "The Soul Reaver isn't just an ability. It's a door. And doors swing both ways."
A sound echoed from the tunnel behind him. Footsteps—not one set, but several. Moving in unison.
Kael was on his feet before he consciously decided to move. Lyra rose too, but slower, her hand drifting toward her belt.
"Lyra."
"I didn't tell them you were here." Her voice was steady, but Kael caught the slight widening of her eyes. "I swear I didn't."
"Then who did?"
The footsteps stopped at the chamber's entrance. Phosphorescent light spilled from the tunnel, casting long shadows across the floor. Three figures stepped into the lantern's reach.
Kael recognized the first: a man in black armor, his face hidden behind a silver mask. The Order of Dawn's enforcement unit. But the symbol on his pauldron wasn't the sun—it was a circle bisected by a vertical line.
The Shadow Court's sigil.
"You came alone." The masked man's voice was flat, mechanical. "Good. That makes this simpler."
"You were following her." Kael's mind raced. "You used Lyra's meeting to locate me."
"Lyra has been useful." The masked man tilted his head. "Her access to the Order of Dawn's archives gave us valuable intelligence. But her usefulness has limits. You, But, are something else entirely."
"What do you want?"
"We want what you've been carrying since you woke up on that execution platform." The masked man took a step forward. "The Soul Reaver. It's not supposed to exist anymore. The last one was supposed to have died three hundred years ago. But here you are. And we need to understand how."
Lyra drew her blade. "Varek, stand down. He's under the protection of the Order of Dawn."
"The Order of Dawn doesn't know what's happening in their own city." The masked man—Varek—gestured to his companions. They wore no masks, but their faces were blank, their eyes empty. Cultists, probably. Devoted. "Zarek's ritual weakened more than just Alpha-7. The seal network is failing across the continent. The Shadow Court has been preparing for this moment for decades. And you, Kael Ashborn, are the key to everything."
"I'm not doing anything for the Shadow Court."
"You misunderstand." Varek raised a hand. "This isn't a negotiation. Take him."
The cultists moved. Kael's hand found the Soul Reaver's mark, pressed against his chest, and the power inside him responded—eager, hungry.
The first cultist lunged. Kael sidestepped, caught the man's arm, and felt the cold rush of harvest as his fingers closed on the cultist's wrist.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Combat Reflexes from the remains. Memory Fragment acquired.]
The cultist's body went limp, dropping like a puppet with severed strings. Kael didn't stop to analyze—he pulled the corpse's spiritual energy into himself, felt the new ability slot into place like a key in a lock.
Combat Reflexes. Stage-two enhancement. Nothing spectacular, but useful.
The second cultist hesitated. Varek's masked face betrayed nothing.
"Interesting," he said. "You harvested him without killing him first. That shouldn't be possible."
Kael didn't answer. He was already moving, closing the distance before the second cultist could recover his nerve. The man's fist swung toward Kael's head—slow, predictable, easily dodged. Kael ducked under the blow, drove his palm into the cultist's solar plexus, and harvested again.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Pain Suppression from the remains. Spiritual Resistance +2 acquired.]
The third cultist turned to run. Kael let him go, attention fixed on Varek.
"Where's Lyra?"
Varek hadn't moved. The lantern's light played across his silver mask, hiding his expression. "She ran. Toward the eastern tunnel. We didn't stop her."
"Why?"
"Because she's more useful to us free than captured. She doesn't know it yet, but she'll come around. They always do, when they realize what we're offering." Varek's head tilted again, that strange mechanical gesture. "You should run too. The Hollows have more than just us. And you're low on time."
Kael felt the harvest energy coursing through him—new abilities, new power, but also a growing awareness of the walls pressing in. The Hollows were unstable. If Varek wanted to bring this section down, he could.
"You know something about the Soul Reaver. About the original."
"I know it didn't die." Varek's voice dropped. "I know it became something else. And I know you're standing on the threshold of the same transformation. The question is whether you cross it willingly or dragged across."
The lantern went out.
In the darkness, Kael heard Varek's footsteps retreating. Heard, too, the distant rumble of the ceiling beginning to crack.
He ran.
---
The eastern tunnel was exactly where Varek had said. Kael found it by following the sound of splashing water, his boots splashing through ankle-deep runoff that stank of rust and something fouler.
Lyra was waiting at the junction, her blade drawn, her breathing ragged.
"Varek let me go."
"I know." Kael kept moving, not slowing. "We need to get out of here. Now."
"What did he tell you?"
"That the Soul Reaver was supposed to be extinct. That they're preparing for something. And that the seal network is failing."
Lyra fell into step beside him. Her face was pale in the phosphorescent glow, but her voice was steady. "The Shadow Court has been saying for years that the seals were weakening. The Orders dismissed it as propaganda. But if Zarek's ritual was a test—"
"Then there are going to be more tests." Kael ducked under a collapsed support beam, feeling the wood scrape his shoulder. "More rituals. More attempts to break through."
"And you're at the center of it. Because whatever the Soul Reaver is, they need it."
They reached the ladder. Kael climbed first, testing each rung before committing his weight. Above him, a square of gray sky appeared—the entrance to an abandoned warehouse on the city's eastern edge.
He hauled himself up, then turned to help Lyra. As she emerged, he noticed something: the seal on her wrist, the Order of Dawn's mark of membership, had been cut away. A ragged scar remained, the skin still pink.
"When did that happen?"
Lyra glanced down, then looked away. "Varek. After I met with him last week. He said the Order of Dawn couldn't protect me anymore. That I needed to make a choice."
"What choice?"
"Between who I was and who I need to become." She met his eyes. "I chose to stay. But I couldn't keep wearing their symbol. Not after what I learned."
"What did you learn?"
Lyra hesitated. For a moment, Kael thought she might tell him. But instead she said: "Later. Right now we need to move. The Shadow Court won't wait long before trying again."
They moved through the warehouse, past rusted machinery and piles of debris that had accumulated over decades of neglect. Outside, the city's evening bells were ringing—the seven o'clock chime, marking the end of the working day.
Kael stopped at the warehouse door, scanning the street. Market stalls were being packed away. Workers trudged toward their homes, too tired to notice two figures emerging from a condemned building.
"Where were you really tonight?" he asked.
Lyra's expression flickered. "What do you mean?"
"The letter. The meeting. If the Shadow Court wasn't behind it, then what was the real purpose?"
For a long moment, she didn't answer. Then: "I wanted to warn you. About what's coming. About what you're going to have to become."
"And what's that?"
"Something more than human. Something that can stand against what's on the other side of the Veil." Lyra's voice dropped. "The original Soul Reaver didn't just harvest abilities. He harvested the power of the Entities themselves. He became a god-killer, Kael. And that's what's going to be required if we want to survive what's coming."
Kael absorbed this. The weight of it pressed down on him, heavier than the earth they'd just escaped.
"You've known this for a while."
"Yes."
"And you didn't tell me."
"Because you weren't ready." Lyra's dark eyes held his. "You're ready now. I can see it in how you fight. How you harvest. The Soul Reaver is waking up inside you, and it needs to be fed. Soon. More than just corpses in alleys. More than just cultists who attack you in tunnels."
"What are you suggesting?"
"I'm suggesting there's a place. A tomb beneath the Sepulcher, sealed three hundred years ago. Inside are the remains of the first Soul Reaver—and the power that made him." Lyra stepped closer. "If you want to understand what you are, Kael, that's where you'll find the answers."
The evening bells faded. Somewhere in the distance, a steamwhistle shrieked—the signal for Thornwick's factories to begin their night shift.
"Take me there."
"Not yet. You need to be stronger. More prepared. The tomb is guarded by things that haven't been disturbed in centuries. If you go in unprepared, you'll die."
"Then I'll get stronger."
"How?"
Kael's hand found the Soul Reaver's mark again. The cold pulse of it answered his question better than any words could.
"By harvesting everything I can. Until I'm ready."
Lyra studied him for a moment, then nodded. "There's a place. The Veiled Market. Down in the Ironbottom district. They sell more than just soul-essence there. They sell information. Contacts. Corpses of Awakened who died on missions the Orders don't want anyone to know about."
"How do I find them?"
"You ask for the Broker. Tell them you're looking for 'fresh product.'" Lyra's mouth twisted. "And Kael? Don't trust anyone. Not the Orders. Not the Shadow Court. Not even me. Everyone in this city is working an angle. Including me."
"Including you?"
"I told you about the cut on my wrist. What I didn't tell you is that I made a deal. With Varek. With the Shadow Court." She looked away. "They gave me something. Something I needed. And in exchange, I've been feeding them information. About the Orders. About the seal network. About you."
The words hung in the air between them. Kael felt the Soul Reaver's mark burning against his chest, felt the new abilities he'd harvested thrumming with potential.
"How long?"
"Since before we met. I was already compromised when I joined the Order of Dawn." Lyra's voice was barely a whisper. "My sister was taken by the Shadow Court two years ago. They offered to release her if I cooperated. What choice did I have?"
"Your sister. Is she alive?"
"I don't know. They stopped sending proof of life six months ago." Lyra's jaw tightened. "That's why I need what the Shadow Court has. Information about the tomb. About the original Soul Reaver. Because whatever power he had, it might be enough to get her back. Or at least to find out what happened to her."
Kael processed this. A spy. A double agent. Someone whose loyalty was fractured across multiple factions, none of which she fully trusted.
"Does this change things?" she asked.
"Does it change what?"
"Between us. Whether you still—"
"You lied to me. For two years." Kael cut her off. "That matters. But so does the fact that you told me now. Before I walked into a trap. Before Varek had a chance to finish what he started."
"That's not an answer."
"No. It's not." He started walking, heading toward the main street where traffic was still flowing. "But I'm not ready to give one yet. What I will say is this: if your sister is alive, if the Shadow Court has her, then we have leverage. They think they're using you, but you've been using them too. Now we need to figure out how to use that."
Lyra fell into step beside him. They walked in silence for half a block, weaving through the evening crowds.
"The Broker will ask for payment," she said finally. "Soul-essence or coin. The Orders' rates, but for information instead of supplies."
"I have essence from the cultists."
"Not enough. The Broker deals in high-value intelligence. You'll need more."
Kael thought of Sera. Of the Mortician's Guild and their unusually generous payments. Of the way she'd pressed that vial of essence into his palm, her dark eyes warning him about secrets.
Maybe it was time to have a different kind of conversation with her.
"What if we had another source?"
"The Mortician's Guild?" Lyra shook her head. "They control the legal soul trade. They won't risk their monopoly for underground intelligence."
"They might if the right pressure was applied."
"What kind of pressure?"
Kael didn't answer. Ahead, the street opened onto a broader avenue, lined with gas lamps and crowded with people heading home for the night. Normal people. People who had no idea that beneath their feet, the seal network was failing. That ancient powers were stirring. That a man who'd been executed three years ago was walking among them with a mark on his chest that shouldn't exist.
"Kael."
He stopped. Turned.
Lyra's face was unreadable in the lamplight. "Thank you. For not walking away."
"Don't thank me yet. We still have to survive long enough to find your sister. To stop whatever Zarek's planning. To figure out what the Soul Reaver is." He resumed walking. "And somewhere in there, I need to decide whether I can trust you. Or whether this is just another game."
"I understand."
"Do you?" Kael glanced at her. "Because from where I'm standing, everyone in this city is playing three sides against each other. The Orders. The Shadow Court. And whoever's pulling the strings that we haven't even seen yet. You're telling me you're on my side. But you're also telling me you made deals with the people who want to use me. How am I supposed to know where you stand?"
Lyra was quiet for a long moment. When she spoke, her voice was barely audible above the street noise.
"I stand where my sister is. Everything else—Orders, Shadow Court, you—that's just politics. She's the only thing that matters."
"And if helping me hurts her?"
"Then I'll choose her. Every time." Lyra met his eyes. "I can't promise you loyalty, Kael. I can't even promise you honesty. But I can promise you that my first priority is getting my sister back. And right now, that means keeping you alive. Because the Soul Reaver is the only weapon that might be strong enough to get her out of whatever the Shadow Court has planned."
The street emptied around them as they turned onto a quieter side road. Shadows pooled between the buildings, deep and watchful.
"We should split up," Lyra said. "The Shadow Court will be watching both of us now. Better to have two targets than one obvious pair."
"Where will you go?"
"Back to the Order of Dawn. I need to maintain my cover, at least for now. I'll dig into their archives, see what they know about the seal network's deterioration." She paused. "Tomorrow night. The Widow's Bridge, on the eastern canal. Midnight. I'll bring what I find."
"And if you're followed?"
"Then I'll find a way to lose them. I'm good at that." Lyra's mouth curved, a ghost of a smile. "Get some rest, Kael. The Veiled Market doesn't open until the second bell after midnight. You'll need your strength."
She melted into the shadows before he could respond. Kael watched her go, tracking the sound of her footsteps until they faded completely.
Alone now. The city hummed around him, indifferent to his presence. He could feel the Soul Reaver's mark pulsing against his chest, hungry, eager for the harvests to come.
Somewhere in this city, there was a tomb containing the remains of the first Soul Reaver. Somewhere, there were answers about what he was becoming. And somewhere in the shadows, the Shadow Court was watching, waiting to see what he would do next.
The first bell rang, marking nine o'clock. Three hours until the Veiled Market opened.
Three hours to find another source of payment.
Kael turned toward the Mortician's district, where Sera was waiting with secrets of her own.
Behind him, in the darkness of the alley he'd just passed, something shifted. A presence. A watching.
The watcher didn't follow. Not yet. But the eyes remained fixed on his retreating back, patient as stone, waiting for the moment to strike.
