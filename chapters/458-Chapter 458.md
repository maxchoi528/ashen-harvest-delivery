The blade of light carved through the cultist's chest, cleaving through bone and boiling muscle. The man crumpled without a sound, his knees striking the wet cobblestones with a sickening crunch. Kael stood over the body, the ambient heat of the skill dissipating from his fingertips as he dropped to a crouch. His fingers pressed against the cooling flesh of the cultist's neck. The skin was already losing its elasticity, turning waxy beneath his touch. The stench of voided bowels and sharp copper rose from the corpse, mixing with the damp, smog-choked air of the alley.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted a few days of residual life-force from the remains. Spiritual essence acquired."
The notification pulsed behind his eyes, a cold pressure against his consciousness. Kael barely registered it. Two years of execution had taught him to harvest without pause, without ceremony. His jaw clenched, his teeth grinding together as the soul-essence flowed into him. It slithered through his veins, filling gaps he hadn't realized were empty. The sensation was cold, alien, like swallowing river water in winter. His Divine senses expanded a fraction more, and the gaslit alley behind the Ironveil Tavern snapped into crystalline focus. He could hear the scrape of rat claws on brick three buildings down. He could smell the rotting vegetable matter beneath the grating to his left. The world sharpened to a cruel edge.
Three more cultists remained inside. Their spiritual signatures burned like candle flames against the night—weak, barely Awakened, but organized in a way that made his skin prickle. The hair on his forearms stood upright. This wasn't random violence. The wards carved into their forearms bore the sigil of the Faceless One, ridges of scarred flesh that oozed a faint, iridescent pus. The coordination suggested training. Drills repeated until obedience replaced thought.
Kael pulled his hand back and wiped the slick residue on his coat. The dark fabric absorbed the fluid without a mark. Blood didn't bother him anymore. The sticky warmth had become routine. What bothered him was the pattern.
The first cultist he'd harvested in Thornwick, days ago, had carried the same sigil. He remembered the way the man's flesh had twitched after death, the sigil glowing with a residual malice. So had the pair found dead in the drainage tunnels beneath the Merchant Quarter, their bodies bloated with sewer gas and dark magic. So had the Ascended Hunter who'd tried to take his head outside the Citadel walls, a massive man whose spiritual pressure had crushed the cobblestones around him before Kael had torn through his defenses. The city was crawling with Faceless One worshippers, and they were moving with purpose.
He needed to know why.
The back door of the Ironveil opened with a groan of wet wood. Kael slipped inside, hugging the shadows that clung to the establishment's corners like cobwebs. The tavern's main room was a wreck—overturned tables with splintered legs, shattered glass crunching beneath his boots, the sour smell of spilled ale mixing with the heavy, metallic scent of copper. His gaze swept the carnage until it landed on the body of the barkeep, throat opened ear to ear. The man's eyes stared at the ceiling, cloudy and vacant. Flies droned above the corpse, their wings catching the faint light that bled through the shuttered windows.
Dead for several hours at least. The cultists had used this place as a staging ground and left no witnesses.
"Stupid," Kael muttered. "Sloppy."
He crouched beside the barkeep and pressed his palm to the man's chest. The fabric of the man's shirt was stiff with dried blood. The harvest came easier with recent corpses, and the barkeep's memories poured into him like water through a cracked dam. They came in jagged fragments. The taste of cheap ale. The sound of the door crashing open. The flash of steel.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted fragmented memories. Information acquired."
The barkeep had seen three hooded figures enter demanding ale and silence. Had heard them speak of "the seal weakening" and "Zarek's promise." Had watched them leave with a leather satchel that bulged with something other than coin. The shape had been wrong—too angular, too rigid for currency. Then nothing, because the cultists had returned at midnight with steel and silence on their minds. The barkeep's final memory was the woman's scarred face, twisted in fervent ecstasy, as the blade opened his throat.
Kael filed the information away and stood. His knees cracked in the silence. Zarek. The name surfaced like a corpse from dark water—the same Zarek who'd framed him for the Merchant Guild massacre two years ago, whose machinations had put the noose around his neck. Kael had escaped that particular grave, but the name still tasted like ash. His throat tightened. His fingers curled into fists, knuckles whitening. Two years of hunting, and the thread finally led back to the man who'd started it all.
The cultists upstairs were his next target. He needed answers they possessed, and he intended to extract them the only way he knew how.
The stairs creaked under his weight as he climbed. Each step sent a dull ache through his calves. Three voices drifted down from above—two men and a woman, arguing about timing and the "Great Ritual." One of them mentioned the Order of Dawn's patrol schedules. Another cursed the Veiled Market for raising prices on sanctified bone-dust. The third, the woman, spoke of "the Shadow Court's eyes" and whether they could trust the broker who'd set this meeting.
Kael smiled. The Shadow Court. He'd heard whispers of that organization since his resurrection—the Orders' deniable blade, supposedly disbanded decades ago but still cutting throats in the dark. If the Shadow Court was involved with the Faceless One cultists, then this conspiracy ran deeper than random worshippers. The web extended into the foundations of the city itself.
He reached the landing and pushed through the door without knocking.
The room beyond was small, lit by a single lantern that cast more shadow than light. The flame guttered, throwing wild shapes across the walls. Two men flanked a woman at a rough wooden table, maps and documents spread before them. The parchment rustled in the draft from the open window. All three looked up at the same moment, hands going to weapons. The sound of steel clearing leather cut through the tension.
"Kael Ashborn." The woman recovered first, her eyes narrowing. She was older than the others, maybe forty, with silver threading her dark hair and a scar bisecting her left eyebrow. The scar tissue was raised, pale against her weathered skin. "We were told you'd been executed."
"Reports of my death were accurate," Kael replied, stepping fully into the room. The floorboards groaned beneath his weight. "I declined to stay dead."
The younger cultist on the left—barely twenty, baby-faced and terrified—lunged for the door. His sandals slipped on the worn wood. Kael caught him by the throat and threw him back into the room. The man crashed into the table, scattering papers and sending the lantern swaying. Hot wax splattered across the scattered maps. The older cultist drew a serrated blade coated in something that shimmered purple in the dim light. The coating caught the lantern's glow and seemed to drink it.
"Entity-touched steel," Kael observed. His voice was flat, clinical. "Expensive. Where'd you get funding like that?"
None of them answered. The woman flicked her wrist, and the sigil on her forearm blazed with sickly light. The air around her warped, bending inward. Kael felt the spiritual pressure roll toward him—a binding spell, meant to immobilize prey. The technique crashed against his Divine-stage defenses and shattered like glass against stone. The backlash sent the lantern flame guttering, throwing the room into momentary darkness.
"What are you?" the woman breathed.
"Someone with questions." Kael closed the distance between them in two strides. His boots hit the floor with heavy thuds. His hand found her throat before she could retreat, lifting her off the ground with casual strength. Her legs kicked, her boots scraping against the table's edge. "Who is Zarek? What's he planning for the Great Rift?"
The woman's face purpled as she clawed at his wrist. Her nails tore thin lines of skin, drawing pinpricks of blood. The younger cultist scrambled to his feet and bolted for the window. Kael caught him by the ankle and dragged him back, letting him scrabble uselessly against the floorboards. The boy's fingernails scraped bloody grooves in the wood.
"Kill him," the woman rasped. "Kill—"
Kael tightened his grip. The tendons in his forearm stood out like cords. "That won't happen. I'm going to walk out of this room with answers, and you're going to give them to me. The alternative is unpleasant."
The older cultist lunged, blade raised. Kael caught the strike on his forearm—the Entity-touched steel bit deep, carving through muscle and grating against bone. Blood welled black and thick, pooling in the crook of his elbow before dripping onto the floor. The impact sent a jolt of electricity up his arm. The man pressed the advantage, sawing at the wound, and for a moment Kael felt something dark stir beneath his skin.
The Faceless One's fragment.
It pulsed in response to the Entity-touched weapon, hungry, reaching. The wound in his arm throbbed with a cold heat. Images flooded his mind—a vast emptiness, a hunger that spanned millennia, a voice that whispered promises of power in exchange for obedience. The fragment wanted this blade. Wanted to consume the Entity's essence embedded in its steel. Kael's stomach lurched. His vision doubled, the room overlaying with glimpses of that endless void.
He slammed his elbow into the cultist's temple. Bone cracked. The man crumpled, and Kael turned back to the woman.
"The Faceless One doesn't make deals," he said quietly. His breath came in short, controlled bursts. "It consumes. Why would your master bargain with something that will devour him?"
The woman's eyes went wide. She stopped struggling, her expression shifting from fear to something else—recognition, maybe. Or resignation. Her limbs went slack in his grip.
"You're one of us," she whispered. "The fragment. I can see it in your eyes."
"I'm nothing like you."
"The fragment marks us all the same." Her voice steadied, gaining the cadence of true belief. The words tumbled out with rehearsed precision. "The Faceless One sees potential in certain souls. It plants pieces of itself, waiting for the moment of awakening. You're not a prisoner, Kael Ashborn. You're a vessel."
The words hit harder than the blade had. Kael's grip faltered for a fraction of a second—long enough for the woman to drive a hidden knife into his side. The blade sank deep between his ribs. She twisted, screaming words in a language that predated the Orders. The syllables tore at the air, each one a jagged sound that scraped against Kael's eardrums.
Kael's vision exploded with light. The Faceless One's fragment surged, responding to the summoning, and he was somewhere else—standing in a vast darkness where dozens of points of light burned like dying stars. The Great Rift's seals. He could see them all, each one connected by threads of golden energy that were fraying. Threads snapped one by one.
And standing at the center of the darkness was a figure made of absence. No face. No form. Just a void that consumed all light and thought. The air around it grew cold, impossibly cold, and Kael's breath crystallized in front of him.
"The vessel wakes," the Faceless One spoke without speaking. "The time approaches."
"I'm not your tool," Kael snarled. His fists clenched at his sides.
"You already are. The question is whether you serve willingly or not."
The darkness pulsed, and Kael felt the fragment inside him grow heavier, denser, as if preparing to birth something terrible. The weight settled in his chest like a stone. He reached for his Soul Reaver ability—the one power that had never failed him—and carved into the projection. The effort cost him. Blood vessels burst in his left eye.
The Faceless One recoiled. Actual surprise rippled through the void.
"You cannot harvest what is infinite," it hissed.
"Watch me."
Kael tore at the darkness, pulling fragments of the Entity's essence through his connection. The harvest notification screamed in his mind, over and over, as raw power flooded his reserves. The pressure built behind his eyes, threatening to split his skull. The Seals sight deepened. He could see the corruption threading through each one, the slow poison of millennia finally reaching critical mass.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted a fragment of Abyssal essence from the Faceless One's projection. Unique ability acquired: Veil Sight."
"Soul Harvest: You extracted corrupted seal-knowledge. The Great Rift's weaknesses are now visible to you."
Kael gasped as the vision shattered. He was back in the Ironveil's room, the woman's knife still buried in his side, her triumphant expression crumbling as she watched his wound close without his assistance. The flesh knitted, pushing the blade out with a wet sound. The knife clattered to the floorboards.
"What are you?" she demanded for the second time.
Kael pulled the blade from his flesh and let it clatter to the floor. "Someone who just learned that the Great Rift isn't going to hold for another year. The seal is already failing. Weeks, maybe. And your master knows it."
He caught her again by the throat, gentler this time. "The cult activity in Thornwick—it's not preparation. It's acceleration. Zarek is pushing the timeline because he knows something we don't."
The woman laughed, blood bubbling at the corner of her mouth. The sound was wet, broken. "You think you're the only one with fragments? You think you're special? The Faceless One marks dozens, hundreds. We're all pieces of the same whole. When the vessel completes, when the Great Rift shatters, we'll ascend together. And you'll either join us or be consumed like everything else."
"The Six Orders won't let that happen."
"The Six Orders are blind." Her laughter turned into a cough. "They've always been blind. They fight among themselves while the real enemy watches from between the cracks. Zarek gave them a common enemy—the Whisper King, a phantom they can unite against—and they never looked deeper. Never asked why the cult activity organized after two centuries of scattered worship."
Kael's mind raced. His pulse drummed in his ears. The Six Orders. He'd known they existed—everyone in Thornwick knew the names, the great powers that kept the Abyss at bay. But hearing this woman speak of them as broken, as blind, as bickering while true threats slipped past their notice—the words settled like ice water in his gut. His jaw tightened. His nails dug into his palms.
"Who is Zarek?" he pressed. "Really."
The woman smiled, blood staining her teeth. "He's the key. The first vessel. The one who walked into the Abyss and walked out with the Faceless One riding his soul. He's been preparing this for two hundred years. Every cultist you've killed, every seal you've seen failing, every whisper of the Whisper King—they're all his work. And now you're here, another fragment, another piece of his puzzle."
She leaned closer, her breath hot against his ear. The copper smell of blood washed over him. "He wanted you to wake up, Kael. Your execution, your resurrection—orchestrated. The Soul Reaver power you carry? A gift from the Faceless One. A leash dressed as a blade. Every corpse you harvest, every ability you steal, feeds the thing inside you. You think you're the predator. You're the host."
Kael dropped her. The woman crumpled, choking, and he stepped back, processing her words. His boots slipped on the blood-slicked floor. His gaze fell on the younger cultist—the baby-faced one who'd tried to flee. The boy was sobbing quietly in the corner, all his zealotry burned away by the violence he'd witnessed. Snot ran from his nose, mixing with the tears on his chin.
"Is it true?" Kael asked. "About Zarek. About the cultivation."
"I don't know," the boy whimpered. His voice cracked. "I just wanted to matter. They said I'd ascend when the Great Rift opens. They'd give me power, make me something. I believed them."
Kael studied the tears streaming down the boy's face. Sixteen, maybe seventeen. Young enough to be salvageable. Young enough to be dangerous. His hands trembled. His shoulders hunched inward.
"Get out of Thornwick," Kael said quietly. "Leave the city and don't come back. If I see you again, I'll know you're choosing this path. And I won't be gentle twice."
The boy scrambled for the door, crashing through it and disappearing into the night. His footsteps faded down the stairs, growing fainter. The older cultist was still unconscious, his breathing shallow and rattling. The woman was bleeding from her mouth and watching Kael with something like pity.
"You'll see," she whispered. "When the moment comes, when the Faceless One calls, you'll understand. There's no resisting. There's only choosing how you fall."
Kael retrieved the Entity-touched blade from the floor. The metal hummed against his palm, vibrating. He could feel the Faceless One's attention fixed on this moment, a pressure behind his eyes. The fragment inside him pulsed, pulling toward the blade.
"I don't fall," he said.
He drove the blade into the woman's heart.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted a few hours of residual life-force from the remains. Spiritual essence acquired."
"Soul Harvest: You extracted fragmented tactical intelligence. Cult command structure partially mapped."
The woman's memories confirmed everything. Zarek operated from a base in the Thornwood, coordinating through a network of brokers and false-front businesses. The Great Ritual was scheduled for the autumn equinox—weeks away. And the cult had agents embedded in all six Orders, feeding information about patrol schedules, seal maintenance crews, and the movements of key personnel.
Kael stood in the blood-soaked room and considered his options. The blood was cooling, thickening. The woman was right about one thing: the Six Orders were fractured. They spent more energy competing with each other than fighting the Abyss. And the Whisper King—the phantom enemy they'd united against—might not even exist. Zarek could be playing them all, using their suspicions to mask the true threat.
But if the cult had agents in the Orders, then not everyone was blind. Somewhere in that tangle of politics and paranoia, someone had to be watching. Someone had to have pieces of this puzzle that Kael lacked.
He needed allies. Information. A way to stop the Great Ritual before the equinox.
And he needed to understand what the Faceless One's fragment meant for his future. The woman had called him a vessel, a piece of a larger design. But Kael had spent two years as the system's property, had watched it harvest souls and grant abilities with mechanical indifference. The Soul Reaver power didn't feel like a gift from any entity. It felt like something that had always belonged to him, waiting to be awakened.
The fragment pulsed. The blade hummed. Power was power, and what mattered wasn't where it came from but what he did with it.
He left the Ironveil through the back alley, stepping over the cultist he'd killed outside. The man's blood had pooled, black and viscous, reflecting the gaslight like a dark mirror. The gaslight from the street cast long shadows, stretching and warping across the cobblestones. Somewhere in the distance, a clock tower chimed. The sound rolled over the rooftops. Thornwick slept fitfully, unaware of the poison threading through its foundations.
Kael walked north, toward the Citadel, where the Order of Dawn maintained their primary garrison. He had questions for them—about the seals, about Zarek, about what they knew and what they chose to ignore. The woman had said they were blind, but blindness could be cured. All it took was the right pressure, the right evidence, the right threat.
The cult was organized. The Shadow Court was watching. The Faceless One had marked him as a vessel, whatever that meant. And somewhere in the Thornwood, Zarek was preparing to crack open the Great Rift and drag the world into the Abyss.
Kael touched the wound in his side. The flesh was still tender, a dull ache pulsing with each breath despite his accelerated healing. Weeks until the equinox. Weeks to find a way to stop a two-hundred-year plan. Weeks to determine whether he was a weapon, a vessel, or something else entirely.
The night swallowed him as he walked, and the Faceless One's fragment pulsed once against his consciousness—a cold weight settling between his ribs. A reminder that he was never truly alone.
The question was whether the thing inside him would help or hinder when the moment came.
He reached the Citadel gates as the first gray light touched the eastern horizon. The sky bled from black to slate. The guards straightened when they saw him coming, hands moving to weapons, expressions shifting from boredom to fear. The metal of their armor clinked.
"Kael Ashborn," one of them said. "You're not supposed to be in this district."
"I need to speak with whoever's in charge," Kael replied. "About the Great Rift. About what's really happening to the seals."
The guards exchanged glances. Whatever they'd heard about him, whatever stories the Orders had spread about the Soul Reaver, it was enough to make them hesitate. The first guard's hand tightened on his pommel.
"We'll take you to Commander Voss," the first guard said. "But understand—if this is a trick, if you've brought danger to these walls—"
"I haven't brought anything except information." Kael spread his hands, showing he was unarmed. "The cult is moving faster than anyone realizes. The equinox isn't a possibility—it's a certainty. And if the Orders aren't prepared, the world ends in weeks."
The words hung in the morning air, cold and absolute. The guard's jaw tightened, the muscles in his cheek bunching. He nodded and led Kael through the gates.
The meeting with Commander Voss would happen soon. The questions would be asked. The truth would be tested against the Orders' assumptions and fears.
And somewhere in the Thornwood, Zarek would learn that his carefully cultivated vessel had slipped the leash—and was coming for answers.
Footsteps echoed behind Kael as he walked deeper into the Citadel. The stone corridor amplified every sound. The guard had fallen in step with him, one hand resting on his sword pommel. Professional. Cautious. Ready.
But the footsteps didn't match the guard's weight or rhythm. They were lighter, almost silent, and they were getting closer.
