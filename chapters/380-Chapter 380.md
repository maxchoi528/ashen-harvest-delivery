The obsidian altar pulsed with sickly violet light, and Kael pressed his back against the crumbling alley wall, breath shallow. Three hooded figures stood in a circle around the bleeding captive, chanting in a language that scraped against his mind like broken glass against stone. The man's screams had faded to wet gurgles twenty minutes ago. Now he hung limp from iron hooks, blood pooling into carved channels that spiraled outward like the arms of some diseased galaxy.
Another ritual. Another city quarter terrorized.
Kael's fingers brushed the cold cobblestones, and he catalogued the scene with practiced efficiency. The cultists numbered three, all spiritually inert by the feel of their auras—ordinary men and women who had traded faith for forbidden knowledge. Two held ritual daggers. One clutched a brass censer that belched acrid smoke, the incense thick with something chemical, something wrong. At their center, the bleeding man wore the torn remnants of a dockworker's coat.
Kael's jaw tightened. Another fisherman. Third one this week.
The city watch had stopped investigating after the first body turned up with organs removed in geometric patterns. The Orders remained silent. Even the Shadow Court's informants had gone dark, which meant either money had dried up or someone important wanted these deaths buried alongside the victims.
Kael had stopped waiting for someone else to care.
He moved from shadow to shadow, keeping low, the cobblestones slick with the evening's drizzle and something darker that stained the mortar between them. The three cultists continued their incantation, faces hidden beneath identical hoods, movements synchronized with the precision of long practice. The ritual wasn't random. Nothing in Thornwick's underground was random anymore.
The realization had come three days ago, slicing through his exhaustion like a blade through rotten fruit. Kael had spent two weeks tracking the fishing village disappearances, following a trail of missing persons reports that led him from the waterfront taverns to this forgotten courtyard in the city's industrial bowels. Every lead had crumbled. Every witness had clammed up or vanished.
But the pattern remained.
The same ritual configuration. The same victim profile—young men who worked the river, spiritual potential barely flickering, exactly the kind of expendable sacrifice that went unnoticed in Thornwick's churning underclass. The same astronomical alignments, pulled from almanacs that shouldn't exist outside Order archives.
Someone was coordinating this. Someone with resources, knowledge, and enough institutional access to predict celestial movements and cover their tracks.
The hooded figure with the censer raised both arms, and the chanting shifted pitch. Kael felt the air pressure change, a subtle wrongness crawling across his skin. Something pressed against the membrane between worlds, testing its limits.
He was out of time.
Kael stepped into the courtyard's edge, letting his presence register. Two of the cultists spun toward him, daggers raised, while the one with the censer continued chanting without pause—the group's anchor, the one maintaining the ritual's flow. Smart. Disciplined. Not amateurs stumbling into forbidden territory, but operatives following a script.
"Wrong place, stranger." The nearest cultist advanced, blade catching the violet light. Female voice, middle-aged, accent from the factory districts. "Walk away. Pretend you saw nothing."
"Three dead fishermen." Kael kept his tone flat, his posture loose. "Same ritual pattern. Same victim profile. You're not gathering power for yourselves. You're opening something."
The woman's stance shifted almost imperceptibly. Surprise. Fear. The dagger's point lowered a fraction of an inch before snapping back up.
"You don't know what you're interfering with." Her voice carried an edge now, something sharper than threatened violence. "The gates must open. The old powers must return. You think the Orders will save you? They sealed the rifts to trap us here, to keep humanity leashed like cattle—"
"Enough." The censer-bearer broke off her chant, voice resonant with genuine authority. "Kill him. We proceed regardless."
The woman lunged.
Kael sidestepped, catching her wrist and twisting until the dagger clattered to the stones. He headbutted her, felt cartilage crunch, and shoved her backward into her companion. The third cultist rushed him with a broken bottle clutched in her fist, glass edges gleaming with desperate conviction.
He broke her elbow with a precise strike, swept her legs, and caught the falling body before it struck the cobblestones. Two down in five seconds, neither seriously injured—Kael had learned long ago that unnecessary brutality invited complications. The woman with the broken arm sobbed into the gutter. The headbutted one groaned, trying to rise.
The censer-bearer hadn't moved.
"You could have walked away." She lowered the brass vessel, smoke curling around her hidden features. "The rifts weaken regardless of your interference. Seventy-two seals failing across the continent, one by one, and you waste your power stopping a single ritual."
"Seventy-two." Kael released the groaning woman and straightened. "The number matters. You know the total count."
Something shifted in the cultist's posture. Wariness. Calculation.
"I know more than you imagine, Soul Reaver."
The words hit him like ice water down his spine. His hand twitched toward the knife at his belt, muscle memory preparing for a threat that hadn't materialized yet. No one in this district should recognize what he was. The Shadow Court's agents used coded phrases. The Orders' hunters attacked first and identified bodies later.
"How do you know that term?"
"Knowledge flows through channels you haven't discovered." The censer-bearer set her vessel on the altar's edge, freeing both hands. Beneath the hood, Kael caught the gleam of eyes that reflected violet light with unnatural intensity. "The Faceless One speaks of you often. Its favorite instrument. Its most promising vessel."
Kael's chest constricted. The Faceless One—the Entity that had marked him during the Ashwood massacre, that had planted fragments of itself in his soul like seeds in poisoned soil. He'd killed three people who carried those fragments. He carried the fourth.
"You're touched." He stated it flatly. "All of you."
"The communion spreads." Pride colored her voice. "Each ritual weakens the seals further. Each sacrifice feeds the Entities' return. The Faceless One promised us positions in the new order, when the Veil falls and the Material and Abyss merge into one."
"Madness."
"Clarity." She spread her arms wide, and Kael saw black veins pulsing beneath the skin of her forearms, threading upward toward her shoulders like roots seeking purchase. "You carry its mark, Soul Reaver. You are already part of what comes. The only question is whether you embrace your purpose or die resisting it."
The bound captive stirred on the altar, a low moan escaping blood-crusted lips. Still alive. Kael had maybe minutes before the ritual resumed.
"You have a choice." The cultist's voice softened, almost gentle. "Join us. The Faceless One offered you power beyond the Orders' comprehension. It showed you visions of the old war, of the first Soul Reavers who walked alongside the Entities as equals. You could be more than a fugitive. You could be a god among mortals."
Kael's mind raced through the implications. Organized cult activity across multiple city quarters. Coordinated rituals timed to celestial alignments. The Faceless One's influence spreading through ordinary citizens like infection through a wound. The Six Orders' silence made terrible sense—they knew, and they were doing nothing.
Or they were too fragmented to respond.
Or someone inside the Orders wanted the seals to fail.
"The first Soul Reavers." He kept his voice steady, testing. "What do you know about them?"
The cultist laughed, a sound like shattering glass. "They were the seals' guardians and the Entities' jailers. They walked the boundary between worlds, harvesting power from the dying to maintain the barriers. But power corrupted them. One by one, they fell—some to the Entities' offers, some to the Orders' persecution. The last of them died eight hundred years ago, and with his death, the knowledge of Soul Reaving was supposed to vanish forever."
"But it didn't."
"It never does. Knowledge persists. Purpose echoes. The Faceless One has been waiting, searching, guiding—and then you appeared." She leaned forward, and Kael saw hunger in her posture, fanaticism burning behind those violet-tinged eyes. "A new Soul Reaver, emerging from the void where all the others fell. Do you think that's coincidence? Do you think your power was random chance, birthed from nothing?"
Kael said nothing. His fingers found the knife's hilt, cold metal grounding him in the present moment.
"The Faceless One shaped you," she continued. "Your awakening, your survival, your path to this precise location at this precise time—it has been cultivating you, Soul Reaver. You belong to it already. You haven't accepted the truth yet."
"On your knees." Kael drew the blade, advancing. "Now."
The cultist's laughter died. "You can't stop what's coming. Kill me, and others will rise. Spare me, and I might tell you where the next ritual occurs. Three nights from now, a larger gathering. A hundred cultists. A genuine rift opening, not just a probe."
"Three nights." Kael's stomach knotted. A hundred followers meant significant spiritual infrastructure, backup plans, escape routes. A genuine rift meant Entity manifestation, casualties beyond counting.
"The old cemetery beyond the Ironwall district. The tomb of the Unremembered King." She smiled beneath her hood. "Yes, we know about the tombs. The Faceless One's knowledge encompasses more than you imagine."
Kael closed the distance between them in two strides, grabbing her collar and dragging her close enough to smell the incense saturating her robes. Up close, her eyes weren't just violet—they flickered with something deeper, something that moved behind the pupils like shadows cast by a distant fire.
"You'll take me there."
"I'll take you to your death. The cult expects interference. They prepared for you specifically."
"Then they'll be disappointed when I survive."
The bound captive's breathing grew labored, wet and rattling. Kael had maybe sixty seconds before shock or blood loss claimed him entirely. The ritual channels glowed brighter now, violet light climbing the altar's carved surface like luminescent moss spreading across stone.
"Last chance." Kael pressed the blade against the cultist's throat, feeling her pulse hammer beneath thin skin. "The location. The numbers. Everything."
"The cemetery. Three nights. A hundred cultists, maybe more. Three Awakened leaders, all touched by different Entities. We will open a rift that hasn't been breached in four hundred years, and when the Entity emerges, it will burn the Thornwick quarter to ash as proof of what comes."
"Your certainty is touching." Kael released her collar, letting her stumble backward. "But you haven't answered my real question."
Her eyes widened. The blade hadn't moved from her throat.
"The other rituals. The fishing villages. The dockworkers." Kael's voice dropped to a whisper. "Why them? What makes their deaths significant?"
Silence stretched between them, broken only by the captive's increasingly terrible breathing and the soft hiss of the censer's smoke.
"The Faceless One requires anchors," she finally said. "Points of contact between the Material and the Abyss. Ordinary souls don't suffice—they lack the spiritual sensitivity to serve as conduits. But dockworkers, fishermen, those who work the boundary between water and land, who spend their lives in the space between worlds..." She smiled, teeth stained with something dark. "They make excellent bridges. Their deaths feed the Entity's attention, preparing it for larger workings."
"You're using them as bait."
"We're accelerating the inevitable." She spread her hands, palms up, black veins pulsing. "The seals weaken regardless. The Entities return regardless. The only question is who survives the transition."
Kael heard it then—a soft scraping sound from the alley mouth behind him. Footsteps, multiple sets, moving with the coordinated rhythm of trained fighters. The cultist's smile widened.
"You walked into a checkpoint, Soul Reaver. Did you think we'd leave the ritual unguarded?"
Kael spun, positioning himself to face both the approaching reinforcements and the cultist he'd been interrogating. Four figures emerged from the shadows, three carrying cudgels and one bearing a crossbow already strung and loaded. All wore the same hooded robes as the others, all moved with the same synchronized precision.
The crossbowman raised his weapon.
Kael hurled his knife at the censer on the altar's edge. Metal struck brass with a ringing clang, and the vessel toppled, scattering coals and incense across the ritual channels. The violet glow flickered, sputtered, died. The bound captive's labored breathing cut off with a final rattling exhale.
The ritual had been interrupted. The sacrifice had been lost.
"Damn you." The cultist's voice cracked with fury. "Do you understand what you've done? The communion requires—"
The crossbow bolt flew.
Kael threw himself sideways, felt the quarrel tear through his sleeve and score a line of fire across his upper arm. He hit the cobblestones rolling, came up in a crouch, and scooped a handful of ritual-dampened earth as he rose. The three cudgel-bearers rushed him in a coordinated sweep, weapons swinging in practiced arcs.
He threw the dirt into the nearest attacker's eyes, ducked under a horizontal swing, and drove his palm into the man's solar plexus. As he doubled over, Kael wrenched the cudgel from his grip and used it to block the second attacker's downswing. Wood splintered against wood. The third attacker circled behind him.
The crossbowman reloaded with steady hands, movements automatic, professional. Kael had maybe three seconds before another bolt flew.
He abandoned the cudgel-block, letting the second attacker's swing connect with his shoulder—pain flared white-hot, but the angle deflected most of the force—and spun to catch the third attacker mid-lunge. Kael's fingers found the man's throat, and he squeezed until cartilage crunched. The cultist dropped, gasping, clawing at air that wouldn't reach his lungs.
Two down. Two more, including the crossbowman.
The remaining cudgel-bearer hesitated, cudgel raised, eyes darting between Kael and the alley mouth. Fear crept into his posture. The Soul Reaver had killed one of their own in under ten seconds. That wasn't in the script.
"Run." Kael straightened, blood dripping from his shoulder wound. "Tell your masters what you saw. Tell them the cemetery gathering is compromised. Tell them Kael Ashborn is coming for every ritual, every cultist, every Entity they try to unleash."
The man ran.
The crossbowman held his ground, crossbow leveled at Kael's chest. A professional. A true believer. Someone who wouldn't break no matter what he witnessed.
"The Faceless One remembers," he said. "It will find you. It always finds its own."
"Tell it I'm looking forward to the reunion."
The crossbow bolt flew.
Kael moved faster than conscious thought, dropping beneath the trajectory and charging forward while the weapon reloaded. His hand closed around the man's wrist, twisting until bone cracked. The crossbow clattered to the cobblestones. A knee to the groin doubled him over. A hammer-fist to the back of the neck dropped him into unconsciousness.
Silence settled over the courtyard, broken only by the gurgling gasps of the throat-crushed attacker and the soft crackle of dying embers from the scattered censer.
Kael stood among the fallen, breathing hard, blood seeping from multiple wounds. The cultist he'd been interrogating was gone—fled during the chaos, vanished into Thornwick's labyrinthine alleys. The altar still hummed with residual energy, violet light pulsing weakly in the ritual channels like a dying heartbeat.
The captive was dead. The sacrifice had been wasted. The ritual had failed.
For now.
Kael approached the altar, studying the carved channels with new attention. The patterns weren't random—they spiraled inward toward a central point, converging on a depression shaped like a cupped hand. A receptacle. The sacrifice's blood had been meant to fill that hollow, completing some kind of circuit.
He pressed his palm against the cold stone, feeling spiritual energy residue clinging to the surface like oil on water. The Law of Soul Conservation meant this energy would dissipate within hours, returning to the Abyss through the weakened Veil. But while it remained, Kael could harvest it.
His Soul Reaver abilities had never worked on residual energy before. He'd always needed fresh corpses, bodies still warm with departing souls. But the altar's configuration suggested something different—a prepared conduit, a focused channel for spiritual power that might accept extraction even from dissipated reserves.
Kael closed his eyes and reached inward, toward the cold knot of power that the Faceless One had planted in his soul. The fragment stirred, responding to his attention with something like recognition. And beneath that recognition, Kael felt hunger—vast, ancient, patient.
The Faceless One wanted to be fed.
*Soul Harvest: You extracted 3 units of spiritual essence from the remains. 7 units of ritual energy harvested from contaminated altar stone. Quality: degraded. Target had minimal spiritual sensitivity.*
Kael opened his eyes, cataloguing the notification's implications. The Soul Reaver ability had expanded. He could harvest from residual energy now, not just fresh corpses. The Faceless One's mark was changing him, granting new capabilities in exchange for deeper integration.
The cost remained unclear. Every harvest brought him closer to the Entity's influence. Every new power forged another link in the chain binding him to the Abyss.
But the cost of inaction was worse. Seventy-two seals weakening across the continent. Cults organizing under multiple Entities' guidance. The Six Orders paralyzed by internal conflict and Concordat restrictions, unable to respond to threats that violated their carefully maintained status quo.
Someone had to fight the Entities. Someone had to walk the boundary between worlds, harvesting power from the dead and the dying. Someone had to be the monster that kept worse monsters at bay.
Kael just hadn't expected the role to feel so natural.
He gathered his knife from the scattered censer debris and cleaned the blade on the nearest cultist's robe. The throat-crushed man had stopped breathing. Kael knelt beside him, pressed his palm against cooling flesh, and reached for the harvest.
*Soul Harvest: You extracted minor combat proficiency from the remains. Quality: degraded. Target had no Awakened abilities.*
Not much. A fragment of street-fighting knowledge, muscle memory from brawls than formal training. But every piece contributed to the mosaic of skills Kael had assembled since his awakening. Combat intuition from a dozen harvested corpses. Tracking instincts from a dead bounty hunter. A merchant's talent for reading lies, extracted from a conman who'd died during a failed con against the wrong marks.
He was becoming something new. Something the world hadn't seen in eight hundred years.
A Soul Reaver in truth, not just name.
Kael rose and surveyed the courtyard one final time. Four unconscious cultists, one dead. The failed ritual. The escaped interrogator. The information about the cemetery gathering three nights hence.
The war had begun in earnest, and Kael had just drawn first blood.
He limped toward the alley mouth, favoring his wounded leg, already calculating his next moves. The Shadow Court's silence demanded investigation—whoever had silenced their Thornwick informants had resources and reach. The cult's organization suggested institutional support, possibly from inside the Orders themselves. And the Faceless One's personal interest in him meant that running and hiding would accomplish nothing except delaying the inevitable confrontation.
Three nights until the cemetery. Three nights to prepare, to gather intelligence, to find allies or at least neutralize enemies.
Kael stepped into the gaslit street, letting the cool night air wash across his wounds. Across the city, similar rituals were occurring in hidden corners, sacrifices being made to Entities that had waited millennia for this moment of weakness. The seals would continue failing. The cultists would continue gathering. The war between the Material and the Abyss would escalate regardless of what any single individual did.
But Kael was not just any individual. He was a Soul Reaver, marked by the Faceless One, hunted by the Orders, feared by ordinary citizens who whispered stories of men who stole souls from the dying.
He was the monster they needed.
And monsters, unlike heroes, didn't hesitate when killing was required.
The gaslight flickered as he passed, casting long shadows that danced across wet cobblestones. Somewhere in the distance, a factory whistle screamed the shift change, and the streets began filling with exhausted workers heading home. The ordinary citizens of Thornwick, oblivious to the war being waged beneath their notice.
Kael turned toward the river district, toward the boarding house where he kept his meager belongings and his carefully maintained anonymity. He needed rest, bandages, time to plan.
Behind him, in the courtyard he'd left, the dead man's eyes snapped open. No pupil showed—just swirling violet darkness that reflected the distant gaslight like polished obsidian. The corpse rose with jerking movements, joints popping, and turned its head toward the alley mouth.
Toward the direction Kael had gone.
The Faceless One had many servants. And it remembered those who escaped its embrace.
The dead thing began walking, following the Soul Reaver into Thornwick's gaslit streets.
