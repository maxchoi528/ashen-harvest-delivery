The blade came down. Kael twisted sideways, felt the air rip where his throat had been a heartbeat before. Steel kissed the damp brick wall, throwing sparks that died in the wet dark.
Three men blocked the alley mouth. Chain-mail glinted under their coats, the links catching the amber glow of the streetlamps. The Mortician's Guild mark tattooed on their wrists—bones wrapped in roses, the ink fresh and brutal. Kael counted exits: one behind him, a rusted grate above, and three bodies between him and the steam-lit street beyond. The stench of the harbor—rotting fish and coal smoke—pressed thick in the narrow passage.
"Ashborn." The lead enforcer smiled. His teeth were bad, his breath worse. "Guildmaster wants a word."
Kael backed toward the grate. His fingers brushed cold iron. Rust flaked beneath his nails. "The Guildmaster can book an appointment."
"That isn't how this works." The man drew a serrated blade, the edge notched and stained. "You killed three of ours in the Dockside raid. Guild law demands—"
"I killed zero of yours." Kael's voice stayed flat. "I wasn't there."
"Funny." The enforcer stepped closer. His boots splashed through a puddle that smelled of piss and machine oil. "Because we have witnesses."
Kael's pulse hammered. Witnesses meant frame job. Zarek's network reached everywhere, but this was the Mortician's Guild—a wall of corpses between him and survival. He needed information, not more blood on his ledger. But they weren't offering negotiation.
The grate groaned under his grip. He pulled. Rust showered down, peppering his hair and stinging his eyes.
"Run if you want." The enforcer's smile widened, cracking his lips. "We'll drag you back either way. Guild justice is thorough."
Kael let the grate swing open. A drop into darkness—fifteen feet to the tunnel floor. The air rising from below tasted of copper and decay. Behind him, he heard boots pounding forward. No time for calculation. He jumped.
The fall hit him hard. Muddy water splashed his ankles, soaking through his boots. The impact jarred his knees, sent a lance of pain up his spine. Above, the grate clanged shut. A rope thudded against stone as someone climbed down after him.
"Smart," the lead enforcer muttered, dropping beside his men. "Underground. Away from witnesses. But we know these tunnels better than anyone."
Kael moved. The tunnel stretched in both directions—left toward the Veiled Market, right toward the old canal system. He went right. The water deepened with each step, rising to his calves. It was thick, sluggish, and cold enough to numb his skin. Behind him, splashing footsteps gave chase.
A junction opened ahead. Three passages branched into darkness. Kael chose the middle one—narrower, but the air tasted cleaner. Less sewage.
He ran.
The passage twisted, doubled back, opened into a cavern that stole his breath. Vaulted ceilings arched overhead, supported by pillars carved with names. Thousands of names, etched in stone. The Catacombs of the Forgotten. He had heard of this place, but never found it. The names on the pillars were worn smooth by centuries of dripping water. The dead remembered here, even if the living did not.
His lungs burned. He pressed against a pillar, controlling his breathing. The stone was slick with moisture, cold against his shoulder.
Torchlight flickered at the cavern's far end. Not the enforcers—a different source. The light was wrong, too steady, too pale. Kael moved toward it, staying in shadow.
A body lay sprawled near a collapsed wall. Fresh, from the smell—copper and voided bowels. Armor marked with the sunburst of the Order of Dawn. A soldier, mid-thirties, face-down in the grime. No visible wounds, but the stillness was wrong. The limbs were too arranged, too deliberate. Kael crouched, studied the corpse.
Poison, maybe. Or something worse.
The enforcers' splashing echoed from the tunnel behind him. Minutes, at most.
Kael reached toward the body, then stopped. Touch required contact. Contact required proximity. Proximity meant discovery.
But this corpse held answers. The Order of Dawn didn't send soldiers into Mortician territory without reason.
Kael moved fast. He rolled the soldier onto his back, checked for belongings. A steel medallion around the neck—an official seal. A pouch at the belt, heavy with coin and something glass. Soul-essence vials. Three of them. And a folded paper, jammed into the armor's inner lining.
Footsteps grew louder.
Kael's hand closed on the soldier's wrist. Cold flesh met his palm. The skin was waxy, the tendons already locking.
The harvesting began.
Pain lanced through his skull—not physical, but deeper. The Soul Reaver's touch burned like ice water flooding his veins. Memories poured into him: patrol routes through Thornwick's underbelly, faces of fellow soldiers, whispered conversations in a command tent, a map drawn in charcoal on a wooden board.
The soldier's final moments hit hardest. Standing at this exact spot, three hours ago. A hooded figure approaching from shadow. Words exchanged—Kael caught fragments: *rifts* and *accelerate* and *the seal won't hold*. Then the soldier's vision blurred, his heart stuttered, and darkness took him.
No weapon. No struggle. Just poison in the wine the figure offered.
The extraction completed. Kael gasped, his mind reeling. His stomach heaved. He braced against the stone, forcing bile back down his throat.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 3 soul fragments from the remains. Memory Shard acquired. You sense latent combat skill: Cleave (Grade C).]
Cleave. A weapon skill. Crude but effective—exactly what Kael needed if this became a fight.
The medallion's seal glowed faintly when he held it. Order of Dawn official. The paper unfolded into a patrol log, written in hasty script:
*Day 47 of the Vigil. North Sewer Junction breached. Third time this month. Command says routine degradation. Bullshit. The seal network is failing faster than projections. We need more Awakened at the Great Rift site. I've requested redeployment.*
Kael shoved the log into his coat. The vials went into his pouch. Three hundred gold sovereigns in soul-essence—enough to buy a week's silence in the Veiled Market, or fuel a small war.
The enforcers reached the cavern entrance.
"There!" one shouted.
Kael ran.
He took the left passage this time, where the soldier's memories showed an exit. The tunnels wound deeper, then climbed. Brick walls gave way to raw stone, then to newer construction—industrial pipes running overhead, steam hissing from joints. The heat grew, pressing against his face. The pipes groaned, venting vapor that smelled of iron and sulfur.
A ladder materialized from shadow. Kael climbed. His hands found a hatch, pushed. Night air hit his face.
He emerged into an alley behind a foundry. Chimneys belched smoke into the stars. Across the street, a tavern's windows glowed orange.
Kael dropped into shadow, counted his assets: Cleave skill loaded, three vials of soul-essence, patrol information, and a dead Order soldier's medallion. Not bad for ten minutes underground.
The Mortician's Guild would know their man was missing by morning. The Order of Dawn would know their soldier never returned from patrol. Kael had one night before both factions started hunting him.
He moved.
The streets of Thornwick roared with steam and flesh. Mechanical carriages clattered over cobblestones, drawn by nothing visible—the city's magitech marvel, driven by compressed spirit-essence. Vendors hawked roasted chestnuts and coal-dusted bread. Whores leaned from windows, calling prices. A patrol of Iron Vanguard soldiers marched past, their armor gleaming with oil and pride.
Kael kept his hood down and his pace normal. Running attracted attention. Normalcy invited invisibility.
The Veiled Market occupied the basement of the old Customs House, three blocks from the waterfront. A nondescript door, a steeper staircase, and a bouncer who recognized Kael's face—or, the scar that split it.
"Ashborn." The bouncer's hand rested on a truncheon. "Thought you were dead."
"Disappointed?"
"Paid either way." The bouncer stepped aside. "Basement's busy tonight. Something's got everyone jumpy."
Kael descended.
The market sprawled across vaults that once held import duties, now cluttered with stalls selling contraband. Soul-essence vials glowed on black velvet. Weapons hung from iron hooks—blades blessed by holy orders, pistols firing spirit-charged rounds. Scrolls and grimoires lined tables, most stolen from Order libraries.
And everywhere, faces. Cutpurses and kingslayers, information brokers and assassins. The Veiled Market served one function: commerce beyond the law.
Kael found his contact at a corner stall, nursing a cup of something that smoked blue.
"Sera." He sat across from her. "I need passage out of Thornwick. Tonight."
Sera looked up. Her eyes were the color of tarnished silver—sign of a Half-Awakened, spiritually sensitive but never fully through the door. She traded in secrets the way others traded in grain.
"Passage costs." She stirred her drink. "Especially for a man the Guild wants alive. What happened to Vance's crew?"
"Left them at the Dockside warehouse." Kael kept his voice neutral. "They tried to kill me. I obliged."
"Three men. Guild-trained." Sera whistled. "You're either lucky or good."
"Both. I need the northern gate, and I need it quiet."
"Northern gate's crawling with Iron Vanguard. They're checking every wagon for contraband—including you, I imagine." Sera sipped her smoke. "But there's another way. The old canal system connects to the river. Smugglers use it for exactly this kind of job. Costs more."
"How much?"
"Fifty sovereigns. Non-negotiable."
Kael calculated. The soul-essence in his pouch would cover it, but that left nothing for bribes or emergencies. "Forty. I also have information."
Sera's eyebrow arched. "What kind?"
"The Order of Dawn knows the seal network is failing. Their patrols are doubling. They're sending soldiers to the Great Rift instead of rotating them out."
Sera's cup froze halfway to her lips. "How do you know this?"
"I have a dead soldier's memories." Kael produced the medallion, set it on the table. "This came with them."
Sera stared at the seal. Her fingers trembled slightly. "Where did you get this?"
"Does it matter? The information is accurate. The Great Rift is accelerating. The Orders know, and they're panicking."
"The Orders never panic." Sera's voice dropped. "They mobilize. There's a difference."
Kael leaned back. "Then mobilize your contacts. Get me out of here, and I'll tell you everything the soldier knew. Patrol routes. Commander names. Whatever he saw before he died."
Sera considered. The market hummed around them—haggling voices, clinking coin, the occasional sharp cry of a dispute turning physical.
"Forty-five sovereigns," she said finally. "And you give me the medallion. It has value beyond information—Dawn seal, limited issue. Collectors pay well."
"Done."
Sera extended her hand. Kael shook it. Her grip was cold, her pulse quick.
"One more thing." Sera released his fingers. "The Mortician's Guild isn't hunting you for Vance's death. They're hunting you because someone paid them to. Big money. Someone wants you found, Ashborn, and it isn't the Guildmaster."
"Zarek."
"Could be. Could be someone else entirely. But whoever it is, they have influence." Sera drained her cup. "The canal boat leaves in an hour, from the old tannery on Marsh Street. Captain's name is Corwin. Tell him Sera sent you, and he'll take your coin."
Kael stood. "Obliged."
"Don't be." Sera pocketed the medallion. "If the seal network fails, Thornwick burns. I'd have someone with your talents where the action is. Whether that action saves us or dooms us—" She shrugged. "Guess we'll find out."
Kael moved through the market, past stalls selling things that would get him killed in daylight. A tooth from an Abyssal Entity, still pulsing with stolen essence. A child's finger, severed and preserved, claimed to grant prophetic dreams. The head of a rogue Awakened, pickled in spirit-formaldehyde, mouth frozen mid-scream.
The world traded in death, and Kael was its best customer.
He reached the exit, climbed the stairs, emerged into the street. The foundry's chimneys still roared. A steam-carriage rumbled past, its driver cursing at a pedestrian who'd stepped too close.
Thornwick at night. A city of masks, where every citizen played a role and the only truth was violence.
Kael walked toward Marsh Street.
The tannery squatted at the waterline, its stench reaching him three blocks away. Leather-curing pits lined the exterior, long abandoned and filled with stagnant water. A single lantern burned above a loading dock.
Corwin waited there. A thick man with a face like weathered leather, arms roped with muscle, eyes that had seen too much to be surprised by anything.
"Sera's client?" Corwin didn't look up from checking his boat's mooring.
"Yes."
"Forty-five sovereigns. Cash. No questions."
Kael produced the payment. Three soul-essence vials—he'd keep the fourth for emergencies. Corwin examined them under lantern light, nodded, and pocketed them.
"Boat leaves in ten minutes. Stay below deck until we clear the city locks. The canal system's dark, and there's things in the water that don't like visitors."
Kael descended the dock. The boat was a narrow barge, maybe thirty feet long, designed for cargo than comfort. Crates filled most of the deck. A small cabin occupied the stern.
He entered the cabin, found a bench, sat.
Corwin's voice drifted from above: "Weighing anchor. Going dark."
The boat shuddered, then glided forward. Kael felt the water's pull, heard the splash of oars or the whisper of a small engine. The cabin had no windows. Only darkness, and the smell of old grain.
He reached for the Cleave skill, let it settle into his awareness. The soldier's combat knowledge merged with his own instincts—where to strike, how much force, the geometry of breaking bone. Not elegant. But effective.
A new tool. Another step toward survival.
The boat picked up speed. Above, someone shouted—Corwin, answering a challenge from the canal gate. Voices exchanged, coins clinked, and the boat passed through.
Kael closed his eyes.
The soldier's memories replayed. The hooded figure. The poisoned wine. The words: *the seal won't hold*.
Someone was accelerating the degradation. Someone wanted the Great Rift open, and they were eliminating anyone who noticed.
Karek? Possibly. The cult had motive, means, and the patience to wait decades for results.
But there were other players. The Shadow Court's internal war. The Orders' competing interests. Even entities beyond human comprehension—the Abyssal Entities themselves, whispering from behind weakening seals.
Kael had walked into a web bigger than he'd imagined. And his survival depended on finding threads that led outward, not deeper into the trap.
The boat's motion changed. Slower now. Climbing—moving uphill through a series of locks. Corwin's boots crossed the deck overhead, heavy and deliberate.
Then the boots stopped.
Silence, except for water lapping against hull.
Kael's hand found his blade.
The cabin door opened. Corwin stood silhouetted against faint starlight, but he wasn't alone. Behind him, two figures held weapons—a crossbow and a short sword. And beyond them, others moved on the dock.
"Change of plans." Corwin's voice was flat. "You aren't leaving Thornwick, Ashborn. Not tonight."
"Who bought you?"
"Does it matter?" Corwin stepped aside. "Get out. Slowly. We have questions, and you'll answer them."
Kael stood. His pulse slowed—the calm that preceded violence. Cleave hummed in his awareness, ready. But there were too many, and the cabin had only one exit.
"Questions about what?" He moved toward the door.
"The seal network. The Order of Dawn soldier. What you learned underground." Corwin's hand rested on a belaying pin. "Someone thinks you're more valuable as a source than a passenger. I'm inclined to agree."
Kael stepped onto the dock. Stars wheeled overhead, obscured by chimney smoke. The tannery's pits reflected faint light—stagnant water, dead surfaces.
"The man who bought me," Kael said. "Is he here?"
"You'll meet him soon enough." Corwin gestured with the pin. "Walk. Toward the tannery. Don't try anything clever."
Kael walked. The wet stones crunched under his boots. The two guards flanked him, crossbow aimed at his spine.
The tannery's main building loomed ahead, a skeleton of beams and rotting wood. Inside, something moved—a lantern, carried by unseen hands.
Kael counted steps. Measured distances. The guards were six feet back, confident in their numbers. Corwin walked beside him, close enough to strike.
Not ideal. But possible.
He stopped at the building's entrance. The lantern inside flared, illuminating a figure seated on an overturned barrel.
The figure rose. Tall, gaunt, wearing robes that bore no Order mark. A hood obscured the face.
"Kael Ashborn." The voice was soft, almost pleasant. "I've heard so much about you."
"Who are you?"
"Someone who knows what you carry. What you can do." The figure stepped forward. "And someone who knows what approaches. The seal network's failure is not natural. It is being unmade, deliberately, by forces that have waited centuries for this moment."
Kael's grip tightened on his blade. "You poisoned the Dawn soldier."
"I gave him a choice. He refused." The figure lowered the hood.
A face like cracked porcelain. Eyes that held no pupils, only swirling darkness.
"Tell me, Kael Ashborn. Do you know what you are? Do you know what the Soul Reaver was, before it was yours?"
The question hung in the air.
Kael said nothing. The swirling eyes watched him, patient as stone.
"Neither do most," the figure continued. "But I do. I was there when the first Reaver walked the Material. I watched him harvest souls for power, and I watched that power corrupt him utterly. The original Soul Reaver—he is not dead, Ashborn. He waits. And your awakening has called to him across the centuries."
Kael's blood chilled. The hidden line—Whisper King's identity, hinted but not named. Dorian's betrayal, lurking in the shadows. The original Soul Reaver's fate, half-revealed.
"Why tell me this?"
"Because you need to understand what hunts you. The Orders call themselves defenders, but they do not know what truly threatens the world. The 72 Entities are not the enemy—they are prisoners, held against their will. The true enemy is the one who imprisoned them, the one who now seeks to break those seals for reasons of his own."
"Who?"
The figure smiled. A terrible expression on that cracked face.
"You've met him, Ashborn. Or, you've met his pawns. Zarek serves a master older than the Orders themselves. A master who was once like you—a Soul Reaver who took too much and lost himself entirely."
The canal water lapped against stone. Somewhere distant, a dog barked. The guards shifted, uneasy.
"You have a choice," the figure said. "Join us, and learn the truth. Fight us, and die unknowing. The seal network will fall within the year. When it does, the world will need someone who can harvest the Entities themselves—someone who can consume power without being consumed by it. That someone is you, Kael Ashborn. Whether you wish it or not."
"And if I refuse?"
The figure extended a hand. Something gleamed on the palm—a ring, silver and black.
"Then take this. It belonged to the first Soul Reaver. It will protect you from certain... Influences. Consider it a gift. A reminder that choices made in darkness often echo longest."
Kael stared at the ring. Ancient metal, inscribed with symbols he didn't recognize.
Corwin's voice came from behind: "Take it or don't. But decide fast. Dawn patrol in twenty minutes."
Kael's mind raced. The figure offered truth, power, answers—but truth was never free. The ring might be protection. It might be a leash.
He reached out.
