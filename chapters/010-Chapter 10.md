The blade found the cultist's throat before the man could scream.
Kael wrenched the steel free, hot blood spraying across the cobblestones in a dark arc. The body crumpled, fingers still twitching against the wet stone. Gas lamps flickered overhead, casting long shadows through the narrow alley where three more figures lurked.
"Four down." Kael wiped his hands on his coat. "Anyone else want to discuss the terms of my surrender?"
No one moved. The remaining cultists wore the same tattered robes, the same sigil of the Whisper King burned into their collars. But these weren't the rabble from the streets. These were the real operatives, the ones who believed.
One of them spoke, voice hoarse. "You don't understand what you've become."
"I've become a problem for your organization." Kael stepped forward, boots squelching in the spreading pool beneath him. "That's not the same thing."
"We have your family."
Kael's stride halted. The cold air bit through his shirt, but it was nothing compared to the sudden ice in his chest. "My family died years ago."
"Did they?" The cultist's lips twisted into something resembling a smile. "Cultist Prime Zarek would be very interested in that conversation."
That name stopped Kael more than any threat. Zarek. The architect of his framing, the hand that had signed Gwen's death warrant, the shadow behind everything rotten in this city. Kael had been hunting him for months.
"You'll take me to him."
It wasn't a question.
The remaining cultists exchanged glances. One of them—younger, broader, with a scar cutting through one eyebrow—shook his head. "The Prime doesn't receive visitors."
"Then tell him the Soul Reaver wants to negotiate." Kael's fingers tightened around his blade's hilt. "I think he'll find that interesting."
Silence stretched between them, broken only by the distant rumble of steam engines and the wet drip of blood from the alley's walls. Kael kept his breathing steady, his stance loose. He needed these men alive—not out of mercy, but because dead cultists couldn't lead him to their master.
The scarred one finally moved, reaching into his robe. Kael's blade came up instantly.
"Easy." The cultist produced a small iron key, its surface etched with spiraling patterns. "Teleportation circle access. One use. It will take you to a safehouse in the Undercroft—the Prime's secondary location."
"And if this is a trap?"
The cultist shrugged. "Then you die faster than you would have anyway. But if you're looking for Zarek, that's your only option. He doesn't use primary locations anymore. Not since the last Soul Reaver started hunting him."
The last Soul Reaver. Those words hung in the air like smoke. Kael's mind raced through possibilities. Was there another Reaver operating in Thornwick? Had someone else awakened the same power?
He pushed the questions aside. Later. Focus on the immediate.
"Give me the key."
"Not yet." The scarred cultist pocketed the iron key. "First, you need to understand what you're walking into. Zarek isn't like us. He isn't even like the other Primes. He knows things. About the Seals. About the Entities. About what you really are."
"I know exactly what I am."
"Do you?" The cultist's scarred eyebrow rose. "Then you know that Soul Reavers don't exist. The technique died with the first one, three hundred years ago. No one alive knows how to make a new Reaver. Which means someone made you. And Zarek wants to know who."
Kael's stomach tightened. That was a question he'd been asking himself for weeks. The power had manifested the moment he'd died on that scaffold, had burned through him like liquid fire while his body convulsed. No ritual. No ceremony. Just death and awakening and the strange, hungry voice that had whispered in his soul.
The Sepulcher tablets. The ones he'd found in the old archives, the ones that had documented the first Reaver's techniques. He'd never told anyone about those.
"Take me to the safehouse."
The scarred cultist reached into his robe again, this time producing the iron key. He tossed it across the gap between them. Kael caught it, feeling the metal's strange warmth against his palm.
"Undercroft entrance is two streets east, behind the old tannery. Follow the red marks on the walls." The cultist stepped back, pulling his companions with him. "When the portal opens, you'll have thirty seconds to step through. Miss that window, and the key burns itself out."
"What happens to you?"
"We disappear." The cultist's smile was thin. "That's the deal. We lead the Soul Reaver to the safehouse, and we get to walk away. Your quarrel is with Zarek, not us."
"Fair enough."
Kael watched them melt into the shadows of the alley, their footsteps fading into the city's constant background noise. Only when the silence had stretched for a full minute did he move, slipping the key into his coat pocket and heading east.

The tannery's stench hit him three blocks before he reached the building. Decayed hide and lye festered in the summer heat, coating his tongue with chemical bitterness. Kael had grown up in the Industrial Quarter, where steam and smoke meant progress and profit, but this was different. This was medieval working conditions bleeding into the modern age.
Red marks painted the walls as promised—crude symbols that most citizens would dismiss as gang signs or children's vandalism. Kael traced them with his fingers, feeling faint spiritual energy pulsing beneath the pigment. Someone had blessed these marks, probably the same cultists who used them as navigation.
The alley behind the tannery was barely wide enough for one person. Kael squeezed through, stone scraping against his shoulders, until he reached a section of wall that looked exactly like every other section of wall.
He pulled out the iron key.
The metal began to glow, heat spreading through his palm like a coal drawn from a fire. The glow intensified, spiraling patterns flaring bright enough to cast shadows across the alley's close walls. Kael pressed the key against the seemingly solid stone.
The world folded.
Not pain—not exactly. But a wrongness, a sense of his body being unmade and reassembled in a different location, his molecules scattered across distances they shouldn't be able to travel. Kael's vision blurred, his equilibrium shattered, and for one horrible instant he felt himself dissolving into nothing.
Then solidity returned. He stumbled forward, catching himself against a wooden support beam as the disorientation faded.
He stood in a cellar.
The space was larger than he'd expected, maybe fifty feet across, lit by phosphorescent moss growing along the ceiling in irregular patches. Wooden crates lined the walls, stamped with shipping marks from a dozen different companies. In the center of the cellar, a circle of interlocking runes had been carved into the stone floor—the teleportation matrix, now dark and inactive.
Kael scanned the space with every sense he possessed. No movement. No sound except the soft drip of water somewhere in the darkness beyond the crates. His Awakened perception extended outward, searching for threats.
Two heartbeats. Both human. Both above him, on what sounded like a wooden floor.
He moved toward a staircase at the far end of the cellar, each step silent on the worn stone. The stairs creaked beneath his weight despite his care. He stopped, listening.
Nothing.
He continued upward.

The safehouse's ground floor was empty, abandoned mid-operation. Maps lay scattered across a large oak table, weighted down at the corners by miscellaneous objects—a human skull, a brass compass, a jar containing something Kael chose not to examine closely. The maps themselves showed Thornwick's districts, with certain locations circled in red ink and annotated with numbers that might have been dates or quantities.
Kael studied them quickly, committing key locations to memory. One circle marked the Docks District. Another highlighted the Noble Quarter. A third—the largest circle, drawn in fresh ink—encircled a location near the city's center that Kael recognized from his years as a street enforcer.
Executioner's Square.
Before the Industrial Revolution had reshaped Thornwick, the square had served as the city's place of public execution. Now it was mostly used for formal ceremonies and the occasional market day. But Kael knew its true purpose. Beneath the cobblestones, accessible only through specific channels, lay the Executioner's Catacombs—a prison complex where the city held those too dangerous for normal incarceration or too politically inconvenient for public trial.
If the cult wanted to communicate with Zarek, that would be the place.
Kael's fingers itched to gather the maps, to search through the scattered papers for more intelligence. But the heartbeats above called to him. Two people. Two potential sources of information.
He left the maps where they lay and moved toward a doorway at the room's rear, behind the oak table. A narrow hallway stretched beyond, lit by guttering candles set in wall sconces. At the hall's end, a door stood slightly ajar, lamplight spilling through the gap.
Voices filtered out, low and urgent.
"—told you, the timing isn't right."
"It will never be right. The Seals are failing faster than projected. If we don't act now—"
"If we act before the Prime's ready, we'll lose everything we've built. The other Primes aren't committed. Half of them think the Great Rift resurgence is a myth."
"It's not a myth. I saw the readings myself. The entity is stirring."
Kael pressed himself against the wall beside the door, straining to hear more. The second voice belonged to a woman—sharp, impatient, carrying the clipped tones of someone accustomed to giving orders. The first voice was male, deeper, laced with an undertone of fear.
"What about the new Soul Reaver?"
The woman's voice turned cold. "What about him?"
"He walked right into our trap. Killed four of our people. And instead of bringing him here to answer the Prime's questions, you let him walk away."
"I didn't let him do anything. He made his own choice." A pause. "The Prime wanted him scared, not captured. A caged animal fights differently than a cornered one."
"And if he doesn't take the bait?"
"Then we're no worse off than we were before. But if he does—if he comes hunting for Zarek—we'll have positioned ourselves exactly where we need to be."
The male voice dropped lower. Kael had to press his ear against the door to catch the words.
"You're playing a dangerous game. The last Soul Reaver nearly destroyed the entire cult. If this one is half as powerful—"
"He won't be. He's just Awakening. Limited reserves. Limited control." The woman's voice hardened. "The Prime knows what he's doing."
"The Prime isn't here. He's at the secondary location, playing host to our new... Associate."
Kael's blood chilled. Zarek wasn't in this safehouse. The cultists had lied—intentionally or not—and sent him to the wrong location. Which meant the real Zarek was elsewhere, conducting business with someone the cult had recently acquired.
The question was: who?
A crash echoed from the room. Wood splintering. The scrape of furniture being overturned. Kael threw the door open, blade already drawn, and stepped into a scene of chaos.
The room had been an office of some kind—desk, chairs, filing cabinets, the trappings of administrative work. But now those trappings lay scattered across the floor, papers strewn everywhere, and in the center of the chaos two figures struggled against each other.
One was the scarred cultist from the alley, now bloodied and wild-eyed, his hands locked around the throat of the woman Kael had been listening to. She was older than he'd expected, gray threading through her black hair, her face lined with the kind of exhaustion that came from years of carrying impossible burdens. Her fingers clawed at his grip, leaving red furrows in his skin.
"The Prime—" she gasped, her voice barely audible. "He's not coming."
The scarred cultist's eyes found Kael in the doorway. Something flickered there—relief, maybe, or desperate calculation.
"Help me," he snarled, "and I'll tell you everything. About Zarek. About the cult. About what they're planning for the Seals."
The woman's hand shot out, grabbing a letter opener from the desk. She drove it toward the cultist's eye. He twisted aside, but the movement broke his grip on her throat. She stumbled back, gasping, and her eyes met Kael's.
"Don't listen to him," she rasped. "He's a traitor. Working for someone else. Someone the Prime doesn't know about."
The scarred cultist laughed, a harsh, broken sound. "Everyone's working for someone else. That's how this city works. The question is whether you want to know who's really pulling the strings."
Kael stepped forward, blade leveled at both of them. "Start talking. Both of you. And if I hear any more lies, I start cutting off fingers."
The woman straightened, her breathing steadying despite the bruises already darkening on her throat. Her eyes measured Kael with cold calculation—the look of someone who weighed lives like currency.
"Kael Ashborn," she said. "The ghost of the scaffold. They told me you were dead."
"Almost."
"And now you're here, in Zarek's own safehouse, demanding answers." She smiled, though there was no humor in it. "The Prime will find that... Amusing."
"Zarek isn't here."
"No. He's not." Her smile widened. "He's at Executioner's Square. Conducting negotiations with someone very important. Someone you know."
The words hit Kael like a physical blow. He forced his expression to remain neutral, but his mind raced. Someone he knew. Someone important enough to warrant Zarek's personal attention. Someone being held in the catacombs beneath the square.
His thoughts went immediately to Lyra. She'd been captured weeks ago, taken by the Shadow Court's enemies while investigating the cult's operations. Kael had assumed she was dead—or worse, turned. But if she was alive, if she was here in Thornwick—
"Where?" His voice came out harder than intended. "Who's at Executioner's Square?"
The scarred cultist spoke first. "A woman. Dark hair. Silver eyes. They called her the Shadow Court's greatest weapon before she defected."
Lyra. It was Lyra.
"And the other one," the woman cut in. "The one who's really driving this. The one who's been feeding us information about the Seals for months. The one who told us exactly where to find you."
"Who?"
The woman's smile became something predatory. "A man named Dorian Crane. Your former partner. The one who testified against you at your trial."

The words hung in the air like poison gas.
Dorian. The man who'd stood beside him through a dozen operations, who'd shared drinks and war stories and secrets. The man who'd looked Kael in the eye while lying on the witness stand, spinning a tale of treachery and murder that had sealed Kael's execution.
Kael's hand tightened on his blade until the hilt creaked. The world narrowed to a single point of focus—Dorian Crane's face, and the overwhelming urge to put steel through it.
"Executioner's Square," he said. "Now."
"You'll never make it." The woman shook her head. "The catacombs have three layers of security. Awakened guards at every entrance. Spiritual wards that will alert Zarek the moment you cross the threshold."
"Then I'll cut through them."
"And die. Like the fool you are." She stepped closer, ignoring the blade's edge. "But there's another way. The cult has an agent inside the Executioner's prison. Someone who can get you past the wards, past the guards, into the heart of the complex. All you have to do is take us with you."
"Us?"
The scarred cultist spoke up. "I'm out. Already sold. Whatever happens, I want no part of what's coming."
The woman shot him a look of pure contempt. "Then crawl back to whoever's paying you and pray they protect you better than they protected the last traitor."

And Zarek is the instrument they've chosen to make it happen."
"Why tell me this?"
"Because you're the only one who can stop him. Soul Reavers can harvest anything—even Abyssal power, if they're strong enough. If Zarek succeeds, if he absorbs even one of the Entities, the world burns. But if you get there first..." She shrugged. "You might be able to tip the balance."
Kael studied her face, searching for deception. The woman held his gaze without flinching.
"The cult wants to control the apocalypse," she continued. "Zarek wants to become the god of a dead world. But there's a third faction—one that's been watching you since you woke up on that scaffold. They've been feeding us information about your movements, your abilities, your connections. They want you to succeed where the cult will fail."

"We call ourselves the Preservation. We believe the Seals can be maintained indefinitely, if the right person holds the right power. We believe that person is you." She extended her hand. "So. Do we have a deal?"
Kael sheathed his blade. "Get me into Executioner's Square."
Vesper's handshake was firm and dry. "The Preservation will provide what support we can. But understand—you're walking into a trap. Zarek knows you're coming. He wants you there."
"Then let's not disappoint him."

The catacombs beneath Executioner's Square were older than Thornwick itself.
Kael descended through layers of history—medieval stonework giving way to Roman foundations, then older structures still, their origins lost to time and deliberate burial. Vesper led the way, her knowledge of the complex's layout suggesting years of planning for exactly this kind of infiltration.
"The outer ring holds common criminals," she explained, her voice barely above a whisper. "The middle ring houses political prisoners and those the city wishes to disappear quietly. The inner ring—where they're keeping your friend—that's where the real power sits."
"What kind of power?"
"Guards are Awakened. Rank doesn't matter; loyalty does. Every guard in the inner ring has been touched by the cult in some way—blackmailed, bribed, or promised a piece of whatever Zarek is planning. They're fanatics. They'll die before they let you pass."
Kael's mind worked through the problem. Fighting his way through would be loud, slow, and ultimately futile. The moment the first guard fell, alarms would sound and Zarek would know he was coming.
But there was another way. There was always another way.
"The body," he said.
Vesper frowned. "What?"
"You said they've been touched by the cult. Which means they've killed for the cult. Sinned for the cult. Those bodies are probably buried somewhere in these catacombs—disposed of quietly, without ceremony."
Understanding dawned in Vesper's eyes. "You want to harvest them."
"I want their skills. Their access. Their memories of this place."
"Soul Reavers can do that?"
"Find me a fresh corpse. Under twelve hours old. And keep the guards occupied for thirty seconds."
Vesper's expression shifted from confusion to something approaching respect. "There's a disposal chamber two levels down. I'll make sure you have your window."
She disappeared into the shadows, leaving Kael alone with the weight of what he was about to do.
The disposal chamber was exactly as she'd described—a stone room lined with niches, each containing a wrapped body waiting for the city's cremation services. Most were old, days or weeks past their expiration. But one niche near the back held something fresher.
Kael pulled back the cloth and examined the occupant.
Male. Mid-thirties. Multiple wounds consistent with blade combat. The body was still cool, rigor mortis just beginning to set in. His spiritual signature was faint but present—residual energy clinging to flesh that hadn't yet fully released its connection to the living world.
Kael placed his palm flat against the corpse's chest.
The harvest was different this time. Usually, touching a dead body felt like plunging his hand into ice water—the soul's departure leaving behind a hollow coldness that his own power had to fill. But this time, warmth greeted him. Not physical heat, but something else. A last ember of will refusing to extinguish.
The notification appeared before his eyes, as real as any physical object in the room.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Blade Rush from the remains. Memory fragment acquired. The skill Blade Rush allows the user to dash forward in a linear trajectory, covering thirty feet in the span of a heartbeat, leaving afterimages along the path. Spiritual cost: 15 points per use. Cooldown: a few heartbeats.]
Kael's breath caught in his throat. A combat skill. An actual combat skill, extracted cleanly from a man who'd died wielding it. The Soul Reaver power was evolving, becoming more precise, more surgical.
But the harvest wasn't complete. Beneath the skill data, another notification pulsed.
[Memory Fragment Detected: Cult Guard Marcus Vell. Access Level: Inner Ring. Recent Activity: Served night watch rotation, third level. Discovered unauthorized presence in holding cell seven. Reported to Supervisor Thane. Was killed by Supervisor Thane for witnessing the transfer of a high-value prisoner.]
Kael absorbed the fragment, watching Marcus Vell's memories cascade through his consciousness. The layout of the catacombs. The guard rotations. The locations of the wards. And most critically—the fact that holding cell seven had contained someone other than the prisoner officially listed.
Lyra. They'd moved her somewhere else after Marcus had stumbled onto the transfer.
Where?
The answer came from the memory itself. Supervisor Thane had muttered something before driving his blade through Marcus's ribs. Something about the Prime's private audience chamber. Somewhere deep in the catacombs, past the inner ring, in a place even most of the guards didn't know existed.
Kael opened his eyes and found that ten minutes had passed. The harvest had taken longer than expected—memory fragments were harder to extract than raw skills, requiring more precision, more care.
He stood, rolling his shoulders, feeling the new skill settle into his spiritual reserves like a missing piece of a puzzle. Blade Rush. Thirty feet of instant movement. Useful for closing distance, escaping traps, or appearing where he shouldn't be.
Now he just needed to find the private audience chamber.

The inner ring's guards fell one by one.
Not killed—Kael lacked the time and the desire to leave a trail of bodies that would warn Zarek of his approach. Instead, he used Marcus's access codes, his memorized patrol routes, his carefully cultivated look of boredom that discouraged conversation. The guards he passed nodded at him, cursed him for taking the worst shifts, complained about the cold seeping through the ancient stone.
Kael absorbed their complaints in silence, walking deeper into the catacombs.
The private audience chamber sat at the end of a corridor that didn't appear on any official map. Kael found it by following the warmth of the walls—spiritual energy radiating outward like heat from a furnace. Someone had poured enormous power into concealing this place, layering ward upon ward to keep it hidden.
Marcus's memories showed the way. The correct sequence of touches, the passwords that changed daily, the counterclockwise turn of the third stone from the left. Kael performed each step with mechanical precision, feeling the wards part around him like curtains drawn back.
The corridor opened into a chamber that stole the breath from his lungs.
It was massive—a cathedral carved from living rock, its ceiling lost in shadow far above. Columns rose at regular intervals, each one carved with scenes of the Abyssal Wars, the Entities depicted in terrible detail. And at the chamber's center, on a throne of black stone, sat the man Kael had been hunting for months.
Zarek was smaller than Kael had imagined. Wiry, gray-haired, with the kind of unremarkable face that slid from memory the moment you looked away. He wore simple robes, no armor, no visible weapons. His hands rested on the throne's arms, fingers tapping a slow rhythm against the black stone.
"Soul Reaver." His voice echoed through the chamber, flat and curious. "I expected you hours ago. Vesper's distraction worked better than I'd hoped."
Kael stepped into the chamber, blade drawn. "Where's Lyra?"
"Your Shadow Court pet?" Zarek's fingers stopped their tapping. "She's been... Cooperative. Her knowledge of the Court's operations has proven invaluable. Though I suspect she's holding something back. They always do."
"I want to see her."
"You will. After we talk." Zarek leaned forward, his unremarkable face sharp with interest. "Tell me, Ashborn. Do you know what you are?"
"A man who wants to kill you."
"No. You're a weapon. The most powerful weapon the world has ever seen, since the first Reaver died sealing the sixty-third Entity. And like all weapons, you need a hand to wield you." His smile was thin and cold. "I've been that hand, Kael. Every move you've made since your execution—every clue that led you here, every thread that unraveled the conspiracy against you—it was me. I killed your family. I framed you for murder. I arranged your execution. And I brought you back."
The words hit Kael like physical blows. He staggered, caught himself, felt the rage building in his chest like a furnace about to explode.
"Why?"
"Because the Reaver power only manifests at the moment of death. Because the first Reaver's technique requires a specific spiritual signature to activate. Because I've spent thirty years searching for someone who could carry that signature, and you're the first candidate in three centuries." Zarek spread his hands. "You were always going to become what you are, Kael. I... Ensured the timing was correct."
"You killed Gwen. My squad. People who trusted me."
"I killed them to make you die. Their deaths were necessary for your resurrection. Surely you understand the mathematics of sacrifice."
Kael's blade trembled in his grip. Every instinct screamed at him to charge, to bury steel in Zarek's chest and watch the light fade from those cold eyes. But something held him back—a sense, deep in his gut, that there was more to learn. That Zarek was still holding cards he hadn't played.
"The Preservation," Kael said. "They're real. Vesper works for them."
"Of course she's real. I created the Preservation twenty years ago. A useful tool for identifying ideal candidates for Reaver conversion. Vesper believes she's working against me, but everything she does serves my ultimate goal."
"Then why send her to me?"
"Because I needed someone to teach you how to use your power. Someone to guide you through the early stages without revealing too much. Vesper's been perfect—showing you enough to survive, holding back enough to keep you dependent." Zarek stood from his throne, descending the steps toward Kael with unhurried grace. "But now you're ready for the final lesson."
"What lesson?"
Zarek stopped ten feet away, close enough for Kael to strike but far enough to react. "The lesson of what Soul Reavers are truly for. Not harvesting skills from corpses. Not extracting memories from the dying. But harvesting the Entities themselves."
Kael's blood ran cold. "The Entities are sealed."
"The Seals are failing. By my design. Every ritual I've performed, every cult I've infiltrated, every piece of the conspiracy I've constructed—all of it leads to the same moment. The moment when the Great Rift opens fully, when the Entity beyond stirs from its slumber, when the choice is no longer whether to break the Seals but who controls what comes through."
"You want to become a god."
"I want to survive what's coming. The Entities will consume this world. Everyone will die—the Awakened, the Orders, the common people, everyone. Unless someone harvests that power before it fully manifests. Unless someone becomes strong enough to reshape reality itself." Zarek's eyes glittered in the torchlight. "That's you, Kael. You're the only one who can do it. The only one who can stand at the Rift and take what the Entity offers. And when you do, when you've absorbed enough power, you'll be able to rebuild the Seals from the inside. Control them. Make them bend to your will."
"Why me?"
"Because the first Reaver's soul still exists. Trapped in the Rift, bound to the Entity he tried to seal three centuries ago. His consciousness, his memories, his techniques—all of it is waiting for a vessel. A new Reaver strong enough to contact him, to download what he knows, to become what he was." Zarek's smile widened. "You were never meant to stop me, Kael. You were meant to become me."
The chamber fell silent. Kael's mind raced, trying to fit the pieces together. Everything Zarek had said, everything the cult had done—it all pointed toward this moment, this revelation. The Soul Reaver power wasn't just a tool for harvesting the dead. It was a key to something far greater. Something that had been waiting for him since before he was born.
And somewhere in this complex, Lyra was being held. Waiting for rescue. Unaware of how the game had truly been played.
"I'll find the original Reaver's tomb," Kael said. "I'll learn what he knew. And then I'll use that knowledge against you."
Zarek laughed, a sound like wind through dead leaves. "I certainly hope so. Because if you fail, if someone else reaches the Rift first, everything I've built will burn. The Entities will pour through the broken Seals. The world will end. And I—" his voice turned soft, almost tender "—I will finally have peace."
Footsteps echoed from somewhere behind Kael. Multiple sets, moving fast, getting closer. His blade came up as he spun, placing his back toward the throne, watching shadows stretch and coalesce into human form.
Six guards emerged from the corridor, weapons drawn, spiritual energy flaring around them like halos of dark fire. Behind them, held between two massive enforcers, was a woman Kael had thought he'd never see again.
Lyra looked worse than he'd imagined. Bruises motted her pale skin. Her dark hair hung limp around a face gaunt with exhaustion. But her silver eyes—those eyes that had always burned with fierce intelligence—found him immediately, widened, and something flickered there that might have been hope.
"Kael." Her voice cracked on his name.
"Put her down." Kael's blade swept toward the enforcers. "Now."
The guards advanced, surrounding him, their formation cutting off any escape route. Zarek watched from the base of his throne, fingers resuming their slow rhythm against the black stone.
"The negotiation," he said mildly, "is over. You can surrender, and I'll let the woman live. You can fight, and she'll die screaming before your eyes. Or you can run, and I'll hunt you both to the ends of the world." His smile never wavered. "Choose, Soul Reaver. Your story ends here—one way or another."
Kael's grip tightened on his blade. The guards moved closer, their spiritual energy pressing against him like a physical force. Lyra struggled against her captors, trying to speak, but one of the enforcers clamped a massive hand over her mouth.
Thirty seconds. He had thirty seconds to make a decision that would shape everything that came after.
Kael's free hand dropped to his belt, finding the small vial he'd taken from the disposal chamber's supply cabinet. Soul essence. Enough to boost his reserves, to push him past his current limits, to do something stupid and desperate and probably fatal.
Or he could wait. Watch. Learn more about Zarek's plans, about the location of the original Reaver's tomb, about what really waited in the Great Rift.
The guards were almost in striking range now. Ten feet. Eight. Six.
And in that moment, with the decision crystallizing before him, Kael heard something that changed everything. A voice, faint and desperate, cutting through the chamber's ambient noise.
"Kael. Behind the throne. The lever."
Lyra. Her eyes were fixed on a point over his shoulder, flickering with desperate intensity. The guards hadn't noticed—she was too well-gagged, too tightly held—but she'd seen something. Something they hadn't.
The lever.
Kael's mind calculated trajectories, distances, timing. Blade Rush could cover thirty feet in a heartbeat. The guards were four feet away. If he moved now, if he used the skill, if he could reach the throne before they could react—
He moved.
