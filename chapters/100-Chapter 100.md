The water had teeth tonight.
Kael pressed himself against the alley wall, watching the flowion snake through the gaslit streets below. Six figures in grey robes carried a covered stretcher between them, the shape beneath bulky and irregular. Whispers drifted upward—fragments about a *convergence*, about the *Old Well stirring*. Steam hissed from a ruptured pipe somewhere in the gutter, masking the sound of their movement.
He had been following cultists for three nights now. Always the same pattern: gather, move, disappear into Fogmire District's labyrinthine underbelly. Tonight marked the seventh congregation he'd documented. Something was building. The Abyssal activity sensors he'd stolen from a dead Order operative two weeks ago had been screaming for days.
The flowion paused at a junction where four streets converged. One cultist—a woman with a silver sigil embroidered on her collar—raised a lantern. Blue light pulsed in a rhythm Kael recognized from his own nightmares. Three answering flashes came from shadows deeper in the fog. They knew he was there.
No—they *expected* him.
Kael's hand drifted to the knife at his hip. His other hand tingled with the familiar cold presence of Soul Reaver, coiled and waiting. Seven days since he'd killed his first cultist. Eleven days since his execution. Seventeen since he'd been a man with a future instead of a walking corpse bargaining for power in a city that wanted him dead.
The woman lowered her lantern. When she spoke, her voice carried clearly through the murk.
"The Whisper King will see you now."
Not *if* you come. *Now*.
Kael's blood crystallized. He had a choice: bolt into the maze of Fogmire's back alleys, lose the tail he was certain existed, return to the abandoned warehouse where Lyra waited with dwindling patience—or walk into whatever trap the cultists had prepared.
The stretcher's cover shifted. Something pale and segmented emerged, glistening wet in the lantern light. A finger—too long, jointed in four places instead of three—waved lazily before retreating beneath the cloth.
The cultist woman waited.
Kael stepped out of the shadows.
---
The descent took nearly an hour. Stone stairs, slick with moisture that smelled of copper and verdigris, spiraled down into the earth. His escort consisted of four cultists who walked in practiced silence, their grey robes absorbing what little light the woman's lantern provided. None carried visible weapons, but Kael counted three bulges beneath fabric that suggested concealed blades and at least one spiritual focus.
Descending stairs carved from living rock, Kael catalogued exit routes in his mind. Seventeen steps. A narrow ledge on the right side at step forty-two. A crack in the wall at step sixty-eight wide enough for a knife. The cultists moved in perfect formation—two ahead, two behind—which meant they'd practiced this route. These were not random zealots. They were professionals.
The temperature dropped sharply around step two hundred. Kael's breath misted in the air. His Ascendant senses prickled, detecting ambient spiritual energy at levels far exceeding anything he'd encountered outside an Order sanctum. The concentration was almost liquid, pooling in the corridor like an invisible tide.
"Steady," the woman said, though she wasn't speaking to him. One of the rear cultists stumbled, catching himself against the wall. His hand came away wet. Dark.
"The Well's proximity affects the spiritually sensitive," the woman continued, glancing back at Kael. Her eyes caught the lantern light, reflecting it strangely—pupils too wide, irises ringed with silver. "You're Ascendant. You should be able to filter most of it."
Kael said nothing. The spiritual pressure pressing against his consciousness felt like standing at the bottom of a frozen lake, watching dark shapes move in the darkness above.
At step three hundred and twelve, the stairs ended.
---
The Whispering Well wasn't a well.
Kael stood at the edge of a cavern so vast that the woman's lantern might as well have been a firefly in a cathedral. The floor dropped away fifty feet below to a circular platform of black stone, carved with concentric rings of sigils that glowed with pale blue luminescence. At the platform's center, a column of water rose from a pool that reflected nothing—no light, no color, no depth. The water defied gravity, climbing toward a ceiling lost in shadow, coalescing into a shape that might have been a throne or a cocoon or something that had never been meant to be perceived by human senses.
Figures moved on the platform. Dozens of them. Kael counted grey-robed cultists, but also others—men and women in clothing that ranged from merchant's wool to Order-issue leather armor. A woman with a blacksmith's arms. A young man with ink-stained fingers. An elderly figure in robes that had once been white.
The Awakened who served the Abyss.
"You're wondering if this is a trap."
The voice came from everywhere and nowhere, resonating from the walls and the water and the spaces between Kael's thoughts. He turned.
A man sat on a stone bench beside the entrance, previously invisible in shadow. He was maybe fifty years old, gaunt but not frail, with grey-streaked hair pulled back from a face that bore the weathered lines of someone who had survived things that would have broken others. His eyes were wrong—not silver like the woman's, but *deeper*, as if they contained additional layers of perception that shouldn't exist.
He wore simple clothing: a grey tunic, black trousers, no visible weapons or spiritual foci. No jewelry. No symbols of rank or allegiance. He looked like what he appeared to be—a tired man in an underground cavern.
But his presence filled the space like a physical force. Kael's Soul Reaver stirred, responding to something the man carried. Not hostility. Recognition.
"The Whisper King?" Kael kept his hand near his knife.
The man smiled without warmth. "One of many names. Sit. You can keep your hand on your weapon if it comforts you. I have no interest in killing you tonight."
"I find that hard to believe."
"Most would." The man gestured to a second bench, positioning himself at an angle that didn't directly face Kael—a deliberate choice that left Kael's peripheral vision clear. "I've been watching you for eleven days, Kael Ashborn. Since your execution. Since you crawled out of a grave with a power that shouldn't exist."
Kael's jaw tightened. "You knew about the execution."
"I knew about the *frame*. I know who ordered it. I know why. I know you're hunting cultists through Thornwick's underbelly, trying to piece together a conspiracy that involves the Order of Dawn, the Shadow Court, and a man named Zarek who believes himself Prime of something that hasn't existed in three hundred years."
The man's words landed like physical blows. Kael forced himself to remain still, though his pulse hammered against his veins.
"How do you know all this?"
"Because I was once like you." The Whisper King rose, walking toward the cavern's edge. The luminescent sigils pulsed brighter as he approached, responding to his presence. "I walked the same path. Harvested the same way. Made the same enemies. I know what it means to carry Soul Reaver in a world that would see you dead than understand what you're capable of."
Kael stood, following. The cavern's edge dropped away into shadow, the platform below appearing like an island in an ocean of darkness. "You're a Soul Reaver."
"I was."
The word choice hung in the air. *Was*. Kael studied the man's profile, noting details he'd missed before: the faint scarring along his left temple, the slight tremor in his hands, the way he positioned himself with his back always partially covered. This was someone who had survived violence and learned to anticipate it.
"What happened?"
"I made a choice that cost me my abilities. A trade. Power for something more valuable." The man turned, and his eyes met Kael's with an intensity that felt like a blade pressing against skin. "You will face that same choice eventually. Maybe sooner than you think."
The water-column throne pulsed. A sound emerged—not a voice, not music, something between a whisper and a scream that bypassed Kael's ears and resonated directly in his skull. He staggered, catching himself against the stone railing.
"Easy." The Whisper King steadied him with a hand on his shoulder. The touch carried warmth, but also something else—a sense of vast distance, as if the man were standing on the far side of an uncrossable gulf. "The Well reacts to strong emotions. It's been waiting for someone like you."
Kael pulled back, breaking the contact. "Someone like me."
"A Soul Reaver who hasn't surrendered to the hunger. Who still asks questions instead of taking answers." The man returned to his bench, settling with the ease of long familiarity. "The cultists you follow—they're not random believers. They're part of an organization that's existed since the Abyssal Wars. They call themselves the Unbound. They believe the 72 Entities should be released, that the seal network is a prison that traps both humanity and the Abyssal forces, that convergence will create something new. Something beyond the Material and the Abyss alike."
Kael's mind raced. The cult activity in Thornwick wasn't random—it was organized, strategic, part of a larger movement. The woman with the silver sigil, the grey-robed followers, the systematic congregations he'd tracked—all of it connected to something that predated the current generation.
"And the Whispering Well? What is this place?"
"A wound in reality. One of the original breach points from the Abyssal Wars, sealed but never closed. The Entities can't cross through, but they can *whisper*. Hence the name." The man's voice carried a note of bitter amusement. "The Unbound have been using this place as a gathering point for decades. I was drawn here the same way you were—by their whispers, by the promise of answers. I stayed because I recognized what they were building toward."
A chill ran down Kael's spine that had nothing to do with the cavern's temperature. "The Great Rift. They're targeting the Great Rift."
"The seal is degrading faster than anyone realizes. The cult's mass unsealing timeline—" The man stopped himself, his expression shifting to something Kael couldn't read. "There are things I can tell you and things I can't. Not yet. Not until you're ready."
"Ready for what?"
The Whisper King stood, walking toward the cavern's entrance. "For the choice you'll have to make. For the truth about Soul Reaver and where it comes from. For understanding why the Shadow Court is watching you and what the Order of Dawn wants with someone like you."
He paused at the threshold, silhouetted against the dim glow from the stairs.
"You came here looking for allies. I'm offering you a choice: you can walk away, return to Thornwick's surface, continue hunting cultists and piece together the conspiracy on your own. It will take years. Zarek will have accomplished his goal long before you understand what's happening. The Great Rift will open. The Entities will spill through. And everything you've tried to protect will be consumed."
"Or?"
"Or you can stay. Learn what I know. Gain access to resources and information that would take you decades to gather independently. The Unbound think I'm their prisoner, their guide, their mystical oracle. They're wrong. I use their gatherings to identify threats, to track movements, to prepare for what's coming." His voice dropped. "I need someone like you, Kael. Someone who can harvest, who can walk among the dead and extract secrets they took to their graves. Someone who can do what I can no longer do."
Kael's mind worked through the implications. The Whisper King was offering alliance, but under what terms? The man had admitted to being a former Soul Reaver, which meant he'd walked the same path Kael was walking. He'd made choices that cost him his abilities. Whatever he'd traded his power for, it had left him diminished—still dangerous, still knowledgeable, but fundamentally limited compared to what he must have been.
"Why should I trust you?"
The Whisper King smiled, and this time it reached his eyes. "You shouldn't. Not yet. Trust is earned through action, not words. But consider this: I've had eleven days to deliver you to the Unbound. To trade you to Zarek for favors or let them harvest you for your power. Instead, I brought you here, to a place they can't monitor, and told you things they don't know I know."
He reached into his tunic and produced a small object—a vial filled with luminescent liquid that glowed faintly blue-green in the darkness.
"Soul-essence. Harvested from a cultist who tried to force my hand three months ago. It's yours, no strings attached. Proof that I understand what you are and what you're capable of."
Kael accepted the vial, feeling its warmth pulse against his palm. The essence inside resonated with his Soul Reaver ability, singing a harmony that made his teeth ache.
"One more thing." The Whisper King gestured toward the platform below, where the gathered Awakened continued their silent communion with the impossible water. "There's a body on that platform. One of the Unbound's recent acquisitions—an Awakened they captured during a raid on an Order supply caravan. He died this morning. Still warm."
Kael's eyes narrowed. "You're offering me a harvest."
"I'm offering you a choice. You can take the essence and leave, or you can touch the body and see what Soul Reaver shows you. The choice will tell me who you are. Whether you're worth the investment I'm considering making."
Below, the water-column throne pulsed again, and Kael caught a fragment of that not-voice whispering at the edge of his perception. Images flickered through his mind—a great seal cracking, darkness pouring through, figures drowning in shadow, a city burning while something vast and terrible watched from above.
He looked at the body on the platform. Even from this distance, he could sense the residual energy clinging to it. Fresh. Strong. Full of whatever secrets the dead man had carried to his grave.
The Whisper King watched him with those too-deep eyes, revealing nothing.
---
Kael descended the stairs to the platform.
The gathered Awakened parted for him without speaking, their faces hidden beneath hoods or masks or shadows. He felt their attention like physical pressure—curiosity, suspicion, hunger. They knew what he was. They could sense the cold presence of Soul Reaver coiled around his soul like a second skin.
The body lay on a stone slab at the platform's edge. A man, maybe thirty years old, with the lean build of someone who lived by violence and the calloused hands of someone who knew how to use weapons. His Order insignia had been torn away, leaving only ragged fabric and the faint scarring of old wounds.
Kael knelt beside the slab. Up close, he could see the signs of struggle—bruising around the throat, defensive wounds on the forearms, the particular stillness of someone who had died fighting. The cultists hadn't killed him quickly.
He placed his hand on the corpse's chest.
The cold hit first, familiar now after dozens of harvests, rushing through his palm like water through a broken dam. Then the sensations came—fragments of memory, emotion, skill—all of it flooding through his consciousness with an intensity that made his vision blur.
**[Soul Harvest: You extracted 847 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Skill acquired: Gravity Well (rare). Memory fragments recovered: 12%.]**
The skill settled into his mind like a stone dropping into deep water. He could feel its weight, its potential, the way it connected to fundamental forces that most Awakened never touched. Gravity. The manipulation of weight and falling, of mass and attraction. Rare. Valuable. Exactly what he needed.
But it was the memory fragments that held his attention. As the harvest's resonance faded, images crystallized in his consciousness—glimpses of what the dead man had seen in his final moments.
A meeting hall filled with hooded figures. Zarek's voice, giving orders. A map of Thornwick with locations marked in red—supply routes, patrol patterns, weak points in the city's defenses. And in the corner of the final memory, barely visible, a figure in white robes watching from shadows.
The Order of Dawn was compromised. Someone inside the Order was feeding information to the Unbound.
Kael withdrew his hand, gasping. His body ached with the familiar exhaustion that followed a harvest, though the spiritual cost was nothing—the Soul Reaver took everything it needed from the corpse itself. The social cost was another matter. Around him, the gathered Awakened had seen what he'd done. Word would spread. In a city where Soul Reavers were hunted, he'd just demonstrated his abilities in front of witnesses.
The Whisper King appeared at his side, having descended without sound.
"You harvested publicly. In front of Unbound."
"They already knew what I was. You made sure of that."
"I made sure they knew *something*. What you just did tells them more than any introduction could." The man's voice carried a note of approval. "You're not afraid of them. That's valuable. Fear makes people stupid, and the Unbound have enough stupid zealots."
Kael wiped his hand on his trousers, ignoring the stares. "The memory fragments. The Order of Dawn—there's a traitor."
"I know. I've suspected for months. The information you've just confirmed came from someone inside their leadership circle." The Whisper King glanced toward the water-column throne, his expression troubled. "The Unbound's mass unsealing timeline is accelerating. They shouldn't have the resources or intelligence to move this quickly. Someone is helping them."
"Someone with access to Order secrets."
"Or someone with access to the Shadow Court." The man's jaw tightened. "The Shadow Court operates through deniable assets and hidden connections. A traitor in the Order of Dawn could be a Shadow Court operative. Or a cultist who infiltrated decades ago. Or something else entirely."
The water-column throne pulsed, louder this time, and the not-voice rose to a shriek that drove Kael to his knees. Around him, the gathered Awakened fell as well, clutching their heads, some bleeding from their ears. The sigils on the platform blazed bright enough to cast shadows.
Then, as as it had started, the noise stopped.
The water-column collapsed back into its pool, spreading across the black stone in a ripple pattern that might have been language. The gathered Awakened slowly rose, some supporting others, all of them shaken.
The Whisper King remained standing, though his face had gone pale. "It knows you're here. It knows what you carry." He looked at Kael with something that might have been concern. "The Entities can't cross the Veil, but they can *reach*. Especially through wounds like this. Your Soul Reaver—it's a beacon to them. A crack in their prison that walks on two legs."
The implications settled over Kael like a shroud. Every harvest, every use of his abilities, was drawing attention from something vast and terrible. The cultists wanted to control him. The Shadow Court was watching him. The Entities themselves were reaching for him through the fabric of reality.
He should have felt afraid. Instead, he felt the cold clarity of someone who had already lost everything and had nothing left to lose.
"I don't care what reaches for me." His voice came out harder than he intended. "I'm going to destroy Zarek. I'm going to tear down whatever he's building. And I'm going to make sure the Great Rift stays sealed even if I have to kill every cultist in Thornwick to do it."
The Whisper King studied him for a long moment. "Then we have work to do."
He extended his hand—not for a handshake, but palm-up, an offering. Waiting.
Kael clasped it. The contact felt like sealing a pact written in blood and necessity. Two Soul Reavers, one diminished and one rising, bound by common cause in a world that wanted them both dead.
"One condition." Kael's grip tightened. "Lyra comes with me. She's part of whatever we're building."
"Lyra Vance." The Whisper King nodded slowly. "The Shadow Court defector. Yes. I know. She's been asking questions in the Veiled Market, trying to trace her sister's location. The Mortician's Guild has her. Alive. In a facility outside the city." He released Kael's hand. "We can use that. Her skills are valuable, and her connections to the Shadow Court could prove useful."
"You've been planning this for a while."
"I've been waiting for someone like you. The timing is recent, but the need isn't." The man walked toward the stairs, gesturing for Kael to follow. "Come. We have much to discuss, and the Unbound's gathering will end soon. They'll expect me back in their midst, playing the oracle. But we can meet again, away from their eyes."
Kael fell into step beside him, ascending toward the surface. The weight of the harvested skill—Gravity Well—pulsed in his mind, alongside the weight of knowledge and alliance and danger.
At the top of the stairs, dawn light filtered through a grate in the alley wall above. Thornwick's steam-fog hung thick and yellow, carrying the smell of industry and desperation. Somewhere in that maze of streets and shadows, cultists were moving toward an unknown goal. Zarek was pulling strings. The Shadow Court watched from darkness. And now, a new player had entered the game—one who had been a Soul Reaver before, who knew secrets that could change everything.
Kael climbed out of the alley, leaving the Whispering Well and its impossible water behind. His mind raced with questions. The man's story didn't add up. Former Soul Reaver, diminished, kept prisoner by the Unbound but using them. It was too convenient, too perfectly positioned to offer exactly what Kael needed.
But convenience was all he had. The conspiracy was too big, the enemies too many, the timeline too compressed for him to work alone. If the Whisper King was playing him, he'd find out eventually. If the man was genuine, he'd gain an ally with knowledge and resources he desperately needed.
Either way, he had a choice now. A path forward.
He turned toward Fogmire District, toward the abandoned warehouse where Lyra waited, toward whatever came next.
Behind him, in the darkness of the alley, something moved. Kael spun, knife drawn, but the shadows were empty. Only the faint whisper of water echoed from below, carrying words he couldn't hear.
The Entity in the Well was still watching.
---
He covered the distance to the warehouse in silence, taking routes that avoided main streets and potential surveillance. Lyra would want answers—he had none to give, only complications. The Whispering Well, the offer of alliance, the gravity skill burning in his mind, the knowledge that the Order of Dawn harbored a traitor. And beneath it all, the creeping certainty that he'd walked into something larger than his personal vendetta against Zarek.
The warehouse door was ajar. Kael pushed through, blade leading, and found Lyra sitting by a makeshift fire, a knife in one hand and a strip of dried meat in the other. She looked up with eyes that had seen too much death and not enough sleep.
"Three days." Her voice was flat. "You said one. You said you'd be back by dawn. Three days, Kael. I was about to leave."
"I found what I was looking for."
"You found trouble. I can smell it on you." She set down the knife, standing. "What happened?"
Kael leaned against a rusted support beam, exhaustion finally catching up with him. "I met someone. A man who calls himself the Whisper King. He claims to be a former Soul Reaver. He knows about my abilities, about Zarek's conspiracy, about the cult activity in Thornwick. He offered me an alliance."
Lyra's expression didn't change, but her posture shifted—a subtle tension that spoke of alertness. "And you believed him?"
"I believed his information. His motives are his own."
"That's not an answer."
"No. It isn't." Kael pulled the vial of soul-essence from his pocket, holding it up to the dim light. The blue-green glow cast strange shadows across his face. "He gave me this. Proof of good faith. And I harvested from a cultist corpse in front of the Unbound's leadership. They're going to spread the word. By tomorrow, every criminal organization in Thornwick will know there's a Soul Reaver operating in their territory."
Lyra swore, low and vicious. "You exposed yourself deliberately."
"The Whisper King wanted to see what I'd do. I wanted to send a message." Kael tucked the vial away. "I'm done hiding. Hiding hasn't protected anyone. The cultists know about me. The Shadow Court knows about me. The Whisper King knows. What's one more enemy when you're already surrounded?"
"You've lost your mind." But there was something in Lyra's voice—not agreement, not approval. Something closer to recognition. "Three days ago, you were talking about taking down Zarek quietly. Now you're making alliances with underground oracles and harvesting in front of cultists."
"Three days changes things."
Lyra studied him for a long moment, then returned to her seat by the fire. "Tell me everything. From the beginning."
Kael sat across from her, the flames casting dancing shadows across the walls. He told her about the cultist procession, the descent into the Whispering Well, the man who claimed to understand what he was and what he could become. He told her about the harvest, the gravity skill, the memory fragments that had shown him the traitor in the Order of Dawn. He told her about the Whispering King's offer—alliance in exchange for help, resources and knowledge in exchange for skills and action.
When he finished, Lyra was silent for a long time.
"The Mortician's Guild." Her voice came out rough. "He mentioned my sister."
"She's alive. Being held in a facility outside the city."
"That's the first real information I've had in months." Lyra's hands clenched, knuckles whitening. "If he's telling the truth, if this isn't a trap—"
"It's a trap. Everything is a trap. The question is whether the bait is worth the risk."
Lyra looked up, and Kael saw something he'd never seen in her before—hope, fragile and desperate and quickly smothered. "My sister is the bait. She's always been the bait. Everything in my life has been about her, even after she disappeared. The Shadow Court used her to control me. Zarek used her memory to drag me back into cult work. Now this Whisper King is dangling her in front of me."
"Is it working?"
A harsh laugh escaped her throat. "Of course it's working. I'd sell my soul to the Entities themselves if it meant seeing her alive again."
Kael leaned forward, firelight reflecting in his eyes. "Then we use that. The Whisper King wants allies. Fine. We become his allies. We get close enough to learn what he knows, to access whatever resources he's offering. And when we find out what he really wants, we decide whether it aligns with what we want."
"And if it doesn't?"
"Then we take what we can and leave. Or we destroy him if necessary." Kael's voice carried no hesitation. "I told you once that I don't trust anyone. That hasn't changed. But I've also learned that sometimes you have to work with people you don't trust to accomplish things you couldn't accomplish alone."
Lyra was quiet for a moment. Then she nodded, slowly. "The Whisper King. Where do we meet him?"
"He'll contact us. He said we have much to discuss, but we need to do it away from the Unbound."
"That's convenient."
"Everything about this situation is convenient. Which means we proceed carefully." Kael pulled the Gravity Well skill from his mind, testing its edges. It felt heavy, dense, like holding a weight that existed in multiple dimensions. "I harvested a skill tonight. Gravity manipulation. It's rare—something most Awakened never access without decades of study."
"Can you use it?"
"I won't know until I try. The harvest gives me the knowledge, but applying it is another matter." He flexed his hand, feeling the way the skill responded to his intention. "We should move. This warehouse isn't secure anymore. If the cultists tracked me to the Well, they'll track me here."
Lyra stood, gathering her few possessions. "I know a place. A safehouse the Shadow Court established years ago. It's supposed to be abandoned, but I have contacts who can confirm."
"The Shadow Court is monitoring me. If we use their resources—"
"Then they'll know exactly where we are. Which might be exactly what we want." Lyra met his eyes. "Sometimes the best way to avoid a trap is to spring it on your own terms. Let them think they're watching us. Let them think they know what we're doing. And when they move to intercept, we'll be somewhere else entirely."
Kael considered the strategy. It was risky—drawing Shadow Court attention could blow back on them in ways they couldn't predict. But Lyra had spent years navigating the Shadow Court's operations. She understood their methods, their blind spots, the gaps in their surveillance.
"Do it. But we stay mobile. No fixed locations for more than one night."
Lyra nodded, shouldering her pack. She moved toward the warehouse door, then paused. "Kael. Whatever the Whisper King is offering—whatever alliance he's proposing—remember that he admitted to being diminished. A former version of himself. He traded something to get where he is now."
"I remember."
"Good." She glanced back, and her expression held something that might have been concern. "Because the moment you stop being useful to him is the moment you'll discover what he's willing to sacrifice to get what he wants."
They emerged into Thornwick's pre-dawn fog, two figures moving through shadows that had become their home. Behind them, the abandoned warehouse smoldered—the fire they'd built had spread while they talked, consuming years of dust and memories. The flames painted the fog orange and red, a beacon that would draw attention.
Let them come. Kael was done hiding.
Ahead lay the Whispering Well and its impossible promises. Behind lay the ashes of the man he'd been before his execution. And somewhere between past and future, the conspiracy that had framed him, killed him, and given him purpose continued its patient work.
He would tear it down. Whatever it cost. Whatever it required.
The Entities could reach for him. The cultists could hunt him. The Orders could condemn him.
Kael Ashborn had already died once.
The second time would be on his own terms.
---
The voice came from everywhere and nowhere, resonating through the fog like a memory of sound.
*You walk with borrowed time, Reaver.*
Kael spun, searching for the source. Nothing. Only the empty streets and the hissing steam pipes and the weight of attention pressing against his skull.
*We have been waiting.*
The whisper faded, leaving only silence and the certainty that something vast had just acknowledged his existence.
Kael's hand drifted to his chest, pressing against the cold presence of Soul Reaver. It pulsed in response—not with alarm, but with something that felt almost like recognition.
The Entities knew him now.
Somewhere in the depths of Thornwick, the Whispering Well waited.
And the choice he'd made in its presence was only the beginning.
