"Move and I'll open your throat."
The blade pressed against the stranger's windpipe. Kael counted three heartbeats before the man stopped squirming. Blood from the earlier scuffle still dripped from his own knuckles, warm where it had begun to cool.
"Who sent you?" Kael asked.
The man spat on the cobblestones. "You think you're the first? They warned us about you. The Reaver who walks among the living."
*They.* Kael filed that word away and pressed the blade a fraction deeper. "Define 'they.'"
"Go to hell."
Kael tilted his head. The gaslight from the nearby lamp post cast long shadows across the alley's wet stones. Above them, on the third-floor window ledge, a figure in black watched without interfering. Not Shadow Court protocol—those operatives always moved in pairs. Solo surveillance meant something else. Something older.
He could work with that.
The man started to convulse. Foam appeared at the corners of his mouth. Kael jumped back as the stranger's body seized once, twice, then went completely rigid. Tongue lolled. Eyes rolled back. When the final shudder passed, the man stood there, breathing but absent.
Truth Poison. Someone had dosed him hours ago, left a deadman's trigger in his blood. The moment he talked about his handlers—
Kael lunged forward and grabbed the man's wrist. Cold flesh. No pulse at the wrist, but the heart still pumped. Barely. He had minutes before the poison finished its work and this source of answers became a corpse.
*Touch required. Touch I have.*
[Soul Harvest: Initiating extraction. Target: Male, mid-30s, Awakened, Stage 1-2. Spiritual residue detected.]
The connection flooded through his palm like ice water up his arm. Images slammed into his consciousness. A hooded figure in a cellar. Diagrams of Thornwick's underground. Coordinates that meant nothing to the dead man but everything to Kael's growing map of the conspiracy.
And a face. Grey-bearded, scarred across both cheeks in ritual patterns. Someone important enough to remember but low enough in the hierarchy that the poison suicide was deemed acceptable.
The man collapsed. Kael caught him—not from mercy, but because a body falling in the street would bring Watch attention. He dragged the still-breathing husk into the shadow of a coal cart.
[Soul Harvest Complete: Target expired. You acquired — Fragmented Intel: Cult Network. Fragmented Intel: Ritual Site Coordinates. Fragmented Intel: Handler Identity.]
Handler identity. Kael's pulse quickened. He focused on that fragment, trying to pull details from the fading impressions. The grey beard. The ritual scars. Affiliation with—
The figure on the third-floor ledge vanished.
No ordinary scout would retreat when their target was distracted. That required either new orders or confirmation that Kael had found something worth reporting up the chain. Either way, he had minutes before this alley became a killing ground.
Kael released the corpse and ran.
He took the rooftops where he could, dropping into narrow passages when the gaps between buildings grew too wide. Thornwick's industrial district sprawled beneath him—foundries with their eternal fires, steam cocks shrieking as pressure released, the constant clatter of machinery that never slept. Common folk worked the night shift here. Safe, mostly. The cultists wouldn't risk exposing their operations with public violence.
*But they'll follow. Watch. Report.*
He needed somewhere public to disappear. Somewhere with enough foot traffic to lose a tail but enough cover to keep moving. The Veiled Market was too obvious—the Shadow Court already knew he used it. The Iron Quarter's gambling houses would card him at the door.
The Mechanist's Fairground.
Kael altered course, heading northeast toward the massive exhibition hall that dominated the district's edge. During the day, inventors displayed their latest contraptions there—steam-powered carriages, automated looms, strange brass devices that purported to do everything from cure baldness to predict the weather. At night, the empty halls became neutral territory. Street children slept there. Refugees camped between booths. No faction claimed it because no faction bothered.
He arrived panting, having cleared the last rooftop jump with inches to spare. The fairground's main entrance gaped open, iron gates rusted half-off their hinges. Inside, darkness swallowed everything beyond the first few rows of abandoned stalls.
Kael slipped between a giant mechanical horse and a brass octopus missing three tentacles. Found a corner where canvas tarps had been draped over unsold merchandise. He pressed himself into the shadows and waited.
Thirty seconds. A minute. Two.
A gas lamp flickered to life forty feet away.
Kael went still. That wasn't street children. That wasn't anyone legitimate. He watched a figure in a long coat approach the light's edge, pausing to check something in their hands. A map, maybe. Or a tracking sigil.
The flame bent sideways despite the absence of wind. Then it died completely.
Silence filled the fairground like water filling a bowl. Kael's ears rang with the sudden quiet. His hand found the knife at his belt. Standard steel. Would do nothing to whatever was coming, but the motion helped.
"So you're the one making noise in my city."
The voice came from everywhere and nowhere. Male, aged, carrying the flat accent of old Thornwick nobility. Kael spun, searching the darkness. Nothing moved.
"Your cultists are sloppy. Your poisons are predictable. Your surveillance protocols are two decades obsolete." A dry chuckle echoed off the exhibition walls. "But you've survived three direct attempts on your life this month alone. That suggests either remarkable talent or remarkable luck."
"I prefer to call it skill."
"Ah. Witty." The voice grew closer without seeming to travel. "That's good. Witty men live longer in my employ."
A figure stepped from the shadow beside the brass octopus. Tall, gaunt, dressed in a suit that had probably cost more than a factory worker's annual wage. Grey-bearded, scarred across both cheeks in ritual patterns.
The handler from the cultist's memories.
Kael's knife cleared its sheath. "You sent the man in the alley."
"I sent several men. You're the first to survive the conversation." The greybeard spread his hands, showing empty palms. "My employer wishes to make you an offer."
"Your employer." Kael kept his blade raised. "Which one? The cult running rituals in my territory? The Shadow Court that's been tracking me since Thornwick? Or someone else entirely?"
The greybeard smiled. Three teeth missing from the lower row. "All three,. They're not as separate as you might think."
That stopped Kael cold. The Shadow Court operated as a deniable arm for multiple Orders, yes. But openly collaborating with Abyssal cultists? That violated every protocol he'd ever heard of.
"You're lying."
"I'm explaining." The greybeard began walking, circling Kael's position without seeming to care about the knife. "The conspiracy you keep poking at isn't what you imagine. It's not one organization. It's an agreement. A compact made sixty years ago when the seals started failing and the Orders proved too rigid to adapt."
" sixty years." Kael tracked his movement, refusing to turn his back. "The seals have been weakening for centuries."
"Slowly. Predictably. Then catastrophically." Greybeard stopped near a display of clockwork birds, adjusting one that had fallen crooked. "You know about the Seventy-Two, of course. Everyone with spiritual sensitivity knows. What you don't know is how the unsealing was supposed to happen. The original design called for gradual release—one entity every decade, give or take. Humanity would adapt. Grow stronger in the gaps between awakenings. Eventually match them."
"That didn't happen."
"The Orders killed that plan when they formed the Concordat." Greybeard's voice hardened. "Six factions, each convinced they alone could manage the Abyss. They sealed the rifts tighter, centralized spiritual knowledge, purged anyone who disagreed. For two hundred years they've maintained control while the seals corrode from within. Do you know what happens when seventy-two entities tear through the Veil simultaneously?"
Kael had nightmares about exactly that scenario. "The world ends."
"The world changes. Radically. Permanently." Greybeard faced him fully now. "My employer believes humanity should guide that change than be swept away by it. The compact ensures that when the unsealing comes, certain... Preparations will be in place."
Kael's mind raced. This was bigger than cultists harvesting essence. Bigger than Shadow Court assassinations. This was decades of planning, multiple factions working toward a controlled apocalypse.
"Why tell me this?"
"Because you're relevant now. The Soul Reaver ability is... Unique." Greybeard pronounced the words carefully, like he expected them to bite. "The original bearer helped design the seal network. When he died, his methods were supposed to die with him. But here you are, walking around with power that should not exist, asking questions that should not be asked."
Kael felt ice forming in his stomach. The Soul Reaver origin. The mystery of where his ability came from. He'd been collecting fragments for hundreds of chapters, and now—
"The first Reaver," he said slowly. "What happened to him?"
Greybeard's expression flickered. Something that might have been respect, or fear, or both. "He learned too much. Tried to stop what he'd helped create. The compact decided he was too dangerous to exist."
"And you think you'll fare better threatening me?"
"I think nothing." Greybeard raised one scarred hand, and the darkness around them deepened perceptibly. "I report. Others decide. My employer has determined that you're more useful alive than dead. For now. That's the offer, Reaver. Work with the compact. Learn what you're really carrying. Help prepare the world for what's coming. In exchange, you get resources, protection, and answers."
"And if I refuse?"
"Then you'll continue making noise until someone less patient than my employer handles the situation." Greybeard shrugged. "The Shadow Court has already been authorized to accelerate their timeline with you. The cult has standing orders to capture or kill depending on what you know. The Orders—" He laughed softly. "The Orders would love to dissect you for study. You're surrounded, Reaver. The only question is which wall you walk into."
Kael considered the knife in his hand. One throw would end the conversation. Greybeard stood close enough that even a mediocre aim would find flesh. But dead handlers only bred more handlers, and the information flowing from this one was worth more than revenge.
"The cult activity in Thornwick," Kael said. "The rituals. What's the purpose?"
"Rituals?" Greybeard seemed genuinely confused. "The Thornwick operations aren't rituals. They're preparation. The cult is identifying ley line convergence points, mapping spiritual currents, cataloguing Awakened populations. Basic reconnaissance."
"That doesn't require human sacrifice."
"Sometimes it requires demonstration. The cult's methods are... Theatrical. But their purpose is sound."
Kael filed that away. The cult was organized, not random. They weren't summoning anything yet. Just preparing.
"The Shadow Court. Why are they monitoring me specifically?"
"My employer's instructions." Greybeard's tone grew careful. "You're either an asset to be cultivated or a variable to be eliminated. The Court hasn't decided which. They're watching to see what you do with the power you've acquired."
"And your employer?"
"Decided years ago. You're an asset." The greybeard smiled again, showing more missing teeth. "The Reaver ability is too valuable to waste on paranoia. Someone needs to carry it forward. Someone needs to understand what it truly is. You're that someone, whether you accept the role or not."
The words settled into Kael's bones with uncomfortable weight. He'd always known his power was strange. Rare. But the implication that someone had been tracking him specifically, waiting for him to awaken, selecting him as a successor—
"The first Reaver," he pressed. "Did he have a name?"
Greybeard shook his head. "Names are dangerous. Especially for someone who wanted to disappear. He left journals. Tablets. Records of what he'd learned and what he'd done. The compact has been hunting those records for two centuries."
The Sepulcher tablets. Kael's pulse spiked. The fragments he'd collected, the recordings supposedly made by the first Soul Reaver—they were the most dangerous artifacts in the world. Not because of what they contained, but because of who wanted them.
"What if I find them first?"
"Then you'll have leverage." Greybeard spread his hands. "The compact would negotiate. The Orders would offer anything. Even the Shadow Court might deal, if approached correctly. Those tablets could reshape the coming conflict in ways no one can predict."
"And you're telling me this why?"
"Because my employer wants you to find them. Wants you to understand what's written there. The first Reaver discovered something about the seal network, something the compact has spent two centuries suppressing. Until you know what he knew, you can't make informed choices about which side to serve."
Kael's blade arm ached from holding the knife raised so long. He lowered it slightly, buying time to think. The conspiracy's scope had just expanded beyond anything he'd imagined. Not one organization but several, cooperating toward a controlled apocalypse while the Orders clung to failing containment protocols. And somewhere in the middle of it all, the Sepulcher tablets held secrets worth killing for.
"Who do I meet?" he asked finally. "If I decide to hear this offer in full."
Greybeard reached into his coat and withdrew a brass coin. He tossed it toward Kael, who caught it one-handed. Heavy. Warm. Engraved with symbols that seemed to shift when he wasn't looking directly at them.
"Press your thumb to the face. Someone will find you within three days." The greybeard turned to leave, then paused. "A word of advice, Reaver. The Shadow Court operative watching this meeting is named Vex. She's impatient. If you plan to negotiate, do it before she decides to act independently."
"Which way did she go?"
"North. Toward the old forge district." Greybeard's silhouette began fading into the darkness. "Clock's ticking, Ashborn. The compact has waited two hundred years. We can wait a little longer. But Vex won't."
Then he was gone, leaving only the echo of footsteps and the weight of impossible choices.
Kael stood in the fairground's darkness, turning the brass coin over in his palm. Somewhere in Thornwick, three separate organizations hunted him. Somewhere, the seals continued their slow decay toward catastrophic failure. And somewhere, the Sepulcher tablets waited to reveal secrets that had already gotten one Soul Reaver killed.
He had three days before the compact sent someone official. He had hours before Vex made her move. And he had no idea which threat to address first.
The knife went back into its sheath. The coin went into his coat pocket, pressing cold against his ribs.
Time to make some noise.
Kael moved toward the fairground's eastern exit, mind already cataloguing assets and allies. Sera knew the Mortician's Guild's secrets. Lyra had Shadow Court contacts she hadn't fully explored. Even the Ironside Brotherhood might prove useful if approached correctly.
The conspiracy was bigger than him. The conspiracy was bigger than anyone. But Kael Ashborn had spent two hundred chapters learning to fight above his weight class. This was just another opponent with more resources than sense.
He slipped out into the pre-dawn streets, brass coin warm against his chest, and started walking toward the only conclusion that mattered: either he mastered the game, or the game mastered him.
Behind him, on the fairground's uppermost balcony, a figure in black watched him go. Vex pressed her palm to the cold iron railing, feeling the Reaver's spiritual residue fade with distance. Three days. The compact wanted him alive for now. But alive was a flexible term.
She'd give him two.
The woman in black vanished into the morning fog, and Thornwick's streets swallowed another secret.
Kael never saw her. He was already three blocks away, plotting his next move, when a child's scream split the quiet air.
He ran toward the sound without thinking. Found a narrow alley between a tannery and a shuttered alehouse. Found three men in unmarked leathers surrounding a girl of maybe twelve, her back pressed against the wall, her eyes wild with terror.
"Leave her alone."
The nearest man turned. Spat on the cobblestones. "This doesn't concern you, friend. Move along."
Kael's hand found his knife again. "I said leave her alone."
Three against one. Bad odds in a dead end. But the girl's terror was real, and the men weren't cultists—he could sense no spiritual energy from them. Just common thugs thinking they'd found easy prey.
The man laughed. Drew a length of iron pipe from his belt. "You want to play hero, hero? Let's see how long you last."
They rushed him together.
Kael ducked the first swing, feeling the pipe whistle past his ear. The second man grabbed for his arm, missed, stumbled into his partner. Kael drove his elbow into the nearest throat, felt cartilage crunch. One down. The pipe-wielder swung again. Kael caught his wrist, twisted, used the man's momentum to spin him into the wall. Crack. The third man hesitated.
"Run," Kael told the girl.
She didn't move. Frozen. Or maybe smart enough to stay out of the fight's way.
The pipe-man recovered faster than expected. Solid hit to Kael's ribs. Pain lanced through his side. He ignored it, drove the knife into the man's thigh, felt the blade scrape bone before twisting free. The man screamed, collapsed.
The third thug decided discretion was the better part of valor. He fled into the fog without looking back.
Kael leaned against the tannery wall, breathing hard. His ribs screamed protest when he moved. Not broken, probably. Cracked at worst. He'd had worse.
The girl hadn't run. She crouched where she'd stood, watching him with eyes too old for her face.
"You're the one," she whispered. "The Reaver. We heard stories."
Kael sheathed his knife. "Stories get people killed. You should—"
"They said you protect people. That you don't charge for the work." Her voice cracked. "My mother. The cult took her three days ago. From our home. In front of my brother. They said they'd take him too if we told anyone."
Three days. The organized cult activity. The greybeard's words about "demonstrations."
"Where did they take her?"
The girl's chin lifted. Defiance replacing fear. "I'll show you. But you have to promise. If you can't save her, promise you'll get my brother out before they come back for him."
Kael studied her for a long moment. This was exactly the kind of involvement that complicated his plans. Exactly the kind of distraction he couldn't afford.
But she'd called him the Reaver who protects people. And somewhere in the city, a woman sat in a cult holding cell waiting for rescue that might never come.
"What's your brother's name?"
"Mira. He's seven."
"And your mother?"
"Lira. She worked the textile mills." The girl's hands twisted in her ragged dress. "Please. You're all we have left."
Kael exhaled slowly. Three days changed things. Three days meant the cult had already processed their captive, extracted whatever they wanted, decided her fate. He might be walking into a trap. He might be too late for anything but vengeance.
But he'd promised Sera. He'd promised himself. No children. No innocent casualties.
He wouldn't let that promise become ash.
"Show me."
The girl scrambled to her feet and led him deeper into the industrial district's maze of alleys and forgotten passages. Kael followed, one hand pressed against his bruised ribs, the other resting on his knife. The brass coin sat heavy in his pocket. The compact's offer sat heavier in his mind.
Three days. Vex waiting in the wings. The conspiracy spreading its web across Thornwick. And now, a child's plea pulling him toward a cult operation he should be avoiding.
Kael Ashborn had survived two hundred chapters by knowing when to fight and when to disappear.
But some fights chose you.
The girl stopped at a rusted cellar door, half-hidden behind a collapsed awning. She pointed downward with one trembling finger.
"They're underneath. In the old sewer tunnels. There's guards. Four at the entrance, maybe more inside." She swallowed. "My brother is in the building above. Third floor. They kept him as insurance."
Kael nodded. "Stay here. Stay hidden. Don't come out until I bring your mother, or until dawn. If dawn comes and I'm not back—"
"I know." The girl hugged herself. "Thank you. For at least trying."
She vanished into the fog before he could respond.
Kael descended into darkness.
The cellar connected to abandoned sewer lines, barely visible in the faint glow of failing phosphorescent moss. Water dripped somewhere ahead. The smell of rot and human waste hung thick enough to taste. He moved by sound mostly, feet testing each stone before committing weight.
Voices ahead. Two, maybe three. The guards the girl had mentioned.
Kael drew his knife and slowed his breathing. Soul Reaver abilities weren't combat powers—they gave him access to skills and knowledge, not direct offensive capability. But the skills he'd harvested from a hundred fallen enemies told him how to move, how to strike, how to read a fight.
He rounded a corner. Found the first guard slumped against the tunnel wall, throat cut. Blood pooled beneath him, black in the dim light.
Kael froze. That kill was fresh. Minutes old, maybe less.
Someone else was in these tunnels.
He pressed himself into an alcove, listening. Water dripping. Distant shouts echoing from somewhere deeper in the network. And footsteps. Measured. Professional. Coming from behind him.
Kael spun, knife raised.
A woman stood in the shadows. Tall, lean, dressed in black that seemed to drink the light. Her face was half-hidden by a hood, but her eyes caught what little illumination existed and reflected it back like a cat's.
"Reaver." Her voice was flat. Unimpressed. "You move quieter than I expected."
"Vex."
The Shadow Court operative tilted her head. "You got my name. Good. That means someone in the compact talked." She stepped forward, and Kael tensed. "I'm not here for you. Not tonight. I'm here for the cult's captive. The woman they took from the textile district."
"Your employers decide to expand their portfolio?"
"My employers are currently negotiating with elements inside the cult leadership. That woman—" Vex pointed deeper into the tunnels. "—she's relevant to those negotiations. Her removal simplifies things."
Kael's grip tightened on his knife. "She's a hostage. Leverage against her children."
"She's a leverage point." Vex's expression didn't change. "I don't make the rules. I follow them. Same as you."
"Different rules."
"Are there?" She moved closer, and now Kael could see the weapons at her belt. Needle-thin blades in sheaths designed for thigh carry. A garrote wrapped around her left wrist. Nothing obvious. Everything lethal. "The compact offered you a seat at the table. The Shadow Court is offering you something more valuable."
"What's that?"
"Honesty." Vex stopped an arm's length away. "The compact tells you pretty stories about controlled apocalypse and humanity's future. The Shadow Court knows the truth. The seals are failing. Not slowly. Not predictably. The rate of degradation has tripled in the last decade. Whatever's causing it, the Orders can't stop it. The compact can't stop it. The only question is who survives the collapse."
"And you're here to—what? Offer me a place in the new order?"
"I'm here to take that woman before the cult finishes interrogating her." Vex's eyes flicked toward the deeper tunnels. "The cult believes she knows something about the Sepulcher tablets. They're wrong. But their methods are... Thorough. Another six hours and there won't be much left to extract."
Kael felt ice spreading through his chest. The cult had Lira. They thought she knew something about the tablets. They were torturing her for information he possessed.
"The Shadow Court wants the tablets," he said slowly. "That's why you've been watching me."
"The Shadow Court wants to know what the first Reaver discovered before he died. The tablets are one path to that knowledge. You're another." Vex reached into her coat and withdrew a small glass vial. Blue liquid swirled inside, glowing faintly. "This is a memory blank. One dose and the target forgets everything from the last seventy-two hours. The cult's interrogator will wake up with a gap he can't explain. Your mother—" She glanced at him. "—will be spared further pain."
"You want me to use this."
"I want you to choose." Vex pressed the vial into his hand. "The cult's holding cells are two hundred yards ahead. Four guards you can see, two you can't. The interrogator is a Stage 3 Awakened with empathic sight. He'll sense your approach unless you use this—" She produced another vial, filled with something that burned Kael's eyes just looking at it. "—to mask your spiritual signature. The woman is in the third cell on the left. She won't be able to walk on her own."
Kael stared at the vial in his palm. A memory blank. Six hours ago, he'd never heard of such a thing. Now someone expected him to walk into a cult stronghold and use it to save a stranger.
"Why help me?"
"Because my employer wants you functional. Because a Reaver burned out on trauma makes poor leverage." Vex stepped back into the shadows. "Also, because the woman doesn't deserve what's happening to her. Sometimes the job and the morality align."
"And if I fail? If the cult kills me in there?"
"Then the Shadow Court loses an asset and gains a corpse to harvest." Vex's voice carried no inflection. "Your survival matters to me only insofar as it serves the mission. Don't mistake efficiency for compassion."
She began fading into the darkness, already becoming indistinguishable from the shadows themselves.
"Vex." Kael's voice stopped her. "The compact. The agreement between the cult, the Shadow Court, and whoever else. Who benefits?"
The Shadow Court operative paused. "Everyone. And no one. The unsealing will reshape everything. The question is whether that reshaping benefits the human species or just delays our extinction." A dry laugh echoed from nowhere. "Two hundred years of planning, and we still don't know which outcome we're building toward."
Then she was gone.
Kael stood alone in the tunnel, holding two vials and a hundred questions. The sounds of the cult's operation echoed from deeper passages. Somewhere in those cells, a woman named Lira was being tortured for information she didn't have. Somewhere above, a seven-year-old boy waited for rescue that might never come.
The compact wanted him as an asset. The Shadow Court wanted him as leverage. The cult wanted him dead or controlled. And everywhere he turned, the conspiracy's tendrils spread wider, deeper, more entangled with everything he'd come to care about.
Kael uncorked the masking vial. The liquid inside burned like cold fire as he drank it. His spiritual signature vanished, leaving him invisible to anything that hunted by sense alone.
He moved forward into darkness.
The cult's guards fell one by one. Not killed—Kael couldn't afford corpses that would be discovered until he was long gone—but unconscious. Unconscious men couldn't explain what had happened. Couldn't raise alarms. Couldn't do anything except wake up hours later with stories no one would believe.
The hidden guards found him anyway. Stage 1 Awakened, barely trained, their spiritual senses too crude to pierce the masking agent's fog. Kael put them down with precise strikes to vulnerable points. Pressure behind the ear. Sharp edge of palm to the solar plexus. He moved like he'd done this a thousand times, because in a sense, he had. Every skill he'd harvested from every fallen enemy contributed to this moment.
Third cell on the left.
Lira lay on a stone bench, her clothes torn, her skin a map of bruises and burns. She wasn't screaming anymore. Wasn't moving at all except for shallow breaths that rattled in her chest. Her eyes were swollen shut.
Kael pressed two fingers to her throat. Pulse thready but present. He pulled the memory blank from his pocket and uncorked it.
"This might hurt," he whispered. "But it's better than what they're doing."
He poured the contents down her throat. Watched her body arch once, then go limp. Her breathing steadied. Her expression smoothed into something approaching peace. Seventy-two hours of memories, gone. Including everything the cult had done to her.
Kael lifted her. Light. Too light. The mills worked people until they broke, and Lira had been breaking for years.
He carried her out the way he'd come.
The girl was waiting at the cellar entrance, exactly where he'd told her to stay. Her eyes went wide when she saw her mother. Then she was running, stumbling, crying in relief and terror.
"Is she—"
"Alive. Hurt, but alive." Kael lowered Lira gently. "The cult doesn't remember her. Doesn't remember taking her. She'll wake up confused, but unharmed."
"What about my brother?"
Kael looked toward the building's upper floors. "Get your mother somewhere safe. Use the old tanner's loft on Miller's street—the building's condemned, but the upper floor is dry. I'll bring your brother."
"Promise?"
Kael hesitated. The boy was insurance. The cult would know by now that something was wrong with their guards. They'd check on the hostage and find him gone. They'd search the building.
But he'd made a promise. No children.
"Get her out. Now."
The girl nodded, finding strength to lift her mother's arm across her shoulders. They disappeared into the fog.
Kael entered the building.
The third floor was quiet. Too quiet. The kind of quiet that meant ambush or evacuation. He climbed anyway, testing each step, listening for movement.
Room three. The door hung open.
Inside: a small bed, a wooden chair, a boy curled in the corner with his knees pulled to his chest. Seven years old, exactly as the girl had said. Eyes red from crying, hands white-knuckled around nothing.
"It's okay," Kael said. "Your sister sent me."
The boy didn't move. Didn't speak. Just watched with the unblinking stare of someone who'd learned that adults meant pain.
Kael crouched, keeping his hands visible. "I'm going to pick you up now. It's going to be fast. We're going to go somewhere safe. Do you understand?"
A tiny nod.
Kael lifted the boy, felt thin arms wrap around his neck. The kid weighed nothing. Had probably been fed just enough to stay alive, nothing more.
They descended the stairs. Crossed the ground floor. Stepped out into the alley where fog curled between buildings like living things.
"Wait."
The voice came from ahead. From behind. From everywhere at once.
Kael turned.
A figure stood at the alley's mouth, backlit by a gas lamp's orange glow. Tall, robed, scarred across both cheeks in patterns that seemed to writhe in the failing light.
The cult's interrogator. Stage 3 Awakened. The one Vex had warned him about.
"You took my patient." The voice was soft. Almost gentle. "That was rude."
Kael set the boy down, positioning himself between the child and the threat. "He was a hostage. Not yours to keep."
"Everyone belongs to someone." The interrogator stepped forward. His eyes caught the light wrong, reflecting it back with too much intensity. Empathic sight, Vex had called it. The ability to read emotional states, detect lies, sense spiritual fluctuations. "You're masked. Clever. But masks fall. And then I see everything."
"Then see this."
Kael drew his knife. Not a threat. Not a challenge. Just a tool he'd learned to trust.
The interrogator laughed. "You're Stage 4. Sovereign, I believe. Against a Stage 3, you have advantage in raw power. But experience..." He spread his hands, and shadows pooled around his fingers. "...experience cannot be harvested from corpses."
The shadows struck.
Kael moved. The skills he'd taken from fallen enemies guided his body before his conscious mind could process the threat. Duck left. Parry with the knife's flat. Feel the shadow's cold bite as it scraped past his arm instead of wrapping around his throat.
First strike missed. Second came from below. Kael jumped, felt something ripple through the air beneath his feet. Third came from the side—the interrogator had circled while his shadows attacked.
Kael rolled. Came up with his knife slashing outward. Felt the blade bite flesh. The interrogator hissed, jumped back, and for the first time, anger flashed across those too-bright eyes.
"You'll pay for that."
"Add it to my tab."
They clashed again. Shadows versus steel. Empathic manipulation versus harvested instinct. The interrogator pressed forward, overwhelming Kael with technique honed over decades. But Kael had something the older Awakened didn't expect.
The Soul Reaver ability had no combat application. That was what everyone believed.
