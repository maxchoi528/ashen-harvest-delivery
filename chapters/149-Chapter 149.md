"Give it back, or I'll take your fingers one knuckle at a time."
The voice came from an alley mouth two streets east. Kael Ashborn pressed himself against the damp brick of a textile warehouse and listened. Two speakers, maybe three. The accent carried the clipped vowels of the Iron District, where factory smoke ate at lungs and children learned to fight before they learned to read.
Footsteps. Running. Someone came his way.
Kael's hand drifted to the knife at his belt—not for the thieves, but for whatever they might be fleeing from. The gaslight overhead sputtered, casting orange ribbons across the wet cobblestones. A cat bolted from a doorway, fur bristling, eyes wild.
The running footsteps grew closer.
Kael stepped into the open. A boy burst around the corner—fourteen, maybe fifteen, wearing the soot-stained coat of a chimney sweep. The kid saw him and stumbled, nearly eating pavement.
"Back door," Kael said, jerking his chin toward an unmarked door behind him. "Go."
The boy didn't hesitate. He vanished into darkness.
Kael turned to face whatever came next. Two men rounded the corner, both built like dockworkers, both carrying clubs wrapped in wire. They saw Kael and slowed.
"Not your business, stranger." The bigger one spoke first. His nose had been broken at least twice. "Walk away."
Kael spread his hands. "Already walked. Standing still now."
The smaller man's eyes narrowed. He was the thinker of the pair—could see Kael's posture, the worn leather coat that fit too well, the way Kael's weight settled evenly on both feet. Fighter's stance. Experience written in every line.
"He's Awakened," the thinker said.
"So what? Half the city is Awakened."
"Second stage. Look at his eyes."
The bigger man did. Kael let him look. Let him see the flat, measuring gaze that came with having died once and come back wrong. The thinker grabbed his partner's arm.
"Forget it. We can find another sweep."
They left without another word.
Kael exhaled. The night air tasted of coal smoke and river rot, the particular flavor of Thornwick's underbelly. He'd been in the city three weeks now, ever since his last harvest had gone bad and forced him to run. The Undercroft Inn served cheap meals and asked no questions. The owner, a woman named Mira, had silver streaks in her hair and a crossbow behind the bar that she'd never once needed to use.
She hadn't asked where he came from. He'd never offered.
Now he moved through streets he'd memorized, heading for the Hollow Grove—a park in the city's center that had died fifteen years ago when a minor rift tore through and contaminated the soil. Nothing grew there anymore. The city had walled it off and posted guards, but the guards changed shifts at midnight, and the eastern wall had a gap that children used for dares.
Kael had different reasons.
The Grove held graves. Not the clean graves of the Mortician's Guild cemeteries, but old ones—marked with stones, forgotten by everyone except the scholars who wrote papers about them and the city council who wanted the land rezoned for development. When rift energy contaminated an area, it left traces that decayed slowly. Corpses buried in that soil sometimes preserved better than they should.
And preserved corpses meant harvestable memories.
The gap in the eastern wall was exactly where he'd been told. Kael slipped through and dropped into the Grove's dead grass. No grass, —just brown stalks that crumbled when touched. The air here felt different. Thicker. A faint hum pressed against his eardrums, like standing too close to a mill wheel.
He walked until he found the markers. Stones, twelve of them, arranged in a rough circle around a central point where the earth had been dug and refilled. Fresh digging. Someone had been here before him.
Kael crouched. The soil was loose, dark, easy to move. He found the edge of the disturbance and began to clear it away.
His fingers touched wood.
A coffin. Old pine, practically rotted through, but intact enough to hold its shape. Kael tore the remaining boards away with his bare hands, sending splinters into his palms. The smell hit him—sweet and heavy, the particular rot of deep burial. Not fresh enough for good harvesting, but not ancient either. A few weeks old, maybe.
He reached down and touched the corpse.
**[Soul Harvest: You extracted 14 Fate Threads from the remains. 8 Fate Threads acquired.]**
**[Soul Harvest: Memory fragment detected. Subject: Male, age 37, occupation unknown. Memory quality: Moderate. Acquired: Fragment of movement training.]**
Kael pulled back, shaking his hands. Fate Threads represented potential—paths the dead person might have taken, skills they might have developed. Eight threads wasn't much, but it wasn't nothing either. The movement training could be useful once he had time to examine it properly.
But the grave bothered him. Someone had buried this man deliberately, in consecrated ground that was supposed to be sealed. That meant ritual. That meant organization.
Footsteps. Not behind him—everywhere.
Kael straightened and turned in a slow circle. Shapes emerged from the dead trees, maybe a dozen figures in dark cloaks that blended with the night. They carried no visible weapons, but their posture spoke of training. Of purpose.
"Leave the grove," Kael said. His voice carried flat and hard. "I haven't harvested anything worth dying for."
"We know what you are." The speaker stepped forward—a woman, older, with gray threading through her black hair. Her eyes held no fear. "We've been watching you since you entered the city."
"Shadow Court?"
Her lips twitched. Not a smile. "We have many names. But you're close enough."
Kael's hand drifted to his knife. The twelve figures spread out, cutting off his escape routes with practiced precision. This wasn't a street fight. This was a trap, sprung perfectly.
"What do you want?"
"The artifact," the woman said. "We know you found it. We know you've been studying the markings."
"I don't know what you're—"
"Don't." Her voice cracked like a whip. "The tablet. The stone tablet you took from the ruins of the Eastern Archive. We want it back."
Kael's mind raced. He hadn't taken any tablet. He'd barely entered the Eastern Archive—had been chased out by Hollow Knights before he'd gotten more than three rooms deep. But someone had, and the Shadow Court believed it was him.
The woman raised her hand. The figures behind her tensed.
"We can do this the easy way or the hard way. Give us the tablet, and you walk out of here. Keep it, and we take it from your corpse. Either way, we get what we want."
Kael weighed his options. Twelve against one, in terrain that favored neither side. His Soul Reaver ability gave him an edge, but not against trained operatives with a plan. He needed information. He needed time.
"What if I told you I don't have it?"
"Then you're lying, and we proceed accordingly."
"What if I told you I'd help you find it?"
Silence. The woman's eyes narrowed, calculating.
"Why would you do that?"
"Because you're right about something. Someone took that tablet, and whoever has it is playing a game that affects all of us. I want to know who. And I want to know why."
The woman's expression didn't change, but something shifted in her posture. A fractional relaxation. "Soul Reavers don't help people. They use them."
"Soul Reavers do what they have to. Right now, finding that tablet serves my interests. Call it enlightened self-interest."
A long pause. The hum of rift-tainted air filled the silence.
"Malik," the woman said. "Test him."
One of the figures stepped forward—a young man with a scar running from his temple to his jaw. He moved with the fluid grace of someone who'd trained since childhood, and his spiritual pressure rolled outward like a wave.
Ascendant. Stage two.
Kael matched it with his own. The air crackled between them.
The woman watched. "Show us what a Soul Reaver can do."
Malik threw the first strike—a palm thrust aimed at Kael's solar plexus. Kael sidestepped, let the blow graze his ribs, and used the young man's momentum to spin him around. His hand found the knife he'd palmed and pressed the edge against Malik's throat.
The grove went still.
"I could kill him," Kael said quietly. "Right now. Take what he has and be gone before any of you react."
He released Malik and stepped back. The young man stumbled, more embarrassed than hurt, and rejoined the circle. The woman studied Kael with new interest.
"You're not what the legends say."
"Legends are stories. I deal in what's real."
"The tablet contains markings. Symbols that predate the Orders, predate the Concordat, predate everything we know about the seals. The last recorded Soul Reaver carved them into stone before he disappeared. Scholars have spent centuries trying to decode them."
"And the Shadow Court wants them because...?"
"Because whoever holds those markings controls the language of the seals. They can strengthen them or weaken them. Open them or close them. The Six Orders have been fighting for that tablet for generations. We want to ensure it doesn't fall into the wrong hands."
Kael turned the information over in his mind. The Six Orders fighting for control of seal language. Cultists trying to open the 72 rifts. The Great Rift's seal degrading faster than projected. It all fit together like pieces of a puzzle he was only beginning to see.
"Zarek," he said. "Prime Zarek. Is he involved?"
The woman's expression flickered. "Why would you ask about him?"
"Because I've had a bad experience with that name."
Another long pause. Then: "Zarek leads the faction that wants the seals destroyed. The cult activity in Thornwick—it's not random. It's organized. And Zarek is at the center of it."
Kael absorbed this. Zarek, the man who'd framed him, who'd set the Orders against him, whose face he saw every time he closed his eyes. Not just a cultist. A leader.
"The tablet," Kael said. "You think Zarek has it?"
"We think someone in the city has it. Someone powerful enough to access the Eastern Archive and escape without triggering a single alarm. That narrows the field considerably."
"And you want me to find it."
"We want you to use your abilities to locate it. Your Soul Reaver power can sense spiritual residue, can't it? Track signatures, find traces that others miss?"
Kael didn't confirm or deny. The woman smiled thinly.
"The artifact is somewhere in this city. Find it, and we'll tell you everything we know about Zarek's operation. Find it before the Orders do, and we'll make sure you walk away clean."
"And if I refuse?"
"Then we leave, and you navigate the next three weeks alone, with the Inquisition hunting you and Zarek's cult closing in. Your choice."
Kael considered. The Shadow Court offered resources, information, protection—all things he needed. But they also offered chains. Every alliance had a cost.
"One condition," he said. "I work alone. You give me information, I work the case. No handlers, no observers, no midnight visits to check my progress."
The woman nodded slowly. "Agreed. My name is Vex. When you have something concrete, leave a mark on the north pillar of the Ironbridge—three horizontal lines, like this." She drew them in the air with her finger. "We'll find you."
She turned and walked into the darkness. The eleven figures followed, melting away until Kael stood alone in the grove.
He looked down at the grave he'd disturbed. The corpse stared back, empty-eyed, already starting to decay again now that the harvest was complete. Who had this man been? What had he known?
The question nagged at him as he climbed back through the wall and into the city's gaslit streets.
The Ironbridge was a rusted span that crossed the Thornmere River near the textile district. Kael found the north pillar and examined it in the dim light—a massive block of corroded metal covered in years of graffiti, rust, and pollution.
He could work with this.
First, he needed to know what the tablet looked like. Vex had described markings, but that wasn't enough. He needed dimensions, materials, any distinguishing features. The Shadow Court had been hunting this artifact for generations; they had to have records, sketches, descriptions.
Kael headed for the Ashworth Archives—a public library in the merchant district that kept records of everything from shipping manifests to historical surveys. The night librarian was a drowsy old man who barely looked up as Kael entered.
"Research?" the man asked.
"Historical."
"Aisle seven. Don't light anything on fire."
Kael found the right section and began pulling volumes at random. Most were useless—histories of the Abyssal Wars that repeated the same propaganda the Orders fed to children. But one book, a crumbling tome labeled "Esoteric Studies, Vol. 3," contained a passage that made him pause.
*The First Reaver carved his knowledge into seven tablets, each representing a different aspect of seal manipulation. These were the Sepulcher Tablets, named for the chamber in which they were first stored. The location of this chamber has been lost to time, but rumors persist of a hidden vault beneath the old temple district, sealed by methods that predate the Concordat...*
Seven tablets. Not one. And a hidden vault.
Kael photographed the relevant pages with a small device he'd acquired—a modified clockwork camera that could capture images in low light. The rest of the book yielded nothing else useful.
He left the archives at nearly three in the morning and walked the empty streets toward the Undercroft Inn. The city slept uneasily, aware on some level that something was wrong, that the seals were weakening, that the Abyss pressed closer every day. But they didn't know how to fight it. Didn't know what to fear.
Kael knew. He'd seen the Entities, felt their hunger through the membrane of reality. He knew what waited on the other side of the Veil.
And now he had a lead on how to stop them—or, more likely, how to use the seals for his own purposes.
The Inn's common room was empty when he entered. Mira was wiping down the bar, her silver-streaked hair escaping from a hasty braid. She looked up as he passed.
"Bad night?" she asked.
"Productive night."
She accepted that without comment. Kael climbed the stairs to his room—a narrow cell with a straw mattress and a window that looked out over the alley behind the building.
He sat on the bed and examined the Fate Threads he'd harvested. They glowed faintly in his palm, faint threads of possibility that connected past to future. With time and practice, he could weave them into new abilities, new skills, new ways of surviving.
But first, the tablet.
He pulled out the photographs from the archives and studied them again. The passage mentioned a vault beneath the old temple district. The old temple district was three miles northwest of his current location, near the city's original founding site. Most of the buildings there had been abandoned for decades after a minor rift contaminated the water supply.
Kael's map showed a network of underground passages that predated the city's current layout. Old sewer systems, forgotten catacombs, the foundations of structures that had been demolished centuries ago. The perfect place to hide something that no one was supposed to find.
He had a direction now. Tomorrow, he'd start exploring.
But as he lay back on the straw mattress, staring at the water-stained ceiling, his mind wouldn't quiet. Vex's words echoed: *The cult activity in Thornwick—it's not random. It's organized.*
Cult activity. The Shadow Court. Zarek. The sealed artifact. The degrading Great Rift. It all pointed toward something larger, something coordinated, something that had been building for years or decades or even centuries.
And somewhere in that conspiracy, Kael Ashborn had been placed—framed for a crime he didn't commit, hunted by the Inquisition, and now recruited by a shadow organization that wanted to use him for their own purposes.
He wasn't a hero. He wasn't a villain. He was a tool, and the only way to avoid being used was to use himself first.
Sleep came slowly, and when it did, he dreamed of doors. Doors that opened onto nothing. Doors that opened onto darkness. Doors that opened onto something so vast and terrible that his sleeping mind flinched away from it.
He woke before dawn with the taste of ash on his tongue and a single thought burning in his consciousness:
The vault was real. The tablet was real. And someone else was looking for it too.
Kael dressed quickly and moved to the window. The alley below was empty, but not silent. Somewhere in the pre-dawn darkness, footsteps echoed against cobblestones. One set. Deliberate. Coming closer.
He reached for his knife.
The footsteps stopped directly below his window.
A voice rose from the darkness—not shouting, not threatening, just speaking. Calm. Measured. A voice that knew it would be heard.
"Mr. Ashborn. The Shadow Court isn't the only organization interested in your talents. When you're ready to discuss the tablet, leave a sign on the Ironbridge's south pillar. Three circles, interlocking."
Silence.
Kael waited thirty heartbeats, then risked a glance through the window. The alley was empty. Whoever had spoken was already gone.
He leaned against the wall, knife still in hand, and let out a slow breath.
The game was getting more complicated. But that was fine. Complicated meant multiple angles. Multiple angles meant multiple chances.
He dressed, gathered his equipment, and headed for the door. The hunt was on, and this time, the prey didn't know it was being hunted.
The streets of Thornwick stretched before him, gray and wet and full of secrets. Somewhere in this city, a vault waited to be found. Somewhere, a tablet held the key to power that the Orders would kill to possess or destroy.
And somewhere, watching from the shadows, someone else was playing the same game.
Kael smiled thinly and stepped out into the morning fog.
Let them watch. Let them scheme. In the end, there could only be one Reaver, and he wasn't planning on being the one who lost.
