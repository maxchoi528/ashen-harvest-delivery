The cultist's blood pooled black against the cobblestones, still steaming in the night air. Kael crouched beside the body, fingers hovering over the wound—not to heal, never to heal—but to take.
Three hours ago, the man had been leading a flowion of hooded figures through the Ashveil Graveyard. Now he was a corpse, throat opened by someone faster, someone who had slit his neck and vanished before Kael could close the distance.
"Why run?" Kael muttered, scanning the fog-shrouded tombs. "You had a whole ceremony going."
The air tasted of copper and something older—brine, rot, the mineral tang of ley-line leakage. Thornwick's underbelly constantly wept spiritual energy through cracks in the city's foundation. The Orders called it a health hazard. The cultists called it a sacrament.
Kael pressed his palm flat against the cooling flesh.
*Soul Harvest: You extracted 3.7 grams of spiritual residue from the remains. Memory Fragment acquired.*
Information flooded his mind. Not the cultist's name—he'd never learned it—but fragments of tonight's gathering. A basement beneath a tannery on Dredger's Row. A woman with silver-streaked hair conducting the ritual. Words spoken in that guttural tongue the Abyssal cultists favored: *She opens. She hungers. The seal thins.*
"Damn." Kael withdrew his hand, wiping residue on his coat. "Organized. That's new."
He'd encountered cultists before—the desperate, the devout, the dying. They operated in cells, paranoid and fractured, each group worshipping different Entities based on which texts they'd scavenged. But this was different. This cultist had carried a brass sigil matching others Kael had seen painted on walls throughout Fogmire District. A stylized eye weeping blood, centered on a six-pointed star.
Someone was consolidating the Abyssal faithful. Someone with resources, with reach, with a plan beyond "summon minor Entity, cause chaos, die badly."
Kael rose and moved deeper into the graveyard, following a trail only he could see. The Soul Reaver ability painted the spiritual world in shades of gray and crimson—living beings burned bright, the dead left fading echoes, and Abyssal corruption manifested as wrongness, a distortion in how shadows fell.
The corruption was everywhere tonight. Thin tendrils of it curled around gravestones, seeped into the soil, hung in the air like perfume. The seals keeping Thornwick's rifts contained were weakening. He'd felt it for weeks now—a pressure building behind his eyes, a taste of ozone before a storm.
The city was sick. And something was making it worse.
A figure emerged from behind a mausoleum, moving with the loose-limbed gait of someone who had stopped being entirely human. Kael's hand found the knife at his belt—not his primary weapon, not against the dead or dying, but useful for the living who needed convincing.
"You're not one of theirs." The figure's voice scraped like metal on stone. "I watched you. You took from him."
"Observant." Kael kept his feet planted, weight balanced. "What are you?"
"Concerned." The figure stepped closer, and moonlight revealed what the shadows had hidden. A man—or what had been a man—with skin like cured leather and eyes that held no whites, just gleaming darkness. Entity-touched. One of the faithful who'd been... Rewarded. "The Order of Dawn burned our gathering last week. Thirty dead. Children among them."
"I'm not Order."
"No. You're something worse." The creature smiled, showing teeth filed to points. "A corpse-eater. A thief of souls. The Orders fear you. The Entities desire you. And tonight, you hunt the wrong prey."
Kael's grip tightened on the knife. "Enlighten me."
"The woman your cultist served—she's not cult. She's Order. Iron Vanguard, to be precise." The creature spread its hands, a gesture of mock openness. "They've been feeding the seals. Weakening them deliberately. The Whispering Convergence wants the rifts opened, yes, but not yet. Not until they're ready. Your Iron Vanguard friends are three steps ahead of everyone."
"Why tell me this?"
"Because you can do what we cannot. You're invisible to them. A shadow among shadows." The creature's dark eyes fixed on him. "Find the Basement Sanctum. Stop the ritual. Or don't. But know that when the seals break, when the 72 wake, the Entities will remember who had the power to stop it and chose not to."
It turned and walked back into the shadows. Kael let it go. Chasing entity-touched through a graveyard at midnight was excellent way to get killed, and he needed his attention focused on the living.
*She opens. The seal thins.*
The cultist's memory hadn't shown him everything, but it had shown him enough. The ritual was tonight. The basement beneath the tannery. And he had hours to investigate before the next phase began.
---
The tannery on Dredger's Row stank like rotting dreams. Kael paused at the alley entrance, cataloguing threats. Two guards at the rear entrance—hired muscle, armed with clubs, barely Awakened if at all. A watchman on the roof, armed with what looked like a crossbow. Standard security for an illegal operation.
Not standard enough.
He circled the building, staying low, using garbage bins and laundry lines for cover. The Soul Reaver ability let him sense the spiritual energy of those nearby—these guards burned with the pale light of the spiritually inert, barely more alive than the corpses he harvested. Easy prey, if it came to that.
It wouldn't come to that. Not yet.
The basement entrance was a trapdoor behind stacked crates of salted hides. Kael slipped inside, dropping into darkness that pressed against his eyes. He landed in a crouch on damp stone, listening.
Voices below. Footsteps. The wet sound of something being poured.
He descended the stairs slowly, keeping his weight on the edges of the steps to minimize creaking. The basement opened into a space that had once been a storage cellar—now transformed into something between a ritual chamber and an operating theater.
Fifteen people occupied the space. Twelve stood in a circle around a central altar, chanting in that guttural tongue. Three more moved among them, directing traffic like stage managers at a particularly apocalyptic play.
And on the altar—
Kael's stomach clenched.
A woman, barely out of her teens, strapped to a stone slab. Her arms and legs were bound with leather straps, her mouth gagged with cloth. Her eyes, wide with terror, found his across the room. She couldn't scream. She couldn't move. She could only watch as one of the robed figures approached her with a ceremonial blade.
*Soul Harvest: Target identified. Living sacrifice detected. Harvesting is not possible on living targets.*
The notification burned in his mind, unwanted and unwelcome. He knew the rules. The dead surrendered their essence freely. The living fought to keep it. Harvesting from the living wasn't theft—it was murder.
And he was not a murderer.
Not like this.
The lead figure turned toward the stairs, raising a hand. The chanting stopped.
"We have a guest." The voice was cultured, educated, utterly calm. "How delightful. I was wondering when the Order of Dawn would send an observer."
Kael stepped into the light. Let them see him. Let them underestimate him.
"Not Dawn," he said. "Just passing through."
"Pity." The figure descended from the altar, pulling back her hood to reveal a woman in her middle years, iron-gray hair pulled back in a severe bun, eyes the color of winter storms. She wore the functional black leathers of the Iron Vanguard, and her hands bore the calluses of someone who had spent decades handling weapons. "I was hoping for entertainment. Still, a soul reaver is almost as useful."
"You know what I am."
"I know what you are. The question is whether you understand what you could become." She gestured at the ritual circle. "You've spent your short career harvesting the dregs—the desperate, the devout, the dying. Small power from small souls. But here, tonight, we offer you something far greater."
She snapped her fingers. Two of the robed figures hauled another body onto a second altar—fresh, still bleeding from the ritual wound in its chest. An Awakened, by the look of the spiritual residue clinging to the corpse. Someone with training, with power, with skills worth stealing.
"Harvest him," the woman commanded. "Show us what a true soul reaver can do. Then we can discuss partnership."
Kael approached the altar. The corpse's eyes stared at the ceiling, empty and accusing. He'd harvested Awakened before—not often, not safely, but he had. The power cost was nothing. The political cost was everything.
The notification pulsed in his mind, waiting for confirmation.
*Soul Harvest: Awakened-class target detected. Estimated yield: 12.3 grams spiritual essence, plus potential skill fragment. Confirm harvest?*
He placed his hand on the corpse's chest. Felt the fading warmth, the lingering resistance of a soul not yet fully departed. It would fight him if he pushed. The Awakened always fought.
Or he could... Take what was offered.
*Soul Harvest: You extracted 12.3 grams of spiritual essence from the remains. Skill Fragment acquired: Iron Skin (lesser).*
Power flooded through him—not much, not for someone at his stage, but enough. The Iron Skin ability would his physical defense for a few hours, letting him shrug off blows that would break ordinary bones. Useful. Practical. The kind of power that kept you alive when the killing started.
"Well done." The woman's smile didn't reach her eyes. "Now. Shall we discuss what comes next?"
Kael withdrew his hand. "I don't partner with people who sacrifice the innocent."
"Innocent?" She laughed, a short, sharp sound. "She's a vessel. A conduit. The Entities we serve have been locked behind seals for millennia, bleeding their power into a world that forgot them. Tonight, we begin the flow of reversal. The seals will thin. The barriers will weaken. And when the 72 finally emerge—"
"They'll burn everything."
"They'll restore balance. The Orders have grown fat and complacent, hoarding spiritual power while the world decays. The Entities will rebuild. Properly, this time. With purpose."
"You're insane."
"I'm practical." She pulled a vial from her belt—soul-essence, high-grade, enough to fuel a dozen Awakened for a month. "This is yours if you walk away. Or you can stay and die with the others when the ritual completes."
Kael looked at the vial. Looked at the bound woman on the altar. Looked at the robed figures who had stopped chanting and now watched him with predatory interest.
Three against fifteen. The bound woman would die either way. The ritual would complete either way.
He couldn't save everyone. He'd learned that lesson in blood and ash on the night of his own execution.
But he could take one more thing. One more piece of power. One more step along the path that had started when he'd crawled out of his own grave.
"Your ritual requires a soul anchor," he said. "Something to bind the energy while the seal thins. That's why you need her alive."
The woman's expression flickered. "Clever."
"But taking her essence while she's still bound—that's not harvesting. That's killing." Kael stepped closer to the altar. "I don't kill the innocent."
"You kill plenty. You've just convinced yourself the ones you murder deserve it."
"Maybe they do. Maybe they don't." His hand found the woman's restraints. "But she doesn't."
The first guard lunged for him. Kael pivoted, driving his elbow into the man's throat, following with a knife strike that opened the guard's femoral artery. Blood sprayed. The man fell.
*Soul Harvest: Target deceased. Harvest available upon contact.*
The basement erupted into chaos. Rooted figures broke their circle, reaching for weapons. The woman—the Iron Vanguard officer—drew a blade that hummed with spiritual energy. The watchman on the roof had descended through a hidden entrance, crossbow raised.
Kael freed the woman's bonds, ignoring the bolts that sprouted from the wall beside his head. One caught his shoulder, punching through coat and flesh before the Iron Skin ability flared, slowing its progress but not stopping it. Pain flared white-hot.
"Run," he commanded. "Stairs behind me. Don't stop."
She didn't argue. The woman scrambled off the altar and sprinted for the stairs, surviving a crossbow bolt that shattered a stone step inches from her foot. She reached the exit and vanished into the darkness above.
Smart woman. Living women were useful women.
The Iron Vanguard officer circled him, blade raised. "You're making a mistake, Reaver. We're offering you a place in the new order."
"I'm already in an order." Kael drew his own blade—a simple steel weapon, nothing special, purchased from a pawn shop two months ago. "The Order of Not Dying Tonight."
He attacked.
The first strike came fast and low, aimed at his kidneys. Kael twisted aside, feeling the blade whisper past his ribs. Iron Skin held. Barely. He countered with a slash that the woman deflected, following with a pommel strike that rattled his teeth.
They exchanged blows in the confined space—another three guards joining the fray, then two more. Kael carved through them methodically, taking wounds he couldn't afford, spending the Iron Skin ability's reserves faster than the notifications could track.
*Skill Depleted: Iron Skin. Physical defense reduced to baseline.*
A blade found his side. Another opened his arm. Kael staggered, blood streaming from a dozen wounds.
The woman pressed her advantage, driving him back toward the stairs. "You're dying, Reaver. Accept it."
Kael let himself fall.
She lunged. Her blade descended.
He caught her wrist with both hands and twisted, using her momentum to pull her off-balance. They crashed into the altar, stone scraping against stone. She tried to rise. He drove his blade into her shoulder, pinning her to the rock.
"Not yet," he gasped.
The cultists had stopped. They watched as their leader struggled against the weapon embedded in her flesh, blood pooling beneath her. The woman raised her hand—surrender or signal, he couldn't tell.
"Go," she whispered. "Run back to your masters. Tell them the seals will hold another day."
They ran. The basement emptied in seconds, leaving Kael alone among the bodies.
*Soul Harvest: 4 targets harvested. Total yield: 23.8 grams spiritual essence. Skill Fragments acquired: Iron Skin (lesser - duplicate), Spiritual Sense (greater).*
The notifications came faster now, his ability processing the deaths automatically. He felt the power settle into his core—not corrupting, not costly, just there. A bank account of stolen souls, waiting to be spent.
He pulled his blade from the Iron Vanguard officer's shoulder. She screamed, then fell silent, eyes unfocused.
"Tell me about the seals," he demanded. "Tell me what you're really doing."
She laughed, blood bubbling at her lips. "Weakening them. Feeding them corruption. The 72 are waking, Reaver. Not tonight. Not this month. But soon. And when they rise, you'll wish you'd taken my offer."
"How soon?"
"Months. Maybe a year. The Whisper King knows the timeline." Her eyes rolled back. "Find him if you want answers. Find him if you want to stop us. But know this—"
She died. Her soul departed, refusing capture, leaving only a body and a mystery.
Kael searched the basement quickly. Found ledgers detailing supply shipments. Found letters bearing the seal of an Iron Vanguard outpost in the city's northern quarter. Found a map of Thornwick's sewer system with certain tunnels highlighted in red.
Someone was using the city's infrastructure to spread corruption. Someone with military training, military resources, military connections.
And somewhere in this mess, the Whisper King held the answers. The Whisper King, who apparently knew when the seals would break.
Kael climbed the stairs, leaving the bodies behind. The night air hit him like a slap, cold and clean after the stench of blood and ritual.
He had leads now. Connections. A map of what came next.
But the wounds were deep, and the night was far from over.
---
Four hours later, Kael sat in the back room of a dead man's shop, stitching his own wounds by lamplight. The smell of alcohol and needle thread filled the air. His hands trembled—from blood loss, from exhaustion, from something he refused to name.
The bound woman's name was Maren. She'd been taken from her home three days ago, chosen as a vessel because her spiritual resonance matched some ancient requirement. She hadn't known why. She hadn't known anything except fear and pain and the certainty that she was going to die.
She'd given him what information she could before disappearing into the city—her captors had spoken of meetings, of a "convergence point" where all the threads gathered. An old temple in the Ashveil District, abandoned since the last Abyssal incursion two centuries ago.
Kael added it to his growing map of the conspiracy. The Iron Vanguard officer had been wrong about one thing: the seals weren't just weakening naturally. They were being actively sabotaged. And if the cult activity was this organized, this well-funded, this widespread—
Then someone very powerful wanted the 72 unleashed. Someone with access to military resources, to ancient texts, to the knowledge of which seals were weakest and how to break them.
Someone like the Whisper King.
*Soul Harvest: You have harvested 8 Awakened-class targets within the past 30 days. Suspicious activity flagged. Recommend reduced operational profile.*
The notification was new. Either his ability was evolving, or something else was watching him through the system. Neither option was comforting.
Kael finished the last stitch and wrapped his torso in clean bandages. The wounds would heal. They always healed, thanks to the spiritual energy saturating his system. But they left scars, and the scars left memories, and the memories reminded him that every harvest was a choice.
He could have walked away. Let the ritual complete. Let the seals weaken a little more.
He hadn't.
Why?
The question lingered as he pulled on a fresh shirt and coat, checking his blades, preparing to move. He told himself it was pragmatism—if the seals broke completely, he'd lose access to the spiritual energy that fueled his abilities. Self-interest, pure and simple.
But Maren's face kept appearing in his mind. Those wide eyes, that desperate hope when he'd cut her bonds.
He'd saved her because he could. Because the cost of not saving her was higher than the cost of trying.
Maybe that made him a hero. Maybe it just made him a fool playing at righteousness in a city that had no room for either.
The door opened. Lyra slipped inside, her dark hair plastered to her face by rain, her expression grim.
"You're hurt."
"I'll live." He grabbed his pack. "What did you find?"
"The Shadow Court knows about the cult activity. They're watching it. Not interfering—just watching." She paused. "Someone there believes the seals need to break. Someone high up."
"The Whisper King."
Lyra's silence was answer enough.
"I need to find him," Kael said. "I need to understand what's really happening."
"That might be impossible. No one knows who he is. No one knows where he operates. He's a ghost story, Kael. A legend cultists tell to frighten each other."
"Then I'll find his followers. Work my way up the chain." He moved toward the door. "The Ashveil District temple. Maren said convergence point. If I'm right—"
"If you're right, you'll walk into a trap set by people who've been preparing for years."
"Then I'll deal with the trap."
Lyra grabbed his arm. Her grip was stronger than expected, her eyes fierce. "You're bleeding through your bandages. You need rest. You need food. You need to stop throwing yourself at enemies you can't defeat."
"When has that ever stopped me?"
"Never. That's the problem." She released him. "Fine. But I'm coming with you."
"It's dangerous."
"So is letting you go alone. The Shadow Court is watching you, Kael. The Orders are watching you. Now the cultists know what you can do. If you keep operating without backup, you'll end up dead in some gutter, and I'll have to explain to your ghost why I let you be stupid."
He considered arguing. Decided against it. "Stay behind me. Don't engage unless I signal. And if things go wrong—"
"Run. I know." She checked her own blade. "Same as always."
They left together, stepping into the rain-slicked streets of Thornwick. The city sprawled before them, dark and hostile, every shadow hiding secrets that wanted to stay buried.
Somewhere in the Ashveil District, a temple waited. Somewhere in the city, the Whisper King plotted the end of everything.
And somewhere in Kael's blood, the harvested power of a dozen dead Awakened hummed with potential, ready to be spent.
The game had changed. The stakes had risen.
And the night was far from over.
Behind them, in the darkness of an alleyway, a figure watched them go. The figure carried no light, wore no identifying marks, and spoke no words.
But when Kael glanced back—instinct, paranoia, the survival sense that had kept him alive this long—the figure raised one hand.
A wave. A greeting. A warning.
Then nothing. Empty darkness where a person had been.
Kael filed it away. One more mystery for the pile.
The night continued. The hunt resumed. And somewhere in Thornwick's rotting heart, the seals thinned, and the Entities stirred in their ancient prisons, and the countdown to catastrophe ticked steadily forward.
Tick. Tock. Tick. Tock.
