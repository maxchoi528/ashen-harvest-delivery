The tomb door groaned as Kael drove his shoulder into it. Stone shrieked against stone, the sound tearing through the silent undercity, dust cascading from ancient hinges that hadn't moved in centuries. The grit coated his tongue, dry and chalky, mixing with the taste of stale air. 
"Careful," Vex muttered, his pale form flickering in the darkness. The wraith's luminescence cast sickly green reflections on the wet stone. "These wards are older than the city above. One wrong trigger and—"
"I know." Kael steadied his breathing, his chest heaving from the effort of breaching the seal. He let his spiritual sense probe the corridor beyond. The air tasted of copper and decay, not the expected mustiness of sealed crypts. It was a sharp, metallic tang that coated the back of his throat. Someone had been here. Recently. 
He stepped inside, his boots crunching on shattered debris. His lantern's flame cast dancing shadows across walls carved with symbols he couldn't read—yet recognized. The same angular script that branded his left forearm whenever he harvested a soul. The mark of the Soul Reaver. The stone walls wept moisture, slick and cold to the touch. Another Reaver had stood in this exact chamber, long ago.
Vex drifted ahead, his ghostly form passing through collapsed pillars and scattered bones. The wraith's expression shifted from curiosity to something approaching reverence. "Kael. You need to see this."
The main burial chamber opened before him, vast as a cathedral. Pillars rose into darkness, their surfaces etched with the same angular script. Alcoves lined the walls, each containing a stone sarcophagus. Dozens of them. Scores. The sheer scale of it pressed down on Kael, the weight of the earth above reminding him of his depth. 
And at the chamber's center, on a raised dais, stood an empty sarcophagus. Its lid had been cast aside and shattered against the far wall. 
"Someone took him," Kael breathed. His voice echoed, swallowed by the vast darkness. "They took the first Reaver."
"The seal on this tomb wasn't meant to keep people out." Vex pointed to the shattered lid. "It was meant to keep something in."
Kael approached the central sarcophagus. His boots scraped against the dusty floor. Spiritual energy radiated from its interior, thick and potent despite the body's absence. It made his teeth ache, a low vibration settling in his jaw. He reached toward the stone edge—
"Don't."
He froze. The voice came from behind him, feminine and cold. Kael turned slowly, hand dropping to the dagger at his hip.
A woman stepped from the shadows between two pillars. She wore the black leathers of the Shadow Court, her face half-hidden by a hood. A silver sigil marked her collar—the same sigil that had appeared on his arm three weeks ago, after he'd harvested a cultist in the Drowning District.
"You're trespassing, Soul Reaver." She didn't draw a weapon. She didn't need to. Kael could feel the pressure of her spiritual presence, Sovereign-class at minimum, pushing against his skin like a change in barometric pressure. "This place is forbidden to your kind."
"My kind?" Kael kept his voice level. "You mean the people whose bloodline you're all watching?"
The woman's eyes narrowed. "You've been busy. The Veiled Market, the cultist ambushes—someone's been feeding you information."
"Someone's been feeding me corpses. I draw my own conclusions."
She laughed, a short bitter sound that bounced off the stone walls. "The first Reaver drew the same conclusions. Thought he understood the world. Thought he could control what he'd become." She gestured at the empty sarcophagus. "We found him three centuries ago. He's still alive, in a manner of speaking. The Entities keep their promises."
Kael's blood chilled. His fingers tightened around the dagger's hilt, knuckles blanching white. "Promises?"
"You didn't think you were the first, did you? The Soul Reaver ability doesn't appear randomly. It was given. To fight the Abyss, a thousand years ago, when the seals were young and the Entities broke through at will." The woman's voice carried the weight of recitation, words memorized and repeated across generations. "The first Reaver succeeded. He sealed the rifts, bound the worst of them. But the power was too dangerous. Too hungry. The Six Orders agreed he had to be contained."
"Contained." Kael stared at the empty sarcophagus. "You mean imprisoned."
"I mean transformed. The Entities offered him eternity. He accepted." She stepped closer, her boots silent on the stone floor. "You're the latest in a long line, Kael Ashborn. The bloodline persists. The ability passes. And every few generations, someone like you finds this place." Her hand moved to her belt, where a small crystal vial glinted in the lantern light. "The Council sent me to collect you. Willingly or otherwise."
"The Council." Kael's jaw tightened. "The Six Orders' Council?"
"The Orders were founded to finish what the first Reaver started. To maintain the seals. To hunt down any... Excess power that threatens the balance." Her eyes glittered in the lantern light. "Your ability is an excess, Reaver. A mistake that keeps recurring. The Council has debated your fate for months. Some wanted you dead. Others wanted you harvested for research." She smiled thinly. "I argued for something more useful."
"Useful." Kael felt his skin crawl. "You want to use me."
"I want to use what you can do. The rifts are failing, Kael. All seventy-two of them. The seals the first Reaver created are rotting faster than we can maintain them. We need—" She stopped abruptly, head snapping toward the chamber's entrance.
Distant footsteps echoed. Multiple sets, moving with military precision. The sound of heavy boots striking stone in unison.
"Ah." The woman's expression flickered. "The Iron Vanguard patrol. We have maybe two minutes before they breach the lower wards."
"You're not Shadow Court." Kael's hand closed around his dagger's hilt. "The Shadow Court doesn't work with the Iron Vanguard."
"The Shadow Court works with everyone. We're the Orders' shadow, remember? The things they can't officially acknowledge." She reached into her cloak, producing a second crystal vial—this one filled with swirling darkness. "This is soul-essence harvested from a Demigod-class Awakened. Worth more than a fleet of warships. The Iron Vanguard commander wants it. The Shadow Court wants the Reaver. We're going to make an exchange, right here, right now."
"And if I refuse?"
"Then the Iron Vanguard takes you by force, the Shadow Court loses its asset, and the seals fail within the decade." She pressed the vial into his free hand. "I'm offering you a choice. Help me close this deal, and I'll tell you where the first Reaver really is. Help me make the Vanguard commander believe you're being handed over, and I'll let you escape with the knowledge you need to do something about those failing seals."
The footsteps grew louder. Kael could hear shouted commands in the old military dialect, the clank of armor, the distinctive hum of spiritual weapons being activated. The vibration rattled the teeth in his skull.
"Why would you help me escape?" Kael studied the vial in his hand. The darkness within pulsed like a heartbeat. "What does the Shadow Court gain?"
"The Shadow Court gains nothing. I do." The woman's voice dropped, her mask of professional detachment cracking for just an instant. "My grandmother was harvested by an Order. Officially, she died fighting cultists near the Crimson Wastes. they drained her soul to fuel a seal repair. I've spent thirty years climbing the Court's ranks, waiting for a chance to—" She stopped herself. "It doesn't matter. What matters is you're going to take that soul-essence, harvest the corpse in the third alcove on your left, and then run when I give the signal."
Kael looked toward the indicated alcove. A body lay there, wrapped in ceremonial cloth. The spiritual energy emanating from it was faint but present—fresh enough to yield something valuable. His Reaver senses tingled.
"Third alcove," he repeated. "A corpse."
"An Awakened corpse. A member of the Order of Dawn, killed in the initial breach two days ago. We preserved him specifically for you." The woman smiled, though it didn't reach her eyes. "Consider it a signing bonus. Everything you extract from him is yours to keep. We only want the Reaver himself."
The chamber's entrance exploded inward. Stone shrapnel shrieked through the air, clattering against the distant pillars. 
Iron-clad soldiers poured through the gap, their formation tight and disciplined. Spiritual energy crackled along their weapons—standard-issue resonance blades, designed to disrupt supernatural threats. At their head strode a mountain of a man, his armor marked with the Iron Vanguard's crossed-hammers insignia. 
"Shadow Agent." The commander's voice boomed through the chamber. "You were instructed to deliver the Reaver, not negotiate."
"I am delivering him." The woman stepped aside, gesturing toward Kael. "The transaction is complete. Payment upon confirmation of delivery."
The commander's eyes swept over Kael, lingering on the dagger, the lantern, the vial of soul-essence clutched in his left hand. "Empty his pockets. Verify the mark."
Two soldiers advanced on Kael. He held still as rough hands patted him down, confiscating his secondary blade, a pouch of coins, and three small vials of harvested essence. They found the mark on his forearm and compared it against a worn parchment.
"Soul Reaver," one soldier announced. "Confirmed. Classified threat level: Omega."
Omega. Kael had heard rumors about that classification. Entities received Omega ratings. Things that could end cities. His pulse hammered against his ribs.
The commander nodded curtly. "Seal him. We'll transport to the Vault for processing."
Processing. Another word that meant harvesting. The Iron Vanguard maintained a prison specifically for Awakened criminals, where the soul-essence of inmates was slowly drained to fuel the seal network. It wasn't execution—it was worse. A living death that could stretch for decades.
The soldiers surrounded him, resonance blades humming. The commander raised his hand to give the signal.
"Now," the Shadow Court woman said quietly.
Kael moved.
He lunged toward the third alcove, ignoring the shouts of alarm. The soldiers pursued, but they were armored, weighted down, while Kael was unencumbered. He slammed his shoulder into the first alcove, dropping to his knees beside the ceremonial-wrapped body. 
His hands found the corpse's face. Cold skin, slack muscles, the distinctive texture of a soul recently departed. He pressed his palms flat against the dead flesh.
The Reaver ability activated.
Soul Harvest: You extracted 7 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Memory Fragment acquired: Order of Dawn defensive formations (partial). Skill Acquired: Resonance Disruption (basic).
Knowledge flooded his mind—the tactical layouts of the Order of Dawn's southern garrisons, the weakness points in Vanguard armor, the emergency protocols for seal breaches. The dead Awakened had been a strategist, a commander who'd seen decades of Abyssal skirmishes.
Kael rose, drawing on the harvested energy. His body felt stronger, faster. The Resonance Disruption skill manifested as a subtle vibration in his fingertips.
"You—" The commander's face contorted with rage. "Kill the Shadow Agent! The Reaver is—"
The Shadow Court woman was already moving. She ducked under the first soldier's blade, drove a poisoned dagger into the second's throat, and hurled a flash-bang crystal at the commander's feet. Light erupted through the chamber, blinding everyone without spiritual shielding. 
Kael used the chaos. He sprinted toward a gap in the soldiers' formation, ducking under a clumsy sword swing, shouldering through a gap between two Vanguard knights. Behind him, he heard the woman shouting in the old military tongue—a retreat signal, fake or genuine.
The corridor beyond the tomb stretched into darkness. Kael ran. His boots splashed through puddles of stagnant water. 
The tomb's lower levels were a maze of collapsed passages and flooded chambers. He navigated by spiritual sense alone, trusting the residual energy of the first Reaver that permeated these walls. The knowledge guided him, leading him deeper instead of up. The air grew colder, damp and thick with the scent of earth.
A hidden passage opened where there had been solid stone. Kael plunged through, leaving the shouts and clash of combat behind. 
He emerged into cold night air. 
The tomb had deposited him outside the city walls, in a copse of skeletal trees near the Thornwick graveyard. Stars wheeled overhead, and the distant glow of the city's gas lamps painted the horizon orange. The frost on the dead leaves crunched beneath his boots. 
Kael bent double, gasping. His hands trembled from the harvest, his mind crowded with borrowed memories. The dead commander wanted to protect the seals. Wanted to believe the Orders were righteous. And the Orders had rewarded that loyalty by feeding him to the machine. 
He looked down at the soul-essence vial still clutched in his grip. The glass was slick with his sweat. The woman had given it to him deliberately. A gift, or a trap?
Footsteps crunched through the frozen undergrowth. Kael straightened, raising his harvested Resonance Disruption, ready to tear spiritual energy apart.
"Easy." The Shadow Court woman stepped into view, her black leathers spattered with blood that wasn't hers. The copper scent of it filled the night air. "The deal stands. You're free to go."
"The commander?"
"Dead. The Shadow Court doesn't tolerate failures, and he failed to secure you." She tilted her head, studying him with renewed interest. "You harvested his subordinate's memories. Smart. That skill will serve you well against the Entities."
"The first Reaver." Kael's voice came out rough. "You said you know where he is."
"I do. But that information will cost you." She produced a small leather folder from her cloak, tossing it to him. He caught it, the leather worn and cold. "Inside is everything the Shadow Court knows about the Soul Reaver bloodline. Names, locations, fates. The first Reaver isn't dead, Kael. He's bound to the seventy-second seal—the deepest one, the one containing the Faceless One."
The Faceless One. The Entity that had planted fragments of itself in three individuals. One of them was Kael.
"Every Reaver eventually hears the Faceless One's call," the woman continued. "It whispers promises of power, of knowledge, of an end to the constant harvesting. The first Reaver answered that call. He allowed a piece of the Faceless One into his soul, and in exchange, the Entity reinforced the seals from within." She paused. "It worked for nine hundred years. The seals held. But the Faceless One is patient, and its fragments are spreading."
She turned to leave, then hesitated. "The Vanguard will hunt you now. So will the Order of Dawn, once they learn their commander's soul was harvested by a Reaver. You have maybe forty-eight hours before the full weight of the Orders comes down on you." A ghost of a smile crossed her features. "I suggest you use them wisely."
"Why help me at all?" Kael demanded. "What do you really want?"
The woman looked back over her shoulder. Moonlight caught her face, and for an instant, Kael saw something other than the cold Shadow Court operative. Something tired. Something grieving.
"I want what you represent, Reaver. You're proof that the system can be beaten. That the Orders aren't invincible." Her voice dropped to barely a whisper. "And maybe, if you survive long enough, you might change something."
She vanished into the darkness between the trees, leaving Kael alone with the weight of stolen memories and an uncertain future. 
He opened the leather folder. Inside were documents—parchment and paper, centuries of accumulated intelligence. Names written in different hands. Locations marked with varying degrees of precision. And at the center of the oldest document, a sketch of a man with angular features and eyes that seemed to look through time itself. 
The first Soul Reaver. A man who had fought the Abyss, sealed the rifts, and then been consumed by the very power he'd wielded.
Kael's left forearm burned. The mark of the Soul Reaver pulsed with renewed intensity, as if responding to the knowledge he'd just gained. Somewhere in the back of his mind, a whisper he hadn't noticed before seemed to laugh. 
The Faceless One's fragment stirred.
Distant thunder rolled across the hills. Not weather—spiritual pressure. Multiple Awakened signatures, converging on his position from three directions. The Iron Vanguard had mobilized faster than expected. 
Forty-eight hours. He had forty-eight hours before the Orders declared open season on the Soul Reaver. 
But the documents in his hand promised something more valuable than safety. They promised answers. Names of other bloodline members who had survived the Orders' attention. Locations of hidden caches where the first Reaver had stashed his research. And one name, circled in red ink, that sent ice through Kael's veins:
Zarek. Prime Cultist. Current holder of the Sepulcher Tablets.
His enemies weren't just hunting him for his power. They were hunting him because they knew what he could become. 
The first Reaver had walked this path before. Had faced the same choice—the Faceless One's bargain, the Orders' persecution, the whisper of easy power. And he had fallen. 
Kael refused. 
He gathered the documents, securing them inside his coat, and turned toward the distant lights of Thornwick. The city was dangerous now, crawling with hunters, but it was also the only place where he could find the resources to understand what he was becoming. 
Somewhere in those gaslit streets, Zarek was making plans. Somewhere in those stone towers, the Orders were mobilizing. 
And somewhere in the depths of reality itself, the Faceless One was waiting. 
The fragment in Kael's soul pulsed again. This time, he could almost make out words. 
Come home, little brother. 
The thunder grew closer. Kael began to run. 
He had forty-eight hours to find answers. Forty-eight hours before the world came for him. 
The game had changed. The Soul Reaver was no longer a secret the Orders could suppress in the shadows. 
Now, they would have to hunt him in the open.
