The ritual circle pulsed with sickly violet light as Kael crashed through the warehouse door, boots splashing through ankle-deep rainwater. Three cultists spun toward him, their hollow chanting faltering for half a heartbeat before they raised curved ceremonial blades.
"Stop the sacrifice!" Kael's voice cut through the rain drumming on the corrugated roof above.
The nearest cultist lunged. Kael sidestepped, caught the man's wrist, and drove his elbow into the cultist's throat. The attacker crumpled with a wet gurgle. The second cultist swung a chain weighted with blessed iron. Kael deflected it with his forearm, felt the spiritual burn as the metal seared his skin, and responded with a palm strike that lifted the man off his feet.
The third cultist fled toward the circle's center, where a woman knelt bound before an altar crackling with unstable energy. Black geometric patterns crawled across the floor, drinking in moonlight that leaked through shattered skylights above.
The countdown had already begun.
---
Kael crossed the distance in three strides. The fleeing cultist reached for a ceremonial dagger on the altar, but Kael's hand closed around his ankle and wrenched. The cultist hit the stone floor face-first, teeth cracking against the surface. Kael kicked the dagger away and pressed his boot against the man's spine.
"Move and I break your back."
The cultist laughed—a broken, feverish sound. "Too late. The Veil bleeds whether you stop me or not."
Behind them, the bound woman lifted her head. Blood matted her hair, and her eyes held the glassy focus of someone who'd already accepted death. "Please," she whispered. "End it. Before they tear me open and feed me to whatever waits on the other side."
Kael's stomach turned. He'd seen enough ritual chambers in the past weeks—too many—each one festering in abandoned buildings at Thornwick's edges, each one plotting to punch holes through reality itself.
"Soul Reaver," the cultist beneath his boot hissed. "They say you walk the battlefield and steal power from the fallen. Do you know what happens when seventy-two seals fail simultaneously?"
Kael didn't answer. He drove his knee into the man's lower back, pinning him flat.
The altar's glow intensified. Hairline fractures spread through the stone, each crack emitting that nauseating violet luminescence. The geometric patterns on the floor began rotating, slow at first, then faster, scraping against the concrete like nails on slate.
He had minutes at best.
---
Kael released the cultist and sprinted toward the altar, ignoring the blood pounding in his ears. The woman flinched as he crouched beside her, hands working at the rope bindings around her wrists.
"Who are you?" she gasped. "How did you know—"
"Luck." He tore through the last knot and hauled her upright. "Can you walk?"
"I think so." She stumbled, caught herself on his arm. "They took me from the shipping district. Said I was... Suitable. Said my death would open the door wider."
The circle's rotation reached a fever pitch. A sound built beneath audible range, a vibration Kael felt in his teeth and marrow. The altar cracked completely, splitting into four chunks that drifted apart while maintaining their positions in the formation.
Within the broken altar, something pressed against reality from the other side. A shadow that was more than shadow. A hunger that radiated through the Veil like heat through glass.
"Not yet," Kael muttered. "Not on my watch."
He extended his hand toward the nearest chunk of altar stone and pressed his palm flat against its cold surface. Spiritual energy surged through his fingertips—familiar now, after months of practice, though still strange in its intensity. The Soul Reaver ability awakened in his cells the night they hanged him in Thornwick's public square, and it had never stopped demanding more.
The harvest was instantaneous. No spiritual cost to him, only to the latent power stored in the ritual components.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 12 units of Stabilized Ritual Energy from the remains. Soul Essence Fragment acquired.]
The altar's glow flickered. The rotation slowed. The pressing weight against the Veil retreated by a fraction—not gone, but no longer clawing at the barrier with desperate hunger.
"What did you do?" the woman stared at him, her face pale in the dying violet light.
"Bought us time." Kael withdrew his hand, flexing fingers that tingled with residual energy. "What's your name?"
"Mirren. Mirren Holt." She clutched his sleeve. "There are others. The cultists—they captured a dozen of us from the Dockside district. They were going to use all of us."
A dozen prisoners. Multiple ritual sites. And the knowledge that whatever he stopped here was only one fracture in a much larger pattern.
The cultist Kael had subdued started laughing again, quieter now, almost peaceful. "You can seal one rift. Can you seal seventy-two?"

Kael dragged the groaning cultist to his feet and slammed him against the nearest wall. The man's robes fell open, revealing a tattoo across his chest—a geometric design that matched the pattern on the warehouse floor, repeated in ink that seemed to drink the available light.
"Where are the other sites?" Kael demanded.
The cultist's smile widened. "The Court moves in shadow. Even now, their agents walk among the Orders themselves."
"Which Court?"
"What do you think the Shadow Court is?" The cultist's eyes rolled toward Mirren, then back to Kael. "A deniable blade. Every Order carries one. They point it at rivals and call it justice. But the true believers—the ones who understand what waits beyond the Veil—they serve something older than your petty faction wars."
Kael drove his fist into the man's gut. The cultist doubled over, vomiting bile and blood, but still he laughed through the pain.
"Seventy-two seals," he wheezed. "Seventy-two wounds in the world. And every year they weaken. Every decade another breaks. Your Orders pour gold into maintenance rituals and call it protection. But protection is an illusion. The Abyss is patient. The Entities are eternal. And when the last seal fails, the membrane between realms dissolves like morning frost."
Mirren grabbed Kael's arm. "We need to move. If there are more cultists coming—"
"There aren't." The wounded cultist straightened despite Kael's grip, his back sliding up the wall until he stood unaided. Blood dripped from his split lip, but his gaze held something beyond pain—fanatic certainty. "The ritual requires a sacrifice. The sacrifice opens the door. Once the door begins opening, additional participants only dilute the energy. That's why we took the innocents. That's why we needed the blood of the spiritually inert."
"You're saying the powerful are useless for your ritual," Kael said slowly. "And the powerless are fuel."
"The Veil cares nothing for strength." The cultist spread his arms, blood-smeared and triumphant. "It cares only for sacrifice. The greater the offering, the wider the door. Your Orders have maintained the seals for centuries by keeping the Abyss fed with minor sacrifices—cursed artifacts, corrupted ley lines, the occasional rogue Awakened. But that was never enough. The Entities demand more. They always demand more."
Kael released him and stepped back. His mind raced through implications. The ritual had been interrupted, but the pattern remained. The sealed rifts weakened over time—that much he'd learned from intercepted communications and torturous confessions. But this cultist spoke of coordinated action, seventy-two simultaneous breaches.
Seventy-two entities. Seventy-two fractures. Seventy-two points where the membrane between worlds grew thin enough to tear.
"Where?" Kael demanded. "Which rifts are they targeting?"
The cultist's smile became a rictus. "The Court's agents will tell you when it no longer matters. When the seals crack and the hunger spills through. You'll understand then what we've always known—that the Abyss isn't a prison. It's a release. The Entities aren't invaders. They're the truth beneath the world."

Kael struck him once more, a controlled blow that dropped the cultist into unconsciousness. He let the body crumple to the floor and turned to Mirren.
"Can you get out of here alone?"
She nodded shakily. "The docks aren't far. I can find my way."
"Good. Stay in crowds. Don't go anywhere isolated until this is over." He pressed a handful of coins into her palm—enough for a week's lodging in Thornwick's less savory boarding houses. "Hide. Don't trust anyone wearing Order insignia until you've verified their credentials twice."
"The Orders?" Her brow furrowed. "But they're supposed to protect us—"
"The Orders protect the system. They protect the seals. They don't always protect the people caught between." Kael moved toward the warehouse exit, then paused. "If you see other prisoners being moved—if you can get word to me—there's a tavern called the Rusty Anchor in the Dockside ward. Ask for Torvus. Tell him the Reaver sent you."
Mirren stared at him, processing everything she'd witnessed in the past ten minutes. The harvested altar. The knowledge of seventy-two rifts. The mention of the Shadow Court operating within the Orders themselves.
"You're him, aren't you?" she whispered. "The one they executed last spring. The one who came back wrong."
Kael's jaw tightened. "I came back."
"The papers said you steal souls. That you desecrate the dead for power."
"They're not wrong." He met her gaze without flinching. "But I don't steal from the innocent. And I don't let things from beyond the Veil tear through into a world full of people who never asked for their gods to die."
He left her standing in the ruined warehouse, beneath the shattered skylights where rain still fell in steady sheets. Outside, Thornwick's gaslights flickered through the mist, and somewhere in the distance, a steam whistle shrieked the change of watch.
The city didn't know what moved beneath its surface. The city didn't know how close the membrane between worlds had come to tearing tonight.
But Kael knew. And now he had questions that demanded answers.
---
The Rusty Anchor stank of cheap tobacco and older secrets. Kael pushed through the crowd of dockworkers and night-shift laborers, ignoring the stares that followed him—the weight of his reputation preceding him like a herald. He found Torvus in the back corner, nursing a pint of something that had probably been alcoholic three drinks ago.
"Reaver." Torvus set down his glass, eyes tracking Kael's approach with the wariness of a man who'd survived too long in Thornwick's underworld. "Heard you were making waves tonight. The cultists in the old Hargrove warehouse—that was you?"
"One of several sites." Kael slid into the seat across from him, his wet coat leaving a puddle on the scarred wooden table. "I need information."
"That'll cost you."
"I have soul essence." Kael produced a small vial from his coat—the fragment he'd harvested from the ritual altar. Even that small amount represented significant value on the black market. "Enough to fund your operation for a month."
Torvus's eyes widened. He glanced around the tavern, then back to Kael, his demeanor shifting from cautious to calculating. "You stopped a ritual. A real one?"
"I interrupted it. Bought time, not victory." Kael set the vial on the table between them. "Tell me about the seventy-two rifts. Tell me which ones are weakening fastest. Tell me what the cultists are really planning."
"Plural?" Torvus leaned forward, his voice dropping. "You think there's more than one site?"
"I know there are more. The cultist I questioned talked about seventy-two wounds in the world. He mentioned coordinated action. I stopped one ritual tonight. How many others succeeded?"
Torvus sat back, his face cycling through several emotions before settling on grim resignation. "I've heard whispers. The Mortician's Guild has been processing bodies at three times the normal rate—high-stage Awakened, most of them, the kind who don't usually die in accidents. The Iron Vanguard lost an entire patrol unit near the Ashford Gap last week. No survivors, no bodies recovered. The Order of Dawn recalled their mobile strike teams from every active assignment and locked down their fortress monasteries."
"Lockdown means they expect an attack."
"Lockdown means they're preparing for something worse than an attack." Torvus drained his drink and set the empty glass down with a sharp click. "The six Orders have maintained the seal network since the Abyssal Wars. Seventy-two rifts, seventy-two Entities, seventy-two locks holding the Abyss at bay. It's the foundation everything else rests on—the economy, the hierarchy, the entire social order. If even one seal fails completely..."
"What happens?"
"The barrier becomes weaker at that point. The Veil thins. Spiritual energy bleeds through in greater quantities." Torvus lowered his voice further. "Awakenings become more common. So do corruptions. The Entities can project fragments of themselves through weakened rifts—not full manifestation, but enough to whisper, to influence, to corrupt. And the more the Veil degrades, the more it degrades further. It's a cascade effect. One failure accelerates the others."
Kael's hands curled into fists beneath the table. "So the cultists aren't trying to break all seventy-two seals at once. They're accelerating the decay. Turning a gradual erosion into an avalanche."
"That's what the smart money says." Torvus tapped the vial of soul essence. "Three weeks ago, this much would have bought you gossip and rumors. Now it buys you survival information. The kind of intelligence that gets people killed if it spreads too far."
"I'm not interested in gossip. I'm interested in stopping them."
Torvus laughed—a harsh, humorless sound. "You're one man. The cultists have been building toward this for decades. The Shadow Court has agents in every major city, probably in every Order. You've got a reputation, sure—a terrifying one. But reputations don't seal rifts. Reputations don't stop fanatics who think their deaths will bring about paradise."
"I stopped one ritual tonight."
"And how many did you miss?" Torvus leaned back, spreading his hands. "Thornwick alone has two hundred thousand people. Thirty-seven confirmed cult sites before tonight's activity. How many sites do you think are running simultaneously? How many innocents are being prepared for sacrifice while we sit here talking?"
Kael stood abruptly, his chair scraping against the floor. The tavern's ambient noise dimmed as nearby patrons noticed the tension.
"You have contacts," Kael said. "People who watch the cultists. People who track their movements. I need names. I need locations. I need anything that tells me where the next ritual will happen before it happens."
"That information isn't free. Not because of money—because of danger. The last person who tried to map cult activity in Thornwick washed up in the river with his tongue cut out."
"Then give me what you have for free. Call it an investment in keeping the city standing."
Torvus studied him for a long moment. The candle on the table guttered in a draft from somewhere, casting shifting shadows across the detective's weathered face.
"There's a woman," he finally said. "Works for the Order of Dawn's intelligence division. Her name is Vera Ashcroft, and she's been feeding information to the underground for months. Whatever she's learned about cult movements, she'll share—if you can convince her you're not another Shadow Court plant."
"How do I find her?"
"She frequents the Midnight Library in the Scholar's Quarter. Midnight to third bell, every third night. She's looking for something—or someone. Haven't figured out which."
Kael pocketed the soul essence vial and turned toward the door. Torvus's voice caught him before he reached it.
"Reaver. One more thing, no charge."
Kael paused, glancing back.
"The cultists who attacked tonight—they weren't the leaders. They were disciples. Sacrificial lambs being fattened for the ritual." Torvus's expression hardened. "The real power behind the seventy-two sites, the ones directing the entire operation—that's who you need to find. And if you find them, you won't be able to stop them alone. The Orders need to know. Even if you can't trust them."
"I don't trust anyone."
"Smart man." Torvus raised his empty glass in mock salute. "But sometimes survival means making enemies of your enemies. The cultists want the Veil to fail. The Orders want to preserve it. For now, at least, that makes your goals aligned."
Kael pushed through the tavern door into Thornwick's rain-slicked streets. The city sprawled before him, gaslights and steam vents and the distant rumble of freight engines. Two hundred thousand souls sleeping, working, dying—none of them aware that the barrier between worlds might be failing beneath their feet.
Somewhere in that city, Vera Ashcroft held answers. Somewhere else, cultists prepared more rituals. And somewhere beyond the Veil, seventy-two Entities pressed against their prisons, sensing the cracks widen, tasting freedom for the first time in millennia.
Kael pulled his collar higher and began walking toward the Scholar's Quarter.

The Midnight Library occupied the basement of a condemned printing house, accessible through a trapdoor disguised as a broken cobblestone. Kael descended narrow stairs that reeked of mildew and old paper, emerging into a space that defied the building above—vaulted ceilings painted with constellations, shelves carved from what appeared to be petrified wood, and reading tables lit by floating orbs of soft blue flame.
A woman sat alone at a table near the far wall, surrounded by scattered documents and open books. She looked up as Kael approached, her grey eyes assessing him with the clinical detachment of someone who'd seen too much to be surprised by anything.
"You're the Reaver," she said. Not a question.
"Vera Ashcroft?"
"Torvus told you I could help." She gestured to the seat across from her. "Sit. We don't have much time."
Kael remained standing. "How do you know I'm not Shadow Court?"
"Because the Shadow Court wouldn't send someone with your reputation. They're subtle. Invisible. They don't parade their abilities in front of witnesses." She tapped one of the documents before her—a map of Thornwick marked with dozens of red pins. "And because I know what the cultists are planning. I've known for weeks. The Order of Dawn refuses to act."
"Why?"
"Because acting means admitting the seals are failing faster than they can maintain them. Because admitting that means explaining to the Council of Six why fifty years of preservation rituals have accomplished nothing. Because the truth would destabilize the entire political system." Vera's voice carried no emotion, just the flat recitation of facts. "The Orders exist to maintain the seals. If the seals cannot be maintained, the Orders have no purpose. They'll protect the illusion until the illusion collapses around them."

If you want to stop it, you'll need to move now and arrive prepared. If you want to walk away, now is the time."

Kael remained at the table, staring at the map with its constellation of red pins. Thirty-seven confirmed sites. Thirty-seven potential breaches in the Veil. Thirty-seven chances for something from beyond to tear through into a world that had spent five centuries trying to forget the Abyss existed.
His palm still tingled where he'd touched the ritual altar. The harvest had been easy—effortless, even. The Soul Reaver ability took what it wanted without asking permission, without regard for consequence. He harvested skills from the dead and gained power that should have been impossible. He harvested memories and learned secrets that would have died with their owners. He harvested fragments of souls and carried them inside himself, a mosaic of dead people's final moments.
Was any of it really his? Did it matter?
Kael pushed back from the table and followed Vera toward the exit. The floating orbs of blue flame drifted aside as he passed, and somewhere in the library's depths, a book fell open on its own, pages rustling to a stop on a passage marked with faded ink.
He didn't stop to read it. He didn't have time.
Outside, the rain had stopped, leaving Thornwick's streets gleaming under gaslight. The city slept, unaware of the war being waged beneath its surface. A war between cultists who wanted the Veil to fall and Orders who wanted it to stand. A war between Entities pressing from beyond and Awakened trying to hold them back. A war that Kael had been drafted into the moment they hanged him and his soul refused to stay dead.
Thirty-six hours until the next ritual. Thirty-seven sites across Thornwick alone. Seventy-two rifts across the entire continent.
He had a map, a name, and the lingering sensation of a dead man's memories stirring somewhere in the back of his mind. It wasn't much. It wasn't enough.
But it was what he had.

The Ashford district occupied Thornwick's eastern edge, where the city's expansion had stalled against terrain too unstable for proper development. Sinkholes pockmarked the ground, and the foundations of abandoned buildings listed at angles that defied architectural logic. The old gasworks rose from the mist like the skeleton of some massive beast, its retort towers black against the pre-dawn sky.
Kael approached through an alley that reeked of chemical waste and rotting organic matter. His boots found purchase on slick cobblestones, and his eyes adjusted to the darkness with an Awakened's supernatural acuity. Every sense he possessed stretched outward, searching for cultist patrols or spiritual anomalies.
Nothing yet. That would change soon.
The church stood at the alley's end—a structure that had once been dedicated to the Sun God, judging by the faded golden sun carved above its doors. The building sagged to one side, its walls streaked with mineral deposits that glittered like frozen tears in the faint light. The windows had been boarded up, but fresh scratches marred the boards—evidence of recent entry.
Kael pressed himself against the wall and listened.
Voices. Multiple voices, chanting in unison. The same hollow, rhythmic cadence he'd heard in the warehouse, but stronger. More confident. They expected this ritual to succeed.
He counted the voices. Eight, maybe ten. More than he'd faced before. And if Vera's intelligence was correct, some of those voices belonged to cultists at the Transcendent stage or higher—Awakened powerful enough to challenge him directly.
The wooden boards over the church's main door had been partially removed, creating a gap just wide enough for a man to squeeze through. Kael moved toward it, keeping his footsteps silent against the debris-strewn ground.
A sound stopped him. Footsteps. Behind him. Multiple sets, approaching from the direction he'd come.
Kael spun, reaching for the knife at his belt, and found himself facing four figures in dark cloaks. Their faces were hidden, but the spiritual energy radiating from them was unmistakable—Awakened signatures, tightly controlled, deliberately masked. Not cultists. Something else.
"Soul Reaver." The lead figure's voice was feminine, cold, precise. "The Shadow Court requests your presence."
"I don't take requests from shadows."
"This isn't a request." The figure raised one hand, and Kael felt a barrier of spiritual energy snap into place behind him, cutting off his retreat. "You have information we need. You have abilities that could prove useful. Resistance will be... Discouraged."
Kael's hand tightened on his knife. The chanting from the church continued, louder now. The ritual was reaching its crescendo. Whatever was happening inside that building, it was about to become permanent.
Behind him, the cultists pressed forward with their ceremony. Before him, four cloaked figures blocked his path, demanding cooperation in the name of an organization he'd spent months trying to avoid.
And somewhere in the depths of his consciousness, the first Soul Reaver's memories stirred—a warning, a fragment of knowledge, something that might have been a name or a location or a terrible truth about to unfold.
The question was whether Kael could grasp it before everything else collapsed around him.
