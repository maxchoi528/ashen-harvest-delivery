Kael pressed deeper into the tunnel, following the blood trail. The overhead pipes groaned, leaking steam and rust-brown water that splashed his boots. Each drop echoed through the passage, a wet metallic percussion that set his teeth on edge. Somewhere above, Thornwick's steam engines roared through another night cycle, but down here the only sounds were his footsteps and the wet drip of condensation on ancient stone.
The blood trail thickened as he descended further. It pooled in the cracks between flagstones, black and viscous in the dim light of his lantern. The copper stench coated the back of his throat, mixing with the mineral tang of old water and the sour rot of the tunnels themselves. He adjusted his grip on the lantern, its flame guttering each time a draft crawled through the passages.
The trail ended at a collapsed section. Rubble blocked the passage, chunks of Victorian masonry and rusted pipe tangled together. Beyond it, shadows moved. Kael's fingers found his knife hilt.
"You're slow, Ashborn."
The voice came from everywhere and nowhere, bouncing off curved walls. Kael spun, blade clearing leather in the same motion. His lantern arm swept wide, throwing wild shadows across the damp stone.
A figure stepped from the darkness. Tall, gaunt, draped in a coat that seemed woven from shadow itself. Pale eyes gleamed in the lantern light—eyes that held no pupils, only swirling silver mist where irises should be. The mist rotated slowly, like oil on water.
"Iron Vanguard's Eyes," Kael said. "You're one of Vane's."
"Observant." The figure smiled, revealing teeth filed to points. The silver mist in his eyes quickened. "The Inquisitor sends his regards. He's been watching your little performance in the underworld. Impressive, really. The corpses you've left behind. The skills you've stolen." The smile widened, splitting his lips. A bead of dark blood welled at the corner of his mouth. "He wants you to know that he doesn't care about any of that."
"Then why are you here?"
"Because you're going to help us."
Kael's blade stayed level. The tip tracked the figure's chest. "Wrong answer."
The figure's hands came up, empty. "I'm not your enemy, Ashborn. I'm here to offer you a choice. The Inquisitor knows about the cult's plans. He knows about the rifts. He knows about Prime Zarek." A pause. The silver mist slowed. "He also knows you didn't kill those children in the Ashborn incident. The evidence was planted. Zarek's work."
The words hit like a physical blow. Kael's grip tightened until his knuckles ached, the leather of his glove creaking. His jaw clenched. The lantern flame wavered.
"Prove it."
The figure reached slowly into his coat, producing a small leather folder. He tossed it across the gap between them. Kael caught it one-handed, never lowering his blade. The folder was warm. Body warm.
Inside were documents. Witness statements. Financial records linking Zarek to the Black Candle cult. A detailed timeline showing how the Ashborn massacre had been orchestrated to frame a dead man who couldn't defend himself. Kael's eyes moved across the pages, absorbing dates, names, locations. His pulse drummed in his ears. His breathing stayed even.
"You're Ascendant now," the figure said. "Soon you'll need to choose sides. The Inquisitor represents Order. Justice. Law. Everything the old world was built on." He tilted his head, the silver mist swirling faster. "Or you can keep walking alone until something eats you in the dark."
"And what do you get from this?"
The figure shrugged. "I get to retire from babysitting duty. Vane's been obsessed with you for months. He thinks you're the key to something."
"To what?"
"If I knew, I'd tell you." The figure stepped back, melting toward the shadows. His outline blurred at the edges, darkness bleeding into him like ink into water. "You have three days, Ashborn. After that, the Inquisitor stops offering deals. Consider this your last chance to be something other than a monster."
The shadows swallowed him whole. Kael stood alone in the tunnel, the weight of the documents burning in his hand. He tucked them into his coat and kept moving.
The tunnel narrowed. The walls pressed closer, brickwork giving way to older stone, then to something that wasn't stone at all—something dark and organic that felt warm beneath his fingertips when he brushed the wall. The air grew thick. His lantern flame shrank, pulling inward as if afraid of the dark.
The passage opened into a workshop.
The space stank of old blood and stranger things. Kael swept the room twice before allowing himself to relax, moving through the cramped space with his blade ready. Shelves lined every wall, crammed with glass containers holding specimens that floated in cloudy fluid. He didn't examine them closely. A workbench dominated the center, its surface scarred with burn marks and dark stains that had soaked so deep they'd become part of the wood grain.
In the corner, a body hung from a meat hook. Fresh. The man's throat had been opened with surgical precision, and his chest cavity lay exposed like a mechanical housing missing its gears. Someone had harvested everything useful and left the rest to rot. Blood dripped from the corpse's bare feet onto the stone floor in a slow, rhythmic pattern.
But they'd missed the hands.
Kael approached, studying the corpse. The fingers were clenched in a death-grip around something. He pried them open, the dead muscles resisting, then giving way with a wet crack. A small iron key and a folded scrap of paper.
The note contained coordinates and a single word: CRADLE.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted [Iron Key] from the remains. [Access Granted to: Unknown Storage Location]"
"Soul Harvest: You extracted [Fragmented Memory] from the remains. [Memory Quality: Poor — Subject died mid-thought]"
Kael closed his eyes, letting the memory wash over him.
A chamber carved from living rock. Pillars arranged in a circle, each one pulsing with sickly light. In the center, a hole in reality itself, bleeding darkness like an open wound.
Voices chanting in languages that predated human speech.
And a face—young, terrified, pressed against glass as something massive moved in the darkness beyond.
The vision shattered. Kael steadied himself against the workbench, his pulse hammering. His stomach lurched. He spat the taste of copper from his mouth and wiped his lips.
The Cradle. The seal chamber. Whatever the cult was building toward, this path led straight to its heart.
"Found you," he whispered.
A sound behind him. Kael spun, blade arcing toward the threat.
A child stood in the doorway. Seven, eight years old at most, wrapped in a ratty blanket that did nothing to hide the bruises on her arms. Her eyes were too old for her face. She flinched at the blade but didn't run.
"You're the corpse man," she said. "The one who eats the dead."
Kael's blade stopped an inch from her throat. "Go home, kid."
"There's no home. There's no anywhere anymore." She clutched the blanket tighter, knuckles white against the frayed fabric. "The man who did this"—she pointed at the corpse—"said the world ends soon. He said everyone who matters is already dead. He said—"
"I don't care what he said."
The girl's lip trembled, but she didn't cry. "He said you'd know what to do. He said the key opens the door and the door opens the way and the way opens the—" She stopped, her small face contorting. "I don't remember the rest. It hurts to remember."
Kael looked at the key in his palm, then at the coordinates on the paper. The Cradle. The seal chamber.
"Stay here," he said. "Don't touch anything. Don't talk to anyone."
He moved toward the door, but the girl's voice stopped him.
"He knows you're coming."
Kael went still. "Who?"
"I heard him. In the dark. He said he's been waiting for you since before you were born." Her eyes went distant, unfocused. The pupils dilated until the color was swallowed. "He said..."
The girl trailed off. Her mouth worked, but no sound came out. Then her knees buckled.
Kael watched her fall. His boot shifted half an inch toward her, then stopped. He looked at the coordinates on the paper. Looked at the door.
The girl hit the stone floor. Her body curled inward, arms wrapped around herself. Her skin was burning. When she exhaled, the breath carried the sharp scent of sulfur and something sweet beneath it. Something rotting.
Spiritual corruption. The girl had been exposed to something toxic, something that was eating her from the inside out. The black veins were visible at her temples, branching like lightning across her skin.
Kael crouched beside her. Checked her pulse. Fast, irregular, the heartbeat of a body fighting itself. He pulled off his glove and pressed his fingers to her forehead. The skin blistered under his touch.
He stood.
The key weighed heavy in his pocket. The coordinates burned in his mind. The girl lay on the cold floor, her breathing shallow, her small hands twitching.
He turned and walked toward the door.
Behind him, the girl's lips moved. Words too quiet to hear. Kael didn't look back. The door slammed behind him, sealing the workshop and its dying occupant away from the world.
The tunnels beneath Thornwick's merchant district were a maze of forgotten infrastructure. Old sewer lines ran alongside abandoned steam conduits, and in the spaces between, smugglers and refugees carved out desperate lives. Kael moved through the chaos like a ghost, following the map his harvested memories had provided.
The Cradle wasn't on any official survey. It existed in the gaps between the city's foundations, in the places where engineers had once drilled too deep and found something that shouldn't have been there.
Hours of crawling through filth brought him to a door that didn't belong. Iron bands on ancient oak, covered in sigils that pulsed with faint light. The sigils weren't painted. They moved. Kael tried the key. It fit. The lock turned with a sound like bone cracking.
Beyond the door, stairs descended into absolute darkness. No torches. No glowstones. Just a void that seemed to swallow even the concept of light. The air rising from below was warm and wet, carrying the taste of copper and ozone.
Kael drew Soul Reaver. The blade caught something from the depths and he began his descent.
The stairs went down for longer than should have been possible. The walls grew slick with moisture that had no business existing this far from any water source. The steps were uneven, some too tall, some too short, as if the staircase had been carved by something that didn't understand human legs. His boot slipped twice. He caught himself, kept moving.
The air thickened. Each breath tasted of metal and something organic, something alive. The darkness pressed against him. He could feel it on his skin.
Finally, the stairs ended.
Kael stepped into the Cradle.
Seventy-two pillars ringed a circular chamber carved from black stone. Each pillar was covered in script that shifted when he tried to read it, the symbols rearranging themselves like living things. Between them, the air shimmered. Through that shimmer, Kael caught glimpses of another place—a place of endless darkness and watching eyes.
The seals. All seventy-two of them, contained in a single chamber.
In the center of the room, a ritual had been prepared. Blood offerings surrounded a central altar, their arrangement forming a pattern Kael recognized from his earlier harvests. The blood was still wet.
"This is where you die, Reaver."
Kael spun, blade raised.
Prime Zarek stepped from the shadows between two pillars. He looked nothing like the man from the Ashborn massacre—no longer a shadow broker in fine clothes, but something transformed. His skin had taken on a waxy pallor, shot through with black veins that pulsed like a second heartbeat. His eyes were solid white, without iris or pupil, and when he smiled, his teeth had been filed to points.
"The Whisper King has been waiting for this moment," Zarek said. "Every step you've taken since your resurrection. Every corpse you've harvested. Every skill you've stolen. All leading here, to this chamber, to me."
"You're a hybrid now," Kael said. "Like the ones in the tunnels."
"I am what comes next." Zarek spread his arms, and the black veins beneath his skin pulsed brighter. "I've touched the Abyss, Ashborn. I've seen what waits on the other side of the Veil."
Kael assessed his options. Zarek was Transcendent at minimum, probably higher. The hybrid transformation had pushed him past human limits. In a straight fight, Kael would lose.
"The Whisper King," Kael said. "Who is he?"
Zarek laughed. "You think I'll tell you? You think anything you learn here matters?" His white eyes tracked Kael's blade. "You're a tool, Ashborn. A useful piece on a board you've only begun to see."
"Answer the question."
"No."
Kael lunged.
His blade caught the light wrong, deflecting off Zarek's raised arm. The hybrid barely registered the impact, his skin harder than any armor. The shock ran up Kael's arm to the shoulder. He twisted, spinning away from the retaliatory strike that cracked the flagstone where he'd been standing.
Soul Sight activated, painting the chamber in shades of spiritual energy. Zarek blazed like a bonfire, his corrupted soul radiating power that made Kael's teeth ache. But there—at the edge of his vision—something flickered.
The altar. The ritual preparations. The seventy-two pillars, each one a seal.
Kael changed direction mid-stride, abandoning his attack on Zarek to sprint toward the chamber's center.
"What are you—"
Zarek's confusion became panic. He gave chase, but Kael had the lead, and more importantly, he had a destination.
The altar.
Kael's hands found the blood-slicked stone, and he began to feed.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted [Corrupted Essence] from the remains. [Energy absorbed]"
"Soul Harvest: You extracted [Ritual Fragment] from the remains. [Pattern Analysis: Incomplete]"
"Soul Harvest: You extracted [Fragments of the First Sealing] from the remains. [Memory Quality: Excellent — Subject witnessed original event]"
The memories hit like a tidal wave.
Seventy-two humans standing in a circle. Not soldiers. Not mages. Ordinary people who had found something terrible in the depths of the earth. Something that wanted to consume.
They couldn't fight it. They couldn't banish it. All they could do was contain it.
So they made a choice.
Each person merged their soul with a piece of the Abyss, becoming living seals. They carved their essence into pillars of stone, anchoring the darkness that had invaded their world. And one by one, they died—from the weight of what they carried.
In the end, seventy-two heroes became seventy-two prisons.
Kael opened his eyes. The ritual fragment, combined with the original sealing memory, painted a picture of what the cult truly wanted. Not just to open the rifts. To reverse the original binding. To free the Abyssal Entities by undoing the sacrifice that had created the seals.
Zarek arrived a moment too late. Kael was already moving, his blade carving through the ritual preparations with brutal efficiency. Glass vessels shattered. Blood soaked the stone. The sigils screamed.
"No!" Zarek's voice cracked. "You don't understand what you're destroying!"
"I understand plenty."
The altar shattered. The carefully arranged blood offerings scattered. And across the chamber, the seventy-two pillars flickered—not brightening, as Zarek had intended, but steadying. The ritual to weaken them had been interrupted.
"You've delayed the inevitable," Zarek snarled. His hands clenched. "He will find another way."
"Then he should have killed me when he had the chance."
Kael's blade swept up, catching Zarek's arm before the hybrid could retract it. Soul Reaver bit deep, channeling energy that made both of them scream. The sound echoed through the chamber, multiplying, distorting.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted [Hybrid Corruption] from the remains. [Quality: Excellent — Subject fully transformed]"
"Soul Harvest: You extracted [Whisper King's Name Fragment] from the remains. [Warning: Incomplete — Full name sealed by unknown force]"
"Soul Harvest: You extracted [Shadow Court Communication Sigil] from the remains. [Access granted to: Private Channel 7]"
Zarek staggered back, his arm hanging useless. The hybrid transformation was already beginning to fail without the ritual's support, his corrupted flesh sloughing away to reveal something older beneath.
"He warned me about you," Zarek gasped. "Said you'd be the one to break the pattern. But you're just another broken piece in his design."
"What design?"
Zarek didn't answer. His body was dissolving, coming apart in sheets of ash and shadow. The extraction had taken too much. Even a hybrid couldn't survive having its core essence carved away.
Kael stood alone in the Cradle, surrounded by pillars that pulsed with stolen light.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted [Arcane Genesis] from the remains. [Skill acquired: You may now create basic magical constructs from harvested essence. Complexity depends on harvested quality.]"
The new skill settled into his mind. Arcane Genesis. The ability to build than take. He turned the concept over, examining it the way a man examines a weapon he isn't sure he knows how to use.
Kael looked at the scattered ritual components, then at the seventy-two pillars. He had time now. Not much—the cult would regroup, Zarek's masters would find another path—but enough to prepare.
Enough to warn someone. The Inquisitor, maybe. Or whoever still had the power to stop what was coming.
He turned toward the stairs, already planning his next move. The Shadow Court's communication sigil burned in his memory. The Whisper King's name fragment teased at the edges of his consciousness. And somewhere in the darkness above, Thornwick's citizens went about their lives, unaware that their world was balanced on a knife's edge.
A whisper of movement behind him. Kael spun, blade raised.
Nothing. Just the pillars, the silence, the endless weight of what had been sealed below.
He took a step toward the stairs—
A hand closed around his ankle from below.
Kael looked down. A face rose from the floor—pale, gaunt, its features stretched into an expression of frozen agony. Eyes like hollow pits stared up at him. The mouth opened. No sound came out. Then:
"Run. He's already here."
The floor rippled like water. The seventy-two pillars flickered in unison. And from the center of the chamber, where the ritual had been broken, something began to rise.
Something that had been waiting for exactly this moment.
Kael ran.
