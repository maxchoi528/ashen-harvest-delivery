Kael's blade carved through the cultist's throat. Steel scraped cartilage with a wet, grinding rasp. Blood arced through the air, a spray of hot copper that splattered across the swirling, gaslit fog. Droplets struck Kael's cheek, warm against the damp chill.
"You shouldn't be here," the dying man wheezed, his hands clawing weakly at the gushing wound.
"I'm aware." Kael stepped over the twitching body, his heavy boots squelching in the muck of Thornwick's Undercroft district. The stench of raw sewage and rotting fish assaulted his nostrils, mixing with the sharp metallic tang of fresh blood. Three more cultists flanked him, their black cloaks marked with the Seventh Trumpet's sigil—a broken horn wrapped in chains. They moved wrong. Too coordinated. Too aware of his positioning. Their boots didn't slip on the slick cobblestones. Their breathing remained steady, controlled.
The Soul Reaver stirred beneath his skin, hungry. A cold heat pulsed behind his sternum, the phantom sensation of claws raking the inside of his ribs.
"By the authority of the Sealed Pact," one cultist intoned, raising a syringe filled with iridescent liquid, "you will surrender your ability, abomination."
"Not today." Kael lunged.
He closed the distance in two strides, his weight shifting to his back foot before pivoting. His blade caught the nearest cultist across the wrist, severing tendons. The hand holding the syringe dropped into the muck. Kael reversed his grip, plunging the sword into the man's chest before wrenching it free and spinning to parry a desperate strike from the second cultist. Steel rang against steel. The impact jolted through Kael's forearm. He ducked beneath a wild swing, drove his elbow into the third cultist's throat, and finished with a sweeping cut that hamstrung the second attacker.
The fight lasted twelve seconds. The cultists fell in a spray of arterial red, and Kael stood breathing hard among the corpses, his heart hammering against his ribs. The silence of the alley pressed against his ears, broken only by the wet gurgle of air escaping punctured lungs. That was too easy. The Seventh Trumpet sent more than three Awakened to capture a Soul Reaver.
Something watched from the shadows.
Kael spun, blade raised. His shoulders tensed, his jaw clenching tight enough to ache. The fog parted around a figure stepping from the drainage tunnel—the source of the drainage canal that ran beneath this entire district. Tall, gaunt, draped in robes that seemed to drink the lamplight. A silver mask covered the face, curved like a beak.
"The Soul Reaver walks among us again." The voice echoed strangely, as if spoken from multiple mouths. "How disappointing. We expected more... Resistance."
"You're the Seventh Trumpet." Kael's grip tightened on his sword, knuckles blanching white. "I've killed four of your disciples."
"You've killed children playing at being soldiers." The masked figure tilted its head, the beak catching a stray beam of gaslight. "I am what remains of the original Trumpet—the seventh seal's guardian, bound to this pit when the first walls cracked."
A Guardian. Not just a cultist, but something that had existed since the Abyssal Wars. The Entities were supposed to be locked behind the seals, their influence mediated through pawns. This thing stood before him, fully manifested. The air around it tasted of ozone and charred bone, pressing against Kael's skin with invisible weight.
"The seals are weakening," Kael said. It wasn't a question.
"Your kind always was quick to notice what should remain hidden." The Seventh Trumpet raised one long arm. Glowing runes crawled across its fingers, casting dancing shadows that moved against the flickering gaslight. "The Underworld stirs, Soul Reaver. The 72 wakes. And you—harbinger of death, thief of souls—you will join us or be harvested yourself."
Kael moved first.
His blade clashed against the Guardian's arm, the impact sending shockwaves through his shoulders. The creature was stronger than anything he'd faced—dense with spiritual energy that pressed against him like a physical weight. Each strike numbed his fingers. Each parry drove him back another step. His boots skidded on the slick stone, searching for purchase.
"You harvest the dead," the Trumpet said, catching his blade mid-swing. The metal shrieked against the creature's palm, held fast by some unseen force. "But have you ever harvested something that refuses to die?"
Heat bloomed from the creature's palm. Kael threw himself sideways as a beam of concentrated Abyssal energy punched through the space he'd occupied, scorching the cobblestones black. The acrid stench of burning sulfur filled the alley.
The Soul Reaver screamed inside him.
Not in pain—in hunger. The creature before him reeked of power, of centuries of stolen lives compressed into a single corrupted form. Kael could taste it on the back of his tongue: the echoes of every soul this thing had consumed, every victim whose death it had claimed. Copper and ash and something older, something that predated language.
He rolled to his feet, breathing hard. Blood dripped from a gash on his forearm where the blade had been forced back against his own skin. The wound throbbed with each heartbeat, warm blood sliding down to his wrist.
"Transcendent stage," the Trumpet observed. "You should be stronger. The Soul Reaver was not meant for such mediocrity."
"I've been busy."
"Evidently. The Whispering Court watches you, little reaver. They see potential. I see waste."
Kael charged again. His muscles burned. His lungs strained. This time he didn't aim for the Guardian's arm—he dropped low, boots sliding through the muck, and drove his blade upward into the creature's thigh. The steel bit deep, grinding against something that felt like bone but pulsed with corrupted energy.
The Seventh Trumpet roared.
Black ichor sprayed from the wound, smoking where it touched the ground. The Guardian's composure shattered, one clawed hand swiping for Kael's head. He ducked, twisted, wrenched his blade free with a wet sucking sound. The creature stumbled, its balance disrupted.
Now. While it was wounded.
Kael dove for the closest corpse—the first cultist he'd killed—and slammed his palm against the cooling flesh. The dead man's skin was already growing clammy, the muscles stiffening beneath Kael's fingers. The Soul Reaver activated.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Fragmented Combat Training from the remains. Memory Shard acquired.]
Skills flooded his mind. Muscle control he hadn't earned. Reflex patterns that belonged to a dead man. The cultist had been Awakened—Stage One, barely—but his training was precise. Military-grade. Kael's body moved with borrowed efficiency as he spun back toward the Guardian, each motion smoother, faster, more deliberate.
The Trumpet had recovered. It stood straight despite the wound, ichor still seeping from its leg. The silver mask cracked down the middle, fragments falling to the cobblestones with a brittle clink.
"You dare." Its voice dropped an octave. "You dare harvest from those under my protection."
"They attacked me."
"They served a purpose!" The Guardian's arms spread wide. Runes blazed across its body, light pulsing in rhythm with something Kael couldn't hear but could feel—a heartbeat, vast and slow, echoing from somewhere far below the stone and sewage. "Every death feeds the seal. Every sacrifice maintains the barrier between worlds. You disrupt the cycle. You steal what should return to the Abyss."
Kael understood then. The cult wasn't trying to break the seals. They were trying to maintain them—through sacrifice, through blood, through the slow feeding of souls into the system. But something had changed. The seals were degrading faster than the offerings could sustain.
"The Great Rift," Kael said. "It's not failing because you're not feeding it enough. It's failing because something's draining it faster."
The Seventh Trumpet went still. The ambient hum of its power faltered, the pulsing runes dimming for a fraction of a second.
"Clever." The word dripped venom. "The Whisper King chose well when he marked your soul."
Kael's blood chilled. His stomach clenched into a knot. The Whisper King. He'd heard that name before—whispered in the criminal underbelly of Thornwick, spoken of in hushed tones by Order agents who pretended not to know. A figure who controlled the Shadow Court, who traded in souls and secrets, who had survived for centuries by feeding on the shadows themselves.
"You're lying."
"Am I?" The Guardian laughed—a terrible, grinding sound like stone scraping against stone. "Who do you think gave you the Soul Reaver, little thief? Who carved that power into your dying flesh and left you to wake in a world that would hunt you? The Whisper King saw potential in you. The same potential he saw in me, once."
The fog thickened around them. Kael's breath came in visible puffs despite the mild air. His Soul Reaver pulsed, offering him something—warning or invitation, he couldn't tell. The cold spread through his chest, a pressure building behind his ribs.
"The Shadow Court knows what you are," the Trumpet continued. "They know what you can become. Every step you take in Thornwick, every enemy you kill, every soul you harvest—they watch. They measure. And when you are ready..."
"I answer to no one."
"Everyone answers to someone." The creature stepped closer, ichor pooling beneath its wounded leg. The smell of rot intensified, wafting from the creature's robes. "The question is whether you answer as master or servant. The Whisper King offers freedom, Soul Reaver. Power without consequence. You could walk into the heart of the Council of Six and take what you need. No one could stop you."
Kael readied his blade. "I'll pass."
"Pity." The Seventh Trumpet's mask fell away, revealing a face that was less human and more an impression of one—hollow eyes, a mouth that opened too wide, skin that shifted like oil on water. The features rippled, never settling, as if the creature couldn't remember what it was supposed to look like. "Then you die here, like the dog they made you."
It moved faster this time. No more playing. Kael barely deflected the first strike, the impact rattling his teeth. He barely twisted away from the second. The Guardian's claws raked across his chest, tearing through leather and flesh. Pain exploded through his torso, hot and immediate, each breath a knife twisting between his ribs.
He staggered back. Blood welled hot and immediate, soaking through his torn shirt.
[Soul Harvest: You touched a being of corrupted soul-essence. Energy Drain initiated.]
The Soul Reaver didn't ask permission. It took.
Power flooded into Kael—raw, Abyssal, tasting of copper and ash and something that burned the back of his throat. His vision blurred. His muscles spasmed, tendons standing out like cords beneath his skin. The creature before him screamed, its form flickering between solid and shadow as Kael drained energy from its core being.
"Not... From... Me!" The Guardian thrashed, clawing at Kael's face. Nails raked his cheek, drawing burning lines.
Kael held on. The drain intensified. He felt the creature's centuries of accumulated power beginning to fracture, souls it had consumed for generations seeping through the connection into his own reservoir. The pain was exquisite. His body screamed. His soul rebelled. Every fiber of his being urged him to release, to stop, to let go before the torrent consumed him.
But he didn't let go.
"Die," Kael rasped. "Give it back."
The Seventh Trumpet shattered.
Not physically—its form remained, collapsed on the cobblestones like a puppet with cut strings—but its power was gone. Sucked dry. What remained was hollow, an empty vessel that would not last the night. The runes across its body went dark, one by one, like guttering candles.
Kael released the connection and collapsed to his knees. His hands shook. His blood dripped onto the stones, mixing with the ichor. His chest wound wept freely, pain radiating with every breath.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 847 units of Abyssal Energy from corrupted Guardian. Stage-appropriate ability unavailable—target incompatible with standard harvest protocols. Soul Essence Vial equivalent: 8.47 standard units.]
Eight vials' worth of soul-essence. More than he'd ever harvested in one sitting. His reserves felt like a churning ocean, threatening to overflow.
But no new abilities. The Guardian's power was too alien, too tied to its original purpose. Kael had drained the energy but gained nothing else.
"Stolen," he muttered. "Always stolen."
The collapsed Trumpet stirred. Its hollow eyes found Kael's face, the light in them guttering like a dying flame.
"You think... This changes... Anything?" Its voice came in fragments now, barely audible above the drip of water and fog. "The seals... Break... Regardless. The 72 wake. The Whisper King... Rises. And you... Little reaver..." A sound that might have been laughter. "You are... Already... Too late."
The eyes went dark.
Kael sat in the blood-soaked fog for a long moment, breathing through the pain. Each inhale tasted of copper and decay. His harvest burned inside him, unstable, threatening to burst free if he didn't process it soon. He needed shelter. He needed to think.
He needed to find out who the Whisper King really was.
Getting to his feet took everything he had. Kael braced himself against a crumbling wall, one hand pressed to his chest. The rough brick scraped against his shoulder blades. The wound was deep but not fatal—he'd had worse. The Soul Reaver accelerated healing in ways that still surprised him, knitting torn flesh with borrowed life force. Already the edges of the gash tingled, new cells knitting together with an itch that made his skin crawl.
A sound echoed from the drainage tunnel. Footsteps.
Kael's blade came up. His vision swam, the fog tilting around him before settling.
"Easy." A woman's voice, calm, measured. "I'm not here to fight."
She emerged from the fog like a ghost. Tall, dark-skinned, wearing the gray leathers of a Shadow Court operative. Her hair was cropped short, her eyes hidden behind a bone-white mask that covered only the upper half of her face. She moved with liquid grace, each step silent on the slick cobblestones.
"Lady Midnight sends her regards," she said, stopping ten feet away. "She wants to know if you're done playing with the Guardian's corpse."
Kael didn't lower his blade. "Who are you?"
"Someone who answers questions with questions. Like: why did the Seventh Trumpet tell you about the Whisper King? Why did it reveal that your power was a gift instead of a curse? Why—" She tilted her head. "—did it want you to know that you were being watched?"
"Manipulation."
"Obviously. But manipulation toward what end?" The woman took a step closer. "The Trumpet serves the seals. The Whisper King serves himself. These are not aligned purposes, Soul Reaver. Someone is playing a longer game."
"Talk plain."
"The Shadow Court has monitored your progress since you woke from your execution. Lady Midnight finds you... Interesting. Not because of what you can do, but because of what you refuse to do." Her masked eyes studied him. "You don't kill children. You don't betray allies. In a world of monsters, you maintain lines. Why?"
"Because some things matter."
"Do they?" She smiled, though he couldn't see it. "In the Underworld, everything is currency. Every choice, every death, every secret. The Whisper King collects these currencies. And you, Kael Ashborn, have accumulated a collection."
The fog swirled around them. Kael became aware of other presences in the darkness—not hostile, but watching. The weight of unseen eyes pressed against his skin. The Shadow Court's reach extended even here, to this blood-soaked alley where a Guardian had fallen.
"The cult activity in Thornwick," he said. "It's organized."
"Finally." The woman sounded pleased. "Yes. Six separate cells, each feeding a different seal. The Seventh Trumpet coordinated their efforts, but its power was bound to the Great Rift. As of ten minutes ago, that binding is severed." She gestured at the collapsed Guardian. "You severed it."
"I was defending myself."
"You disrupted a balance maintained for three hundred years. The seals will accelerate their decay now. The 72 will stir faster. The Whisper King's timeline has changed, and he will not be pleased."
Kael felt the weight of his actions for the first time. He'd killed the Trumpet to survive, not thinking about the consequences. In Thornwick, survival was a luxury. Consequences were currency.
"What does the Shadow Court want from me?"
"Information. We know the Soul Reaver originated from the First Sepulcher, where the original wielder recorded the True Names of the 72 Entities. Those tablets have been lost for centuries. We believe you can find them."
"I don't know where they are."
"You will." The woman's confidence was absolute. "Lady Midnight sees patterns where others see chaos. She's seen this pattern before—a Soul Reaver walking the path, collecting power, moving toward a confrontation that will reshape the world. It happened once. It will happen again."
She turned to leave.
"Wait." Kael's voice stopped her. "The Whisper King. You know who he is."
"I know many things." She looked back over her shoulder. "I know he was once human. I know he made a choice that preserved his life at the cost of everyone he loved. I know he has waited centuries for someone like you to appear—someone who can harvest the seals themselves and unmake what was made." A pause. "I know he will offer you everything you desire. And I know that whatever you decide, you will be alone when you decide it."
"That's not reassuring."
"It wasn't meant to be." She disappeared into the fog. Her voice echoed back. "The Shadow Court will be watching, Soul Reaver. We always watch."
Then silence.
Kael stood alone among the corpses, his blood cooling in the night air. The drainage tunnel yawned before him, dark and endless. The cult's cells were scattered throughout Thornwick, each one feeding a seal that kept the Abyss at bay. He'd disrupted one feeding. What happened to the others?
His chest throbbed. His hands wouldn't stop shaking.
He needed to move.
Kael limped toward the tunnel entrance, careful to step over the bodies. His boots slipped on the slick stones, each step sending fresh jolts of pain through his chest. The Trumpet's corpse flickered occasionally, its remaining essence bleeding into the stones. In a few hours, nothing would remain.
A familiar hunger stirred. He could harvest the Guardian's remains—wipe away this last trace of the creature that had nearly killed him. But something held him back. The hollow shell wasn't worth the effort. Its power was already his.
The tunnel swallowed him whole.
Water dripped from ancient pipes, the sound amplified by the narrow stone walls. Gaslights flickered in their sconces, casting dancing shadows on the sweating surfaces. The air grew colder, damper, carrying with it the mineral smell of deep earth and something else—something organic, like the breath of a sleeping beast. This was the Undercroft—a maze of forgotten passages that predated Thornwick itself, built by a civilization that had collapsed when the Abyssal Wars ended.
Kael walked for what felt like hours. The wound in his chest had stopped bleeding, though the pain remained—a dull, persistent ache that flared with each deep breath. His harvest continued to settle, the raw Abyssal energy slowly transforming into something he could use. It churned in his gut, an unstable warmth that pulsed and shifted.
A junction appeared ahead. Three tunnels diverged into darkness.
Kael stopped. One tunnel was marked with the Seventh Trumpet's sigil—broken horn and chains. Another bore the symbol of the Order of Dawn—a sun rising over a broken seal. The third was unmarked.
He knew which one the Shadow Court would want him to take. He knew which one led toward the truth about his power.
The question was whether he wanted that truth.
Footsteps echoed behind him. Multiple sets. Moving fast.
Kael drew his blade and pressed himself against the wall. The cold stone leeched warmth from his back. The footsteps grew louder, accompanied by the clank of armor and the hiss of spiritual energy.
"Contact ahead!" A man's voice, rough with authority. "Spiritual signature matches the reports. It's the Soul Reaver."
The Order of Dawn had found him.
Kael's mind raced. Three paths. Two enemies. One chance.
He could fight—but he was wounded, exhausted, and outnumbered. He could flee—but the Order would track him, and his trail would lead them to whatever secrets lay in these tunnels. He could hide—but the darkness wasn't deep enough, and Awakened senses were sharper than shadows.
The footsteps stopped.
"Moving into position. Ready suppression array."
Kael made his choice.
He dove into the unmarked tunnel, sprinting through darkness so complete it felt like drowning. The blackness pressed against his eyes, his outstretched hands scraping against rough stone walls. Behind him, the Order of Dawn began their pursuit, spiritual light chasing him into the unknown.
The tunnel curved. Dropped. Kael's boots found no solid ground.
He fell.
And as he fell, the Soul Reaver whispered to him—showing him a vision of what lay below. An abyss within the Abyss. A place where the seals met and the 72 Entities screamed in their chains. A place where the Whisper King waited.
A place where Kael would finally learn the truth about what he had become.
He hit water. Cold. Black. Filled with the echoes of dead men's final breaths.
The surface broke above him. Gaslight, distant now, flickered on a stone platform. Kael dragged himself onto it, coughing water and blood, his body screaming protest. The stone was slick beneath his palms, his fingers numb from the cold.
The platform stood at the edge of a vast underground lake. The ceiling vanished into darkness. The far shore was invisible. And rising from the center of the water, connected by a narrow bridge, stood a temple that should not have existed—its architecture wrong, its stones carved with symbols that hurt to perceive.
The Underworld. Not a metaphor. A place.
Kael lay on his back, staring up at impossible architecture, and laughed. It hurt. He didn't care.
"Alright," he gasped. "Show me what you've got."
Above him, the Order of Dawn's voices faded. The tunnel had sealed behind him, stone grinding against stone.
He was alone.
But not for long.
The water began to ripple.
Something was rising.
