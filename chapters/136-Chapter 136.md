The Bloodglass Construct moved like frozen lightning.
Kael's blade caught its crystalline arm an inch from his throat. Shards of glass exploded outward, slicing his cheek, his shoulder. The Construct didn't pause—it twisted, its other arm whipping toward his ribs with enough force to shatter bone.
He dropped beneath the strike, rolled through glass-littered cobblestones, came up with Soul Reaver singing in his veins. The weapon felt alive tonight, hungrier than usual, as if it sensed something worth taking.
"You're faster than the last one," the Construct spoke with a voice like grinding ice. Its body refracted the gaslight into rainbow fragments, each piece a mirror showing Kael a different angle of his own battered face. "But the Abyss remembers its debts."
Kael steadied his breathing. The alley behind him dead-ended at a warehouse wall. No retreat. He'd chosen this ground for that exact reason.
"Talk is cheap." He raised his blade. "Show me what you're made of."
The Construct laughed—a sound like a thousand windows breaking at once.
They clashed again.
Three months of hunting cultists in Thornwick's underbelly had taught Kael the rhythm of these fights. Constructs were tireless, emotionless, built for a single purpose. But they had tells. The way their crystalline lattice buzzed before a strike. The faint delay between attack and adjustment.
He exploited that delay now, ducking under a sweeping arm, driving his blade into a gap between chest plates. The construct shrieked—not pain, something else, something mechanical trying to imitate pain.
Glass exploded in his face.
Kael stumbled back, vision swimming. Blood ran freely from a dozen cuts. His left eye blurred with it. The Construct reformed its damaged section even as he watched, crystal knitting together with visible growth.
"You've killed four of my siblings." The Construct's tone shifted, colder now, analytical. "The Shadow Court is... Curious about you."
There it was. The hook.
"I'm flattered." Kael wiped blood from his eye. His fingers came away red. "Tell your masters I'll put them on the list too."
"You misunderstand." The Construct took a step closer. "The Shadow Court doesn't want you dead, Kael Ashborn. They want you *recruited*."
The name hit him like a physical blow. He hadn't given this Construct any name. Hadn't spoken it aloud tonight.
A door creaked open behind him.
Kael spun, blade raised, but it was only an old woman clutching a bundle of rags to her chest. Her eyes—too old, too knowing—flicked between Kael and the Construct with an expression he couldn't read.
"Not here," she whispered. "The Undercroft. Tonight. Bring the Construct's heart."
Then she was gone, vanishing through the door like smoke.
The Construct's mirror-surfaces flickered. For the first time, something like uncertainty crossed its fractured features.
"Who was that?" Kael demanded.
"You tell me." The Construct's voice had lost its confidence. "I've never seen her before."
They faced each other across three paces of broken glass. Both of them, Kael realized. Both being played.
But the old woman's words echoed in his skull: *Bring the Construct's heart.*
If it meant what he thought, killing this thing would require more than his blade.

The fight resumed with renewed fury. The Construct attacked without restraint now, its movements faster, more erratic. Fear, Kael realized. It was afraid.
Good.
He let it drive him back toward the warehouse wall. Every parry sent shockwaves up his arms. His blade arm trembled with exhaustion. Ascendant stage couldn't sustain this kind of prolonged combat—not against something that didn't tire, didn't bleed, didn't care.
But Kael wasn't fighting to win. He was fighting for time.
When the Construct's crystalline arm descended in a killing stroke, he didn't dodge. He dropped.
The arm struck the cobblestones where his head had been. The impact shattered a three-foot section of road. Dust and debris exploded upward.
Kael rolled forward, inside the Construct's reach, and slammed his free hand against its chest.
Soul Reaver woke.
The connection sparked through him like lightning seeking ground. He felt the Construct's essence—cold, geometric, alien—try to pull away. Too late.

The Undercroft existed in Thornwick's memory, not its maps.
Kael found the entrance beneath an abandoned chapel three streets from the warehouse district. The door was iron, rusted with age, marked with symbols that made his eyes water when he looked directly at them.
He knocked. Three times, then twice, then once.
Nothing happened for a long moment. Then the door swung inward without sound.
The corridor beyond was carved from living rock, lit by phosphorescent fungi that cast everything in sickly green. Water dripped somewhere in the darkness. The air tasted of copper and old death.
Kael descended.
The tunnel opened into a vast chamber, natural but clearly modified. Stalactites hung like teeth from the ceiling. In the center, a pool of black water reflected nothing. Around it, figures stood in loose circles.
The old woman waited beside the pool, her bundle of rags discarded. She wore robes now—deep purple, embroidered with silver thread that formed patterns Kael didn't recognize.
"You came." She didn't sound surprised. "And you brought the heart. Good."
"I came because you know my name." Kael held the crystalline sphere up. "How?"
"I've known your name since before you woke from your execution." The old woman's smile revealed teeth filed to points. "I've known what you carry since the first Soul Reaver died."
The words hung in the phosphorescent air.
"What do you want?" Kael kept his blade ready. "And don't tell me recruitment. I'm not joining whatever shadow organization this is."
"Not shadow." The old woman gestured to the figures around the pool. "Underworld. Different thing entirely."
As his eyes adjusted, Kael began to distinguish the figures. They weren't cultists. They weren't Order members. They wore mismatched clothing—peasant work shirts alongside noble velvets, military jackets without insignia. All of them had one thing in common: spiritual pressure so subtle it barely registered.
Awakened, but hiding it. Storing it.

We fight the Abyss in its own territory. We harvest where harvesting is forbidden. We take from the dead because someone must, and the living won't."
She nodded toward his hand.
"Let me see that."
Kael hesitated. Every instinct screamed trap. But the old woman's knowledge gnawed at him. She knew things. Things that could answer questions he'd carried since waking from death.
He crossed to the pool and held out the crystalline heart.
Orin's fingers brushed its surface. Her eyes unfocused, drifting inward.
"Beautiful craftsmanship. The Shadow Court has improved their assembly process." She looked up. "This Construct held eleven souls. Eleven people who made the mistake of trusting the wrong faction. Their families thought them dead. The Orders marked them as casualties of the Abyssal Wars. We know better."
"You know who made it."
"We know who funds its creation." Orin set the heart on the pool's edge. "The Shadow Court is a consortium, Kael. Not a single organization, but many, working toward similar goals. Some want the seals broken. Others want the Soul Reaver power for themselves. A few—very few—want to understand what the original Reaver truly achieved before he vanished."
Kael's pulse quickened. "The original Soul Reaver."
"You didn't think you were the first."
It wasn't a question. Orin studied him with eyes that had seen centuries.
"The first Soul Reaver walked this world three hundred years ago. He discovered the ability to harvest from the dead—not just essence, but memory, skill, fate itself. He built the Sepulcher Tablets to record his findings. Then he disappeared."
"Disappeared how?"
"We don't know. His tomb has never been found, if he has one. His tablets are scattered across the continent, hidden by those who fear their knowledge. But his legacy remains." She tapped the crystalline heart. "The Shadow Court builds Constructs from harvested souls using techniques derived from the original Reaver's research. They've spent centuries refining the flow. Your ability to harvest directly from corpses? That power frightened them enough to orchestrate your execution."
The pieces clicked into place. Zarek framing him, the false charges, the convenient death sentence—all designed to eliminate a threat they couldn't control.
"Why tell me this?" Kael kept his voice level. "What's the angle?"
"The Underworld Compact has no love for the Shadow Court. They poach our recruits, corrupt our networks, turn our own against us when it suits their purposes. You've killed four of their Constructs in three months. That's more than we've managed in two years."
"I'm not working for you."
"No. You're working for yourself. That's precisely why this might work." Orin stepped back. "We're offering information, not employment. Locations of Shadow Court operations. Names of their agents in the Orders. The location of the next Sepulcher Tablet fragment. In exchange, you continue doing what you've been doing—killing Constructs, harvesting from their remains, making the Court's life difficult."
"What's the catch?"
"The catch is that the Shadow Court has been watching you since before your execution. They're not the only ones who want you recruited—or destroyed. The Constructs you've been killing? They're not random attacks. They're tests. The Court has been measuring your power, your potential, your weaknesses."
Orin's expression hardened.
"They know you're Ascendant stage now. They know your harvest radius, your combat style, your preferred hunting grounds. In three months, they've gathered enough data to make a serious attempt at your capture. Which means you need to grow faster than they expect. The Compact can help with that."
Kael looked at the crystalline heart. Inside, eleven souls remained trapped in frozen agony.
"These eleven people. Can they be freed?"
Orin's expression flickered—surprise, then something like respect.
"Maybe. The Underworld Compact specializes in extraction. We have techniques the Orders have forgotten, tools the Shadow Court would kill to possess. But freeing them would cost you. We don't work for free."

"Two things. First: the Cult of the Burning Eye is preparing a mass ritual at the Thrice-Broken Rift. We need them stopped before they weaken the seal further. Second: we need a sample of your harvested essence. Pure, unprocessed. A demonstration of your capabilities."
Kael's stomach turned. Giving away harvested essence meant weakening himself. But eleven souls trapped in eternal torment...

Lyra. Working parallel cases without knowing they were connected.

The harvest was painless—strange, after so much pain. A thread of crimson energy flowed into the vial, carrying fragments of everything he'd taken tonight: crystalline structure, frozen reflexes, memories of Shadow Court meetings.

The walk back to the surface felt longer than the descent.
Kael emerged from the chapel into Thornwick's gray dawn. Smoke rose from factory chimneys. Steam carriages rumbled through distant streets. The city had no idea what moved beneath its foundations.
He found a quiet alley and sat with his back against cold brick. His hands shook. Not from exhaustion—his reserves would recover within hours. Something else. Something deeper.
The old woman had known things. About him, about Soul Reaver, about the first user of this power. She'd hinted at a tomb, at scattered tablets, at knowledge that could change everything.
But she'd also confirmed what Kael had suspected: the Shadow Court had orchestrated his execution. Zarek was a pawn, or a willing participant. Either way, the man had a debt coming.
His thoughts turned to Lyra. She'd been investigating the Cult of the Burning Eye without telling him. Working her own angles, her own deals. The Shadow Court connection to her past—Dorian's betrayal, her squad's death—kept circling back no matter how many times he tried to set it aside.
He needed to talk to her. Soon. Before the cult's ritual, before the Shadow Court's next move.
But first, he needed rest. Blood loss and spiritual exhaustion pulled at his edges. Tomorrow, he'd hunt the cultists. Tonight, he'd recover.
Kael closed his eyes. Sleep came fast, dreamless, mercifully empty.

Moonlight silvered the headstones. Fog crept between graves, carrying the scent of turned earth and older things. The Thrice-Broken Rift squatted at the cemetery's heart—a wound in reality, three intersecting fractures sealed by symbols that glowed faint red in the darkness.
Figures moved among the graves. Dozens of them, robed in black, faces hidden. They carried torches that burned with wrong-colored flame—green, purple, colors that had no place in the natural world.
At the rift's edge, a man in crimson robes raised his arms. His voice carried across the cemetery, chanting words that made Kael's ears ring.

The cultists took up the chant. Their voices rose, discordant and hungry.

Unless he didn't fight them at all.

Dawn found Kael in his rented room above a tanner's shop. His wounds had closed, his reserves had stabilized, and the harvested memories replayed behind his eyes like fever dreams.
The Whisper King. A name that made even Shadow Court agents nervous. Someone who commanded cult networks across the continent, who ordered squads to die, who wanted the seals broken badly enough to sacrifice anything.
Kael sat at the room's small table, turning the crystalline heart over in his hands. Inside, eleven souls still cried for release. The Underworld Compact had offered extraction—for a price. Tonight, he'd decide whether to pay it.
A knock at the door.
He rose, blade in hand, and opened the door a crack.

*"Your mother's grave is not where you think. Come to the Drowned District, pier 19, midnight. Come alone."*
No signature. No explanation.
Kael's blood ran cold.
His mother had died when he was seven. She'd been buried in a pauper's field outside Thornwick's walls—or so the orphanage records claimed. No one had ever told him differently.

A trap, almost certainly.
He read the note again, searching for clues. The handwriting was precise, controlled. Not desperate. Not threatening.
Invitation.
Kael thought of the Underworld Compact's words. *They've been measuring your power, your potential, your weaknesses.* Thought of the Shadow Court's tests.
Thought of his mother's face—faded, half-remembered, belonging to a woman who'd died before he could truly know her.
Pier 19. Midnight.
The Drowned District was the worst part of Thornwick. Abandoned after a flood thirty years ago, never rebuilt. Criminal dens, cultist meeting grounds, places where Awakened went to disappear.
Going there alone would be stupid.
Going there would also be the only way to learn who knew his mother's secrets.

The game was still being played. He just hadn't realized how many pieces were already on the board.
