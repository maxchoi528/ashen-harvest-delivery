"Sera, your sister is alive." The words hit like a blade between ribs.

"She's being held in a Mortician's Guild facility," Kael continued, his voice low. The Soul Reaver marks on his forearm pulsed faintly, remnants of yesterday's harvest still tingling beneath his sleeve. "North quarter. Underground. Sera's breath caught. "That's not Brennan."
"No." Kael drew a slender knife from his belt, the edge catching lamplight. "It's not."

"Open it," Sera whispered. "Brennan said only he knows this signal."
Kael crossed the room in three strides. His free hand found the iron latch, and he wrenched the door inward.
A woman stood in the corridor. Silver threaded through her dark hair, crow's feet at the corners of steel-gray eyes. She wore the crimson-trimmed robes of the Order of Dawn, but something in her posture suggested she wasn't here in any official capacity.
"Kael Ashborn." Her voice carried the weight of authority, accustomed to obedience. "We need to talk about what's really happening in this city."
Kael didn't lower the knife. "You're Dawn's spymaster. Vance Morren's right hand."
"I'm someone who's watched you stumble into the middle of a conspiracy you don't understand." She glanced past him to where Sera stood frozen by the table. "I also know about your sister, Magistra. The one the Mortician's Guild has been keeping comfortable but very much a prisoner."

Kael's mind raced through possibilities. The Order of Dawn was the largest of the Six Orders, their Cathedral dominating Thornwick's highest hill. If their spymaster had come personally, the stakes were higher than he'd calculated.
"Talk fast," he said. "Why should I trust an Order operative?"

Sable's gaze shifted to Kael. "The Mortician's Guild has been harvesting prisoners for years. Everyone assumed they were selling to the Veiled Market, maybe funneling souls to rogue Awakened. The truth is worse. They're feeding a ritual network. Someone is building a reservoir of soul-essence large enough to crack a sealed rift."
The words hung in the air like smoke.
Kael's pulse hammered against his throat. "The 72 sealed rifts. You're saying someone wants to open one deliberately?"

This—" She tapped a point across the city. "—is a cellar beneath the old tanneries. And this—" A third location, near the docks. "—is where the ritual components are being assembled."

"I need someone with nothing to lose and everything to gain." Sable met his gaze directly. "A Soul Reaver. Your kind walks between the worlds others can't touch. You harvest what we cannot take without consequence. And right now, Thornwick is drowning in fresh corpses."
Kael thought of the bodies he'd passed on the streets. The covered shapes near the market square, carted away by Guild workers with blank expressions. Deaths attributed to disease, to poverty, to accidents. How many had been killed specifically to feed this ritual?
"What do you want from me?"
"Information first. Then action." Sable rose from her chair. "The cellar beneath the tanneries—visit it tonight. There will be bodies. Harvest what you can. Find proof of the connection between the Mortician's Guild and whoever is funding this operation. Bring me names."

Sera stepped forward. "My sister. You said you have resources."
"I have leverage." Sable inclined her head slightly. "The Guild's facility is protected by wards keyed to specific bloodlines. Magistra's blood is one of those keys—she was taken specifically because of her lineage. But I can get you inside. I can ensure your sister walks free."

Sable smiled, though the expression held no warmth. "Then the cult completes their ritual. Rune 31 opens wider, and the Entity beyond tears through Thornwick's eastern districts before anyone can respond. Thousands dead. The Orders scramble to contain the breach. By the time the dust settles, your sister's usefulness expires, and the Guild disposes of loose ends."

"Then we move at sundown."

The cellar beneath the old tanneries reeked of old blood and older secrets.
Kael descended first, his boots splashing through inches of stagnant water that seeped through cracked stone. The walls sweated with moisture, and somewhere in the darkness, rats scattered from the light of his hooded lantern.
Sera followed close behind, her breath misting in the cold. She'd refused to stay behind, arguing that if her sister was connected to this operation, she deserved to see the truth firsthand.
The passage widened into a vaulted chamber. Rows of tables stretched into the gloom, each one supporting shapes covered in oilcloth. Kael counted twelve. Thirteen. Fifteen.
"Gods preserve us," Sera whispered.
Kael approached the nearest table and pulled back the covering.
The corpse was fresh. Male, middle-aged, dressed in the roughspun clothes of a dockworker. His chest had been opened with surgical precision, the sternum sawed through and peeled back to expose an empty thoracic cavity.
No heart. No lungs. Only hollow space where organs should be.
"Harvested," Kael said quietly. "Someone extracted everything useful and left the shell."
"Extracted for what?"
He didn't answer immediately. His hand hovered over the body's chest, fingers brushing the cold flesh. The Soul Reaver marks on his forearm flared with heat.
[System Notification: Fresh Corpse Detected. Proximity harvest available. Estimated yield: Low-grade spiritual residue, dock worker's physical resilience. Quality: Degraded.]
Kael pressed his palm flat against the corpse's sternum.
The harvest hit him like ice water flooding his veins.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 3 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Acquired: Dock Worker's Endurance (Minor) — +5% stamina recovery when exerting physical labor. Duration: three days.]
Fragments of memory surfaced unbidden. The man's face, weathered by salt wind. The weight of cargo on his shoulders. A woman waiting by a doorway, holding an infant. A shadow that followed him through the warehouse district, patient and silent.
Kael pulled his hand back, shaking off the phantom sensations. "He was a normal man. Unawakened. No spiritual gifts to speak of."
"Then why harvest him?" Sera moved to the next table, pulling back the cloth with trembling fingers. Another empty corpse. Then another. All the same—surgically stripped, organs removed, soul residue drained.
"These aren't random victims." Kael's voice came out harder than he intended. "The surgical precision, the timing of the deaths—it's coordinated. Someone is selecting specific people for specific traits."
"Traits?"
"Soul-essence isn't uniform." He moved to a third table, this one holding a woman's body. Younger. Her hands still clutched the fabric of her dress, knuckles rigid with final terror. "An Awakened's soul carries their spiritual gifts. An ordinary person's soul carries... Everything else. Their resilience. Their vitality. Their emotional bonds. Extract enough of that from enough people, and you can fuel rituals that would normally require dozens of Awakened sacrifices."
Sera's face went pale in the lantern light. "They're using commoners as fuel."
"More efficient than risking Order attention. Dead Awakened get investigated. Dead dockworkers get forgotten."
Kael lifted the woman's covering fully, examining the incision pattern. The same technique. The same instruments. Guild-certified surgeons worked these tables—he recognized the characteristic incision angles from his own medical education, back when he'd still believed healing was possible without cost.
A sound echoed from deeper in the chamber. A door opening, maybe, or boots on stone.
Kael killed the lantern. Darkness swallowed them whole.
"Behind the tables," he breathed. "Don't move. Don't speak."
They pressed themselves against the cold stone wall, breathing through their mouths to stay silent. Footsteps approached. Measured. Unhurried.
A sphere of pale blue light drifted into the chamber, illuminating the passage from which it came. Behind it walked two figures in gray robes, their faces hidden behind featureless masks of white porcelain.
"—completed the inventory," one was saying. "Forty-seven specimens this cycle. Adequate for the secondary reservoir, but the primary will require additional specimens from the Awakened pool."
"The Guild is nervous about accelerating the timeline." The second voice was female, clinical in its detachment. "Master Zarek insists the window is narrowing. Rune 31's deterioration has accelerated beyond projections."
Kael's blood froze at the name.
Zarek. The same Zarek who had framed him for murder. The same Zarek who had orchestrated his original execution. Here, in this cellar, discussing ritual networks and soul harvesting like a merchant tallying inventory.
"The Soul Reaver complicates matters." The first speaker gestured toward the tables. "If he's as capable as the reports suggest, he could expose the entire operation."
"Then we eliminate him before he becomes a problem. The Shadow Court is monitoring his movements. At the first sign of interference—"
The pale light swept toward Kael's position.
He held his breath. Beside him, Sera's fingers dug into his sleeve, her nails drawing crescents through his coat.
The light paused. Held.
Then continued past, drifting toward the chamber's far end where another door waited.
"—need to verify the ward integrity before dawn," the female voice continued, fading into the distance. "The last thing we require is an unexpected visitor stumbling into the wrong room."
The door closed behind them. Their footsteps receded. Silence returned.
Kael didn't move for a full minute afterward.
"Zarek," Sera finally whispered. "The man who destroyed your life is running the operation."
"Yes." The word tasted like ash. "And now I know where to find him."

They emerged from the tannery district as dawn broke over Thornwick's eastern spires.
Kael's mind churned through implications. The cult wasn't just operating in the city—they were embedded within its infrastructure. The Mortician's Guild. The Shadow Court. And Zarek, at the center of it all, orchestrating rituals that could crack open a sealed rift and unleash an Abyssal Entity.
The weight of it pressed against his chest.
"What did you see?" Sera asked as they navigated the morning crowds. Workers streamed toward the docks, carts rattled over cobblestones, and steam-whistles shrieked from the factories along the river. Normal life, oblivious to the horrors beneath its surface.
"Confirmation. The cult is harvesting ordinary people to fuel their ritual." Kael kept his voice low. "They're coordinating with the Mortician's Guild, using official infrastructure to process bodies and extract soul-essence. The Shadow Court is involved—actively monitoring the situation, possibly running intelligence for whoever's pulling the strings."
"And Zarek?"
"Zarek is calling the shots." The rage that had sustained Kael through months of exile and survival flared hot in his gut. "He's not just some cult fanatic. He's planning something massive. Rune 31 is just the beginning."
Sera grabbed his arm, pulling him into a narrow alley away from the foot traffic. "If he's the key to all of this, why hasn't the Order of Dawn moved against him?"
"Because they don't know who he is." Kael's eyes swept the alley's entrances, checking for observers. "The name Zarek means nothing to them. I recognized it from my own history—the man who testified against me at the tribunal, who presented evidence I couldn't refute. But that testimony was sealed. Classified. Only a handful of officials know what Zarek represents."
"And you think Sable is one of them."
"I think Sable knows more than she's telling." Kael leaned against the alley's damp brick wall. "She sent us to that cellar for a reason. Not just to gather information—to confirm what she already suspected. She wanted proof she could use."
"To move against the cult?"
"To move against anyone who gets in her way." Kael rubbed his temples, where the first hints of a harvest headache pulsed. "The Order of Dawn isn't interested in saving Thornwick. They're interested in consolidating power. If Zarek's ritual succeeds, they lose influence. If we stop it without their involvement, they lose credit. Sable is playing a political game with our lives as the stakes."
Sera was quiet for a long moment. When she spoke, her voice carried an edge Kael hadn't heard before. "Then we play a different game. Forget Sable. Forget the Orders. We take the information we have and we use it ourselves."
"How?"
"My sister is in that facility. The one Sable marked on her map." Sera's jaw tightened. "If the Mortician's Guild is connected to the cult, then their facility is a legitimate target. We don't need Order permission to conduct a rescue."
Kael studied her face. The desperation he'd seen earlier had hardened into resolve. This wasn't just about family anymore—it was about.
"You're talking about assaulting a fortified position with limited resources and no backup."
"I'm talking about taking back what was stolen from me." Sera met his gaze without flinching. "You have a knife. I have information about the facility's layout. We have surprise and motivation. That's more than most rescue operations start with."
She wasn't wrong. The facility would be guarded, but probably not by Awakened—the Guild preferred mundane security for locations that weren't supposed to exist. Wards and locks instead of warriors.
"The timing matters," Kael said slowly. "If Zarek is accelerating the ritual timeline, he'll be focused on that operation. Less attention on secondary facilities. Less security."
"Which means tonight. Before anyone realizes we've moved."
Kael nodded, the decision crystallizing in his mind. "Tonight. But we do this smart. No charging in blind. I need to harvest from a fresh enough source to get combat capabilities, not just dock worker's endurance."
"Where do we find a fresh corpse?"
"The Guild itself." Kael pushed off from the wall. "They process bodies for transport. Somewhere in their supply chain, there's a mortuary with recent arrivals. I harvest one of their workers—a guard, preferably—and we gain access credentials along with fighting skills."
"That's cold."
"That's necessary." He started walking, and Sera fell into step beside him. "Soul Reaving isn't clean. Every corpse I touch takes something from them. Memories. Personality fragments. The residue of who they were." His hand drifted unconsciously to his forearm, where the marks pulsed faintly. "But it's also the only advantage I have. The only way to fight monsters with equal footing."
They emerged from the alley into the morning bustle. The sun climbed higher, burning off the river fog. Somewhere in the city, Zarek was preparing his ritual. Somewhere else, Sable was waiting for intelligence that would never come through official channels.
And in a Guild mortuary on the city's north side, fresh bodies waited to give Kael the strength he needed.

The Mortician's Guild facility operated under the official name "Thornwick Memorial Resting House."
Kael casing the building from across the street recognized the architecture—three stories of gray stone, narrow windows, a chimney that belched smoke from the crematorium in the basement. The front entrance bore a modest brass plaque and a painted sign depicting an angel with closed eyes.
But the back told a different story. A delivery entrance opened into an alley where carts arrived throughout the day, carrying covered shapes that never emerged through the front. The frequency was wrong for a resting house. Too many bodies, arriving too regularly, from too many different districts.
"The transport schedule," Sera whispered. She'd taken position beside him, her breath visible in the cold morning air. "Based on what I saw when they took my sister, deliveries happen at noon and midnight. Fresh arrivals are processed immediately—sorted, documented, and routed to either storage or extraction."
"Extraction being the Guild's euphemism for soul harvesting."
"Or redistribution to clients." Sera's voice carried bitter certainty. "The Guild isn't just a mortuary. It's a distribution network. They harvest what they can, sell what they harvest, and dispose of what's left."
Kael watched two workers emerge from the delivery entrance, wheeling a gurney toward a waiting cart. The shape beneath the cloth was small. A child, maybe, or a slender adult.
His stomach clenched. The antihero code he'd set for himself warred with tactical necessity. He didn't kill children. He didn't betray genuine allies. But everyone else?
"How do we get inside?"
"The service entrance." Sera produced a folded paper from her coat. "Layout blueprints, copied from Guild archives by a contact who owed me favors. The delivery area connects to the flowing wing through a shared corridor. If we time it right, during a shift change, we can slip through without being noticed."
"And once inside?"
"You find a recent corpse. I find my sister." She folded the blueprint back into her pocket. "We have thirty minutes before the noon delivery arrives. That's our window to get positioned, harvest what you need, and reach the detention level."
Kael calculated distances, guard rotations, extraction routes. The plan was thin. Rushed. But it accounted for the variables they could control and accepted the risks they couldn't.
"Let's move."
They crossed the street as the church bells tolled eleven. The morning crowd provided cover—workers heading to lunch, merchants closing their stalls, street vendors hawking their wares. Kael kept his collar turned up and his hat pulled low, though with his reputation, anonymity had become a luxury he couldn't afford.
The alley behind the facility smelled of formaldehyde and rotting flowers. Sera led him to a side door half-hidden behind stacked crates, its lock a simple mechanical device that yielded to her picks in under thirty seconds.
Inside, the corridors stretched in labyrinthine patterns, walls painted institutional green, floors scuffed by decades of gurney wheels. They moved through shadows, ducking into alcoves when voices echoed from adjacent rooms.
The detention level lay two floors below ground.
Sera pressed her ear to a door marked "STAFF ONLY." Her hand signals told Kael what he needed to know—the room beyond was empty.
They descended a narrow staircase, emerging into a basement corridor lit by flickering gaslight. The walls here were reinforced concrete, the doors steel-reinforced with small windows at eye level. Prison cells, not preparation rooms.
"Section C," Sera whispered, consulting her mental map. "Twenty meters ahead."
Kael's attention caught on a door slightly ajar at the corridor's end. Medical equipment peeked through the gap—a surgical table, instrument trays, the unmistakable gleam of chrome.
"A morgue," he breathed.
"Go." Sera squeezed his arm. "I'll find my sister. Meet you at the service entrance in twenty minutes."
She disappeared around the corner before he could respond.
Kael approached the morgue door, his heart hammering against his ribs. Beyond lay what he needed—fresh corpses, recent harvests, spiritual essence waiting to be claimed. And with it, the fighting skills that might mean the difference between success and a cell of his own.
He eased the door open.
Three tables occupied the room. Two held covered shapes. The third was empty, instrument tray positioned beside it, drains in the floor ready to catch whatever fluids accumulated.
Kael crossed to the nearest table and pulled back the cloth.
The corpse was male, early thirties, dressed in the Guild's gray uniform. A worker's badge clipped to his lapel identified him as a "Processing Technician." His chest had been opened already—the Guild extracting whatever soul-essence remained before disposal.
But the body's neck told a different story. Purple bruising radiated from beneath the collar. Ligature marks. This man hadn't died on the table.
He'd been killed elsewhere and brought here for processing.
[Soul Harvest: Corpse age several hours. Spiritual residue: Moderate. Technician skills detected. Proceeding with extraction?]
Kael's palm found the cooling flesh.
The harvest flooded through him.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 15 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Acquired: Combat Training (Guild Security) — Proficiency in unarmed restraint techniques, bludgeoning weapons, and defensive positioning. Duration: Permanent acquisition.]
Fragments of the man's memories crashed into Kael's consciousness. Training exercises in a cramped basement. Learning where to strike to incapacitate without killing. The satisfaction of a job well done, a steady paycheck, a corner of the world he understood. And beneath it all, a growing unease—whispers about what happened to the bodies after processing, questions he learned not to ask.
Kael withdrew his hand, flexing fingers that felt different. Sharper. More precise.
"Soul Reaver."
The voice came from behind him.
Kael spun, every muscle coiling for combat. A woman stood in the doorway, her features sharp, her eyes colder than the corpses on the tables. She wore Guild robes, but the sigil on her collar marked her as something else entirely.
Shadow Court.
"Iron Zarek's pet spymaster sent me to watch you," she said, stepping into the room. A second figure appeared behind her, blocking the exit—a hulking man with hands like sledgehammers. "He predicted you'd come here eventually. Your obsession with the Guild made it inevitable."
Kael's mind raced. Zarek had anticipated this. He'd been expected.
"What does Zarek want?"
"Everything." The woman's lips curved into something approximating a smile. "And you're going to help him get it. One way or another."
The door behind her slammed shut. Locks engaged—audible clicks of steel sliding into place.
Kael's new combat instincts screamed warnings. Two opponents, one exit, limited space. The woman carried a blade at her hip. The man's stance suggested grappling experience.
And somewhere above him, Sera was walking into a trap of her own.
The woman's hand drifted toward her weapon. "You can cooperate, or you can die here. The Soul Reaver abilities transfer to new hosts fairly reliably. Your choice."
Kael's fingers closed around his knife. The harvest's edge sang through his blood.
"Funny," he said. "I was thinking the same thing."
He moved.
The woman's blade cleared its sheath a heartbeat too late. Kael's knife took her in the wrist, severing tendons before she could draw. She screamed, clutching the ruined hand, and he used her body as a springboard to launch at the larger man.
Combat Training activated without conscious thought. His body knew angles, distances, the precise points where force would disable than only hurt. He ducking under a haymaker meant to crush his skull, driving his elbow into the man's kidney, following with a knee to the groin that doubled him over.
A palm strike to the temple dropped the brute like a sack of grain.
Kael stood over both bodies, breathing hard, his hands steady despite the adrenaline flooding his system. The Combat Training had performed beyond expectations—the Guild had trained their security well.
He retrieved his knife from the woman's grip, wiping the blade clean on her robes. "Tell Zarek I'm coming for him. Personally."
The woman laughed through gritted teeth, blood streaming from her wrist. "He's counting on it."
Kael kicked the door open and sprinted toward the detention level. Sera needed warning. The facility needed to burn. And somewhere in the chaos, he needed to find the truth about Zarek's connection to the cult and the ritual that threatened to crack open the world.
He took the stairs two at a time.
Footsteps echoed from the corridor ahead—rapid, frightened. Sera burst around the corner, her face ashen, her eyes wild.
"Kael, they're coming. The whole facility—"
The lights went out. Emergency lanterns flickered on, painting the hallway in crimson.
And from somewhere below, a deep vibration shook the floor.
Not footsteps. Not machinery.
Something waking up.
The vibration intensified. Dust sifted from the ceiling. The walls groaned under pressure that had nothing to do with structural stress.
"Something's been summoned," Kael breathed. "Or released."
Sera grabbed his arm. "The service entrance. Now. Before they seal the building."
They ran.
The corridor stretched impossibly long, shadows dancing in the emergency light. The vibration grew stronger, accompanied now by sounds that defied easy description—wet meat slapping stone, bones grinding against bone, a rhythm that matched no heartbeat Kael had ever heard.
The service entrance appeared ahead. Freedom, if they could reach it.
The door exploded inward.
A shape filled the frame. Massive. Wrong. Its body was stitched together from a dozen corpses, limbs mismatched, faces fused into a screaming mass where a head should be. Spiritual energy radiated from its form like heat from a forge.
"Guild construct," Kael snarled. "They built a puppet from harvested souls."
The creature moved.
Faster than anything that size should move. Its arm swept toward them, fingers tipped with blades scavenged from surgical equipment.
Kael pushed Sera aside and met the attack head-on.
His knife found a gap between the construct's stitched joints. Combat Training guided the strike to a critical point—a nexus where harvested souls overlapped, their combined will creating a fragile unity the blade could shatter.
The construct screamed. Not one voice but dozens, a chorus of harvested souls crying out as the binding broke.
It collapsed, bodies separating, the unified horror dissolving back into component parts.
Kael didn't wait to see what emerged from the remains. He grabbed Sera and ran.
They burst into daylight, tumbling onto the cobblestones of the alley beyond. The facility's back door hung crooked on its hinges, smoke pouring from within.
Behind them, the construct reformed. Slower this time, its component bodies struggling to reassemble, but rebuilding nonetheless.
"We need to destroy the source," Kael said, forcing himself to his feet. "The construct draws power from something inside. The soul reservoir, probably."
"We can't go back in there."
"No." He watched the creature's shape coalesce, arms lengthening, faces multiplying. "But I can bring the source to it."
He reached into his coat and withdrew a small glass vial—the soul-essence he'd harvested from the dockworker, still potent, still volatile.
"You can't be serious."
"Watch me."
Kael drew a sigil on the vial's surface with his knife's tip. The mark flared briefly—a crude approximation of the binding formula he'd glimpsed in the construct's spiritual anatomy. Imprecise. Dangerous. But sufficient.
He hurled the vial through the broken door.
For a moment, nothing happened.
Then the explosion tore through the facility like thunder made flesh.
The construct absorbed most of the blast, its component souls destabilizing as the stolen essence responded to the binding disruption. The building groaned, walls buckling, floors collapsing inward.
Kael pulled Sera away as debris rained down around them. The construct's reformation halted, bodies falling apart faster than they could reassemble, the driving force behind them extinguished.
When the dust settled, nothing remained of the service entrance but rubble and flame.
"We need to go," Kael said. "The Shadow Court will have reported this. Zarek will know I'm involved."
"Your sister?" Sera asked, though her expression suggested she already knew the answer.
"The detention level collapsed. If she was down there..." Kael trailed off. The words weren't necessary.
Sera's face crumpled. For a moment, she looked every bit as lost as she'd been when they'd first met—the woman whose family had been torn away, whose life had been reduced to survival and obsession.
Then the grief hardened into something else.
"Then we make it matter." Her voice carried an edge that hadn't been there before. "Zarek did this. The cult did this. They killed my sister to fuel their ritual, and they're going to keep killing until someone stops them."
"We'll stop them," Kael said. "But not today. Today we survive. Tomorrow we plan."
Sera nodded, wiping her eyes with the back of her hand. "Where do we go?"
Kael considered the options. The Order of Dawn was compromised—Sable had admitted as much. The Shadow Court wanted him dead or controlled. The Mortician's Guild would be hunting them now.
But there was one place in Thornwick where no faction held sway. One place where the rules of the Awakened world didn't apply.
"The Veiled Market." He started walking, Sera falling into step beside him. "We'll find allies there. People who owe me favors. People who hate Zarek more than they fear what I am."
"And then?"
Kael's jaw tightened. "Then we bring down the conspiracy. One piece at a time. Until Zarek has nowhere left to hide."
The sun climbed higher over Thornwick, indifferent to the fires still burning in the Guild facility. Somewhere in the city, Shadow Court operatives would be reporting what they'd witnessed. Somewhere deeper, Zarek would be recalculating, adjusting his plans to account for a variable he'd thought he understood.
The Soul Reaver was coming for him.
And this time, Kael Ashborn wouldn't stop until the man who had destroyed his life had nothing left to lose.
Behind them, the flames consumed what remained of the facility. Ahead, the streets of Thornwick bustled with ordinary people living ordinary lives, unaware that the world they knew was balanced on a knife's edge.
