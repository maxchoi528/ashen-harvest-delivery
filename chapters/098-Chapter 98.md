"Soul Harvest: You extracted Lightning Affinity from the remains. Skill acquired: Chain Lightning."
Kael's fingers trembled against the assassin's cooling forehead. The notification burned through his consciousness like a brand, brighter than any he had received before. Chain Lightning. A combat skill worth dying for—, in this bastard's case.
The underground passage stank of lamp oil and old blood. Kael withdrew his hand, wiping the residue on his trouser leg. Three bodies lay scattered across the tunnel's floor: the assassin he'd just reaped, and two others who'd attacked him moments after he'd entered the Veiled Market's lower tunnels.
"You're even more disgusting than the rumors claimed."
Gwen emerged from the shadows, her silver eyes reflecting the distant glow of a hand-lantern. She leaned against the damp stone wall, arms crossed, watching him with an expression caught somewhere between fascination and revulsion.
"Those men worked for the Veiled Market's enforcers," she continued. "They won't stop sending people. You realize that, yes? You've made yourself a target."
Kael stood, rolling the tension from his shoulders. His new skill hummed at the edge of his awareness, eager and crackling. "I was already a target. At least now I'm getting something out of it."
From somewhere deeper in the tunnel, footsteps echoed. Multiple sets, moving with purpose.
"We need to move," Gwen said. "The Market's security will be here in two minutes. There's a passage behind the third grate on your left—it leads to the Ashfall Sewers. From there, you can surface in Fogmire."
Kael glanced at the bodies. "What about them?"
"The rats will handle cleanup." Gwen pushed off from the wall and started walking. "Unless you want to reave those two as well. Might be worth it—they were Awakened. Market pays well for trained muscle."
He considered it. The corpses were fresh, killed only minutes ago in the crossfire when the assassin had tried to put a knife in his back. Kael had deflected the blade with a burst of harvested strength and returned the favor with a shattering strike to the man's throat.
But the footsteps were closer now, and Gwen was already disappearing around a corner.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted Battle Meditation from the remains. Skill acquired: Battle Meditation."
He wasn't sure why he did it. Maybe curiosity. Maybe the growing certainty that he needed every advantage he could get. Kael dropped beside the nearest corpse and pressed his palm to its forehead.
The extraction hit him like ice water flooding his veins. Memories of endless training sessions, of discipline carved into bone, of a childhood spent learning to find calm in the eye of a storm—all of it poured through him in a rushing torrent. When it finished, Kael gasped and nearly fell backward.
[Skill Acquired: Battle Meditation — You can enter a meditative state during combat, reducing the spiritual cost of all abilities by 30%. Duration: a few minutes. Cooldown: a few hours.]
Two skills in one night. His luck was holding.
But the footsteps had reached the tunnel's entrance. Kael saw torchlight bouncing off the walls, heard the clatter of armored boots on stone.
He ran.
The passage behind the third grate was barely wide enough for his shoulders. Kael squeezed through, feeling ancient iron scrape against his back, and emerged into a wider tunnel that sloped downward into darkness. The smell changed immediately—from the Market's blend of commerce and violence to something older, mustier, like centuries of accumulated decay.
Gwen was nowhere to be seen. She'd abandoned him the moment he'd hesitated.
Typical.
Kael activated his newly harvested Battle Meditation, feeling the skill settle over his mind like a cool cloth. The panic he'd been starting to feel receded, replaced by calm assessment. Three exits ahead. Water dripping from the left. Air moving from the right. He chose the right, moving as quietly as he could through ankle-deep water that stank of rot and worse.
The Ashfall Sewers. He'd heard of them—everyone in Thornwick had. The ancient drainage system that predated the current city by centuries, built by people who'd been dead long before the Abyssal Wars began. Legends said the tunnels connected to places that shouldn't exist anymore, including the sealed entrance to one of the original rifts.
Kael didn't believe the legends. Mostly.
He emerged from the sewer into a maintenance chamber, climbed a rusted ladder, and pushed open a grate to find himself in a narrow alley between two crumbling buildings. Fog curled around his ankles, thick and yellow-gray, carrying the familiar tang of the Fogmire District's perpetual miasma.
Night had fully fallen while he'd been underground. Stars glittered above the rooftops, but they seemed dimmer here, filtered through layers of coal smoke and spiritual contamination. Thornwick never truly slept—it just changed what it did in the darkness.
Kael climbed to a rooftop and surveyed his surroundings. Fogmire stretched out below him, a maze of crooked streets and leaning buildings that seemed to lean away from each other. Lights flickered in windows: the desperate brightness of gambling dens and the fevered glow of underground clinics, the soft red of venues that didn't advertise and the harsh white of establishments that didn't need to.
Somewhere down there, Zarek's cult was organizing. Somewhere down there, Lyra was probably drinking herself to sleep in whatever hole she'd crawled into. Somewhere down there, the answers to questions Kael didn't even know he should be asking were waiting to be harvested.
He took a moment to steady his breathing, to catalog what he'd learned and what he'd gained.
Chain Lightning. Battle Meditation. Two new abilities that would make him significantly more dangerous in a fight. His spiritual reserves remained stable—no cost for Soul Reaving meant he could keep doing this indefinitely, as long as corpses were available.
But there was something else. Something he'd noticed during the extraction from the Market assassin.
When he'd touched the man's forehead, he'd felt a spike of foreign emotion—terror so intense it had nearly overwhelmed his own senses. The assassin hadn't been afraid of Kael. He'd been afraid of something else, something that came before.
And in that flash of borrowed fear, Kael had glimpsed an image: a massive stone door, covered in carved symbols that seemed to writhe when he looked at them directly. Figures in dark robes, chanting in voices that sounded like grinding stone. And behind the door, something vast and patient, waiting for the seal to break.
Kael shook his head, trying to dispel the lingering echo. The vision had been fragmented, incomplete, but it confirmed what he'd suspected. Zarek's cult wasn't just gathering power—they were working toward something specific. Something that involved the old rifts.
The Six Orders had been fighting the Abyss for generations, maintaining the seal network that kept reality intact. If that network failed, if even one of the 72 Entities managed to break through...
He didn't want to think about it. He'd spent too long in the darkness already.
Movement below caught his attention. A figure emerged from a doorway across the street—tall, lean, moving with the careful precision of someone who knew they were being watched. Kael recognized the silhouette immediately.
Dorian Ashford. The man who'd helped him escape the Shadow Court's custody, who'd fed him information about Zarek's cult, who'd claimed to be an enemy of the organization that had destroyed his family.
Dorian glanced up at the rooftop, met Kael's eyes for a moment, and then deliberately looked away. He started walking toward the eastern edge of Fogmire, where the district gave way to the Ashfall Wastes.
It was a summons. Kael was certain of it.
He considered his options. Dorian had been reliable so far, but reliable and trustworthy weren't the same thing. The man had his own agenda, his own reasons for helping Kael. If those reasons ever stopped aligning with Kael's survival, the alliance would end.
But Dorian knew things. He'd known about the cult's activities, about Zarek's role, about the Shadow Court's interest in Kael. Whatever game he was playing, it involved information that Kael needed.
Kael dropped from the rooftop and followed.
They walked for nearly twenty minutes, passing through streets that grew progressively darker and more decayed. The buildings here were older, their stone foundations predating the current structures by centuries. Some of them had been abandoned so long that trees grew through their floors, pale roots reaching toward the dim starlight like fingers grasping for something lost.
Finally, Dorian stopped in front of a building that might once have been a temple. Its doors hung open, revealing darkness within. Symbols were carved into the stone frame—old symbols, the kind that predated the current Order iconography.
"You've been busy," Dorian said without turning around. "The Veiled Market is talking about a Reaver in their tunnels. They say you killed three of their men and took something valuable."
"I defended myself," Kael replied. "They attacked me."
"They always do." Dorian stepped through the doorway. "Come. There's someone who wants to meet you."
Kael hesitated. Every instinct screamed that this was a trap, that walking into dark buildings after strange summons was exactly how careless people died in Thornwick.
But his instincts also said Dorian wasn't lying about wanting to help him. The man's body language, his voice, the subtle tells he'd learned to read during his time in the prison system's interrogation chambers—all of it pointed toward genuine intention.
So either Dorian truly wanted to help him, or Dorian was the best liar Kael had ever encountered.
He followed.
The temple's interior was vast, a single chamber with a ceiling lost in shadows above. Moonlight filtered through gaps in the roof, creating pools of silver illumination that seemed to float in the darkness than rest on the floor. Dust motes drifted through the beams like slow-falling snow.
A figure stood at the far end, near what might once have been an altar. Kael couldn't make out details—the darkness was too thick—but he could feel the person's presence. Powerful. Ancient. Patient.
"Kael Ashborn," the figure said. The voice was feminine, cultured, with the faint accent of the old northern provinces. "You've been causing a stir. The Shadow Court has taken notice. So has the Order of Dawn. So has the cult that calls itself the Whisper King's faithful."
"I didn't ask for attention."
"None of us do." The figure moved, and moonlight fell across her face for a moment. She was older than he'd expected—middle-aged, with gray streaking her dark hair and lines carved deep around her eyes and mouth. But her eyes were sharp, calculating, the eyes of someone who had survived things that would have broken lesser people. "I am Magistra Yennefer Vane. Formerly of the Order of Dawn, currently independent."
Kael felt his stomach tighten. The Order of Dawn was one of the Six Orders—the one that focused on light-based abilities and the maintenance of the seal network. If this woman had really been part of them and left...
"You're a deserter," he said.
"A deserter implies I ran from something." Her smile was thin, humorless. "I left by choice. The Order's methods stopped aligning with my principles. It happens to the best of us."
Dorian stood to one side, watching the exchange with careful neutrality. He wasn't taking sides, Kael realized—he was facilitating. Bringing them together so they could decide for themselves whether to trust each other.
"Why am I here?" Kael asked.
Magistra Vane stepped closer. "Because you're a Soul Reaver, and Soul Reavers are the only people who can stop what's coming. The cult you've been hunting—they're not random fanatics. They're part of a larger organization, one that has been working toward a single goal for over two hundred years."
"The Whisper King."
Her eyes widened slightly. "You've heard that name."
"I know he exists. I don't know anything else."
Magistra Vane nodded slowly. "The Whisper King is the leader of a cult that believes the seal network should be destroyed. Not weakened, not negotiated with—destroyed entirely. They believe the Abyss is humanity's true inheritance, that the Entities waiting behind the Veil are our salvation than our doom."
"That's insane."
"Most forms of faith are, when taken to extremes." She gestured to the altar behind her. "But they're not acting on faith alone. They have a plan. A ritual that would shatter the primary seals simultaneously, creating a cascade failure that would bring all 72 Entities into the Material at once."
Kael thought of the vision from the assassin's memories. The stone door, the chanting figures, the vast presence waiting behind it.
"That's impossible," he said. "The seals were built to last forever."
"Nothing lasts forever, child. The seals are weakening—the Abyssal Wars weakened them more than the Orders admit, and time has done the rest. The Whisper King's cult has been working for generations to find the weak points, to identify which seals can be broken and which must be maintained to create the cascade they need."
Dorian spoke up. "Three weeks ago, one of the primary seals failed in the eastern provinces. The official story is that it was natural degradation. But I have contacts in the Order of Dawn—they say it was sabotage. Someone tested their ritual on a minor seal, and it worked."
Kael felt cold despite the temple's stagnant air. "How many seals do they need to break?"
"Seven," Magistra Vane said. "The Great Rift and six others, positioned in a geometric pattern that would allow the cascade to propagate across the entire network. Break those seven, and the rest follow automatically."
"That's insane. It would destroy the world."
"It would transform it. The cult believes humanity would be remade in the Abyss's image, elevated to a new form of existence." Her voice carried no judgment, only the flat recitation of facts. "Whether that belief is correct, I cannot say. No one can. But I do know that if they succeed, everything changes. Everyone dies or becomes something else. There is no middle ground."
Kael processed this, his mind racing through implications. If the cult had already broken one seal, they were further along than anyone realized. They had three weeks of lead time. Three weeks of working toward their goal while the Orders probably still thought everything was under control.
"Why tell me this?" he asked. "Why not go to the Orders yourself?"
Magistra Vane's expression hardened. "Because the Orders are compromised. I told you—I left the Order of Dawn because their methods stopped aligning with my principles. What I didn't tell you is that I discovered evidence of corruption at the highest levels. Someone inside the Order is working with the cult, feeding them information about seal weaknesses and security protocols."
Dorian's face was grim. "The Shadow Court suspects the same thing. That's part of why they've been watching you, Kael. They know about the Whisper King's cult, and they know the Orders can't be trusted. They want to recruit you."
"Recruit me for what?"
"To do what the Orders won't. To infiltrate the cult, to stop them from the inside, using abilities they can't refuse and methods they won't sanction."
Kael laughed, the sound harsh in the temple's emptiness. "I'm a death-row prisoner who broke out of execution. I'm wanted by every Order in existence. And you think the Shadow Court wants to recruit me?"
"The Shadow Court doesn't care about your past," Dorian said. "They care about your abilities. A Soul Reaver who can harvest skills from the dead is invaluable for intelligence gathering. And a Soul Reaver who can do it without spiritual cost..." He shrugged. "You're a weapon they've never seen before."
Magistra Vane stepped forward again, close enough that Kael could see the fine lines of exhaustion around her eyes. "I'm not here as a representative of the Shadow Court. I'm here as someone who has spent twenty years trying to stop the Whisper King's cult, and who has failed every single time. They're too well-organized, too entrenched, too patient. They have resources and connections that dwarf anything the resistance has managed to assemble."
"But you think I can succeed where you've failed."
"I think you might be the only one who can." She reached into her robe and withdrew a small object—a ring, silver, set with a stone that seemed to absorb light than reflect it. "This belonged to the first Soul Reaver. It was recovered from a tomb three centuries ago and has been passed from hand to hand ever since, always to someone who might be able to use it, always with the same result. Failure. Death. Disappearance."
"What's the ring do?"
"Nothing. That's the point. The Soul Reaver who wore this never achieved anything remarkable. Never stopped the cult. Never changed the world. But he was the first—the prototype, if you will. The original bearer of the ability that you now possess."
She held out the ring. "I'm giving it to you because the alternative is to keep passing it to people who will fail, and I'm tired of that cycle. Maybe you'll succeed. Maybe you'll fail. But at least you'll have a chance, and this will give you something tangible to anchor your purpose."
Kael stared at the ring. It looked ordinary—deceptively so—but when he reached out to take it, he felt something. A resonance, faint but unmistakable. Like recognizing a voice you'd never heard before.
"Soul Reaver Origin," he murmured. The mystery of where his power came from. This might be connected.
Or it might be a meaningless trinket. He couldn't tell yet.
He took the ring and slipped it onto his finger. It fit perfectly, as if it had been made for him specifically.
"There's something else," Magistra Vane said. "Something you'll need to know before you can even begin to fight them. The cult's leadership—they're not ordinary fanatics. Several of them are former Awakened, people who traded their loyalty to the Orders for power in the cult. And one of them, the Whisper King's right hand, is a former Soul Reaver."
Kael's blood went cold. "That's impossible. Soul Reavers are hunted by everyone. How could one join a cult?"
"The same way anyone joins a cult. Desperation. Ideology. The promise of something the legitimate powers couldn't or wouldn't provide." Her expression was unreadable. "I don't know the details. I only know that the Whisper King trusts this person completely, that they've been with the cult almost since its founding, and that their abilities make them nearly unstoppable in direct confrontation."
"Abilities?"
"All Soul Reavers are dangerous. But this one has had two centuries to accumulate power and skills. They've harvested from hundreds of corpses—maybe thousands. Their abilities are..." She paused, searching for the right word. "Extensive."
Kael absorbed this. A former Soul Reaver, working against everything he represented. Someone who had followed the same path he was walking and arrived at the opposite destination.
"Does this former Reaver have a name?" he asked.
"The cult calls them the Pale Surgeon. The few survivors of encounters with them have used that name, and nothing else. I don't know their true identity, if they even have one anymore."
The Pale Surgeon. Two centuries of harvesting. Skills accumulated over generations, extracted from the dead and made his own.
Kael felt a strange mixture of fear and anticipation. He was Ascendant stage—powerful by ordinary standards but still far from the peak of what Awakened could achieve. The Pale Surgeon, if the Magistra's information was accurate, was on an entirely different level.
And yet Kael had something the Pale Surgeon didn't. Something the Pale Surgeon couldn't have, because it was unique to this moment, this path.
He could harvest without limit. No spiritual cost meant no exhaustion, no depletion, no slow degradation of his soul's reserves. Given enough time and enough corpses, he could become more powerful than anyone who had ever carried the Soul Reaver ability.
The question was whether he would have enough time to stop the cult before they broke the seals and ended the world.
"What do you want me to do?" Kael asked.
Magistra Vane smiled for the first time, and the expression transformed her face from stern to almost grandmotherly. "Tonight, you rest. Tomorrow, you begin training properly. You have abilities, but you don't know how to use them to their full potential. Dorian will take you to a safe house in the southern districts. In the morning, I'll come to you with a plan."
"And if I refuse? If I walk away and pretend I never heard any of this?"
"Then the cult will continue unchecked, the Orders will remain compromised, and eventually the seals will break and everything will end. You might survive—Soul Reavers are notoriously hard to kill—but everyone else will be transformed or destroyed."
She turned and walked back toward the altar. "I'm not asking you to be a hero, Kael Ashborn. I'm asking you to be practical. Fighting the cult serves your own interests. They'll come for you eventually, once they realize what you are and what you can do. Better to strike first."
Dorian gestured toward the temple's entrance. "This way. The safe house is a twenty-minute walk."
Kael followed, his mind churning with questions and plans and fears. The ring on his finger felt heavier than it should have.
Outside, the fog had thickened, wrapping Thornwick in gray silence. Somewhere in the distance, a bell tolled the hour—three chimes, marking the middle of the night.
And in the darkness between the buildings, Kael could have sworn he saw eyes watching him. Pale eyes, reflecting the distant lamplight with an eerie luminescence.
The Pale Surgeon?
Or just another predator, drawn by the scent of power?
He didn't stop to find out. Dorian was already disappearing into the mist, and Kael followed, leaving the temple and its secrets behind.
Tomorrow would bring training, plans, and the beginning of a war that might determine the fate of everyone in Thornwick.
Tonight, he would rest.
But even as he walked, Kael knew that rest would be elusive. The eyes hadn't been his imagination. Something had seen him emerge from that temple, something that knew what he was.
The hunt had begun. He just didn't know yet who was hunting whom.
The fog swallowed them whole, and Thornwick's gaslit streets stretched ahead like the entrance to a grave.
Kael kept walking. He had no choice. The alternative was to stop, and stopping meant dying. In Thornwick, in the world beyond, motion was the only thing that kept you alive.
The ring gleamed on his finger, catching stray light from a passing lantern.
The first Soul Reaver had worn this ring.
That Soul Reaver had failed.
Kael intended to succeed.
But first, he needed to survive until morning. And based on the footsteps he could hear echoing in the fog behind them—footsteps that were getting closer—he wasn't the only one who had noticed the temple's meeting.
"Trouble," Dorian whispered, drawing a blade from beneath his coat.
Kael activated Battle Meditation, feeling the calm settle over his mind like a familiar shroud. His newly harvested Chain Lightning ability crackled at his fingertips, eager for release.
"I know," he said. "I can hear them too."
The fog parted ahead, revealing three figures in dark clothing, blocking the narrow street. They moved with the coordinated precision of trained fighters, spreading out to cut off retreat.
More footsteps behind. At least five more.
Surrounded.
Dorian's voice was steady, but Kael could hear the tension beneath it. "Cultists?"
"Doesn't matter." Kael stepped forward, putting himself between Dorian and the approaching threat. "They want me. You should run."
"I'm not leaving you."
"Then stay and die. Your choice." Kael raised his hands, lightning dancing between his fingers like serpents of pure energy. "But I didn't survive the gallows to die in a fogbank. So if you want to help, stay out of my way."
The three figures ahead stopped, holding position. From the fog behind, a voice called out—calm, cultured, familiar.
"Kael Ashborn. The Whisper King requests your presence. I suggest you come willingly."
The Pale Surgeon.
It had to be.
Kael smiled, feeling the Chain Lightning surge through his veins, ready to be released. His first real confrontation with the cult's leadership. The first real test of his abilities against someone who knew what he was and what he could do.
"The answer is no," he called back. "Tell your master I'll be seeing him soon. On my terms."
Lightning erupted from his palms, arcing through the fog toward the three figures ahead.
The night exploded into light.
