The door to Lyra's safehouse shattered inward. Kael was moving before the splinters finished falling.
He rolled left, shoulder slamming into the wall, and a bolt of spiritual energy screamed past his ear. The heat of it seared the side of his neck. Three figures poured through the breach—black cloaks, no insignia, moving with coordinated precision. Their footsteps overlapped. Trained killers.
"Behind you!" Lyra's voice cut through the chaos. She materialized from the shadows of the upper landing, twin daggers drawn, her Awakened presence flaring.
Kael didn't waste breath on questions. The lead attacker lunged, a blade coated in something that reeked of corruption—Entity-touched steel, designed to eat through spiritual defenses. The smell hit the back of his throat, copper and rot. Kael caught the wrist, twisted, and felt bone give way under his grip. The man screamed. Kael headbutted him into the second attacker, buying two seconds.
Two seconds was enough.
Lyra dropped from the landing, her daggers finding throats and hamstrings. One cultist managed to raise a crossbow; she kicked it aside and opened his chest from hip to rib. Blood hit the floorboards in a thick spray. The third tried to run. Kael grabbed his cloak and hauled him back, slamming him face-first into the floor. Bone cracked. The man wheezed, choking on teeth and blood.
"Talk." Kael's hand found the man's collar, hauling him up. "Who sent you?"
The cultist grinned, blood streaming over his chin. "The Court knows what you are, Reaver. The Court knows everything."
Lyra went rigid.
Kael caught it. The slight widening of her eyes, the way her weight shifted to her back foot, the fractional pause before her breathing steadied. He filed each detail away the way a butcher cataloged cuts—by instinct, without sentiment.
"Lyra."
"We need to move." She was already stripping weapons from the bodies. "More will come. They're never alone."
"Lyra." He didn't raise his voice. "What did he mean, 'the Court'?"
She wouldn't meet his eyes.
That was answer enough.
Kael looked down at the cultist. The man was still grinning, blood bubbling between his lips. His other hand was moving—slow, deliberate—toward something tucked in his belt. A vial. Dark liquid, faintly luminescent. The glass was warm when Kael's fingers closed around it. The substance inside thrummed against his palm.
Entity essence. Refined.
The cultist's grin faltered. "You don't know what—"
Kael drove his thumb into the man's shattered nose. The cultist convulsed, then went limp. Not dead. Not yet. But the vial was what mattered.
He held it up. The liquid inside shifted, pressing against the glass like something alive. "They came prepared to take me alive. This would have suppressed spiritual pressure. Paralyzed the channels." He turned to Lyra. "Who makes this?"
"The Shadow Court." She said it like she was pulling a tooth. "They've been monitoring you since you woke up from your execution. Since you first used the Reaver."
The words landed like stones in Kael's gut. He turned the vial in his fingers, watching the essence crawl along the inner glass. "You work for them."
"I worked for them. Years ago. Before I—"
"Before you what?"
She turned to face him. Her jaw was clenched so tight the tendons in her neck stood out like cables. Her eyes were dry, but the muscles around them twitched—a struggle beneath the surface, something being held down by force. "Before I found out what they really are. Before I realized that everything I believed was a lie, and everyone I trusted was a puppet, and the whole world is burning down around us."
Her voice cracked on the last word. She pressed a hand to her mouth. Held it there. Her fingers trembled against her lips.
Kael waited.
"They want you," she said, the words muffled behind her palm. She dropped her hand. "The Shadow Court, the cultists, the Abyssal fanatics. They want what you can do. The ability to harvest from the dead. Every faction in this city wants to own you or kill you. And I've been trying—" Her throat worked. "I've been trying to keep you alive long enough to figure out which one is worse."
He let her talk. Sometimes people needed to empty themselves before truth could fit inside.
She told him about her recruitment. About the Shadow Court's purpose—not conspiracy or espionage, but research. Studying the Abyss. Cataloging the Entities. Preparing for a future the Orders refused to acknowledge.
"The seals are failing," she said. "The Court's scholars calculated it decades ago. Centuries, maybe, before the membrane between realms collapses completely. And the Orders? They're fighting over scraps while the world ends. The Sun God's Chosen build temples. The Iron Vanguard forge weapons. The Arcane Spire debates theory. No one's doing anything."
"So they're unsealing the rifts themselves." Kael watched the vial's luminescence pulse in his palm. "They're not fighting the Entities. They're summoning them."
"They're preparing for them." Lyra shook her head. "The Court believes humanity can't win a conventional war against the Abyss. The Entities are too powerful. But some of them might be negotiated with. Allied with. The Court's been making contact with the weaker Entities for decades. Buying time. Finding allies."
"And where do I fit?"
Lyra hesitated. The pause was too long. "Soul Reavers can harvest from anything. Living, dead, Entity-touched. The Court wants that ability. They want to create Reavers who can extract power from the Abyss itself. Weaponize the Entities."
"That's insane."
"That's survival." Her voice hardened. "You think the Orders will save everyone? The Concordat is a joke. If we wait for the Orders to act, we'll be serving as sacrifices by the time they finish debating procedure."
Kael tucked the vial into his coat. The glass pressed against his ribs, a cold reminder. "You've been reporting my movements to them."
"I stopped. When I realized—" She stopped. Started again. "When I realized what they wanted to do with you."
"And what changed your mind?"
She stepped closer. Her pupils were dilated, the green of her irises swallowed almost completely. "You're not what they said you were. You're not a tool. You're a person who's been given a monstrous gift and is trying every day not to become a monster. That's why I stopped reporting."
Kael looked at her face—gaunt, rain-streaked where it had come through the window, older than her years. Her pulse beat visibly in her throat. She believed what she was saying.
That didn't mean she was right.
"You're coming with me," he said.
Lyra blinked. "What?"
"If the Shadow Court's Hound is coming, you're the only one who knows how she operates. I need that."
"I'm a liability. They'll target me the moment they see us together."
"They were already targeting you. The cultists knew you were protecting me. Which means they know you've turned." Kael pulled the paper she'd given him from his coat. "Whatever you did for the Court, you've been running, hiding, watching your back. That ends now. I'm not offering forgiveness. I'm offering a chance to do something useful."
Lyra stared at him. Rain dripped between them. Her lips pressed together, thinned, then relaxed. "The Hound's name is Vex. She's Ascendant-class, stage three. Can manipulate shadow itself, create perfect duplicates, slip through walls like water. She's killed seventeen Reavers in the last decade. Doesn't need to touch them. Just needs to get close enough to plant her mark, and then you're hers."
"Can she be killed?"
"Yes. But not easily. She carries a wardstone that regenerates her body. You'd need something that bypasses that regeneration. Something that strikes the soul directly."
Soul Reaver abilities.
"The Hound is coming," Lyra repeated. "We need to move. Now."
They took the maintenance tunnels toward the Mortician's Guild district. Lyra led, her footsteps silent on the metal grating. Kael followed close behind, watching the shadows.
The tunnels stank of canal water and old death. Kael could feel spiritual residues clinging to the walls—fragments of souls released by the Mortician's harvesting operations. Standard practice. Legal. The Guild had contracts with families, extracted essence from the recently deceased, refined it into the vials that fueled Thornwick's Awakened economy.
His fingers brushed the vial in his coat. The essence inside pulsed against his touch. Warm. Hungry.
He pushed the sensation aside.
The tunnel narrowed. Water dripped from overhead pipes, each drop echoing in the confined space. The sound was rhythmic, almost mechanical. His boots splashed through puddles that tasted of iron when the spray hit his lips.
He stopped.
"Lyra."
She halted. Turned.
Kael tilted his head. Listened.
The dripping had changed. Not faster, not slower. The intervals had shifted. Something was altering the acoustic pattern of the tunnel. Something ahead.
"What do you—"
He raised his hand. She went quiet.
There. A pulse in the spiritual fabric of the world. Rhythmic. Hungry. Coming from ahead, from the junction where four tunnels met beneath a rusted grate.
They crept forward, emerging into the junction. Dim light filtered through cracks in the street above. Rain dripped down in thin streams, catching what little illumination existed and fracturing it into dirty ribbons.
And in the center of the junction, three bodies lay in a circle.
Cultists. Their black cloaks were soaked with rain and blood. Their faces were frozen in expressions of absolute terror—mouths stretched wide, eyes bulging, tendons standing out in their necks like ropes about to snap. And around each body, carved into the stone floor, was a symbol that made the hair on Kael's arms stand upright.
"Soul anchors," Lyra breathed. "They're using them to siphon energy from the dead."
Kael approached the nearest corpse. The body was fresh—within hours. He could see the harvest markers glowing faintly around the wounds. Someone had already extracted what they could.
He knelt. The stone was cold through his trousers. He could feel the carved symbol beneath his knee, humming with residual power. It vibrated against his kneecap, a frequency that crawled up his femur and settled in his hip.
He touched the body's chest.
The golden threads of the Soul Reaver system stirred. Familiar. Inevitable. The connection formed before he could think to resist it—before the wrongness of it registered.
Power flooded into him. A skill fragment, degraded and corrupted, jammed into his consciousness like a splinter driven under a nail. His vision blurred. His stomach clenched, and bile surged up his throat. He swallowed it down, tasting acid and something else—something that tasted of shadow and old grief.
Images followed. A woman in a dark coat, her face obscured. A voice giving orders. The word "Hound" repeated. A location—the Ashworth Foundry, abandoned since the last Abyssal incursion. A promise: The Reaver will be delivered. The First's tomb will open.
And a sensation. Not an image. A feeling of being watched from the inside, as though something had crawled through the harvest and was now sitting behind his eyes, looking out.
Kael wrenched his hand back. His fingers were shaking. The skin where he'd touched the corpse was cold—not the cold of a dead body, but the cold of something that had reached through the body to get to him.
He looked down at his palm. The lines of his hand had darkened, as though ink had been drawn along them. The discoloration was already fading, but the sensation of being touched from the inside lingered.
"That was Vex," Lyra said. She hadn't moved. "The Hound did this."
"She's ahead of us."
"She knows we're coming. She left this for us to find." Lyra's face had gone the color of old candle wax. "A message. A threat."
Kael stared at the symbol on the floor. He knelt again, this time without touching the body. The carving was precise, almost elegant. Designed to channel spiritual energy into a central point. A modified soul anchor. Not for storage—for transmission.
"She's figured out how to harvest at range," he said. "Using the anchors as intermediaries. The symbols are the interface. Touch one, and you become part of the network."
Lyra's hand went to her dagger. "Then the tunnels—"
"Every symbol in Thornwick is a potential harvest point. Every dead body connected to the network is a conduit." Kael stood. His knees ached from the cold stone. "She's been building an infrastructure. Testing it on cultists who won't be missed."
"That would give her unlimited power."
"She's not just here for me. She's here to complete the network. I'm the excuse." Kael's eyes swept the tunnel junction, counting the symbols. Eight in total. Eight harvest points feeding into a larger pattern he couldn't see. "The Ashworth Foundry. That's where she's completing it."
"That's suicide."
"It's the only move that makes sense. She's expecting us to run. The moment we go toward the Foundry, her plan breaks down. She'll have to adapt."
Lyra stared at him. Then she laughed—a short, harsh sound that echoed off the tunnel walls. "You know the Court was right about one thing. You are exactly what they feared. Someone who thinks like a Reaver."
They moved.
The Ashworth Foundry loomed at the edge of the Ironwork District, a cathedral of rust and dead machinery. Once, it had forged the weapons that won the Abyssal Wars. Now it was a tomb for steam engines. The seals here were thin. The air tasted of ozone and copper. Kael's tongue prickled with it, a faint electrical charge that made his fillings ache.
Spiritual pressure built against his senses. Something massive was stirring in the depths.
They moved through the factory floor, stepping over debris and dead machinery. Shadows pooled in corners, deeper than they should have been. The darkness had texture—thick, viscous, clinging to his ankles as he walked through it. Lyra's daggers were drawn. Kael kept his hands loose.
A voice echoed from the darkness ahead.
"Kael Ashborn. The corpse who refused to rot."
The shadows coalesced. A woman stepped forward—tall, gaunt, her skin pale as bone. Her eyes were pits of absolute darkness, and her smile was the cruelest thing Kael had ever seen. Her presence hit him like a wall of cold water. Stage three Ascendant. The pressure bore down on his chest, compressing his lungs. His breath came short.
"The Shadow Court sends its regards," she said. "And its invitation."
Kael's jaw tightened. The spiritual pressure was real. His channels strained against it, his body working harder than it should to maintain coherence under the weight of her presence. The corrupted skill from the cultist's corpse was still lodged in his consciousness, a splinter of shadow that pulsed with each heartbeat. He could feel it sitting there, alien and wrong, pressing against the edges of his self.
He tried to reach for it. The golden threads of the Reaver stirred—
Nothing happened. The skill sat there, inert. Locked behind something he couldn't name. He pushed harder. His temples throbbed. A vein pulsed in his forehead. The skill remained stubbornly still, a door that wouldn't open no matter how hard he shoved.
He was too distracted. Too scattered. The harvest had jammed something into him that he couldn't use, and Vex was standing fifteen feet away with the power to kill him before he took another step.
Vex raised her hand. A sphere of darkness formed, and within it, an image crystallized. A tomb. Ancient. Covered in symbols that matched the soul anchors in the tunnels. And at the center of the tomb, a figure on a stone bier—the preserved body of a man with hollow eyes.
"The First Reaver," Vex said. "Your predecessor. Your source. The Shadow Court has spent two centuries looking for him. And we finally found him."
Kael's pulse hammered in his ears. The image in the sphere was clear—too clear. He could see the carving on the tomb walls, the same symbols as the soul anchors, arranged in a pattern that spiraled inward toward the body. The First Reaver's corpse lay with its hands folded across its chest, and even in death, Kael could feel the residual power emanating from it through the image. A pressure against his consciousness. A recognition.
Something deep inside him responded. The Reaver stirred—not the golden threads, but something older. Something that lived below the system, below the skills, below the harvest. It recognized the tomb the way a body recognized a wound.
He shut it down. Forced his breathing to stay even. His hands were clammy inside his gloves.
Vex's smile widened. "We've been waiting for you, Kael. Waiting for the key that opens the door. And now that you're here, we can finally finish what we started."
She gestured. The shadows behind her pulsed.
"Come with me willingly, and your friend lives. Resist, and we take you both."
Kael's gaze swept the foundry. Shadows shifted on the catwalks above—two figures, patient, positioned for crossfire. Behind them, three more spiritual signatures pressed against his awareness. The trap was complete. Six attackers plus Vex. The exits covered. The darkness itself was hers, threaded through with her presence, an extension of her will.
The corrupted skill pulsed in his skull. Still locked. Still unreachable. He shoved against it again, and pain lanced through his temples—sharp, bright, like a needle driven through his left eye. His vision swam. He tasted copper.
Vex stepped closer. Her shadow pooled around her feet, spreading across the floor in dark tendrils that reached toward Kael's boots. Where they touched the metal grating, the metal corroded, flaking into rust.
"You're fighting it," she said. Her voice was almost gentle. "The skill you harvested. It's corrupted. Your system can't integrate it because you're resisting what it is. What you are."
Kael said nothing. His hands were fists at his sides. The tendons in his forearms burned from the tension.
"Stop fighting," Vex whispered. "Let it in. Let it show you what the First Reaver knew."
He looked at Lyra. She stood rigid, daggers drawn, her weight on the balls of her feet. Ready to die. Her eyes were wide, and sweat beaded at her temples despite the cold.
The corrupted skill pulsed again. This time, instead of pushing against it, Kael stopped pushing.
He let it in.
The pain was immediate and total. It started behind his eyes and radiated outward through his skull, down his spine, into his extremities. His vision went white, then black, then resolved into something else entirely. The foundry dissolved. He was standing in a space between spaces, a darkness so complete it had weight. And in the darkness, the golden threads of the Reaver system were visible for the first time—not threads at all, but chains. Links of light and shadow, connecting him to something vast and old and hungry.
The corrupted skill slotted into place like a key turning in a lock. The chains shifted. Power flooded through the connection—dark, twisted, and utterly wrong. It tasted of ash and old blood. It felt like wearing a coat made of dead skin.
But it was functional.

Vex's eyes widened.
Kael reached through the connection. Not into Vex, but into the network she'd built. The web of soul anchors across the city. The pathways of stolen energy converging on this point. He could feel them now—every anchor, every conduit, every dead thing connected to the system. They hummed in his awareness like plucked strings.

Vex screamed. The shadows around her convulsed, tearing apart as the energy that sustained them was ripped away. She staggered, her perfect composure shattering. Her duplicates—the ones Kael hadn't even seen—flickered and died across the foundry floor, dissolving into wisps of darkness.

Her skin was cold. Ice cold. The contact sent a jolt through his palm, up his arm, into his shoulder. Her wardstone pulsed beneath her coat—regeneration trying to kick in, trying to repair the damage the network collapse had caused.
Kael squeezed. The bones in her wrist ground together. She gasped.
"The wardstone," he said. "Take it off."
"You don't know what you're—"
He twisted. Something in her wrist snapped. She screamed, and her other hand went to the wardstone at her throat, clutching it. Kael grabbed her by the hair and slammed her into the floor. The wardstone cracked against the metal grating. He stamped on her wrist until her fingers released it, then kicked the stone away into the darkness.
She lay still, breathing in ragged gasps. Her shadow was gone. Her duplicates were gone. The network was gone. She was a woman on a cold floor, bleeding and broken.
Kael crouched beside her. His hands were steady now. The power had settled into something manageable—a dark current running beneath his skin, polluting every channel it touched. His vision was clear. His breathing was even. The corrupted skill sat in his consciousness like a tumor, and he could feel it changing him, cell by cell, thought by thought.
He didn't care.
"You were going to cage me," he said. "Use me. Harvest me when I'd served my purpose."
Vex's eyes met his. The absolute darkness in them was fading, shrinking, revealing something human beneath. Fear. Her pupils were dilated so wide her irises had vanished. Sweat plastered her hair to her skull. Her lips were blue.

Kael's hand closed around her throat. Not squeezing. Not yet. Just holding. Feeling the pulse hammer against his palm. Feeling the life in her, the spiritual energy that animated her, the skills and memories and experiences that made her Vex.

"Kael." Lyra's voice, behind him. Quiet.
He didn't turn. His eyes were on Vex's face. On the terror there. On the recognition that she was about to die, that nothing she had done or built or become was going to save her from this moment.
His thumb found the hollow of her throat. The pulse beneath it was frantic. A bird's heartbeat. A rabbit's.
He pressed.
Vex's body convulsed. Her eyes went wide, then glassy. Her mouth opened, but no sound came out—just a thin wheeze, air escaping a punctured lung. The spiritual energy in her flickered, guttered, and began to leak.
Kael harvested.
It was different from harvesting the dead. She was still alive—barely—and her power fought him. Her shadow manipulation was a living thing, writhing and snapping as he pulled it free from her channels. It tasted of darkness and cold water and something that burned the back of his throat. His left hand clenched involuntarily. His jaw locked.
He held on.
The last of Vex's spiritual energy drained away. Her body went limp. Her eyes stared at the ceiling, empty. The absolute darkness in them faded to ordinary brown.

"Kael." Lyra's voice again. Closer now. "Your eyes."

He pocketed the stone and turned toward the exit. His body ached. Every muscle felt like it had been wrung out and hung up to dry. The corrupted skill sat in his consciousness like a splinter of shadow, and Vex's stolen abilities circled it nervously, predator and prey forced into the same cage.

Kael's coat clung to his shoulders. The weight of the water and the stolen power pressed down on him simultaneously.

Lyra nearly collided with him. "What—"
He raised his hand.
Across the street, carved into the stone wall of a shuttered countinghouse, a symbol glowed faintly in the rain. A soul anchor. Freshly carved. The edges were crisp, unweathered. Hours old at most.
This was not Vex's work. The lines were wrong—thinner, more angular, the geometry subtly different. Vex's anchors had been functional, designed for transmission. This one was something else. The patterns spiraled inward in a way that made his eyes refuse to track them, each line slipping sideways the moment he tried to follow it.
Kael's pulse slowed. His breathing went shallow. The hair on his arms stood upright, and a cold sensation crawled up the base of his skull—the feeling of being observed by something that didn't have eyes.

"Kael." Lyra's voice was tight. She'd seen it too. "That's not the Court's work."

The rain fell harder. Somewhere in the distance, a factory whistle screamed the changing of a shift.
Kael memorized the symbol. Every angle. Every line. Every place where the geometry refused to resolve into something his eyes could hold.
Then he turned and kept walking.
Lyra fell into step beside him. She didn't speak. Her daggers were still drawn. Her knuckles were white around the grips.
Behind them, in the foundry, Vex's body lay cooling on the metal grating. Her communication stone was dark. But in the shadows beneath the catwalks, in the spaces between the dead machinery, something moved. Not a person. Not a shadow. A presence that had no shape and made no sound.
It settled over Vex's corpse like a shroud.
