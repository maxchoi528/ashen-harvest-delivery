The gaslight sputtered twice before the darkness swallowed it whole. Kael's palm scraped against damp brickwork, the cold seeping through the leather of his gloves. The reek of coal smoke and rotting fish flooded his sinuses. He narrowed his eyes, waiting for the black to shift to gray. 
Footsteps echoed from the alley mouth. Two sets, moving in lockstep. Their boot heels struck the cobblestones with military precision.
"You are the one they call the Reaver." A woman's voice, clipped and devoid of inflection. "The Orders have been asking about you."
Kael shifted his weight to the balls of his feet. His Sovereign-stage senses traced their spiritual pressure through the dark. One blazed with the dense, heavy heat of an Ascendant peak cultivator. The other pulsed weaker, a flickering Transcendent flame. Trained killers. 
"I'm a man who asks questions," Kael replied, letting his hand drift toward the hilt of Shadowveil.
"Questions about the Sepulcher tablets. About the seals." The second voice belonged to a youth trying to pitch his tone lower than nature intended. "The Whisper King knows."
The name hit Kael like a physical blow. A muscle jumped in his jaw. He lunged before the youth could finish the breath. 
Shadowveil cleared its sheath with a whisper of steel. The Ascendant woman reached for her weapon, but Kael was already inside her guard. The blade drew a silver arc through the pitch-black alley. Hot spray splashed across Kael's cheek and the brickwork behind her. The wet slap of her body hitting the mud drowned out the youth's gasp.
Kael's free hand closed around the young cultivator's collar, yanking him forward. He slammed the youth against the wall. 
"The Whisper King," Kael said, his voice stripped of everything except a cold, surgical curiosity. He pressed the flat of Shadowveil against the youth's neck. "Talk."
The cultivator's pulse hammered against Kael's knuckles. The youth's eyes darted to the cooling corpse of his superior. "He knows about the seals. All of them. He's been planning this for centuries."
A cold itch prickled at the back of Kael's skull. The system that lived in his blood acknowledged the kill. A subtle shift in his spiritual sea, a sensation of absorption, foreign memories bleeding into his own. He dismissed the notification before it could fully form. He didn't have time to admire the harvest. 
"Who told you this?" Kael pressed the blade harder, drawing a bead of blood. 
"He told me! He tells his people things to keep us loyal!" The youth's voice cracked. "He said the Reaver would come, and when he did, I was supposed to—"
A crossbow bolt punched through the youth's chest with a dull, wet thwack. The body jerked, a ragged exhalation rattling past broken ribs, and collapsed at Kael's feet. 
Kael didn't flinch. He lowered his blade and looked up. Three stories above, a silhouette detached itself from the chimney shadows.
"You should have let him finish, Kael Ashborn." The voice drifted down, muffled by a heavy canvas mask. 
Kael didn't wait for a second bolt. He sprinted for the drainpipe, his boots finding the iron brackets nailed into the brick. He scaled the wall in rapid, vicious bursts of strength, his muscles burning with the familiar heat of exertion. He crested the rooftop ledge, rolled onto the slick tiles, and broke into a dead run. 
The rooftops of Thornwick were treacherous in the fog. Kael followed the metallic tang of iron and pine oil, the archer's scent cutting through the city's smog. His Sovereign-stage body sliced through the wind resistance, his boots striking the rain-slicked slates with practiced precision. 
The archer fled eastward, toward the harbor district. Open ground meant Kael could close the distance, but the archer knew the city's skeleton. A path through collapsed tenements and narrow service alleys forced Kael to slow. He vaulted over a broken chimney stack, his leather soles skidding on loose mortar. 
The pursuit ended at the old harbor. Warehouses loomed like sleeping giants. The archer scaled a rusted loading crane, seeking altitude. Kael reached the crane's apex three heartbeats later. 
The hooded figure held a second bolt aimed at Kael's throat. The crossbow's mechanism gleamed with runic etching. Silver-threaded. Order-forged.
"Order of the Shroud?" Kael asked, catching his breath. 
"Shadow Court." The voice was genderless, muffled by the mask. "I serve the Whisper King."
"Everyone keeps saying that." Kael stepped out onto the narrow iron beam. "Who is he?"
"You are not ready to know."
"Try me."
The crossbow didn't waver. "The Whisper King was the first. Before the Orders. Before the seals. He walked the Abyss when it still touched the Material. He made the first pact with the Entities, and when humanity couldn't pay the price, he carved out the rifts and sealed them with his own hands."
"Mythology." 
"Truth." The archer's head tilted. "He created Soul Reaving. You think you are special? You think you are the only one?"
Kael's stomach dropped. His grip on the iron beam tightened until his knuckles ached. "Explain."
"The first Soul Reaver served him. Harvested the power to seal the Abyss. When the work was done, the Whisper King discarded him. Left him to rot in a tomb no one was supposed to find." A dry, rattling laugh echoed from behind the mask. "Until you."
The wind gusted, carrying the stench of brine and rotting fish. Below them, rats scurried through puddles of stagnant water. Kael kept his breathing even, forcing his heart rate to slow. 
"The Sepulcher tablets," Kael said. "They are connected to him."
"The True Name. Yes." The crossbow lowered an inch. "The seals are failing. You have felt it. The Abyss pressing against the membrane. The Entities stirring. The rifts won't hold."
"What does he want?"
"To replace the old seals with something better. Something that requires a Soul Reaver's touch." The archer's fingers whitened on the crossbow's grip. "He needs you, Kael Ashborn. But he doesn't want you to know it yet. That is why he sent me to kill you."
Kael struck. His hand caught the crossbow's frame and wrenched it aside. The bolt fired wild, spinning into the fog below. They grappled on the narrow platform. A hidden blade scraped against Kael's ribs, tearing leather and drawing a hot line of blood. 
The archer was fast. Transcendent-stage fast, with combat training that spoke of years of violence. But Kael had spent years fighting things that didn't follow human conventions. He trapped the blade arm, wrenched it backward until the joint popped, and drove his elbow into the archer's sternum. 
The archer crumpled. Kael caught the falling body and slammed it against the crane's support beam. 
"The Whisper King," Kael said, close enough to smell the fear-sweat beneath the canvas hood. "Where do I find him?"
"You can't." Blood flecked the archer's lips, bubbling with each exhale. "He finds you. He always—"
Kael drove his fist into the archer's ruined ribs. The scream died in a wet choke. "Wrong answer."
"The cult meeting," the archer gasped, body convulsing. "Three days from now. The warehouse on Blackthorn Dock. They are receiving a shipment from the Mortician's Guild. Soul-essence. Prime Zarek will be there."
Zarek. The name landed like a stone in still water. Kael's vision tunneled. The air in his lungs turned to ice. Zarek, the man who had framed him for murders he didn't commit, who had pushed for his execution, who stood between Kael and every quiet moment he tried to carve out for himself. 
"You are lying," Kael said, his voice a low scrape.
"Why? I am dying anyway." The archer's head lolled. "You broke something inside. Internal bleeding. I can feel it filling my lungs."
Kael loosened his grip on the hood, letting the canvas fall back. He didn't ask for trust. He didn't care about the archer's motives. He only needed the truth. "Zarek is involved in this."
"Zarek is expendable. A useful tool. Kill him if you want. It won't change anything."
"Why would the Whisper King let me kill his own agent?"
"Because Zarek doesn't know who he serves. He thinks the Shadow Court is his own organization. He thinks he is playing everyone." The archer's eyes, bloodshot and dimming, locked onto Kael's. "You are both pieces on the same board, Ashborn. The Whisper King moves you where he wants."
The crane groaned. Rust flaked beneath Kael's palm. The archer's breathing grew shallow, a death rattle vibrating in the chest. 
"The soul-essence shipment," Kael pressed. "What are they planning to do with it?"
"Accelerate the seal degradation. Open a path for the Whisper King. The cult has been doing it for years. Small punctures. The Orders think it is random. It isn't." A convulsion shook the archer's frame. "They are almost ready. The Great Rift opens, and then—"
The archer died. Kael lowered the body to the crane's platform. He stood, wiping the blood from his knife on the archer's cloak. The cold itch in his skull returned. He closed his eyes, letting the foreign memories bleed into his mind. Combat reflexes, spatial awareness, the muscle memory of a trained killer. He took what he needed and discarded the rest. 
He dropped from the crane, the impact jarring his knees. He needed to move. He needed a place to think.
The Ashveil District was a graveyard of crumbling tenements. Kael picked his way through the ruins of a building the Shadow Court had abandoned. The walls bore ritual markings, protective sigils scraped into the plaster. He found the locked chest beneath a false floor. The lock yielded to his blade. 
Inside: correspondence. Coded, but decipherable with patience. Names. Dates. Locations of cult cells across Thornwick. References to the Convergence Protocol and instructions to prepare assets for the Final Descent. 
Kael spread the documents across a rotting table. The Shadow Court wasn't a secret society. It was an instrument. And Zarek's signature appeared on dozens of authorizations. Soul-essence shipments. Payments to cult operatives. 
A knock at the ruined doorway. Kael drew Shadowveil and pivoted on his heel. Sera stepped into the gray morning light. Dark circles bruised the skin beneath her eyes. Her clothes were rumpled, her hair pulled back in a hasty knot.
"You are up early," she said, her gaze scanning the scattered documents.
"You are out late."
"Couldn't sleep. Kept thinking about the files we found." Sera moved into the room, stepping over broken glass. "What is all this?"
"Cult correspondence. Cell locations. The Convergence Protocol." Kael shoved a folder across the table. "The breaches are deliberate sabotage of the seal network."
Sera rifled through the pages. Her jaw tightened. Her fingers paused on a leaf of parchment. She traced a line of text, her breath hitching. "Kael. There is a reference to my sister's facility. The Mortician's Guild location where they are holding her."
Kael looked at the document. The cold weight in his gut solidified. He said nothing. He didn't offer empty comforts. He pointed to the relevant line. "They are extracting soul-essence from the patients. Testing Awakening rituals."
"She is a resource." Sera's voice went flat, her knuckles whitening on the edge of the folder. "They want a supply of soul-essence ready when the seals break."
"That is ambition," Kael said. "The Whisper King doesn't want to destroy the world. He wants to control it. When the seals fail, he will be the only one who can offer protection."
Sera stared at him. "How do we stop him?"
Kael didn't answer. He looked down at his hands. The power coiled in his spirit, the same power the archer claimed the Whisper King had designed. He thought of the first Soul Reaver, discarded in a tomb. He thought of the True Name. 
He wanted to unmake Zarek. He wanted to tear the Shadow Court apart. The thought of saving the world didn't enter his mind. He wanted to survive, and he wanted those who had wronged him to bleed. 
"The Sepulcher tablets contain the True Name of Soul Reaving," Kael said. "What if the True Name is control? The Whisper King wants me compliant. Useful. He wants me to replace his seals."
He stopped talking. His eyes narrowed, tracing the edges of the room. 
Footsteps outside. Multiple sets. Moving with purpose. 
Kael motioned to Sera. She drew her knife. Kael moved to the window, peering through a gap in the boards. Four figures approached from the street. Two wore crimson robes. One wore gray bureaucratic dress. The last wore nothing but skin and ritual scars. Their spiritual pressure pressed against the safehouse walls.
"Company," Kael said.
"Yours or mine?" Sera asked.
"Order of Dawn." 
The lead figure, a woman with silver threading her dark hair, stopped at the broken doorway. "Kael Ashborn. The Order of Dawn has questions about your activities in the harbor district last night. Step out peacefully."
Kael pushed the door open with his boot. "I was defending myself."
"You were hunting." The commander's hand rested on her sword hilt. "The Shadow Court has been accelerating their timeline. Your involvement spooked them."
"Or forced their hand."
"Possibly." The commander's eyes took Kael's measure. "The Council of Six is convening an emergency session. They want answers about the seal degradation. About Prime Zarek's involvement."
"You know about Zarek?"
"We suspected. We lacked proof." The bureaucrat stepped forward, holding a leather folio. "Your evidence changes things. But it also makes you valuable. And dangerous."
Kael's grip tightened on Shadowveil. "I am not a specimen."
"No. You are a variable." The commander smiled, a thin stretching of lips. "The Whisper King has been moving pieces across the board for two centuries. We didn't even know there was a board. Now we do."
"I am not interested in being anyone's pawn."
"Good. Neither are we." The commander extended her hand. "The Order of Dawn has monitored the Abyss since the first seal was crafted. Work with us, and we might survive what is coming."
"Why should I trust the Order that tried to execute me?"
"You shouldn't." The commander lowered her hand. "But we want the seals to hold. On that point, your interests and ours align."
Kael considered the offer. He didn't trust them. He didn't want to save the city. But Zarek was at Blackthorn Dock, and the Order of Dawn had swords. 
"Three days," Kael said. "There is a cult meeting at Blackthorn Dock. Zarek will be there."
The commander's eyebrows rose. "You are inviting us?"
"I am telling you where to point your weapons." Kael stepped back from the door. "If the Order of Dawn wants to fight something larger than politics, this is your chance."
"The Shadow Court has no Concordat protections," the commander confirmed. "We will be there."
"Then get out of my safehouse." Kael's voice carried a low growl. "And tell whoever is watching from the rooftops that I know they are there."
The commander paused. "You can sense them?"
"I can sense everything in this district." 
The Order of Dawn withdrew. Their footsteps faded into the city's morning traffic. Kael moved to the table and pulled the first Sepulcher tablet from his coat. 
The stone burned against his palms. The carved symbols resonated with the power coiled in his spirit. He traced the ancient text, letting the foreign concepts bleed into his understanding. 
Soul is neither created nor destroyed. It transforms. It transfers. 
The tablet spoke of the True Name. To speak the True Name was to understand the origin of the power. The origin existed beyond instruction. It must be witnessed. 
Kael closed his eyes. He pressed his thumbs against the primary sigil. 
The room dissolved. He stood in a vast, cold darkness. The membrane between the Material and the Abyss pressed against his awareness. Figures moved in the dark. Dozens of them. Hundreds. All wearing his face, variations of age and decay. Soul Reavers. Every one who had ever existed, gathered at the origin point. 
At the center stood a man half in the Material, half in the Abyss. His body was a conduit for energies that made Kael's spiritual sea churn. 
The figure looked at Kael. He smiled. 
"You found the tablets faster than I expected."
Kael's pulse hammered against his ribs. He kept his voice level. "Whisper King."
"In the flesh. Or what remains of it." The figure gestured to the gathered Reavers. "They all served me once. They harvested the power that sealed the rifts. When their usefulness ended, I preserved them here."
"You want me to seal the rifts."
"I want you to choose." The Whisper King stepped closer. The weight of centuries pressed against Kael's chest. "The first Reaver couldn't choose. He was a tool. You have built a network. You have options."
"Options."
"Work with me, and the seals hold. The Abyss remains contained. Refuse, and the seals fail. The Entities emerge. The resulting chaos kills everyone you care about."
"You are asking me to trust a man who killed his own followers."
"I am asking you to trust the logic of survival." The Whisper King's form rippled. "Keep harvesting. Keep learning. The True Name isn't a word you speak. It is an understanding you achieve. Three days until Blackthorn Dock. Zarek will try to kill you. The Order of Dawn will try to capture you. And when both fail—"
The darkness snapped back. Kael gasped, the tablet clattering to the table. His hands shook. 
Sera stared at him. "What happened? You vanished."
"The Whisper King." Kael retrieved the tablet. "He showed me the origin."
"What choice?"
Kael met Sera's gaze. He wanted to tear the Whisper King's plan apart. He wanted to show the ancient entity that he was not a tool. He wanted to harvest enough power to break Zarek. 
"I need to harvest more souls," Kael said. "The True Name won't reveal itself without preparation."
"Where do we start?"
Kael thought of the unmarked graves. Of the bodies waiting in the dark. 
"The cemeteries," he said. "Them."
The Blackveil Cemetery held the bodies of those who died without family. Kael moved through the fog-shrouded rows. His Sovereign-stage senses mapped every grave, every subtle variation in the spiritual energy clinging to the interred. 
He found three fresh graves. 
The first was a dockworker. Kael placed his palm on the cold dirt. The system in his blood reached out, threading through the soil, finding the corpse below. He extracted the combat training and dock labor. The memories tasted of salt and sweat. 
The second was a woman killed by the Shadow Court. Kael harvested her fragments of cult rituals and tactical knowledge. He pulled a name from her fading mind: Brother Covenant. 
The third corpse was an elderly man. The harvest revealed decades of service to the Order of Dawn. Kael extracted a new perception, the ability to sense the structural integrity of nearby seals. And he pulled a secret shame from the dead man's mind: the Order of Dawn had known about the Shadow Court for decades. They had chosen to contain the corruption than eliminate it. 
Kael sat back on his heels. The commander who had offered an alliance had known. The emergency Council of Six session was another layer of cover-up. 
"You are trespassing."
Kael rose, turning to face the speaker. A tall, gaunt figure stood at the cemetery's edge. A mechanical eyepiece glowed in the darkness. 
"Veiled Market," Kael said, reading the spiritual pressure. 
"You are the Reaver." The figure stepped forward, smiling with teeth filed to points. "The Market has an interest in your activities. A Reaver at Sovereign stage, unconstrained by the Law of Conservation."
Kael's hand drifted toward Shadowveil. "I am not for sale."
"Everything is for sale. The question is only price." The figure stopped ten feet away. "I represent buyers who would pay handsomely for your services. The Orders know what you are. Soon, everyone will know."
"And if I am not interested?"
"Then we become interested in you. Not as a customer. As inventory." The figure extended a black card edged in silver, depositing it on the nearest headstone. "Three days until Blackthorn Dock. We want to ensure you are not interrupted before completing your business."
Kael stared at the card. "You are offering to help me kill Zarek?"
"We are offering to ensure no one interferes with your personal vendetta." The figure stepped back into the fog. "The Whisper King approaches his endgame. The question is whether you want to be a piece on his board or a player at the table."
The fog swallowed the figure. Kael stood alone among the graves. He picked up the black card, feeling the slick surface against his thumb. 
A sound reached his ears. A low, rhythmic chanting, rising from the deepest part of the cemetery. The ground beneath his boots began to vibrate. The spiritual pressure in the air shifted, growing thick and acrid. The seal beneath Blackveil, the one he had just sensed with his newly harvested skill, was tearing open.
Kael pocketed the card and drew Shadowveil. He moved toward the chanting, the shadows of the graveyard twisting around him. He crested the shallow hill, looking down into the open pit where the Mortician's Guild deposited the unclaimed dead. 
A circle of robed figures stood around the mass grave. In the center, standing atop a pile of fresh corpses, was a man in a crimson coat. 
Zarek. 
Kael stopped at the edge of the treeline. His grip on the hilt of his blade turned his knuckles white. Zarek raised his hands, dark energy pouring from his palms into the earth. The corpses beneath his feet began to twitch. 
The chanting of the robed figures intensified. The ground split. A deep, yawning chasm tore through the cemetery, swallowing headstones and damp earth. A cold, abyssal wind rushed upward, carrying the stench of sulfur and decaying souls. 
Kael took a step forward. A dry twig snapped beneath his boot. 
Zarek lowered his hands. The chanting stopped. The crimson-coated figure turned, his eyes locking onto the shadows where Kael stood. A slow, wide smile stretched across Zarek's face. He raised a single hand, pointing one finger directly at Kael's chest. 
The corpses in the pit stopped twitching. They turned their hollow, rotting gaze toward the treeline. They opened their mouths in unison, releasing a single, deafening shriek.
