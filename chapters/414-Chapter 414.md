Kael's blade found the cultist's throat before the man could finish his prayer. Blood sprayed across the crumbling altar, hot and copper-bright against the cold stone. Three more figures in tattered robes scattered into the fog-choked alley behind the temple, their bare feet slapping against cobblestones still slick with last night's rain.
"Zarek sends his regards," Kael said, though Zarek had sent nothing. The name was a weapon of its own, designed to make survivors spread panic faster than any plague.
The dying cultist's eyes went wide—not with pain, but with recognition. His lips moved, forming words that came out as wet gurgles. "...you're the one from the pit... They said you burned..."
Kael knelt, pressing his palm flat against the cooling flesh. The Soul Reaver's hunger stirred, eager and patient.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Shadowstep Lv.3 from the remains. Agility +2 acquired.]
The notification flickered across his vision, clean and clinical. The cultist's body went slack, all tension bleeding out as Kael absorbed what remained of his stolen gifts.
Shadowstep. A movement technique used by those who served the darkness between worlds. Kael had no idea what this corpse's owner had done with it—probably nothing good—but the ability itself was neutral. Tools didn't choose how they were wielded.
He rose, wiping his hand on his coat. The fog had thickened while he worked, turning the narrow street into a grey labyrinth. Somewhere in the distance, a steam-whistle shrieked from the Thornwick docks. Three in the morning. The city that never slept, even when it should.
"Another one down." Mira's voice drifted from the rooftop above, where she'd been serving as lookout. Her boots found purchase on the rain-slick tiles as she dropped down beside him. "But there's something wrong."
"Everything is wrong." Kael sheathed his blade. "That's why we're here."
"Not like this." She crouched beside the corpse, her pale eyes—milky with the blindness that came from her particular gift—tracking something he couldn't see. "His spirit was... Screaming. Not in fear. In warning. They all are, tonight."
Kael felt it then. A pressure against his senses, like standing at the edge of a cliff in total darkness. The air itself had grown heavier, charged with something that made his teeth ache.
"The rift," he said.
"That's what I was thinking." Mira straightened, her hand moving to the short blade at her hip. "Three blocks east. Bigger than last week."
Three blocks east was the Ashworth textile district, home to a hundred sweat-soaked workers and their families. Three blocks east was also where the third seal was located, one of the seventy-two bindings that kept the Abyss from spilling into the world.
And according to every report Kael had read in the past month, three blocks east was where Cult Prime Zarek had been concentrating his efforts.
"We need to move." He started walking before the words finished leaving his mouth.
Mira kept pace beside him, her unseeing gaze fixed ahead. "Kael. The seal isn't just weakening. Something's pushing against it from the other side. Something big."
The streets narrowed as they entered the industrial quarter. Smokestacks rose like blackened fingers against the clouded sky, pouring ash and cinder into the air. Workers trudged past them in both directions, too exhausted from their shifts to pay attention to two Awakened moving at a purposeful clip.
A child darted between the legs of a loading dock worker, laughing at some private game. The sound hit Kael harder than it should have. He kept walking.
"You can't save everyone." Mira's voice was soft, as if she'd read his thoughts.
"I know."
"Then why do you keep trying?"
The question hung between them. Kael didn't answer, because the answer was complicated and involved ghosts and a prison cell and the specific weight of a blade in his hand. Instead, he focused on the growing wrongness ahead.
The pressure intensified with every step. Other Awakened would have felt it as discomfort, a vague sense of unease that encouraged retreat. For Kael, it was a clarion call. The Soul Reaver's gift let him sense death in all its forms—physical, spiritual, conceptual. And right now, the death-sense was screaming.
They turned the corner into Miller's Passage and stopped.
The textile factory had been transformed. What had been a squat brick building with smoke-blackened windows was now a wound in reality, edges flickering between stone and absolute darkness. The Veil itself was visible here, thin as moth wings, tearing at the seams as something pressed against it from beyond.
Cultists ringed the building in silent prayer. Dozens of them, kneeling in concentric circles around the rift, their voices rising in a language that predated human speech. The words burrowed into Kael's skull like parasites, trying to nest in his thoughts.
"—we reject the binding—" one voice intoned.
"—we welcome the return—" another answered.
"—blood for the hungry—" a third continued.
Kael's stomach turned. He'd heard rumors about mass rituals, about what happened when cultists pushed too hard against the seal network. But rumors were abstractions. This was visceral, immediate, threatening to spill over into catastrophe within the hour.
"The Watcher is going to blow." Mira's hand found his arm, her grip tight. "We need to get inside."
"Agreed." Kael assessed the cultist positions. "We go through them or around them."
"Around takes too long. Through is faster."
"Through it is."
He drew his blade and stepped into the light.
The nearest cultist turned, her face a mask of ecstasy and terror. She opened her mouth to scream a warning, but Kael was already among them, his steel finding flesh with mechanical precision. Not killing—caring. Precision strikes to joints and nerve clusters, dropping bodies without opening arteries. He had no desire to harvest from zealots if he could avoid it. The abilities they carried were corrupted, twisted by exposure to Abyssal influence, and the system warnings about contamination grew louder every time he tried to process them.
Mira moved like water beside him, her blind eyes tracking every threat before it materialized. A cultist lunged with a rusted knife; she sidestepped, brought her elbow down on his wrist, followed with an open palm to his nose. The crunch of cartilage was deeply satisfying.
"How many more?" she asked.
Kael counted bodies, then estimated the remainder. "Fifteen. Maybe twenty."
"We'll be in the middle before we reach the door."
"Then we make it faster."
He activated Shadowstep for the first time, letting the stolen ability flood through his limbs. The world took on a greyish tint as his perception shifted, time stretching like taffy as he moved between moments. To an outside observer, he would have been a blur, a smear of motion that left bodies crumpling in his wake.
To Kael, it felt like drowning in ice water.
The technique was meant for assassins, for those who moved through shadows and struck without warning. He hated it immediately. His own gifts required proximity, required him to touch death than flee from life. Shadowstep was the opposite of everything he embodied.
But it worked.
He covered the distance to the factory doors in heartbeats, leaving a trail of groaning bodies in his wake. Mira arrived seconds later, slightly out of breath but otherwise unharmed.
"Remind me to teach you that," she said.
"It's not mine. Doesn't fit right."
"Does it matter?"
It did. It mattered a great deal,, but now wasn't the time for philosophical discussions about the ethics of ability theft. Kael threw open the factory doors and stepped into the screaming dark.
The interior had been gutted. Machinery that once produced cloth now formed a rough circle around the rift itself, boiler pipes and gear assemblies repurposed into something that looked almost organic. The metal twisted and pulsed with bioluminescent veins, dark purple light bleeding from joints and seams.
And at the center, pinned to the floor by chains of solidified shadow, was a woman.
Kael recognized her instantly. Order of Dawn insignia on her collar. Silver hair matted with blood. Eyes that were open but saw nothing, fixed on the nightmare above her as her life slowly drained away.
"Sister Thorne." The name came from memory, pulled from briefings he'd been forced to memorize during his time in the prison system. High Inquisitor Thorne of the Order of Dawn, one of the Six's primary enforcers in the Thornwick region. She'd been declared missing three weeks ago.
Now Kael understood why.
The rift pulsed. Through the tear in reality, he could see something moving—not clearly, but in impressions. A shape that defied geometry. Eyes that multiplied and divided. Limbs that bent in directions that hurt to contemplate.
The Faceless One's servants had been busy.
"The seal." Mira was already moving toward the binding circle at the edge of the rift. "Someone broke the tertiary locks. That's why it's destabilizing."
"Can you fix it?"
"I can try. But I need time." She knelt beside the circle, fingers tracing symbols that glowed faintly as she activated them. "Keep whatever that is contained. Don't let it push through."
Kael sheathed his blade and drew it again. The woman—Thorne—was beyond help. He could see that now. Her spiritual reserves had been emptied, fed to the rift like wood thrown on a fire. All that remained was an empty shell, slowly being unmade by exposure to raw Abyssal energy.
The Soul Reaver's hunger stirred again, demanding attention.
"She might have something left." The thought crept into his mind unbidden. "Even a fraction of what she was carrying would—"
He crushed the impulse. Thorne had been an enemy of the state, yes. A member of the establishment that had condemned him to death. But she'd also fought against the things in the Abyss, had given her life to slow whatever Zarek was planning. Harvesting her now would be practical. It would also make him no better than the cultists kneeling outside.
He refused.
Instead, he positioned himself between the rift and Mira, drawing on every ounce of spiritual energy he possessed to create a barrier. The power flowed out of him in waves, his Demigod reserves burning hot as they interacted with the Abyssal presence. The temperature around him dropped thirty degrees in seconds. Frost formed on his coat, his hair, his eyelashes.
The thing in the rift noticed.
IT IS YOU. The voice wasn't sound. It was pressure, meaning pressed directly into his thoughts. The voice of something that had existed before humanity crawled from the primordial muck. THE ONE WHO WALKS WITH DEAD.
Kael held his ground. "I'm nobody."
YOU CARRY MY BROTHER'S GIFT. THE SOUL REAVER. IT WAS STOLEN FROM ME, EONS AGO. I REMEMBER ITS TASTE.
The words burrowed into his skull like parasites, trying to nest in his thoughts. He crushed the mental probe with brute force, refusing to let the entity find purchase. His gift had come from a corpse in a prison cell, harvested in extremis by a dying man who had wanted revenge more than survival. There was no ancient history there. There couldn't be.
"I'm just passing through," Kael said.
The entity laughed. The sound was like breaking glass, like screaming children, like the death of stars. The rift expanded another inch, chains of shadow straining against the boundary between worlds.
KAEL ASHBORN. The name emerged from the darkness like a blade. THE EXECUTED. THE DEAD WHO WALKS. ZAREK TOLD ME YOU WOULD COME.
The world tilted. Zarek. The name that had haunted Kael since before his resurrection, the architect of his false conviction, the man who had framed him for crimes he didn't commit. Zarek, who had disappeared into the cult's upper echelons after Kael's "execution." Zarek, who knew exactly who Kael was and what he could do.
"Where is he?" The words came out harder than Kael intended.
THE PRIME IS EVERYWHERE. THE PRIME IS NOWHERE. BUT HE SENDS A MESSAGE. The entity's presence pressed against the barrier, testing its strength. THIRTEEN MORE SEALS WILL BREAK. TONIGHT. ALL ACROSS THE CONTINENT. AND WHEN THEY DO, YOU WILL CHOOSE SIDES.
"Zarek wants me on his side?"
ZAREK WANTS YOU DEAD. BUT HE IS PATIENT. HE KNOWS THAT THE REAVER LINEAGE CANNOT SURVIVE WHAT IS COMING. ONE WAY OR ANOTHER, YOU WILL JOIN THE DEAD. AGAIN.
The pressure intensified. Kael felt his barrier buckling, hairline fractures spreading through the spiritual architecture he'd constructed. He pushed harder, pouring everything he had into holding the line, but the math was brutal and unforgiving.
He was one Demigod against something that had been old when the world was young. The numbers didn't work.
Mira. How much longer?
"Nearly done." Her voice was strained. "Just need another minute."
"You have thirty seconds. Maybe less."
The entity pushed harder. The barrier cracked further.
KAEL ASHBORN. The voice was almost gentle now, almost kind. THE FACELESS ONE HAS BEEN WATCHING YOU. WE KNOW WHAT SLEEPS INSIDE YOUR SOUL. WE KNOW WHAT YOU TRULY ARE.
The words hit him like a physical blow. The Faceless One. One of the original seventy-two Entities, ancient beyond measure, powerful beyond comprehension. And according to the briefing documents he'd memorized, it had been sealed in the largest of the rifts—the Great Rift, the wound in reality that had spawned the Abyssal Wars and necessitated the creation of the Six Orders in the first place.
That couldn't be right. The Great Rift was contained, watched by every Order simultaneously, reinforced by the combined spiritual weight of six hundred years of accumulated power. The Faceless One couldn't possibly be—
THINK OF THE THREE FRAGMENTS. The entity's voice was patient, almost pitying. THINK OF WHO CARRIES THEM.
Three fragments. Kael's mind raced. Three individuals, marked by pieces of something that had been shattered long ago. One of them was him—he'd known that since the day he'd woken up in his own coffin, the Faceless One's splinter lodged in his soul like a splinter of glass.
Who were the other two?
The barrier shattered.
Kael threw himself sideways as tendrils of pure darkness lashed through the space he'd occupied. They found the far wall instead, punching through brick and mortar like it was wet paper. The factory groaned, structural supports melting as Abyssal energy turned solid matter into something else.
Mira screamed. Not in pain—in frustration. "I need ten more seconds!"
"You've got five!"
He was on his feet again, blade raised, soul-energy blazing around him in a corona of pale light. The entity couldn't fully manifest—not yet, not while any part of the seal remained intact—but what it could send through was more than sufficient to kill him.
Kael ran toward the rift.
The cultists had called this the Reckoning, he remembered. The moment when the barrier between worlds would thin enough for the old powers to return. He'd thought it was propaganda, apocalyptic fantasy designed to keep the faithful in line.
Now he understood. The Reckoning wasn't a single event. It was a flow, a cascade failure that would accelerate until the whole system collapsed.
Thirteen more seals. All across the continent. Zarek's plan, whatever it truly was.
Kael's blade found the edge of the rift and carved a line through reality itself.
The entity howled.
The cut wasn't deep—couldn't be, not with his current power—but it was precisely placed. Kael had spent years learning anatomy, human and otherwise, and the Abyssal Entities followed similar principles. Slices to key points could disrupt energy flow, create momentary gaps in an otherwise impenetrable defense.
It bought Mira the time she needed.
The binding circle blazed to life, silver-white light erupting from the patterns she'd traced. The symbols rose from the floor, hovering in the air around the rift, forming a cage of pure spiritual law. The tendrils that had been reaching for Kael recoiled, batting against the barrier like moths against glass.
"No," the entity said. Its voice had lost the cosmic grandeur, becoming something smaller and more desperate. "NOT YET. THE TIME IS—"
The cage contracted. The rift compressed, edges sealing together as Mira poured everything she had into the repair. Her face was bloodless, veins standing out on her neck, spiritual energy visibly draining from her body.
Kael watched, blade still raised, as the tear in reality shrank to a pinprick.
Then to nothing.
The factory went dark. Not Abyssal darkness—ordinary shadow, the kind that came when a light source was extinguished. The pressure vanished, leaving Kael's ears ringing and his thoughts scattered like leaves in a storm.
Mira collapsed.
He caught her before she hit the ground, lowering her gently against the cold stone. Her pulse was thready but present, her breathing shallow. She'd pushed too hard, burned through reserves that should have lasted a week.
"You idiot," he muttered. "You magnificent idiot."
"Still... Working..." Her lips barely moved.
"I know. Rest."
He laid her down, then stood to survey the damage. The cultists outside had fled—most of them, anyway. The rest lay where they'd fallen, bodies broken by the shockwave that had accompanied the rift's closure. Their blood pooled beneath them, dark in the dim light filtering through the factory's few remaining windows.
Kael walked among them, touching each one in turn.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Void-Touched Resilience Lv.2 from the remains. Resistance +3 acquired.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Blood Prayer Lv.1 from the remains. Minor healing factor acquired.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Fragment of Frenzy Lv.4 from the remains. Combat +5 acquired.]
The notifications scrolled past, each one a small victory against the darkness. The cultists' abilities were degraded, corrupted by exposure to Abyssal energy, but they were still functional. Still useful. The Law of Soul Conservation in action—energy couldn't be created or destroyed, only transferred.
Kael had just transferred a great deal.
When he finished, he returned to Mira's side. She was conscious now, sitting up against a fallen beam, her face drawn but alert.
"Three blocks east," she said. "That's what you said. But the seal wasn't the primary target. It was a distraction."
"Distraction from what?"
"The real rift. The one they've been opening for months, right under the Council's nose." She met his gaze, her blind eyes seeing straight through to his soul. "It's in the Undercroft, Kael. Beneath the old cathedral. And whatever's been sealed there is a thousand times worse than what we just stopped."
The Undercroft. Kael knew the place—everyone in Thornwick did. It was where the Council of Six held their annual meetings, a neutral ground supposedly protected by every Order's combined pledge. If the cult had penetrated that far...
"How long until it opens?"
Mira's expression told him everything he needed to know. "Hours. Maybe less. Zarek wanted the distraction to pull the Watchers away. To empty the city while he finished his work."
Zarek's name echoed in the space between them. The man Kael had sworn to kill, the architect of his suffering, the thread that connected every thread of this nightmare. He was always three steps ahead, always one move beyond comprehension.
"I need to get to the Undercroft."
"Alone?"
"I work alone."
Mira laughed, a weak sound that turned into a cough. "You just said you work alone. Two minutes ago, you told me the same thing. And yet here I am, lying on the floor of a textile factory, having just saved your miserable life."
Kael offered her his hand. "Can you walk?"
"I can run. Probably." She gripped his fingers and let him pull her upright. "But I'm coming with you. Someone needs to watch your back while you do something stupid."
"Fine. But if you die—"
"Then harvest whatever's useful and move on. I know the rules." She straightened her coat, checked her blade, and nodded toward the door. "Let's go find out what's really been sealed beneath the city."
They stepped out into the fog-choked street. The pre-dawn darkness pressed against them from all sides, heavy with the promise of rain that hadn't fallen yet. Somewhere in the distance, a church bell tolled four times.
Four hours until sunrise. Four hours until whatever Zarek had planned came to fruition.
Four hours to prevent the end of the world.
Kael ran.
The streets blurred past as he moved through Thornwick's sleeping heart. Shadowstep faded in and out as he needed it, conserving energy for the moments when pure speed wasn't enough. Mira kept pace beside him, her feet finding paths through the darkness that shouldn't have been possible for someone who couldn't see.
They didn't speak. There was nothing to say that hadn't already been covered in the factory's aftermath. The facts were simple, brutal, and immutable.
Somewhere beneath the city, something ancient was waking up.
Somewhere in the shadows, Zarek was waiting.
And Kael—executed, resurrected, damned—would have to face both.
The old cathedral rose ahead of them, its spires cutting through the mist like broken teeth. The Council's meeting hall stood at its heart, built on foundations that predated human settlement of the continent. No one remembered who had constructed the Undercroft or why. The Orders had claimed it, transformed it into neutral territory, made it sacred through centuries of use.
Now that sanctity was about to be violated.
Kael slowed as he approached the cathedral steps. His senses stretched outward, searching for threats, for guards, for any sign that the Watchers had returned to their posts.
Nothing.
The silence was wrong. Complete. Even at this hour, there should have been patrols, sentries, the ambient spiritual pressure of Awakened maintaining their vigilance. Instead, the cathedral stood empty, windows dark, doors hanging open.
"They really did pull everyone out," Mira whispered.
"Zarek's work."
"Smart. Effective. Terrifying."
Kael drew his blade and descended the steps into darkness.
The Undercroft opened before them like a wound. Spiral stairs wound downward, carved from stone so old it had turned almost black with age. The air grew colder with every step, damp with moisture that had no business existing this far underground.
And beneath it all, the pressure returned. Worse than before. Strong enough to make Kael's eyes water and his teeth ache.
"Something's different," he said.
"I know." Mira's hand found his arm, fingers digging in. "The seal here... It's not failing. It's being undone. Someone inside is opening it from this side."
Kael's blood went cold. "That's impossible. The seals require—"
"Not the seals. The lock. There's a difference."
The stairs ended in a vast chamber, illuminated by sourceless light that cast no shadows. At the center stood a circle of carved symbols, each one glowing with power that made Kael's Soul Reaver senses scream warnings. And kneeling within that circle, hands pressed against the floor as if in prayer, was a figure he recognized.
Zarek.
The man who'd framed him for murder. The cultist who'd orchestrated his execution. The puppet master who'd known exactly where Kael would be and what he would do.
Zarek looked up and smiled.
"Took you long enough." His voice echoed strangely in the chamber, multiple tones layered over each other. "I was beginning to think you'd miss the main event."
"Step away from the circle." Kael's blade rose, tip leveled at Zarek's throat.
"No, I don't think so." Zarek rose to his feet, and the light shifted around him. His eyes had changed—no longer human, but filled with something that burned like cold fire. "You see, this is the moment I've been planning for two hundred years. The moment when the Faceless One finally returns to full power. And you—"
His smile widened.
"—are the key to making it happen."
The floor beneath Kael's feet began to shake.
"What did you do?"
"I planted a fragment inside you. Gave you a piece of my master, as a gift." Zarek spread his arms, power radiating outward in waves. "When I open the final seal, that fragment will wake. And it will drag your soul into the Abyss, where it will be devoured. Leaving your body empty. Leaving your gift without an owner."
He stepped closer, walking through the circle as if it posed no obstacle.
"Leaving the Soul Reaver lineage free to be claimed by someone who truly understands its potential. Someone who has spent centuries preparing to wield it."
The shaking intensified. Cracks spread across the chamber floor, darkness bleeding up from below.
"Your gift was never meant for you, Kael. It was always mine. And tonight, I finally take it back."
The darkness erupted.
Kael's world collapsed into screaming.
