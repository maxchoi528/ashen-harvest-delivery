The scream came first—a raw, animal sound torn from a throat three trenches away. Kael's head snapped toward it even as his knee drove into a cultist's stomach, the man's body crumpling with a wet gurgle.
Another fanatic lunged from the smoke. Kael sidestepped, caught the wrist, wrenched it sideways. Bone cracked. The cultist screamed and kept screaming until Kael's palm connected with his forehead, and then there was only the wet thud of a body hitting mud.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted Air Step (basic) from the remains. Essence acquired."
The notification pulsed in his vision, neither welcome nor unwelcome. Air Step. Not powerful, but functional—another tool for the arsenal he built from corpses.
Behind him, the Iron Vanguard line buckled. A massive shape—something that had once been human, now bloated to twice normal size with black veins crawling beneath translucent skin—smashed through a cluster of soldiers. They flew like ragdolls. Three impacts. Three screams. Three silences.
"Demigod-touched," someone yelled. "Fall back to the second—"
No one was listening. The soldiers nearest the creature had already broken, their formations dissolving into individual panic.
Kael's boots churned through churned earth as he ran toward the thing. He could feel the spiritual energy radiating off it—thick, oily, wrong. It turned toward him with the leisurely confidence of a predator that had never been challenged. Its mouth opened. Teeth like broken glass gleamed.
He didn't stop. Didn't slow. Demigod-stage power flooded his channels, and he channeled it all into his right arm.
The punch landed on its chest with enough force to crater stone. The creature's eyes bulged. Black ichor sprayed from its mouth. It staggered back—Loss of balance, the word from some long-dead fighter whispered in his mind—and Kael pressed the advantage, hammering blow after blow into its torso until something vital gave way with a wet, tearing sound.
It collapsed.
Kael stood over the thing, breathing hard, his knuckles split and bleeding. Blood dripped onto the mud. Around him, the remaining cultists had frozen, their fanatical zeal momentarily overridden by shock.
"Anyone else?" His voice carried across the churned battlefield.
They ran.
He let them go. There would be more—always more—and chasing stragglers meant leaving the line undefended. Instead, he crouched beside the creature's corpse, pressing his palm against the cold, rubbery flesh.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted Entity Fragment (degraded) from the remains. Essence acquired. Warning: Corruption traces detected. Extended exposure may affect spiritual pathways."
Kael pulled his hand back, wiping the residue on his coat. Corruption. Another cost he would pay later, if there was a later.
The Iron Vanguard soldiers were slowly returning to their positions, casting uncertain glances at him. He ignored them. He wasn't here to be a hero or a symbol. He was here because the Ashenmoor Wastes were a harvesting ground unlike any other—a place where death came in waves, and every corpse was potential power.
"Adjutant!" A young officer ran toward him, face streaked with grime and terror. "Commander Aldric requests your presence at the forward command post. Immediately."
"Tell him I'm needed here."
"He said—" The boy's voice cracked. "He said you'd say that. He said to tell you that the Six Orders' delegation arrived, and they're demanding to speak with the 'Reaver of Ashenmoor.'"
The words hit like a physical blow. Six Orders. Delegation. Reaver.
The world had finally caught up with the rumors.
---
The command post was a fortified bunker dug into the hillside, reinforced with iron plates and spiritual barriers. Kael ducked through the entrance, blinking in the dim lantern light.
Commander Aldric stood at a table covered with maps and reports. He was a broad man, weathered by decades of war, with steel-gray hair cropped close to his skull. His eyes—sharp, calculating—found Kael immediately.
"Close the door."
Kael did. The heavy iron swung shut with a clang that echoed in the confined space.
"You're a hard man to reach, Ashborn." Aldric's voice was flat, giving nothing away. "I've sent three requests for a meeting. You ignored all of them."
"I was busy."
"Busy." The commander repeated the word like it tasted sour. "Burying how many of my men today?"
"None. I haven't killed any Vanguard soldiers."
"Physically, no. But the cultists you butcher—they're not just rabble. Intelligence suggests they're part of a coordinated network. Every time you take one down, they adapt their tactics. They've started targeting our wounded specifically because they know you'll try to harvest them afterward."
The accusation hung in the air. Kael met Aldric's gaze without flinching.
"What do you want?"
"What I want is irrelevant." Aldric stepped aside, revealing two figures Kael hadn't noticed in the shadows. A woman in white robes embroidered with golden suns—a member of the Order of Dawn. Beside her, a gaunt man in gray bearing the mark of the Pale Seers.
"And what do they want?"
The woman spoke first. Her voice was cultured, precise, each word measured. "The Six Orders have monitored your activities since the Thornwick incident. Your abilities are... Unprecedented. A Soul Reaver operating openly represents both a threat and a potential asset."
"Asset?" Kael's lip curled. "I thought your kind hunted Reavers."
"Hunted, yes. Hunted those who acted without purpose or restraint." She stepped forward, her gaze cataloging him like a specimen. "You have neither joined the cults nor used your abilities for personal tyranny. Your harvesting appears selective. This suggests either exceptional discipline or exceptional ignorance."
"Take your pick."
"Discipline, then. The Orders can work with discipline." She extended a hand. "I am Inquisitor Seren Valdris of the Order of Dawn. I speak for the unified Six Orders in this matter. We offer you a choice: submit to our oversight and fight alongside us, or continue as an independent agent and face the full weight of our enforcement divisions."
Beside her, the Pale Seer made a soft sound that might have been agreement or warning.
Kael looked at the offered hand. Then at Aldric, whose expression remained unreadable. Then back at the Inquisitor.
"Let me make sure I understand. You want me—an illegal soul-harvester whose existence your organizations have spent centuries condemning—to join you voluntarily so you can monitor me more effectively?"
"The situation has changed. The Abyssal cults have activated multiple rift points across the continent. All seventy-two seals are weakening simultaneously." Seren Valdris's composure cracked, just for a moment. "The war we thought we were winning? We're losing. Badly. Every Awakened we can field matters now more than ever."
Seventy-two seals. All weakening. The information landed in Kael's mind and took root.
"How simultaneous?"
"Within the past six months. It began with isolated incidents—a cult here, a rogue shrine there. We thought they were unconnected. We were wrong." Valdris's jaw tightened. "Someone is orchestrating a coordinated assault on the seal network. If they succeed, the Abyss will consume everything."
"And you need Reavers because...?"
"Because Reavers are the only ones who can harvest spiritual energy without depleting our own reserves. Every Awakened who falls in this war takes their power with them—power we cannot reclaim. But a Reaver..." She let the implication hang.
"They can recycle it." Kael finished. "Turn your dead into fuel."
"Yes."
"And if I agree, what guarantees do I have that you won't use me until I'm empty, then discard me?"
"You have my word."
"Your word." He laughed, short and harsh. "Your word means nothing. I've seen what your 'word' produces—concords that crush the weak, treaties that protect the powerful, and a hierarchy that keeps the spiritually inert as slaves. Why should I trust any of you?"
Valdris didn't react to the accusation. "Because you need us as much as we need you. The seal network requires maintenance. The original Reaver—the first of your kind—knew the True Names needed to strengthen the barriers. Those names are recorded on the Sepulcher Tablets. Without them, the seals will fail within a year."
The Sepulcher Tablets. The word struck something deep in Kael's memory—a fragment of knowledge he couldn't place.
"Where are the tablets now?"
"Unknown. They were lost after the original Reaver's death." Valdris tilted her head. "You seem interested. Maybe you know something?"
"Maybe." He gave her nothing.
A heavy silence settled over the bunker. Outside, the distant sounds of battle continued—the moans of the wounded, the clash of steel, the occasional explosion of spiritual power.
Aldric finally broke the quiet. "Ashborn. I'm not going to pretend I understand your methods or approve of what you do. But I've watched you fight for three days. You've saved more of my soldiers than you've harvested. That counts for something."
"Saved them so I could harvest them later? You're more cynical than I thought, Commander."
"I know soldiers. I've seen the way you fight—controlled, efficient, never wasteful. You could have let the Demigod-touched tear through our lines. Instead, you killed it. Alone." Aldric crossed his arms. "Whatever your reasons, you're an asset. The question is whether you're an asset we work with or one we eliminate."
Two paths. Two futures. Kael weighed them both.
The door to the bunker exploded inward.
Wood splinters scattered across the floor. Kael moved instinctively, ducking behind the table as a blast of oily darkness rolled into the space. The Pale Seer screamed—high and piercing—before the shadows coalesced into a humanoid shape and drove a clawed hand through his chest.
Blood fountained. The man twitched twice, then went still.
"Soul Harvest: Target deceased. Insufficient contact for extraction."
Kael cursed under his breath. Too far. He couldn't reach the corpse in time.
Valdris was already on her feet, golden light blazing around her palms. "Assassin! The barrier—"
"Compromised," Aldric finished grimly, drawing a blade that ignited with white fire. "They knew we were meeting. Someone told them."
The cult had moles inside the command structure. Of course they did. Why wouldn't they?
The shadow creature turned toward Kael. Its face was featureless except for a mouth that split wide, impossibly wide, revealing rows of spinning teeth.
"REAVER," it rasped. "THE COURT SENDS ITS REGARDS."
Kael rose from behind the table, letting his Demigod-stage power flare openly. The bunker trembled. Dust rained from the ceiling.
"The Shadow Court, you mean." His voice was calm, even as his heart hammered. "Tell me—what do they want with a Reaver?"
"POSSESSION. CONTROL. THE FIRST REAVER SERVED THE COURT. YOU WILL SERVE ALSO."
"The first Reaver is dead."
"DEAD THINGS STILL SPEAK. THE COURT HAS FOUND THE WAY."
The creature lunged. Kael met it head-on, channeling every ounce of harvested power into his fists. They collided in midair, light against shadow, flesh against void.
The impact sent everyone else in the bunker staggering. Valdris's barriers flickered and died. Aldric was thrown against the wall, his blade skittering across the floor.
Kael and the assassin traded blows in a blur of motion too fast for normal eyes. Every strike carried spiritual force. Every block deflected raw Abyssal energy. The bunker walls cracked. The floor buckled.
"YOU CANNOT WIN," the creature hissed. "WE ARE MANY. YOU ARE ONE."
"Then I'll just have to become more."
Kael's hand found an opening. His fingers closed around the thing's throat, and he pulled it close, close enough to see his own reflection in its void-dark eyes.
"Soul Harvest: Initiating extraction from Entity-Touched host. WARNING: Host corruption levels extreme. Extraction may cause permanent damage."
He didn't care. The power flooded into him—dark, alien, tasting of rot and starlight and something older than humanity.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted Shadow Step (advanced) from the remains. Essence acquired. Soul Corruption +15%. Warning: Approaching threshold. Further extraction from corrupted sources inadvisable."
The creature dissolved into ash, its stolen form unable to survive the harvesting. Kael stood in the center of the ruined bunker, his body smoking, his eyes flickering between human and something else.
Valdris stared at him with a mixture of horror and calculation. Aldric hadn't moved, but his grip on his recovered blade tightened.
"You're compromised," the Inquisitor said softly. "Fifteen percent corruption. In a Demigod, that means you have maybe six months before you lose yourself entirely."
"Six months." Kael's voice came out rougher than intended. "That's more time than most people get."
"It's not just about time. The corruption will spread. It will affect your judgment, your humanity, your—"
"My ability to harvest without consequences?" He laughed, and the sound echoed strangely in the damaged space. "You're worried I'll become a monster. Isn't that what you wanted? A weapon you could point at your enemies?"
"A weapon can choose to turn in its master's hand. A corrupted weapon chooses nothing."
"Then I suppose you'd better make me an offer I can't refuse." Kael straightened, meeting her gaze directly. "Full partnership. Not oversight, not monitoring—partnership. I want access to Order resources, information, and a voice in strategy. In exchange, I fight your war and share what I harvest."
"That's—" Valdris faltered. "That's not how this works. The Orders don't negotiate with—"
"The Orders are losing a war against seventy-two seals and a coordinated cult network. Your old ways don't work anymore." Kael gestured at the carnage around them. "You can keep pretending you control the board, or you can accept that the board has changed and bring in players who adapt faster."
Silence stretched between them.
Finally, Aldric spoke. "Commander Aldric of the Iron Vanguard. I accept his terms."
"Commander—" Valdris started.
"You weren't on the front lines, Inquisitor. I was." The old soldier's voice was tired, resigned. "He's killed more cultists in three days than my entire battalion. He's saved soldiers I thought were dead. And right now, he's the only one who's faced a Shadow Court operative and won." He looked at Kael. "Not because he had to. Because he chose to."
Kael hadn't expected support from that quarter. He filed it away for later consideration.
Valdris took a long, slow breath. "The Order of Dawn cannot speak for all Six Orders. But I can offer you provisional status as an Allied Operative, answerable to a joint command structure. That grants you legal protection and access to basic Order resources."
"Provisional." He tasted the word. "And when the provisional period ends?"
"Then we revisit the arrangement based on results." Her eyes met his. "I hope you understand—trust doesn't come easily to us. Not after centuries of watching Reavers fall to corruption or go rogue."
"I understand better than you know."
Outside, the battle sounds had grown more intense. Someone was shouting orders. The ground shook with the force of spiritual explosions.
Aldric moved to the bunker's remaining exit. "Whatever you're deciding, decide fast. The cult assault is escalating. If they're hitting command posts now, they're planning something bigger."
"The mass unsealing," Valdris whispered. "They must be attempting it early. Without the Sepulcher Tablets to reinforce the seals, if they break even one..."
"They break them all. The chain reaction would tear the Veil apart."
Kael was already moving toward the door. "Which seal?"
"The Great Rift. The largest. If it fails, the others follow within hours."
The Great Rift. The name echoed in his mind, stirring something he couldn't identify. A memory? A warning?
"Then that's where I'm needed."
He didn't wait for permission. The bunker door was barely open before he was through it, sprinting toward the distant horizon where the sky had begun to bleed black.
---
The Ashenmoor Wastes stretched before him like a wound in the world—a blasted landscape of ash and bone where the Abyss pressed closest to the Material plane. The Great Rift was visible even from here, a jagged tear in reality that pulsed with sickly light.
Between him and it: a thousand cultists, a dozen corrupted Awakened, and whatever else the Shadow Court had prepared.
Kael slowed to a walk, letting his Demigod-stage power settle into his bones. His hands no longer trembled. His mind was clear, sharp, focused.
Six months. Maybe less.
He could spend that time hiding, hoarding his remaining humanity, waiting for the end. Or he could spend it making sure there was a world left when the corruption finally took him.
The choice was obvious.
"Soul Harvest: Shadow Step (advanced) ready for deployment. Current corruption: 15%. Maximum safe threshold: 40%."
He stepped forward, and the shadows swallowed him whole.
When he emerged, he was fifty feet closer to the Rift—and surrounded by cultists who hadn't seen him coming.
The first one died with a look of surprise. The second fell reaching for a blade. The third screamed.
Kael harvested them all.
---
The battle raged for hours. Kael carved a path through cultist after cultist, harvesting skills and essence with mechanical efficiency. Shadow Step carried him through enemy lines. Air Step gave him the height advantage. Other abilities— unnamed, undefined—filled the gaps, each one a gift from the dead.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted Iron Will (basic) from the remains. Essence acquired."
"Soul Harvest: You extracted Pain Suppression (intermediate) from the remains. Essence acquired."
"Soul Harvest: You extracted Blade Mastery (advanced) from the remains. Essence acquired."
The notifications blurred together. Kael stopped reading them. There would be time for accounting later—or there wouldn't.
He reached the front line of Awakened combat as the sun began to set. Here, the fighting was different—slower, more strategic, more deadly. Cultist Champions faced Order Champions in duels that crackled with spiritual energy.
One of the Order champions was failing. A woman in blue robes bearing the mark of the Azure Depths, her techniques slick and drowning in appearance, now slow and clumsy as corruption seeped into her movements. Her opponent—a hulking figure wreathed in void—pressed the advantage with savage efficiency.
Kael didn't hesitate. He intervened.
The cultist champion turned, surprise flickering across his corrupted features. "Reaver? You're supposed to be—"
"Supposed to be where?" Kael's fist connected with the man's jaw, sending him staggering. "Letting you kill her so I could harvest the body?"
The Azure Depths champion seized the opening, driving a trident of condensed water through the cultist's shoulder. The creature howled and twisted away, blood mixing with void.
"You—" it snarled. "You dare interfere with the Court's design?"
"The Court's design involves you dying here." Kael advanced, shadows coiling around his fists. "Let's see if I can help it along."
They fought together—an uneasy alliance against a common enemy. Kael's raw power and brutal efficiency complemented her fluid, technical style. Where he broke bones, she found arteries. Where he harvested, she blocked retreat.
Finally, the cultist champion fell. Kael moved to claim the body—
"Soul Harvest: Target deceased. Extraction successful. Soul Fragment recovered."
—but the Azure Depths woman grabbed his wrist before he could make contact.
"That one is mine." Her voice was hoarse, her face pale with blood loss. "By the laws of engagement, his essence belongs to the Order that felled him."
"Your laws don't apply to me."
"Your extraction gave us the opening we needed. That makes him a joint kill." She didn't release his wrist. "Or have you forgotten that some of us still have standards?"
Kael studied her. She was dying, though she didn't know it yet—corruption had spread through her spiritual channels, and her core was cracked beyond repair. Minutes remained. Maybe an hour.
"I could take it anyway," he said quietly. "You couldn't stop me."
"No." Her grip tightened. "But I'd like to see you try."
Something in her eyes made him pause. Not fear. Not defiance. Just... Quiet certainty.
He released the wrist.
"Keep your corpse." He turned away. "You won't need the essence where you're going."
She laughed—a wet, broken sound. "The Sun God's realm? You sound certain for a man who harvests souls for a living."
"I harvest power. The destination..." He hesitated. "That's between you and whatever you believe."
"Spoken like someone who's seen the truth."
Kael didn't answer. He couldn't.
She died ten minutes later, surrounded by allies who mourned her loss and cursed the war. Kael watched from a distance, making sure no cultists claimed her body, and then—when the moment was right—he harvested what remained.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted Tidal Mastery (advanced) from the remains. Essence acquired. Soul Fragment preserved. Azure Depths Champion Mira Ashveil: 'The sea takes all, but some things sink deeper than others.'"
A name. A memory. A final message from a dying woman to a man who had no right to hear it.
Kael stored the fragment carefully, not knowing why, only knowing that it mattered.
---
Night fell over the Ashenmoor Wastes.
Kael stood at the edge of the Great Rift, close enough to feel its pull, far enough to resist its lure. The tear in reality stretched hundreds of feet tall, its edges crackling with unstable energy. Shadows moved within—shapes that might have been entities or might have been hallucinations.
The cult assault had been repelled, but barely. The Orders had lost hundreds. The cultists had lost thousands. And the Rift remained unsealed, its corruption spreading like a cancer through the land.
"You're the Reaver."
Kael turned. A figure approached from the darkness—not cultist, not Order, something else. Tall, gaunt, wrapped in robes that seemed to drink the light. A face pale as bone, eyes like pits of eternity.
"And you are?"
"Someone who remembers." The figure stopped a few feet away, studying Kael with terrible intensity. "The first Reaver was my student. I taught him everything he knew about harvesting, binding, and the price of power."
The words hit Kael like a physical blow. "Impossible. The first Reaver died centuries ago."
"Died, yes. Was destroyed, yes. But memory..." The figure spread its hands. "Memory persists. Even after death. Even after harvesting. You should know—you've been collecting fragments long enough."
Kael's mind raced. The hidden traces. The hints about the original Reaver. This was it—the secret that had been circling him since the beginning.
"What do you want?"
"What the Court wants. What the Orders want. What everyone wants." The figure smiled, a rictus of ancient amusement. "I want you to succeed where he failed. To finish what the first Reaver started. To strengthen the seals before the seventy-two break free."
"And in exchange?"
"In exchange, I offer knowledge. The location of the Sepulcher Tablets. The True Names you need to reinforce the barriers." The figure leaned closer. "All I ask is one small thing in return."
"What?"
The smile widened.
"When you find the first Reaver's tomb, don't open it. What rests inside isn't meant for resurrection. It's meant for containment."
Before Kael could respond, the figure dissolved into shadow, leaving only a whisper in the wind:
"The Court knows you're coming, Reaver. The question is whether you'll arrive before they finish their work—or after the world has already ended."
Kael stood alone at the edge of the Rift, the weight of impossible choices pressing down on him.
The night stretched ahead, dark and endless.
And somewhere in the distance, the sound of footsteps echoed—slow, deliberate, approaching from the shadows.
Kael tensed, his harvested power coiling in his hands.
The footsteps grew louder.
He didn't know if he was ready for what came next.
But he knew, with absolute certainty, that there was no running away from it now.
