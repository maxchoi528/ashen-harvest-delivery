The crate hit the cobblestones with a sound like thunder, splinters raining across the alley mouth. Kael dropped into a crouch behind a steam-drain pipe, fingers finding the cold iron of his blade. Three figures in ash-gray cloaks emerged from the fog—two flanking, one dead center. The seal on the crate they'd dropped pulsed with sickly green luminescence.
"You shouldn't have come here, Reaver." The lead cultist pulled back his hood, revealing ritual scars carved into his scalp. "The Underworld doesn't forgive trespass."
Kael's heart hammered against his ribs. The crate's seal looked like nothing from Order records—older, rougher. Dangerous. Behind him, footsteps echoed from the other end of the alley. They'd herded him into a kill box.
"Funny," he breathed, drawing his blade slow. "I was thinking the same thing about you."
The scarred cultist smiled. "Prime Zarek sends his regards."
Kael moved before the words finished leaving the man's mouth. He lunged left, using the steam-drain as a springboard, and his blade carved through the first cultist's wrist before the man could raise his hands. Blood arced through the fog. The second cultist's spell fizzled against the cobblestones where Kael had stood half a heartbeat ago.
The scarred one wasn't panicking. He was chanting.
Kael's blade found the man's collarbone. The chant died in a wet gurgle. But the crate's seal was already opening, green light bleeding into the alley like poison into a wound.
**[Soul Harvest: You extracted spiritual essence from the remains. 15 units acquired.]**
Not enough. He needed more power for whatever that seal was about to unleash.
The third cultist stood frozen, terror twisting his face. Kael crossed the distance in two strides and grabbed the man's robes. "Tell me what's in that crate."
"Nothing—it's just—please—"
Kael's free hand closed around the cultist's throat. "Last chance."
"The Sepulcher! It's from the Sepulcher!" The words tumbled out. "They said it was a weapon! A weapon to kill the—"
A crack split the air. The crate's lid exploded upward, and something massive and wrong-shaped pushed through the opening. The cultist in Kael's grip went limp, soul fleeing his body before Kael could harvest. Too late.
The thing from the crate unfolded itself like a nightmare origami. Too many joints. No face that Kael could recognize as a face. It moved wrong, as if the rules of geometry were suggestions than laws.
**[WARNING: Unstable Soul Residue Detected. Harvest Unsafe.]**
Kael dropped the corpse and ran.
The Underworld stretched beneath Thornwick in layers—each stratum dug deeper, older, more dangerous. Kael had descended through the first three before finding the sealed chamber, his torch guttering in the chemical-reek of ancient air. The walls here bore carvings that predated the Orders by centuries. He traced the symbols with his fingertips, feeling the grooves worn smooth by countless hands before his.
The chamber was circular, maybe thirty feet across. At its center stood a pedestal of black stone, and upon that pedestal rested a box no larger than his two fists. The box was made of a metal that seemed to drink the torchlight. No locks, no visible mechanisms. Just seamless, hungry darkness.
"The Sepulcher tablets mentioned this." Kael's voice sounded small against the weight of stone above. "A vessel. A prison."
He'd read the fragments Sera had decoded—the scattered notes of someone called the First Reaver. The man who'd discovered how to harvest souls, who'd mapped the Abyss, who'd ultimately vanished into it. The tablets spoke of a weapon he'd forged and then hidden. A failsafe. Something strong enough to kill an Entity if the seals ever failed completely.
Kael reached for the box.
His fingers stopped an inch from the surface. Not from hesitation—from resistance. Spiritual energy crackled between his skin and that hungry metal, a pressure that made his teeth ache. Whatever was inside, it didn't want to be touched.
"Show-off," he muttered, and pushed through.
The moment he made contact, the chamber vanished.
Kael stood in darkness. Not the darkness of the Underworld—true darkness, the kind that existed before light was invented. Then stars bloomed around him, and he realized he was standing in space, surrounded by a map of the cosmos rendered in fire and void.
A voice spoke from everywhere and nowhere.
**YOU ARE NOT THE FIRST. YOU WILL NOT BE THE LAST.**
The stars rearranged themselves, forming patterns Kael recognized—the seal network, the seventy-two points of light arrayed around a central sun that burned with cold silver fire. Seventy-two Entities. Seventy-two prisons.
And at the center of the map, visible only because the surrounding stars dimmed to reveal it, a throne. Empty. Waiting.
**THE VESSEL WAS MADE TO HOUSE WHAT CANNOT BE KILLED. WHAT CANNOT BE IMPRISONED. WHAT CANNOT BE FORGIVEN.**
Images cascaded through Kael's mind. A man standing before an Entity, harvesting its essence strand by strand. The same man building a box of darkness, feeding it fragments of his own soul. The same man walking into the Abyss, carrying the box, refusing to let go even as the void consumed him.
**HE DID NOT SUCCEED. HE DID NOT FAIL. HE SIMPLY BECAME.**
Kael's breath caught. "The First Reaver."
**THE FIRST BECAME THE VESSEL. THE VESSEL BECAME THE PRISON. THE PRISON HOLDS THE THING THAT CANNOT BE KILLED.**
A face emerged from the star-map. The First Reaver's face. It looked like Kael's face. Same sharp jaw, same haunted eyes, same scar across the left eyebrow. The resemblance couldn't be coincidence.
**YOU CARRY HIS GIFT. HIS CURSE. HIS PURPOSE. THE VESSEL REMEMBERS.**
Kael's hand was still touching the box, he realized. But now it was open.
Inside, resting on a bed of crystallized void, lay a shard of absolute nothing. Not darkness—absence. The absence of light, of matter, of meaning. It looked like a tear in reality, frozen mid-rip.
**THE WEAPON IS INCOMPLETE. THE FIRST COULD NOT SACRIFICE ENOUGH OF HIMSELF. MORE IS REQUIRED. MORE WILL ALWAYS BE REQUIRED.**
"What happens if someone takes it?" Kael asked.
**IF THE VESSEL IS EMPTY, THE PRISON EMPTIES. IF THE PRISON EMPTIES, THE THING THAT CANNOT BE KILLED WALKS FREE.**
The stars began to collapse inward. The vision was ending.
**YOU WILL BE TESTED, HEIR OF THE FIRST. THEY COME. THEY ALWAYS COME. THE UNDERWORLD REMEMBERS WHAT YOU ARE.**
Kael snapped back into his body.
The chamber was no longer empty. Six figures stood at the cardinal and ordinal points, surrounding him, their movements synchronized with the precision of a hunting formation. They wore the colors of no Order Kael recognized—deep violet marked with silver thread, forming symbols that seemed to writhe when he looked at them directly.
"Easy, Soul Reaver." The speaker was a woman, middle-aged, with steel-gray hair cropped close to her skull and eyes that reflected no light. She held no weapon, but her hands crackled with contained energy. "We're not here to fight."
"Could've fooled me." Kael's blade was still in his grip. He hadn't lowered it once since the vision released him. "You're blocking my exit."
"You're standing in the center of our sacred chamber." One of the others spoke—a young man with a scholar's build and a fighter's stance. "We could say the same."
"Enough posturing." The woman's voice carried command. "Kael Ashborn. Ascendant stage, Soul Reaver classification, marked for execution by the Order of Dawn, currently hunted by Inquisitor Vane." She smiled without warmth. "We know who you are. We know what you carry."
"Then you know I don't like company."
"And yet." The woman gestured at the open box in his hands. "Here you are. Here you are touching something that hasn't been touched in three hundred years." She took a step closer. "The First Reaver walked into the Abyss carrying that vessel. No one has seen it since. No one has been able to open it since." Her eyes narrowed. "You did. In minutes. What we've been trying to do for centuries."
Kael looked down at the vessel. At the shard of absolute nothing resting inside it. The First Reaver's failsafe. His prison for the Entity that couldn't be killed.
The woman extended her hand. "My name is Valdris. I'm the Keeper of the Sixth Seal. And we need to talk about what happens next."
"Why would I talk to you?"
"Because if you don't, the thing in that vessel walks free. And not even a Soul Reaver can stop what's inside."
Kael's grip tightened on the box. He could feel the shard's presence like a splinter in his mind, a hole in reality that pulled at everything around it. If he closed the lid, sealed it away again, he could walk out of here. Take the vessel somewhere safe. Use it as leverage, as a weapon, as a bargaining chip.
Or he could listen.
"You said you're the Keeper of the Sixth Seal." Kael kept his voice level. "I've read the histories. The Orders maintain the seal network. Six orders, seventy-two seals, one seal per Order."
"Twelve seals per Order," Valdris confirmed. "Maintained through ritual, sacrifice, and will. The network holds. Has held for two thousand years."
"But?"
"But the network is weakening. The seals are failing faster than we can repair them. Our divinations show escalation—a coordinated effort to breach multiple points simultaneously." Her jaw tightened. "Someone is trying to unmake the containment system. And whoever they are, they've been planning this for decades."
"The cultists." Kael thought of the scarred man in the alley. "Prime Zarek."
"The name is a title, not a person. There have been six Prime Zareks since the organization's founding. Each one has pushed the cult's agenda further." Valdris began pacing, her movements sharp with barely contained frustration. "They call themselves the Unbound. They believe the Entities should be released, that humanity has no right to cage gods. They've infiltrated Orders, governments, merchant guilds. They have people everywhere."
"And they're working on unsealing the rifts."
"Not just unsealing. Mass unsealing. A coordinated breach of multiple seals at once, overwhelming the Orders' response capacity. If they succeed, if even three Entities break free simultaneously—"
She didn't finish the sentence. She didn't need to.
Kael looked at the vessel in his hands. "This is connected."
"The First Reaver built that box to contain something worse than any Entity. Something born from the Abyss before Entities existed. The Primordial Hunger, some texts call it. The thing that eats gods." Valdris stopped pacing. "The First Reaver didn't seal it away to protect humanity. He sealed it away because it was the only thing that could consume an Entity. He wanted a weapon. He got a monster."
"And now?"
"And now it sits in your hands, accessible to anyone with the will to open it. Which you've just proven is possible." The scholar spoke up again, his voice tight. "The vessel needs a guardian. Someone strong enough to contain the shard's influence, someone with the authority to use it if necessary. The First Reaver was that guardian. When he walked into the Abyss, the position remained empty."
"Until now."
Valdris met his gaze. "Until you."
Kael laughed. It came out harsh, brittle. "I'm marked for death by the Order of Dawn. An Inquisitor is hunting me through Thornwick as we speak. I'm barely stable at Ascendant stage, and you want me to guard a prison for a god-eating monster?"
"Yes."
"Why?"
"Because you opened it. Because the vessel recognizes you as the First Reaver's heir. Because—" She hesitated, and for the first time, something like uncertainty crossed her features. "Because we have no one else."
The silence stretched. Kael turned the box over in his hands, feeling the weight of it. Three hundred years of darkness. Three hundred years of waiting. And, impossibly, it had chosen him.
"What happens if I say no?"
"Then we take the vessel by force. We find another way to seal it. We lose years, possibly decades, while the cult accelerates their timeline." Valdris's voice was flat, clinical. "And when the seals fail and the Entities break free, there will be nothing left to stop them."
"You'd kill me for a maybe."
"We'd try." She smiled, and it didn't reach her eyes. "But I don't think it would come to that. The vessel wouldn't let you die. It needs a guardian too badly."
Kael considered his options. He could fight his way out—six opponents, all at least his stage, probably better trained. He could run, take the vessel and disappear into the Underworld. Or he could listen, learn more, and decide from a position of knowledge than desperation.
He was so tired of running.
"Fine," he said. "Talk. But I'm not promising anything."
Valdris nodded, and something like relief flickered across her face before she suppressed it. "The Six Orders maintain the seal network in shifts. Each Order is responsible for twelve seals, rotating monitoring duties every quarter-century. The Underworld isn't just a dungeon—it's a maintenance tunnel system, allowing access to the seals for inspection and reinforcement."
"I know the structure. I've been down here for weeks."
"Then you know the deeper levels are unstable. Seals weaken the closer you get to the Abyss." She gestured, and one of her companions—a silent woman with scars covering her arms—unrolled a map. "This is the current status of all seventy-two seals. Red indicates critical degradation. Yellow is concerning but stable. Green is secure."
Kael studied the map. The red dots were scattered across the network, but three stood out—clustered together in the deepest Underworld, where the veil between worlds grew thin as paper.
"The Rift of Endings. The Breach of Silence. The Wound of First Light." Valdris pointed to each in turn. "These three seals are failing simultaneously. The cult has been weakening them for months, maybe years. If they breach even one, the Orders can respond. If they breach all three at once—"
"The Orders get overwhelmed."
"The Orders get annihilated. And everything else goes with them."
Kael's mind raced. This was bigger than him. Bigger than Vane, bigger than Zarek's vendetta, bigger than proving his innocence or finding a safe haven. This was an apocalypse in progress, and he was standing at ground zero with a weapon he didn't understand and enemies on all sides.
"The Shadow Court," he said slowly. "They're involved."
Valdris's expression tightened. "You've encountered them."
"They're watching me. Have been for weeks."
"Not just watching. Recruiting." She moved closer, lowering her voice. "The Shadow Court is a deniable operations arm. They work for whoever pays, which means they work for everyone. Including cultists who want Soul Reavers under their control."
Kael thought of Lyra. Of her guilt, her fear, her desperate need to escape whatever she'd done in the Shadow Court's service. "They approach Soul Reavers specifically."
"Because you can harvest abilities. Because you can touch the dead and extract power that no one else can access. A Shadow Court controlled by Soul Reavers would have access to techniques lost for centuries. Secrets buried with their original owners." Valdris's voice hardened. "They're not protecting you, Kael. They're cataloging you. Learning your limits. Waiting for the right moment to make their move."
"And if the cult unseals the rifts?"
"Then the Shadow Court wins either way. They can sell to the highest bidder, or they can take what they want from the chaos." She paused. "The only ones who genuinely oppose them are the Orders. And right now, the Orders are tearing themselves apart."
The scholar spoke up again. "The corruption runs deep. We don't know how far it extends. There are Orders—maybe more than one—that have been compromised from within. The cult's reach is longer than we feared."
"Inquisitor Vane," Kael said. "He's hunting me. Is he part of this?"
"Unknown. Vane is a true believer in the Order of Dawn's mission. Whether that mission aligns with the cult's goals or the Orders' survival depends on who he's really working for." Valdris shrugged. "Welcome to the Underworld. Trust no one."
Kael turned back to the vessel. The shard of nothing pulsed gently, as if breathing. As if aware.
"I need time to think."
"You have until dawn." Valdris stepped back, her companions forming ranks around her. "After that, we take the vessel whether you're ready or not. And we deal with you But necessary."
"Generous."
"We can't afford generosity. Not anymore."
The six figures moved as one, retreating into the deeper tunnels of the Underworld. Their torchlight faded, leaving Kael alone in the chamber with the pedestal, the open box, and the weight of the world pressing down on his shoulders.
**[Soul Harvest: You extracted spiritual essence from the remains. 15 units acquired.]**
The notification brought him back to the present. He looked down at his hands, still holding the vessel, and realized he'd been touching the corpse of one of Valdris's people. He didn't remember grabbing it. Didn't remember harvesting.
The shard pulsed again, and Kael understood.
The vessel wasn't just a prison. It was a hunger. And it was feeding on him whether he wanted it to or not.
He closed the lid. The pull faded, leaving him gasping in the sudden quiet. Whatever the First Reaver had become, whatever the vessel demanded, Kael wasn't ready to pay that price. Not yet. Maybe not ever.
But the cult was coming. Valdris was coming. Vane was out there somewhere, tracking him through the fog-shrouded streets above. And the seals were failing, one by one, dragging the world toward an ending that even the Entities couldn't stop.
Kael Ashborn stood in the darkness beneath Thornwick, holding a weapon he couldn't use and a choice he wasn't ready to make.
Footsteps echoed from the tunnel behind him. Light flickered against the walls—torchlight, moving fast. Someone else had found the chamber.
Kael dropped into a crouch, blade ready, and waited.
A figure emerged from the shadows. Familiar build. Familiar gait. Familiar way of holding a weapon like an extension of the arm.
"Dorian." The name left Kael's mouth before he could stop it.
His former friend stepped into the torchlight, and Kael saw the insignia on his collar. The silver sunburst of the Order of Dawn. And beside it, barely visible, a symbol he'd seen before—on the cultists in the alley.
The symbol of the Unbound.
"Hello, Kael." Dorian's smile was cold, measured. "Long time no see."
Behind him, more footsteps. More light. Kael counted at least five others, maybe more, emerging from the darkness like ghosts.
"You sold me out." Kael's grip tightened on his blade. "Gwen's squad. You sent them into a trap."
Dorian's expression flickered—something like guilt, quickly suppressed. "I did what I had to do. You wouldn't understand."
"Try me."
"The cult needed a demonstration. A proof of concept. They needed to show the Orders that the seals were vulnerable." Dorian's voice was flat, rehearsed. "Gwen's squad was expendable. You were supposed to die with them. The Reaver classification was supposed to stay buried."
"But I didn't die."
"No. You didn't." Dorian drew his sword, and the others spread out, surrounding Kael with practiced efficiency. "So now we have a different problem. A much bigger problem."
Kael's mind raced. Six opponents minimum. Dorian, trained by the same masters, fighting at peak condition. The vessel heavy in his grip, its hunger a constant pressure. And behind him, Valdris's people, waiting to see what he'd decide.
"The cult wants the vessel." Kael kept his voice steady. "That's why you're here."
"The cult wants a lot of things. I want survival. There's a difference." Dorian advanced, his blade catching the torchlight. "Give me the box, Kael. Walk away. I can make sure they don't come after you."
"And if I don't?"
"Then I take it from your corpse." Dorian's smile returned, colder now. "I always was the better fighter. You just never wanted to admit it."
The footsteps behind Kael grew louder. Valdris's people, returning. Or more cultists, reinforcements for Dorian's ambush. Kael couldn't turn to check. Couldn't look away from the blade pointed at his chest.
"Last chance, Kael."
The vessel pulsed against his palms. The shard of nothing called to him, promising power, promising an end to all of this.
Kael closed his eyes.
Then he moved.
His blade took Dorian's strike on the diagonal, redirecting the force into a spin that carried him past his former friend's guard. He felt the edge slice across Dorian's arm, drawing blood, and kept moving. The first cultist on his left went down with a shattered kneecap. The second stumbled back, clutching a ruined hand.
"Kill him!" Dorian roared.
The chamber erupted into chaos. Steel rang against steel. Someone screamed. Kael harvested as he fought, pulling essence from every body that fell, feeding it into his reserves, buying seconds with stolen power. The vessel stayed closed, its hunger unsatisfied, and he was grateful for that small mercy.
Valdris's people hit Dorian's ambush from behind. The sound of combat shifted, doubled, became a cacophony of fury and desperation. Kael found himself fighting beside strangers, enemies, people who wanted him dead and people who wanted him alive, all of them blended into a single blur of survival.
A blade found his shoulder. He blocked the follow-up. Harvested from the corpse at his feet. Used the stolen essence to fuel a burst of speed that carried him through the press of bodies and out into the tunnel beyond.
Behind him, Dorian was shouting something. Valdris was shouting something else. The vessel was screaming in its box, hungry, demanding, needing.
Kael ran.
He ran until the sounds of battle faded. Ran until the tunnels twisted into passages he didn't recognize. Ran until he found a small chamber, barely larger than a closet, and collapsed against its wall.
**[Soul Harvest: You extracted spiritual essence from the remains. 142 units acquired.]**
The notification pulsed in his mind. More than he'd ever harvested in one fight. Enough to matter. Enough to change things.
But the cost—
Kael looked at his hands. They were shaking. Not from exertion. From the vessel, still clutched to his chest, still pulsing with that terrible hunger. The First Reaver had given part of himself to build this prison. Part of himself to maintain it.
How much of himself had he given?
The door to the chamber slammed open. Kael's blade rose on instinct—
"Kael! Stop!"
Sera stood in the doorway, breathing hard, her eyes wild. Behind her, the tunnel was dark and empty. She'd come alone.
"The cult," she gasped. "Dorian. I tracked them. I heard—" She stopped, looking at the vessel in his hands. "What is that?"
"Something I shouldn't have touched."
She crossed the chamber in three strides and grabbed his arm. Her fingers were cold, urgent. "Listen to me. The cult isn't just attacking the Underworld. They're attacking everywhere. My sources say three different cities are reporting seal breaches simultaneously. The Orders are overwhelmed. Whatever Dorian was trying to do here, it was just one piece of something bigger."
"Mass unsealing." Kael's voice sounded distant to his own ears. "Valdris told me."
"Valdris?" Sera's eyes narrowed. "The Keeper of the Sixth Seal? She's real? The legends said—"
"She's real. And she wants me to guard the vessel." Kael laughed again, that same brittle sound. "She says I'm the First Reaver's heir."
Sera went quiet. She looked at the vessel, then at Kael, then at the shadows pooling in the tunnel beyond.
"The First Reaver walked into the Abyss carrying that thing," she said slowly. "Everyone assumed he died. That the Entity consumed him. But if the vessel survived—"
"It survived."
"—then maybe he did too. Maybe he's still in there. Somewhere." She met Kael's eyes. "Maybe the First Reaver is waiting for someone to finish what he started."
The shard pulsed against Kael's palms. Hungry. Patient. Ancient.
"What happens if I open it again?"
Sera shook her head. "I don't know. No one knows. That's the problem with artifacts from the Age of First Seal. They're beyond the Orders' understanding. Beyond anyone's understanding." She gripped his arm tighter. "But I know this—if Dorian wanted it badly enough to ambush you in the Underworld, if the cult is attacking on multiple fronts, then the vessel is important. Important enough to kill for. Important enough to die for."
"Any chance you'd talk me out of keeping it?"
"Not a chance." Her smile was grim. "Besides, I have my own obligations. My sister is still alive, Kael. The Mortician's Guild is holding her. If the cult unseals the rifts, if the world burns, she burns too. I need the Orders to stay intact long enough to get her out."
"So we help them."
"We help ourselves. Same thing, different framing."
Kael looked at the vessel. At the hunger coiled inside it. At the weight of three hundred years of darkness pressing against his grip.
The First Reaver had been alone when he walked into the Abyss. No allies. No support. Just a weapon he couldn't use and a choice he couldn't escape.
Kael wasn't alone. Not anymore.
"Alright." He pushed himself to his feet, the vessel still clutched to his chest. "We survive tonight. We figure out what the cult is planning. And then—" He met Sera's eyes. "Then we find a way to use this thing without letting the monster inside free."
Sera nodded. "Sounds like a plan."
"Sounds like a suicide note."
"Same thing, different framing."
They moved into the tunnel together, leaving the chamber behind. Above them, the city of Thornwick burned with gaslight and conspiracy. Below them, the Abyss pressed against weakening seals, hungry for release. And in Kael's hands, the vessel of the First Reaver pulsed with a patience older than civilization.
Somewhere in the darkness, Dorian was regrouping. Valdris was planning. The cult was moving pieces into position for a play that had been centuries in the making.
And in the deepest Underworld, where no one had walked for three hundred years, something stirred in its prison.
Waiting.
Always waiting.
