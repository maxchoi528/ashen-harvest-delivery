The stench hit him first—copper and rot mixing in the fog-thick air.
Kael Ashborn pressed his back against the damp brick of an alley wall, fingers curling around the hilt of his blade. Three bodies lay sprawled across the cobblestones ahead, their uniforms torn and blackened. City Watch. Two of them still clutched their truncheons, though their hands had stopped responding hours ago.
"Hold position," Lyra's voice whispered from the rooftop above. Her shadow blended with the steam rising from a broken pipe. "Something's moving in the courtyard."
Kael didn't answer. He was counting the seconds, watching the pulse of residual energy emanating from the corpses. Recent deaths—within the last hour. The spiritual signature still crackled with fading life force, bright as candle flames in the darkness that only an Awakened could perceive.
A fourth body twitched.
Not a reflex. A deliberate movement. One of the fallen Watchmen raised his head, neck cracking at an impossible angle. His eyes had vanished, replaced by two pits of absolute nothing—doorways into a void that breathed cold against Kael's soul.
The thing wearing the corpse smiled. Bones grinding, it rose to its feet.
"Found you," it said in a voice that scraped like metal on stone. "The Whisper King's hounds have been searching for a long time."
Lyra dropped from the rooftop, landing in a crouch beside Kael. Her twin daggers gleamed with faint azure light—Order-forged weapons humming with trapped spiritual energy. "That's not a man anymore. That's an Abyssal Echo."
"I can see that." Kael's pulse hammered. The Echo wore the Watchman's body like a suit of rotting armor, flesh sloughing off with each movement. Behind it, shadows pooled unnaturally, stretching toward the alley mouth like hungry fingers reaching for prey.
Echoes were fragments—pieces of the 72 Entities that had slipped through weakened seals. They couldn't fully manifest in the Material without a vessel, which made possessed corpses their preferred method of operation. Kill the host, banish the fragment back through the Veil. Leave the body intact, and the Echo just found another.
The Whisper King. Kael had heard that title whispered in the Veiled Market's darkest corners—a cult leader who preached the "liberation" of the Entities from their prison. That name shouldn't mean anything to a common death-row prisoner. Except Kael wasn't common anymore. And someone had been hunting him since his Awakening.
"Two more coming," Lyra reported, her eyes tracking movement in the fog. "Left alley, right alley. We're boxed."
The Echo-touched corpse tilted its head, studying them with those hollow voids. "The Soul Reaver. You carry something that was stolen long ago. Return it, and the King offers mercy."
"I'm not in the habit of negotiating with hollow shells."
"No?" The Echo's mouth stretched too wide, splitting the face in a grotesque parody of amusement. "Then you will learn the cost of defiance."
It lunged.
Kael moved on instinct—months of survival in Thornwick's underbelly had honed his reflexes past the point of conscious thought. His blade carved an arc through the fog, striking the possessed corpse across the midsection. Flesh parted, but no blood flowed. Instead, viscous shadow poured from the wound, reforming even as the Echo staggered.
"Physical weapons won't kill it!" Lyra's daggers intersected with a second Echo that had crept too close, the azure light searing black marks into its spectral form. It shrieked—a sound that existed somewhere between hearing and memory—and recoiled.
Kael's mind raced. Order-trained Awakened carried blessed weapons, soul-essence grenades, binding sigils. He had none of those. He had a stolen blade, a handful of copper coins, and the thing coiled in his chest that marked him as an abomination in human skin.
The Soul Reaver. A power that should have died with its original owner centuries ago. A power that let him reach into the dying and pull out pieces of their souls—skills, memories, fragments of fate itself. It cost him nothing spiritually. The world, though, charged a different price.
The Echo reset its stance, shadows coiling around its ruined hands like gloves of living night. "Resisting only delays the inevitable. The seals weaken. The King's awakening approaches. You cannot—"
"Sorry," Kael interrupted, "I don't have time for villain monologues."
He darted forward, not toward the Echo, but past it. His target wasn't the possessed corpse. His target was the corpse behind the Echo—the actual Watchman who had died here. The one still bleeding onto the cobblestones.
The Echo realized his intent a heartbeat too late. It whirled, reaching for him with fingers that trailed void-stuff, but Kael was already sliding across the slick stones. He hit his knees beside the fallen Watchman, ignoring the blood soaking through his trousers, and slammed his palm against the cooling flesh.
The world inverted.
Colors bled away. Sound became texture. Kael felt himself plunge into the residue of a dying soul—not the Echo's borrowed presence, but the man's own fading essence. Thomas Rensen. Forty-three years old. Twenty years on the Watch. A wife in Millhaven. Three children. A mortgage he'll never pay.
And beneath it all, buried in the final moments of consciousness: the memory of hooded figures emerging from the fog. The flash of a ritual circle drawn in salt and ash. The words "The seal requires blood" spoken by a voice he trusted.
Soul Harvest: You extracted Combat Reflexes from the remains. 3 vitality acquired. Warning: Fragmentary memory extracted.
Kael's body jerked as the harvest completed, new data flooding his neural pathways. His muscles snapped taut, reactions sharpening as the Watchman's decades of training merged with his own desperate instincts. He rolled clear of a shadow-claw strike, came up with his blade ready, and felt the difference immediately.
Faster. Stronger. Better.
The Echo recoiled. It had sensed what he'd done—what he was. "Abomination. Soul Reaver. The King's warnings spoke of your kind."
"Your king can file a complaint." Kael's voice came out steadier than he felt. "I'll be sure to ignore it."
He struck.
The Watchman's borrowed reflexes guided his blade in ways his own body had never learned. He carved through the possessed flesh with precision that bordered on brutal efficiency, each cut targeting the shadow-core at the entity's center. The Echo screamed as its vessel disintegrated, void-stuff pouring from wounds that finally, catastrophically, refused to seal.
"Lyra! The others!"
"On it!"
Her daggers sang through the fog, finding the two additional Echoes that had circled behind them. Azure fire erupted where blade met shadow, the Order-forged weapons channeling enough spiritual energy to banish the fragments back to the Abyss. Kael watched the darkness curl and compress, shrinking into pinpoints before vanishing entirely.
Silence fell over the courtyard. The fog crept back, thick and grey, swallowing the evidence of their battle.
Lyra stood over the smoking remains of one Echo, breathing hard. Her left arm hung at an odd angle—dislocated, probably, from that final strike. She didn't seem to notice. "That's the third Echo sighting this week. Something's definitely wrong with the seals."
"Tell me something I don't know." Kael wiped his blade clean on his coat, then crouched beside another fallen Watchman. This one still had flesh on her bones—young, maybe twenty, with training scars on her knuckles and a look of surprise frozen on her face. The Echo that had worn her had been banishment-class, leaving almost nothing behind.
But almost wasn't nothing.
He touched her hand.
Soul Harvest: You extracted Spirit Sight (Minor) from the remains. 7 vitality acquired.
A new sensation bloomed behind his eyes—not entirely sight, but the suggestion of it. He could perceive spiritual energy now, not just feel it. The residual auras of the dead flickered like dying embers around him. The Echo's departure had left scorch marks in the local spiritual atmosphere. And far above, too high to see but inexplicably present, a lattice of light and power that could only be one thing.
The seal network. The ancient bindings holding the 72 Entities in place.
He could see them. See how some flickered brighter than others. See how the one directly above Thornwick—the seal corresponding to whatever entity called the Whisper King master—pulsed with sickly, irregular rhythms.
"You're staring at the sky," Lyra observed, approaching with her good hand pressed against her injured shoulder. A sharp twist and a grunt later, the joint popped back into place. "What do you see?"
"The seals." Kael rose, blinking away the new overlay of perception. "Or at least, one of them. It's not doing well."
Lyra's expression hardened. She was Shadow Court trained, which meant she knew exactly what degrading seals meant for the world at large. "How bad?"
"Bad enough that cultists are walking around possessing City Watch like it's a hobby." He gestured toward the corpse he'd harvested first. "This one had memories. Thomas Rensen. He saw the people who did this. Said they mentioned something about seals needing blood."
"Fresh sacrifices." Lyra's jaw tightened. "They're deliberately weakening specific points. A few more killings, and that seal fails entirely."
"And whatever the Whisper King wants to pull through comes with him."
They stood in silence for a moment, the burden of the situation settling between them. This wasn't random violence. This wasn't even organized crime. This was the opening moves of a war that the general public didn't know was being waged beneath their feet.
"We need to tell someone," Lyra said finally. "The Orders. The Council. Anyone who—"
"And tell them what?" Kael cut her off. "That a death-row prisoner with a stolen power has been seeing Abyssal activity? That the cult specifically called me out by name? I'm already on three watch lists, Lyra. If I walk into an Order enclave claiming to have information about seal degradation, I'll be detained before I finish my sentence."
"The Order of Dawn has a outpost in Fogmire. Commander Vance—"
"Vance reports to the Council of Six, which means half my message gets buried in political maneuvering while the cultists finish their work." Kael ran a hand through his hair, dislodging clots of dried blood. "No. We handle this ourselves. At least until we know what we're dealing with."
Lyra looked like she wanted to argue. For a long moment, the only sound was the hiss of steam pipes and the distant clatter of a late-night carriage. Then she exhaled, the tension draining from her shoulders.
"You have a plan."
"I have the beginning of a plan." Kael retrieved his blade from the ground where he'd dropped it, sheathing it with practiced ease. "The cult needs sacrifices to weaken the seals. That means rituals. Rituals need preparation. Preparation requires supplies, locations, personnel. Which means organization."
"And you want to find where they're organizing."
"I want to find where they're *meeting*. Get close enough to harvest one of them, get memories of their operation, and figure out what the Whisper King is truly planning." He met her eyes steadily. "It's risky. Probably suicidal. But it's better than waiting for the sky to fall."
Lyra studied him for a long moment—assessing, calculating. The Shadow Court had trained her to weigh risks against rewards, to see patterns in chaos. Whatever she found in Kael's expression must have satisfied her, because she nodded slowly.
"There's a man in the Veiled Market. Calls himself Whisper. He deals in information—sees everything that moves in Thornwick's underground. If anyone knows about cult activity, it's him."
"Information costs money."
"You harvested vitality from those corpses. Vital essence can be converted at the right vendor."
Kael grimaced. The idea of selling his harvest proceeds felt like betraying the dead twice over. But Thomas Rensen and the young Watchman were beyond caring about where their extracted essence went. And if their deaths could prevent more deaths—could stop whatever horror the Whisper King was engineering—then maybe that was payment enough.
"Fine. We find this Whisper, buy what he knows, and take it from there."
Lyra's lips curved in a thin smile. "Look at us. Becoming heroes."
"Please. I'm just trying to survive long enough to find out who's been framing me for crimes I didn't commit." Kael stepped over the corpse of the Watchman he'd harvested, careful not to look down at her face. "And you're trying to escape your past. Heroes we are not."
"Define 'hero,'" Lyra murmured, falling into step beside him as they moved toward the alley mouth. "Because from where I'm standing, you're about to—"
"English."
She blinked. "Sorry. Force of habit. I was going to say you're about to walk into a hornet's nest of cultists to stop something terrible from happening. That's pretty heroic by most definitions."
"It's stupid is what it is." But Kael didn't argue further. He was too busy cataloguing the new sensations flooding his awareness. Spirit Sight painted the world in layers—physical, spiritual, the shimmering lattice of seals above, the dark threads of Abyssal corruption below. It was like seeing behind a curtain he'd never known existed.
The fog parted around them as they emerged onto Marshgate Street, gas lamps casting pools of sickly yellow light across the wet cobblestones. A few late-night pedestrians hurried past, pulling their coats tight against the chill. None of them looked at the alley they were leaving. None of them wanted to know what had happened there.
That was Thornwick. That was humanity. Ignore the horror until it directly affects you, then panic when it's too late.
"Hey." Lyra's hand closed on his arm, stopping him. "You good? That last harvest looked... Intense."
Kael considered the question. Physically, he felt fine—the vitality boost from the harvesting had left him energized fairly than drained. Mentally, the fragments of Thomas Rensen's memories still swirled at the edge of his consciousness, not yet fully integrated. Emotionally...
"I'm functional," he said. "That's enough for now."
"It won't be, eventually. The memories pile up. The dead start whispering." Lyra's voice dropped low. "I know what it's like to carry other people's ghosts. If you need to talk—"
"I don't."
The words came out harsher than intended. Kael softened his tone, adding, "I appreciate it. Really. But right now, I need to focus on the mission. The rest..." He let the sentence hang.
Lyra nodded, releasing his arm. "The Veiled Market's three blocks east. We should move before the Watch arrives. Or worse."
"Worse?"
"Shadow Court observers. If they noticed those Echoes being banishment, they'll have reported it. Someone might come looking for the source."
Kael didn't ask what kind of someone. The Shadow Court's deniable operations arm operated on principles of plausible deniability that made the cult's activities look straightforward by comparison. If they decided Kael was a threat, he wouldn't see the blade that killed him.
They moved through the fog-shrouded streets at a pace just short of running—fast enough to cover ground, slow enough to avoid drawing attention. Kael's new Spirit Sight painted the city in shades of meaning he was still learning to interpret. Most of the people they passed registered as dim grey flickers, spiritually inert. A few blazed with the distinctive signatures of Awakened, though whether friend or foe remained to be seen.
One signature drew his attention—a dense knot of spiritual energy pulsing from a building ahead. It felt wrong inexplicably, like looking at a wound that refused to close. The essence there was corrupted, tainted by something that didn't belong in the Material realm.
"Lyra."
She was already stopping, her own Awakened senses clearly picking up the same anomaly. "I feel it. What is that?"
"A meeting. Or a ritual. Something's happening inside." Kael crouched beside a steam grate, letting the mist curl around him. The corrupted energy signature pulsed in rhythmic waves, each surge accompanied by a subsonic vibration that resonated in his chest like a second heartbeat.
"That building." Lyra pointed to a former textile warehouse, its windows boarded up and its facade marked with the faded sigils of abandoned industry. "That's the old Blackthorn factory. Closed down after the Vein Collapse three years ago."
"Perfect place for illegal cult activity."
"Almost too perfect." Lyra's hand drifted toward her daggers. "We could report this to—"
"And miss our chance to harvest some answers?" Kael was already moving, circling toward the building's side entrance. "By the time the Orders send someone to investigate, the cultists will be long gone. But if we can get close enough..."
"You want to infiltrate a cult ritual."
"I want to harvest a cultist." Kael's voice carried no hesitation. "One of them has to know something. Schedules, locations, the Whisper King's plans. Better to take it from them directly than trust information filtered through bureaucrats."
Lyra caught up with him, her expression caught between admiration and concern. "You're insane."
"Probably. But I'm also right."
The side entrance was locked—physically, not spiritually. Kael waited until a pair of Watch patrols passed the intersection, then produced a thin metal pick from his boot. Thomas Rensen's memories surfaced unbidden, showing him the technique with the ease of long practice. The lock clicked open in seconds.
Inside, the warehouse smelled of dust and old cloth and something else beneath—incense, maybe, or the metallic tang of spilled blood. Kael let Spirit Sight expand, mapping the interior in shades of corrupted gold and sickly green. He could see them now, a dozen figures arranged in a circle around a central point where the floor had been cleared of debris.
A ritual circle. Salt and ash and something darker, traced in patterns that hurt to look at directly.
"They're in the middle of something," he breathed. "We can't interrupt without triggering whatever defenses they've prepared."
"So we wait?"
"We position. When it's done, when they're dispersing, we take one from the edge." Kael gestured toward a shadowed alcove near the building's far wall. "From there, we can see who comes and goes. And if opportunity presents itself..."
"Harvest first, ask questions never."
"Something like that."
They slipped inside, moving through the warehouse's skeletal remains of machinery and forgotten inventory. Kael's Spirit Sight tracked the ritual's progress—a steady build of corrupted energy focusing toward a central point where the air itself seemed to tear. Not a full rift, not yet. But the preparing of one. The ritual was designed to weaken a seal, to punch a temporary hole in the Veil through which greater power could flow.
The Whisper King was building toward something massive. And this gathering was just one node in a larger network.
"Kael." Lyra's whisper cut through his concentration. "Look."
One of the cultists had stepped forward from the circle, raising his arms in a gesture of supplication. He wore no hood, revealing a face that Kael recognized instantly—not personally, but from wanted posters and whispered warnings in the Veiled Market's darkest corners.
Abyssal cultist. High priest. Known associate of Prime Zarek.
The man who had ordered the death of Gwen's squad. The man who had framed Kael for crimes he didn't commit. The man who had set this entire nightmare in motion.
Zarek stood at the center of the ritual, his voice rising in words that existed somewhere outside human language, and he began to chant.
The corrupted energy surged. The Veil trembled. And in that moment of maximum focus, neither Zarek nor any of his followers noticed the two figures watching from the shadows—waiting, watching, preparing to strike.
The ritual reached its crescendo. Light blazed from the circle's center, and somewhere far above, Kael felt a seal shudder under pressure it wasn't designed to withstand. Then the energy dispersed, the chant ended, and the cultists began to mill about in the aftermath of their success.
"This is it," Lyra breathed. "They're about to scatter."
Kael nodded, his hand closing around his blade. One cultist on the circle's edge was already moving toward the exit, peeling away from the group with the nervous energy of someone eager to escape. Young. Low-ranking. Vulnerable.
The perfect target.
"Stay here," Kael whispered. "Watch my back. If this goes wrong—"
"Don't be a martyr. Just get the harvest and get out."
He was already moving, slipping through the shadows with borrowed reflexes guiding each step. The cultist hadn't seen him—hadn't even conceived of the possibility that someone might be watching. He was reaching for the door when Kael's hand closed over his mouth and a blade pressed against his throat.
"Don't scream," Kael murmured against his ear. "Don't struggle. Just come with me quietly, and maybe I'll let you keep your tongue."
The cultist's eyes went wide with terror. He tried to speak, to cry out, but the blade bit into flesh just enough to remind him of the stakes. Kael dragged him backward into the deeper shadows, past Lyra's watchful position, toward a corner where the darkness was thick enough to hide murder.
"Please," the cultist whimpered. "Please, I haven't done anything, I just watched, I didn't—"
"You joined a cult trying to break the seals holding back the Abyss." Kael's voice was flat, clinical. "That makes you complicit. That makes you harvestable."
He touched the man's forehead.
Soul Harvest: You extracted Ritual Knowledge (Fragmentary) from the remains. 12 vitality acquired. Memory acquired: Cult meeting locations, hand signs, current operational objectives.
Information flooded through Kael's consciousness—the cultist's memories playing out in rapid succession. Meetings in the sewers beneath the old docks. Hand signals passed during Market transactions. The location of a safe house in the Ashwick district. And most damning of all: the identity of the Whisper King's lieutenant currently coordinating the Thornwick operation.
Dorian Vane. A name that made Kael's blood run cold. Because Dorian was an Awakened. A former member of the Order of Dawn who had supposedly died in the Great Rift incident fifteen years ago.
Dorian was alive. And he was leading the cult's assault on the seal network.
Kael released the unconscious cultist, letting him slump to the ground. His mind churned with implications. If Dorian was involved, this wasn't just some fringe cult's desperate bid for power. This was an inside job. Someone with deep knowledge of the seal network, of Order protocols, of Thornwick's spiritual infrastructure.
Someone the Orders had thought was dead.
"Kael." Lyra's voice cut through his reverie. "We need to move. The ritual's finished, and people are leaving."
"Right. Right." He forced himself to focus, to push the revelations into a mental compartment he could examine later. "The safe house in Ashwick. We know where it is now."
"We know where they meet. That doesn't mean we can take them."
"No. But it's a start." Kael checked his blade, his hands steady despite the adrenaline coursing through him. "We gather more information. We build a picture of their operation. And then we find a way to burn it all down."
Lyra's expression flickered with something unreadable. "And Zarek? You saw him. The man who framed you."
Kael's jaw tightened. The anger was there, coiled in his chest like a serpent. But he forced it down, forced himself to think strategically. "Zarek's the head. Removing him might cripple the operation—or it might just create a power vacuum that someone worse fills. We need to understand the whole structure before we start making cuts."
"That's the smart play."
"That doesn't mean I like it."
They slipped out the way they'd come, leaving the unconscious cultist slumped in the shadows. By the time anyone found him, Kael and Lyra would be long gone—disappearing into Thornwick's endless maze of fog and steam and secrets.
The night air hit Kael's face as they emerged onto the street, cold and sharp and tasting of coal smoke. Somewhere above, the lattice of seals pulsed with uneven rhythms. Somewhere below, the cultists were already planning their next move.
And somewhere in Thornwick, a dead man named Dorian Vane was orchestrating an apocalypse.
"We need allies," Lyra said as they walked. "People with resources, knowledge, access."
"Anyone in particular?"
"Commander Vance at the Order of Dawn outpost. Gwen, if we can find her. Maybe the Mortician's Guild—they have contacts everywhere, and they hate the cult for disrupting the soul trade."
Kael considered the options. The Order would be suspicious of him at best. Gwen might be reachable, but she'd need convincing. The Morticians dealt in death, which made them natural allies for someone who harvested from it.
"The Veiled Market first," he decided. "We buy what Whisper knows, then we plan our next move."
"And after that?"
"After that, we start picking apart the Whisper King's operation. One thread at a time. Until we find out what he really wants. And then we take it away from him."
Lyra laughed—a short, sharp sound with no humor in it. "You know, when I agreed to help you clear your name, I didn't expect to end up trying to save the world."
"Save the city. The world has six Orders to handle the big stuff."
"And we're just two Awakened with stolen powers and too many enemies."
"Three Awakened," Kael corrected. "Don't forget the Soul Reaver thing. That's a third awakening, technically."
Lyra stared at him for a moment, then shook her head. "You're impossible."
"So I've been told."
They walked on, two figures swallowed by Thornwick's eternal fog. Behind them, the warehouse stood silent, its secrets hidden behind boarded windows and forgotten industry. Ahead, the city waited with all its dangers and opportunities, its corruption and its fragile, stubborn hope.
Kael checked his spiritual reserves—still full, still ready. The harvests had cost him nothing except time and contact with the dead. Thomas Rensen's reflexes were his now. The Watchman's Spirit Sight painted the world in new dimensions. And somewhere in his mind, the cultist's fragmentary knowledge simmered, waiting to be examined.
He had threads. He had leads. He had enemies coming out of every shadow.
But for the first time since his Awakening, he also had a plan.
The fog closed around them as they disappeared into Thornwick's depths, heading toward the Veiled Market and whatever answers waited there. And above, unseen by mortal eyes, the seals continued to pulse with rhythms that grew more irregular by the hour—a countdown no one had thought to measure, toward an awakening that would change everything.
The Whisper King's game had begun.
And Kael Ashborn had decided to play.
Footsteps echoed from the alley behind them. Kael spun, blade drawn, Spirit Sight flaring as he tried to identify the approaching figure. The spiritual signature was faint—deliberately suppressed—but present. Someone who knew how to hide from Awakened perception.
Someone dangerous.
"Kael." Lyra's hand found his arm. "Don't."
The footsteps stopped. A figure stepped out of the fog—a woman, tall and angular, with silver-streaked hair and eyes that held the flat, calculating look of someone who had seen too much to be surprised by anything.
"Gwen." The name escaped Kael's lips before he consciously recognized her. "What are you doing here?"
Gwen Marsh—former Order of Dawn ranger, current ghost in Thornwick's underworld, and the only person besides Lyra who knew the truth about what Kael had done to earn his death sentence. She stepped closer, her gaze moving between them with unsettling precision.
"Following you. Watching you take down those Echoes." Her voice was quiet, almost conversational. "Impressive work. But sloppy. You've drawn attention—more than you realize."
"What kind of attention?"
Gwen's expression didn't change, but something in her eyes flickered. "The Shadow Court knows what you are, Kael. They've known since the night you Awakened. And they're not the only ones."
The fog swirled around them, thick and cold. Kael felt the burden of unseen gazes pressing against his awareness—observer after observer, each watching, each waiting for something.
"You need to disappear," Gwen continued. "Tonight. The Whisper King's people are already moving. And when they find you—and they will find you—the entire Abyssal host will descend on Thornwick to claim what's rightfully theirs."
"What's rightfully theirs?"
Gwen's lips curved in a thin, humorless smile. "The Soul Reaver, Kael. It's not just a power. It's a key. And you're standing in the lock."
She turned to leave, then paused.
"The Veiled Market. Whisper's information will cost more than you can pay. But I can get you in." She glanced back over her shoulder. "Midnight. The Iron Pot. Come alone, or don't come at all."
She vanished into the fog before either of them could respond.
Lyra exhaled slowly. "Well. That's not ominous at all."
Kael sheathed his blade, mind racing. Gwen had saved his life once—had helped him escape the Order's custody after his Awakening. But she was Shadow Court, trained to manipulate and maneuver, to see everyone as a piece on a board she was always arranging.
Her help came with strings. They always did.
"Midnight," he said. "The Iron Pot."
"You believe her?"
"I don't have a choice." Kael started walking again, setting his course toward the Market district. "We need information. She's offering a path to it. If it's a trap, we'll handle it. If it's not, we survive another day."
"And if the Whisper King's people find us first?"
He didn't answer. The fog swallowed his silence, leaving only the sound of footsteps on wet cobblestones and the distant rumble of steam engines driving Thornwick's endless mechanical heart.
Somewhere in the darkness, someone was watching. Waiting. Preparing.
The game was still being played.
And Kael was running out of moves.
