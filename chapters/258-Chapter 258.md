The blade found Kael's ribs before he registered the footsteps.
Pain lanced through his side as he twisted, steel grinding against bone. His attacker's face emerged from the smoke—young, gaunt, eyes like chips of obsidian reflecting the burning warehouse around them.
"You're the one they call the Reaver." The woman's voice carried no tremor. "I've been hunting you for three weeks."
Kael's hand closed around her wrist. "Bad move."
He pulled.
The woman's eyes went wide. Her legs buckled, knees cracking against the soot-blackened floor. A scream tore from her throat—raw, animal, the sound of something essential being ripped away.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Fragmented Memory (Quality: Moderate) from the remains. Spiritual Essence +12 acquired.]
She collapsed forward, still breathing but empty. Kael caught her before she hit the ground, lowering the body with unexpected gentleness. A Soul Reaver, then. She carried the taint of it—faint traces of harvested essence beneath her skin like oil beneath water. Young, maybe nineteen. Powerful for her age. Stupid for her choices.
Smoke burned Kael's lungs. The warehouse groaned around them, support beams surrendering to flames that licked at crates of condemned alchemical supplies. He'd come here chasing rumors of cultist activity. Found something worse instead.
"What—" The woman stirred, fingers scrabbling at his coat. "What did you take?"
"Your shortcut to power." Kael stood, checking the wound at his ribs. Shallow. She'd been going for his kidney. "How many?"
"Go to hell."
"Already did. Woke up." He crouched again, studying her face. "You're working for someone. Who?"
The woman's laugh turned into a cough. "You think you're the only one?"
Around them, the fire spread. Outside, bells began to toll—city watch responding to the flames. Kael needed to move, but the woman's words gnawed at him. She spoke like someone who knew something.
"The Shadow Court?" he pressed.
Her eyes flickered. A tell.
"They monitoring me specifically, or every Reaver in Thornwick?"
Silence. Then: "You don't even know what you are."
Kael pulled the woman to her feet, keeping her between himself and the nearest window. Glass exploded inward a heartbeat later. Shrapnel hissed through the air, embedding in wood and brick. Kael shoved her behind him, feeling the blade wound protest at the movement.
A figure dropped through the gap—silent, precise, gear-clad boots touching down without sound. Hooded cloak. Segmented staff held in a reverse grip. The symbol on their shoulder: a circle bisected by a wavy line.
Order of the Veil. Information brokers. Assassins when the price was right.
"Your harvest registered on our sensors." The figure's voice came modulated through a vocer mask. "Step away from the asset."
"Asset." Kael's grip tightened on the woman. "She's a person."
"She was. Now she's a resource." The Veil operative advanced, staff spinning lazily. "The Soul Reaver phenomenon presents unique challenges to regional stability. You've been flagged for recruitment or termination."
"And her?"
"Collateral recovery."
The woman Kael held laughed bitterly. "They're going to harvest me. You think you're different?"
The Veil operative's mask betrayed nothing. "The Orders require raw soul-essence to maintain the seal network. Legal harvesting produces insufficient yield. Unregistered Reavers represent both threat and opportunity."
"And if I refuse to hand her over?"
"Then we have a problem."
Kael weighed his options. One wounded Reaver with depleted reserves. One Veil operative, stage unknown but competent. Fire closing in from all sides. The woman he'd just reaved struggling to stay conscious in his grip.
"What if I offered an alternative?"
The operative paused. "Speak."
"The Veiled Market deals in soul-essence. Legal exchange, even if the source isn't." Kael kept his voice level. "I know suppliers. Middlemen who work the docks, the Underbelly, the places the Mortician's Guild doesn't touch."
"You would betray your own kind?"
"She tried to kill me. Nearly succeeded." Kael looked down at the woman in his arms. "Her memories are fragmented already—I didn't get much. But I got enough to know she's connected to something bigger. Cult activity. Thornwick specifically."
The Veil operative's posture shifted. Interest, maybe, or calculation.
"The Six Orders have discussed the resurgence pattern for months. Cultists growing bolder. Rifts destabilizing faster than projected." The staff lowered a fraction. "Your intelligence has value."
"My intelligence comes from harvesting. It's not clean, but it works." Kael stepped forward, dragging the woman with him. "Let me walk out of here. I'll feed you what I learn. Names, locations, patterns. You get your asset either way—she's empty anyway, no combat capability left."
"And the Reaver phenomenon? Your kind destabilizes the economy. Drives prices down. Disrupts carefully maintained supply chains."
"My kind keeps the seals from cracking while your Orders fight amongst themselves over doctrine and territory."
Silence stretched. Flames crackled. Somewhere distant, a building collapsed.
"Initial contact established," the Veil operative finally said. "Delivery coordinates will reach you within three days. Fail to appear, and you become a termination priority."
The figure moved—not toward Kael but toward the window, staff disappearing into their cloak. They paused at the edge.
"The woman. Her name is Vex. She was harvesting for the Crimson Chalice before she decided to freelance. That should tell you something about the current state of your competition."
Then they were gone, swallowed by smoke and darkness.
Kael exhaled. His hands trembled—from blood loss, he told himself, not from the harvest still echoing through his nerves. The woman—Vex—hung limp in his grip, breathing shallow.
He needed to move.
The back of the warehouse had collapsed entirely, creating an exit into the narrow alley beyond. Kael dragged Vex through the debris, glass and metal scraping against his coat. The night air hit him like a slap—cold, thick with the stench of the Thornwick slums.
He dumped Vex against a wall and searched her pockets. A handful of coins, mostly copper. A vial of murky liquid—probably diluted soul-essence, maybe three weeks' worth for a common Awakened. A folded paper.
Kael unfolded it with numb fingers. A list of names, some crossed out, others circled. Locations scattered across Thornwick's district map. And at the bottom, circled three times in red ink: **The Ossuary. Midnight. Third lunar fall.**
"Looking for something?"
Kael spun. Another figure stepped from the shadows—but this one moved differently. No weapon visible. No aggressive stance. Just a woman in servant's garb, face half-hidden by a hood, standing with the particular stillness of someone who expected to be noticed and wasn't particularly worried about it.
"Lady Seren," Kael said.
"Mr. Ashborn." The woman pushed back her hood, revealing features too fine for her clothing. "You hunt well. The Order of the Veil does too, when properly motivated."
"You're the Shadow Court's handler."
"I ensure communication channels remain open." Seren smiled thinly. "The Court has interest in your... Career trajectory."
"I'm not joining."
"No one is asking you to join. The Court serves many masters, Mr. Ashborn. Some of them would be pleased to see you dead. Others see value in your continued existence." She glanced at Vex's crumpled form. "The girl's faction represents a threat to that existence. She wasn't working alone."
"I figured."
"Crimson Chalice, yes. But also something else. Something older." Seren reached into her robes and withdrew a small package wrapped in oilcloth. "A gift. Consider it an investment."
Kael didn't take it. "In exchange for what?"
"Nothing. Yet." She set the package on a nearby crate. "Inside you will find documentation. Locations of known cult activity. Names of individuals who have recently vanished. And a single fragment of soul-essence extracted from an Abyssal Entity—one of the 72, sealed centuries ago but weakening."
"Entity-touched essence. Priceless."
"Or damning, depending on perspective." Seren stepped back into the shadows. "The Crimson Chalice seeks to unseal the rifts. The Shadow Court seeks to prevent that outcome. Your interests and ours align—for now."
"And if they stop aligning?"
"Then we become another enemy." Her voice came from everywhere and nowhere. "The world has many enemies, Mr. Ashborn. Choose which ones you can afford."
Then she was gone, leaving only the smell of jasmine and the package sitting on the crate.
Kael stared at the oilcloth wrapping. His instincts screamed caution. The Shadow Court played games within games, using pawns without caring if those pawns survived. But the information could be valuable. Could help him track down Zarek, find answers about his own nature, understand what he was becoming.
He grabbed the package and tore it open.
Inside: papers covered in cramped handwriting. A map of Thornwick with locations circled in red. A list of names, including three he recognized from previous investigations. And the vial of essence—it glowed faintly, pulsing with colors that had no business existing in the visible spectrum.
The essence called to him. Not, but in the way a flame called to a moth. The temptation to harvest it, to take what power it offered, pulled at something deep in his chest.
[Soul Harvest Available: Entity-Touched Essence detected. Extraction would yield significant power gain. Warning: Entity-touched essence carries corruption risk. Quality: Exceptional.]
Kael's hand hovered over the vial. He could feel the entity within—vast, patient, waiting. It had been sealed for centuries. It knew things. Remembered things. Things about the first Soul Reaver, maybe. About where the ability came from. About what he was supposed to become.
His fingers closed around the glass.
Pain lanced up his arm.
Not physical—something deeper. The entity within the essence recognized him. Felt the Soul Reaver power coiled in his soul and responded with something that might have been hunger, might have been joy, might have been a greeting from something that had waited too long for contact.
Images flashed through his mind. A tomb beneath a mountain. Chains of light binding something vast. A figure standing over a body, hands pressed to cooling flesh, drawing out the last threads of existence. A voice, ancient and tired: *Take it. Take it all. Someone must carry this burden.*
Kael wrenched his hand back. The vial cracked but held.
His heart hammered against his ribs. The wound from Vex's blade had stopped bleeding, but new pain radiated from his palm where the essence had touched him. Something had changed. Something in his perception of the world had shifted.
He could see it now—the layer beneath the layer. Spirits clinging to the warehouse ruins, wisps of residual energy from the dead and dying. The great threads of power running through Thornwick's foundations, the ley lines that fed the seal network. And above it all, a darkness pressing down like a weight, vast and patient, waiting for something to give.
The Veil had thinned. Not everywhere, but here, in this moment, in this place where an entity's essence had touched his flesh. For a heartbeat, Kael glimpsed what lay beyond: the Abyss itself, crawling with shapes that defied geometry, entities older than humanity moving through dimensions humans couldn't perceive.
The 72. All of them. All waiting.
The vision snapped shut. Kael gasped, aware of how long he'd been standing motionless. The sky had shifted. Stars wheeling overhead indicated hours had passed, not minutes.
His palm bore a mark now—a small circle bisected by a wavy line, burned into his flesh like a brand.
He didn't recognize the symbol.
But Vex did. When he looked down, he found her staring at his hand with an expression caught between terror and awe.
"You touched it," she whispered. "The thing in the vial. You touched it."
"It burned me."
"That's not—" She struggled to sit up, wincing at the movement. "That's not what happens. When you touch Entity essence directly, you should die. The corruption should consume you. That's why no one harvests them directly."
"Maybe I'm different."
"You're not." Vex's voice shook. "You're exactly the same. That's what scares me."
"What do you mean, exactly the same?"
She looked away. "The first one. The original Soul Reaver. They found a way to touch Entity essence without being consumed. Harvested power directly from the Abyss itself. That's where the ability came from—stolen from something that shouldn't exist in our world."
"And what happened to them?"
"I don't know. No one does." Vex's laugh held no humor. "But I've seen the records. Partial, censored, buried under centuries of classified protocols. The original Reaver wasn't destroyed. They were... Transformed. Changed into something else. Something the Orders couldn't control."
Kael stared at his palm. The brand pulsed faintly, a second heartbeat he couldn't control.
"You're saying I'm becoming like them."
"I'm saying you're already like them. The question is whether you know what that means." She met his gaze. "The Crimson Chalice isn't just trying to unseal the rifts. They're trying to bring back the original. They think a Soul Reaver touched by Entity essence could serve as an anchor—keep the door open while they pour Abyssal power through."
"And you? What were you trying to do?"
"I was trying to stop them." Vex's voice hardened. "I harvested my first corpse when I was fourteen. Stealing power from the dead. I told myself it was survival. Then I told myself it was justice. Then I told myself it was necessary." She looked at the ruins of the warehouse. "Now I'm not sure what I believe."
"You tried to kill me."
"You interfered with my operation. Three cultists escaped because you spooked them the week before. I needed to send a message."
"Charming."
"The world isn't charming, Mr. Ashborn. It's ugly and broken and full of people doing ugly, broken things because they can't think of another way." She struggled to her feet, swaying. "But you—you could change things. If you survive long enough. If you don't become what they're trying to make you."
Kael watched her stumble into the darkness. He should stop her. Extract more information. Force answers from her fragmented memories.
Instead, he looked at the vial in his other hand.
The entity within pulsed. Waiting. Patient. It had waited centuries. It could wait a little longer.
But Kael couldn't. The Conspiracy was accelerating. The Crimson Chalice moved with purpose, coordinating cult activity across Thornwick. The Shadow Court watched and waited, playing games with pieces they didn't fully understand. The Orders prepared for a war that might never come—or that might end everything if it did.
And somewhere in the background, the Whisper King pulled strings that stretched back generations.
Kael tucked the vial into his coat. The papers Seren had provided would require careful study. The mark on his palm would require careful hiding.
He turned toward the distant lights of central Thornwick. Dawn would come in a few hours. The city would wake, oblivious to the wars being fought in shadows and silence.
His phone buzzed. A message from an unknown number: coordinates and a time. Three days. The Veil wanted their meeting.
And beneath the message, a second line of text he hadn't expected:
*Zarek moves tonight. The dock warehouses. He doesn't know you're coming.*
Kael read the message twice. Three times.
This could be a trap. Probably was a trap. Someone wanted him dead, and this was the simplest way to accomplish it—lure him to Zarek, let the two of them destroy each other, clean up the survivors.
But the information felt true. The timing felt right. Zarek had framed him for crimes he didn't commit, destroyed his life, forced him down a path that had led to execution and rebirth and something that might be worse than death.
The opportunity to end that—to finally confront the man who'd ruined everything—
Kael's hand closed around the phone.
He thought of Vex's words. The first Soul Reaver, transformed into something the Orders couldn't control. The entity essence waiting in the vial, vast and patient, recognizing something in him that he didn't recognize in himself.
He thought of Seren's offer, the Shadow Court's endless games, the conspiracy that moved pieces across a board he'd only begun to see.
He thought of the seals weakening, the rifts spreading, the 72 entities pressing against boundaries that had held for centuries.
The phone's screen glowed in the darkness. The coordinates waited. Zarek waited.
Kael started walking toward the docks.
Behind him, in the ruins of the burned warehouse, something moved. A shape that might have been a man, or might have been something wearing a man's shape. It watched Kael's retreating form with eyes that held no light at all.
Then it smiled.
And followed.

The docks spread before him like the bones of some great beast—wooden piers reaching into black water, warehouses squatting along the shore, cranes frozen mid-lift by the cessation of night work. The smell hit him next: salt and fish and the chemical tang of alchemical preservatives, underlaid with something fouler. Something organic.
Kael moved between the shadows, following the route the message had provided. His wound had stiffened during the walk, the dried blood pulling at his shirt with every step. He needed rest, treatment, time to process what he'd learned about Entity essence and the original Soul Reaver.
Time was a luxury he didn't have.
The warehouse at the end of Pier Seven sat dark and silent. No lights in the windows. No guards visible. Just a building like a thousand others along the Thornwick waterfront, waiting to be forgotten.
Kael approached anyway.
His palm tingled where the brand had burned itself into his flesh. The entity's essence pulsed in his coat pocket, responding to proximity to something. Power, maybe. Or corruption. Or the presence of others who carried the Soul Reaver taint.
He touched the warehouse door. It swung open without resistance.
Inside: darkness. The smell of old timber and stagnant water. And voices—low, urgent, coming from somewhere deeper in the structure.
Kael stepped inside.
The voices resolved into words as he moved closer, his eyes adjusting to the blackness. Two speakers, maybe three. And beneath their words, a sound that shouldn't exist in a warehouse full of crates and inventory.
Breathing. Heavy. Wet. The kind of breathing that came from lungs filling with liquid.
Kael pushed through a doorway and found them.
Zarek stood at the center of a ritual circle, hands raised, chanting words in a language that made Kael's teeth ache. Around him, bodies lay scattered—dockworkers by their clothing, throats opened in precise lines, blood pooling into channels carved into the wooden floor.
And in the middle of the circle, something moved.
It might have been human once. Now it was a collection of parts that didn't fit together correctly—a torso too long, arms that bent in directions arms shouldn't bend, a head that turned all the way around despite the spine that should have prevented such movement.
An Entity. Not one of the 72—the seals would have screamed if that were true—but something from the space between, a fragment of Abyssal energy given form and purpose.
"Crimson Chalice." Kael's voice cut through the chanting.
Zarek stopped. Turned. His face held no surprise.
"They told me you'd come." Zarek's smile split his features like a wound. "The Veil, the Shadow Court, even my own superiors. Everyone wants to see what happens when the two of us meet."
"You framed me."
"I freed you." Zarek spread his hands. "You were rotting in a cell, waiting to die for crimes you didn't commit. I gave you purpose. Direction. The power to become something more than a victim."
"You destroyed my life."
"I gave you a new one." Zarek's smile widened. "Question is—what will you do with it?"
Kael's hand found the vial in his coat. The entity essence pulsed against his palm, the brand on his flesh burning with sudden intensity. He could feel the thing in the ritual circle reaching toward him, tasting his power, recognizing something familiar.
"End this," Kael said.
"End what? The ritual? The conspiracy? Your own existence?" Zarek laughed. "You can't end anything, Kael. You're just a piece on a board that's been playing for two hundred years. The Whisper King moved you into position. The Shadow Court monitors your progress. Even the Orders who hunt you do so according to a plan you can't see."
"Then I'll tear up the board."
"You'll try." Zarek raised his hands again, continuing the chant. "But first, watch what I've built. Watch what the Crimson Chalice has been preparing for decades. Watch the door open, and the power pour through."
The ritual circle flared with light. The thing in the center shrieked—a sound that existed in frequencies Kael could feel in his bones. The blood in the channels began to flow upward, defying gravity, feeding the thing that was becoming.
Kael lunged.
His hand closed around the vial just as his body crashed into Zarek's. They went down together, rolling across the bloody floor, fists and elbows trading impacts. Zarek fought with the precision of someone trained by multiple Orders—strikes to nerve clusters, joint locks, attempts to find vital points.
Kael fought dirty. He bit. He clawed. He used the brand on his palm to burn Zarek's face when he got the chance.
The vial cracked in his grip.
Entity essence spilled across both of them.
The world went white.
When Kael's vision cleared, he stood alone in the warehouse. Zarek lay crumpled against the far wall, smoke rising from his convulsing form. The ritual circle had shattered, the channels broken, the thing that had been forming dissolved into wisps of shadow that dissipated in the stale air.
The Crimson Chalice's work, undone in seconds.
But Kael had paid a price.
His veins burned. The entity essence had touched him again, deeper this time, the brand on his palm spreading tendrils of light across his skin. He could feel it inside him now—not possession, not control, but connection. A thread linking him to something vast, something patient, something that had been waiting for exactly this moment.
*You came to us,* a voice whispered in his mind. Not words, exactly. More like impressions, meanings that bypassed language entirely. *The first one came too. Carried our gift. Bore our mark.*
"I'm not the first."
*No. But you might be the last. The seals fail. The door opens. Only those who carry our essence can survive what comes through.*
"What comes through?"
*Everything.*
Kael fell to his knees. The voice faded, but the connection remained—a constant hum at the edge of his consciousness, a reminder that he'd crossed a line tonight from which there was no return.
Behind him, Zarek stirred.
"The... The essence..." Zarek's voice came out broken, scorched. "You took it. Both vials. It's inside you now."
"I know."
"You're insane. The corruption will—"
"Already started." Kael forced himself to stand. "But I can control it. Use it."
"You think that's what the first Reaver thought? Think they believed they could master the gift?" Zarek laughed weakly. "They were wrong. And you will be too."
"Guess I'll find out."
Kael walked toward the exit. His body felt strange—lighter,, but also more present. Every surface around him seemed to pulse with energy he could almost touch. The ley lines running beneath Thornwick blazed in his enhanced perception. The seals maintaining the Veil flickered like candles in a hurricane.
And somewhere in the distance, he felt them.
The 72. All of them. Pressing against their prisons. Testing the chains that bound them.
Waiting.
The brand on his palm pulsed once more, then faded to invisibility. But Kael could still feel it—the connection to the Abyss, to the power that waited beyond the Veil, to the destiny that had been carved for someone like him two hundred years ago.
He stepped out of the warehouse into the pre-dawn darkness.
A message waited on his phone. Not from the Veil this time. Not from the Shadow Court. From a number he recognized from his old life—the criminal network he'd been framed for infiltrating.
*They know you're back. All of them. Run while you can.*
Kael deleted the message.
Running had never been his style.
He started walking toward the city center, toward the places where power accumulated and decisions were made. The conspiracy was accelerating. The Crimson Chalice pushed toward their goal. The Shadow Court watched and waited. The Whisper King pulled strings from the shadows.
And somewhere in the darkness behind him, something followed.
It had no face to show.
It had no name to speak.
But it watched with eyes that held the patience of centuries, and it waited for the moment when the board would finally tilt in its favor.
Kael could feel it there. Just at the edge of perception.
The game wasn't over.
It was barely beginning.
