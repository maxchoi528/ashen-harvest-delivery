The dead man twitched.
Kael froze mid-step, one boot hovering over the cracked cobblestones of Merchant's Row. Rain hammered his shoulders, soaking through the wool of his coat. Three heartbeats of silence, filled with the hiss of water on hot iron. Then the corpse's fingers curled, scraping against the wet stone with a sound like nails on slate.
"Stay down," he breathed.
The body ignored him. Ribs cracked as it dragged itself forward, leaving a dark smear in the rain. Hollow sockets tracked toward Kael with terrible purpose—something had reanimated this corpse, and it wanted him dead.
He dropped into a combat stance, drawing a blade from his coat. Rain hammered the streets, turning Lantern-light into bleeding halos. Behind him, a cart horse screamed and bolted, its hooves sparking against iron grating.
The reanimated corpse surged upright. Its jaw hung loose, tongue lolling black against pale teeth. Spine bowed at an angle that no living body could achieve. Spiritual energy poured from its chest in sickly waves—not the normal residue of death, but something injected. Corrupted. A Hollow Knight's puppet.
"Someone's watching this," Kael said to no one. "Someone wanted me to see this."
The puppet lunged.
Kael sidestepped, boots skidding on slick stone. His blade sang through rain. Steel bit into the corpse's neck, but the head stayed attached, jaw snapping inches from his forearm. The stench hit him—rot and copper and something chemical beneath. He twisted the blade, severing the spine at the joint. The body collapsed, limbs spasming.
He stood over the remains, breathing hard. Around him, Thornwick's night market had emptied in seconds—vendors and customers alike fleeing into the maze of alleys and steam-fog. Smart people. A tipped-over fruit cart leaked pulped apples across the cobblestones. Gas lamps flickered in their iron cages, their flames guttering before steadying again.
Kael wiped his blade on his coat and crouched beside the corpse. Touching it would mean harvesting whatever remained, assuming anything worthwhile survived the puppet's animation. The Law of Soul Conservation meant energy couldn't be created or destroyed, only transferred. This thing had been driven by stolen reserves—someone's essence poured into a dead shell.
"Show me what you were," he murmured.
His fingers found the corpse's forehead. The cold hit first, biting through his fingertips. The familiar sensation of his Soul Reaver ability stirring—zero spiritual cost, but the world didn't know that. The world only saw a monster stealing from the dead.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 2 units of corrupted essence from the remains. Quality: degraded. Nothing acquired.]
Kael hissed through his teeth. "Waste of a corpse."
The corruption itself was interesting, though. He probed the residual energy, reading its signature like a fingerprint. Not standard Hollow Knight work—too refined, too controlled. Whoever animated this puppet had skill. The energy tasted of iron and ash, with an undercurrent he couldn't place. Unfamiliar. That alone set his teeth on edge.
Rain continued its relentless assault. Water pooled in the corpse's hollow eye sockets, giving the dead man the appearance of weeping.
Footsteps echoed from the alley mouth. Multiple sets, approaching in formation.
Kael rose and melted into the shadows, leaving the twitching corpse behind.
Later, Kael stood in the doorway of the Crossed Antlers tavern, shaking rain from his coat. The establishment catered to Awakened who preferred anonymity—no questions, no Order registration, no questions about the bloodstains that occasionally decorated one's clothing.
"Ashborn." The barkeep, a heavyset woman with a scar bisecting her left eyebrow, tossed him a copper token. "Room's ready. Your guest arrived a short while ago."
"Guest." Kael caught the token. "I don't remember scheduling a meeting."
The woman's expression didn't change. "You didn't. He insisted."
Room three, then. Kael climbed the narrow stairs, each step groaning beneath his weight. The hallway smelled of pipe smoke and old ale, with an undercurrent of mold blooming behind the wallpaper. He paused outside his door, listening.
Breathing. Two patterns—one deep and controlled, the other shallow and nervous.
Kael entered.
The man sitting at the small table looked like a clerk: thin, nervous, wearing the drab clothing of the spiritually inert middle tier. His eyes flicked to Kael with barely concealed terror.
The woman beside him was different. Stocky build, practical clothes, a scar running from her temple to her jaw. She held a mug of ale and gave Kael a nod of professional assessment.
"Kael Ashborn," the nervous man said. His voice cracked. "I—I have information. About the Hollow Knights. About what's happening in the Undercity."
"Sit down," Kael said, closing the door. "Start from the beginning."
The nervous man's name was Edric Marsh, a clerk in the city's Sealing Office—the government body responsible for monitoring the rifts and maintaining the seal network. The woman, who called herself Torv, said nothing and offered no surname.
"The seals," Marsh said, hands trembling around his ale. "They're failing faster than the reports suggest. The Order of Dawn's been falsifying data, covering up degradation in the outer districts."
Kael leaned against the wall. "Why would they do that?"
"Because opening them is the point." Marsh's laugh bordered on hysteria. "The cultists—they're not trying to break the seals randomly. They have a schedule. Seventy-two rifts, seventy-two days, and then—"
"Then what?"
"Then the membrane between realms tears open completely." Marsh drained his ale in one long swallow. "The Abyss pours through. Everything dies."
Torv set down her mug. "He's been feeding information to my organization for months. We believe him."
"Your organization." Kael's gaze sharpened. "Which would be?"
"The Shadow Court." She didn't flinch from the admission. "We monitor threats the Orders ignore. This cult, the one calling itself the Whisper Covenant—it's been growing for two years. Structured. Funded. Someone powerful is backing them."
Kael felt ice crystallize in his stomach. "The Shadow Court. You're telling me a shadow organization monitors threats, and you're sitting in my room telling me about it openly."
"We're offering an alliance." Torv leaned forward. "You have abilities we need. We have information you need. The Whisper Covenant has something in the Undercity—something they've sealed away, something connected to the original Soul Reaver. We want it destroyed. You want answers about what you are."
The words hung in the air like smoke.
"The original Soul Reaver," Kael repeated slowly. "You've seen records."
"Fragments." Marsh's voice steadied slightly. "The Order of Dawn sealed most of it after the Abyssal Wars ended. But they couldn't destroy everything. The original Reaver was... Different. Not a monster like the histories claim. A weapon, yes, but one that chose its wielder."
"And the artifact?"
"Chamber deep beneath the old cathedral. Sealed with blood-wards that only a Soul Reaver can open." Torv's eyes glittered. "The cult has been trying for months. They can't break through. But you could."
Kael studied them both. The Shadow Court operative who admitted her affiliation without hesitation. The clerk who knew too much and was clearly terrified of what would happen if he stopped talking. Information asymmetry—he had no way to verify their claims, and they knew it.
"Why me?" he asked. "Why not approach the Orders directly?"
"Because the Orders are compromised." Torv stood, moving to the window. "We've traced funding. The Whisper Covenant receives support from within at least three of the Six Orders—Iron Vanguard, Arcane Spire, and possibly the Order of Dawn itself. The Council of Six won't act. The Inquisitors are hunting you. And the cult's Prime is closing in on something that could end the world."
"Prime." The word tasted bitter. "Zarek."
Marsh nodded frantically. "The reports I've seen— he's been seen near the Undercity entrance twice this week. Always alone, always at night. Whatever they're looking for, he's the one meant to find it."
Kael's jaw tightened. Zarek. The man who'd framed him for Gwen's squad death. The man he'd sworn to kill when the time came.
"You're asking me to descend into the Undercity, break into a cult-controlled vault, and retrieve an artifact that the original Soul Reaver sealed away centuries ago." He ticked the points off on his fingers. "All while being hunted by Inquisitors, monitored by the Shadow Court, and racing a cult that wants the world to end."
Torv turned from the window. "Yes."
"Give me the location."
She slid a folded paper across the table. Kael unfolded it—a hand-drawn map of the Undercity's upper levels, with a location marked in red ink. A maintenance shaft leading down from the old cathedral's crypt.
"We'll provide support where possible," Torv continued. "But the final approach must be yours. The wards respond only to Soul Reaver energy. Any other spiritual signature triggers a cascade failure."
"And if I decide this is all a trap? If I decide the Shadow Court's the real enemy here?"
Torv smiled without warmth. "Then you'll never know what's in that chamber. And neither will we. But the cult will eventually break through—and when they do, the world burns whether you're involved or not."
Kael pocketed the map. "The artifact. Do you have any idea what it does?"
"Something about True Names." Marsh spoke as though reciting from memory. "The original Reaver used it to bind the seventy-two Entities. Not seal them—bind them. Make them answer to a mortal will. The Orders don't teach this, but the Entities aren't evil. They're just... Vast. Wrong for our reality. The original Reaver found a way to force compliance than imprisonment."
"A leash for gods," Kael said quietly.
"That's one interpretation." Torv moved toward the door. "The chamber's been sealed since the Reaver's death. Whatever's inside has waited centuries for someone to open it. Consider carefully what you want to find."
She left without waiting for a response. Marsh lingered, fidgeting.
"There's one more thing." His voice dropped to a whisper. "The Hollow Knight that attacked you tonight—I've seen the animation reports. Three others struck in the outer districts. Same signature, same corruption pattern. They're not random attacks. They're a message."
"To whom?"
"To anyone who might interfere with the Covenant's plans." Marsh stood, unsteady on his feet. "To you, specifically. They know you're Ascendant. They know you can harvest. And they want you scared."
Kael considered the dead man's words. Scared. He'd been scared plenty in his life—on the scaffold where they'd executed him, in the months since his resurrection, every time he touched a corpse and felt the world's hatred pour through him like ice water.
"I'm not scared," he said. "I'm angry. There's a difference."
Marsh fled.
The Undercity entrance stank of centuries.
Kael descended the maintenance shaft, one hand trailing along damp stone walls covered in luminescent moss. The light it cast was weak, sickly, barely enough to see by. Below, the darkness pressed close like a living thing.
He reached the bottom and found himself in a tunnel that stretched beyond the reach of his vision. Ancient architecture—the original foundations of Thornwick, built before the steam engines and the Order halls and the careful civilization that existed above. Down here, the world was raw. Forgotten.
His Soul Reaver senses opened automatically. Residual energy clung to every surface, decades of it layering like sediment. He could feel where the tunnel branched, where presences had moved through, where deaths had occurred and their essence dissipated slowly into nothing.
The cult had been here. Recently. He traced their passage by the disturbed residue, the particular flavor of corrupted energy they left behind like fingerprints.
A stretch of walking brought him to a sealed door.
Blood-wards covered the surface, old blood dried to rust-brown stains that formed intricate patterns. No keyhole. No mechanism. Just the door and its terrible warnings.
Kael studied the patterns. The original Soul Reaver's work—he recognized the signature now, the particular resonance of soul-energy shaped through will alone. This was what he'd become, centuries ago. This was the power that let a single human bind gods.
He pressed his palm flat against the door.
The wards recognized him instantly. Not because of who he was, but because of what he carried—the Soul Reaver ability, passed down through whatever means the original had used. The blood-stains flared with light, patterns rearranging, and the door groaned inward.
Beyond lay a chamber.
Kael stepped inside and the world changed.
The space shouldn't have existed—this deep below the city, there should have been only stone and darkness. Instead, he found himself standing in a circular room with walls that curved upward into shadow. Candles burned in iron sconces, impossibly still lit after centuries. At the chamber's center stood a pedestal, and on the pedestal rested a small box of black wood.
The air hummed with contained power. Kael felt his skin prickle as the energy washed over him, ancient and vast and patient.
"Someone's been here," he said aloud.
The chamber disagreed. Dust covered every surface except the pedestal's immediate vicinity. No footprints marred the floor. Whatever this was, the cult hadn't reached it yet.
Kael approached the pedestal.
The box was small enough to fit in one hand, carved from wood so dark it seemed to drink the light. Symbols marked its surface—not the blood-wards of the door, but something older. More personal.
He reached for the box.
"Don't."
Kael spun, blade drawn. The voice had come from behind him, but the chamber stood empty. No doorway. No figure. No source.
"I'm not your enemy." The voice was calm, measured, and faintly amused. "Though I understand why you'd think otherwise. The first Soul Reaver, speaking across the centuries—it's a lot to process."
"The original." Kael kept his blade raised. "I thought you were dead."
"I am. Or I was. This is... Complicated." The voice seemed to come from everywhere and nowhere. "What you're holding is my last project. I didn't have time to finish it before they caught up with me."
"They?"
"The Orders. The Council. Everyone who feared what I could do." A pause. "What you can do. The Soul Reaver ability isn't a gift, Kael Ashborn. It's a curse I invented and then couldn't control. Every Reaver since has suffered for my mistakes."
Kael's grip on the blade tightened. "How do you know my name?"
"Because I've been waiting for you. Specifically you." The voice grew stronger, more present. "There are things the world doesn't know about the Abyss, about the Entities, about what really happened during the wars. I discovered them. I tried to tell people, and they killed me for it."
"And the artifact?"
"A failsafe. A way to control the Entities without destroying them—or us. But it requires someone with the Soul Reaver ability to attune it. Someone willing to bear the weight of seventy-two True Names."
Kael stared at the box. "You're asking me to bind gods."
"I'm asking you to choose. The Covenant wants to tear open the Abyss and let the Entities flood through. The Orders want to keep the seals weak enough to maintain their power, their hierarchy, their control. And you—you could do something else. Something that serves humanity without committing genocide."
"And if I refuse?"
"Then the Covenant wins eventually. Or the seals fail on their own. Or Inquisitor Vane catches you and the world loses its only Soul Reaver." Another pause. "I'm not asking you to trust me. I'm asking you to look inside the box and decide for yourself."
Kael lifted the lid.
Inside, resting on black velvet, lay a small crystal sphere no larger than a walnut. Its surface swirled with inner light—colors that shouldn't exist, patterns that hurt to look at. When he focused on it, he heard whispers at the edge of his hearing. Seventy-two voices, speaking in unison.
The True Names. The essence of every Entity, compressed into something a mortal could carry.
[Soul Harvest: You detected 72 sealed consciousness fragments. Potential acquisition: UNKNOWN. WARNING: This entity exceeds current Soul Reaver capacity. Attunement may result in irreversible consequences.]
Kael closed the box.
"The system doesn't approve," he said.
"The system is a tool. Tools don't approve or disapprove—they respond to how they're used." The voice grew fainter. "I've given you what I can. The rest is your choice."
"How do I contact you? How do I know any of this is real?"
"You don't. But you've always known how to separate truth from lies. Your instincts kept you alive when the scaffold should have ended everything. Trust them now."
The presence faded. Kael stood alone in the chamber, the weight of the box heavy in his hands.
Seventy-two Names. A way to control gods. And a dead man's voice promising answers that could change everything.
He needed time to think. Time to verify. But time was a luxury the cult wouldn't give him.
Kael pocketed the box and turned toward the chamber's exit.
The door was no longer open.
Iron bars had descended from the ceiling, blocking the only way out. Candle flames guttered and died. In the new darkness, eyes opened along the walls—dozens of them, pale and hungry, watching from alcoves he hadn't seen before.
A voice echoed from somewhere beyond the bars. Cultists. The Covenant had known about this chamber after all.
"Ashborn." The speaker's tone was conversational, almost friendly. "We've been waiting for you to do our work for us. Step away from the pedestal. Place the artifact on the ground. And maybe we'll let you live long enough to tell us where the original Reaver's tomb is located."
Kael's hand found his blade.
"Afraid I can't do that," he said.
"Then you die here. And we take what we need from your corpse."
The bars hummed with spiritual energy. The eyes multiplied. And somewhere in the darkness, he heard the grinding of mechanisms—more locks engaging, sealing him in.
He was alone in a chamber beneath the earth, surrounded by enemies, holding a box that could either save or destroy the world.
The cult wanted the True Names. The Shadow Court wanted the artifact destroyed. The Orders wanted him dead.
And Kael wanted answers.
"Sorry," he said, raising his blade. "But I've got questions first."
He charged.
