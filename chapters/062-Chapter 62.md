They hadn't seen him. Not yet.
Kael peeled himself from the wall and moved, silent as a shadow, through the narrow alley that cut through Fogmire District. The cobblestones slick with condensation, the stench of coal smoke and rotting vegetables thick in his nostrils. Above him, steam vents hissed from the nearby factory, masking any sound his boots might make.
He needed to reach Lyra. Needed to tell her what he'd seen at the warehouse district three nights ago—the symbols painted on the walls, the bodies arranged in that precise geometric pattern, the lingering wrongness in the air that spoke of something far worse than simple murder.
The Order of Dawn thought they were hunting cultists. They weren't wrong, but they were looking in all the wrong places.
A shout echoed from his left. Kael dropped into a crouch behind a stack of rotting crates, pressing himself into the darkness. Two figures rounded the corner—Order enforcers in their white cloaks, spiritual energy flaring around their hands like guttering candles.
"Soul Reaver activity reported in this sector," one said. "Third sighting this week."
Kael's stomach tightened. They'd connected the dots, even if they didn't understand what they were connecting. Someone was talking. Someone who knew what he'd been doing at the crime scenes, harvesting from the dead, taking what the Orders claimed belonged to no one.
*Zero spiritual cost*, the voice in his head reminded him. *But the world doesn't care about costs it can't measure.*
The enforcers passed. Kael counted to thirty before moving, sliding through a gap in the fence that led to the abandoned textile mill where he'd established a temporary base. The building groaned around him, timbers settling in the evening chill.
Lyra waited inside, perched on a stack of bolts like a hunting cat. Her dark hair was pulled back, revealing the thin scar that ran along her jawline—the one she'd earned in that brawl at the Rust Anchor two weeks ago.

The implications hung in the air between them. Coordinated cult activity meant organization, resources, leadership. It meant they weren't dealing with a few fanatics drawing pentagrams in blood. They were dealing with something structured, something that had survived long enough to develop hierarchy and logistics.
"The Orders will want to know about this," Lyra said carefully.
"They'll want to know how I found it." Kael's voice was flat. "They'll ask questions I can't answer without explaining what I really am."
Lyra set the paper down on an overturned crate, her fingers lingering on its edge. "You could still turn yourself in. Claim you stumbled onto the cultists by accident, that you're just a citizen trying to help."

The words came out sharper than he'd intended. Lyra didn't flinch, but something shifted in her posture—a slight withdrawal, like she'd touched a hot surface.

Kael studied her face, reading the tension in her shoulders, the way her hand drifted unconsciously to the dagger at her belt. The Shadow Court was a deniable operations arm for multiple Orders, a shadow network that operated in the spaces between official sanction. Getting involved with them meant entering a web of obligations and debts that might never be paid.
"You trust them?" he asked.
"I trust that they want to survive as much as I do." Lyra met his gaze directly. "That's more reliable than trust."
Outside, a whistle shrieked—the patrol's signal that they'd completed their sweep. Kael moved to the window again, watching as the white-cloaked figures converged on the street corner. They hadn't found anything. Not tonight.
"We need more information," he said. "Before the cult moves again, before the Orders lock down the entire district. If those seventeen sites are where they think they're operating—"

"No." Lyra smiled, but it didn't reach her eyes. "We do what you do best."
The textile mill fell silent except for the settling of old wood and the distant rumble of steam engines. Kael turned away from the window, his mind already running through possibilities. Seventeen sites meant seventeen chances to find evidence, to track patterns, to identify leadership. And if those sites were active ritual locations, they meant something else entirely.
Fresh corpses.
"I can work with that," Kael said finally. "But we do it my way. No unnecessary risks, no drawing attention until we have to."
"Agreed." Lyra extended her hand. "Partners?"
Kael clasped her forearm in the old way, grip firm. "Partners."

Nothing obvious. Which meant either the cult was confident in their secrecy, or they had defenses that were invisible to someone at his stage.

He found the entrance on the building's blind side, a cellar door half-hidden beneath a collapsed awning. The lock was old, the mechanism corroded. One solid kick sent it crashing inward.

The cellar was empty except for dust and the skeletal remains of rats. The symbol Kael had seen at the warehouse. The same pattern he'd found in his nightmares since waking from his own execution.
*Soul Reaver marks.*
His stomach clenched. Someone else had walked this path before him. Someone who understood what the dead could give, what they could be made to surrender. And that someone had been teaching others.

Six months. The Great Rift degrading faster than expected. The cult planning something massive enough to require six fresh bodies for a single binding.
And they called him a Reaver.

Kael considered the offer. The Shadow Court was dangerous—everyone knew that. They were the hidden hand that moved pieces on the board, the knife in the dark that cut throats and disappeared before the body cooled. But they also had resources he desperately needed, and right now, resources were the only thing standing between him and a very messy death.
"What's the catch?" he asked.
"The catch is that we infiltrate the ritual together. I provide intelligence and distraction. You—" Lyra's eyes glittered. "You do what you do best."
*Harvest from the dead.*
It wasn't a clean operation. It wasn't the kind of work that left him sleeping easy at night. But it was information, and information was survival in a city like Thornwick.
"Show me the map," Kael said.

Ashmore Hill Cemetery was a sprawling maze of crumbling monuments and overgrown paths, the kind of place where people buried their dead and then forgot they'd ever existed. The evening fog rolled through the iron gates like a living thing, swallowing the gaslights and leaving only shadows.
Kael moved through the darkness like he'd been born to it, which in a sense he had—born again, that was, in the moment the executioner's blade had fallen and the Soul Reaver had woken inside him. The cemetery's dead whispered against his senses, their residual energy fading but not yet gone. Some were recent, their souls departed but not forgotten. Others had been here for decades, their essence slowly returning to the Abyss from which it came.
*Seventy-two sealed rifts*, he reminded himself. *Seventy-two Entities waiting to break free.*
The Six Orders maintained those seals. The cult wanted to break them. And somewhere in between, Kael was trying to survive.
He found the ritual site at the cemetery's center, where a crumbling mausoleum stood with its door hanging open. Torches burned in iron braziers, casting flickering light across a scene that made his stomach turn.
Six bodies lay arranged in a circle, their throats cut, their blood pooling in channels carved into the ancient stone. The bodies weren't the focus of attention—four cultists in gray robes knelt at cardinal points around the circle, chanting in a language that scraped against Kael's ears like broken glass. At the circle's center stood a fifth figure, hooded and tall, his hands raised toward the sky.
The air *warped* around him. Spiritual energy gathered in visible streams, flowing toward the raised hands like water toward a drain.
Kael pressed himself behind a crumbling angel statue, watching, waiting. He could feel the power building, could sense the wrongness intensifying with every passing second. Whatever the cult was trying to bind, it was something massive. Something that had been waiting for a very long time.
A bell tolled in the distance. One of the cultists screamed, his voice cutting through the chanting. The energy streams shuddered, destabilized.
"They're early!" The tall figure's voice was sharp with panic. "Complete the binding! Now!"
The cultists pressed harder, their chanting rising to a fever pitch. The bodies at their feet began to twitch, blood flowing upward in defiance of gravity, joining the swirling vortex of energy.
And then Kael saw something that froze the blood in his veins.
One of the bodies—a young woman with dark hair—opened her eyes.
Not in the way of the living. Her eyes were black, solid black, like holes into a void that had no bottom. And she was looking directly at him.
*He sees us*, something whispered in his mind. *The Reaver sees us.*

He could harvest from all six. Take their knowledge, their abilities, whatever fragments of power remained in their departed spirits. *Zero spiritual cost*, the voice reminded him. *But the social, political, and moral consequences apply.*

Somewhere out there, the cult was licking its wounds and planning its next move. And somewhere in the shadows, the Shadow Court was watching, waiting to see what the Reaver would do next.
He didn't have all the answers. He didn't even have most of them. But he had momentum, and he had allies, and he had a list of names that started with Dorian.
For tonight, that was enough.
"Let's move," he said.

Three hours later, Kael stood in the shadows across from the Blackmoor Forge, watching the night shift change. Two guards at the main entrance, regular patrol patterns, no visible spiritual wards. It looked almost normal—an industrial facility running late shifts, nothing that would draw attention from the city watch.
Almost normal. But the memory fragment from the harvested cultist had pointed here, and Kael had learned to trust those fragments even when they didn't make sense.
"The Shadow Court confirmed it," Lyra said quietly, rejoining him in the shadows. "Their sources say there's been unusual activity around the forge for the past month. Increased security, shipments that don't appear on any manifest, workers who go in but never come out."
"The workers are cultists?"
"Or sacrifices." Lyra's voice was flat. "Hard to say which is worse."
Kael considered the options. Infiltrating the forge meant risking exposure, confrontation, possibly death. But it also meant answers—about the cult's operations, about Dorian's role, about what the cult had been trying to accomplish with their ritual.
And it meant more chances to harvest. More souls to take what he needed.
*No*, he corrected himself. *Not more souls. Just the ones I can't avoid.*
