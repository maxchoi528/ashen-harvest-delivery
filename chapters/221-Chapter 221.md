Zarek. The name landed like a stone in still water. Kael had history with that man—a debt of blood that remained unpaid. If Zarek was involved in whatever was happening in Thornwick, Kael would need to find him. Soon.
Footsteps echoed somewhere above. The loading doors rattled on their hinges.

Steam vents from the industrial quarter painted the sky in sickly orange. Somewhere out there, Zarek was pulling strings. Somewhere, the Whisper was waiting.
Kael slipped through the window and onto the rooftops.

"You're late," Wren said. The woman sat behind a makeshift desk of stacked crates, her sharp features half-hidden by the hood of a travel-stained cloak. "And you smell like blood."
"Occupational hazard."
"Sit down. Tell me what you found."
Kael remained standing. "The Mortician's Guild is involved."
Wren's expression didn't change, but her fingers stilled on the desk's edge. "You're certain?"
"I killed four of them tonight. One wore the insignia openly."
"That's... Significant." Wren pulled a clay pipe from her belt and began packing it with dried herbs. "The Guild's supposed to stay neutral. They handle the dead, nothing more. Politics is for the living."
"Someone's violating that neutrality. The cultists I fought were running a ritual—one that involved a Rift Shard. They weren't just worshipping the Abyss. They were collecting pieces of the seals."
The pipe smoke curled upward, carrying the scent of something sharp and herbal. Wren took a long draw before responding.
"That's not the work of a fringe group," she said finally. "Rift Shards don't just appear. They have to be... Harvested. Carefully. From sites where the seals have been deliberately weakened."
"Deliberately weakened by who?"
"The Orders would be my first guess. The Accord has held for two hundred years, but the seals are failing faster than anyone admits. Each Order has access to certain rifts—the ones they're supposedly monitoring and maintaining. If one of them wanted to extract something from a rift without the others knowing..."
She didn't finish the sentence. She didn't need to.
"Which Order?" Kael asked.
"I don't know. That's the problem. Every faction points fingers at the others, but nobody can prove anything. The Shadow Court, though—" Wren's voice dropped. "—they've been asking questions about you specifically."

"Relax. I'm not here to sell you out. But you should know: someone offered a substantial bounty for information about Soul Reavers in Thornwick. The posting came through the Veiled Market three days ago. Anonymous, but the coin was real."
"Who posted it?"
"That's what I can't figure out. It wasn't any of the major players. The payment routing was... Strange. Like someone wanted to hide their involvement but didn't care about the trail leading back to a dead drop."
The pieces were arranging themselves in Kael's mind. A bounty on Reavers. Mortician's Guild involvement. Cults running rituals with Rift Shards. Zarek's name connected to it all. The Whisper King, whoever that was.
"The cultists mentioned a Whisper," Kael said. "Someone who speaks through Zarek."
Wren's pipe stopped halfway to her lips. For the first time since he'd entered, genuine surprise crossed her features.
"The Whisper King," she said quietly. "That's what the older texts call him. Or her. No one knows for certain."
"What do the older texts say?"
"Nothing useful. Most of the historical records about the Whisper King were destroyed during the Second Purge. What's left is fragments and rumors. Centuries. If the Whisper King had been manipulating things that long, then everything Kael thought he knew about the current crisis might be nothing more than surface ripples over a much deeper current.
"Anything else?" he asked.
"The Six Orders are meeting in emergency session tomorrow. Something about increased cult activity in multiple cities simultaneously. Thornwick, Grayhaven, Saltmere, Ironcove. Four cities, all experiencing the same thing at once." Wren's eyes met his. "That's not coincidence."
No. It wasn't. Someone was orchestrating a coordinated assault on the seal network. And if Kael understood anything about the Abyss and its inhabitants, it was that they were patient. A coordinated assault would be designed to overwhelm the Orders' response—to punch holes in the barrier before anyone could react.

Kael took the map and tucked it inside his coat. "Why the investment?"
"Because I've been in this business a long time, and I've learned to recognize when something big is about to break. The last time I felt this feeling was thirty years ago, right before the Silver Compact collapsed and started the Third Order War." Wren's pipe had gone out, but she didn't relight it. "Whatever's happening in Thornwick isn't local. It's not even continental. This is something that's been building for generations."
"You think the seals are going to fall."
"I think they're already falling. The only question is whether anyone can slow it down."
Kael turned toward the door. The tenement's floorboards creaked beneath his boots.
"One more thing," Wren said. "The bounty on Reavers. It's still active. If I were you, I'd be careful about who sees me harvest."
"They can't stop me from taking what I need."
"No. But they can make the cost higher than you're willing to pay."
He didn't respond. He already knew the truth of her words—had lived with their weight every day since his Awakening. The world hated what he was. The Orders would hunt him. The Shadow Court wanted to control him. And somewhere in the shadows, the Whisper King was pulling strings that reached back centuries.
But Kael had never been afraid of costs. He'd been dead once, and death had failed to keep him.
That gave him a perspective most people lacked. Death was just another door. The only thing that mattered was what you carried through it.
He stepped out into the Thornwick night.

The Drowned Quarter occupied the city's lowest elevation, where the canal system's overflow collected in stagnant pools and crumbling warehouses. Gaslights were sparse here, their flames guttering in the damp air. The residents—those who couldn't afford to live anywhere else—moved through the muck with the practiced silence of prey animals.
Kael found the safehouse marked on Wren's map three hours before dawn. It was a converted fish-processing plant, its windows boarded, its doors reinforced with iron bands. Six cultists patrolled the perimeter. Their movements were disciplined, coordinated. Former soldiers, maybe, or trained enforcers.
He killed the first two with thrown knives, taking them down before they could raise the alarm. The third died with Kael's blade in his throat. The fourth managed to shout before a second blade silenced him.
That left two. They fell back toward the building's main entrance, clearly intending to warn whoever was inside. Kael sprinted after them, his Transcendent-enhanced body eating the distance in strides that covered ten feet each.
He tackled the fifth cultist at the threshold, driving him through the wooden door in a shower of splinters. They rolled across the floor, grappling for control of the blade. The cultist was strong—Awakened, probably, though at a lower stage than Kael. His strikes landed with bruising force.
But Kael had been fighting since before his first death. His body remembered combat the way other bodies remembered breathing. He twisted, broke the man's grip, and drove his elbow into the cultist's temple. The cultist's eyes rolled back.
The sixth cultist stood at the top of a staircase, a crossbow aimed at Kael's chest.
"Zarek sends his regards," she said.
The bolt flew true.
Kael threw himself sideways. The bolt punched through his sleeve, grazing his ribs, drawing a line of fire across his skin. Pain flared, then faded as his Transcendent resilience kicked in.
He threw a knife. The cultist ducked, the blade spinning past her head. She loosed another bolt. Kael was already moving, charging up the stairs with his blade raised.

He stood over her, breathing hard.

"I don't know. They wear a mask. But—" She swallowed. "—they carry a staff with a white crystal. One of the old relics from before the Orders."
A staff with a white crystal. That matched fragments Kael had pulled from harvested memories—the same symbol appearing again and again in the cult's rituals.

"Tonight. Sunset. But if you go down there, you'll find the passage empty. He uses it as a decoy."

He said the Abyss would give us power beyond the Orders' reach." She laughed, a broken sound. "But the Whisper doesn't want to free anyone. It wants to consume everything. And Zarek knows. He's known all along. He just doesn't care."
That aligned with everything Kael had learned about the Abyss and its Entities. They weren't liberators. They were predators, drawn to worlds where the barriers had fallen, feeding until nothing remained but void.
The Whisper King, if such an entity existed, would be no different. A parasite wearing the face of a benefactor.

Kael's mind raced. This was the conspiracy's deepest level—the layer beneath Zarek, beneath the Whisper King, beneath even the Orders' knowledge. And it offered him everything he wanted: power, answers, revenge.

Kael had harvested from the living once, in his early days—a desperate act that had saved his life but left him with nightmares for weeks. He understood the warning.

The Sepulcher. The place where the first Soul Reaver had been imprisoned. The place where the conspiracy's leaders would gather to break the seals once and for all.

Kael stood alone in the ancient chamber, his mind reeling with new knowledge and new questions. The first Soul Reaver wasn't dead—they were imprisoned, bound to a form that allowed them to exist but not act. They wanted Kael to stop the Hollow Covenant, to destroy the Whisper King's plans.
But they also wanted to remain hidden. Why? What were they afraid of?
And the bounty—who had posted it, and why?
The questions would have to wait. The Covenant was in three days. Kael needed to move.
He left the chamber, climbed the passage, and emerged into the pre-dawn darkness of the Drowned Quarter. The warehouse's upper floors were silent. The cultists he'd killed earlier would be discovered eventually. The Orders would investigate. The conspiracy would shift and adapt.
But they wouldn't adapt fast enough.
Kael moved through the shadows toward his next destination. He needed to warn the Orders without revealing himself. He needed to prepare for a confrontation with Zarek. And he needed to learn more about the Whisper King—about what they really wanted, and why the first Soul Reaver feared them.

Nothing. Or, nothing he could identify. Something was there—he was certain of it. Something that knew how to hide from spiritual awareness.
A watcher. An enemy. Or maybe another player in the conspiracy's endless games.

The night was far from over.
And somewhere in the darkness, the Whisper King was smiling.
