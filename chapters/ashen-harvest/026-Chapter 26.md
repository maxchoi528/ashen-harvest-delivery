"The corpse twitched under my hand. I pressed harder."
Kael Ashborn knelt in the drainage tunnel, rainwater soaking through his coat, the dead man's collar bunched between his fingers. The body was fresh—maybe two hours cold. Fog crept through the tunnel's grate above, carrying the stench of the Thornwick docks.
[System Notification] No soul fragment detected. Insufficient spiritual residue.
"Damn." Kael released the corpse. Another failed harvest attempt. The body belonged to a dockworker, his throat cut clean. Not enough spiritual energy remained to capture. Whoever killed him had drained the soul completely.
The Veiled Market had paid good coin for that kind of work. Kael wasn't interested in the why. He needed passage out of Thornwick, and information cost money he didn't have.
A rat scurried across his boot. Kael ignored it.
Three days in the city. Three days since he'd crawled out of the execution pit with Soul Reaver burning in his chest like a second heartbeat. Three days of sleeping in condemned buildings and eating scraps while the Orders tore the city apart searching for him.
The map in his pocket was old, hand-drawn, showing tunnels that predated Thornwick's founding. The Sepulcher tablets mentioned these passages. Underground routes the first Soul Reaver used to move unseen.
Footsteps echoed from deeper in the tunnel.
Kael rose, pressing his back against the damp stone wall. His hand found the knife at his belt—not much against an Awakened, but better than nothing. He had no abilities to speak of yet. Just the Reaver's hunger and nothing to feed it.
The footsteps grew louder. Two sets, maybe three.
A lantern bobbed into view, its light cutting through the fog. Kael held his breath.
"Easy, boy." A voice like gravel scraping rust. "We saw your light. Not here to fight."
The lantern came closer. Behind it walked an old man, bent with age, wearing a coat that had once been fine. White hair spilled over his shoulders, and his eyes gleamed like wet stones in the lamplight. Beside him stood a younger man, broad-shouldered, arms covered in tattoos that pulsed faintly with spiritual energy.
Kael didn't lower his knife. "Who wants to know?"
"I do." The old man stopped ten feet away. "Name's Crowley. Some call me Old Crowley. I run things down here in the deep."
"The Mortician's Guild?"
A laugh, dry as old bone. "Them? They run the pretty death. The licensed harvesting, the soul trade for the Orders. I run the real business." He gestured at the tunnel around them. "This is my city, boy. And you—walking around with a Reaver's mark and no idea how to use it—well. That's interesting to me."
Kael's grip tightened on the knife. "How do you know what I am?"
"Because I've seen one before. Long time ago, before the Orders erased everything." Crowley's eyes flicked to Kael's chest, where Soul Reaver pulsed beneath the skin. "You're not hiding that thing very well. Every Awakened in Thornwick can feel it. You're like a bonfire in a world of candles."
The younger man shifted his weight. Kael tracked the movement, calculating distance, angle, the position of the tunnel's branching paths behind him.
"I don't want trouble," Kael said.
"Neither do I. But trouble finds us all down here." Crowley stepped closer. His lantern illuminated the tunnel walls—carved symbols, some familiar from the Sepulcher tablets, others unlike anything Kael had seen. "The Orders know about you. The cult knows about you. Even the Thralls know."
"Thralls?"
"Drowned worshippers. They serve something old that lives in the deep places. Something that hungers." Crowley's face hardened. "They've been coming up through the old rifts more often lately. Something's waking them."
A distant splash echoed from somewhere in the darkness. The younger man's hand moved to the sword at his hip.
"Walk with me." Crowley turned, not waiting for an answer. "I have things to show you. Information you need if you want to survive the week."
Kael hesitated. Trusting strangers meant dying sooner. But starving in a tunnel meant dying anyway.
He followed.
---
The tunnels twisted downward, passing through chambers that had once been grand—ballrooms, throne rooms, temples carved for gods no one remembered. Crowley moved through them like he knew every stone.
"You've been trying to harvest," the old man said. Not a question.
"That corpse back there. No soul left."
"Because someone harvested it first. The Drowned take everything. Leave nothing behind but meat." Crowley paused at a massive door, its surface covered in chains. "This is where I keep my collection."
The younger man produced a key and unlocked the chains. The door groaned open.
Beyond lay a cavern converted into something between a museum and a tomb. Glass cases lined the walls, each containing objects—bones, weapons, scrolls, artifacts that hummed with dormant power. At the center stood a table covered in maps, papers, drawings.
"Soul Reavers were not always hunted," Crowley said, walking among his collection. "Before the Orders consolidated power, before the Concordat, we had a different name. We were the Wardens. We kept the seals strong. We harvested the Entities themselves when they slipped through."
Kael stared at a glass case containing what looked like a rib bone the size of his forearm. Black veins ran through it, pulsing faintly.
"That belonged to the Whisper King," Crowley said, following Kael's gaze. "Not the current one. The first. Before he became what he is."
"First?"
"Everything has a beginning, boy. The Reaver you carry—it came from someone. Someone who walked these tunnels, who fought the Drowned, who kept the old bargains." Crowley turned to face him. "You're standing in his footsteps right now."
Another splash, closer this time. Water dripping from stone that should have been dry.
"Thorne." Crowley snapped his fingers. The younger man drew his sword.
The water came first. It seeped from the cracks in the ceiling, thick and dark, smelling of salt and rot. Then came the sound—wet dragging, like something heavy being pulled across stone.
The door exploded inward.
What came through was human once. Now it was a ruin of flesh and barnacles, eyes replaced with pearls, mouth stretched too wide. Its skin glistened with seawater, and where it walked, the stone cracked and weed sprouted.
[System Notification] Thrall of the Drowned detected. Spiritual signature: Degraded Entity-touched. Warning: Combat capability present.
"Stay behind me!" Crowley shouted, but Kael was already moving.
The Thrall lunged. Thorne intercepted it, his blade flashing with spiritual energy, carving a line of light across the creature's chest. The Drowned thing shrieked—a sound like ships breaking apart—and swept its arm. Thorne flew backward, crashing into one of the glass cases.
Kael had no weapon that could hurt this thing. His knife would bounce off scales.
But Soul Reaver pulsed in his chest, hungry, waiting.
The Thrall turned toward him. Those pearl eyes fixed on his chest. On the mark.
It knew what he was. What he could become.
The creature charged.
Kael dove sideways as claws raked the air where his head had been. He hit the ground rolling, came up with his hand pressed flat against the stone floor.
"Physical contact required," he muttered. "Have to touch the corpse."
But this thing wasn't dead. Yet.
Thorne staggered upright, blood streaming from a gash on his forehead. Crowley had retreated behind the central table, pulling something from his coat.
The Thrall came again. Faster this time.
Kael dropped low, letting claws pass over his shoulder, and drove his knife into the creature's forearm. The blade sank deep, but the Drowned thing barely slowed. It grabbed him by the throat and lifted.
Webbed fingers closed tight. Kael's vision darkened at the edges. The creature's breath stank of brine and graves.
"You carry the mark," it rasped. Water poured from its mouth. "We have waited. We will wait longer."
It threw him.
Kael hit the wall hard enough to crack stone. He slumped to the ground, tasting copper. His ribs screamed. His knife was gone.
Thorne charged again, spiritual blade singing. This time he aimed for the neck. The sword bit deep. Black ichor sprayed. The Thrall's head hung by a thread of flesh.
But it didn't fall.
It reached back, grabbed Thorne by the face, and *squeezed*.
Bone cracked. The younger man's scream cut short.
Then Crowley was there, driving something into the Thrall's back. A needle, long as a forearm, filled with liquid that glowed silver-blue. The creature convulsed, releasing Thorne, who crumpled to the ground.
"Run!" Crowley shouted. "It won't stay down long!"
Kael crawled toward Thorne's body. The young man wasn't moving. Blood pooled beneath his head at an wrong angle.
He wasn't breathing.
"Dammit." Kael pressed his hands against Thorne's chest. Still warm. Still fresh.
The Thrall staggered, the needle's contents burning through its system, but it wouldn't stop. Wouldn't die. Entities didn't die easily.
Kael placed both palms flat against Thorne's torso.
[System Notification] Corpse detected. Freshness: Optimal. Soul harvest available. Initiating extraction...
The Reaver woke.
Power surged through Kael's arms—not painful, but intense, like plunging his hands into lightning. Something pulled from the dead man's chest, flowing upward, filling channels in Kael's soul he hadn't known existed.
[System Notification] Soul Harvest: You extracted Combat Instinct (Minor) from the remains. Spiritual Resonance Acquired. Minor enhancement to reaction time and fighting awareness.]
[System Notification] Soul Harvest: You extracted Spiritual Blade Fragment from the remains. Partial ability acquired. Duration: Limited. Cost: None.]
Kael opened his eyes. His hands glowed with faint silver light—the same light that had burned from Thorne's sword.
The Thrall turned toward him. Its ruined face registered something like surprise.
"No," it gurgled. "Not yet. Not—"
Kael moved.
He didn't think about it. His body knew what to do. The Combat Instinct fed him angles, timing, the precise spot where the creature's neck connected to its spine. His glowing hands sliced through corrupted flesh like it was wet paper.
The head came free.
The body collapsed. Water rushed out of it, spreading across the floor, and the smell of rot intensified. Kael stood over the corpse, breathing hard, his hands still burning with borrowed light.
"Soul harvest," he said. "Requires a corpse."
He'd been wrong. Thorne wasn't the corpse. The Thrall was.
[System Notification] Soul Harvest: You extracted Water-Touched Resistance from the remains. Partial Entity resistance acquired. Warning: Unstable integration.]
[System Notification] Soul Harvest: You extracted Memory Fragment from the remains. Information acquired. Accessing...]
Information flooded Kael's mind. Not memories, exactly. More like echoes. A glimpse of vast dark spaces. Voices singing in a language that hurt to hear. A message meant for someone called the Keeper.
*They are waking. The old seal weakens. Bring the Reaver to the deep chamber.*
"What did you see?" Crowley demanded. He'd moved to Thorne's body, closing the dead man's eyes with rough efficiency.
"Instructions." Kael's voice came out steadier than he felt. "The cult wants me brought somewhere. A deep chamber."
"That'll be the old rift temple. Under the harbor." Crowley straightened, age showing in every line of his face. "That's where they keep the worst ones. The ones too dangerous to kill."
The silver light faded from Kael's hands. The borrowed abilities sank back into his soul, waiting to be used.
"Thorne," Kael said.
"Dead. Nothing to be done." Crowley's voice was flat. "He knew the risks."
"He's—was he your son?"
"No. He was my student." The old man looked at the shattered glass cases, the scattered artifacts. "The third one this month. The Drowned don't usually hunt in groups, but something's changed. They're organizing."
Kael bent down and retrieved his knife from the floor. His hands weren't shaking. Strange. He felt hollow, like something had been scooped out, but not empty. More like a bowl waiting to be filled.
"You said you wanted to show me something."
"I did." Crowley walked to the central table, pushing aside maps until he found a scroll, ancient and brittle. "This is from the old days. Before the Reavers were hunted. Before the Orders rewrote history."
He unrolled it carefully. The parchment showed a diagram—a human figure surrounded by six symbols, each one representing an Order.
"The original Reavers worked with the Orders. Not as servants. As partners. We kept the seals. We harvested the Entities when they slipped through. We did the work that broke normal men."
The diagram showed something else. A chain of symbols running from the Reaver figure down into the earth.
"Every hundred years, the seals need reinforcement. Blood for the barrier. Soul energy for the locks. The Reavers could provide both. We could harvest from the Entities themselves, turning their power against them."
"But you stopped."
"Because we became too powerful. Too independent. The Orders couldn't control us." Crowley's finger traced the symbols. "So they made us the enemy. Wrote us out of history. Taught their children that Reavers were monsters."
Kael stared at the scroll. "And now?"
"Now the seals are failing. Seventy-two rifts, each one holding something that wants to devour this world. The Orders are fighting among themselves instead of maintaining the barriers. And you—" Crowley looked at him with something that might have been pity. "You're a weapon that doesn't know what you're for."
The Thrall's corpse had stopped bleeding. The water spreading from it was receding now, draining through cracks in the floor.
"What do you want from me?"
"Help me stop what's coming. The Drowned, the cult, the Entities waking beneath the city." Crowley picked up a small object from the table—a medallion, tarnished with age, marked with the same symbols from the scroll. "This belonged to the first Reaver. It will help you control your abilities. Master the harvest. Learn what you can become."
He held it out.
Kael didn't take it. "Why should I trust you?"
"You shouldn't. But you're going to take it anyway, because you have nowhere else to go and the people hunting you will find you within a day if you stay on the surface." Crowley's smile was thin, humorless. "I'm offering direction. A purpose. The choice is yours what to do with it."
The medallion gleamed dully in the lamplight.
Kael reached out and closed his fingers around the cold metal.
[System Notification] Ancient artifact acquired. Soul Resonance detected. Bonding... Bonding interrupted. Insufficient integration. Complete bonding requires proximity to active rift.]
"What does that mean?"
"It means you'll need to visit one of the old temples. Get close to the source." Crowley rolled up the scroll. "There are several in the city. The rift temple under the harbor is the most dangerous, but also the most powerful. The Drowned will be guarding it."
A crash echoed from deeper in the tunnels. More water. More dragging.
"They're coming," Kael said.
"They're always coming. The question is whether you're ready to face them."
Kael looked at Thorne's body, then at the medallion in his hand. The combat instincts from the harvest still hummed in his muscles, sharpening his awareness of the tunnels around him. He could hear water approaching from three directions now.
"I need to get to the surface."
"There's a passage. Through the old temple, then up through the collapsed district. It'll put you near the eastern gate." Crowley pointed to a narrow crack in the cavern wall. "Go now. They'll follow you if they sense the medallion."
"And you?"
"I've survived this long. I know how to hide." The old man picked up his lantern. "Find me when you're ready to learn more. The address is written on the back of the medallion. Come at midnight."
Another crash, closer. The water was spreading faster now.
Kael slipped the medallion into his coat and squeezed through the crack. Stone scraped his shoulders. Darkness swallowed the lamplight behind him.
He ran.
---
The old temple was a ruin of dripping water and crumbling pews. Kael moved through it by touch more than sight, one hand on the wall, the medallion warm against his chest. Behind him, the sounds of pursuit faded. Either the Drowned had lost his trail, or they were taking a different route.
He found stairs leading up. Ancient steps, worn smooth by centuries of feet. At the top, a rusted grate blocked the way.
Kael pressed against it. The metal groaned but held.
He could feel Soul Reaver stirring again, hungry, wanting more. The harvests from Thorne and the Thrall had been satisfying, but incomplete. The Reaver wanted deeper connections. More power.
*I could harvest from living things,* he thought. *If I killed someone, I could take everything they had.*
The idea should have horrified him. Instead, it felt natural. Like breathing.
He pushed the thought aside. Not yet. Not like that.
The grate finally gave way. Kael pulled himself up into cold night air, rain pattering against his face. He was in the collapsed district—buildings half-fallen, streets choked with rubble. Ahead, through the rain, he could see lights. Thornwick's eastern quarter. Safety, for now.
He walked.
The streets were empty at this hour. Kael kept to the shadows, using the Combat Instinct to sense movement before it happened. A cat fled from a doorway. A drunk slept against a wall, oblivious. Nothing threatened him.
The medallion pulsed once against his skin. A warning or a welcome—he couldn't tell which.
In an alley near the eastern gate, Kael stopped. Someone was watching him. He could feel the weight of their attention like a hand on the back of his neck.
"Coming or going?" A woman's voice, sharp with amusement.
She stepped from the shadows. Young, maybe twenty, wearing leathers too fine for the district. A sword hung at her hip. Her eyes gleamed in the rain.
"Doesn't matter," Kael said. "Move along."
"Easy." She raised her hands. "Just passing through myself. Name's Mira. I work for someone who wants to meet you."
"I'm not interested."
"You will be." She glanced down the alley, then back at him. "He knows about the cult's plans. The seventy-two rifts. What they're planning to unseal."
Kael's hand drifted toward his knife.
"The Whisper King is moving pieces into place. The Drowned are just one faction. There are others—worse ones." Mira's voice dropped. "They're not trying to weaken the seals. They're trying to break them. All of them. At once."
The rain intensified. Kael thought about the memory fragment from the Thrall. *They are waking. The old seal weakens.*
"Why tell me this?"
"Because you're the only one who can stop them." Mira smiled, thin and knowing. "Soul Reavers can harvest Entities. Turn their power against them. The Orders don't know how anymore, but you might figure it out."
"And what do you get out of it?"
"The Whisper King's plans failing would suit me just fine." She stepped back toward the shadows. "Think about it. If you want answers, I'm at the Hanging Lamp tavern. Tomorrow night."
She vanished into the darkness.
Kael stood in the rain, thinking about power, about purpose, about seventy-two rifts waiting to tear the world apart.
The eastern gate loomed ahead. Beyond it, the road led out of Thornwick. Away from the cults, the Orders, the Entities stirring in the deep.
The medallion pulsed again.
He had choices to make. The Reaver hungered. The world was ending.
Kael started walking toward the gate.
Behind him, in the darkness of the collapsed district, something ancient opened its eyes.
It had felt the medallion. Felt the echo of the first Reaver's power.
*Not yet,* it thought in a voice like ocean depths. *But soon.*
The rain fell harder.
