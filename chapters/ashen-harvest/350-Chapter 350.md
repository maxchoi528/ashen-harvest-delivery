The abandoned warehouse on Miller's Wharf had been his refuge for two weeks. The roof leaked, the floorboards creaked, and the previous occupants had left behind enough rats to populate a small city. Somebody was planning something massive. And the Orders were too busy fighting each other to see it coming.

He read through the morning, hunched in a corner where the roof didn't leak. The cipher was old, the handwriting cramped and hurried, but the content...
The first Soul Reaver had been a woman named Seren Blackthorn. She'd Awakened in the aftermath of the Abyssal Wars, when the seal network was new and fragile. She'd discovered her ability by accident—touching a dying soldier, feeling his memories flood into her like water through a broken dam.

*Harvesting grants power*, she wrote. *But it takes something too. A piece of the harvester's soul bonds with each corpse consumed. Too many harvests, and the boundary between self and other blurs. The memories of the dead begin to overwrite your own. Their desires become your desires. Their fears cage you in ways you cannot escape.*
*The only way to survive long-term is to choose carefully. Harvest only those whose remnants strengthen you. Refuse the easy kills, the plentiful corpses, the temptation to consume everything you touch.*
*Or become a monster. One that looks human. One that doesn't even know it's lost itself.*
Kael read the passage three times. He'd never considered the possibility that harvesting could change him—not spiritually, not mentally. He felt fine. Normal. Himself.
But how would he know if he wasn't?
The answer, he realized, was in the journal. Seren had developed techniques for maintaining her sense of self. Mental exercises. Discipline. Regular meditation to separate her memories from those she'd consumed. She recommended keeping a journal of personal experiences—something to anchor identity when the boundaries blurred.
He'd been so focused on survival that he'd never thought about preservation.
Seren had also written about her discoveries regarding the seal network. The journal contained detailed observations about the 72 rifts and the methods used to maintain the seals. But more importantly, it contained her analysis of the system's vulnerabilities.
*The seals are not passive barriers*, she wrote. *They require active maintenance. Spiritual energy must be channeled through the network regularly, or the rifts widen. The Orders perform this function through their rituals, but the rituals are designed to fail if any single faction withdraws support.*
*This makes the system fragile. Any faction that controls enough of the network can force the others to comply with its demands. Any faction that falls can trigger a cascade failure.*
*And the Abyssal Entities know this. They have been working for centuries to corrupt the Orders from within, to create fractures that can be exploited when the time is right.*
Kael's blood ran cold. The cultists weren't just attacking the seal network directly. They were exploiting existing divisions, accelerating the corruption Seren had identified centuries ago.
He kept reading. The final entries were dated thirty years before Seren's disappearance. The last page contained a single line:
*The truth is worse than I imagined. The Entities are not our enemies. They are our creators.*

The cultists weren't fanatics—they were soldiers in a war that had been fought in shadows for centuries.
And he was standing in the middle of the battlefield without a map.

Fifty sovereigns. That's what he was worth to the people of Thornwick. A reward for anyone willing to sell him out. He'd been reported eleven times in a month—which meant at least eleven people had considered turning him in.
Maybe more.

Someone who came before him.

The Mortician's Guild chapel on Harrow Street. He'd passed it twice in the last week without knowing what lay beneath. The Guild was one of the Six Orders—the organization responsible for harvesting soul-essence from the dead and maintaining the legal supply of spiritual currency. *Kael Ashborn—*
*You have been reported to the Iron Vanguard. They are mobilizing a kill squad. You have until sunset to leave the city or die within it.*
*If you want to survive, meet me at the old clock tower. Come alone.*
*A friend.*
Kael read the note twice. The handwriting was feminine—similar to the girl's from the alley, but more controlled. More practiced.
The Shadow Court? Another faction entirely?
He had no way of knowing. But the mention of the Iron Vanguard was credible—they'd been hunting him for weeks, and his escape from their patrol two days ago would have humiliated them. A kill squad was exactly the kind of response they were capable of.
Sunset. Four hours away.
He could run. Abandon the warehouse, leave Thornwick, find somewhere else to hide while he processed the information he'd gathered. That was the smart play. The safe play.
But Seren Blackthorn hadn't survived by being safe. She'd survived by being dangerous—and by knowing when to take risks that others wouldn't.
Kael gathered the contents of the hidden compartment and secured them in his coat. He kept the medallion around his neck, tucked beneath his shirt where it pressed against his skin like a second heartbeat.
The vial he placed in his inner pocket, wrapped in cloth to prevent accidental exposure. The letter he read once more, memorizing the details, then burned.
The clock tower was twenty minutes away if he moved quickly. The Iron Vanguard barracks was closer—fifteen minutes at a brisk walk. The Mortician's Guild chapel on Harrow Street was eight minutes in the opposite direction.
He had four hours. Enough time to gather intelligence, but not enough to do everything.
Kael stepped out of the warehouse and into the afternoon light. The streets of Thornwick bustled around him—merchants hawking their wares, workers trudging home from factories, children playing in the gutters. Normal people living normal lives, oblivious to the war being waged beneath their feet.
He wondered how many of them knew about the seals. How many understood that reality itself was fraying at the edges. How many cared.
Probably not many. People rarely did, until the monsters came through the walls.
Kael moved through the crowd, keeping his head down, his spiritual signature suppressed by the dampening effect of the warehouse still clinging to his coat. He reached the clock tower district as the sun began its descent toward the horizon.
The old clock tower rose above the surrounding buildings like a monument to a forgotten age. Its face was cracked, its hands frozen at a quarter past three—stuck there for as long as anyone could remember. The building had been condemned decades ago, deemed unsafe for occupation, but it remained standing out of stubbornness or sentiment.
Kael entered through a gap in the boarded-up door. The interior was dark, dusty, filled with the debris of abandoned occupation. Broken furniture. Old newspapers. The skeletons of rats.
And at the base of the stairs leading upward, a figure waiting in the shadows.
"You came." The voice was familiar—the girl from the alley, but steadier now. More confident. "I wasn't sure you would."
Kael didn't reach for a weapon, but he kept his hand close to his coat pocket where he'd stored a short blade. "You set me up. The bounty notice, the timing of your appearance. You were testing me."
"I was assessing you." The girl stepped forward into a shaft of light. Up close, he could see her more clearly—young, maybe seventeen now, with sharp features and calculating eyes. "The Shadow Court doesn't recruit from the gutter. But I was raised in the gutter. I know how to spot someone who might be worth saving."
"Someone might be worth saving," Kael repeated flatly. "You have a high opinion of me."
"I have an accurate opinion of you." She pulled a crumpled paper from her pocket—the bounty notice. "Fifty sovereigns. That's what they'd pay for you. Do you know how much my father owed the Guild before they took everything? Forty sovereigns. My sister's treatments cost another twenty. We're drowning in debt, and the Orders don't care. They never cared."

"Because money doesn't solve the real problem." The girl's jaw tightened. "My sister is dying. The best healers in Thornwick have given up on her. But there's a treatment—a real one—that the Mortician's Guild keeps hidden. Soul-essence therapy. They could save her, but they won't. They hoard their supplies for the Awakened, for the Orders, for anyone with power. The rest of us can rot."
She was angry. The kind of anger that came from years of watching people you love suffer while the powerful looked away. Kael knew that anger. He'd lived it.
"What do you want from me?"
"The Mortician's Guild has a vault beneath their chapel on Harrow Street. I've seen their records—I'm a clerk, before you ask, one of the many invisible people who make the city's bureaucracy function. The vault contains their excess supplies. Soul-essence, treatments, artifacts. Enough to save my sister and a hundred others like her."

"Because I put those supplies there." The girl's mouth twisted. "I've been skimming from their shipments for two years. I know the security patterns, the access codes, the guard rotations. But the vault door is sealed with something I can't break. A spiritual lock keyed to the medallion of a Guild Master."
She looked at him—really looked at him—and Kael understood what she was asking.
"The medallion," she said. "Where did you get it?"
"I didn't. I found it. In a cache left by someone who came before me." Kael reached into his shirt and drew out the silver medallion. It caught the fading light, its symbols glowing faintly. "A friend."

Kael considered the offer. It was risky—breaking into a Guild facility, stealing their hoarded supplies, violating every protocol the Orders had established. If he was caught, he wouldn't just be hunted. He'd be executed publicly, his body displayed as a warning to other potential criminals.
But if he succeeded, he'd have access to resources he couldn't get anywhere else. Soul-essence. Treatments. Information. And a potential ally in a city where allies were scarce.
He also had the journal's warnings to consider. Seren had written about the corruption in every Order, including the Mortician's Guild. Stealing from them might mean stealing from the rot itself—or it might mean taking weapons from one nest of vipers to fight another.
"Show me the vault," Kael said. "I'll decide after I see what's inside."
The girl exhaled sharply—relief, surprise, something else mixed together. "The sun sets in two hours. We need to move now."
She led him out of the clock tower, through back alleys and side streets, always avoiding the main thoroughfares where guards and patrols congregated. Kael followed, memorizing the route, noting the security patterns she pointed out, filing away every detail.
The Mortician's Guild chapel on Harrow Street was an imposing structure—all dark stone and stained glass, designed to evoke mortality and the promise of spiritual transcendence. The facade was decorated with carved skulls and weeping angels, their stone faces worn smooth by centuries of rain.
The girl stopped at a side entrance, hidden behind a moss-covered statue of Saint Mortis, the Guild's patron saint. She produced a key, unlocked the door, and gestured Kael inside.
The corridor beyond was dimly lit by chemical lanterns, their greenish glow casting long shadows across the walls. The air smelled of chemicals—preservatives, disinfectants, the sharp tang of spiritual energy being processed and refined.
"Guard rotation changes in twelve minutes," the girl whispered. "We have a window."
They moved quickly, silently, the girl leading and Kael following. She knew this place intimately—the shortcuts, the blind spots, the places where the lanterns didn't reach. She'd been skimming supplies for two years. She'd mapped every inch of the facility in her head.
The vault entrance was at the end of a long hallway, marked by a heavy iron door inscribed with spiritual symbols. The girl's hand trembled slightly as she pointed to the lock mechanism—a circular depression designed to accept a medallion.
"That's it. That's the keyhole."
Kael approached the door. The symbols etched into its surface pulsed with faint energy, responding to his presence. His medallion grew warm against his chest, resonating with the lock.
He didn't hesitate. He pressed the medallion into the depression.
The door shuddered. The symbols blazed with light. Then, with a grinding roar that echoed through the entire building, the vault opened.
Inside was a treasure trove.
Shelves lined the walls, loaded with soul-essence vials in every size and quality. Healing artifacts gleamed in the chemical light. Medical equipment—devices Kael didn't recognize but could guess the purpose of—stood ready for use. And in the center of the room, on a raised platform, a single crystal container holding a swirling mass of concentrated spiritual energy.
The girl made a sound somewhere between a gasp and a sob. "There must be hundreds of vials here. Thousands. Enough to save everyone."
Kael stepped inside, his eyes scanning the room. The spiritual energy in the air was overwhelming—thick, potent, almost suffocating. He could feel his senses straining to process the density of soul-essence surrounding him.
And then he saw it.
On a shelf in the corner, partially hidden behind a stack of medical supplies, a familiar object. A journal. Leather-bound, aged, its pages yellowed with time.
Seren Blackthorn's journal wasn't the only record of the first Soul Reaver's existence.
There was a second one.
Kael crossed to the shelf and pulled down the journal. The handwriting on the first page was identical to the journal Vex had given him—but the contents were different. This was an earlier version, written before Seren had fully understood what she was discovering.
The first entry was dated three hundred years ago.
*Today I learned the truth about the Entities. They are not invaders. They are not enemies. They are what waits on the other side of death—the next stage of existence, the realm where souls finally become what they were always meant to be.*
*The Orders know this. They have always known. The seal network was never meant to protect us from the Abyss. It was meant to trap us here, in flesh and limitation, feeding the Entities through our deaths without ever allowing us to join them.*

Kael read faster, his eyes devouring the words. Seren had pieced together the conspiracy through decades of investigation. The Orders weren't defenders of humanity. They were controllers—keeping humanity trapped in the physical realm because the Entities needed a constant supply of souls to maintain their existence.
When people died naturally, their souls returned to the Abyss and were consumed by the Entities. But when souls were harvested through spiritual rituals, their energy could be captured and used before it returned to the cycle. The Mortician's Guild existed to manage this flow—harvesting soul-essence from the dying and dead, processing it into vials that could fuel spiritual advancement, and ensuring that the Entities received only what they were owed.
The seal network wasn't failing because of external attack. It was failing because the Orders were letting it fail. A controlled collapse would give them leverage over the Entities—threaten to open the rifts entirely unless their demands were met. The corruption Vex had mentioned wasn't weakness. It was strategy.
