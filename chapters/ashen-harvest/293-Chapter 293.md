"Move it, corpse-bait. Daylight's burning."
The guard shoved Kael forward through the rusted doorway. His boots scraped against wet cobblestones as he stumbled into the Mortician's Guild processing facility, the smell of lye and copper hitting his nostrils like a physical blow. Three days of "voluntary" labor detail for questioning—officially. The unofficial reason sat in his pocket like a coal: a forged requisition bearing the seal of a dead courier, promising access to the Guild's "secondary storage wing."
The secondary storage wing. Where they kept the bodies that weren't supposed to exist.
"You again." The foreman, a heavyset man with forearms like smoked hams, looked up from his ledger. His eyes performed a dismissive sweep of Kael's frame. "Thought they rotated you lot through the bleaching vats by now."
"Short-staffed," Kael said. His voice emerged rough from three days of minimal talking. "They need able bodies for the transit preparations."
The foreman snorted. "Able. Right." He stamped the requisition and shoved a ring of iron keys across the counter. "Bay fourteen. And if anything comes up missing, the fault falls on your head. Understood?"
Kael pocketed the keys and walked deeper into the facility, his footsteps echoing through corridors lined with clay jars and canvas-wrapped bundles. The overhead lamps cast pools of sickly yellow light, separated by stretches of shadow. His Sovereign-stage spiritual senses stretched outward, tasting the ambient energy of dozens of dead—not departed, but lingering, their residual essence slowly bleeding into the ley lines beneath the city.
Bay fourteen sat at the end of a narrow corridor, its door marked with a symbol Kael recognized from childhood nightmares: a circle bisected by a vertical line, representing the separation between the living and the dead. The Mortician's Guild claimed it as their foundational sigil. Kael knew it as something older, something that predated the Orders by centuries.
The lock clicked. The door swung inward on oiled hinges.
The chamber beyond held no bodies.
Instead, rows of glass cylinders filled with luminescent fluid lined the walls, each one containing a suspended form—human shapes curled like sleeping children, their eyes closed, their flesh preserved in perfect stasis. A dozen, maybe more. Kael's breath caught as he stepped closer, his heart hammering against his ribs.
These weren't corpses. These were living people, suspended in a state that felt neither alive nor dead, their spiritual signatures flickering like dying candles.
"What the hell..."
A whisper brushed against his awareness—not sound, but intent. Kael spun, one hand dropping to his belt where a blade would hang if he'd been allowed to carry one. The chamber remained empty. The cylinders hummed with their strange preservation fields. But something had changed; a pressure against his spiritual senses, a presence that hadn't been there a moment before.
"You're early," a woman's voice said from behind the door he'd just closed. "The records said you'd arrive tomorrow."
The door swung open. A woman stepped through, her features obscured by a deep hood, her body wrapped in robes that seemed to drink the lamplight. She moved like someone accustomed to commanding attention through presence alone.
Kael's hand closed around a loose pipe he'd spotted leaning against the wall. "You knew I was coming."
"I know many things, Soul Reaver." The title landed like an accusation. "I know what you did to the three guards at the Veiled Market. I know about your conversation with Aldric at the Sunspire Chapel. And I know you're looking for the woman called Sera—whose sister happens to be in bay seventeen, cylinder seven."
Each revelation hit like a physical blow. Aldric's betrayal hadn't been subtle, but hearing it confirmed by a stranger stirred something cold in Kael's chest. "Who are you?"
The hood fell back. The woman's face was striking in its severity—high cheekbones, a scar bisecting her left eyebrow, eyes the color of tarnished silver. Kael recognized her from wanted posters scattered across half the city's bulletin boards.
"Whisper," he said, the name escaping before he could stop it.
"Among other names." The woman—whoever she was—smiled without warmth. "The Shadow Court has taken an interest in your activities, Kael Ashborn. We've observed your harvests. Your investigations. Your... Persistence in matters that would be wiser to abandon."
"You followed me here."
"We own this facility. The Mortician's Guild is one of our oldest assets in Thornwick." She gestured at the cylinders. "These are our subjects. Volunteers for experiments in spiritual preservation. The flow is imperfect, but promising."
Kael's grip tightened on the pipe. "You're keeping living people in jars."
"We're keeping them out of the Abyss." Her eyes glittered with something that might have been passion or madness. "The seals are failing, Soul Reaver. Not gradually, not slowly—in weeks, not decades. The 72 Entities will pour through the rifts, and everything this world has built will collapse into the void. Unless we find a way to anchor reality before the membrane tears."
"That's not your call to make."
"No. But it's the only call that matters." She stepped closer, her voice dropping to an intimate murmur. "The Whisper King has watched you for a long time, Kael. He sees what you could become—what you could do, if you were properly guided. The Soul Reaver ability is not random. It is not a curse. It is a key, and the lock it fits opens paths most Awakened cannot even perceive."
The words triggered something in the back of Kael's mind—a flash of memory he couldn't grasp, a sensation of recognition that made his skin prickle. The Soul Reaver ability had always felt like a tool, a means to an end. But hearing this woman speak of it as something deliberate, something designed, opened doors in his understanding he wasn't sure he wanted to explore.
"I'm not interested in being anyone's pawn," he said.
"Everyone is someone's pawn." The woman's smile sharpened. "The question is whether you know whose game you're playing, and whether you've found a way to win."
She produced a small leather folder from her robes and extended it toward him. "The location of Sera's sister. Proof that the cult activity in Thornwick is not random but organized—coordinated from a central command that answers to Prime Zarek himself. And a name: the warehouse where they'll move the suspended subjects within forty-eight hours, before the Council of Six inspection team arrives."
Kael didn't take the folder. "What's the price?"
"Your presence at a meeting. Nothing more. The Whisper King wishes to speak with you directly." She pressed the folder into his hand before he could refuse. "Refuse, and Sera's sister becomes another casualty in the Mortician's Guild's 'processing errors.' Accept, and you gain an ally with resources, information, and the ear of powers you've only begun to understand."
She turned toward the door, then paused. "One more thing, Soul Reaver. The six Orders exist to fight the Abyss. That is their purpose, their justification, their reason for being. But they are also divided—corrupt, inefficient, each hoarding secrets from the others. The seal network requires all six signatures to maintain full strength. If even one Order falls, or defects, or is compromised..."
She didn't finish the sentence. She didn't need to.
The door closed behind her with a soft click, leaving Kael alone among the humming cylinders and the frozen faces of the suspended.
He stood motionless for a long moment, processing the implications. The Shadow Court—long suspected to be a deniable operations arm for multiple Orders—was apparently something far more ambitious. They weren't just conducting covert missions. They were preparing for an apocalypse, using methods that would make them monsters in any conventional moral framework.
And they wanted him.
Kael opened the leather folder. Inside, a single sheet of paper bore an address: the old Ashworth textile mill, abandoned since the steam strikes of '73. Beside the address, a time: midnight, three days hence.
The location of Sera's sister: bay seventeen, cylinder seven. A small map showed the route through the facility's service tunnels.
And at the bottom of the page, a name he didn't recognize, followed by a title that made his stomach clench:
*Lord Varen Ashworth, Keeper of the First Seal.*
The First Seal. The oldest of the 72, supposedly placed at the founding of the Orders, its location known only to the Keeper and the Council of Six. If Varen Ashworth was the Keeper, and he was working with the Shadow Court...
Kael folded the paper and tucked it into his coat. His mind raced through possibilities, discarding theories as quickly as they formed. One thing remained certain: whatever game was being played in Thornwick, he was already a piece on the board.
The question was whether he could find a way to change the rules.
He moved through the facility's service tunnels, following the map's sketched lines. The corridors here were older, their stonework predating the Guild's official founding by centuries. Kael's fingers traced symbols carved into the walls—warnings, he thought, or maybe wards. The deeper he went, the stronger the ambient spiritual pressure became, pressing against his senses like deep water.
Bay seventeen sat at the end of a branching passage, its door reinforced with iron bands and sealed with a lock that glowed faintly purple—the telltale signature of Arcane Spire enchantment. Someone had gone to considerable expense to keep people out. Or to keep something in.
Kael pressed his palm against the lock, feeling the enchantment's structure with his Sovereign-stage perception. The wards recognized unauthorized access and began to discharge—but his own spiritual energy was already flowing outward, not to break the enchantment but to convince it that he belonged. A delicate operation, requiring precise control. Three months ago, he would have destroyed the lock entirely. Now, he could afford subtlety.
The purple glow faded. The door swung inward.
Bay seventeen held only a single cylinder, larger than those in the main chamber, filled with fluid that swirled with bioluminescent patterns. Within it floated a young woman—barely more than a girl, her features hauntingly similar to the portraits Kael had seen of Sera's missing sister. Her eyes were open, staring at nothing, but her lips moved in a soundless rhythm.
Kael's hand found the cylinder's access panel. His fingers hovered over the release mechanism, then stopped.
The woman inside the cylinder wasn't breathing. Her chest remained motionless, no rise or fall, no indication of the biological processes that should accompany living flesh. But she wasn't dead either—her spiritual signature burned brighter than any of the other suspended subjects, a fire that refused to be extinguished.
She was trapped between states. Suspended in a limbo that kept her body preserved while her consciousness drifted somewhere else entirely.
"Gods forgive me," Kael whispered.
He released the catch.
The cylinder's seal hissed as it disengaged, fluid draining into channels in the floor. The woman collapsed forward, and Kael caught her before she hit the ground, lowering her gently as the last of the preservation fluid drained away. Her skin was cold, her pulse absent—but her eyes moved, tracking his face with desperate intensity.
"K... Kael..." Her voice emerged as a croak, barely audible.
"I'm here. I've got you."
She seized his wrist with fingers that shouldn't have had the strength to grip. "They took... They took the others. Moving them tonight. Before the inspection. Zarek wants them at the warehouse, at the... The anchor point."
"What anchor point?"
"The seal. They're using us to strengthen the seal, but not the way they say. Not to close it." Her eyes rolled back, showing whites. "To open it. Just a little. Just enough for... For what's waiting on the other side to come through."
The words hit like ice water down Kael's spine. "The cult isn't trying to prevent the rifts from opening. They're accelerating it."
"The Whisper King... He needs a bridge. A living soul anchored to the Abyss, but still tethered to the Material. Enough to reach through, to grasp, to..." She convulsed, her grip on his wrist spasming. "Sera. My sister. She's not here. They moved her two days ago. The warehouse. She's one of the bridges now."
The warehouse. The same warehouse where the Shadow Court had promised to reveal Sera's sister's location. The same warehouse where the meeting with the Whisper King was scheduled.
A trap. Or an opportunity. Or both.
Kael stood, the woman's body still clutched in his arms. Her eyes had closed, her breathing shallow but present—she'd pushed herself beyond her limits, speaking through the prison of her suspended state. He laid her down carefully, arranging her limbs in a position that wouldn't restrict her nonexistent circulation.
"I'll find her," he said to no one. "I'll find them all."
The service tunnel stretched ahead, leading toward the facility's loading docks. Kael moved through shadows, his Sovereign-stage senses sweeping for threats. The facility's guards had rotated to the main building, leaving the secondary wing unmonitored. Convenient. Almost as if someone had arranged for him to find what he'd found.
The loading dock opened onto an alley behind the Guild's main building, where horse-drawn wagons waited in the gaslight. Workers moved between them, loading canvas-wrapped bundles that Kael knew contained more than old cloth. The midnight shift, preparing for an operation that would move human cargo to a warehouse on the city's eastern edge.
A hand landed on his shoulder.
Kael spun, one arm sweeping toward the attack. His elbow connected with something solid, and a grunt of pain rewarded his reflexes. In the dim light of a single gas lamp, he made out the figure of a young man—gaunt, hollow-eyed, clutching a bloody nose.
"Sorry, sorry!" the man gasped. "I wasn't attacking. I'm not... I'm with them. The ones who sent you."
Kael's stance didn't relax. "Who?"
"The Shadow Court. I'm..." The man glanced nervously at the loading dock. "I'm a runner. They told me to intercept you, to give you this."
He thrust a folded paper toward Kael. The seal on the back bore the symbol of a whispering face—a symbol Kael now recognized as the Shadow Court's primary identifier.
"The meeting's been moved up," the runner said in a rush. "Midnight tonight, not three days from now. The Whisper King wants to speak with you before the warehouse operation begins. He'll be at the old clock tower, the one overlooking the eastern docks. Alone."
"Why?"
"I don't know. I just deliver messages." The runner was already backing away, his bloody nose forgotten in his hurry to escape. "Please, I have a family. They made me deliver your location to the cultists too. If they find out I'm talking to you..."
He vanished into the shadows before Kael could respond.
The message in Kael's hand contained only a single line of text, written in elegant script that seemed to shimmer in the gaslight:
*Tonight, the clock strikes twelve. Do not be late.*
Kael crumpled the paper and shoved it into his coat with the other documents. His mind churned through possibilities, implications, risks. The Shadow Court wanted him at a meeting. The cult was moving "bridges" to a warehouse. Sera's sister was already there. And the Whisper King—whoever he was, whatever he truly wanted—was preparing to make contact.
Three hours until midnight.
The clock tower loomed against the night sky, its face illuminated by a ring of spectral flames that burned without fuel or heat. Kael climbed the external stairs, his boots ringing against iron that had rusted into patterns like frozen waves. The city sprawled below him, gaslit streets and steam vents and the distant roar of the river.
The tower's peak held a single chamber, its walls lined with gears and counterweights that had once controlled the clock's massive hands. Now the mechanism sat silent, frozen at eleven fifty-seven.
The Whisper King waited in the center of the room.
He was not what Kael had expected. An old man, frail and bent, wrapped in robes that hung from his shoulders like funeral shrouds. His face was a map of wrinkles, his eyes milky with cataracts—but when those eyes found Kael's, something ancient looked back. Something that had seen centuries turn and empires crumble.
"You came," the old man said. His voice carried the rasp of disuse, as if speaking was an effort he rarely undertook. "I wasn't certain you would."
"You knew I'd find your message."
"I knew you'd find what you were looking for. The information about Sera's sister. The confirmation of your suspicions about the cult. The invitation that seemed too good to refuse." The Whisper King's lips twitched—a smile, maybe, or the memory of one. "Everyone finds what they're looking for, when they look in the right places. The question is whether they recognize it when they find it."
Kael remained near the doorway, his hand resting on the pipe he still carried. "Who are you?"
"I'm what's left of someone who once walked your path. A Soul Reaver, like you. Different era, different world, but the same hunger." The old man gestured to a chair opposite his own. "Sit. We have much to discuss, and little time to discuss it."
"You killed people."
"Many. I harvested. I grew. I became something more than human, and something less." The Whisper King's milky eyes never wavered. "I made choices that haunts me still. But I also learned things—secrets that the Orders have buried for two hundred years, truths that could reshape this world if they were ever spoken aloud."
"And you want to tell me."
"I want to offer you a choice." The old man leaned forward, his voice dropping to a whisper that seemed to echo from everywhere at once. "The seals are failing. Not gradually—catastrophically. The cult has found a way to accelerate the flow, to punch holes in the Veil and let the Entities through. They're not madmen, Kael. They're believers. They think they can control what comes through, negotiate with powers that existed before humanity took its first breath."
"They're wrong."
"Completely. The Entities don't negotiate. They consume. If the cult succeeds, this world becomes an extension of the Abyss—a feeding ground for beings that have no concept of mercy or restraint." The Whisper King's hands trembled as he reached into his robes, producing a small object that glowed with inner light. "This is what I came to offer you. The original Sepulcher Tablet, the one recorded by the first Soul Reaver before he vanished. It contains the True Name of the Entity that rules the Great Rift—the key to controlling the largest seal, or destroying it entirely."
Kael stared at the tablet. His Soul Reaver senses screamed warnings he couldn't articulate, instinct crying out that the object was significant beyond measure. "Why give this to me?"
"Because you're the only one who can use it. The tablet responds to Soul Reaver energy, but most of my kind burned out before they could attune to its frequency. You..." The old man studied him with those blind eyes. "You're different. Your harvests, your growth pattern, the abilities you've developed—I've watched you since Thornwick. You're not just a Reaver. You're an heir."
The clock struck midnight. The sound reverberated through the tower, through the stone, through Kael's bones.
"Take it," the Whisper King whispered. "Use it to find Sera's sister before the cult completes their ritual. Then decide what you believe—about the Orders, about the cult, about the choice that awaits us all."
Kael's hand moved before his mind could stop it. His fingers closed around the tablet, and the world shifted.
Light poured through him—not external, but internal, a radiance that seemed to emanate from his very soul. The tablet pulsed against his palm, warm and alive, and in that moment of contact Kael understood something he hadn't known he'd needed to understand.
The Soul Reaver ability wasn't a gift. It wasn't a curse. It was a key.
And somewhere in the darkness beyond the Veil, something had noticed him turning it.
[System Notification: Soul Harvest. You have acquired: Sepulcher Fragment (attunement in progress). The True Name is fragmentary. Full attunement requires proximity to the Great Rift.]
The light faded. Kael stood in the empty tower, the tablet clutched to his chest, the sound of the clock's final chime still echoing in his ears.
Below him, in the streets of Thornwick, a convoy of covered wagons moved toward the eastern warehouse. Sera's sister was inside one of them. The cult's ritual would begin at dawn.
And in the shadows that clung to the clock tower's base, Kael became aware of presences watching him—multiple figures, their spiritual signatures masked by techniques that even his Sovereign-stage senses couldn't penetrate. The Shadow Court? The cult? Or something else entirely?
His grip tightened on the tablet as he descended the stairs, his mind racing through the implications of what he'd learned. The Whisper King was a former Soul Reaver, just as the hidden line had suggested. The original Soul Reaver's fate remained unclear, but the tablet's existence proved that his legacy was more than legend.
And now Kael carried a piece of that legacy into a conflict that was about to escalate beyond anything he'd faced before.
The warehouse waited. Sera waited. Dawn approached.
Kael moved through the midnight streets, a ghost among the living, his soul burning with questions that demanded answers and a power that demanded blood.
He would find the warehouse. He would find Sera's sister. And then he would make the Whisper King explain exactly what the True Name could do—and what price the world would pay if the cult spoke it first.
