The blade sank into the cultist's throat before he could scream. Kael wrenched the weapon free, stepping over the twitching body as smoke billowed from the collapsed building behind him.
"Contact down. One confirmed." He wiped the edge on his sleeve, eyes scanning the narrow alley. "Squad, sound off."
"Corvin here. North flank clear."
"Mira holding south. We've got movement two streets over."
Kael's jaw tightened. "How many?"
"At least a dozen. They're moving in formation."
Formation. That word lodged beneath his ribs like a splinter. Cultists operated in cells, never coordinating beyond their own groups. Someone wasing these people like soldiers.
The Soul Reaver pressed forward through Thornwick's industrial quarter, boots crunching over shattered glass and fallen bricks. Steam hissed from ruptured pipes, mingling with the acrid bite of burning timber. Three hours ago, his contact at the Veiled Market had died getting this intelligence to him—a map marking cultist positions in the district, schedules for their movements, names.
The information cost a man's life. Kael intended to make it worth something.
He reached the rendezvous point, a gutted warehouse half-collapsed from yesterday's fighting. Corvin waited by the entrance, his weathered face unreadable in the dim light filtering through gaps in the roof. The grizzled veteran had fought with Kael for two years now, never asking questions about the bodies Kael collected after battles.
"Six cultists spotted heading toward the old textile mill," Corvin reported. "Armed, but no manifestation circles visible."
"Abyssal-touched?"
"Didn't get close enough to tell."
Mira emerged from the shadows behind them, her slight frame barely making a sound on the debris-strewn floor. She was younger than Corvin, faster, and carried two short blades she'd taken from corpses during the Siege of Ashford. "The mill has three exits. We hit them from the east and west simultaneously, cut off the northern retreat."
"Agreed." Kael studied the crude map drawn on oilcloth. "But this isn't random patrol activity. Look at their routes."
Mira leaned closer. Her brow furrowed. "They're... Circling something."
"Or someone." Corvin tapped a intersection on the map. "Every patrol passes through here. That building used to house the district's water pumping station."
"An anchor point," Kael said.
Mira's eyes widened. "They're trying to corrupt a municipal anchor? That's—the Orders would never allow—"
"The Orders are stretched across six fronts right now." Kael folded the map and tucked it inside his coat. "What they allow and what they can stop are different problems."
The three of them moved through the ruins of Thornwick's industrial quarter, past shuttered factories and collapsed worker tenements. The district had been evacuated two weeks prior, when the first cultists appeared. Now only the dead remained, and those who'd chosen to die with their homes than flee.
Kael counted seventeen corpses as they traveled three blocks. Civilians, all of them—spiritually inert workers and their families who'd lacked the means or will to escape. The cultists had been thorough.
"Here." Mira held up a fist, dropping low behind a toppled cart.
Kael followed her gaze. Figures moved through the fog ahead, their movements synchronized in a way that made his skin prickle. Six cultists, dressed in ragged robes dyed the sickly gray of corrupted spirit energy. They carried swords and short spears, weapons that pulsed with faint luminescence.
Corvin's breath hissed between his teeth. "Those blades are awakened steel. Low-grade, but real."
Someone was arming these cultists. Someone with access to the Orders' own armories or connections to black market forges that could imbue metal with spiritual properties.
Kael watched the patrol pass, then checked the position of the sun through breaks in the smoke-choked sky. "We follow at distance. I want to know where they're based."
"That's risky." Mira's voice carried an edge. "If they're using formation tactics, they have a commander somewhere. A competent one. We get too close, they'll spot us."
"Then we don't get close." Kael was already moving, keeping to the shadows of collapsed walls and burned-out structures. The cultists moved with purpose, never deviating from their path, never breaking formation even when obstacles forced them to reroute. Military discipline from people who should have been farmers and tradespeople drawn to fanatical belief.
Something had changed in the cults. Something beyond random violence and desperate rituals.
They followed the patrol for nearly an hour, tracking them through the district's maze of streets and alleys. The cultists never returned to any central location, instead moving between observation posts—basements, abandoned shops, rooftop positions with clear sightlines. Intelligence gathering. The organized kind.
"Twelve positions mapped so far," Mira whispered as they crouched behind a collapsed wall. "They've got eyes on the entire eastern quarter."
"Someone's planning something big." Corvin scratched his beard, a nervous habit Kael recognized. "Coordinated observation like this—it's preparation for a strike."
"A strike against what?" Mira asked.
Kael considered the question. The industrial quarter held no strategic value beyond its factories, and those had already been damaged by earlier fighting. No military installations, no Order halls, no noble estates worth raiding.
Unless they weren't planning to raid. Unless they were planning something else entirely.
"The water pumping station." He said it aloud, and the pieces rearranged themselves in his mind. "They've been corrupting anchor points. What if they're not trying to destroy them?"
"Taking control?" Corvin shook his head. "That's blood magic. Twisted, inefficient. You can't hold an anchor through corruption—it fights back, tears itself apart."
"Not if you control the corruption's source."
Mira went pale. "They're not corrupting the anchors. They're using the anchors to open something."
The Abyssal membrane theory. Kael had read about it in texts recovered from the Sunken Archives—scholars who'd theorized that sufficiently corrupted anchor points could punch holes through the Veil, allowing direct passage between the Material and the Abyss. The practice had been theoretical, forbidden, abandoned centuries ago.
Someone had revived it.
Movement ahead. The patrol had stopped, gathering around a figure emerging from an alley. New arrival, taller than the cultists, wrapped in a dark cloak that obscured features. The cultists bowed— bowed—as the figure approached.
Kael's hand found his blade's hilt. Something about the figure's posture, the way the cultists defer to it. Not just a commander. Something more.
"Shadow Court," Mira breathed.
Kael's attention snapped to her. "What?"
"The way they move. The deference." Mira's voice dropped to barely audible. "I recognize the training. That's Shadow Court protocol."
Shadow Court operatives didn't work with Abyssal cults. The Court's entire existence was built on deniable actions for Order interests, not direct alignment with the enemy. Even corrupt elements of the Court wouldn't openly side with cultists.
Unless this went beyond corruption. Unless someone inside the Orders had decided to abandon their posts entirely.
The hooded figure spoke to the cultists, too quiet for Kael to hear the words. Then the group dispersed, three heading north, three south. The hooded figure remained, turning slowly as if scanning the surroundings.
Kael felt the weight of observation settle over him like a physical force. The figure was looking directly at their position—not at the wall behind which they crouched, but at him. At Kael specifically.
"Move," he hissed.
They retreated through the ruins, doubling back twice before making for the pumping station. The fog had thickened as evening approached, turning the district into a maze of shadows and half-seen shapes. Kael's heart hammered against his ribs, each beat carrying the same question: how had the figure known?
The pumping station loomed ahead, its brick chimney a black finger against the orange-stained sky. The building had seen better decades—paint peeling, windows shattered, the massive water wheel at its side frozen mid-rotation after some mechanism had failed. But the structure itself remained solid, and that meant it could serve as an anchor point.
Corvin took point, blade drawn. Mira flanked right, her blades catching what little light remained. Kael hung back slightly, watching their approach, watching the shadows that pooled around the building's foundation.
Something was wrong.
The door hung open, one hinge broken, the other bent outward as if by tremendous force. Dark stains marked the threshold—old blood, several days gone by the color. Inside, the darkness absolute.
"Wait here," Kael said.
"Kael—" Mira started.
"I mean it. If I'm not out in five minutes, fall back to the secondary rendezvous. Report what we found to the Iron Vanguard."
"And leave you here?"
"I'll find my own way out." He met her eyes, letting her see the certainty there. "But someone needs to survive to carry this intelligence. You know that."
She didn't like it. He could see the refusal forming on her lips. But Mira was pragmatic, had survived this long by making hard choices. After a moment, she nodded.
Kael entered the pumping station alone.
The darkness inside was thick, almost liquid. His eyes adjusted slowly, revealing shapes in the gloom: massive pipes running along the walls, a rusted catwalk overhead, the frozen wheel visible through gaps in the floorboards. The smell hit him next—rot and copper and something else, something that made his throat constrict.
Corruption. The spiritual kind, not only moral.
He moved forward, one hand trailing along the wall, feeling for traps or ambush points. His boot struck something soft. He knelt, fingers finding fabric, then skin, then the empty cavity where a face should have been.
More bodies. At least six, arranged in a circle around what appeared to be a pit dug into the station's foundation. Kael's hand found the edge of the pit, and he recoiled as spiritual energy prickled against his palm.
A ritual circle. Partial, incomplete, but active. The spiritual pressure radiating from it made his teeth ache.
Someone had been using this location for dark workings. The bodies weren't sacrifices—they were fuel, their deaths feeding energy into whatever the circle was meant to accomplish. And the ritual wasn't finished.
Kael stood, mind racing. The cultists' coordinated movements made sense. They'd been protecting this site while their master worked, guarding against interruption. But the figure he'd seen—the Shadow Court operative—had been directing them away from here. Away from discovery.
Why?
Movement above. On the catwalk.
Kael's blade cleared its sheath as he dropped into a crouch, eyes tracking the shadow that resolved into a figure standing at the railing. The figure hadn't attacked. Wasn't even holding a weapon. Just stood there, watching.
"You shouldn't be here." The voice was female, calm, almost conversational. "The ritual requires four more days to complete. Your presence now would destabilize the entire process."
Kael's grip tightened. "Who are you?"
"My name is of no consequence. What matters is that you understand what you're looking at." The figure gestured at the pit below. "This anchor point connects to the Deep Veil—the membrane between our world and the true Abyss, not the thin barrier that the Orders monitor. When the ritual completes, we will open a passage that has been sealed since before the Abyssal Wars."
"Impossible. The Deep Veil is myth—"
"Another lie the Orders tell to maintain control." The figure descended the catwalk ladder, each movement precise and unhurried. As she reached the floor, Kael got his first clear look at her face: middle-aged, sharp features, eyes that reflected light like a cat's. "The first Soul Reaver knew the truth. That's why they created the Sepulcher Tablets—to record how the Deep Veil could be breached."
Kael's blood chilled. "You're working with the cults."
"I'm working with those who understand that the world must change. The Orders have grown complacent, corrupt, useless. The seal network weakens while they squabble over territorial boundaries and political advantage. Forty years ago, the Great Rift would have been contained within days. Now it festers, spreading corruption across the continent."
"And opening passages to the Abyss is the solution?"
"The Entities aren't demons, boy. They're alternatives. Beings of pure potential, unconstrained by the laws that bind your precious Orders. The cultists understand this, even if they lack the sophistication to implement it properly." She stepped closer, and Kael's blade rose higher. "But you—you could be the bridge. The Soul Reaver abilities were designed for exactly this purpose. Harvesting energy, yes, but also channeling it. Directing spiritual forces in ways that Awakened cannot."
"You want me to join you."
"I want you to understand that you're already part of this. Your existence is an affront to the natural order—a walking contradiction that the Orders should have destroyed centuries ago. Instead, they tolerate you because you're useful. But when the time comes, when they realize what you truly represent, they will burn you like any other abomination."
The words struck harder than Kael wanted to admit. He'd suspected as much, of course. The wary looks from Order representatives, the careful distance maintained even by those who claimed to be allies. But hearing it spoken aloud, by someone who clearly had access to information most people never saw...
"Why tell me this?"
"Because I want you to choose. Not for us, not for the Orders, but for yourself. The ritual will complete in four days. When it does, the Deep Veil will tear open, and what emerges will reshape this world. You can be part of that reshaping, or you can die with the old guard." She spread her hands, a gesture of openness that seemed deliberately provocative. "The cultists you've been hunting? They're pawns. The real players have been moving pieces for decades, preparing for this moment. Even now, three other anchor points across the continent are being prepared. The Orders know about none of them."
Three anchor points. That aligned with intelligence Kael had gathered about cult activity in other cities—but he'd assumed it was coincidence, separate cells operating independently. Organized assault changed everything.
"How do I know you're not lying?"
"You don't. But consider this: I've had three opportunities to kill you since you entered this building. Instead, I'm offering information that could save your life." She turned toward the exit. "The Shadow Court has been monitoring you for months. We know about your... Harvesting. We know what you can do. The Orders will learn eventually. When they do, your usefulness will matter less than your threat potential."
She was halfway to the door when Kael spoke again. "What's your stake in this? Really?"
The woman paused. "I was there when the first Soul Reaver fell. I watched the Orders tear apart everything they'd built because they feared what he'd become. I've spent forty years preparing for another chance." She glanced back over her shoulder. "The Sepulcher Tablets contain more than True Names. They contain instructions. Instructions that were never meant to be destroyed, only hidden until the world was ready to accept them."
"And you think it's ready now?"
"The world is dying. The seals fail, the Entities stir, the Orders rot from within. 'Ready' stopped being relevant decades ago." She stepped through the doorway. "Four days, Soul Reaver. Decide what you want before then."
Kael stood in darkness, surrounded by the dead and the incomplete ritual, the woman's words echoing in the space between his thoughts. Four days. That was all the time he had before whatever she was planning came to fruition.
Corvin and Mira found him a minute later, weapons ready. "We heard voices," Mira said. "What happened?"
"Information." Kael moved toward the exit, pausing at the threshold to look back at the ritual circle. "The cult activity in Thornwick isn't random. They're preparing anchor points for a coordinated breach attempt."
"Breach?" Corvin's weathered face went pale. "They mean to open the Veil directly?"
"Not the normal Veil. Something deeper." Kael didn't elaborate. He wasn't sure he believed it himself, but the evidence surrounded him—the bodies, the ritual circle, the woman's certainty. "We need to get this intelligence to the Orders immediately."
"Which ones?" Mira asked. "The Iron Vanguard? The Order of Dawn?"
Kael considered the question. The woman had mentioned the Shadow Court monitoring him, which meant information shared with any Order could easily reach Court ears. But keeping silent meant risking everything on his ability to stop the ritual alone.
"The Order of Dawn," he decided. "They're the most likely to take Abyssal threats seriously. And they have the resources to investigate anchor points across multiple cities."
They moved through the ruined district as darkness settled fully over Thornwick, past bodies and burning ruins and the ever-present fog. Kael's mind churned with everything he'd learned, every implication spinning outward into new possibilities and terrors.
Four days to prevent the end of the world. Four days to decide whether the woman had been telling the truth. Four days to figure out who he could trust.
A sound behind them. Footsteps, following at a careful distance.
Kael raised a hand, signaling the others to halt. He turned, scanning the shadows, every sense straining for confirmation of what he already knew.
Someone was tracking them. Someone skilled enough to match their pace through darkness and rubble without being detected until they wanted to be.
The footsteps stopped. Silence pooled around them, thick and expectant.
"Show yourself," Kael called out.
A figure emerged from the gloom. Not the woman from before—a different shape, smaller, more mobile. A blade glinted in their hand.
Corvin's blade cleared its sheath. Mira dropped into a combat stance.
But Kael held up a hand, stopping them. He recognized the figure's posture, the way they held themselves. Not an enemy.
"Lyra."
The Shadow Court operative stepped forward, her face half-hidden by a hood. Behind her, two other shadows waited—additional operatives, positioned to cut off retreat routes.
"Kael." Her voice was flat, carrying none of the warmth it once had. "You need to come with me."
"Based on whose authority?"
"Mine." Lyra's hand rested on her blade's hilt. "The Court knows what you've learned tonight. We know what you're planning to do with that information. And we need to have a conversation about your next steps."
"I'm not interested in conversations with the Court."
"This isn't a request." Her eyes flicked to Corvin and Mira. "Your companions can leave. You cannot."
Kael's grip tightened on his own blade. He'd known this moment would come eventually—had felt it gathering since the day he'd awakened with Soul Reaver abilities still burning in his blood. The Orders tolerated him because he was useful. The Court monitored him because they feared what he represented. And now both had converged on this moment, in this ruined district, with the fate of the world hanging in the balance.
"Four days," he said quietly. "Is that what you're here to discuss?"
Lyra's expression flickered—surprise, quickly suppressed. "You've spoken with her already."
"She told me about the Deep Veil. The ritual. The other anchor points." Kael took a step forward, closing distance. "She also told me the Court has been watching me for months. Watching my harvesting. Knowing what I am and saying nothing."
"We were deciding what to do with you."
"And now you've decided."
Lyra didn't answer directly. Instead, she tilted her head slightly, a gesture Kael remembered from their first meeting—a sign that she was communicating with someone not present, using some technique of the Awakened that he'd never fully understood.
"Yes," she finally said. "We've decided."
The two shadows behind her shifted position. Corvin and Mira tensed, ready for violence.
Kael evaluated his options in the space of three heartbeats. He could fight—his abilities gave him advantages that conventional Awakened couldn't match, and he'd killed enough enemies to know he wasn't squeamish about adding more. But Lyra had been an ally once, a genuine connection in a world of temporary alliances and convenient lies. Killing her would cost something, even if he won.
The question was whether that cost was worth paying.
"Talk first," he said. "Then we decide together what comes next."
Lyra studied him for a long moment. Then, slowly, she nodded.
"Walk with me. Away from your guards."
Kael gestured for Corvin and Mira to hold position, then followed Lyra deeper into the ruins, to a place where the shadows gathered thick enough to hide them from casual observation. When they stopped, they stood in what had once been a worker's garden—overgrown now, weeds choking out whatever flowers had once grown there.
"The woman you met," Lyra began without preamble. "She's not wrong about the threat. The Deep Veil ritual is real. Three other anchor points are being prepared in Verantum, Kesswick, and the Iron Coast. When they all activate simultaneously, the barriers between realities will tear permanently."
"You knew this and did nothing?"
"We were gathering intelligence. Understanding the full scope of the threat before moving to counter it." Her voice carried an edge now. "Something you might consider before charging into situations where you don't belong."
Kael's jaw tightened. "The cultists have been operating for months. People have died."
"People die every day. That's not the issue." Lyra turned to face him fully, and in the dim light, he could see the strain written across her features—exhaustion, frustration, something that might have been fear. "The issue is that stopping this ritual won't end the threat. It's only one phase of a larger plan."
"What phase?"
"I don't know. That's why I need you."
The admission hung between them, unexpected and heavy. Lyra, who had always seemed in control, who had navigated Court politics with calculated precision, was asking for help.
"Explain."
"The ritual requires specific conditions to succeed. One of those conditions involves soul energy harvested from a Soul Reaver." She watched his face carefully. "Not you specifically. But a Soul Reaver. Any Soul Reaver."
Kael's blood went cold. "They need someone like me."
"More than someone like you. They need someone with access to the Sepulcher Tablets, because those contain the activation sequence. And the only person we've ever confirmed as reading those tablets is you."
The pieces fell into place with sickening clarity. The woman's visit wasn't coincidence. The information she'd shared wasn't generosity. She'd been scouting him, testing his reactions, evaluating whether he could be turned or whether he needed to be eliminated.
"They'll come for me."
"They're already coming. Tonight was a test. The woman reported your response to her handlers, and now they're deciding whether to accelerate their timeline." Lyra reached into her cloak, producing a small vial that caught the faint light. "This contains a tracking compound. Once you ingest it, we'll be able to locate you anywhere in the city. If the cults move against you before we're ready, we can intervene."
"And if I refuse?"
"Then you die alone, without backup, without support. The cultists are hunting you specifically now. Every hour you remain outside our protection is an hour they have to plan your capture."
Kael looked at the vial, then at Lyra's face. The woman he'd known months ago would have presented this as a choice, offered alternatives, respected his autonomy even when it conflicted with her goals. This Lyra was different—harder, more desperate, operating under pressures he couldn't fully see.
"How deep does this go?" he asked quietly. "Inside the Court. How many are working with the cults?"
Lyra's silence was answer enough.
"The woman from the pumping station. She was Shadow Court once, wasn't she?"
"Her name is Vesper. Former First Blade of the Order of Dawn, executed for heresy forty years ago." Lyra's voice dropped lower. "Except she wasn't executed. She was extracted, her death faked, and she's been working in the shadows ever since. Building networks, cultivating assets, preparing for exactly this moment."
"The Orders know?"
"The Order of Dawn knows. They voted to suppress the information than admit their own former champion had turned." Bitterness edged into her tone. "By the time the other Orders learned anything useful, it was too late to act. Vesper had already established herself too deeply in the cult infrastructure."
Kael processed this, fitting it into the larger puzzle. A former Order champion leading the cults. Shadow Court operatives compromised at multiple levels. Anchor points across three cities being prepared simultaneously. And somewhere in the middle of it all, a Soul Reaver whose abilities made him either the solution or the weapon.
"Four days isn't enough time to stop all four rituals."
"No. It's barely enough time to choose which one to prioritize." Lyra held out the vial again. "Take it. Let us help you. Then we can start planning."
Kael stared at the vial, feeling the weight of the decision pressing against him. He didn't trust the Shadow Court—had never trusted them, not fully, despite his working relationship with Lyra. But trust was a luxury he couldn't afford when the alternative was walking into the cults' trap blind.
He took the vial.
Lyra's shoulders relaxed slightly. "Thank you."
"Don't thank me yet. This alliance only works if you share everything. Vesper's plan, the cultist networks, the Deep Veil ritual specifics. No more intelligence gaps."
"Agreed." She pulled a folded paper from her cloak. "This contains the locations of the three other anchor points, along with what we know about their defenses and personnel. Study it before dawn."
Kael accepted the paper, tucking it inside his coat beside the map. "What about the cultists in Thornwick? The ritual here is still active."
"We're handling it. A strike team is already being assembled. By the time you finish reviewing the intelligence, the pumping station will be secured." Lyra paused. "One more thing. Your guards—Corvin and Mira—they're compromised."
Kael's hand moved toward his blade. "Explain."
"Corvin has been receiving payments from a Veiled Market intermediary for the past six months. We don't know what information he's shared, but he's been talking. Mira's cleaner, but she's connected to the Order of Dawn through her family. If the Order learns what you've discovered before we're ready to release it, they'll move to contain you."
"You want me to kill them?"
"I want you to be careful around them. Watch their reactions. See if they guide your decisions in specific directions." Lyra stepped back into the shadows. "We have until dawn. After that, the cults will know we're moving against them, and everything accelerates."
She was gone before he could respond, melted into the darkness like she'd never been there. Kael stood alone in the ruined garden, the vial heavy in his hand, the paper heavy in his coat, and the weight of impossible choices pressing down on his shoulders.
Four days. Three cities. Two supposed allies, one of them possibly a traitor. And somewhere in the world, a woman who'd been dead for forty years, preparing to tear open reality itself.
He walked back to where Corvin and Mira waited, watching their faces in the darkness. Corvin looked worried—always worried, always calculating odds. Mira looked angry, still bristling from being excluded from the conversation.
"Well?" Mira demanded. "What did she want?"
Kael weighed his options. Lyra's warning echoed in his mind: watch their reactions, see if they guide your decisions. But he couldn't test their loyalty by confronting them directly—not without risking the alliance she'd offered.
"Intelligence exchange," he said carefully. "The Shadow Court has information about the cult activity. They want to coordinate our efforts."
"Since when does the Court share intelligence?" Corvin's brow furrowed. "They hoard information like dragons hoard gold."
"Since the threat became too big for them to handle alone."
Mira's eyes narrowed. "You believe them?"
Kael considered the question. Did he believe the Shadow Court? Did he believe Lyra, specifically, after everything that had changed between them? The answer was complicated, tangled up in history and suspicion and the simple fact that he had no better alternatives.
"I believe they're afraid," he said finally. "And people who are afraid make interesting allies."
He didn't mention the vial in his pocket, or the paper in his coat, or the suspicions Lyra had planted about his companions. Some information had to stay close, had to be tested before it could be trusted.
They moved through the ruins toward the secondary rendezvous point, four people in the darkness—three maybe loyal, one definitely compromised in ways he didn't fully understand. The fog swirled around them, thick and persistent, and somewhere in the distance, something howled.
The war had finally found him. And now Kael Ashborn had four days to decide whether he was the world's salvation or its final destruction.
The choice, as always, would be his alone.
