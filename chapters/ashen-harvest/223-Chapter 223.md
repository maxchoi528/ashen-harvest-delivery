Kael's boot struck the cobblestone with the weight of a man who had already died once. The alley behind the Mortician's Guild reeked of lye and old copper—standard for a district where body disposal happened around the clock. His fingers tightened around the knife at his belt. Three days of tracking cult movements through Thornwick's underbelly had led him here, to a dead end that wasn't dead at all.
"You move like a grave-robber." The voice came from the shadows pooling beneath a rusted drainpipe. A figure stepped forward, lantern raised. The light carved harsh angles across a face Kael recognized from wanted notices across six districts. "Vane. The Order of Dawn's favorite attack dog."
Vane's laugh was dry as parchment. "And you're the man who crawls out of execution graves. Impressive survival instinct, Ashborn." He set the lantern on a broken crate. "The Shadow Court sent me to discuss an arrangement."
"I don't negotiate with assassins."
"I'm not here to kill you." Vane spread his hands, palms empty. "I'm here because something in Thornwick is waking up, and it doesn't care which side of the Orders' war it burns."
A door creaked open at the alley's far end. Kael pivoted, blade drawn, only to find a young woman in Mortician's Guild grey emerging with a body bag slung over her shoulder. She froze at the sight of the two men—one a wanted criminal, the other a decorated Order operative.
"Evening," Vane said pleasantly. "We'll be another few minutes."
She didn't need to be told twice. Her footsteps echoed as she fled back inside, presumably to fetch help.
"You have maybe two minutes before she brings the Guild's spiritual enforcers down on us." Vane's tone remained conversational. "So let me make this simple. Zarek isn't working alone. He has people inside every Order, inside the Council itself. Whatever he's planning for the sealed rifts, it requires something from a Soul Reaver to complete."
Kael kept his blade level. "You want me to believe the Order of Dawn cares about stopping cults?"
"I don't care about the Orders." Vane's expression hardened. "I care about the Abyss staying sealed. My sister died holding a line against Entity incursion fifteen years ago. The Concordat means nothing if there's nothing left to concord."
A bell rang in the distance—standard alarm for spiritual disturbance in the district. Kael calculated distances, exits, the angle of moonlight through the rooftops. His senses had grown sharper since reaching Transcendent, picking up details that would have escaped him at lower stages. He could feel the subtle wrongness in the air now, the way certain shadows didn't move correctly when wind passed through them.
"You're being watched," he said. "Right now. Northeast rooftop, second chimney."
Vane didn't turn. "I know. Three of them. They arrived an hour before you did."
"Then why are we talking instead of fighting?"
"Because they're not my enemies." Vane finally moved, stepping closer until the lantern light fell fully on his face. Scars traced his jawline, the kind left by spiritual burns. "They're yours. The Shadow Court has been tracking your every movement since you crawled out of that grave. You've become the most interesting variable in their calculations."
"And you're here to warn me? The Order of Dawn playing protector now?"
"I'm here because you're the only one who can read the Sepulcher tablets." Vane reached into his coat and withdrew a folded paper, stained at the edges with something dark. "This fell out of a cult courier's satchel three nights ago. It's written in the old tongue—the one only Soul Reavers can fully translate."
Kael didn't take the paper. "Give me one reason to trust anything from the Order of Dawn."
"Because I pulled it from a dead man's hand." Vane's voice dropped. "A watcher. One of the Shadow Court's. He'd been gutted and left in the canal district with this clutched to his chest. Whoever killed him wanted us to find it. Wanted you to find it."
The alarm bells multiplied. Running footsteps now, multiple sets, heading their direction. Vane tucked the paper back into his coat.
"The Mortician's Guild enforcers will be here in ninety seconds. They're not friendly to either of us—you're a wanted Reaver, and I'm suspected of Shadow Court ties. We can fight our way out separately, or we can move together and save the energy for what's hunting us."
"What's hunting us?"
Vane's gaze flicked upward, toward the rooftops where the watchers lurked. "The same thing that's been hunting every Soul Reaver for the past century. The same thing that made the original Soul Reaver disappear." He paused. "The same thing that's getting impatient."
Kael sheathed his blade. Not trust—pragmatism. The enforcers would arrive in seconds, and even with his Transcendent abilities, fighting a full Guild enforcement squad alongside dealing with Vane would drain reserves he couldn't afford to waste.
"Where?"
"The old lighthouse at Thornwick's northern point. It's been abandoned since the last Entity breach forty years ago. Meet me there at midnight." Vane extended his hand. "And bring that blade of yours. We're going to need it."
Kael clasped the offered hand briefly, felt the calluses of someone who'd spent decades wielding spiritual weapons. The contact lasted exactly long enough to confirm no hidden attacks, no spiritual binding rituals. Vane released and moved toward the alley's opposite exit, vanishing into shadow with the practiced ease of someone who'd made hiding an art form.
The enforcers arrived moments later—six of them, led by a woman whose spiritual pressure radiated the cold precision of someone who'd killed Entity-touched cultists for sport. Her eyes swept the empty alley, found nothing, and her expression suggested she'd be filing a very frustrated report.
Kael was already gone, climbing the drainpipe with silent efficiency, moving across rooftops the way he had during his years as a runner for the Veiled Market. The city spread beneath him in layers of gaslight and steam, a organism that never fully slept. He paused at a junction where three alleyways met, checking his mental map of Thornwick's districts.
The old lighthouse. He knew the place—everyone did. It marked the spot where the Abyss had broken through during the Third Incursion, where the Orders had thrown everything they had to seal the rift before it consumed the entire northern coastline. The battle had killed hundreds of Awakened, left the lighthouse a shattered husk that no one had bothered to rebuild. The surrounding blocks had been condemned, sealed off, forgotten.
Perfect meeting place for a conversation no one wanted overheard.
He reached the lighthouse an hour before midnight, finding a perch in a collapsed building across the street that gave him a clear view of the main entrance. The watchers Vane had mentioned were still there—he could feel them now, three distinct spiritual signatures that moved with disciplined patience. Professional. Well-trained. Definitely Shadow Court, not common cultists or Guild enforcers.
The Moon rose to its zenith. A door creaked open.
Vane emerged from the lighthouse's gutted interior, carrying the same lantern from before. He set it on a stone foundation that might have been a fountain, its basin long since cracked and dry. His posture was relaxed, but Kael had learned to read the tension beneath apparent ease. Vane was ready for a fight. He'd chosen not to start one.
"You're late," Vane called up to the rooftop. "Thought you'd stand me up."
"You thought wrong." Kael dropped from his perch, landing in a crouch that absorbed the impact silently. "Let's skip the games. What did the courier know?"
Vane handed over the paper. This time Kael took it, unfolding the brittle sheet to reveal dense script in a language that seemed to shift and writhe at the edges of perception. Only Soul Reavers could read it fluently—the words weren't just symbols, they carried resonance, echoes of whoever had written them centuries ago.
His breath caught. The tablet. It referenced the Sepulcher tablets.
"It's a shopping list," Kael said slowly, translating the flowing script. "Ingredients for some kind of ritual. Soul-essence from a Reaver, blood from an Entity-touched, the heart of a seal-keeper..." He looked up. "They're not trying to open one rift. They're trying to open all of them."
"Every single one of the seventy-two." Vane's face had gone pale. "The cult has been collecting the components for decades. The items on that list—they've been acquiring them one by one, pulling from the black market, stealing from Order vaults, sacrificing cultists who volunteered for Entity-touch."
"And the Soul Reaver component?"
"That's what I need you to understand." Vane stepped closer. "Soul Reavers aren't just useful for the ritual. They're necessary. The ritual specifically requires someone who can harvest the dead without destroying the soul's integral structure. Regular soul-essence extraction shatters the spiritual matrix. Only a Reaver can extract it intact."
Kael's grip tightened on the paper. "And you think I'm the backup Reaver they've been looking for."
"I think you're the reason they've accelerated their timeline." Vane met his eyes. "When you crawled out of that grave, when you demonstrated the ability to harvest from your own execution, you proved something the cult had given up hoping was possible. A Soul Reaver who could fully access the old abilities. Who could potentially read the tablets that might tell them how to unmake the seal network entirely."
The wind picked up, carrying the salt-rot smell of the distant harbor. Kael thought of Thornwick spreading below them, millions of souls living their lives in blissful ignorance of what was gathering in the shadows beneath their city. The cultists weren't random. They weren't even just fanatics. They were an organized force with resources, intelligence, and a clear objective that required his specific abilities to accomplish.
"Where's Zarek?" Kael asked. "The cult leader. Where is he operating from?"
"If I knew that, I'd have sent an Order strike team instead of coming to you." Vane's frustration was evident. "Zarek has been operating out of nowhere for six months. He moves like smoke. Recruits cultists through dead drops and coded messages, never meets anyone directly. The only time he appears is during rituals, and those locations are chosen at the last moment."
"Then how do we find him?"
"We use you as bait."
Kael's hand moved to his blade. "Explain. Quickly."
Vane raised both hands in a placating gesture. "Not like that. The cult knows you're a Soul Reaver. They've been watching you. If we make it look like you're about to harvest something important—something that could advance their ritual—they'll have to intercept. We'll control the location, control the timing. Lure them out on our terms."
"What makes you think they'll take the bait?"
"Because the list includes one more component you haven't read yet." Vane pointed to the paper. "Keep translating."
Kael's eyes moved down the script, past the initial ingredients, to a section written in smaller, more cramped letters. The resonance grew stronger, painful almost, like a pressure behind his eyes.
"Transcendent essence," he read aloud. "Harvested at the moment of breakthrough. Required to bridge the gap between Material and Abyss."
"Every Soul Reaver has zero spiritual cost for harvesting," Vane said. "But you're not just any Reaver. You're Transcendent stage. You've touched abilities that most Awakened will never access. If they could harvest you at the exact moment you broke through to Sovereign—"
"They'd have enough power to crack multiple seals simultaneously." Kael completed the thought. "They don't need to find me. They need to catch me at the right moment."
"Which is why you're not going to break through. Not until we've dealt with Zarek and his network."
"That's your plan? Keep me from advancing?"
"That's my plan to keep you alive long enough to stop them." Vane's voice hardened. "The cult isn't the only threat, Ashborn. The Orders are fractured. The Concordat is failing. Half the Council wants to use the chaos to eliminate rivals. The other half is too paralyzed by protocol to act. And the Shadow Court—" He stopped, jaw tightening.
"What about the Shadow Court?"
"They're not just watching you. They're deciding what to do with you." Vane's admission came reluctantly. "The Shadow Court has resources, intelligence networks, centuries of accumulated secrets. If they decide a Soul Reaver is too dangerous to exist, they'll eliminate you. If they decide you could be useful, they'll try to control you. Right now, they're doing both. Watching. Waiting. And they're getting impatient."
The watchers on the rooftop shifted. Kael felt their attention sharpen, their spiritual signatures pulsing with something that might have been anticipation or warning.
"Your friends are getting restless," he observed.
"They're not my friends. They're my contact's contacts." Vane glanced upward briefly. "I was supposed to deliver a message after this meeting. An assessment of whether you could be turned, controlled, or eliminated."
"And what's your assessment?"
Vane was quiet for a long moment. The harbor bells chimed midnight, twelve long notes that echoed across the ruined district. When he spoke, his voice was lower, stripped of its earlier calculation.
"My assessment is that you're going to do whatever you want regardless of what I recommend. My assessment is that you crawled out of your own grave because you're too stubborn to die when the world tells you to. My assessment is that every Order in Thornwick wants you dead or captured, and you're still walking around making them nervous." He paused. "My assessment is that you're the only person I know who might be able to stop what's coming."
"That's not an answer."
"It's the only answer I have." Vane retrieved his lantern. "The Shadow Court expects me back within the hour. If I tell them you're uncooperative, they'll move to contain you by force. If I tell them you're considering alliance, they'll give us time to gather intelligence. Neither option is perfect."
"How much time?"
"A week. Maybe two. Enough to find Zarek's operation and burn it to the ground." Vane moved toward the lighthouse door. "Enough to get you closer to whoever's really pulling the strings."
"Wait." Kael stepped forward. "The cult. The ones running through Thornwick's districts, gathering supplies, recruiting followers. You said they're organized. How organized?"
"Cell structure. Compartmentalized information. No single operative knows more than they need to complete their task." Vane stopped but didn't turn. "Standard Order tradecraft. Someone with serious training designed their network."
"The cult has someone inside the Orders."
"Or someone who used to be inside the Orders. Or someone who learned from defectors." Vane's shoulders rose and fell. "The Shadow Court's best analysts think the network traces back to a figure from the Abyssal Wars. Someone who was there when the seals were created. Someone who knows their weaknesses."
Kael's mind raced through possibilities. The first Soul Reaver had created the Sepulcher tablets. Had that person been working with the Orders, or against them? Had the original Reaver discovered something about the seal network that others wanted buried? The tablets contained the True Name—which, according to every account Kael had encountered, was the key to controlling or destroying the Abyssal Entities.
"We need the Sepulcher tablets," he said. "If the cult wants them, if they need a Soul Reaver to read them, then the tablets themselves are the real prize. Not just the components for their ritual. The actual knowledge of how to unmake the seals."
"The tablets are lost. No one knows where the original archive is located."
"Someone knows." Kael thought of the fragmented memories he'd harvested from his execution, the flashes of a life that ended on a scaffold but continued in ways that shouldn't have been possible. "The cult. Zarek. They've been searching for the tablets for years. That kind of obsession doesn't come from nothing. Someone told them the tablets exist. Someone knows where they might be."
"Or someone wants them to think the tablets exist," Vane countered. "A legend to draw cultists out of hiding, expose their networks, identify their leadership."
"Is that what you believe?"
Vane finally turned, lantern light casting half his face in shadow. "I believe that in forty years of service to the Order of Dawn, I've seen the seal network weaken faster than any model predicted. I've seen cult activity spike in patterns that suggest intelligence than coincidence. I've seen Order resources diverted in ways that make no strategic sense. And I've seen the same symbol appearing in places it shouldn't—the mark of an Entity that was supposedly sealed during the First Incursion."
He reached into his coat and produced a small token—a metal disc etched with a spiraling pattern that seemed to move when viewed directly. The symbol Kael had seen scratched into walls, painted on abandoned buildings, carved into the flesh of cultist sacrifices.
"Know what this represents?"
"The Whisper King. One of the Entities."
"One of the Entities that was supposedly destroyed during the First Incursion." Vane's voice dropped to barely above a whisper. "Except the seal network still lists it as active. The original binding still exists. Which means the Whisper King wasn't killed. It was imprisoned. And it's been trying to get free for three hundred years."
The wind died. The watchers above had gone completely still. Even the distant harbor bells had fallen silent.
"You think the cult is working for an Entity," Kael said. "Not just using its power. taking orders from something that's been locked behind a seal for centuries."
"I think the Whisper King found a way to communicate with the Material. I think it's been whispering to desperate people, promising power, salvation, revenge. I think it's built an organization that spans every Order, every major city, every level of society. And I think when it finally breaks free, it won't just open one rift." Vane met his eyes. "It will tear down every seal holding back the Abyss. And then we'll find out what the world was like before the Orders existed."
The silence stretched. Kael felt the weight of it pressing against his chest, the crushing realization that everything he'd been fighting—the cultists, the corrupt officials, the shadow networks—might be symptoms of something far larger. Something that had been patient enough to wait three centuries for the right moment.
"Why tell me this?" he asked. "Why now?"
"Because you're the only one who can do what needs to be done." Vane extended the token. "Because Soul Reavers can harvest the dead, and the dead remember everything. Because somewhere in the remains of whoever's been helping the cult, there's information that could lead us to Zarek. To the Whisper King's contact point. To the truth about the seal network."
"And if I say no?"
"Then I walk back to my handlers and tell them you're a lost cause. They move to eliminate you within the week. The cult continues its work. Zarek completes his ritual. And in six months, the world ends." Vane's expression was unreadable. "But you already knew that. That's why you came tonight. Why you've been tracking cult movements for three days. You're not looking for answers, Ashborn. You're looking for permission."
Kael took the token. The metal was cold, almost painful against his palm, and the symbol seemed to pulse with a rhythm that matched his heartbeat. Or maybe his heartbeat had started matching the symbol. Hard to tell with Entity-touched artifacts.
"The cult cell in the canal district," he said. "I've been watching them for two weeks. They meet every third night in the old warehouse near the fish market. I've counted twelve regular members, three who appear to be Awakened. One of them—I saw her use a technique that felt like Order training. Academy-style binding circles."
Vane's eyes narrowed. "You're certain?"
"Academy of Dawn. Third Ward pattern. She used it to contain something they pulled out of a crate. Something that screamed." Kael handed the token back. "Meet me there tomorrow night. Third bell. I'll have bodies by then."
"Bodies?"
"You said the dead remember everything. I'll harvest what I need from their corpses. Someone in that cell knows something useful. Someone always does."
Vane pocketed the token and stepped back toward the lighthouse door. At the threshold, he paused.
"Ashborn. One more thing." He didn't turn around. "The Shadow Court has a file on every Soul Reaver who's appeared in the past century. Most of them died within months of Awakening. Disease, accidents, Order executions, cult attacks. The longest any of them survived was three years, and that one went mad at the end. Claimed something was speaking to him through the Reaver abilities."
"Something like the Whisper King?"
"Something worse. He said it was the original voice. The first Soul Reaver, trapped in the ability itself, waiting to be freed." Vane's shoulders tensed. "I don't know if it's true. But if it is—if the Soul Reaver power comes with baggage we don't understand—then you might be walking into something none of us can help you with."
"I walked into something when I woke up on that execution platform," Kael replied. "Haven't stopped walking since."
Vane left. The lighthouse door closed behind him with a sound like a coffin lid.
Kael stood alone in the ruined district, surrounded by the shadows of a battle that had ended forty years ago. The watchers above hadn't moved, but he could feel their attention like a physical weight. They were reporting to someone. Someone who would be reporting to someone else. The chain of command stretched back through layers of secrecy that made the Orders' bureaucracy look transparent by comparison.
He looked down at his hands. The knife at his belt. The blade that had been reforged from execution steel. The abilities that let him reach into death and pull out fragments of power that should have stayed buried.
The Moon began its descent. Dawn was still hours away, but Kael could already feel the pressure building—the city's spiritual circulation shifting as Thornwick's millions slept and dreamed and inadvertently fed the Entities that pressed against the Veil in their endless hunger.
Twelve cultists in a warehouse near the fish market. Three Awakened. One with Academy training. And somewhere among them, or somewhere they reported to, the trail that might lead to Zarek. To the cult's leadership. To whatever was whispering from behind the seal that should never have been broken.
Kael started walking. His boots echoed against the cobblestones, a steady rhythm that marked his progress through the city's sleeping districts. He had work to do. Bodies to find. Memories to harvest. And a conspiracy to unravel that went deeper than any he'd encountered before.
The watchers followed at a distance, their spiritual signatures pulsing with curiosity and something that might have been hope. The Shadow Court was interested. The Shadow Court was watching. And somewhere in the hierarchy of powers that ruled Thornwick's underworld, someone was starting to believe that a grave-walking Soul Reaver might be exactly what the world needed.
Kael intended to prove them right.
The streets wound through districts that never fully slept—past the gambling houses of the Merchant Quarter, through the cramped alleys where factory workers rented bunks by the hour, across the bridge where toll collectors had long since abandoned their booths to drink away their wages. Thornwick in the small hours belonged to the desperate, the deviant, and those who had learned that daylight brought its own dangers.
He found his target at the edge of the Dockside, in a building that had once been a chandler's workshop. The sign had been taken down, but the smell of old tallow still clung to the walls. Kael circled the structure twice, mapping entrances, exits, the patrol patterns of the two men lounging near the back door.
Awakened. Both of them. Low stage, barely past the threshold of true perception, but enough to sense spiritual disturbances. If Kael approached directly, they'd alert the others. Better to wait for an opportunity.
He settled into a shadowed doorway across the street, patient as stone. Minutes accumulated like sediment. The Moon continued its descent. The street lamps burned lower, their gas flames guttering in the pre-dawn stillness.
Movement. A side door opened, spilling lamplight across the cobblestones. A figure emerged—female, wearing the nondescript clothing of someone who didn't want to be remembered. She moved quickly, checking corners with the automatic vigilance of long practice.
Kael recognized her. The woman from the canal district. The one with Academy training.
She walked directly toward his position. Not toward the main street—toward an alley that connected to the network of passages threading through the Dockside district. A route that bypassed the main thoroughfares, avoided the watch posts, reduced the chances of encountering anyone who might ask questions.
Perfect.
Kael moved, silent as smoke, closing the distance with the predatory economy of someone who had learned that wasted motion meant wasted survival. She heard nothing until his hand closed over her mouth and his blade pressed against her throat.
"One sound," he murmured against her ear, "and you die here. Nod if you understand."
She nodded. Her body had gone rigid, every muscle locked against the instinct to struggle. But her eyes—her eyes were calculating, assessing, searching for an opening that wouldn't come.
"You work for Zarek," Kael said. Not a question. "You've been running supplies through the canal network for months. The crates that appeared in the warehouse—you brought them in through the underwater passage beneath the old fishmonger's."
Her eyes widened fractionally. Surprise. Real surprise. She hadn't expected him to know about the passage.
"I'm going to release you now. You're going to tell me where Zarek is. And then you're going to take me to him." Kael eased the pressure on her throat slightly. "If you try to run, I'll harvest you where you stand. If you try to fight, I'll harvest you where you fall. If you scream for help, I'll harvest you before anyone can respond. Nod if you understand."
She nodded again, slower this time. Something had shifted in her expression. The fear remained, but beneath it, Kael detected something else. Recognition. As if she'd been expecting this. Preparing for this.
"My name is Cira," she said. Her voice was steadier than he'd anticipated. "I've been waiting for you, Ashborn. The Whisper King told us you would come."
The words hit like a physical blow. Kael's grip tightened on the knife, on her throat, on the reality he thought he understood.
"The Whisper King," he repeated. "You've been in contact with it."
"Directly. Every night. It speaks through the dreams of those who've been marked." Her laugh was bitter, cracked. "You think we built this network on ideology alone? The Whisper King shows us things. Shows us what the world could be if the seals were broken. Shows us the power waiting on the other side of the Veil."
"It's using you."
"We're using each other." She turned her head slightly, not enough to escape his grip but enough to meet his eyes. The look in her gaze was one Kael had seen before—in cultists during their final moments, when the Entity-touched madness had fully claimed them. "The Whisper King promised us transcendence. Real transcendence, not the half-measures the Orders offer. It promised us that when the seals break, we'll be the first to cross over. The first to become something more than human."
"And you believe that?"
"I know it. I've touched the Abyss. I've felt what waits on the other side." Her voice dropped to a whisper. "You have too, Ashborn. Every time you harvest, every time you reach into death and pull out fragments of power—you're touching the Abyss. The Whisper King is patient. It will wait for you to come to it willingly."
Kael's blade pressed harder. A thin line of blood traced down Cira's neck, catching the moonlight. "Where is Zarek?"
"At the heart of things. At the beginning." Her smile was terrible in its serenity. "The old lighthouse. Where the seal was first broken. Where the Whisper King first spoke to mortal ears. He's been there for months, preparing the way. Preparing the sacrifice that will finally free our master."
"Vane is in the lighthouse."
"Vane has been expected. The Whisper King knows everything that moves in Thornwick. It knew the Order of Dawn would send a dog to sniff around. It knew you would meet him. It knows—" Her voice caught. "It knows you're different. That you're the one we've been waiting for. Not just a Soul Reaver. The Soul Reaver. The one the prophecies speak of."
"What prophecies?"
"The first one. The one who would come from death and walk between worlds. The one who could read the True Name and speak it aloud." Her eyes blazed with feverish certainty. "You're supposed to free the Whisper King, Ashborn. That's your destiny. That's what the original Soul Reaver intended when he created the tablets, when he encoded the True Name where only another Reaver could find it. He was preparing the way. Preparing the world for liberation."
The information crashed through Kael's defenses, each revelation a hammer blow against everything he'd believed about his power, his purpose, his future. The original Soul Reaver. The tablets. The True Name. It all connected—had always connected—and he'd been walking toward this moment since the first time he'd reached into a corpse and pulled out something that shouldn't have been possible.
"The original Reaver," he said slowly. "What happened to him?"
"No one knows. Some say he was killed by the Orders, afraid of what he'd discovered. Some say he transcended, became something more than human. Some say he's still waiting." Her smile widened. "But we know the truth. He's in the tablets. His consciousness, his memories, his power—all encoded in the True Name. When you speak it, when you read it aloud in the proper way, you'll release him. You'll release everything."
"And then the seals break."
"And then the seals break," she confirmed. "And the world begins again."
Kael stood in the alley, holding a cultist who worshipped the monster she'd glimpsed in her madness, and felt the weight of choices that had never been his to make. The Whisper King had been planning this for centuries. The cult was just one arm of something far larger. And he—grave-walker, Reaver, the man who had crawled out of his own execution—was supposed to be the key that opened the door to apocalypse.
Or was he?
Every cultist was certain of their cause. Every Entity-touched believer had touched something that convinced them of their righteousness. And this woman—Cira—had spent months under the Whisper King's influence, feeding on its promises, drowning in its whispers. Her testimony couldn't be trusted. Her certainties were manufactured.
But the information she'd provided. The old lighthouse. Zarek. The prophecy about a Soul Reaver who would speak the True Name. These things had the ring of truth precisely because they were terrible. Because they implied that Kael's power wasn't just unusual—it was expected. Needed. Part of a design that stretched back to the Abyssal Wars themselves.
He made his decision.
"You're going to take me to Zarek," he said. "You're going to walk me through whatever security he's established. And when we arrive, you're going to introduce me as a convert. Someone the Whisper King sent to help with the final preparations."
Cira's eyes widened. "You—you believe?"
"I believe you think you know what you're talking about. I believe the Whisper King has been planning something for a very long time. I believe the cult has resources and reach I didn't appreciate until tonight." Kael released her throat but kept his blade close. "And I believe that the best way to stop a conspiracy is to get close enough to see its full shape. So yes. I'm going to play along with your prophecy. At least until I find Zarek and put my knife through his heart."
"That won't stop the ritual. The preparations are too far along."
"Then I'll find another way." Kael stepped back, gesturing toward the lighthouse's distant silhouette. "Move. And if you try to warn them, try to run, try anything except exactly what I say—the Reaver abilities aren't pretty when used on the unwilling. I know because I've done it."
