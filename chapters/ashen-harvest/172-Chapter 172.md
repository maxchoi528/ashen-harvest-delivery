The blade caught the lamplight as it swept toward Kael's throat. He dropped beneath the arc, felt the wind of steel pass inches above his scalp, and drove his elbow into the attacker's solar plexus.
"Contact in the eastern district!" someone shouted. Kael rolled behind a steamvent, the cobblestones slick with condensation. Three figures emerged from the fog. Their movements carried the fluid precision of Awakened training, but their attire bore no Order insignia.
Shadow Court. The name surfaced from Lyra's fragmented memories, still half-integrated after his last harvest.
The leader raised a hand crackling with violet light. "Kael Ashborn. The Court requires your presence."
"And if I decline?"
"You won't." The woman's smile held no warmth. "Your friend Dorian has already accepted our invitation. Cooperate, and he lives."
Kael's stomach turned to ice. Dorian. The name echoed through the alley, carrying implications he couldn't afford to examine right now.
Behind him, an iron gate slammed open. Kael spun, placing his back against the vent's corroded housing. His hand found Soul Reaver's hilt—always sheathed, always ready.
"Stand down." The voice belonged to Commander Gwen Vex. She stepped through the gate flanked by two Iron Vanguard soldiers, their ceremonial armor reflecting the gaslight in dull bronze waves. "These streets fall under my jurisdiction."
"Your jurisdiction ended when you abandoned your post at the Thorngate seal." The Shadow Court's leader didn't lower her weapon. "The Court has prior claim on this asset."
"Asset?" Gwen's scarred face hardened further. "This man is wanted for questioning regarding multiple homicides. He's not your experimental subject."
Kael's mind raced. Shadow Court wanted him alive—probably for his Soul Reaver abilities. The Iron Vanguard wanted him for questioning—probably about the bodies he'd left behind. Neither option appealed.
"I'm going to walk out of here," Kael said. "Anyone who tries to stop me will regret it."
The Shadow Court woman laughed. "Bold words for a Transcendent facing three of us."
"Transcendent?" Gwen's eyes narrowed. "He's not Awakened. He's—"
A shriek split the night. It came from somewhere above, a sound that scraped against the inside of Kael's skull like fingernails on slate.
Everyone froze. Kael felt the disturbance ripple through his spiritual senses—something had torn through the Veil, just for an instant, just enough to send tendrils of wrongness slithering across his awareness.
"What was that?" one of the Vanguard soldiers demanded.
The Shadow Court woman's expression flickered. For the first time, uncertainty crossed her features. "The Court didn't authorize—"
Another shriek, closer now. Kael saw it emerge from the fog: a shape that defied geometry, angles that shouldn't exist, surfaces that seemed to fold into dimensions the human eye couldn't process.
Abyssal. Not a full Entity, but a fragment. A piece that had slipped through the cracks.
"DEMONS!" The Vanguard soldier broke formation, sprinting down the alley. The Shadow Court woman shouted something in a language Kael didn't recognize. Her hands wove patterns in the air, weaving barriers that lasted seconds before the thing tore through them like paper.
Kael moved without thinking. Soul Reaver sang free of its sheath, and he felt the familiar cold presence settle into his awareness—the blade that drank souls, that took what he needed from the dead.
The fragment lunged. He met it halfway.
Contact produced no sensation of impact. Instead, Kael felt the thing's alien mind pressing against his consciousness, searching for purchase, trying to understand what it had collided with. Soul Reaver answered with hunger. The blade began to pull.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted residual Abyssal essence from the remains. Fragmented Entity Memory acquired.]
The fragment screamed—not in pain, but in something like recognition. It recoiled from Soul Reaver, leaving wisps of corrupted essence clinging to the blade's edge before dissolving into the night air.
Silence fell. Kael stood alone in the alley. The Shadow Court operatives had fled. Gwen and her soldiers remained, though both men had drawn their weapons and pointed them at him.
"What," Gwen said slowly, "was that?"
"Something that shouldn't be here." Kael wiped Soul Reaver's blade on his coat, ignoring how the gesture drew the soldiers' attention. "The Veil is weakening. Whatever you've heard about the seals, it's worse."
"You're lying. The Council of Six—"
"The Council of Six doesn't know." Kael sheathed the blade. "They only see what the Orders report. And the Orders have been selective about what they report."
Gwen's jaw tightened. She was calculating something behind those cold eyes—risk assessment, political advantage, the calculus of a woman who'd survived decades in the Iron Vanguard's ruthless hierarchy.
"The Shadow Court wanted you alive," she said finally. "That's unusual. Most wanted you dead."
"Most who?"
"Cults, primarily. A few Order factions. That woman—" Gwen nodded toward where the Shadow Court operative had stood. "She wasn't lying about Dorian. We intercepted communications. He's being held in the Drowning Ward, sub-level three."
Kael's hands curled into fists. Dorian. The friend who'd stood beside him when the world decided he was a monster. The man who'd helped him escape when the noose was already around his neck.
"Why tell me this?"
"Because you can get in where we can't." Gwen stepped closer, lowering her voice. "The Drowning Ward falls under Shadow Court protection. Officially, it doesn't exist. If I send soldiers, the Council will have my head for violating the Concordat. But a man with your... Talents?"
She didn't need to finish. A Soul Reaver could enter anywhere. A Soul Reaver could extract information, eliminate targets, remove obstacles—all without the messy political complications of official involvement.
"You're asking me to break into a Shadow Court facility to rescue my friend."
"I'm asking you to do what needs doing." Gwen's eyes held his gaze without flinching. "The Shadow Court made their move tonight. They're consolidating power, pulling strings, preparing for something. Whatever it is, Dorian knew something worth dying for. Help me find out what."
Kael considered the proposal. Every instinct screamed trap—this reeked of manipulation, of Gwen using him for her own agenda. But Dorian...
"When did they take him?"
"Three hours ago. The Shadow Court operates fast."
Three hours. The corpse would still be fresh enough to harvest from, if it came to that. If Dorian was dead, Kael could—
He shut down that line of thought. Dorian was alive. He had to be.
"The Drowning Ward. Sub-level three. How many guards?"
"Rotating shifts of six. Two at each entrance, one mobile patrol."
Kael nodded, already mapping routes in his mind. "And the favor you owe me?"
Gwen's expression flickered—surprise, quickly suppressed. "What do you want?"
"Zarek. The cult leader. I want everything your intelligence has on him—names, locations, connections to the Shadow Court."
"Kael—"
"You've been holding back. I can tell." He stepped forward until barely an arm's length separated them. "The cult isn't random activity. It's organized. Coordinated. Someone's pulling the strings, and I intend to find them. Help me, and I'll help you. Let me walk away, and I'll find another way to get what I need."
The silence stretched. Steam hissed from the pipes overhead, filling the gap between them.
Gwen extended her hand. "You have a deal. One week. After that, all bets are off."
Kael clasped her forearm in the old soldier's grip. "One week."

The Drowning Ward occupied the forgotten foundations beneath Thornwick's old temple district. Kael descended through access tunnels that hadn't seen maintenance in decades, his boots splashing through water that stank of rot and copper.
His spiritual senses stretched ahead, probing the darkness. The Veil thinned here—he could feel it, a pressure against his consciousness like standing at the bottom of a deep well. Fragments of Abyssal energy drifted through the stone, remnants of rituals performed in these depths.
The first guard died without knowing Kael was there. A single touch to the back of the skull, and Soul Reaver drank deep. The man's memories flooded through Kael's awareness: patrol routes, shift changes, the location of the holding cells. Useful information, but something else lingered in the harvest.
Fear. The guard had been terrified. Not of enemies, but of what his masters might do if he failed.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted combat training and patrol knowledge from the remains. Guard's Memory acquired.]
Kael lowered the body into the stagnant water, watching it drift into the shadows. The notification pulsed once in his vision, then faded.
Two more guards fell the same way. Kael moved through the corridor with their knowledge guiding his steps, avoiding pressure plates and tripwires that only Awakened training would recognize.
Sub-level three. The Drowning Ward proper.
Cells lined both walls, each containing a single occupant suspended in tanks of murky liquid. Figures floated motionless, tubes connecting their bodies to machines that gurgled and hissed. Some were clearly dead—their skin bloated, their eyes clouded white. Others breathed in shallow gasps, barely clinging to existence.
Dorian. Kael's gaze swept the room, searching. One of these could be his friend. One of these could be—
"There." The word escaped before he could stop it.
Dorian floated in the third tank on the left. His eyes were closed, his skin pale beneath the grime, but his chest rose and fell. Alive.
Kael moved toward the tank, but a voice stopped him cold.
"I wouldn't touch that if I were you."
He spun. A man stood in the doorway behind him—tall, gaunt, dressed in robes that seemed to absorb the light. His face bore the marks of advanced spiritual disease: black veins crawling up his neck, eyes that reflected too much, too bright.
"Lord Vey," Kael said. The name surfaced from Lyra's memories. "Shadow Court Inner Circle."
"Kael Ashborn." Vey's smile revealed teeth filed to points. "We meet at last. The Court has watched your progress with considerable interest."
"Your Court tried to kill me three hours ago."
"A test. A necessary formality." Vey waved a hand dismissively. "The woman you faced was ambitious. She wanted to bring you in before proper preparations could be made. I've had her... Corrected."
Kael's grip tightened on Soul Reaver's hilt. "Let him go."
"Dorian Blackwood." Vey glanced at the floating figure. "A faithful servant, in his way. He stumbled onto something he shouldn't have. Questions about the Great Rift. About what lies beneath Thornwick. About what the Court truly intends."
"Then tell me. I've got all night."
"Ah, but do you?" Vey's head tilted at an angle that didn't match human anatomy. "You've noticed the Veil thinning. The fragments slipping through. The seals weakening faster than they should. Do you know why?"
Kael said nothing. Information asymmetry—he needed to learn without revealing his own ignorance.
"I'll tell you." Vey stepped closer, black veins pulsing beneath his skin. "The seals were never meant to hold forever. They were designed to fail. Carefully. Controlled. When the time came, when humanity had grown strong enough to survive what comes next—"
"What comes next?"
"The Reckoning." Vey's voice dropped to a whisper. "When all seventy-two rifts open simultaneously. When the Entities finally walk the Material unchecked. When the cycle completes and everything begins anew."
"You're insane."
"The Court knows the truth the Orders hide. Death is not an ending. It's a transformation. The Entities don't destroy—they refine. They burn away weakness and forge something stronger." Vey spread his arms, his robes falling open to reveal a chest covered in ritual scars. "Join us, Kael. Your Soul Reaver ability is a gift. You could serve the Court's purpose. You could harvest the weak and strengthen the worthy. You could—"
Soul Reaver cleared its sheath in a silver arc. The blade stopped a hair's breadth from Vey's throat.
"Where's the control room?" Kael asked. "The one that operates these tanks."
Vey didn't flinch. "You could kill me. But then you'd never find Dorian's release code. The tanks are on timed cycles. Miss the window, and his lungs fill with the solution. Slowly. Painfully. You have maybe ten minutes."
Kael's jaw clenched. "Control room. Now."
Vey smiled. "As you wish."
He turned and walked toward a door Kael hadn't noticed before, half-hidden behind one of the larger tanks. Kael followed, Soul Reaver steady at the man's back.
The control room was smaller than expected—a single chamber filled with gauges, levers, and glass tubes containing liquid that glowed with sickly light. Vey gestured toward a console covered in switches.
"Release code is sequence fourteen, forty-two, nine. But I wonder..."
Kael's spiritual senses screamed warning half a second before the attack came.
Vey moved with speed that shouldn't have been possible for someone so thin, so diseased. His hand closed around Kael's wrist, and light exploded between them—violet, sickly, carrying the taste of corruption.
Soul Reaver screamed.
Not in Kael's mind, but in the blade itself. The metal shook, vibrated, seemed almost to struggle against whatever Vey was doing.
"Ah." Vey's eyes widened with something like wonder. "You feel it too. The connection. The resonance."
Kael wrenched free, stumbling backward. His hand throbbed where Vey had touched it, and through the pain he felt something new—a whisper at the edge of his consciousness, fragments of memory that didn't belong to him.
Soul Reaver had a history. Soul Reaver had been used before.
"The first Reaver," Vey breathed. "You carry a fragment of the first Reaver. Do you know what that means?"
"It means I can do this."
Kael lunged. Soul Reaver bit deep into Vey's shoulder, and the blade drank with a hunger that felt different from before—deeper, more desperate, as if the weapon itself was feeding something it had long been denied.
Vey screamed. The sound carried harmonics that made Kael's teeth ache.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted corrupted spiritual essence from the remains. Fragmented Memory of the First Reaver acquired.]
The shadow broker collapsed. Vast knowledge crashed through Kael's mind—rituals, locations, names. The Whisper King. The Undying Accord. The cult's true structure, hidden beneath layers of misdirection and false flags.
And something else. Something Vey had tried to hide even in his final moments.
A truth about the Soul Reaver's origin. A hint about who had forged it, and why.
But before Kael could examine these revelations, the notification continued.
[Alert: Incompatible essence detected. Soul Fragment integrity compromised. Reaver stabilization required.]
Kael felt it immediately—a wrongness in his spiritual core, a tremor in the ability he'd come to rely on. The harvest had been too much. The essence too corrupted.
He had minutes to stabilize, or the Reaver would reject him permanently.
Kael slammed the release code into the console. Fourteen, forty-two, nine. The tanks hissed and drained. Bodies slumped in their restraints, some moving, others still.
Dorian fell from his tank. Kael caught him before he hit the ground.
"Kael?" Dorian's voice came out cracked, barely audible. "They said... They said you were dead. They said—"
"They lied." Kael hauled his friend over his shoulder. "Can you walk?"
"Give me... A minute."
"You've got thirty seconds. We need to move."
Footsteps echoed from the corridor. Heavy boots. Multiple people.
Kael didn't wait to count. He grabbed Dorian's arm, pulled him toward a maintenance shaft barely visible in the far corner of the room. Behind them, the door burst open.
"HALT!"
Kael ignored the command. He shoved Dorian into the shaft, followed, and slammed the cover shut above them. The shouts continued, muffled by metal and distance.
They climbed. Kael's muscles burned, his spiritual reserves churned with that new instability, and the Reaver hummed with something that felt almost like warning.
Somewhere above, rain began to fall. Kael could smell it through the gaps in the shaft—fresh air, cold water, the first breath of escape.
But as he pulled himself toward the surface, another sensation cut through the noise of pursuit and exhaustion. A presence. Ancient. Patient. Watching from somewhere beyond the Veil.
The Whisper King, maybe. The shadow broker Vey had served.
Or something else entirely.
Kael emerged into Thornwick's rain-slicked streets. The fog had thickened while he was below, turning the gaslights into smeared orange halos. Dorian slumped against a wall, breathing hard but alive.
"You came for me," Dorian whispered. "They said no one would come."
"People say a lot of things." Kael scanned the rooftops, the alleyways, the shadows between buildings. "Where did they take you? What did you learn?"
Dorian's eyes widened. "The Great Rift. The cult isn't just weakening the seal—they're channeling power from all seventy-two rifts. Building something. A beacon, they called it. To call the Entities when the time is right."
"When?"
"Three weeks. Maybe less." Dorian grabbed Kael's arm with desperate strength. "There's a meeting. High-level operatives, discussing final preparations. It's tomorrow night, at the old lighthouse. If we could get inside—"
A sound interrupted him. Footsteps, approaching from two directions simultaneously.
Kael pulled Dorian up, positioning them back-to-back. The fog swirled, and shapes emerged from its embrace—Shadow Court operatives, a dozen at least, surrounding them in a perfect encirclement.
"Kael Ashborn." A new voice, female, cold with authority. "You've caused considerable trouble tonight. Lord Vey was... Irreplaceable."
The operatives advanced, their weapons drawn.
"Last chance," the woman continued. "Come with us, and Dorian Blackwood lives. Resist, and you'll both join the tanks. Permanently."
Kael's hand found Soul Reaver's hilt. The blade trembled—eager, hungry, still unstable from the corrupted harvest.
Behind him, Dorian laughed weakly. "They're really going to try this again, aren't they?"
"Apparently."
"Want me to take the left side?"
Kael's mouth curved into something that wasn't a smile. "I'll let you know when I decide."
The fog pressed closer. Rain drummed against the cobblestones. Somewhere in the distance, a steamwhistle shrieked, marking the hour.
And Kael stood at the crossroads once more, surrounded by enemies, carrying a weapon that whispered secrets he wasn't ready to hear, facing a choice that would determine everything.
The Shadow Court's woman raised her hand, ready to signal the attack.
The seconds stretched into eternity.
Kael drew Soul Reaver, felt the blade's strange resonance with something older than memory, and made his decision.
"What was that you said about permanent?"
