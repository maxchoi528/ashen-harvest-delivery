The lantern swung overhead, casting jagged shadows across the alley wall. Kael pressed deeper into the recessed doorway, his breath shallow, his ears straining for the boots that had marked his position three streets back.
A patrol. Two of them, moving in formation, their armor catching the gaslight in intermittent flashes. The Order of Dawn never traveled alone—squads of four minimum, spiritual senses permanently extended, hunting for exactly what Kael had become.
One hundred feet. Seventy-five.
Kael's fingers found the cold iron of a drainpipe. Above him, the rooftops offered escape, but rooftops meant exposure. Below, the sewer grates promised concealment but no speed. He had maybe ten seconds before the patrol's lead tracker reached the intersection where he had broken line of sight.
"Split up." The second patrolman's voice, low and professional. "He can't have gone far."
Kael chose the rooftops.
His shoulder slammed against the pipe, legs driving upward. Muscle memory from a hundred desperate climbs in the Ashwick slums guided his hands to the first windowsill, then the second, then higher still. Brick scraped his palms raw as he pulled himself onto a flat tar roof, rolling across gravel and staying low behind a chimney stack.
The patrol passed below without stopping.
Kael allowed himself three breaths. Then he moved.
The rooftops of Thornwick formed a labyrinth of pitched slate, chimney pots, and narrow walkways. Steam hissed from vents below, obscuring the streets in ephemeral clouds. Somewhere to the east, a factory whistle screamed the midnight hour—the first shift change, when guard rotations overlapped and attention waned.
He needed that gap. Every night since his awakening in the execution chamber, the Order had tightened its net. His face hung on wanted posters in every precinct. His description—average height, dark hair, distinctive scar across the throat—served as breakfast conversation for half the city's informants.
They knew a Soul Reaver walked among them. They just didn't know which Soul Reaver.
The irony burned in Kael's chest. Twelve days ago, he had been Kael Ashborn, condemned murderer, guilty of crimes he had never committed. Now he carried a power that made his alleged offenses seem like child's play. The Order hadn't hunted him for the lives he was said to have taken. They hunted him for what he could steal from the dead.
A skill without equal. A curse without limit.
He paused at the edge of a three-story drop, gauging the gap to the adjacent building. Eight feet. Doable, if the rain held off and his legs cooperated.
The rain, of course, chose that moment to begin.
Droplets struck the heated chimney stacks, sending up ribbons of steam that coiled through the night air. The rooftops grew slick. Visibility dropped from bad to worse.
"Contact." The voice came from below, muffled by distance but unmistakable in its cold authority. "Spiritual signature, rooftop level, eastern quadrant."
Kael moved.
He crossed the gap in a sprint, feet sliding on wet slate, arms pinwheeling for balance as gravity threatened to claim him. The far roof received his landing with a crash of loose tiles. He tumbled, caught himself, kept moving.
More voices now, coordinating. The patrol had split again, and one of them had bloodhound senses sharp enough to track his spiritual residue. The Soul Reaver left traces the way a fire left smoke—a byproduct of its function, impossible to suppress entirely.
Third building. Fourth. The steam vents provided intermittent cover, but the gap between them widened as the industrial district gave way to the older residential blocks. The Order knew these streets better than he did. They had grown up in them.
A hand caught his ankle.
Kael went down hard, his chin cracking against the rooftop. Stars burst across his vision. Above him, a figure in Dawn armor rose from a crouch, blade already drawn, spiritual energy crackling along its edge.
"Caught you." The patrolman grinned, teeth filed to points. "The bounty's mine."
Kael's free leg swept upward, driving into the man's knee. Cartilage buckled. The patrolman howled, toppling sideways. Kael wrenched free, scrambled to his feet, and saw the second patrolman landing on the adjacent roof, sword raised.
Three against one. Poor odds.
The filed-teeth patrolman wasn't finished, though. His hand closed around Kael's ankle again, this time with force multiplied by spiritual energy. Pain lanced up Kael's calf as the grip tightened, muscles screaming.
"I said I caught you." The man hauled himself upright, using Kael's leg as an anchor. "Dawn takes Soul Reavers alive. Orders from the top."
What the Order did with captured Reavers, Kael didn't know. The stories varied—exorcism, imprisonment in lightless cells, dissection for study. None of them were pleasant.
The second patrolman closed in from the left, cutting off retreat. Steam curled around his boots, his sword-arm steady.
"Resistance is pointless." He raised his blade. "You know what you are. You know what we do to—"
The Whisper King stepped out of the steam.
One moment the rooftop was empty save for Kael and his two captors. The next, a figure stood among them, wreathed in mist that clung to his robes like living shadow. He was tall—impossibly tall, proportions subtly wrong in ways that defied conscious analysis. His face was a ruin of scar tissue over bone, features worn smooth by what might have been decades of healing.
Or centuries.
"Gentlemen." The Whisper King's voice came from everywhere and nowhere, resonating in the spaces between heartbeats. "This one isn't yours."
The filed-teeth patrolman released Kael's ankle and spun, blade flashing. "Stand aside, civilian. Order of Dawn business."
"I'm not civilian." The Whisper King tilted his head, considering the patrolman with eyes that reflected no light. "I'm not anything you have a word for. But if you insist on categorization—call me interested."
The second patrolman's sword arm trembled. Whatever he sensed in the figure before him, it exceeded his ability to categorize. "Spiritual pressure... Off the scale. Who are you?"
"Someone who remembers when your Order was called by a different name." The Whisper King raised one hand, fingers spread. "Someone who remembers what was sacrificed to build your precious seals. And someone who has waited a very long time for a Soul Reaver to walk these streets again."
Kael pushed himself upright, his ankle throbbing. "I don't know you."
"No." The Whisper King turned to face him, and the ruin of his features conveyed an expression of profound sadness. "You don't. But I know you, Kael Ashborn. I know what you carry. I know what it costs. And I know what will happen if you continue alone."
The filed-teeth patrolman lunged.
The Whisper King didn't move. One moment the man's blade was descending toward his skull. The next, the patrolman was on his knees, hands clasped over ears that bled thin streams of crimson down his cheeks. He screamed without sound.
"Discretion." The Whisper King lowered his hand. "I prefer discretion. Your Order has forgotten the value of subtlety."
The second patrolman ran. He made it three steps before the steam around his ankles thickened into something solid, wrapping his legs, bearing him to the rooftop face-first. He didn't scream. He didn't struggle. He lay there, breathing in quick, shallow gasps.
The Whisper King walked toward Kael, each step deliberate, robes dragging across wet slate. Up close, the wrongness of his proportions became more apparent. His arms were too long. His fingers had too many joints. His shadow fell in a direction that didn't match the ambient light.
"The Order will send more." The Whisper King stopped an arm's length away. "They always do. And unlike these two, they won't underestimate what you are."
"What do you want?" Kael's hand found his own ankle, testing the damage. Sprained, possibly broken. He wouldn't be running anywhere for a while.
"I want to help you survive long enough to become useful." The Whisper King extended one too-jointed hand. "Come with me. There are things you need to know about your ability, about the Orders, about the seventy-two seals that hold the Abyss at bay. Things the Orders would kill to keep hidden."
"And why would you know those things?"
The Whisper King's ruined face shifted into something that might have been a smile. "Because I helped design the system your power was meant to break. Come, Kael Ashborn. The night is young, and your enemies are many."
A sound cut through the moment— bells, ringing in a pattern Kael didn't recognize but understood instinctively. Alarm. The Order had been alerted.
"You have until those bells stop to decide." The Whisper King began to fade, his form dissolving into the steam. "Find me in the place where the river runs backward. You know where that is, even if you don't remember learning."
Kael stood alone on the rooftop, rain streaming down his face, the sound of approaching boots echoing from a dozen directions. The filed-teeth patrolman had recovered enough to crawl toward the edge, seeking escape. The silent one remained prone.
The Whisper King had offered answers. He had also admitted to designing the system Kael's power was meant to break.
Nothing came for free in Thornwick. Especially not salvation.
Kael made his choice.

Father Thomas Everard stood at the window of his study, watching the bells of the Order of Dawn flare orange against the night sky. Another hunt. Another failed capture. The city had become a pressure cooker since the Soul Reaver's escape, and the pressure was building toward something explosive.
"The latest report." Brother Marcus entered without knocking, a sheaf of papers in his hands. The boy was barely twenty, fresh from his Awakening, still bright-eyed with the thrill of spiritual power. That would fade. It always did. "The Reaver evaded three full patrols. acquired assistance."
"Assistance?" Everard turned from the window, his priest's robes stirring in the draft from the heating vents. "What manner of assistance?"
"Unidentified figure. High-level spiritual pressure, possibly Transcendent, possibly higher. Our men reported... Physical anomalies." Marcus hesitated. "The official report calls it 'combat disorientation,' but the survivors aren't talking. Three are in the infirmary, two with permanent hearing damage."
Everard accepted the papers, scanning the details. His fingers paused on a single phrase: *spoke of seals, mentioned the old name.*
"The Veil take us." He set the papers aside. "Get me a meeting with Commander Vane. Now."
"Father, it's nearly one in the morning—"
"Now, Brother Marcus."
The boy fled. Everard returned to the window, his reflection ghosting across the glass. Sixty-three years he had served the Order. Sixty-three years of hunting abominations, sealing rifts, maintaining the fragile membrane between a world and its hungry reflection. He had buried more Awakened than he could count. He had made decisions that haunted his dreams.
But he had never seen a Soul Reaver walk free before.
The first had been executed two hundred years ago, after the Abyssal Wars. The original Soul Reaver—a being of terrible power, capable of harvesting not just abilities but entire personalities, rewriting himself with each consumption. The Orders had united to destroy him, suffering losses that nearly broke the seal network. What remained of his knowledge had been sealed in the Sepulcher Tablets, guarded by the Mortician's Guild, studied by no one.
Until twelve days ago, when a death-row prisoner had awakened with that same impossible gift.
"The prophecy," Everard murmured to the empty room. "The old texts speak of a breaker. A liberator. One who walks between death and the Abyss, carrying the key to the seventy-two prisons."
The key to the seventy-two prisons. Or the key to opening them all.
A knock at the door. "Commander Vane is here, Father."
"Send him in."
The door opened, and Commander Aldric Vane entered like a man carrying the weight of worlds. His armor was a patchwork of old victories and new failures, ceremonial today but functional always. Behind his eyes, the particular exhaustion of a hunter who had stopped believing in his quarry.
"Everard." Vane didn't bow. They were beyond such formalities. "Your message said urgent."
"A new development." Everard gestured to a chair. "Sit. We have a great deal to discuss, and very little time to discuss it."
Vane sat, a man of fifty winters lowering himself into worn leather. "The Reaver?"
"The Reaver." Everard poured two glasses of amber liquid from a crystal decanter. The good stuff. The very good stuff. "But also something else. Something that hasn't walked these streets in two centuries."
Vane accepted the glass, frowned. "You're speaking in riddles."
"I'm speaking in the only way the Council will allow when they learn what I've learned." Everard drained his glass in a single swallow. "The entity that aided the Reaver tonight—our people have a name for it. The Whisper King. And according to our oldest records, it died during the First Sealing."
"Spirits don't die."
"No. But beings from the Abyss do. Beings that were never meant to cross the Veil in the first place." Everard set down his glass. "Vane, I've served the Order for sixty-three years. I've seen things that would shatter lesser minds. But tonight, reading this report, I felt something I haven't felt since the night the Great Rift first cracked."
"Fear?"
"Clarity." Everard met the commander's eyes. "The seventy-two seals are failing. We've known this for decades, managed it as best we could. But the Whisper King's appearance confirms what I've suspected for months—someone is actively working to accelerate that failure. Someone with knowledge of the original sealing rituals. Someone who wants the Entities free."
"And you think this Reaver—"
"I think this Reaver is either our salvation or our destruction. Possibly both." Everard stood, moved to a locked cabinet, withdrew a leather-bound journal older than the building around him. "Two hundred years ago, the original Soul Reaver was executed not because of what he could do, but because of what he knew. He was the last living witness to the First Sealing. The others who participated—dead or imprisoned beyond recovery. Only the Soul Reaver carried the knowledge of what was truly done to contain the Entities."
"And you think this new Reaver carries that same knowledge?"
"I think this new Reaver is a copy, or a rebirth, or a fragment of something that should not exist. The Awakening rituals, the forbidden texts, the circumstances of his execution—all of it points to deliberate action. Someone wanted Kael Ashborn to become what he is. Someone wanted the Orders to hunt him. Someone wants exactly what's happening right now."
Vane set down his untouched glass. "If that's true, then we're not hunting a criminal. We're dancing to someone else's tune."
"Yes." Everard opened the journal, revealing pages covered in a script that predated the common tongue. "And I intend to find out who composed the melody."
The bells outside had stopped ringing. In the sudden silence, the city felt like a held breath, waiting for something inevitable to arrive.
"There's one more thing." Vane's voice had dropped, rough with reluctance. "My source in the Shadow Court passed a message tonight. Apparently, they're taking an interest in the Reaver as well. Someone called the Lady of Echoes has placed a priority acquisition order."
Everard's blood chilled. "The Shadow Court's First Circle? They've never moved on a Reaver before."
"They've never had one to move on."
The priest and the commander looked at each other across decades of shared service, shared secrets, shared burden. The world they had sworn to protect was changing beneath their feet, and neither of them could see where the change would end.
"I need to find the Reaver before the Shadow Court does." Everard closed the journal. "And I need to do it without alerting the rest of the Council."
"You want my men to stand down?"
"I want my men to look for someone else entirely. Someone who matches the Reaver's description but isn't him. A decoy. A distraction." Everard's smile held no warmth. "We have resources the Order doesn't acknowledge. It's time we used them."
Vane rose, armor creaking. "If this goes wrong—if the Council learns what you're planning—"
"Then we lose everything. But if we do nothing, we lose it anyway." Everard moved to the door, opened it. "The Reaver is the key, Vane. He always was. The only question is whether we shape the lock or let someone else do it for us."
The commander left without another word. Everard stood in the doorway, watching him go, the old journal clutched against his chest like a talisman.
Somewhere in the city, Kael Ashborn was running. Somewhere else, the Whisper King was waiting. And somewhere in the shadows between, the Lady of Echoes was moving pieces into place.
The game had begun.

Kael ducked through the service entrance of an abandoned tannery, his ankle screaming with each step. The building reeked of old chemicals and rotting hides, but it provided cover and darkness and a moment to think.
The Whisper King had known his name. Had known what he carried. Had claimed to have designed the system Kael's power was meant to break.
The system. The seals. The seventy-two prisons.
Kael had always known his ability was unusual. The way it worked, the zero cost, the perfect extraction—it defied everything the Orders taught about spiritual limitations. But the idea that he was connected to the original Soul Reaver, to events two centuries past, to the very foundations of the seal network...
It was too much. Too big. Too dangerous.
He needed to think. He needed information. And he needed both before the Order found him again.
The tannery's main floor was a maze of rusted vats and collapsed scaffolding. Kael picked his way through the debris, testing each footfall before committing his weight. The floorboards groaned in protest, threatening to give way into flooded basements below.
A sound. Faint. Movement in the darkness ahead.
Kael froze, hand going to the makeshift knife at his belt. He had nothing else—no weapons, no tools, no resources beyond the power that let him steal from the dead.
"I know you're there." The voice was female, cold, precise. "I can smell the steam on your clothes. You were rooftop running during the alarm."
A figure emerged from behind a vat, no more than a silhouette against the faint light from the boarded windows. She moved like a predator—fluid, economical, dangerous.
"Answer one question," she continued. "The Whisper King. Where did you meet him?"
Kael's grip tightened on his knife. "Who are you?"
"Someone who's been looking for that particular monster for a very long time." The figure stepped closer, and details emerged from shadow—a woman's face, sharp-featured and scarred, eyes that reflected the dim light like a cat's. "My name is Lyra. I work for the Shadow Court. And I have a proposition for you."
The Shadow Court. Even Kael had heard of them—Order-sanctioned deniable operations, the dirty work that the Orders couldn't acknowledge. Assassinations, sabotage, intelligence gathering. They operated in the spaces between law and chaos.
"I don't work for anyone." Kael kept his voice steady. "Especially not the Orders."
"Good. Neither do I. The Shadow Court is a tool, not a master." Lyra circled him slowly, appraising. "You have a choice to make, Soul Reaver. You can run from the Order of Dawn until they catch you. You can trust the Whisper King and learn secrets that will burn your mind. Or you can come with me and gain something neither of those paths offers."
"Which is?"
"Context." Lyra's smile was a knife's edge. "The history of your power. The truth about who created it. And the identity of the person who's been hunting your kind for two hundred years."
Kael's ankle throbbed. The Order was still searching. The Whisper King had offered answers in exchange for something undefined. And now a Shadow Court operative stood before him, promising truths that might be salvation or damnation.
"Suppose I say no." He kept his knife raised. "Suppose I walk out of here right now."
"Then you walk into the patrol we've tracked three blocks east. They've set up a containment perimeter. The only way out is through us." Lyra spread her hands, palms up, empty. "I'm offering an alternative. The Whisper King is old and dangerous and doesn't play by any rules you understand. The Order wants to dissect you. The Shadow Court wants to use you. But I? I'm offering you a chance to choose your own war."
Behind Lyra, a door creaked open. Footsteps echoed across the tannery floor—measured, deliberate, accompanied by the soft clink of armor.
"Well." Lyra glanced over her shoulder. "It seems our window just closed. Make your choice, Soul Reaver. Now."
The footsteps grew louder. Torchlight flickered at the far end of the building, orange against the darkness. And beyond it, Kael could hear the coordination of a tactical team—four, maybe five Awakened, closing in from multiple angles.
He had seconds.
The knife felt absurd in his hand—a dead man's weapon against fully-armed Awakened. His ankle would slow him. The exits were blocked. The Order had cornered him exactly where they wanted.
But Lyra stood at the edge of the shadows, waiting, her offer hanging in the air like an unanswered question.
And somewhere deep in Kael's chest, the Soul Reaver stirred.
It had been twelve days since his awakening. Twelve days of running, hiding, surviving on instinct and desperation. He knew almost nothing about what he could do—only that it worked, that it cost nothing, that the dead gave up their secrets when he touched them.
The footsteps were thirty feet away. Twenty-five.
Kael sheathed his knife.
"What's your alternative?"
Lyra's smile widened. She reached into her coat, withdrew a small crystal vial filled with something that pulsed like captured moonlight. "This is a phase key. Single-use, leads to a pocket dimension the Orders don't know about. We go in together, they can't follow, and you and I have a conversation about what you really are."
"And why would you help me?"
"Because the Whisper King killed my partner six months ago. Because I want answers he won't give. And because—" Lyra's expression flickered, something human surfacing through the professional mask. "—because someone needs to stand between you and whatever he's planning. And I'd it be someone who can fight back."
Twenty feet. Torchlight now, blinding after the darkness.
Lyra pressed the vial into Kael's palm. "Break it when I tell you. Three seconds. That's all I need."
Kael felt the crystal's cold weight, sensed the spiritual energy coiled within. A phase key. He had heard of them—rare, expensive, impossible to track.
What did a Shadow Court operative want badly enough to spend resources like this?
"How do I know this isn't a trap?"
"You don't." Lyra's eyes met his, and for a moment, he saw something other than calculation there. "But you knew the Whisper King's offer was a trap too. And you'll take it anyway, because the alternative is dying in this tannery with nothing to show for it."
Fifteen feet. A voice called out in the formal challenge of the Order: "By authority of the Dawn, surrender or be taken."
Lyra's hand moved to her blade. "Now, Kael."
He broke the vial.
Light erupted—cold, silver, absolute. The world dissolved into geometric patterns, reality folding around them like origami. Kael felt himself pulled in a direction that had no name, weightless, dimensionless, existing only as a point of consciousness hurtling through spaces between spaces.
When it stopped, he stood in a garden.
Not a real garden—the proportions were wrong, the plants unfamiliar, the sky a void of stars that moved in patterns no constellation could explain. But it was solid, walkable, real enough to trust.
Lyra stood beside him, composing herself. "Welcome to the Hollow. My own pocket of unreality. The Orders can't track us here."
Kael looked around, taking in the impossible geometry, the plants that seemed to grow downward, the sense of being suspended between worlds. "How long have you had this?"
"Long enough." Lyra moved to a stone bench, sat, gestured for him to do the same. "Sit. We have much to discuss, and the phase key only lasts an hour before it resets."
Kael sat, his damaged ankle grateful for the rest. "The Whisper King. You said he killed your partner."
"Six months ago. We were tracking him—had been for years, ever since the old records surfaced. My partner thought he had information about the seal network. He was right. He was also wrong about being able to handle the meeting alone."
"You want revenge."
"I want answers." Lyra's jaw tightened. "The Whisper King isn't just some Abyssal entity. He was human once. More than human, —a Soul Reaver, like you, but far more powerful. He mastered the ability to harvest not just skills and memories, but entire soul-essences. He could absorb personalities, rewrite his own identity, become anyone he wanted."
Kael felt ice crystallize in his stomach. "That's what my power can do?"
"That's what your power was designed to do. You're not the first, Kael. There have been others—seven that we know of, all executed or destroyed. But you might be the last, if the Whisper King has his way." Lyra leaned forward, intensity sharpening her features. "The Whisper King was the original. The template. Two hundred years ago, he tried to unseal all seventy-two rifts at once, to drown the Material realm in Abyssal energy and remake reality in his image. The Orders stopped him—barely—but they couldn't kill him. So they sealed him away in a place between dimensions, bound by the very power he had taught them to understand."
"And now he's out."
"Now he's out. And he's been reaching out to every Soul Reaver that has awakened since, trying to reclaim what was taken from him. Trying to rebuild his power by transferring his essence into new vessels." Lyra's voice dropped. "You're not just a target, Kael. You're a potential host body. If the Whisper King can merge with you, absorb your awakened abilities while maintaining his own ancient knowledge..."
"I become exactly what he was. But worse."
"Infinitely worse. An ancient Soul Reaver with two centuries of harvested power, merged with a fresh awakening that hasn't been corrupted by the Orders' detection methods." Lyra stood, pacing among the impossible plants. "That's why the Order of Dawn is hunting you so aggressively. They don't just fear what you can do. They fear what the Whisper King will do through you."
Kael stared at his hands, seeing nothing special in them. Just hands. Hands that had touched dead bodies and stolen their essence. Hands that carried a power he barely understood.
"The Whisper King said he wanted to help me survive."
"Would you trust the man who told you that the sky was red while standing under blue skies? The Whisper King is a liar, a manipulator, and a predator. He doesn't help people. He uses them."
"But he said he designed the system my power was meant to break."
Lyra stopped pacing. Her expression shifted to something Kael couldn't read—shock, maybe, or recognition. "He said that. Word for word?"
"Something like it. Why?"
"Because that's what he told my partner before he killed him. Word for word." Lyra's voice was barely a whisper. "He's playing a game, Kael. A very old game. And you're the most important piece on the board."
The garden around them seemed to press closer, the strange plants rustling without wind. Kael became aware of his heartbeat, loud in the silence.
"What game?"
"I don't know. Not all of it. But I know the goal." Lyra returned to the bench, sat closer this time, her voice urgent. "The Whisper King wants the seals opened. All of them. Not gradually, not controllably—all at once, in a single massive event that will flood the Material realm with enough Abyssal energy to permanently merge the two dimensions."
"That's insane. The resulting energy discharge alone would—"
"Destroy everything? Yes. That's the point. In a merged reality, the Whisper King wouldn't be an anomaly. He'd be a god among insects. The only being capable of surviving in a world where the laws of physics are suggestions and the membrane between life and death has been dissolved."
Kael tried to imagine it—a world where death meant nothing, where every corpse was a potential vessel, where the boundary between self and other had been erased. The horror of it settled into his bones.
"The Orders know about this?"
"The Order of Dawn knows pieces. The Council of Six knows fragments. But the full picture?" Lyra shook her head. "They've been managing the seal decay for decades. They think they have time. They think they can fix it through gradual reinforcement. They don't understand that someone is actively working to accelerate the flow."
"Who?"
"That's the question, isn't it?" Lyra stood again, moving to the edge of the garden where the void of stars began. "The Whisper King can't act directly—he's still bound by his ancient prison, limited to projections and manipulations. He needs agents. Cultists. Corrupt Order members. And somewhere, pulling strings, there's someone who knows exactly what the Whisper King wants and is determined to give it to him."
Kael thought of the Order of Dawn's hunt, the way they had descended on his case with unusual ferocity. The way Commander Vane had personally overseen his transfer to the execution chamber. The convenient timing of his awakening in the moments after his heart stopped.
"Someone in the Order of Dawn is working with the cult."
Lyra turned, and her eyes glittered with something that might have been respect. "Now you're starting to understand. The Order that hunts Soul Reavers the hardest might be the one most compromised by those who want the seals destroyed."
"But why? What do they gain?"
"Salvation, probably. The cult promises that the merger will elevate those who serve it, transform them into something beyond human. They believe they're preparing for a new world, not the end of everything." Lyra's laugh was bitter. "The Whisper King is a master of false promises. He offered my partner exactly what he wanted—knowledge, power, the truth about his dead family. And when the moment came, he took everything anyway."
The garden's strange light shifted, shadows lengthening. The phase key's hour was running out.
"I need to think." Kael pressed his palms against the stone bench, grounding himself. "There's too much here. I can't process it all right now."
"Then don't. Focus on what matters: you're in danger. The Order wants to capture you, the Shadow Court wants to use you, the Whisper King wants to consume you, and somewhere there's a cultist working to open the doors he's been imprisoned behind." Lyra's voice softened, almost gentle. "But you're not helpless. Your power is the one thing that can stop all of this. The Soul Reaver ability isn't just a weapon—it's a key. The original Reaver designed it to interface with the seal network, to maintain and repair the barriers that keep the Abyss contained. But it can also be used to close rifts permanently, to drain Abyssal energy faster than it can leak through."
"And you know this how?"
"Because the Sepulcher Tablets tell us. The records of the original Soul Reaver, written before his execution. We've had them for two hundred years, waiting for someone who could read them." Lyra met his gaze. "Waiting for you."
Kael's mind raced. The Whisper King offered to teach him about his power. Lyra offered the Tablets that could unlock it. Two sources of knowledge, two different agendas, neither fully trustworthy.
"What do you want in return?"
"The location of the original Soul Reaver's tomb. The Tablets reference it, but we've never been able to find it. If the tomb exists, it might contain the original key—the one the Whisper King used to create your ability in the first place." Lyra hesitated. "And I want to kill the Whisper King. Not for revenge. For prevention. As long as he exists, no seal is truly secure."
"And you think I can help with that."
