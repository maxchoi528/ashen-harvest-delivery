The cultist's scream tore through the crypt like shattered glass.
Kael pressed his back against the damp stone, counting heartbeats. Three seconds. Four. The echoing wail faded into gurgling silence. Blood slapped against ancient flagstones somewhere ahead, rhythmic as a failing pulse.
He'd tracked this one for six blocks through the Ashfall District's steaming gutters. The robed figure moved with the frantic desperation of prey, not predator—yet something in the air told Kael the prey carried teeth.
His fingers found the Soul Reaver's hilt beneath his coat. The weapon hummed against his palm, hungry. Through the crypt's entrance, he glimpsed candlelight dancing against vaulted ceilings, casting shadows that moved wrong.
The cultist had stopped running.
"Reaver." The voice bounced off tombs. "I know you're there. I can smell the death on you."
Kael stepped through the archway. The cultist stood in a circle of dying flames, robes torn, face half-hidden beneath a corroded mask. Blood poured from a wound at his side where Kael's throwing knife had found purchase. Behind him, a rift pulsed—violet edges bleeding into the stone wall, reality fraying at the seams.
Not just a cultist. A rift-touched.
"You broke into Lord Vex's tomb." The cultist laughed, wet and broken. "Did you think we wouldn't notice? Every grave in Thornwick belongs to the Cause now."
Kael's eyes swept the chamber. Three other bodies lay crumpled near the rift—cultists, throats opened by what looked like ritual knives. Sacrifices. "What were you opening?"
"72 gates." The cultist spread his arms wide. "72 eternities waiting to pour through. You can't stop it. The Whisper King's disciples are everywhere."
Kael took a step forward. His boot squelched in something warm.
"You're standing in a blood circle, Reaver. Step any closer and—"
The cultist's eyes flashed violet. The rift behind him bulged, membrane stretching toward breaking.
"—and I pull this whole district into the Abyss. Take me with you. I don't care anymore."
Kael's hand tightened on Soul Reaver. The blade caught candlelight, edge gleaming like frozen malice. "The rift won't open that fast. You're burning your own life to force it."
"Smart Reaver." Blood bubbled at the cultist's lips. "So smart. So dead. You think this is about winning? We're beyond winning. We're beyond everything. The Whisper King showed us what's coming—true death, true oblivion, nothing left but—" His voice cracked. "—nothing left at all."
The rift screamed.
Kael moved.
His blade carved through the blood circle's edge, shattering the ritual pattern. The cultist howled as backlash erupted through his veins, violet light crawling beneath his skin like worms writhing in rotten fruit. Kael drove his elbow into the wound at the cultist's side, slamming him backward into the rift's edge.
Not close enough to be pulled in. Close enough to hurt.
"The Whisper King." Kael leaned close, voice low. "Who is he?"
The cultist laughed again, delirious now. "You don't know? You carry that blade and you don't know?" His bloody fingers reached toward Kael's weapon. "It used to be his. Before he became... Before he became the voice in the void. He gave it away. Threw it into the Material like trash. Like he could escape what he was."
Kael's grip faltered for half a heartbeat.
Soul Reaver pulsed warmth against his palm. Hunger. Recognition. Something older than memory stirred in the weapon's steel.
"You're lying."
"Am I?" The cultist's eyes rolled back, showing white crescents. "Ask the tombs. Ask the first Reaver's grave. Ask—ask why no one who holds that blade ever keeps it long. Why they always die. Why they always—"
Kael drove Soul Reaver through the cultist's chest.
The blade sank home with a sound like a drowning man's last breath. The rift collapsed inward, seal reforming with a grinding shriek. The cultist's body went rigid, then crumpled, violet light draining from his skin as the last of his stolen Abyssal energy dissipated.
Silence crashed over the crypt.
Kael stood over the corpse, Soul Reaver still buried to the hilt. His hand trembled—not from exhaustion. From something else.
*Soul Harvest: You extracted 847 units of spiritual energy from the remains. Target was Abyssal-touched. Energy converted to base reserves.*
*Soul Harvest: You extracted a skill fragment from the remains. Ability Acquired: [Void-Touched Resistance] — Partial immunity to Abyssal corruption effects. Duration: two days.*
*Soul Harvest: Memory fragment extracted. Fragment integrity: 23%. Contents: Ritual locations, organizational hierarchy, naming conventions.*
Kael wrenched the blade free. Blood didn't cling to Soul Reaver's edge—it never did. The weapon drank everything, leaving corpses grey and hollow.
He crouched beside the body, pressing his palm against the cultist's cooling forehead. The memory fragment unfolded in his mind like a flower rotting in reverse.
Thornwick's map bloomed behind his eyes. Red pins marked locations he'd never seen—abandoned factories, collapsed sewers, the basement of the Mortician's Guild's eastern chapter house. Not random attacks. Not desperate madmen tearing at reality's seams.
A coordinated assault. A timetable. Cells reporting to handlers reporting to someone higher. The cult wasn't fragments falling apart. It was an organism with a brain.
*They know about me*, Kael realized. *They've been mapping Awakened movements. Recording who hunts them. Noteing my name next to the word "priority."*
The thought should have frightened him. Instead, it kindled something cold and useful in his chest.
He stood, wiping his hand on his coat. The crypt's candles guttered, shadows stretching long fingers across tombs that had stood for centuries. Lord Vex's effigy stared down at him with stone eyes, mouth frozen mid-curse.
"The Whisper King." Kael tested the name aloud. It meant nothing. It meant everything. The cultist had spoken of the first Soul Reaver—the blade's original owner—as if recounting scripture. As if the Whisper King had become something beyond human, something the cult worshipped than served.
Soul Reaver hummed in his grip. Affirmation. Warning. Recognition.
*It used to be his.*
Kael walked deeper into the crypt. Lord Vex's sarcophagus dominated the chamber's center, marble lid carved with symbols that predated the Orders' founding. The cultists had pried it open, discarded the ancient noble's bones across the floor like refuse.
But something else lay beneath the sarcophagus. Kael felt it before he saw it—a depression in the stone floor, hidden passage leading down into darkness older than the city above.
They'd been digging. Searching for something.
Kael lowered himself into the hole, Soul Reaver extended before him. The blade's edge provided just enough light to navigate crumbling steps that spiraled into the earth. Moisture dripped from unseen heights. The air tasted of copper and age.
At the bottom, a door waited.
Not stone. Not wood. Something that looked like bone arranged into architectural form, joints and hinges grown than carved. Symbols covered its surface—not the Standard Seals the Orders used, but older patterns that made his eyes water when he tried to focus on them.
Kael's hand reached for the handle.
*Don't*, something whispered. Not Soul Reaver. Something deeper. Something that felt like his own instinct screaming through layers of ice.
He paused.
The door's symbols pulsed with faint light. Waiting. Patient.
"This is a trap." Kael spoke to the darkness. "Isn't it?"
No answer came. The bone door remained still, its surface ancient beyond reckoning. Whatever waited behind it had waited for centuries. It could wait longer.
Kael withdrew his hand. His fingers shook— shook, despite everything he'd survived, despite the corpses he'd reaped and the enemies he'd butchered. The bone door terrified him in a way nothing else had managed.
He turned back toward the stairs.
A footstep echoed from above.
Kael froze. Another step. Another. Whoever moved at the crypt's entrance descended the same stairs he'd used, weight distribution suggesting training, weapon familiarity suggesting lethality.
He pressed himself into the alcove beside the bone door, Soul Reaver raised. The footsteps grew louder.
A figure emerged from the stairwell's darkness. Tall. Female. Long coat stained with the Ashfall District's distinctive grey mud. Silver hair cropped close to her skull, and eyes that reflected no light.
Kael's finger tightened on his blade's trigger.
The woman stopped. Her gaze swept the chamber, cataloging the corpses, the collapsed blood circle, the bone door. When her eyes found Kael's hiding spot, they didn't widen. Didn't narrow.
She smiled.
"Kael Ashborn." Her voice carried the clipped precision of military training. "The Soul Reaver who escaped execution. The butcher of the Ironclad Depths. The monster who keeps defying every law of nature and death." She tilted her head. "You're smaller than the reports suggested."
"Who sent you?"
"Does it matter?" She stepped forward, hands visible at her sides. No weapons that Kael could see, but that meant nothing with Awakened of her caliber. Her spiritual pressure filled the chamber like floodwater, dense and suffocating. "The Shadow Court has been watching you for months. Your little crusade against the cultists. Your... Harvests."
Kael's blood chilled.
"You know what I am."
"We know what you do." The woman stopped ten feet away, close enough to strike, far enough to react. "We've watched you take abilities from the dying. Skills you never trained for. Powers that should be impossible." Her smile widened. "Fascinating. Horrifying. Useful."
"I don't work for the Shadow Court."
"No one works for the Shadow Court, Mr. Ashborn. We... Cooperate toward mutual interests. Right now, we share a common enemy. The cultists who defiled this tomb—they belong to a faction that threatens both of us. They want the bone door opened. They want what waits behind it released."
"What's behind it?"
"Does that matter either?" She spread her hands. "The cult wants it. We don't. That makes us allies by circumstance." Her eyes glittered. "The Shadow Court offers information. Resources. Protection from the Orders who would see you dissected for study. In exchange, we ask only that you continue doing what you do best."
"Harvesting."
"Hunting." She corrected. "The cult has become bold. Organized. Their Prime, the one they call Zarek, coordinates cells across the continent. The Shadow Court knows his face. His hiding places. His weaknesses." She paused. "We know why he framed you for the Ironclad Depths massacre."
Kael's blade rose an inch.
"Tell me."
"Not here. Not now. Information has value—ours isn't free." She reached into her coat, withdrew a small card, and tossed it onto the stone floor between them. "When you're ready to discuss terms, light that card. I'll find you within the hour."
She turned toward the stairs.
"Wait." Kael's voice echoed off bone. "You said the cult knows about me. How? I've been careful."
The woman paused. Didn't look back.
"You've been hunting cultists who've been hunting you in turn. Every kill feeds information back to their network. Every corpse they recover tells them something new about their enemy." Her footsteps resumed ascending. "The question isn't whether they'll come for you, Mr. Ashborn. It's how many they'll sacrifice to see you dead."
She vanished into the stairwell's darkness.
Kael stood alone in the chamber, Soul Reaver humming against his palm. The bone door pulsed behind him, patient and terrible. The card lay at his feet, blank except for a single symbol he didn't recognize.
He picked it up. Folded it into his coat.
Above him, the city of Thornwick churned with a thousand secrets. Cult cells operating in coordinated silence. The Shadow Court watching from shadows within shadows. The Whisper King—ancient, terrible, possibly connected to the blade he carried.
And somewhere in that chaos, Zarek laughed.
Kael climbed the stairs, leaving the bone door sealed behind him. He had work to do. Leads to follow. Enemies to harvest.
The cult wanted organized warfare? He could provide that.

Dawn painted Thornwick's rooftops the color of dried blood as Kael emerged from the Ashfall District's drainage tunnels. The city's steam-whistle symphony had already begun—factory shifts changing, the Ironworks' massive pistons cycling through their endless rhythm.
He needed rest. A safehouse. Time to process the memory fragment he'd harvested.
Instead, his feet carried him toward the Veiled Market.
The illegal bazaar operated in the spaces between legitimate commerce, hidden in warehouses and basement levels throughout the Merchant Quarter. Dealers sold soul-essence vials without asking questions. Fences moved stolen goods. Information brokers traded secrets like currency.
Kael sought one broker in particular.
Mira ran her operation from a converted textile factory, three stories of looms standing silent while her clients conducted business in the shadows between. She specialized in Order gossip—movements, disputes, internal corruption. The kind of information that got people killed if spoken aloud in the wrong company.
The factory's front entrance stood guarded by two Awakened that Kael didn't recognize—hired muscle from their stance, not true believers in any cause. They saw him coming and stepped aside without a word.
Kael had negotiated safe passage here before. Mira valued his custom.
He found her in the old overseer's office, surrounded by papers and tobacco smoke. She was older than him by maybe two decades, grey threading through dark hair she kept bound tight. Her eyes tracked him the moment he entered, cataloging injuries, mood, intent.
"You look terrible," she said.
"Insightful as always."
She gestured to a chair. Kael remained standing.
"The Shadow Court approached me last night."
Mira's cigarette paused halfway to her lips. "Which faction?"
"Faction?" That surprised him. "There are multiple?"
"The Shadow Court isn't unified, Ashborn. Never has been. Different cells answer to different masters—Orders, corporations, old families who've accumulated enough secrets to build their own power bases." She resumed smoking. "Which cell made contact?"
"A woman. Silver hair. Military bearing. Knew my name, my reputation, my..." He touched Soul Reaver's hilt. "She offered information about Zarek. About why he framed me."
"Terms?"
"Implied. Said information has value. Wanted to discuss cooperation."
Mira laughed—short, sharp, without humor. "Of course she did. The Court doesn't offer. It trades. Everything has a price, and the price is always more than you expect."
"I know how negotiations work."
"Do you?" She blew smoke toward the ceiling. "The Shadow Court doesn't trade in coin or soul-essence, Ashborn. They trade in leverage. They gather secrets the way a magpie hoards bright objects. When they offer information, they're really offering a chain. Pull one end, and you realize the other end wraps around your throat."
Kael's jaw tightened. "You think the offer was a trap."
"I think accepting without knowing the cost is always a trap." She stubbed out her cigarette. "But I also think the information they possess is real. The Court has eyes everywhere—inside the Orders, inside the cult, inside places no honest person would venture. If they know why Zarek targeted you specifically, they know something valuable."
"The cultist I killed last night mentioned a name. The Whisper King."
Mira went still. Not the subtle stillness of surprise—the rigid stillness of fear.
"Where did you hear that name?"
"In his last words. He spoke like a fanatic. Said the Whisper King showed them what's coming."
"Stop talking." Mira stood, moved to her office door, and checked the corridor outside. Satisfied, she closed it and engaged a lock that clicked with spiritual resonance. "What I'm about to tell you isn't common knowledge. The Orders have buried it. The cult has corrupted it. But in certain circles, the Whisper King is considered the worst possible thing that could happen to this world."
"Define worst."
"Not an Entity. Not a demon. Not something from beyond the Veil." She lowered her voice. "A man. The first Soul Reaver."
Kael's blood turned to ice.
He thought of the bone door. The cultist's delirious words. Soul Reaver's hunger when it recognized its former master's disciple.
"The blade I carry—"
"Came from him. Yes." Mira's eyes fixed on the weapon at his hip. "The legends say he discovered how to harvest souls before the Orders existed. Before the seal network. He built something beautiful and terrible, and when he realized what he'd become, he tried to destroy it. Tried to destroy himself." She shook her head. "He failed. The blade survived. The man... Didn't. Not entirely."
"What happened to him?"
"The Abyss took him. Remade him. Made him into something that exists between the two realms—a voice that whispers from every rift, every shadow, every crack in reality. The cult believes he's guiding them toward some great revelation. The Orders believe he's a threat to be contained." She met his gaze. "But here's the truth the Orders won't admit: they don't know what he wants anymore. Maybe he doesn't either."
Kael touched Soul Reaver's hilt. The blade offered no guidance, no warning. Just that faint, constant hunger that had become as familiar as his own heartbeat.
"The cultists I killed—they were preparing to open a bone door. Something older than the seal network. Something they wanted released."
Mira's face drained of color. "You saw the door?"
"In the crypt beneath Lord Vex's tomb. I didn't open it."
"Thank every god that's ever been worshipped." She pressed her palms against her desk. "The bone door predates the Abyssal Wars. Predates everything. Whatever's behind it, the cult has been trying to open it for decades. Maybe centuries."
"Information," Kael said. "They had information about me. Locations. Habits. They'd been tracking my movements."
"Of course they had. You're carrying something they desperately want—the original Soul Reaver's blade. With it, they could theoretically access the bone door's seal. Weaken it. Break it." She straightened. "They'll send more. Better-trained. Better-prepared. You killed cultists in a disorganized cell. Next time, they'll send something else."
"How do you know this?"
"Because the Shadow Court knows it. And if they're approaching you, it means the situation has escalated beyond normal parameters." She pulled open a drawer and withdrew a small vial—soul-essence, high-grade, the color of liquid moonlight. "Take this. Consider it an investment."
Kael looked at the vial. "I don't use soul-essence."
"You don't need to. The vial itself carries information—encoded in its spiritual signature. Hold it to your third eye and you'll see what I see: every cult cell location in Thornwick that my network has mapped. Every handler. Every asset the Shadow Court hasn't already recruited or eliminated."
"Why help me?"
"Because I'm tired of watching the world burn while the Orders argue about jurisdiction." Mira pressed the vial into his hand. "And because you've survived this long by being smarter than your enemies. Don't stop now."
Kael pocketed the vial. "The Shadow Court—can I trust their offer?"
"You can trust that they want something. The question is whether what they want aligns with what you're willing to give." She returned to her chair. "Zarek is in Thornwick. Not the outskirts. Not hiding in some rural bolt-hole. He's inside the city, coordinating cells from a position of absolute security. The Orders know this. They've known for months. They won't move against him because doing so would expose their own failures."
"Then who will?"
"Someone who doesn't care about exposure. Someone who exists outside their system and answers to no one's authority." Mira's eyes glittered. "Someone like you."
Kael left the factory as morning traffic clogged the streets. Workers streamed toward factories. Merchants opened shops. The steam-trams groaned through their routes, belching smoke that mixed with fog until the sun couldn't penetrate.
He walked the crowded avenues like a ghost, Soul Reaver hidden beneath his coat, the soul-essence vial heavy in his pocket. Mira's information burned in his mind—names, locations, timelines. Zarek had been in Thornwick for six weeks. Long enough to establish networks. Long enough to prepare.
Long enough to know Kael was hunting him.
The cultist's words echoed: *Every kill feeds information back to their network. Every corpse they recover tells them something new about their enemy.*
He'd been the hunter. Now he understood he'd always been the prey.

Night fell over Thornwick like a closing fist.
Kael chose his safehouse carefully—a condemned tenement in the Gutterfall District, condemned so many times that the city had stopped bothering to demolish it. Three rooms, collapsing floors, and a basement that leaked sewage on rainy days.
Perfect. No one looked twice at a figure entering a building everyone had written off.
He barricaded the door, checked the windows for watchers, and settled onto a mattress that had seen better decades. The soul-essence vial glowed faintly in the darkness as he pressed it against his forehead.
Information flooded through.
Maps. Diagrams. Names and faces linked by threads of relationship and debt. The cult's Thornwick network sprawled across his consciousness like a diseased tree—trunk at the center, branches spreading outward, leaves representing hundreds of and supporters.
Zarek's location pulsed like a malignant heart: the abandoned steel mill on the city's eastern edge, the Greymount Processing Facility, Sector 7.
Kael memorized every detail. The cult used that facility for storage—weapons, supplies, ritual components. Zarek himself rarely appeared in person, communicating through intermediaries. But the Shadow Court's intelligence suggested he would be present tonight. A meeting. Important visitors.
The perfect opportunity.
Or a perfect trap.
Kael opened his eyes. The vial had gone dark, spent. He set it aside and stared at the ceiling's water-stained plaster while his mind raced through possibilities.
The Shadow Court wanted him to move against Zarek. Mira's information came from that source. The woman's offer, the broker's investment—both pointed toward the same conclusion: someone wanted Kael at the Greymount facility when something happened.
But the cult also wanted him dead. The bone door's connection to Soul Reaver meant he'd become a target of opportunity, not just a threat.
Every path forward led through danger.
Kael rose. He cleaned his blade, checked his supplies, and dressed in dark clothing that wouldn't catch light or sound. When he moved through Thornwick's night, he became another shadow among thousands.
The Greymount facility dominated its sector—a cathedral of rust and industry, smokestacks reaching toward clouds that swallowed starlight. Kael approached from the south, using maintenance tunnels that threaded beneath the city's foundations.
He emerged in a loading bay, crates stacked high around him. Voices echoed from deeper within. The cult spoke openly here, confident in their secrecy.
Kael moved.
The facility's layout matched the Shadow Court's maps. He navigated past storage rooms and abandoned processing lines until he reached the central operations floor—a massive space where conveyor belts had once carried ore from mine to furnace.
Now the furnace housed a rift.
Not the wild, unstable tears the cult usually created. This one pulsed with steady rhythm, violet light painting the walls like blood. Figures moved around it—dozens of cultists in their tattered robes, some working arcane devices, others standing guard with weapons drawn.
And at the rift's center, directing everything with sharp gestures and sharper commands, stood a man Kael had last seen across a courtroom.
Zarek.
The cult leader hadn't changed. Same angular face, same cold eyes, same the smile of someone who enjoyed cruelty. He wore no robes—instead, formal attire that would have looked appropriate at a diplomatic function. As if he'd stepped from a council meeting into a nightmare.
"The Whisper King speaks of patience," Zarek was saying, voice carrying through the chamber. "The great work cannot be rushed. We strengthen the seals we must break. We prepare the world for transformation. Those who cannot adapt will fall—that is not cruelty. It is evolution."
Kael pressed himself into a shadow between crates. His hand found Soul Reaver's hilt.
"You seem troubled, Marcus." Zarek addressed someone in the crowd. "Speak freely."
A cultist stepped forward—tall, broad-shouldered, face hidden by a hood. "The Ashborn Reaver. He's hunting us openly. My cell lost four members last night."
"Four sacrifices for valuable intelligence." Zarek's smile widened. "We've learned much about our hunter. His methods. His preferences. His..." He paused. "Vulnerabilities."
"He killed my brothers."
"And we'll kill him. Soon. The opportunity approaches." Zarek turned toward the rift, spreading his arms. "The great unsealing requires three things: the bone key, which we now possess. The Reaver blade, which our hunter carries. And a sacrifice of sufficient spiritual magnitude." He looked back over his shoulder. "I wonder what greater sacrifice the Orders could possibly offer than their own monster?"
Laughter rippled through the crowd.
Kael's blood burned. They wanted Soul Reaver. They wanted him. They'd been maneuvering toward this confrontation since before his execution.
He'd walked into their trap.
Or—
He stepped out of the shadows.
"Kill me now, and you lose the blade." His voice cut through the chamber's noise. "I have to touch a corpse to harvest. You have to keep me alive until the ritual."
Zarek spun, eyes widening. Cultists reached for weapons. The rift pulsed faster.
"Kael Ashborn." Zarek recovered quickly, smile returning. "The prodigal Reaver. Come to surrender?"
"Come to negotiate." Kael walked forward, each step deliberate. "You want the blade. You want me. But the ritual requires consent or something close to it, doesn't it? You can't just take what you need. You need me willing."
"We have methods to encourage willingness."
"And I have thirty seconds to kill everyone in this room before you can use them." Soul Reaver cleared its sheath, edge gleaming with hungry light. "Watch me prove it."
Silence stretched. Cultists looked to their leader.
Zarek studied Kael for a long moment. Then he laughed—not the cruel bark from the courtroom, but something genuine. Delighted.
"You've grown. The broken thing they dragged before the tribunal wasn't half so interesting." He gestured, and his followers lowered their weapons. "Very well. Let's talk. Tell me what you want, Reaver."
"The truth. Why you framed me. Why you want the seal broken. What the Whisper King promised you that makes your cult worth dying for."
"All in good time." Zarek stepped closer. "But I'll give you something now—a gesture of good faith. Ask me one question. I'll answer honestly."
Kael's mind raced. So many possibilities. So many lies hidden in potential truths.
"The Whisper King," he said finally. "Is he real? Or just a story the cult tells to keep believers in line?"
Zarek's expression flickered—something old and tired passing behind his eyes.
"He's real, Ashborn. As real as you and I. And unlike you, he remembers what it felt like to hold your blade." He glanced at Soul Reaver. "Ask it. Ask the weapon if it recognizes its master's voice. Ask it why it hungers so constantly, so desperately."
Kael's grip tightened on the hilt. Soul Reaver hummed—not denial. Confirmation.
"Why should I believe you?"
"Because we both know the truth, Kael. The blade chose you because you were already broken. Already dying. Already closer to the void than any living man should be." Zarek spread his hands. "The Whisper King didn't frame you. I did. But the blade? The blade has been waiting centuries for someone like you."
Something scraped behind Kael. Metal on stone. Multiple footsteps, approaching from different directions.
He spun.
Cultists poured from every exit—armed, organized, surrounding him with practiced precision. The loading bay had become a killing floor. Zarek's negotiation had been a stalling tactic.
"Still want to kill everyone in the room?" Zarek asked. "You're welcome to try."
Kael's mind raced. Too many enemies. Too few exits. The rift pulsed at his back, close enough to feel its heat.
*Soul Reaver*, he thought. *What do I do?*
The blade offered no guidance. Just that endless, patient hunger.
Zarek stepped forward. "Here's what's going to happen. You're going to give me the blade. You're going to submit to the ritual. And then—"
A scream cut him off.
Then another. And another.
The cultists nearest the rift staggered backward as something erupted from its violet heart—a shape made of shadow and hunger, formless yet terrible, flowing through bodies and leaving collapse in its wake.
"RIFT BREACH!" someone shouted. "THE SEAL IS FAILING!"
Zarek's composure shattered. "No—not yet—we haven't finished the preparations—"
The shadow-thing turned toward Kael. Ancient eyes formed in its mass, ancient hunger focused on the blade he carried.
*Come home*, something whispered in his mind. *Return what was taken.*
Kael raised Soul Reaver as the chaos consumed everything.
