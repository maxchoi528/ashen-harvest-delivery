The iron door to Kael's safehouse burst inward, hinges screaming against their frame. He rolled from the cot, fingers closing around the blade beneath his pillow before his feet touched the floor.
Three figures in ash-gray cloaks poured through the opening. No weapons drawn—only hands raised, palms outward. The middle one spoke first, voice like gravel scraping bone.
"You are Kael Ashborn. The Reaver."
Kael's grip tightened. "Depends who's asking."
"We are the Order of the Ashen Veil. We know what you carry. We know what it costs." The speaker pushed back her hood, revealing a face half-mapped with scars and a single eye that gleamed with something beyond intelligence. "We also know Zarek's cult moves against you. Thornwick burns unless you listen."
Outside, distant bells began to toll—not the city's clock towers, but something older. Something that made the floorboards vibrate beneath Kael's bare feet.
"Talk fast," he said.
The woman smiled without warmth. "First, you tell us why a death-row prisoner carries the only weapon that can unmake the seal network. Second, you explain how a man executed weeks ago still breathes."
The question hung between them. Kael felt the familiar cold weight stir beneath his ribs—waiting, hungry, indifferent to whether he lived or died.
"I'll do you one better." He lowered the blade a fraction. "I'll show you exactly what this power is. But not here. Not with Zarek's dogs already circling."
The scarred woman exchanged glances with her companions. Something passed between them—a silent calculation.
"The Whisper King's Catacombs," she said finally. "Under the old cathedral. We move now, before dawn breaks the seal on his resting place."
Kael's blood chilled. The Whisper King—a name from fragments he'd pulled from dying cultists, never enough to form a complete picture. A name that made even the Orders speak in whispers.
"You want me to visit a dead god's tomb."
"We want you to understand what you carry." The woman's single eye fixed on him with terrible intensity. "And why every faction in Thornwick would see you dead before sunrise."
A crash echoed from the street below. Shouts. The wet crack of flesh against stone.
"Your choice," she added, already moving toward the back passage. "Die here in your bed, or walk into the one place in this city where the dead still speak."
Kael followed. Some choices weren't choices at all.
The catacombs stretched beneath Thornwick like the roots of a rotting tree—corridors branching into chambers, chambers opening into deeper corridors still. Gaslight flickered in iron sconces, casting shadows that seemed to move of their own accord.
Kael's escort moved in practiced silence. The scarred woman—Maren, she'd called herself—took point without hesitation, her ruined face illuminated by the wavering torchlight. The two others flanked him with the easy vigilance of professionals who'd survived by assuming everyone was a threat.
They passed walls lined with niches. Empty niches. Kael counted thirty, forty, fifty openings in the stone—each one sized for a corpse.
"Where are the bodies?" he asked.
Maren's laugh was hollow. "Where do you think? The cult has been collecting. Every spirit-sensitive soul in Thornwick, funneled to a single purpose." She paused at an intersection, consulting some internal map. "The Whisper King was the first Entity to bargain with humanity. Before the Sun God. Before the Blood God. He offered us knowledge—how to bind the Abyss, how to create weapons against entities that couldn't be killed."
"And the Orders erased him."
"The Orders consumed him. Ate his power, stripped his knowledge, scattered his remains across sealed vaults." Maren resumed walking, her boots echoing in the confined space. "All except his tomb. This one. They couldn't destroy it because destroying it would release what he knew."
Kael processed this as they descended a spiral staircase carved from living rock. The air grew colder with each step. Thicker. His skin prickled with a sensation like static charge, the kind that preceded storms or spiritual manifestation.
"What does this have to do with me?"
Maren stopped at a massive iron door, its surface etched with symbols that seemed to writhe when Kael looked at them directly. She pressed her palm to the center of the design.
"Because the weapon they used to consume him—the thing that made a Soul Reaver possible—was a fragment of his own soul. A piece he willingly gave, hoping to return." She turned to face him. "You carry that fragment, Kael Ashborn. You are the reason the Whisper King has been screaming in his tomb for three hundred years, waiting for someone who could finally hear him."
The door groaned open.
Beyond lay a circular chamber, its walls covered in the same shifting script. At the center stood a sarcophagus of black stone, its lid carved with the figure of a man whose face had been deliberately obliterated. And rising from the sarcophagus like smoke from a dying fire—a translucent form, humanoid but wrong, with too many joints and a mouth that opened where its chest should be.
"FINALLY."
The voice bypassed Kael's ears entirely, arriving as pressure behind his eyes, as vibration in his teeth. His jaw clamped shut. His hands shook.
"You carry my burden. My curse. My purpose." The spectral form drifted closer, and Kael's hand went to his blade by pure reflex—but the thing passed through steel as if it weren't there. "You have questions. I have answers. But first, you must understand what you are."
"I'm a Reaver," Kael said. "I harvest from the dead."
"NO." The word hit Kael like a physical blow, driving him to one knee. His vision swam with images he couldn't process—flashcuts of battles, of cities burning, of a figure standing atop a mountain of corpses with light pouring from its eyes. "YOU HARVEST FRAGMENTS. PIECES. SPLINTERS OF WHAT DEPARTED. BUT A SOUL IS NOT A THING TO BE TAKEN—IT IS A RIVER, CONSTANTLY FLOWING BETWEEN WORLDS."
Kael's lungs burned. His knees cracked against stone. The cold weight beneath his ribs pulsed in recognition—resonating with the entity's voice, vibrating at frequencies that made his bones ache.
"THE SIX ORDERS FEAR YOU BECAUSE YOU REMIND THEM OF WHAT THEY SACRIFICED TO ACHIEVE THEIR POWER. THEY CONSUMED ME. ATE THE WHISPER KING WHO GAVE THEM EVERYTHING. AND WHEN THEY FOUND THEY COULD NOT FULLY CONTAIN WHAT THEY HAD STOLEN—"
The spirit made a gesture, and Kael felt the presence beneath his ribs surge, flooding his veins with ice.
"THEY SCATTERED IT. HID IT IN THE BLOODLINES OF THE WORLD. HOPING IT WOULD DIE OUT. HOPING NO ONE WOULD EVER CARRY IT AGAIN."
"Zarek's cult," Kael gasped, fighting to his feet. "They're trying to—"
"UNMAKE THE NETWORK. YES. BUT NOT FOR THE REASON YOU BELIEVE." The Whisper King's form flickered, its too-many joints creaking as it turned toward Maren and her companions. "THEY DO NOT WISH TO RELEASE THE ENTITIES. THEY WISH TO CONSUME THEM. AS THEY CONSUMED ME. THE SEVENTY-TWO RIFTS ARE NOT PRISONS—THEY ARE MEALS, PREPARED FOR A FEAST THAT HAS BEEN PLANNED FOR THREE CENTURIES."
Maren's hand flew to her blade. "That's impossible. The Concordat forbids—"
"THE CONCORDAT IS A SHAM. A STORY THE ORDERS TELL THEMSELVES TO SLEEP AT NIGHT. THE REAL GAME BEGAN WHEN THE FIRST SEAL WAS CARVED. EVERY RIFT, EVERY ENTITY, EVERY DEATH IN THORNWICK—ALL OF IT CALCULATED. ALL OF IT PART OF A PLAN TO GIVE SIX MORTALS THE POWER OF GODS."
Kael's mind raced. The conspiracy wasn't just cultists and chaos—it was the Orders themselves, playing a game so long that living memory had forgotten the starting move.
"The Sepulcher tablets," he said. "The True Names—"
"THE KEYS TO THE FEAST. NAMES THAT BIND ENTITIES TO THEIR RIFTS. ONCE ALL SEVENTY-TWO ARE PRONOUNCED BY ONE POSSESSED OF THE REAVER'S FRAGMENT, THE SEAL NETWORK BECOMES A GATE. AND THE GATE BECOMES A MOUTH."
The chamber trembled. Dust rained from the ceiling. Somewhere above, the tolling bells intensified—a warning or a signal, Kael couldn't tell.
"Zarek has some of the tablets," Maren said, her voice tight.
"HE DOES. THE REMAINING TABLETS ARE SCATTERED ACROSS THE SIX ORDERS, HIDDEN IN VAULTS, SACRED TEXTS, AND THE MEMORIES OF THE DEAD." The Whisper King's form was fading now, its edges dissolving into wisps of pale light. "BUT SOMETHING HAS CHANGED. SOMETHING YOU HAVE DONE, REAVER. YOUR AWAKENING DISTURBED THE PATTERN. THE SEALS ARE WEAKENING FASTER THAN CALCULATED."
Kael's stomach dropped. The Great Rift's degradation. The cult's sudden aggression. The assassination attempts that had been escalating for weeks.
"I triggered this," he said. It wasn't a question.
"YOU ACCELERATED IT. THE NETWORK FAILS. AND WHEN IT FAILS—"
The spirit's mouth opened impossibly wide.
"—SIX HUNGRY GODS WILL BATTLE FOR THE PRIVILEGE OF EATING WHAT REMAINS. HUMANITY WILL BE A SIDEDISH."
The apparition collapsed inward, spiraling toward Kael like water down a drain. He tried to dodge, but the spirit moved faster than thought, passing through his chest with a sensation like ice and fire and something else—something that didn't belong in any language he knew.
Kael gasped, his hands flying to his chest. The chamber spun around him. When his vision cleared, he was on his knees, and Maren was pulling him upright with surprising strength.
"What did you see?" she demanded. "What did he show you?"
The information moved through him—not as memories, but as understanding. Fragments of the Whisper King's knowledge, slotted into place like tumblers in a lock. The names of the six who had consumed him. The structure of the seal network. The method by which True Names bound entities to their rifts.
But it was incomplete. Gaping holes remained—locations hidden behind veils the Whisper King himself could not penetrate, timelines obscured by the very seals he'd helped create. The spirit had given what he could. What remained was for the living to discover.
"The ritual," Kael said, his voice rough. "The binding circle. It's powered by the rift itself. Every entity bound to the network feeds energy into the circle. When I approach, the circle will try to absorb my power—same as it does with any Awakened."
"Which means—"
"Which means I don't need to break the circle. I need to redirect it." Kael's eyes gleamed with terrible understanding. "The Whisper King showed me how the seals work. They're not walls. They're funnels. Energy flows in one direction—toward the entities, toward the feast. If I can create a feedback loop, redirect that energy back toward the rift—"
"You could collapse the entire binding."
"I could collapse every binding. Every seal. Every entity. All seventy-two rifts, opened at once."
Maren stared at him. "That's insane. The resulting backlash would—"
"Would kill me. Probably. But it would also kill Zarek, destroy the cult, and release the entities without the consumption ritual." Kael began walking toward the chamber's exit. "The conspiracy wants to feed the world to six gods. I'm offering an alternative. Seventy-two gods, fighting each other, too busy to bother with humanity."
"That's not a plan. That's a suicide bombing."
"That's a negotiation." He glanced back at her. "And it only works if Zarek doesn't see it coming. Which means I need you and your people to create enough chaos that he focuses on the obvious threats."
"The Ashen Veil is not your distraction force."
"No. You're my cover. The Orders are mobilizing. The Shadow Court is moving. Everyone's converging on the rift, and everyone thinks they're the only ones who understand what's happening." Kael's voice hardened. "Let them keep thinking that. Let them throw themselves at the cult's defenses while I find another way in."
"And if you fail?"
"Then humanity gets eaten by gods. But that was always the plan, wasn't it? The conspiracy's plan. The Orders' plan. Everyone's plan except mine."
He left her standing in the chamber, the Whisper King's remnants still echoing in the carved walls. Behind him, the city stirred with preparations for a battle that would reshape reality.
But Kael needed more than knowledge. Knowledge alone wouldn't save him. He needed power. Fresh power. The kind that came from the dead.
"The cultists you've killed," he said when Maren caught up to him in the corridor. "Where are the bodies?"
Maren's expression flickered. Her jaw tightened. A muscle in her cheek jumped.
"We've been disposing of—"
"I need to harvest them. Every corpse you have access to. I'll process them before we move."
"Processing takes time. And if my superiors learn—"
"Your superiors sent you to recruit a Soul Reaver. This is what that looks like." Kael met her eye without flinching. "I can fight through cultists. I cannot fight through them exhausted and underpowered."
Maren was silent for a long moment. Then she nodded.
"There's a cold storage three levels down. Bodies recovered over the last week."
Kael felt the presence beneath his ribs stir with anticipation. "Let's see what they've got."
The cold storage smelled of ice and copper. Bodies lay on stone tables, each one tagged with a number and a date. Kael moved between them, one hand extended, his power awakening with each touch.
The first corpse yielded combat instincts—muscle memory from a cultist who'd trained for years. The second gave him a fragment of entity-touched essence, foreign and uncomfortable but useful. The third—
Kael's perception shifted. The walls of the storage chamber became translucent, revealing streams of energy flowing through hidden channels. The bodies pulsed with fading light. And beyond them, far above, he sensed the presence of Maren and her companions—two human-shaped voids against the ambient spiritual glow of the city.
He harvested faster. A skill for suppressing fear. A technique for moving without sound. A memory fragment containing the layout of a cult safehouse. Each acquisition slotted into place, building toward something coherent.
But as he reached the last table, he stopped. The body before him wore the uniform of a Thornwick city guard. Young. The jaw slack, the skin waxy. No visible wounds. The tag read a date from days past.
Kael's enhanced senses traced the spiritual residue clinging to the corpse. The guard hadn't died in combat. He'd been harvested—his spiritual essence stripped clean by something that left no physical mark. The technique was crude, wasteful, and utterly recognizable to anyone who understood how such things worked.
Not the cult's method. Not the Orders' either. The Ashen Veil's signature.
He looked up. Maren stood in the doorway, watching him. Her single eye revealed nothing.
"Where did you find this one?" Kael asked.
"Patrolman. Found near the western canal. We assumed cult activity."
"Assumed." Kael let the word hang. He touched the corpse's forehead. A faint echo of the guard's final moments surfaced—confusion, then darkness. Whoever had killed this man had done it quietly and without struggle. "This wasn't the cult. The technique is wrong. Too controlled. Too clean."
Maren's face remained stone. "Meaning?"
"Meaning someone else is operating in Thornwick. Someone with access to the guard rotations." Kael released the body and straightened. "How many more like this?"
"We've recovered what we could. The situation is chaotic. Bodies come from many sources."
"That's not an answer."
"It's the only one I have." Her tone flattened. "Focus on what you need, Reaver. The dead won't keep."
Kael studied her for a long breath. Then he returned to his work. But the guard's face stayed with him—a young man who'd died without a mark, his soul stripped by hands that weren't Zarek's.
The Ashen Veil wasn't what it claimed to be. Or it was exactly what it claimed to be, and that was worse.
He finished the last harvest and straightened. His body hummed with accumulated power. His mind held knowledge that would take years to fully process. And his gut understood, with the certainty of instinct, that he was exactly where the conspiracy wanted him.
A pawn on a board he couldn't see. A piece in a game that had been playing out for three centuries.
But pawns could change the game.
He walked back to Maren.
"I'm ready," he said. "Let's go kill a god."
The exit from the catacombs opened into an alley behind the old cathedral, dawn light bleeding across rain-slicked cobblestones. The city's bells had fallen silent, but Kael's enhanced senses picked up the distant sounds of mobilization—boots on stone, the clatter of weapons, voices barking orders. Everyone converging.
"The western approach," Maren said, pointing toward the industrial quarter. "Abandoned factories. The cult hasn't fortified that route—they're expecting us through the sewer system."
"And you want me to walk into a trap?"
"I want you to walk through one. The western approach is where they're weakest because they think it's impregnable. Awakened guards. Cultists. A lesser wraith."
Kael's new senses prickled. Lesser wraiths were remnants of spiritual energy that clung to places of violent death. They couldn't be killed in the conventional sense—only dispersed or bound. And they fed on the living, growing stronger with each death they touched.
"You're sending me through a feeding ground."
"I'm sending you through the only path the cult hasn't sealed. The wraith is a territorial creature, not a deliberate guard. It kills anything that enters its domain, cultist or otherwise. The cult avoids the western approach because of it." Maren's scar twisted. "Use it."
Kael weighed the options. Every path was soaked in danger. The conspiracy had been preparing for this moment longer than he'd been alive. Every advantage he thought he had was accounted for.
But the Whisper King's knowledge burned in his mind, offering insights no one else possessed. The cult's ritual had a flaw. A single point of failure that would unravel everything if struck at the right moment.
"The ritual site," he said. "The central platform. What's the defense?"
Maren's expression tightened. "A binding circle. Zarek at the center, performing the True Names. The circle prevents interference by anyone below a certain threshold of power."
"So I need to surpass that threshold."
"You need to surpass it before Zarek finishes. While fighting. Without any additional harvests." Maren's single eye held his. "Can you?"
Kael's mouth curved. Not a smile. "Good thing I'm not planning to fight through them."
He pulled the knowledge from the Whisper King's fragment. The ritual site. The binding circle. The specific currents of energy required to maintain it.
"The circle isn't powered by Zarek. It's powered by the rift itself. When I approach, the circle will try to absorb my power. I don't need to break it. I need to redirect it—create a feedback loop, send the energy back toward the rift. Collapse every binding. Every seal. Every entity. All seventy-two rifts, opened at once."
Maren stared at him. "That's insane. The resulting backlash would—"
"Would kill me. Probably. But it would also kill Zarek, destroy the cult, and release the entities without the consumption ritual." Kael began walking toward the industrial quarter. "The conspiracy wants to feed the world to six gods. I'm offering an alternative. Seventy-two gods, fighting each other, too busy to bother with humanity."
"That's not a plan. That's a suicide bombing."
"That's a negotiation." He glanced back at her. "And it only works if Zarek doesn't see it coming. Which means I need you and your people to create enough chaos that he focuses on the obvious threats."
"The Ashen Veil is not your distraction force."
"No. You're my cover. The Orders are mobilizing. The Shadow Court is moving. Everyone's converging on the rift, and everyone thinks they're the only ones who understand what's happening." Kael's voice hardened. "Let them keep thinking that. Let them throw themselves at the cult's defenses while I find another way in."
"And if you fail?"
"Then humanity gets eaten by gods. But that was always the plan, wasn't it? The conspiracy's plan. The Orders' plan. Everyone's plan except mine."
He left her standing in the alley, dawn light painting his shadow long across the stones. Behind him, the city stirred with preparations for a battle that would reshape reality. Above him, the sky churned with clouds that hadn't been there before—spiritual pressure made manifest, the world's awareness of the catastrophe approaching.
Kael walked toward the industrial quarter, alone, carrying the fragment of a dead god and the knowledge of a conspiracy that spanned centuries.
The path ahead was soaked in blood and fire. The ending was uncertain.
But for the first time since his execution, he understood exactly what he was fighting for.
Not survival. Not revenge. Not even justice.
A world where the truth about death could finally be told.
The western approach opened before him—a canyon of crumbling brick and rusted iron, the factories long abandoned, their chimneys jutting against the sky. The gas lamps here had been smashed. The only light came from the pale glow of dawn filtering through broken skylights.
Kael's enhanced senses mapped the space. Cultist positions—scattered through the ruins, their spiritual signatures flickering. Three Awakened among them, brighter, denser. And deeper in the industrial quarter, a cold spot where no life registered at all. The lesser wraith's hunting ground.
He moved forward. The cold spot expanded with each step, pressing against his awareness. The air thickened. Frost began to form on the broken brickwork around him, crystalline patterns spreading across surfaces.
Then the temperature dropped further, and Kael's breath came out in white clouds. The wraith was close.
A sound reached him—not a voice, not a scream, but something between. A vibration in the air that bypassed his ears and settled directly into his bones. The wraith's presence was a pressure against his skull, a weight on his chest.
And then it appeared.
The lesser wraith coalesced from the cold itself—a shape like a man stretched wrong, elongated limbs trailing vapor, a face that was all mouth and hunger. It didn't approach. It didn't need to. The cold radiating from it reached Kael first, biting through his clothes, sinking into his muscles.
Kael's hand went to his blade. The steel vibrated in his grip—responding to the entity's proximity, to the fragment of the Whisper King's power humming beneath his ribs.
The wraith opened its mouth. The sound that emerged was the last sound its victims had made—a chorus of dying breaths, wet and ragged and desperate. It fed on death. It was made of death. And it was very, very hungry.
Kael's grip tightened. The wraith's cold intensified, frost crawling up his arms, his chest, his throat. His lungs burned with each breath. The Whisper King's knowledge offered a path—lesser wraiths were fragments of spiritual residue, bound to the place of their formation. They couldn't be killed. They could be consumed.
His power stirred. The cold weight beneath his ribs rose, eager, reaching toward the wraith with an instinct that transcended thought.
The wraith recoiled.
For a single heartbeat, the entity's hunger warred with something older and deeper—an instinct that recognized what Kael carried and wanted no part of it. The wraith's form flickered, its mouth closing, its limbs drawing inward.
Kael stepped forward. The wraith recoiled further. The cold retreated a fraction.
He stepped again. The wraith's form began to dissolve, vapor retreating into the cracks between bricks, into the dark spaces beneath collapsed rooftops. It was afraid. Not of his blade. Not of his power. Of what he was. What the Whisper King's fragment made him.
The path cleared. The cold receded to a manageable chill, the wraith's presence reduced to a distant pressure at the edge of his awareness.
Kael moved through the wraith's territory without stopping. The cultists beyond were another matter. He could sense them ahead—dozens of spiritual signatures clustering around the approach to the rift.
But the wraith's retreat had cost him. The cultists would notice the cold's absence. They'd know something was coming.
Kael drew his blade and kept walking.
The first cultist came at him from behind a collapsed wall—a woman with ash-gray robes and a blade that gleamed with entity-touched steel. Kael sidestepped her lunge, caught her wrist, and drove his own blade through the gap between her ribs. She dropped without a sound.
The second and third came together. Kael ducked under a swinging chain, redirected a thrust, and used the first attacker's body as a shield against the second's blade. Blood sprayed across the frost-covered brickwork. He harvested as they fell—the combat instincts flowing into him, the entity-touched essence adding fuel to the fire burning beneath his ribs.
More cultists. The narrow passages of the industrial district worked in his favor—they could only come at him two or three at a time, and each death fed his power. The Awakened among them were harder. The first one nearly took his arm off with a blast of concentrated spiritual energy, and Kael only survived by triggering the fear-suppression skill he'd harvested from the cold storage.
The second Awakened was stronger. Kael felt the impact of her power like a hammer blow to the chest, his ribs creaking, his vision swimming. He countered with the Whisper King's knowledge—not power, but understanding. He redirected her attack, channeling the energy past him and into the cultist behind her. She screamed as her own power consumed her ally.
Kael harvested them both. The rush of accumulated essence was intoxicating. His body hummed with it—too much, too fast, the power threatening to overflow its containers. But he pushed forward. The rift was close. He could feel it now—a vast emptiness in the world's spiritual fabric, a hole that breathed.
And beyond the cultists, beyond the ruined factories, the Great Rift opened.
Kael stood at the edge of the industrial quarter and stared. The rift was not what he'd expected. Not a crack or a fissure or a tear in reality. It was an absence—a space where the world stopped existing, replaced by a darkness that was not darkness but something older. Something that existed before light was invented.
At its center, a platform of black stone floated. On the platform, a circle of symbols burned with white fire. And within the circle, a figure stood with arms raised, voice chanting words that made the air itself flinch.
Zarek.
The binding circle pulsed with power. Kael could see it now—the energy flowing from the rift, through the seals, through the network, feeding into the circle. The Whisper King's knowledge mapped every current, every flow, every point where the system could be broken.
He had one chance. One moment to create the feedback loop, to redirect the energy, to collapse every seal simultaneously.
Kael drew a breath. The cold weight of Soul Reaver settled into his bones, ready.
He stepped toward the rift.
