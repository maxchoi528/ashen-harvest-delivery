The blade caught firelight as it swept toward Kael's throat.
He ducked, feeling the heat graze his hair, and drove his shoulder into the assassin's sternum. Bones cracked. The figure folded around his thrust and hit the cobblestones three feet away. Kael didn't pause to check if the man stayed down. He rolled left as a crossbow bolt punched through the space where his head had been.
"Reload that," he said, rising behind a stack of crates. "I want you loaded when I come for you."
The Thornwick dockside warehouse reeked of salt and rotting fish. Moonlight squeezed through gaps in the corrugated roof, painting silver stripes across the blood already pooling beneath the fallen. Kael counted seven bodies. His breathing came hard—he'd killed three of them himself, and the other four had been his before they'd arrived. Someone had known he was coming. Someone had sent a welcoming committee of seven Awakened mercenaries to a dead-drop meeting.
Kael wiped his blade on a corpse's coat and crouched lower.
"Ashborn." The voice came from the main door, female, cultured, laced with amusement. "You look terrible. Have they been feeding you at all, or has prison cuisine gotten even worse since I was inside?"
A woman stepped into the warehouse, flanked by two hulking guards whose skin had a metallic sheen. Iron Vanguard. Heavy-class Awakened, stage two or three. The woman herself wore traveling clothes of expensive cut, her silver hair pinned back in a style that screamed old money. She was maybe fifty, with the kind of face that had been beautiful once and had decided to become something more interesting instead.
"You must be the Thornwood Matriarch," Kael said. "I was wondering when you'd stop sending puppets."
"And reveal myself instead? How reckless." The Matriarch smiled, showing teeth that were slightly too sharp. "Though I confess, watching you handle my puppets was instructive. The Mortician's Guild vouches for your skills, but seeing is believing. You killed Marcus and his crew in under four minutes. That's impressive for someone supposedly fresh out of prison."
"They came at me with blades. I'm a fast learner."
"So I've heard." She moved closer, her guards maintaining formation. "But skills aren't why I'm here. I'm here because you have something that belongs to me, and I've come to collect."
Kael kept his stance loose. The dockside exit lay fifteen feet behind him, blocked by one guard. The loading bay door was closer but equally guarded. The skylight directly above him was his only clear route, and he wasn't sure he could make the jump without leaving himself exposed.
"What exactly do you think I have?"
"Don't play coy. The Sepulcher tablets. Fragment three, specifically." The Matriarch's eyes glittered in the moonlight. "I know the Shadow Court has been whispering in your ear about those fragments. I know they want them. But what you don't know—what they haven't told you—is that fragment three was stolen from my family three hundred years ago, during the Night of Broken Tables. It belongs to the Thornwood bloodline by right of inheritance. And I want it back."
Kael's mind raced. The Sepulcher tablets. He'd heard rumors about them, fragments of ancient knowledge supposedly recorded by the first Soul Reaver. Fragment one had surfaced in the Black Archives of the Arcane Spire, fragment two was supposedly in the possession of the Whisper King, and fragment three had vanished sometime during the chaos following the Abyssal Wars. No one knew where it was.
Until now, apparently.
"And if I don't have it?"
"Then we have nothing to discuss, and I leave. My guards here are capable of killing you if I ask them to, but I'd prefer not to waste the resources. You're useful. I'd you remained useful." She tilted her head. "But if you're lying about not having it, well. That would be unfortunate."
Kael sheathed his blade. "I don't have it. I've never even seen it."
"Hmm." The Matriarch studied him for a long moment. "You know, I believe you. Your soul doesn't carry the resonance of a liar. That makes this more complicated, not less." She began walking a slow circle around him, her guards tracking his movement. "Because if you don't have the tablet, then someone else does. Someone who wanted you to come here tonight. Someone who wanted us to meet."
"The Shadow Court?"
"They're involved, certainly. But they're not the only ones playing this game." She stopped directly in front of him. "Let me tell you something that you probably don't know, Ashborn. The Shadow Court isn't one organization. It's three. The Council of Whispers controls political operations. The Veiled Hand controls military operations. And the Hollow Crown controls something else entirely—something they've been cultivating for centuries. A plan to break the seal network and let the Abyss flood through."
"That sounds expensive."
"It's not about money. It's about power. The Abyssal Entities aren't demons, Ashborn. They're not even hostile, not in the way the Orders teach. They're... Investors. They made deals with certain bloodlines during the Abyssal Wars, promises of power in exchange for services rendered. The Thornwood family was one of those bloodlines. Our ancestors helped build the seal network. We also helped ensure it would eventually fail."
Kael felt ice crawling up his spine. "You're telling me your family wants to destroy the seals."
"I'm telling you my family wanted to destroy the seals. Three hundred years ago, someone disagreed. Someone hid fragment three, the key to activating the emergency protocol built into the seal network, and they scattered the other fragments to prevent the Hollow Crown from ever completing their plan. The Shadow Court has been hunting those fragments ever since. And now you've appeared—a Soul Reaver, carrying the one ability that can extract the tablet's location from the dead if anyone still has it."
"A useful tool."
"A useful threat." The Matriarch smiled coldly. "The Hollow Crown wants to control you, Ashborn. The other two factions want to destroy you. And I want to hire you, because I think you're the only person in Thornwick who might want to see the seals stay intact. Isn't that right? You spent twelve years in Thornwick Prison for a crime you didn't commit. The Shadow Court framed you. The Orders ignored your evidence. You have every reason to hate them. But you also know—, I suspect you know—that if those seals break, it won't be the Orders who suffer first. It'll be everyone."
Kael said nothing. His hand rested on his blade's pommel, but he didn't draw. The Matriarch was talking, and talking meant she wanted something. People who wanted to kill you didn't talk this much.
"What do you want me to do?"
"Find the tablet. Bring it to me. In exchange, I'll give you something the Shadow Court never will—information. Names. Faces. The identity of the person who ordered your arrest, and the location of everyone in Thornwick who answered to them." She reached into her coat and produced a small glass vial filled with something that glowed faintly amber. "And to prove I'm serious, here's an advance payment. Stage-two soul-essence, harvested from a Hollow Knight. Your Soul Reaver ability can absorb it directly, without processing. Consider it a gesture of good faith."
Kael stared at the vial. Soul-essence in that concentration was worth a small fortune—enough to buy a house in the merchant quarter, or fund a small army for a month. The Matriarch was either very generous or very desperate.
"Why me? You have resources. Guards. Connections. Why trust a death-row prisoner with this?"
"Because I'm being watched. The Hollow Crown has agents in my household—I haven't identified them all yet, and until I do, I can't move openly. You, on the other hand, are already their target. They can't watch you any more closely than they already are. And you have something I don't—the ability to harvest memories from the dead. If the tablet is hidden in a corpse somewhere, you can find it. If someone knows where it is and they're dead, you can extract that knowledge. You're a scalpel, Ashborn. Sometimes you need a scalpel instead of a hammer."
Kael took the vial. The glass was warm against his palm, and he could feel the essence inside pulsing in time with his heartbeat. His Soul Reaver ability stirred, hungry, recognizing what it was.
"One condition," he said. "The person who framed me. I want them alive. I don't care what you do with them after, but I want to be the one who finds them."
"Done." The Matriarch extended her hand. "We have an agreement. I'll send word when I have a lead. Until then, try not to die. You're more valuable alive."
She left as abruptly as she'd arrived, her guards flanking her through the loading bay door. Kael stood alone among the corpses, the vial warming his hand, his mind churning through everything she'd told him.
The Shadow Court was three organizations. The Hollow Crown wanted to break the seals. The Thornwood family had been complicit, centuries ago. Someone had stolen fragment three to prevent catastrophe, and now the Shadow Court was hunting it.
And somewhere in Thornwick, someone knew where it was.
Kael knelt beside the nearest corpse—a mercenary with a crossbow, killed by his own bolt. He placed his palm against the man's cooling forehead and reached inward.
The Soul Reaver awakened.
It was like plunging his hand into ice water and fire simultaneously. His consciousness split, one half staying in his body and the other sliding through the veil into the realm of the dead. He felt the mercenary's soul—not gone, but departing, streaming toward the Abyss in ribbons of fading light. Kael reached out and caught them, pulling the threads toward himself.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 2.3 spiritual units from the remains. Memory fragment acquired: combat training from the Order of Iron.]
The mercenary had been Order-trained. That wasn't unusual—many mercenaries had served before leaving or being discharged. But the memories Kael pulled next made his breath catch.
Images cascaded through his mind. A warehouse, different from this one, filled with crates marked with the Mortician's Guild sigil. A meeting. Three figures in hoods, their faces obscured. A voice speaking—female, educated, familiar. The Matriarch's voice.
"The Soul Reaver will come to the dead-drop. We ensure he meets the Thornwood representative. When they clash, we eliminate both and retrieve the tablet ourselves."
"Suppose he kills her first?"
"Then we harvest the tablet's location from her corpse. Either way, we get what we want."
Kael wrenched his hand back. His palm was numb, his head pounding, and the corpse beneath his fingers had gone cold and empty. The soul was completely consumed now—no chance of resurrection, no fragment left for any other Reaver.
The Matriarch had lied. She'd been part of the ambush, not a neutral party. She'd wanted to kill him and harvest the tablet's location herself, using his own ability to extract information from her body if necessary. The vial of soul-essence was probably poisoned or trapped, designed to track him or cripple him when he tried to absorb it.
Kael dropped the vial and crushed it under his heel.
He had maybe three minutes before reinforcements arrived. The Matriarch would know the ambush had failed—she'd probably set up a psychic link with her guards, or had witnesses waiting nearby. She'd send more people, better people, and they wouldn't offer him bargains this time.
He needed to move.
The skylight was his best option. Kael sheathed his blade and sprinted for the stacked crates beneath the opening. He jumped, caught the edge, and hauled himself up onto the roof. The night air hit him like a slap, cold and sharp with the smell of chimney smoke and distant industry. Below him, the docks stretched toward the harbor, a maze of warehouses and quays where ships from a dozen nations unloaded their cargo.
He ran.
The rooftops of Thornwick were treacherous—loose tiles, sudden gaps, steam pipes that vented scalding vapor without warning. Kael had spent years navigating them after dark, back when he was still a free man running smuggling routes for the Veiled Market. He'd thought those days were behind him.
He'd been wrong about a lot of things.
Behind him, he heard shouts. The Matriarch's reinforcements, spilling into the streets below. Someone with a spyglass would spot him soon, track his movement, anticipate his destination. He needed to lose them completely or find somewhere defensible where he could make a stand.
The Underworld. That was the only option.
Thornwick was built on layers—surface streets above, underground passages below. The old sewer system had been expanded over centuries into a network of tunnels, chambers, and forgotten spaces that connected the entire city. The Underworld was controlled by a dozen factions, from the Mortician's Guild's harvesting rights to the Thieves' Guild's smuggling routes to the strange territorial boundaries that the Awakened had established for their own purposes. No one faction controlled all of it, which meant no one faction could control him once he entered.
Kael dropped from the rooftops into an alley, hit the ground rolling, and kept running. Three blocks later, he found what he was looking for—a cellar entrance marked with the symbol of the Underworld's neutrality. A set of stairs leading down into darkness, guarded by a single figure who looked up as Kael approached.
"Ashborn." The guard was old, scarred, missing two fingers on his left hand. Kael didn't recognize him, but the man clearly recognized Kael. "You're supposed to be dead."
"Reports of my death were exaggerated. I need passage."
"Passage costs."
"I have nothing."
"Then you pay later. With interest." The guard stepped aside, revealing the stairwell. "The Hollow Court is holding meetings in the Drowned Hall tonight. Word is they've got something big. You might want to be there when it happens."
Kael descended.
The Underworld of Thornwick was a city within a city, lit by phosphorescent fungi cultivated in the deep tunnels and by the occasional lantern hung by travelers who couldn't see in the dark. The air was thick, humid, carrying the smell of the underground river that ran beneath everything and the more distant tang of industry—tanneries, breweries, chemical works that needed to dump their waste somewhere the city wouldn't notice.
He walked for twenty minutes before he found the Drowned Hall.
It was an old reservoir, drained centuries ago and converted into a gathering space. The floor was still flooded in places, black water reflecting the light of a hundred torches hung from iron sconces. Figures moved through the chamber—dozens of them, maybe more, representing every faction that had business in the Underworld. Kael saw Mortician's Guild representatives in their white robes, Thieves' Guild enforcers in black leather, even a handful of Order agents who were supposed to be neutral but never really were.
And at the far end of the hall, seated on a throne carved from the prow of an ancient ship, sat a woman Kael had hoped never to see again.
Lyra.
She looked different from when he'd last seen her—older, harder, with silver threading through her dark hair and scars on her hands that hadn't been there before. But her eyes were the same, that particular shade of gray that seemed to see through walls and into the souls of everyone around her. She was Shadow Court. Had been Shadow Court, once, back when she'd been his handler during his smuggling days. She'd sold him out to the Inquisition to save herself, or so he'd thought.
Now she sat on the Hollow Court's throne, and Kael understood with sudden, terrible clarity what the Matriarch had been hinting at.
Lyra wasn't just Shadow Court.
Lyra was the Hollow Crown.
"You found the Underworld faster than I expected." Lyra's voice carried across the water without effort. "I suppose prison didn't dull your instincts as much as I feared."
"Lyra." Kael stopped at the edge of the flooded floor, keeping his hand near his blade. "I thought you were dead."
"I thought so too, for a while. Turns out the Inquisition's methods of determining death are slightly less reliable than they claim." She smiled, and there was no warmth in it. "Welcome to the Drowned Hall, Kael. Please, come closer. We have much to discuss."
"I don't think so."
"You don't have a choice." Lyra gestured, and figures emerged from the shadows around the hall—Awakened warriors, stage two and three, weapons drawn. "The Underworld isn't neutral territory, not anymore. I bought it six months ago. Every guard, every faction leader, every thief with a stake in these tunnels works for me now. You're surrounded, Kael. The only question is whether we talk first or fight first."
Kael evaluated his options. Eight warriors, maybe ten. He could take three, maybe four, before they overwhelmed him. The water would slow his movement, give them time to coordinate. His Soul Reaver ability required touch, and they wouldn't let him get close enough to harvest unless he killed a few first.
"What do you want?"
"I want what I've always wanted. The truth." Lyra rose from her throne and descended the steps toward him. "You were framed, Kael. You know that. I know that. But you don't know who gave the order, and more importantly, you don't know why. You think it was because of your smuggling, or your association with me. You're wrong. You were framed because of what you are. A Soul Reaver."
"I've always been a Soul Reaver."
"Yes. But you weren't always awakened to it. Something happened in that prison—something triggered your abilities to fully manifest. And someone in Thornwick noticed. Someone who needed a Soul Reaver controlled, contained, or eliminated. Someone who knew what you could become if you had time to grow."
"Who?"
Lyra stopped ten feet away from him. "The person who ordered your arrest was Inquisitor Vane. He's been hunting Soul Reavers for twenty years, ever since the first one—a man named Aldric Thorne—disappeared under mysterious circumstances. Vane doesn't just want to arrest you, Kael. He wants to study you. Understand how Soul Reavers work. And if he can't understand, he'll settle for destroying."
Kael felt something cold settle in his chest. Inquisitor Vane. He knew the name—everyone in Thornwick knew the name. The Order of Dawn's most feared hunter, stage three Awakened, specializing in tracking down rogue practitioners and cultists. Vane had been responsible for a dozen executions Kael had heard about, and probably a hundred more that never made the news.
"Why are you telling me this?"
"Because we have a common enemy. Vane is coming for you. He's been building a case, gathering evidence, preparing to move. In three days, maybe four, he'll have enough to justify a public arrest. And when he takes you, he won't bother with a trial. He'll drag you to the Inquisition's black site outside the city and dissect you to understand how your soul works."
"Then I'll run."
"Where? The seals are weakening. The Abyss is pushing through at seventeen points across the continent. In a year, maybe two, there won't be anywhere that's safe from what's coming. The only way to survive is to be strong enough to fight. And the only way to get strong fast is what you already have—the Soul Reaver."
Lyra extended her hand. "Work with me, Kael. Not for me. With me. I have resources, intelligence, connections. I know where the Sepulcher tablets are hidden. I know the weaknesses of every major faction in Thornwick. I know the names of every Shadow Court operative in the city and half the Order agents who take bribes. I can give you what you need to grow powerful enough to face Vane. All I ask in return is your help when the time comes to break the seals."
"Break the seals?" Kael shook his head. "You want to let the Abyss through. That's insane."
"Is it? The seals have been failing for centuries. Every year, more energy leaks through, more Entities manifest, more corruption spreads. We're not preventing an invasion—we're delaying an inevitability. The question isn't whether the Abyss comes through. It's whether humanity controls the transition or gets swept away by it. The Hollow Crown believes we can manage it. We can channel the Abyssal energy, use it to elevate ourselves, become something more than what we are now."
"You're talking about becoming like the Entities."
"I'm talking about becoming their equals. Partners, not servants." Lyra's eyes glittered in the torchlight. "The Entities aren't monsters, Kael. They're beings of pure potential. They've offered deals to certain bloodlines before—deals that made those families powerful beyond measure. The Thornwoods, the Ashborns, a dozen others that most people have forgotten. We carry that potential in our blood. The seals suppress it. Break the seals, and we can finally access what was always meant to be ours."
Kael stared at her. The Ashborns. She said it like it meant something, like it was significant.
"What do you know about my family?"
"I know they were one of the original six bloodlines that helped build the seal network. I know they had access to the Sepulcher tablets, helped record the True Names of the Entities in exchange for certain considerations. And I know that when the seals were completed, the Ashborns were... Removed. Their bloodline was supposed to be extinct. But here you are, a Soul Reaver, walking into my hall like you have every right to be here." Lyra smiled. "Doesn't that tell you something?"
Kael's mind raced. The Ashborns. He'd never known his family name growing up—his mother had died when he was young, his father before that, and the orphanage records had been destroyed in a fire when he was twelve. He'd pieced together fragments over the years, but nothing coherent. Nothing that explained why he'd awakened as a Soul Reaver when no one in his family had ever shown any signs of spiritual ability.
"You're saying I'm descended from the people who built the seals."
"I'm saying you're descended from the people who knew how to break them. And that makes you the most valuable person in Thornwick, Kael. More valuable than you know."
Kael reached for his Soul Reaver ability, testing the air. The Entities existed beyond the veil, massive presences that had been imprisoned for millennia. Their True Names were recorded on the tablets. If he could read those Names, he could potentially communicate with them, bargain with them. Or command them.
The power to control the Entities. The power the original Ashborns had wielded before they were erased.
Lyra watched him work through it, her expression unreadable.
"You have a choice," she said. "Join me, and we'll unlock that power together. Refuse, and I'll let you walk out of here. But Vane is coming, Kael. He won't stop. And once he has you, he'll tear apart everything you are until he understands how you work. Then he'll destroy you, because understanding a Soul Reaver is the only way he knows to neutralize the threat. Your choice is simple: ally with me, or die alone."
Kael looked around the Drowned Hall. The warriors were still watching, weapons ready. The various faction representatives had stopped their conversations, sensing the tension. The black water reflected torchlight and shadow, painting everything in shades of uncertainty.
"I need time to think."
"You have until dawn. After that, Vane will have positioned his agents. It will be harder to move openly." Lyra turned back to her throne. "There's an empty chamber to the east. Stay there tonight. We'll talk again in the morning."
She didn't wait for his response. The warriors closed around him, not threatening but not giving him any choice either, and guided him toward the eastern passage. Kael went along, his mind churning through everything he'd learned.
The Shadow Court was three organizations. The Hollow Crown wanted to break the seals. His family had helped build them and had the knowledge to undo them. Inquisitor Vane was hunting him. The Matriarch had tried to kill him. And somewhere in Thornwick, fragment three of the Sepulcher tablets was hidden, waiting to be found.
Dawn was six hours away.
Six hours to decide whether to trust Lyra, fight Vane, find the tablet, or try to run.
Kael sat in the empty chamber, back against the cold stone wall, and let his mind work. The Soul Reaver stirred in his chest, hungry for more essence, more memories, more power. He could feel the pull of the Abyss beyond the weakening seals, vast and patient, waiting for the moment when the barrier finally gave way.
Somewhere in the darkness of the Underworld, water dripped. Distant footsteps echoed. And Kael waited, knowing that whatever he decided, the world would never be the same.
He needed to move before dawn. But first, he needed information.
The Hollow Crown's chamber was guarded, but guards could be bypassed. Lyra's quarters were nearby, filled with documents and artifacts she probably considered secure. Kael could slip in, harvest some memories from anyone unfortunate enough to be there, and learn what the Hollow Crown was really planning.
Or he could find the tablet himself. Fragment three contained the True Name of at least one Entity—maybe more. If he could read it, he could understand what his ancestors had known. What they had been willing to die to protect.
Or destroy.
Kael rose from the floor. His blade felt heavy at his hip, but his soul felt heavier.
The footsteps in the corridor grew louder. Closer. More than one set.
Kael's hand dropped to his weapon.
The door to the chamber exploded inward.
