The thing wearing Commander Vane's face smiled with teeth that didn't belong to him.
Kael stumbled backward through the rain-slicked cobblestones, his newly Divine senses screaming warnings he couldn't process fast enough. The thing that had been Vane stretched its neck with a series of wet cracks, and beneath the rain-dark uniform, something writhed.
"Two Soul Reavers walking the same path." The voice came from everywhere and nowhere, layered with harmonics that scraped against Kael's consciousness. "The Veil does appreciate symmetry."
The plaza around them had emptied in seconds. Bodies lay scattered where they'd fallen—City Watch soldiers, their throats opened in precise lines, and among them, three figures in the black leather of the Shadow Court. Kael counted six corpses within arm's reach. His fingers itched.
[SYSTEM NOTIFICATION: Soul Harvest Available. Six corpses within harvesting range. Corpse freshness: 2-several minutes.]
He couldn't move. The thing wearing Vane's body had pinned him with something more than physical force—a spiritual pressure that made the air itself feel heavy. Kael's Divinity blazed against it, pushing back, and for a moment he saw truth through the cracks in the illusion.
Vane wasn't inside anymore. Something else had hollowed him out and filled the empty space with Abyssal hunger.
"The Faceless One gave me such a gift." The false Vane took a step forward, and the ground beneath it blackened and cracked. "Your predecessor tried to stop me. Such a waste. But you—you came willingly."
"Predecessor?" Kael's voice came out steadier than he felt. His hands hung at his sides, inches from the nearest body.
"Oh, the Orders buried that truth deep." The thing laughed, a sound like glass breaking underwater. "Soul Reavers are not born, Ashborn. They are made. The first one cut a deal with an Entity. Every Reaver since carries that original sin."
The rain fell harder. Thunder rolled across Thornwick's skyline, and somewhere in the distance, the bell towers of the Cathedral of Dawn began to ring—the warning pattern for Abyssal incursion.
Kael stopped calculating escape routes. The truth of what the thing said resonated with something deep in his soul, something that felt less like memory and more like programming. The Faceless One had touched him. Fragmented itself into his essence. Was this what it meant?
"Your friend Lyra knows." The creature tilted its head at an angle spines shouldn't bend. "She's known since she recruited you. Ask her about the Harvester Protocol. Ask her what the Shadow Court does with broken Reavers."
Lyra. The name cut through his confusion like a blade. She'd stood beside him through execution and escape. She'd bled for him. She couldn't—
"Can't believe that, can you?" The thing smiled wider, and for just a moment, Kael saw what hid beneath—something with too many angles and eyes that reflected light that didn't exist in the Material. "Doubt is such a useful tool. But I'm not here to convince you. I'm here to deliver a message."
The pressure intensified. Kael's legs buckled, but he forced himself upright through sheer will. Divine power flooded his meridians, hot and unfamiliar and terrifying in its scope. He could level this plaza. He could burn the creature from existence.
But something held him back. The fragment of the Faceless One inside him stirred, not with malice but with something that felt almost like recognition.
"The Whisper King sends his regards." The creature wearing Vane raised one hand, and reality folded around it like paper. "Enjoy your divinity, Ashborn. It won't last. Nothing does."
The thing vanished. The pressure lifted. Kael collapsed to his knees on the rain-soaked stones, gasping.
Around him, the dead waited. Six corpses, fresh enough to harvest. His Soul Reaver ability sang in his blood, promising power without cost. He could take everything from them—skills, memories, spiritual essence. He could know what happened here. He could find Lyra and demand answers.
He could become exactly what the thing said he was.
The rain hammered down, washing blood into the gutters. Kael reached for the nearest body—a Shadow Court operative, her eyes still open and accusing—and felt the familiar cold of harvesting surge through his fingertips.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted skill fragment (Shadow Step) from the remains. Memory fragment acquired: Shadow Court protocols, Sector 7 safehouses.]
The notification pulsed in his vision, golden text against the grey world. The skill settled into his consciousness like a puzzle piece finding its slot. He could use Shadow Step now. He could vanish into darkness and reappear twenty feet away.
He reached for the next body. And the next.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted spiritual essence (47 units) from the remains. Memory fragment acquired: patrol routes, Thornwick eastern district.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted skill fragment (Iron Will) from the remains. Memory fragment acquired: incomplete—the operative died mid-transmission.]
Six harvests. Six fragments of truth. The information assembled itself in Kael's mind like a map being drawn by invisible hands. The Shadow Court had been running operations in Thornwick for months, coordinating with something beneath the city. The cultists weren't random worshippers—they were an army waiting for a signal.
And Lyra—Kael's hands stopped over the fifth corpse. The memory fragment he'd pulled from the third operative contained her name. Coordinates. A meeting that had happened three nights ago.
Lyra had met with a figure called the Pale Architect. They'd discussed "the Ashborn contingency." The recording was corrupted, but one phrase survived: *If the Reaver breaks, we harvest what remains.*
Kael stood. The rain plastered his hair to his skull, but he barely felt the cold. Divine power crackled through his veins, and the fragment of the Faceless One pulsed in time with his heartbeat.
He knew where to find answers.
---
The Cathedral of Dawn rose above Thornwick's eastern quarter like a spear thrust into the heavens. Its bells had stopped ringing, replaced by the low hum of wards activating across the district. Kael moved through streets that had emptied at the first warning—citizens retreating to their homes, Awakened mobilizing for potential incursion.
No one stopped him. His Divine presence acted like a cloak, bending the attention of lesser beings away from his passage. The Soul Reaver ability had always been invisible to spiritual senses, but now Kael understood why. He wasn't just harvesting from the dead. He was becoming something that existed between life and death, between the Material and the Abyss.
The question was whether that made him more dangerous or more vulnerable.
He found Lyra in the cathedral's lower sanctum, surrounded by members of the Order of Dawn's inner circle. She stood at a massive table covered in maps and scrying artifacts, her dark hair pulled back in a severe knot. When Kael entered, her eyes met his without surprise.
"Kael." Her voice carried the weight of someone who'd been expecting this confrontation for a long time. "You found the plaza."
"Eight dead." He didn't move from the doorway. "Shadow Court operatives. And Vane—or what used to be Vane."
Lyra's jaw tightened. The other Order members shifted uncomfortably, but no one spoke. Kael's Divine presence filled the room like a held breath.
"The Faceless One's fragment," Lyra said quietly. "It's accelerating. I've seen the projections. If we don't act within the month—"
"I'm not asking about projections." Kael stepped forward, and the temperature in the room dropped ten degrees. "I'm asking about the Harvester Protocol. I'm asking what the Shadow Court does with broken Reavers. I'm asking what *you* know about what I am."
Lyra closed her eyes. When she opened them again, something had changed in her expression—a wall coming down, or maybe going up.
"Everything," she said. "I know everything."
She gestured, and the other Order members filed out without protest. The doors sealed behind them with a sound like distant thunder. Lyra remained at the table, her hands flat on the maps as if anchoring herself.
"You want truth?" She looked at him directly, unflinching. "Soul Reavers are made, not born. The first one—Ironmaw of the Dawn Chorus—made a pact with an Entity during the Abyssal Wars. He traded his afterlife for the ability to harvest souls. Every Reaver since carries a fragment of that original deal."
"That's what the thing in Vane's body told me."
"Then it told you true." Lyra's voice didn't waver. "The Shadow Court was founded to manage Reavers. To use you as weapons and dispose of you when you became... Inconvenient. The Harvester Protocol is what happens when a Reaver refuses to cooperate. We break them. We take back what they stole. And if necessary, we destroy them."
Kael's hands curled into fists. Divine power flared around him, hot enough to make the air shimmer.
"And you?" The word came out like broken glass. "When did you know? When you recruited me? When you watched me die on that platform?"
"I knew before." Lyra's composure cracked, just slightly. "I've known since the first Reaver emerged. I was there, Kael. I watched Ironmaw make his choice. I've watched every Reaver since choose power over conscience, until they had nothing left to lose."
"You're immortal?"
"I'm old enough to remember the world before the Orders unified." She released a breath that seemed to carry decades. "The Shadow Court recruited me because I was useful. I've spent two centuries trying to fix what we created. But Reavers keep appearing. The Faceless One keeps fragments of itself in new hosts. And every time one of you awakens, the seal network weakens a little more."
Kael's anger fought with something else—something that felt dangerously like understanding. Lyra had lived centuries. She'd watched generations of Reavers rise and fall. She'd tried to save them, and they'd all chosen destruction in the end.
"What changed with me?"
Lyra reached into her coat and withdrew a small crystal vial. Inside, something dark writhed against the glass. "This is a sample of your soul-signature. I've been monitoring it since your execution. No Reaver has ever shown your pattern. You're not just harvesting skills and memories—you're preserving them. The fragments you take don't dissipate. They're integrating."
"Integrating into what?"
"I don't know." The admission seemed to cost her. "I've never seen anything like it. The Faceless One put a piece of itself in you, but it's not controlling you. It's... Cooperating. As if you're teaching it something it forgot."
The fragment in Kael's chest pulsed in response. He could feel it now that he knew to look—intelligence that wasn't human, layered beneath his own thoughts like sediment beneath a river. It wasn't hostile. It wasn't friendly. It was curious.
"The Whisper King," Kael said. "The thing that spoke through Vane mentioned him. What's his plan?"
Lyra shook her head. "I've spent two centuries investigating. The Whisper King isn't an Entity, not exactly. He's something that emerged from the spaces between Entities. A consciousness that exists in the Abyss itself. He wants to collapse the Veil permanently—not to destroy humanity, but to merge the Material with the Abyss. He thinks it would be... Better. Cleaner."
"And you?"
"I think it would be annihilation." Lyra met his eyes. "But I'm an old woman with tired convictions. The real question is what *you* think, now that you're Divine. The power to reshape reality sits in your hands. The question is what you'll do with it."
Kael walked to the table and looked down at the maps. Thornwick sprawled beneath them in ink and parchment, its districts marked with symbols he was only beginning to understand. Red X's marked recent cultist activities. Blue circles marked Order strongholds. And in the center of the city, a single black line circled the location of the Great Rift.
"Tell me about the rifts," he said. "All seventy-two of them."
Lyra began to speak. The Orders, the Entities, the seals that had held for centuries. The Whisper King's agents, working through cults and corrupt officials. The Shadow Court's role in monitoring Reavers who might tip the balance. Two hundred years of accumulated knowledge, poured out for an audience of one.
Kael listened. He absorbed. His Divinity gave him the capacity to process information at speeds no human mind could match, and the fragments he'd harvested added perspectives he'd never possessed. The Shadow Court operative's tactical training, the City Watch soldier's knowledge of Thornwick's underground, even the dying echo of a cultist's fanatical conviction—all of it fit together into a framework he could use.
The revelation came an hour into their conversation, when Lyra showed him a diagram of the seal network.
"The seals are connected," she explained, tracing lines of spiritual energy on the parchment. "They're not separate protections but a single system. The Faceless One, the Whisper King, the other Entities—they're not imprisoned by the seals. They're imprisoned by each other. Every seal that weakens affects the others."
"So if enough seals break..."
"The system collapses. Not gradually. All at once."
Kael stared at the diagram. The black line around the Great Rift made terrible sense. They weren't trying to break one seal. They were trying to destabilize the entire network.
"The Faceless One put a fragment in me," he said slowly. "You're saying it's connected to the seal?"
"I think the Faceless One *is* a seal. Or was, once. One of the original anchors that held the system together." Lyra's voice dropped. "Something went wrong. The Entity fractured, and its pieces started finding hosts. Reavers. Anyone spiritually sensitive enough to carry them."
"And now there are fragments in three people."
"Three that we know of." Lyra's expression tightened. "Including you."
The implications settled over Kael like a shroud. The Faceless One wasn't trying to escape or destroy. It was trying to reassemble. And if it succeeded—if all its fragments came together—it might become whole enough to break free entirely.
Or it might become whole enough to repair the seal network permanently.
The fragment in his chest pulsed with something that felt like anticipation.
---
They worked through the night. Lyra showed him Order archives she'd never shared with anyone, documents dating back to the Abyssal Wars. Kael absorbed them all, his Divine mind cataloging details faster than he could consciously process. By dawn, he understood the scope of what faced them.
The Whisper King's network stretched across the continent. Cultists in every major city. Corrupt officials in half the Order hierarchies. A slow-motion coup that had been building for two centuries, waiting for the right moment to strike.
But the Orders weren't blind. Intelligence reports from the Iron Vanguard, the Arcane Spire, even the reclusive Celestial Observatory—all of them had noticed the patterns. Disinformation campaigns masked the true scope of the threat. Counter-operations targeted cult infrastructure. A war was being fought in shadows, and the public saw nothing.
Kael fit into this picture in ways he was only beginning to understand. As a Soul Reaver, he could harvest information directly from cultists and Entities. As a Divine being, he could stand against threats that would destroy lesser Awakened. And as a fragment-bearer, he carried the key to either saving the world or ending it.
Lyra laid out three options as the sun rose over Thornwick's spires.
"First, we contain you. The Shadow Court has facilities designed for Reavers who've lost control. You'd be imprisoned, monitored, and ultimately harvested for research. Your abilities would survive. You wouldn't."
"No."
"Second, you join the Orders openly. We declare you an asset than a threat. You'd work under our direction, harvesting enemies and providing intelligence. But you'd still be a weapon. Someone else's weapon."
Kael shook his head. "And the third option?"
Lyra hesitated. For the first time since he'd known her, she looked uncertain.
"The third option is that you become something new. Not a Reaver. Not an Order operative. Not an Entity-host. Something that transcends the categories we've built." She met his eyes. "The Faceless One fragments in you are cooperating with your soul-signature. If you could learn to merge with them than fight them—or contain them—you might be able to access abilities no one has ever possessed."
"Or I might lose myself entirely."
"Yes."
Kael stood. His body ached from hours of tension, but his mind felt clearer than it had since awakening in his own grave. He knew what the right choice was. He'd always known.
"I'm not joining the Orders. I'm not becoming a weapon." He walked to the window and looked out over the city he'd been executed in, the city that had tried to kill him, the city he might now have to save. "But I'm not running either. The cultists want to break the seals. The Whisper King wants to merge the realms. The Shadow Court wants to control or destroy anyone who threatens their power structure."
He turned to face Lyra.
"None of those options save the world. So I'm going to find a fourth one."
"And what is that?"
"I don't know yet." Kael's mouth curved into something that wasn't a smile. "But I have seventy-two dead gods to negotiate with and a fragment of something ancient growing in my chest. I think I'll figure it out."
Lyra stared at him for a long moment. Then, for the first time since he'd known her, she laughed—a genuine sound, tired but warm.
"You sound like Ironmaw," she said. "Before everything went wrong."
"Good." Kael moved toward the door. "Because I'm going to finish what he started."
He stepped into the dawn light of Thornwick, where the gaslit streets were beginning to fill with morning traffic and the scent of fresh bread from bakeries that had no idea how close they'd come to annihilation. His Divinity felt different now—less like borrowed power and more like something that belonged to him.
The fragment of the Faceless One stirred, and for just a moment, Kael heard a voice that wasn't his own:
*The seals remember what was broken. Maybe you can teach them to forget.*
He pushed the voice aside and focused on the present. He needed to train. He needed to understand his abilities. He needed to find the other two fragment-bearers and determine whether they were allies or enemies.
But first, he needed answers about the Soul Reaver lineage. The thing in Vane's body had implied that Reavers were made through a pact. If that was true, then somewhere in the world was the original bargain—the source of every ability Kael possessed.
And if he could find it, he might finally understand what he was becoming.
Kael walked into the crowded streets, invisibility falling around him like a cloak. Behind him, Lyra watched from the cathedral window, her ancient eyes filled with something that might have been hope or might have been fear.
In the eastern district, a patrol of Shadow Court operatives moved through their morning routes. In the harbor, cultist ships prepared to dock with cargo that would feed the coming war. In the depths beneath the city, something vast and patient waited for the seals to finally shatter.
And somewhere in Thornwick's endless maze of alleys and secrets, two other fragment-bearers went about their lives, unaware that the third had just begun to awaken.
The Reckoning was coming.
Kael could feel it in his bones—Divine power thrumming beneath his skin, the accumulated weight of seventy-two broken seals and two centuries of secrets. He didn't know if he could stop it. He didn't know if he should.
But he knew one thing with absolute certainty:
The dead would speak today.
A carriage rolled past him on the cobblestones, its wheels rattling against uneven stone. Inside, through the darkened windows, Kael caught a glimpse of pale features and hollow eyes. The figure inside turned, as if sensing his gaze.
The carriage stopped.
The door opened.
And Kael found himself looking at the third fragment-bearer for the first time since his execution.
"Get in," the figure said. The voice was feminine, cold, and carried harmonics that matched the fragment in Kael's chest. "We need to talk about what happens next."
The carriage waited. The city moved around them. And in the depths of his being, the Faceless One pulsed with something that felt like recognition.
Kael stepped forward into whatever came next.
