Sera's blade pressed against Kael's throat, biting cold through the collar of his coat. Steel kissed skin, and he tasted copper at the back of his tongue — the metallic tang of adrenaline flooding his system. Blood dripped from her split lip where someone had struck her, each drop catching the faint light before spattering against the cobblestones. Her eyes held the desperate wildness of a cornered animal, pupils blown wide, the whites threaded with crimson. "You have to trust me," she hissed. "The Shadow Court knows everything. They're coming for you tonight."
Above them, glass shattered. Two figures crashed through the ceiling of the alleyway, their robes catching fire from the alchemical lamps they'd shattered on descent. One landed badly, spine twisting at an angle that meant he'd never walk again — the crack of vertebrae echoing off brick, followed by a wet gurgle as the man tried to breathe around a collapsed lung. The other rolled to his feet, shadows already gathering around his fingers, coiling like smoke drawn to a flame.
Kael had three heartbeats to decide. Trust the woman who'd been feeding him information for months, or assume this was the elaborate trap his instincts screamed it might be. His jaw clenched. His knuckles whitened around the edge of his coat.
He chose to grab her wrist and run.
They burst from the alley into Thornwick's steam-choked streets, the gaslights casting orange pools across wet cobblestones. The fog clung to their ankles, thick and acrid, carrying the taste of coal and sulfur. Somewhere behind them, the cultist's shadow tendrils scraped against brick, the sound of a blade drawn across stone. Sera pulled him left, through a side passage so narrow their shoulders scraped both walls, leaving smears of grime and rust on their coats.
"My contact is dead," she gasped as they ran. "I have the information they died to protect, and now they know I have it. We have minutes before the entire cell converges on this district."
"And you came to me." Kael's boots splashed through a puddle of something darker than rainwater. The splash echoed too loud. "Why not take it to your handlers in the Order of Dawn?"
"Because my handler is the one who sold me out."
She stopped so abruptly that Kael nearly collided with her. They stood in a courtyard between two crumbling tenements, the space choked with dead ivy and the rusting skeletons of old machinery. A broken water pump dripped in the corner, each drop counting seconds. Sera reached into her coat and produced a leather tube, sealed with wax that bore no sigil.
"This is everything," she said. "Names, locations, the ritual structure they've been building around the rifts. It's not random, Kael. Nothing about this has been random."
The notification pulsed behind his eyes, and with it came a flood of images — not his own, but borrowed from the dying cultist whose corpse he'd brushed against during their escape. Stone corridors. Candles arranged in geometric patterns. Seventy-two points of light connected by threads of blood. The memory carried the sensation of cold stone beneath bare knees, the taste of copper on the tongue, the whispered syllables of a language that predated human speech.
Kael froze mid-reach for the tube. His fingers hovered, trembling. "Alpha-7," he whispered.
Sera's face went pale. "How did you—"
"I touched him when we ran. His memories are in my head now." Kael gripped her arm, tight enough to bruise. His nails dug crescents into her sleeve. "Tell me everything. Now. Before someone finds us."
She looked at the hand on her arm, then back at his face, and whatever she saw there made her decision.
"The cult isn't splintered," Sera said. "Thornwick's cells report to a single coordinator. The Shadow Court has known about this for months — they've been feeding the cult intelligence, funneling resources, ensuring the ritual sites stay ahead of the Orders' investigations. Someone inside one of the Six is orchestrating the unsealing of all seventy-two rifts."
The courtyard felt smaller, the walls pressing in. Kael's pulse hammered against his ribs. Seventy-two rifts meant seventy-two Entities breaking free, tearing the Veil apart, flooding the Material with Abyssal energy until nothing recognizable remained. His stomach knotted. His throat constricted.
"Who?" The word came out harder than he intended.
"I don't know. The coordinator uses proxy names, shifts locations constantly. But I know where they're meeting next." She broke the seal on the tube, pulled out a sheaf of papers covered in cramped handwriting. Ink smudged her fingertips. "Three days from now. The Ossuary beneath St. Varen's Cathedral. They'll have at least a dozen Awakened there, probably more."
Kael scanned the papers. Names he didn't recognize, addresses he didn't know, dates that meant nothing without context. But the ritual diagrams pulled at something in his mind, echoes of the memories he'd harvested. Lines and circles that matched the candle arrangements from the dead cultist's final moments.
"The Shadow Court," he said slowly. "They're monitoring me specifically. Why?"
Sera's laugh was bitter, cracked. "Because you're a Soul Reaver. You can do something none of us can — harvest information directly from the dead. If the cult has any members who've died during their operations, any corpses left behind at ritual sites, you could extract the truth that would unravel their entire network."
"And you're telling me this because..."
"Because my sister is in a Mortician's Guild facility three streets from here." Sera's voice broke on the word. "They took her six months ago when she tried to expose what they were doing with the harvested souls. She's alive, Kael. She's alive and they're using her for experiments, and the only way I'm getting her out is with information valuable enough to trade."
The pieces clicked together. Sera's appearance, her desperate need for his help, the terrified honesty in her eyes. A desperate woman gambling everything on the one person who could give her what she needed.
"You're not Order of Dawn," Kael said.
"I was. I'm not anymore. They burned my credentials when I started asking questions about where our soul-essence purchases were really coming from." She tucked the papers back into the tube, handed it to him. "Take this. Memorize what you can. If I don't make it to the rendezvous point by dawn, take it to someone you trust."
"I'm not leaving you."
"You might have to." Sera drew a second blade, smaller than the first, and pressed it into his palm. "This isn't about us anymore. If the coordinator succeeds, if even one more rift unseals beyond what they've already done, the seal network starts cascading. The Abyss bleeds through at seventy-two points simultaneously. That's not a war. That's an extinction event."
Kael turned the blade over in his hand. The edge gleamed with an oily iridescence — poison, or something worse. The metal was cold, unnaturally so. Outside the courtyard, a whistle shrieked. The cultist signal, calling others to converge.
"They've found our trail," Sera said. "There's a passage through the old coal chutes that comes out near the river. Take it."
"Not without you."
She smiled then, and it was the saddest expression he'd ever seen on a living face. "I have to go get my sister. If I run now, they'll know I talked. They'll move her somewhere I'll never find her." She stepped back, positioning herself between Kael and the courtyard's entrance. "You want to help me? Be at St. Varen's in three days. Be ready for a fight."
The courtyard's far door exploded inward.
Three cultists poured through, their movements too fluid, their eyes reflecting light with an amber tint that meant Entity-touched blood. Behind them came a fourth figure, hooded and unhurried, who raised one hand and spoke a word of power that drove shards of ice through the air.
Kael tackled Sera sideways as a spear of frozen air carved through the space where they'd stood. They hit the cobblestones hard, the impact driving breath from Kael's lungs. Cold air rushed in to replace it, sharp and biting. The second cultist was already lunging, a curved blade sweeping toward his neck.
Kael caught the wrist, redirected the strike, and drove his elbow into the man's throat. Cartilage crunched. The cultist crumpled, gagging.
The third cultist came high, trying to vault over Kael's guard. Sera's poisoned blade caught him in the stomach, and he arched backward with a strangled cry before hitting the ground. She was already moving to the fourth, her empty hand weaving patterns that left afterimages in the gaslight.
"Hold," the hooded figure commanded. The word carried weight, spiritual pressure that pressed against Kael's skull like a vice. "The Reaver is not to be killed. The woman is irrelevant."
"Flattering," Kael growled, and threw the small blade.
It wasn't meant to kill — he hadn't loaded it with his own energy, couldn't afford to deplete himself before the real fight. But it didn't need to kill. It only needed to buy a heartbeat of distraction.
Kael surged forward, closing the distance before the cultist could complete whatever defense he'd been preparing. His hands found the man's robes, found the flesh beneath, and he pushed.
The harvest was nothing like extracting skills from the dead. This was violent, invasive, pulling something from a living soul while it still burned. The cultist screamed — a sound that shattered windows and drew shouts from the street beyond — and Kael felt the man's memories tear loose like pages ripped from a binding. The taste of stolen life flooded his mouth, copper and ash and something else beneath, something that tasted of ozone and rot.
The hooded figure collapsed, body going rigid as Kael rifled through stolen thoughts. Names. Faces. A meeting schedule that matched what Sera had shown him. And underneath it all, a thread leading back to someone called the Whisper King.
Kael released the body. The cultist's eyes had gone white, and blood leaked from his ears and nose. He wouldn't be leading anyone anymore.
Sera finished the remaining two cultists while Kael steadied himself against a wall. His hands were shaking — not from the spiritual expenditure, but from the wrongness of taking from the living. The technique had worked, but the taste of it sat in his mouth, coating his tongue.
"Living harvest," Sera said, staring at the corpse Kael had dropped. "I've read about it. I didn't think anyone could do it."
"I can't." Kael wiped his palms on his coat. The fabric came away damp. "I mean, I did, but it costs something. Not energy. Something else."
They had minutes at best before more cultists arrived. Kael pocketed the papers Sera had given him and checked his gear. Two knives remaining, three essence vials he'd taken from earlier harvests, and a head full of stolen memories that might or might not prove useful.
"St. Varen's," he said. "Three days. I need to know where they're keeping your sister before then."
"There's a facility beneath the Mortician's Guild's main hall. Sub-level three, if the documents I stole are accurate." Sera was already moving toward the coal chutes, gesturing for him to follow. "I have blueprints. Entry points. Guard rotations. But I can't get in alone."
"You need me to open the path."
"I need you to harvest the warden." She ducked into the passage, her voice echoing in the darkness. "His soul holds everything — access codes, override sequences, the location of every prisoner they've moved in the last year. If you can get that from him, my sister walks out of there alive."
The coal chute yawned before them, a throat of ancient stone that smelled of damp and forgotten years. Kael followed Sera into the dark, his eyes adjusting to the blackness while his mind worked through the implications.
A trusted ally with a secret. The secret wasn't that Sera was using him — that much he'd guessed the moment she appeared with blood on her face. The secret was that she'd been compromised from the start, feeding information to the Shadow Court while simultaneously feeding the Shadow Court's secrets to him. A double agent playing both sides, and now forced to choose.
"You're taking a big risk," Kael said. "Trusting me with this."
"I'm taking the only risk that matters." Sera's footsteps echoed ahead. "If my sister dies because I played it safe, nothing else I do with my life means anything."
They emerged later on the banks of the Thorn, the river churning black and silver under a sky full of stars obscured by steam. The water's roar filled the silence between them, constant and indifferent. Thornwick's industrial district rose on the far shore, its smokestacks coughing fire into the night. The glow painted the clouds orange and amber. Somewhere in that maze of iron and coal, the cult was regrouping. Somewhere, the Whisper King was learning that his operative had been harvested.
"You're a wanted man now," Sera said. "They'll put your name on the kill list right next to mine."
"I'm already on it. You just made it more official." Kael stretched cramped muscles, felt joints pop. "Tomorrow night. Mortician's Guild. Walk me through the entry."
They spent the next hours planning, huddled in the shadow of a bridge piling while barges passed overhead. The stone beneath them leached cold through their clothes. Sera's information was detailed, precise — patrol times, blind spots in the warden's vision, the weight and balance of every door between the surface and sub-level three.
"The warden is Ascendant stage," she said. "Third level, almost fourth. He'll sense you coming unless you're very, very careful."
"I don't need to get close. I need to get him alone." Kael's mind was already cycling through possibilities. "If I create a distraction on the upper levels, draw the other guards away..."
"He'll be the last line. He won't leave his post." Sera shook her head. "You need to get to him directly. No distractions, no tricks. Just you and him and whatever it takes."
The weight of it settled over Kael. An Ascendant-stage Awakened, fully alert and expecting trouble. And he needed to touch that man, reach into his soul, and extract the information that would free Sera's sister and potentially expose the entire conspiracy.
"One more thing," Sera said. Her voice had gone quiet, the planning tone giving way to something rawer. "The Soul Reaver who started all this — the first one, the one the tablets talk about — my sister found something about him before they took her. Something about where he went."
Kael's pulse quickened. "What kind of something?"
"She wouldn't say. She was scared, Kael. Not of the Morticians, not of the cult. Scared of what she learned about the original." Sera met his eyes, and in the starlight he could see the fear she'd been hiding. "Whatever the first Soul Reaver became, whatever happened to him after he made the seals — it's connected to this. To all of it. And if the cult is trying to unseal the rifts, they might be trying to undo what he did."
The river churned below them, black water catching fragments of reflected light. Somewhere in the city, cultists were hunting. Somewhere in the shadows, the Shadow Court was watching. And somewhere beneath the streets, in a cell that smelled of death and disinfection, a woman waited who held answers to questions Kael hadn't known to ask.
"Three days," he said. "We get your sister first. Then we stop the ritual. Then we find out what the original Soul Reaver really did."
Sera nodded, but her expression didn't lighten. "And if finding out changes everything you think you know about yourself?"
Kael had no answer for that. He had the weight of the blade she'd given him, the stolen memories burning behind his eyes, and the certainty that the next three days would reveal secrets that could never be put back.
"Then I'll deal with it," he said. "The same way I deal with everything else."
They parted at the bridge's far end, Sera heading north toward a safe house Kael didn't know the location of, and Kael south toward the undercity where his own refuges waited. The streets were quiet, the hour late enough that even Thornwick's perpetual motion had slowed to a crawl. Fog curled between buildings, swallowing sound.
But sleep wouldn't come tonight. Kael knew that already. His mind was too full of hooded figures and amber eyes, of ice-speak and living harvests, of a conspiracy reaching into every corner of the world he'd thought he understood.
The first Soul Reaver. The seventy-two seals. The Whisper King pulling strings from the shadows.
And Sera — desperate, double-dealing, utterly human Sera — who had put her sister's life in his hands and trusted him not to break it.
Kael walked until he found an abandoned warehouse with a view of the sky, settled into a corner where the shadows were deepest, and began to memorize the papers he'd taken. Names. Locations. Ritual structures that matched the fragments from his harvest. The ink smudged under his thumbs. The paper smelled of old leather and fear.
Three days until St. Varen's. Three days until he faced whatever waited in the Ossuary.
But first, the Mortician's Guild. First, the warden. First, a harvest that would either give him everything he needed or kill him trying.
The night stretched on. The city breathed steam and smoke. And in the darkness, Kael Ashborn planned an infiltration that would either save a woman's life or damn them both.
He never noticed the figure watching from the rooftop across the street, nor the second pair of eyes in the gutter drain below. The Shadow Court's surveillance had been upgraded while Sera thought herself invisible, and every move Kael made from this moment forward would be reported to handlers whose loyalties remained in question.
The game had changed. The pieces were moving. And the board itself was beginning to crack.
Kael closed his eyes and let exhaustion take him, not knowing that when dawn broke over Thornwick, his world would be smaller by two allies and larger by one impossible truth.
