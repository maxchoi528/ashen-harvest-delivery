Zarek screamed.

[Soul Harvest: You extracted a fragment of memory from Zarek's remains. Skill acquired: Shadow Veil (Minor).]
But this wasn't a corpse. Zarek was still alive, still struggling, still fighting. The harvest was pulling from his life force directly—painful, damaging, but not lethal. Not unless Kael pushed harder.

"Interesting," Kael said, examining his own hand. The skin had blistered where Zarek's fire had touched him, but it was already healing. The Divine stage accelerated regeneration beyond anything he'd experienced before. "You're connected to the Faceless One. The entity touched your soul directly, didn't it? That's why you're so much stronger than your stage suggests."

The rain intensified, drumming against the cobblestones like impatient fingers. Somewhere in the distance, a bell tolled the hour.

Understanding.
"You think you're winning," Zarek said softly. "You think the Faceless One is my master. But you've got it backwards, Kael. I'm not its servant. I'm its cage. The rituals, the cult attacks, the weakening seals—it's all containment. Delaying the inevitable."

"In seventy-two days, the bandages come off. The Entities return. And there's nothing you or anyone else can do to stop it."
"Why tell me this?"
"Because I want you to understand what's coming. Because watching you struggle against the inevitable is the sweetest revenge for what you did to me." Zarek gestured to his guards. They withdrew, melting into the shadows like they'd never been there. "Enjoy your borrowed time, Soul Reaver. We'll meet again when the world burns."
He walked away, disappearing into the rain.
Kael stood alone in the alley, rain streaming down his face, his shoulder still smoking from the wound. The Faceless One stirred in the back of his mind, a cold presence that never went away.
*He lies*, the Faceless One whispered. *Not lies. Incomplete truth. The cycle exists, but the outcome can change. You could change it.*
"Could I?" Kael asked quietly.
*You have what no other being has. The ability to harvest from the divine. The power of the Reaver, unchained. The Entities are just souls wearing different masks.*
"You're one of them. One of the 72."
*I was. Now I'm something else. A fragment in your shadow, waiting.*
The connection between them pulsed—uncomfortable, intimate, invasive. Kael had carried the Faceless One's fragment since the second rift opened, since the entity had tried to use him as a conduit. He'd never managed to remove it, and attempts to harvest it had failed.
It was part of him now. A passenger he couldn't evict.
He turned away from the alley and began walking toward the eastern district. The wound on his shoulder had already closed, new skin pink and tender. At Divine stage, his body rebuilt itself faster than conventional weapons could damage it.
But the Soul Reaver was more than physical regeneration. It was the ability to take, to consume, to grow stronger from the fallen. The Orders called it an abomination. The world called it evil.
Kael called it survival.

The basement of the abandoned tannery reeked of old hide and stagnant water. Kael lit a single candle and set it on a crate, casting long shadows across the walls. He'd used this safehouse before—a forgotten corner of Thornwick where the gas company had yet to extend their pipes.
Lyra was waiting for him, sitting on a pile of moldy leather with her arms crossed. Her grey cloak was soaked through, her dark hair plastered to her face. She looked exhausted.
"You're hurt," she said, noting the burn on his shoulder.
"It'll heal." Kael sat across from her, positioning the candle between them. "What did you find?"
"The Shadow Court has people inside the Mortician's Guild. At least three handlers coordinating the attacks on harvesting sites." Lyra pulled a folded paper from her cloak and slid it across the crate. "Names, locations, schedules. Everything."
Kael unfolded the paper, scanning the contents. His memory had always been sharp, but the Soul Reaver enhanced that too—every detail locked into place like a photograph.
"Why?" he asked. "Why risk exposure inside the Guild? The Shadow Court has operated covertly for decades."
"The Guild controls legal soul-essence distribution. If they can compromise the supply chain, they control the market." Lyra leaned forward, her eyes intent. "Think about it. An Awakening ritual requires soul-essence. A breakthrough requires more. If the Guild can't provide, the Orders have to turn to... Alternative sources."
"The Veiled Market."
"And who controls the Veiled Market?" Lyra's smile was bitter. "The Shadow Court. They've been building a monopoly on spiritual power for thirty years. The cult attacks aren't random violence—they're strategic sabotage."
Kael set the paper down. The implications were staggering. If the Shadow Court controlled soul-essence distribution, they controlled who could Awaken, who could advance, who could threaten them.
"This goes deeper than the Faceless One," he said. "This is a coup."
"The Orders are already fractured. The Concordat is dying. If the soul-essence supply collapses at the same time the seals fail..." Lyra shook her head. "It wouldn't take a military conquest. They'd absorb the pieces as they crumble."
"And Zarek knows this."
"Zarek *orchestrates* this." Lyra's voice dropped. "He's been playing all sides for years. The Shadow Court, the cultists, even the Entities themselves. He thinks he can ride out the Reckoning and emerge as king of the ashes."
Kael thought about the conversation in the alley. Zarek had wanted him to know. Wanted him to understand the scope of what he was fighting. That wasn't arrogance—that was strategy. A final cruelty, delivered to a man Zarek believed was already defeated.
But Kael wasn't defeated. Not yet.
"The cult activity in the southern district," he said. "It's connected to this?"
"The rift there is one of the first seals scheduled to break. They're not just weakening it—they're preparing it. Ritual sites, supply caches, escape routes. Whatever's coming through that rift, they want to be ready to greet it."
"Or control it."
"Same thing, with cultists." Lyra reached into her cloak and produced a second item—a small glass vial containing something that shifted like liquid shadow. "I found this in one of the handler's rooms. Recognized it immediately."
Kael took the vial, holding it up to the candlelight. The substance inside recoiled from the illumination, pressing against the glass.
"Entity essence," he said. "Pristine quality. This is worth—"
"Hundreds of vials of standard soul-essence. More, on the black market." Lyra watched his face carefully. "It came from a demigod-class Entity. Someone harvested it recently and passed it up the chain."
A cold certainty settled in Kael's chest. "Zarek."
"Probably. The timing matches his recent movements. He visited the southern district three times in the last month, always alone, always at night."
The Soul Reaver pulsed in recognition. Entity essence was the purest form of spiritual energy available—capable of jumpstarting Awakening, accelerating breakthrough, even repairing soul damage. If Zarek had access to that kind of power...
"How do you know so much?" Kael asked. "The Shadow Court's internal structure, their supply networks, their plans—this isn't reconnaissance. This is intelligence that takes years to develop."
Lyra was quiet for a long moment. The candle flickered, shadows dancing across her face.
"I was Shadow Court," she finally said. "Before they exiled me. Before I met you."
Kael had suspected, of course. Lyra's knowledge of dark markets, her comfort with violence, the way she moved through Thornwick's underworld like she owned it. But suspicion wasn't confirmation.
"When?"
"Fifteen years. I was their best infiltrator. Their fixer for the eastern territories." Lyra met his eyes, unflinching. "I did things I'm not proud of. Things I can't undo. When I found out what they really were—what they were building toward—I tried to stop it from inside. Failed. They branded me traitor and left me for dead."
"Except you didn't die."
"No. I got better. And I've spent every day since then trying to undo what I helped create." She gestured at the papers and the vial. "This is what that looks like. One piece at a time. One name at a time. One step at a time."
Kael turned the vial over in his hands. Entity essence. Shadow Court operations. Cultist preparations for the Reckoning. It all fit together, a tapestry of conspiracy woven across decades.
"The Six Orders," he said slowly. "Do they know?"
"Most of them suspect. The Order of Dawn has been investigating the Shadow Court for years, but they can't prove anything. The Iron Vanguard is compromised—too many veterans with ties to black-market soul-essence. The others are too busy fighting each other to notice the real threat."

"A rubber stamp. A relic of a treaty that died fifty years ago." Lyra's voice was flat, devoid of emotion. "The only thing holding the Orders together is habit and fear. The moment something bigger than their petty disputes appears, they'll tear each other apart."
She wasn't wrong. Kael had seen it himself—the way the Orders circled each other like wounded animals, too paranoid to cooperate even when cooperation meant survival.
"I need to get to the southern district," he said. "The cult's ritual site. If I can disrupt their preparations—"

"I'm a Soul Reaver." Kael's hand closed around the vial. "One man is all I need to be."
The Faceless One stirred, amused. *Bold. Foolish. But bold.*

The southern district had been dying for decades. Factories closed, workers fled, buildings crumbled into haunted husks. The gas company had stopped maintaining the streetlamps years ago, leaving pools of darkness between islands of failing light.
Kael moved through that darkness like a predator, his senses extended beyond normal perception. At Divine stage, he could feel the spiritual pressure of every Awakened within a hundred meters—a constant background hum of power that most people couldn't detect.
Three distinct signatures ahead. Strong ones. Sovereign-class, just as Lyra had said.
He stopped at the edge of a ruined factory yard, counting the guards. Four at the main entrance. Six more patrolling the perimeter. Dozens of smaller presences inside—cultists, probably, or prisoners awaiting sacrifice.
The ritual site was in the basement. He could feel it, a wrongness in the Veil that made his teeth ache.
*Careful*, the Faceless One warned. *The seal here is already damaged. If you disrupt the ritual incorrectly, you could trigger a cascade failure. The rift would open prematurely, and everything within five miles would be consumed by the Abyss.*
"Then I do it correctly."
*You don't know how.*
"I'll learn."
He approached the factory from the east, using the rubble of a collapsed wall for cover. Two guards stood watch there, their attention focused on the main entrance. They never saw him coming.
The first died silently, Kael's hand clamped over his mouth while the Soul Reaver did its work. The harvest notification appeared:
[Soul Harvest: You extracted combat memory from the remains. Skill acquired: Pressure Point Strike (Minor).]
A basic technique, nothing special, but it gave him insight into how these cultists fought. Pressure points, incapacitation strikes, the kind of dirty fighting that didn't rely on raw power.
The second guard was harder. She spotted him at the last moment, mouth opening to shout. Kael threw the first corpse at her, using the body as a weapon. She staggered back, and he was on her before she could recover.
This harvest was cleaner. Muscle memory, specifically—years of training encoded in her nervous system. The notification:
[Soul Harvest: You extracted combat experience from the remains. Technique proficiency increased: Unarmed Combat (Intermediate).]
He dragged both bodies into the shadows and continued toward the factory entrance. The guards there hadn't noticed anything yet. That would change the moment he breached the building.
Instead of the front door, he found a side entrance—a rusted loading dock half-hidden by overgrown weeds. The lock had been cut recently, the metal still bright where the bolt cutters had bitten.
Someone else had been here. Recently.
Kael dropped into the loading dock and moved through the factory's guts—abandoned machinery, rotting conveyor belts, the skeleton of an industry that had died before he was born. His footsteps echoed despite his care, bouncing off the concrete and corrugated steel.
The basement stairs were at the back of the building, behind a makeshift barrier of wooden pallets and rusted barrels. A single candle burned at the bottom, casting a dim glow up the stairwell.
He descended.
The basement had been transformed. Symbols covered every surface—walls, floor, ceiling—painted in something that looked like blood but smelled like copper and ozone. Candles arranged in precise geometric patterns marked the corners of a central circle, and at the circle's heart...
A woman hung from chains, her body marked with the same symbols that covered the walls. She was still alive, barely—her chest rose and fell in shallow, agonized breaths. A spirit-sensitive, probably. The kind of person whose soul could amplify ritual power.
"Terrible decor choices," Kael muttered.
He stepped into the circle.
The symbols flared, crimson light erupting along their paths. Kael felt the trap spring around him—a binding formation, designed to hold Awakened in place while the ritual harvested their spiritual energy.
He laughed.
The Soul Reaver didn't just consume souls. It consumed *power*. Binding formations were just structured spiritual energy, and spiritual energy was food.
He reached out, touching the nearest symbol, and began to eat.
The formation screamed—a sound that existed somewhere between light and feeling—as it unraveled under his assault. The candles blew out. The basement plunged into darkness.
Then the three Sovereign-class cultivators appeared at the top of the stairs, their spiritual pressure flaring like beacons.
"Kill him," one of them ordered. "The ritual must not be interrupted."
They descended.
Kael met them in the center of the broken circle, positioning himself between the cultists and the chained woman. The Soul Reaver sang in his blood, eager for the harvest ahead.
"Three Sovereigns," he said. "I was hoping for a challenge."
The first one attacked—a broad-shouldered man wielding spiritual energy like a blade. The strike came fast, faster than Kael could dodge, but he didn't need to dodge. He caught the blade on his palm, the energy slicing into his flesh, and *pulled*.
The cultivator screamed as his power began to drain. Kael absorbed the spiritual energy directly, using it to regenerate the wound on his hand while simultaneously building toward something else.
The second cultivator tried to flank him. Kael sidestepped, using the first as a shield, and drove his elbow into the man's throat. Cartilage crunched. The cultivator stumbled back, choking.
The third hung back, watching, calculating. A mage, probably. The dangerous kind—the ones who let others take the hits while they prepared something devastating.
Kael finished draining the first cultivator. The notification appeared:
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 847 units of spiritual energy from the remains. Skill acquired: Iron Skin (Minor).]
The man collapsed, alive but emptied. He'd survive, but he'd never Awaken again. The Soul Reaver had taken everything.
The second lunged at Kael, rage overriding caution. Kael caught him by the face and planted him into the ground, then knelt on his chest while the Soul Reaver extracted his essence.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 1,203 units of spiritual energy from the remains. Memory acquired: Cultist Protocols.]
Cultist Protocols. Now he knew their organizational structure, their signal systems, their emergency procedures. Valuable intelligence, delivered directly into his mind.
The mage finally moved, arms weaving through complex patterns. A binding spell, stronger than the formation—Kael could feel its edges closing around him like invisible chains.
He broke free by sheer force of will, shattering the spell's threads with a burst of absorbed energy. Then he was on the mage, closing the distance before another spell could form.
The mage's eyes went wide. He tried to retreat, but Kael was faster.
This harvest was different. The Soul Reaver touched the man's mind directly, pulling not just energy or skills but *knowledge*. Years of study, decades of practice, entire libraries of magical theory—all of it flooding into Kael's consciousness.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 2,156 units of spiritual energy from the remains. Knowledge acquired: Advanced Ritual Theory (Fragmentary).]

"You can't. The ritual is already complete. The seal is broken."

Just not all the way. Not yet.
"How long?" he asked.
"Days. Maybe hours. The first Entity will emerge soon, and then—" She laughed, a broken sound. "Then the Reckoning truly begins."
He broke the chains with a surge of spiritual energy, letting her drop to the floor. She curled into herself, weeping silently.
"Get out of here," he said. "Go to the Order of Dawn. Tell them what you saw."
"They won't listen. No one listens until the sky turns black and the dead start walking."
"Then make them listen." Kael moved toward the stairs. "I have work to do."
The Faceless One stirred, more present than it had been in months. *The rift calls to me*, it whispered. *I can feel it. The seal weakens, and my kin draw near.*
"Your kin," Kael repeated.
*The 72. The Abyssal Entities. We're not demons, not monsters—we're fundamental forces of existence, trapped in a prison that was never meant to hold us forever.* The voice grew urgent, almost pleading. *Let me go. Let me return to the Abyss. In exchange, I'll tell you how to close the rift. Permanently.*
"Deal."
Kael felt the Faceless One's surprise. It hadn't expected acceptance.
*You're... Willing?*
"You're part of me. A splinter I've carried too long." Kael's hand found the stair railing, steadying himself. "But you're right. This rift has to be sealed. And I can't do it while you're eating at my edges."
*The ritual requires a sacrifice. Someone with the power to anchor the Veil in place. Someone whose soul is strong enough to hold reality together.*
"Soul Reaver," Kael said flatly. "You want me to die sealing the rift."
*Not die. Transform. Your soul would become part of the seal itself. You'd exist, but not as you are now. You would be... Distributed. Spread across every rift in the world, holding them closed forever.*
"And you would return to your kin."
*Yes.*
It was a good deal. A generous deal, even. Kael would save thousands of lives, maybe millions, and in exchange he would cease to exist as an individual. The Soul Reaver would become something else—a force of nature, a lock on a door that had been swinging open for centuries.

"No."
*What?*
"I'm not sacrificing myself for a world that wants me dead. I'm not becoming a lock on a door I never built." Kael started up the stairs. "If this rift opens, I'll close it myself. With my own hands. Not by dying—by living. By being stronger than whatever comes through."
*You cannot—*
"Watch me."
He emerged from the factory into a world that had changed while he was below. The sky flickered with colors that didn't belong in nature—shifting purples, bleeding crimsons, the aurora of a reality folding into another. The Veil was thinning, reality itself groaning under the pressure.
Somewhere in the distance, something *screamed*. Not human. Not animal. Something older and stranger, clawing its way into existence.
The Reckoning had begun.
Kael walked toward the sound, his boots crunching on broken glass. Behind him, the factory's basement began to glow with fresh light—the rift within growing stronger, the seal finally giving way.
He had seventy-two days. Seventy-two rifts. Seventy-two chances to stop the end of the world.
Or seventy-two ways to ensure it.
He didn't know which outcome awaited him. The Soul Reaver offered power, but power wasn't answers. The Faceless One offered truth, but truth came with chains. The Orders offered alliance, but alliance meant compromise.
Kael walked alone through the dying district, rain beginning to fall again, his shadow stretching long behind him. The city of Thornwick sprawled before him—gaslit streets, steam-belching factories, a million lives unaware of what approached.
He would not warn them. He would not save them. He would do what he'd always done—take what he needed, grow stronger, and face whatever came.
The first Entity was emerging. He could feel it, a pressure against his senses that made his skin crawl.
Kael smiled, and the Soul Reaver laughed with him.
"Let's see what you're made of," he said to the sky.
Somewhere in the darkness, something answered.
