The corpse twitched beneath Kael's palm.
He jerked back, boot scraping wet cobblestones, and the thing on the ground spasmed again. A severed arm—Cultist, judging by the black robes torn at the shoulder—still gripped a ceremonial dagger in calcified fingers. The fingers flexed once, twice, then went slack.
"Stay down," Kael muttered to the body. The flesh was cold, the death hours old, but residual spiritual energy crackled beneath the skin like static before a storm. Something had kept this one animated after the heart stopped beating.
The alley reeked of brimstone and copper. Two more cultist corpses lay sprawled across the uneven stones—one gutted, one with a crushed skull. Their killer had moved fast and brutal. Steam rose from a burst pipe overhead, obscuring the gaslight's sickly yellow glow. Somewhere in the distance, Thornwick's night watch hammered on a brass bell, the rhythm panicked and irregular.
Kael crouched beside the headless torso of what had once been a large man. The robes identified him as a Void acolyte, one of the lower ranks that performed the grunt work of Abyssal worship—holding candles during rituals, hauling bodies to sacrifice sites, bleeding themselves for blessings that never came. This one had been cut down mid-ritual. A chalk circle surrounded him, half-finished, the symbols smeared where the body had fallen.
Someone had interrupted the ceremony.
His fingers hovered over the cooling flesh. The Soul Reaver hummed in his chest, eager and patient, the hunger that never truly faded drawing him toward the kill. Three corpses meant three chances to learn what the cult had been attempting—and Thornwick's underbelly had no shortage of fresh bodies for those who knew where to look.
The bell stopped.
Silence pressed against his ears, thick and wrong. The steam dissipated slowly, revealing the alley's dead end—a rusted iron door marked with the Mortician's Guild sigil, three interlocking circles representing the body's spiritual, mental, and physical aspects. The Guild used these doors to collect their harvest from the city's poor. Someone had left it ajar.
Kael touched the Void acolyte's chest.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted residual spiritual energy from the remains. 12 essence points acquired.]
[Soul Harvest: Fragment of ritual memory detected. Fragment extracted.]
The extraction felt like swallowing ice water. Information flooded his mind—unbidden, unwelcome, precise. The acolyte had been drawing power from the chalk circle, channeling it toward a specific location. The Great Rift. Kael saw it in fragmented flashes: chains of silver light, a seal cracked down its center, something vast pressing against the membrane between worlds. The acolyte's purpose had been simple. Maintain the circle. Feed the crack.
Someone had silenced him before the feeding finished.
The door creaked.
Kael spun, blade already singing free of its scabbard. The edge caught the gaslight and threw dancing shadows across the alley walls. A figure stood in the doorway, tall and gaunt, draped in the grey robes of a Mortician's Collector. A bone-white mask covered their face, the kind worn by those who dealt with the spiritually inert dead and wanted no contamination from the departed's final expressions.
"You harvest from Guild property." The voice was genderless, muffled by the mask's filtration. "That is theft."
"These cultists attacked me." Kael's lie came automatically. "Self-defense."
"The wounds are consistent with ritual combat." The Collector stepped forward, each footfall deliberate. "But the extraction patterns are not. A standard Awakened cannot pull memories from the dead. The spiritual residue degrades too quickly. Yet you pulled something from that acolyte—I watched the extraction finalize." The masked head tilted. "What are you?"
The question hung in the air like poison.
Kael sheathed his blade. "A survivor. Same as you."
"I do not survive. I collect." The Collector gestured toward the other corpses. "These three were part of a larger group. Seventeen cultists entered the Undercroft beneath the old foundry two hours ago. They carried sealed crates marked with symbols I do not recognize. When they emerged, twelve hours later, they were fewer. The survivors fled in all directions." A gloved hand extended toward the headless torso. "You touched one. What did they carry?"
Information asymmetry. The Collector wanted something, which meant the cult's activity had escalated beyond standard concerns. Seventeen cultists, sealed crates, Undercroft access—this wasn't random harassment. This was organized deployment.
"The rift feeds," Kael said. "The cultists were channeling energy toward it."
"Yes. That much I sensed." The Collector moved closer, close enough that Kael could see the intricate bone carvings along the mask's edges—patterns that matched the Mortician's Guild sigil but older, more elaborate. "The question is what they fed it. The Sealed Crates. What was inside?"
"I didn't see inside. I only harvested from the dead."
"Yet you know about the crates." The Collector's tone hardened. "Harvesting provides fragments. You have pieces. Share them, or I report a Soul Reaver operating in Thornwick's lower districts."
The threat landed exactly where intended. Soul Reavers were hunted by the Orders, and the Mortician's Guild served as the Orders' unofficial eyes in places where authority couldn't reach openly. Being identified meant hunters. Being identified meant questions about every death Kael had touched since his awakening.
The Collector waited.
Kael made his choice. "The acolyte's memories showed chains. Silver light. A crack in something massive." He met the mask's eye holes, seeing nothing but darkness beyond. "A seal. The Great Rift's seal is compromised, and they were feeding it power to widen the breach."
Silence stretched between them.
The Collector's gloved hand rose slowly, removing the bone-white mask. The face beneath was surprisingly young—late twenties, maybe—with silver threading through dark hair at the temples. A scar ran from the left eyebrow to the cheekbone, pale against brown skin. The eyes were the unsettling part: pupils that seemed too large, irises that flickered between black and gold depending on how the light struck them.
"My name is Seren." She folded the mask and tucked it into her robes. "I lead the Mortician's Guild's Hollow Investigation Division. We handle deaths that touch the Abyss—possessions, rifts, Entity incursions." She studied him with those strange eyes. "You just confirmed what our analysts suspected. The Great Rift's seal is deteriorating faster than projected. The cultists are accelerating the degradation deliberately."
"You could have led with that."
"I needed to know what you extracted. A Reaver who harvests from cultists is either stupid or useful. You don't seem stupid." Seren crouched beside the headless acolyte, examining the chalk circle with practiced efficiency. "The symbols aren't standard cult scripture. They're older. Pre-Concordat, if I'm reading them correctly. Someone revived techniques that should have died with the Abyssal Wars."
Kael watched her work, noting the precision of her movements. "You said seventeen entered. Twelve hours of ritual work. What came out?"
"Fourteen, according to my scouts." Seren's fingers traced the chalk lines. "Thirteen fled into the city. One returned to the surface and walked directly to the Council Watchtower. He surrendered himself to Inquisitor Vane."
The name hit Kael like a fist to the sternum.
Vane. Inquisitor. The man hunting him for crimes he hadn't committed, the hunter who never stopped, never tired, never accepted failure. Kael had survived their first encounter through luck and desperation. A second would be less certain.
Seren noticed his reaction. "You know him."
"I've heard the name."
"Everyone's heard the name. But your pulse spiked twelve beats per minute. Your hand moved toward your blade." She stood, brushing chalk dust from her robes. "I'm not going to ask what happened between you and Inquisitor Vane. The Guild stays neutral in Order politics. But if you're operating in the Underworld while Vane is hunting cultists, your paths will cross whether you want them to or not."
The alley felt smaller. The steam, the corpses, the rusted door—all of it pressed inward, suffocating. Kael forced his breathing to steady. Panic served no purpose. Vane was a problem for later.
"The cultist who surrendered," he said. "Why would he turn himself in?"
"That's the question, isn't it?" Seren produced a small notebook, flipping through pages of cramped handwriting. "He was wounded—deep lacerations across his chest, consistent with ritual scarification. When my people examined him, they found something inside the wounds. Sigils carved into the flesh, sealed with what appears to be Abyssal wax. He wasn't surrendering. He was delivering something."
"What kind of something?"
"We don't know yet. Our spiritual analysts are examining the body." She closed the notebook. "What I do know is that fourteen cultists emerged from twelve hours of ritual work, and now one has delivered a package directly to the man hunting you. Whatever the cult is building, it's close to completion."
The pieces clicked together in Kael's mind, forming a picture he didn't like. Organized cult activity. Accelerated seal degradation. A delivery mechanism aimed at Inquisitor Vane, who was simultaneously hunting Kael for crimes Zarek had framed him for. Too many threads converging on the same point.
"I need to see that cultist's body," Kael said.
Seren's eyebrows rose. "You want to harvest from a corpse held by the Mortician's Guild?"
"I want to know what he was carrying. If it's connected to the Great Rift, it's connected to everything."
"Harvesting from Guild property is theft. I explained this."
"The body is evidence. Examining evidence isn't theft."
"Harvesting isn't examination. It's consumption." Seren studied him for a long moment. "Soul Reavers take. That's what makes you terrifying. The Orders have killed dozens of your kind because consumption without consent violates the fundamental law of soul conservation. You're not just taking power from that cultist—you're removing energy from the world's spiritual pool permanently."
"The world's spiritual pool is bleeding into the Abyss through a cracked seal. I'm redistributing it."
"Redistributing to yourself."
Kael met her gaze without flinching. "I'm the only one who can read what cultists carry. The memories degrade for anyone else. You said it yourself—standard Awakened can't pull fragments from the dead. Your analysts will get fragments, maybe. I get entire pages."
"You get entire pages by consuming what's left."
"Yes."
The admission hung between them, ugly and honest. Seren turned away, pacing the length of the alley, her boots crunching on debris. The steam continued to rise from the burst pipe, painting ghostly shapes in the gaslight. When she spoke again, her voice was quieter.
"The Shadow Court has been active in Thornwick's lower districts. My people have identified three separate cells coordinating their movements. They're not working with the cult—their sigils don't match—but they're watching the same locations. Following the same targets." She stopped pacing. "A week ago, they started watching you."
Kael's blood chilled. "Watching me how?"
"Scribes posted at your boarding house. Shadows that don't cast properly in alleyways. Messages left in places only someone with training would recognize." Seren faced him again. "The Shadow Court doesn't for pleasure. They monitor threats and assets. You're one of them now."
The Shadow Court. Lyra's former masters. The deniable operations arm that multiple Orders used to wage wars they couldn't acknowledge. If they were watching him, it meant someone with authority had decided he was worth tracking. The question was whether they wanted to control him or eliminate him.
"Does the Shadow Court know what I am?" Kael asked.
"A Soul Reaver? Almost certainly. They have sources inside the Orders—everyone does. If they don't know directly, they suspect strongly enough to justify surveillance." Seren pulled a small vial from her robes, the glass catching light with an inner luminescence that spoke of concentrated soul-essence. "This is a peace offering. The cultist's body is in the Guild's examination chamber beneath the old foundry. The Undercroft the cultists used is adjacent to it. I can give you access to both."
"And in exchange?"
"You tell me what you harvest. Not everything—I'm not fool enough to demand full disclosure from a Soul Reaver. But whatever relates to the seal's deterioration or the cult's purpose. Information, not power."
Kael considered the offer. The Guild's resources against his abilities. Access to evidence he couldn't obtain otherwise. A chance to learn what the cult had been building and whether it connected to the Sepulcher Tablets or Zarek's machinations.
But the Shadow Court was watching. Vane was hunting. And somewhere beneath Thornwick, fourteen cultists were carrying packages that might accelerate the end of the world.
"Deal," he said.
Seren handed him the vial. "This will mark you as a Guild associate to my people. Don't waste it."
Kael pocketed the soul-essence, feeling its weight against his chest. "When do we leave?"
"Now, if you're ready. The examination chamber has a direct access point through the Undercroft." Seren replaced her mask, her features disappearing behind bone-white again. "Stay close. The tunnels aren't safe—there's been Abyssal contamination in the lower levels since the seal started weakening. Some of the things down there don't stay dead the way they should."
They moved through the rusted door into a narrow stairwell descending into darkness. Seren conjured a pale light in her palm—not flame, but something colder, steadier, that illuminated the walls without casting shadows. Kael followed, one hand on his blade, the other trailing along damp stone that grew colder with each step.
The temperature dropped sharply as they descended. Frost crept along the walls, glittering in Seren's pale light. The air tasted of copper and something older, something that made his teeth ache.
"Fungal growth," Seren said, noticing his reaction. "Abyssal contamination metabolizes stone. The crystals it produces interfere with spiritual senses. Awakened below Ascendant stage often can't perceive threats until they're being attacked."
"I'm Ascendant."
"Then you'll be fine. Mostly."
The stairwell opened into a vast chamber, its ceiling lost in darkness above. Pillars of carved stone rose at irregular intervals, their surfaces etched with symbols that pulsed faintly with residual energy. At the chamber's center, a circular pit descended further into the earth—the Undercroft itself, visible as a wound in the floor where the darkness seemed thicker, more alive.
Seren led him to a side passage marked with the Mortician's Guild's interlocking circles. The examination chamber lay at its end: a sterile space lined with tables, each supporting a body covered in white cloth. Six tables. Six corpses.
One was empty, its cloth pooled on the floor like shed skin.
Seren stopped at the threshold. Her pale light flickered once, twice, then steadied. "There were six cultists in the group that returned from the Undercroft. The others are being held in separate facilities. This one..." She moved to the empty table, examining something on its surface. "Someone cut the restraints. The body was removed less than an hour ago."
Kael approached the table. Dark stains marked the stone—old blood, new blood, and something else. A residue that gleamed like oil and smelled of burning sulfur.
"Entity-touched," he said. "The corpse was compromised."
"From the inside, if I'm reading the damage correctly." Seren traced the burned patterns on the table's surface. "Something used this cultist as a host. When it finished, it cut its way out."
The implications settled over Kael like a shroud. An Entity had walked out of the Undercroft wearing a cultist's skin. And if it had escaped the examination chamber, it was loose in Thornwick's underbelly—or worse, heading toward the surface.
"The sigils," Kael said. "The ones carved into the cultist who surrendered to Vane. If this one was hosting an Entity..."
"Then Vane might be dealing with the same thing." Seren pulled a communication stone from her robes, speaking rapidly into it. Her words were clipped, urgent, in a language Kael didn't recognize. When she finished, she turned to him. "My people will secure the tunnels. You should—"
A scream echoed from somewhere below. High, thin, and cut brutally short.
Seren's pale light extinguished. In the darkness, Kael heard movement—wet, dragging sounds, accompanied by a smell that made his eyes water. Something was climbing the stairs from the Undercroft.
"Back," Seren hissed. "The side passage."
They retreated as the dragging sounds grew louder. Kael's night vision adjusted slowly, revealing shapes in the darkness: the empty table, the remaining corpses, and something emerging from the stairwell. A form that might have been human once, standing wrong, limbs too long, joints bending in directions that hurt to observe.
The Entity-touched cultist stared at them with eyes that held no whites, no irises—only absolute black that seemed to drink the light.
"You carry the hunger," it said in a voice like grinding glass. "The Reaver. The Hollow King stirs. He calls."
Kael drew his blade. The steel caught some distant illumination and threw it back in a line of desperate hope. "Who is the Hollow King?"
The creature smiled with too many teeth.
"You are."
It lunged.
Kael moved on instinct, his blade singing through the air. The strike connected with the creature's shoulder, severing something that wasn't muscle, wasn't bone. Black ichor sprayed across the examination chamber, hissing where it touched stone. The creature didn't slow. A hand—if it could be called that—closed around his throat and lifted.
Air stopped. Light stopped. Everything narrowed to the pressure building behind his eyes and the creature's dead gaze boring into his soul.
"The Hollow King remembers," it whispered. "The tomb opens. The Reaver returns."

The Whisper King.
Kael saw him clearly for a fraction of a second: tall, gaunt, draped in robes that seemed woven from midnight itself. A face that might have been human once, now marked with sigils that pulsed with stolen power. Eyes that held the weight of ages and the hunger of something that had consumed civilizations.
The Whisper King turned, as if sensing the observation, and smiled.

Kael struggled to sit up. His hands trembled. The vision's echoes still reverberated through his skull—the Whisper King's face, his voice, the casual claim of ownership over everything Kael had become.

The implications spiraled outward, each one more terrifying than the last. The Whisper King— connected to the first Soul Reaver—was using cultists to accelerate seal degradation. The Shadow Court was monitoring Kael specifically. And somewhere above them, Inquisitor Vane was examining a cultist's body that might contain the same Entity-touched corruption.
"The cultist who surrendered to Vane," Kael said. "We need to warn—"

"Patrol routes are mapped in my—"

"You'll be walking into a trap. The Shadow Court is watching—"
"Then they'll see me walk into a trap and learn nothing useful." Kael paused at the threshold, looking back at Seren. "You said you lead the Hollow Investigation Division. You deal with Entity incursions, rifts, Abyssal contamination. This is your jurisdiction. So stop telling me what I can't do and start telling me what you can."
Seren stared at him for a long moment. Then she pulled a second vial from her robes—this one filled with something that shifted between silver and black—and tossed it to him.
"Shadow-walker essence. One dose. It will let you move through darkness without detection for approximately ten minutes." She pulled up her hood, concealing her face. "My scouts will converge on the Undercroft's main entrance. If you encounter the Entity before they do, use everything you have. And if you see Vane..." She hesitated. "Tell him the Guild offers its services. Pro bono. For the good of Thornwick."

"Because if the seal breaks, everyone dies. Even hunters. Even Reavers." She turned toward the tunnels. "We all face the Abyss eventually."
Kael watched her disappear into the darkness, then looked down at the vial in his hand. Shadow-walker essence—the kind harvested from creatures that existed between the Material and the Abyss, valuable enough to buy a small estate in Thornwick's upper district. Seren had given it freely, which meant she was either desperate or playing a longer game.
Neither option was comforting.
He uncorked the vial and drank. The essence slid down his throat like liquid shadow, cold and thick, spreading through his limbs with unnatural speed. The world shifted. Colors bled away, replaced by gradients of grey and black that painted everything in shades of concealment. Sound became muffled. Light became irrelevant.
He climbed the stairs toward Thornwick's surface, toward the Watchtower, toward Inquisitor Vane.
The streets were quieter than they should have been. Midnight had passed an hour ago, but Thornwick never truly slept—the steam engines ran continuously, the night markets never closed, and the city's vast underclass moved through shadows regardless of the hour. Yet the streets Kael traversed were empty. Doors locked. Windows shuttered. The silence broken only by distant bells and the occasional cry of something that wasn't human.
The Watchtower rose against the skyline, its iron bones wrapped in brass plating that gleamed under gaslight. Six stories of Order authority, home to Inquisitor Vane and whatever remained of the cultist's corrupted body. Guards patrolled the perimeter with disciplined precision, their formation tight, their expressions tight.
Kael circled the building, looking for access. The shadow-walker essence muted his presence, but it wouldn't hide him completely—not from someone like Vane, whose spiritual senses had been honed through decades of hunting. One mistake, one moment of carelessness, and he'd walk into a confrontation he couldn't win.
A side entrance. Service access, unmarked, unguarded. The kind of door that existed for deliveries and maintenance than arrests. Kael slipped through as a patrol passed three meters away, their conversation fading as they moved toward the building's main entrance.
Inside, the Watchtower smelled of oil, metal, and something floral—incense, maybe, or the candles that Order members burned during meditation. He climbed stairs that spiraled upward through the building's core, each floor marked by heavier doors and tighter security. At the fourth floor, he found what he was looking for: a sign indicating Examination Suite Two.
Voices from beyond the door. Two speakers, one low and measured, one high and strained.

Vane.
