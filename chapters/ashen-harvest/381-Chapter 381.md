The spectral guardian lunged, its form flickering like a dying flame.
Kael threw himself sideways as claws of pure soul-energy carved through the air where his throat had been. The tomb's ancient stone screamed as the attack shredded through solid rock, sending chips raining against his leather armor. He rolled behind a crumbling pillar, tasting dust and copper on his tongue.
"You carry the mark." The guardian's voice resonated from everywhere and nowhere. "Yet you are not him."
"I'm nobody." Kael pressed his back against cold stone, counting his breaths. "Just passing through."
"Lies."
The specter materialized above him, its hollow gaze burning with accumulated centuries of sentry duty. One skeletal hand descended, fingers elongated into killing points.
Kael surged upward, meeting the attack with arms crossed. Soul-energy crackled between them, a sound like breaking bones. His Demigod-stage power held firm against the guardian's assault, though the effort drove him to one knee.
"Then know this." The guardian's form twisted, its skull elongating into something almost noble. "The First Reaver sleeps deeper than any grave. You cannot wake what was willingly surrendered."
*First Reaver.* Kael's heart stuttered. "I don't want to wake anything. I just need—"
"Need?" The word dripped contempt. "The inheritors always need. They need power. They need answers. They need to become more than they are."
Another strike. Kael deflected it into the ground, creating a crater that smoked with residual spiritual discharge. The tomb trembled, dust cascading from cracks in the ceiling.
"What inheritors?" he demanded.
The guardian laughed, a sound like shattering glass. "You think yourself singular? The Soul Reaver's legacy passes through bloodlines, through bargains, through infection. Every generation births another claimant. Every generation seeks this place."
Kael's mind raced. *Bloodlines.* His parents had been nobody—farmers in a village that no longer existed on any map. *Bargains.* He'd made none before awakening. *Infection.* The Faceless One's fragment nested somewhere behind his sternum, a splinter of alien consciousness he'd never asked for.
"Who else has come here?"
"Many." The specter drifted backward, its form becoming more solid, more imposing. "Most died in the catacombs below. Some breached the outer sanctum. None have entered the inner chamber."
"Why?"
"Because I am still here."
The guardian's body coalesced from scattered light, taking on the appearance of a warrior in ancient plate armor. A sword manifested in its grip—a weapon that seemed forged from crystallized moonlight. Its edges hummed with frequencies that made Kael's teeth ache.
"Your power is considerable." The First Reaver's guardian studied him with something approaching respect. "But power without understanding is a blade without a hilt. You will cut yourself before you cut your enemies."
Kael stood, rolling tension from his shoulders. "Then teach me."
"I owe you nothing."
"You owe whoever made you everything." Kael spread his hands, a deliberate vulnerability. "I'm not your enemy. I'm trying to stop something worse than any inheritor squabble."
The spectral blade lowered an inch. "Explain."
"The seals are failing. The Great Rift specifically—Zarek's cult is working to break it open. If they succeed, the Abyss spills into the Material. Every soul on this continent becomes a battery for Entity consumption."
"You speak of the old wars."
"I speak of now. This generation. Today." Kael stepped forward, closing distance despite every instinct screaming retreat. "Whatever the First Reaver was fighting for, it's about to be undone unless someone stands in the gap."
The guardian's hollow eyes searched his face. Seconds stretched into eternities.
"The First Reaver did not triumph through strength alone," it finally said. "He understood that the Abyss cannot be conquered—only contained. The seals are not walls. They are wounds. And wounds can be reopened."
"Then help me keep them closed."
"I cannot leave this place. My consciousness is bound to these stones, to the ritual that created me." The guardian gestured toward the chamber's far end, where darkness pooled like liquid shadow. "But within that sanctum lies the First Reaver's final gift. He left instructions for those who might follow. For inheritors who proved themselves worthy of carrying his burden."
Kael's pulse quickened. "How do I prove worthiness?"
"You already have." The guardian's form began to flicker, its energy spent from the confrontation. "You came seeking knowledge, not domination. You faced a superior opponent and did not flee. You offered truth when deception would have served you better."
The spectral warrior's edges blurred, light bleeding from its outline. "The sanctum will test you. Not your power—your choices. Remember: the Soul Reaver's gift was never the ability to harvest. It was the ability to choose what to do with what you harvest."
"I'll remember."
"Hope so." The guardian's voice faded to a whisper. "The last one who entered made the wrong choice. His bones rest beyond the threshold. Yours may join them."
The specter dissipated entirely, leaving only moonlight seeping through cracks in the ancient ceiling.
Kael stood alone in the tomb of the First Soul Reaver.
His breath echoed against walls carved with symbols he couldn't read—angular shapes that seemed to shift when viewed directly. The air tasted of old copper and older secrets. Somewhere in the darkness beyond his vision, water dripped with metronomic patience.
*Last one made the wrong choice.*
He forced himself to move forward, each footstep deliberate on flagstones worn smooth by centuries of vigil. The sanctum's entrance gaped before him—a doorway twice his height, its frame inscribed with warnings in languages that predated the modern Orders.
Kael crossed the threshold.
The chamber beyond defied expectation. He'd anticipated another burial vault, another memorial to dead greatness. Instead, he found a workspace. A laboratory. A wizard's study frozen in time.
Tables lined the walls, covered with glass containers holding specimens he refused to examine closely. Shelves overflowed with journals bound in leather that crumbled at the edges. And in the center of the room, illuminated by a shaft of pale light from some unseen source, stood a pedestal bearing a single book.
Not a journal. Not a manual.
A tablet.
Kael's throat tightened. *Sepulcher tablets.* He'd heard rumors—whispers in the Veiled Market, cautionary tales from Order instructors. The first Soul Reaver had recorded his discoveries on stone, believing it more permanent than parchment. The tablets were supposed to contain the True Name of the Abyss, the conceptual key that could unmake reality itself.
The Orders had destroyed every copy they'd found. Legend claimed only fragments survived, scattered across inaccessible vaults and forgotten catacombs.
And here one sat, waiting for him.
Kael approached the pedestal, his shadow stretching long across the stone floor. The tablet itself was unremarkable—gray granite roughly the size of his palm, edges rough where tools had shaped it. The inscription covering its surface used a script that predated any language he recognized.
But as he reached for it, his Soul Reaver senses flared.
*Residual energy.* Strong. Recent. Someone had touched this artifact within the last day.
His fingers closed around cold stone.
The world inverted.
---
Kael knelt in a burning village, smoke choking the sky. Figures in dark robes moved between ruined homes, dragging bodies into geometric patterns that hurt to observe directly. At the formation's center stood a man in chains—tall, gaunt, his face obscured by shadow.
*Memories,* Kael realized. *Not mine. The tablet's.*
The chained man spoke, his voice carrying impossible distances. "The seal requires a living anchor. A soul bound to the boundary between realms. They chose me because I was already dying."
A robed figure struck him. "Silence."
"I was the first to harvest." The man's laugh cracked with pain. "Did you think that was coincidence? They needed someone who could touch the Abyss and return unchanged. Someone who could gather what we needed without becoming what we feared."
Another blow. The chained man continued regardless.
"The True Name is not a weapon. It is a warning. The Abyss knows its own name. Speaking it reveals the wound—the place where our world bleeds into theirs. Seventy-two wounds. Seventy-two seals. And every seal requires—"
The vision shattered.
---
Kael gasped, the tablet falling from numb fingers. His hands trembled as he examined them, checking for damage that wasn't visible. The vision had lasted seconds, maybe less, but it had conveyed volumes.
*Seals require living anchors.* The knowledge settled into his understanding with uncomfortable weight. *Soul Reavers don't just fight the Abyss. They become part of the containment system.*
He retrieved the tablet, more carefully this time. Its surface had warmed against his skin, pulses of residual energy matching rhythms he recognized from his own body.
Soul Reaver to Soul Reaver. A connection spanning centuries.
The book-shaped impression on the pedestal's surface caught his attention. Something had rested there once, removed recently enough that dust hadn't reclaimed the space. The Sepulcher tablet wasn't the primary artifact here—it was supplementary. Reference material for something larger.
*What did the First Reaver use?*
Kael scanned the cluttered workspace, searching for patterns amid the chaos. His eyes settled on a cabinet against the far wall, its doors hanging open. Inside, velvet cushions held empty depressions—display slots for objects now missing.
A cold certainty crystallized in his chest. Someone had been here before him. Someone had taken whatever the First Reaver had considered most important.
The residual energy he'd sensed wasn't from the tablet's handling. It was from the cabinet. From whoever had emptied it.
"Looking for something?"
Kael spun, power surging to his fingertips. A figure stood in the doorway he'd entered through—tall, feminine, draped in shadows that clung like living things.
Lyra.
She stepped into the sanctum's pale light, and his stomach dropped. Her eyes held the cold calculation he'd learned to fear. The soft compassion she'd shown in recent weeks had vanished, replaced by the predator who had once commanded Shadow Court operations across three provinces.
"You're early," he said, keeping his voice level. "I thought the Council meeting ran until midnight."
"It did." Lyra moved deeper into the chamber, her gaze sweeping across the workspace with professional efficiency. "I had other business in the district. Imagine my surprise when my sources reported a trespasser accessing restricted catacombs."
"Restricted by whom?"
"The Order of Dawn claims jurisdiction over Pre-War archaeological sites. Section Seven of the Concordat, Amendment Twelve." She smiled without warmth. "I'm sure you studied the fine print during your years as a model citizen."
Kael set the tablet on the pedestal, buying time. "You're here alone. That's either arrogance or desperation."
"Neither." Lyra's fingers traced the edge of a nearby table, collecting dust. "I'm here because the Shadow Court has been tracking something for three years. A rumor. A legend. The tomb of the First Soul Reaver."
"The Shadow Court isn't an Order. You don't have jurisdiction."
"The Shadow Court has interests that supersede jurisdictional concerns." Her eyes fixed on the cabinet, on the empty slots. "I see we're not the only ones interested in this collection."
Kael followed her gaze. "You know what was here?"
"Enough to know it's missing." Lyra turned to face him fully, and he saw the tension coiled in her shoulders, the readiness that spoke to combat training. "The First Soul Reaver didn't just seal the rifts. He created a failsafe. A method for the Orders to re-seal any rift that degraded beyond recovery."
"That's valuable knowledge."
"That's dangerous knowledge." Lyra's voice hardened. "Do you understand what you're standing in the middle of? Every cult, every power-hungry Archon, every Entity-aligned faction would burn this city to ash to possess what was stored in that cabinet."
Kael's mind raced through implications. Zarek's cult. The Faceless One's agents. The Shadow Court's own operatives. All of them potentially converging on a single artifact that could control the seal network itself.
*Or destroy it entirely.*
"Who took it?" he asked. "You arrived after me. You've been tracking this tomb for years. You must have suspects."
Lyra's expression flickered—frustration, maybe, or something darker. "I have several. None of them should have been able to breach the outer sanctum. The guardian alone should have stopped any conventional approach."
"The guardian is gone. Dispelled, maybe. Or just exhausted."
"It was bound by the First Reaver's will. It should have persisted until the seals themselves failed." She moved closer, shadows trailing her like a cloak. "Something has changed. Something accelerated the timeline."
Kael thought of the vision. The chained man speaking of seals requiring living anchors. The Abyssal Wars ending not in victory but in desperate containment.
"The seals aren't just weakening," he said slowly. "They're being actively sabotaged. Someone is working to open them faster than they degrade naturally."
"Zarek's cult."
"Probably. But cults don't have the resources to access Pre-War sites like this. They don't have the knowledge." Kael gestured at the workspace, at the centuries of accumulated research. "They need help. Insider access."
Lyra's jaw tightened. "You're suggesting infiltration of an Order."
"I'm suggesting someone with historical expertise and magical capability is playing multiple sides." He met her gaze directly. "The Shadow Court has been operating in Thornwick for decades. You've watched cult activity escalate. You've seen seals fail ahead of projected timelines. What do you know about who's coordinating it?"
Silence stretched between them, filled with dripping water and distant tremors in the earth above.
"I know the Shadow Court answers to masters who hide behind enough layers that I've never seen their true faces." Lyra's voice dropped, vulnerability cracking through her professional mask. "I know they've given me orders that made no sense. I know they wanted me here tonight, in this exact chamber, at this exact moment."
"And you followed those orders."
"No." She pulled something from within her cloak—a journal, leather-bound and ancient. "I followed evidence. The orders were a trap. I was supposed to find you here and eliminate you. Instead, I'm offering you this."
Kael accepted the journal, turning it over in his hands. The cover bore no title, but the binding felt older than anything he'd touched outside the tablet. Spiritual energy radiated from its pages like heat from a banked fire.
"It belonged to the Fourth Soul Reaver," Lyra said. "One of the few who survived long enough to document their experiences. She hid it within the Shadow Court's archives, encoded in a cipher only someone with Soul Reaver abilities could read."
Kael's fingers traced the cover's embossing, and his talent stirred—recognition sparking between his power and the artifact's composition.
"Why give this to me?"
"Because you're right. Someone is coordinating the cult activity, and they're using the Shadow Court as a pawn. If the seals fail completely, it won't matter whose side we're on." Lyra stepped back, shadows gathering around her form. "The Shadow Court that raised me is dead. The one that exists now serves masters whose goals I can no longer support."
"You're asking me to trust you."
"I'm asking you to read that journal and understand what we're fighting." Her outline began to blur, shadow magic gathering for transport. "The First Soul Reaver didn't just seal the rifts. He sealed himself inside one. Every Reaver since has been tapping into that original prison. We're not guardians. We're jailers. And someone wants to open the door."
The shadows consumed her entirely.
Kael stood alone in the sanctum, holding two artifacts that rewrote everything he'd believed about his power. The tablet containing warnings in a dead language. The journal pulsing with a fourth Reaver's final observations. Both pointing toward a truth the Orders had buried for a millennium.
*The seals aren't walls. They're wounds.*
He opened the journal.
---
Pages fell open to entries written in multiple hands across multiple generations. The Fourth Reaver had compiled records from her predecessors, weaving them into a narrative that traced the Soul Reaver lineage from its bloody inception to its eventual suppression.
*...the First understood that containing the Abyss required sacrifice beyond death. The Entities cannot be destroyed—their existence predates our reality's formation. They can only be bound, isolated, separated from the Material by barriers that require constant maintenance...*
*The seals are not physical structures. They are conceptual frameworks maintained by the living. Each rift requires an anchor—a soul bound to both realms, serving as a tether that prevents the wound from widening...*
*When an anchor dies, the tether weakens. When a tether fails entirely, the rift beneath grows. We have lost four anchors in the past century. The remaining seals strain under the weight of what they contain...*
Kael's vision blurred as understanding crashed through him.
*He's still alive.* The realization struck like a physical blow. *The First Soul Reaver didn't die. He became an anchor. He's been holding the Great Rift closed for a thousand years, and he's running out of strength.*
The journal's final entries grew increasingly frantic, the handwriting deteriorating as the Fourth Reaver approached her end.
*They want to replace me with a stronger vessel. The Orders speak of sacrifice, of duty, of legacy. They do not mention that the anchor experiences every moment of imprisonment. Every Entity that pushes against the seal. Every year that passes while the Material forgets the wars that created it...*
*The First has endured for centuries. I have endured for decades. What kind of person survives this? What kind of person would choose this?*
*I asked him once, through the connection all Reavers share. He said: "Someone who loves a world worth saving."*
*I didn't understand then. I pray I never have to.*
---
A sound echoed from beyond the sanctum.
Kael's head snapped up, journal clutched against his chest. Footsteps. Multiple sets, moving with military precision through the catacombs he'd fought to reach this place.
He had minutes at best.
The tablet went into his pack. The journal followed. He ran for the chamber's far wall, where secondary passages promised escape routes the tomb's original architects had surely included.
Behind him, voices called out in coordinated calls.
"Seal the perimeter. No one leaves."
"I want the Reaver alive. The Court needs his testimony."
Kael's blood froze. *The Court.* Not cultists. Not Zarek's lackeys.
The Shadow Court had sent cleanup crews.
He found a narrow passage half-hidden behind collapsed shelving, squeezed through, and ran blind through darkness that pressed against him like physical weight. The footsteps behind him multiplied. Someone had anticipated his route.
*Lyra didn't know about this,* he realized. *She was the distraction. The real threat was waiting for her to flush me out.*
The passage narrowed, then opened into a vertical shaft. Handholds carved into the stone promised upward progress. Moonlight filtered from above—real moonlight, not the pale glow of magical illumination.
Kael climbed.
His Demigod strength made the ascent trivial, muscles responding to demands that would have exhausted ordinary Awakened. He emerged through a hidden hatch onto Thornwick's rooftops, the gaslit city sprawling beneath him in a maze of smoke and shadow.
He didn't stop moving until three districts separated him from the tomb's entrance.
---
A dead drop in the Iron Quarter provided temporary sanctuary—a basement storage space belonging to a fence who owed him favors and knew better than to ask questions. Kael barricaded the door, lit a single candle, and spread his acquisitions across a dusty crate.
The tablet. The journal. And in his pack's inner pocket, the fragment of the Faceless One's consciousness that had marked him as a potential vessel.
Three pieces of a puzzle he couldn't yet assemble.
He began reading the journal again, searching for anything that might explain the Faceless One's interest in Soul Reavers, in the seal network, in the First's ancient imprisonment.
Hours passed. The candle burned low.
Near dawn, he found it—a marginal note in the Fourth Reaver's hand, almost illegible from age and deliberate obscuring.
*The Faceless One was the First's bonded Entity. When he chose imprisonment over power, it tried to claim him. The seal trapped them both. The Entity wants freedom. The Reaver wants containment. Everything the cult does serves one master or the other.*
Kael stared at the words until they blurred.
*Zarek serves the Faceless One.*
The cult wasn't trying to destroy the seal network. It was trying to free its architect—the Entity that had been imprisoned alongside the First Reaver for a thousand years.
And if the First fell, if the Great Rift's anchor failed, every other seal would collapse in chain reaction.
The city above him stirred with morning traffic. Merchants opening shops. Workers heading to factories. Citizens who had no idea that their world balanced on the sanity of a single man holding back the Abyss through sheer stubborn will.
Kael gathered the artifacts and prepared to move.
He needed allies. He needed information. He needed to find out who had emptied that cabinet and what they'd taken.
But most urgently, he needed to understand what the First Reaver had meant when he'd told the Fourth that someone who loved a world worth saving would make the choice he had.
Because if that anchor failed, Kael might be the only one who could replace him.
The thought followed him into the dawn like a cold hand pressed against his spine.
