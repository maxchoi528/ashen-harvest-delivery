The corpse's fingers had been cold when Kael pressed his palm against the dead man's chest.
"No, no, no—" The cultist's companion scrambled backward across the cobblestones, his robes soaking up rainwater and grime. "Stay away from me, Reaver!"
Kael didn't look up. The notification pulsed behind his eyes, welcome and familiar.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Combat Reflexes from the remains. Quality: Partial — subject died over one hour ago.]
Three-quarters of a skill's worth. Better than nothing. He withdrew his hand, wiping residue on his coat.
"You're next," he told the surviving cultist.
The man bolted. Kael let him go. Running cultists occasionally led somewhere interesting.
Twelve hours underground in the Thornwick Sewers, and Kael had bagged four corpses and one living witness. The witness was currently tied to a cast-iron support beam three alleys away, breathing through a gag but otherwise intact. The corpses were scattered where they'd fallen—two near the collapsed tunnel entrance, one in a drainage junction, and this latest specimen beside an illegal still that reeked of fermented cabbage spirits and something fouler beneath.
Rain hammered the grate above. The underground tunnel network stank of rot, standing water, and decades of abandoned infrastructure. Somewhere above, Thornwick's Gaslight District lived its nightly life—steam-whistles, distant laughter, the rumble of late carriages. Down here, the only sounds were dripping water and the occasional skitter of rats.
Kael crouched over the body, searching pockets. The cultist wore no insignia, carried no papers. A working man's gear: flint knife, tinderbox, a few copper pence, a scrap of bread that had seen better days. But clutched in his dead hand—
A bronze key, half the length of Kael's palm. Unmarked. Heavy.
He turned it over. The metal was warm, impossible in this chill, and faintly humming.
Kael's enhanced senses picked up the vibration a moment later. Subtle. Rhythmic. Like a heartbeat that didn't belong to anything alive.
The other corpses had carried nothing. This one had been positioned near a wall that looked like every other wall—cracked brick, mineral stains, centuries of accumulated grime. But when Kael stood and pressed his free hand against the surface, the vibration intensified.
There was something behind this wall.
He pulled out the map he'd purchased from a sewer-mapper two days ago. The layout showed a service passage connecting to an old municipal reservoir that had been sealed off before Kael's grandmother was born. No marked entrance in this section. The cultists had sealed it themselves.
Kael retrieved his prisoner from the drainage junction. The man was older than he'd seemed in the torchlight—middle-aged, weathered, with the callused hands of someone who'd done real labor in a past life. Not a true believer, Kael guessed. Press-ganged or desperate.
"Water," Kael said, removing the gag. "I can offer you water and a quick death, or I can offer you answers. Choose wrong and I take what I want anyway."
The prisoner's throat worked. "You won't believe me."
"Try me."
"There's a vault. Below the reservoir." The man's eyes darted toward the still. "We were sent to guard it. Six of us, rotating shifts. The others—" He swallowed. "The others went to deal with something topside. They said they'd be back before dawn."
"How many cult cells operate in Thornwick?"
"I don't know. We only knew our group."
"What are you guarding?"
The prisoner shook his head. "I don't know that either. Nobody tells us anything. They just say the Vault must not be opened until the appointed time."
Kael leaned closer. "What time?"
"Can't say. Truly." Genuine fear in the man's voice—not of Kael, but of whoever had recruited him. "But they talk about the Seals. The 72. They say most of them are weakening. They say the appointed time is when the last one falls."
The Underground Arc. This was it—organized cult activity, not random cells but a coordinated operation with a unified goal. And now he had a key and a direction.
"Show me where."
---
The entrance wasn't hidden behind the wall. It was hidden beneath it.
Kael found the trapdoor after the prisoner led him twenty paces east. Three inches of rusted iron, covered by a layer of grime so thick it looked like natural sediment. The cultists had been stepping over it without knowing—their training had focused on watching for patrols, not examining the ground.
The lock was old. Pre-Concordat, maybe older. The bronze key fit perfectly.
Below, a ladder descended into darkness. The air rising from the shaft carried the smell of dust, copper, and something Kael's Reaver senses identified before his conscious mind caught up.
Old blood. Years of it, soaked into stone and never cleaned.
Kael descended.
The chamber at the bottom was larger than expected—a natural cavern, maybe, or a quarry cut long ago and forgotten. His enhanced vision painted the space in shades of gray and blue: stone pillars supporting a vaulted ceiling, pathways worn smooth by ancient footsteps, alcoves carved into the walls like cells in a tomb.
And in the center, on a plinth of black granite, a box.
Not a chest. Not a reliquary. A box, perfect cubes, each face carved with symbols that seemed to shift when he looked at them directly. The bronze keyhole was on the eastern face. The humming was stronger here, resonating in Kael's chest like a second heartbeat.
"Stay here," he told the prisoner.
"Wasn't planning on following you into the dark," the man muttered.
Kael approached the plinth. The symbols on the box's surface matched nothing in his experience—not Order glyphs, not Abyssal script, not any of the merchant cants he'd learned in the Veiled Market. They felt older. More fundamental. Like the language that existed before humans had words for things.
He inserted the key. Turned it.
The box didn't open. Instead, the lid slid sideways, revealing an interior lined with faded velvet. And resting in that interior, a stone tablet.
The Sepulcher Tablet.
Kael's breath caught. He'd heard rumors—Whispered tales in the darker corners of the Veiled Market, arguments between collectors who swore they'd seen fragments, scholarly debates about whether the originals had ever existed or were only legend. The Sepulcher Tablets were supposed to contain the True Names of the 72 Entities. Knowledge that would give its reader power over the Abyss itself.
He reached for it.
The moment his fingers touched the stone, pain lanced through his skull. Not physical pain—a collision of information, too much data compressed into a format his mind couldn't process. Flashes: a battlefield of bones, a figure wielding light like a blade, the sound of screaming that went on for centuries, a door opening onto something that should never have been seen—
Kael wrenched his hand back. The tablet remained in the box, undisturbed. But he could feel it now, a presence at the edge of his awareness, waiting.
[Item Identified: Sepulcher Fragment (Corrupted)
Status: Sealed
Contents: Partial record of the First Reaver's observations. Access requires: Soul Reaver lineage + Sovereign stage or higher.]
His lineage. Not just any Soul Reaver could read this. Someone with a connection to the First—the original, the one who'd discovered the ability and maybe created the Soul Reaver system itself—had to activate it.
Kael's hands trembled. He steadied them through force of will.
The First Reaver. The name his own ability whispered in the quiet moments, when he harvested and felt those echoes of consciousness brush against his own. Whoever had first touched the dead and learned to take from them. Whoever had mapped the Abyss and counted the Entities and carved that knowledge into stone.
He looked at the tablet again. Now that he knew what to look for, he could see faint lines on the stone's surface—writing too small to read, dense with meaning just beyond his grasp.
"Still locked," he murmured. "Not yet."
A sound echoed from above. Footsteps on the ladder. More than one person.
Kael turned, positioning himself in shadow between two pillars. His Reaver senses stretched outward, counting heartbeats: four individuals, descending quickly, trained in silence but not enough to fool enhanced perception.
One of them was stronger than the rest. Ascendant-stage, maybe approaching Transcendent. Not a cultist—something in the way the spiritual energy moved suggested Order training, discipline, purpose.
Shadow Court, then. They operated through deniable assets, and they had interests in anything involving the Abyss.
"Clear," one voice called down. "Just the prisoner. The cultist must have—"
"Wait." A second voice, female, sharp-edged. "There's a scent. Fresh. Someone's been here."
Kael held his breath. The four figures reached the bottom of the ladder, spreading into a formation he recognized from Order tactical manuals. They carried crossbows with silver-tipped bolts and blades that glowed faintly with bound spirit-fire. Standard Abyssal-hunting loadout.
The leader stepped forward. Tall woman, dark coat, silver insignia on her collar that marked her as Iron Vanguard auxiliary—no, not auxiliary. The insignia was wrong. Different configuration. Someone from an Order that didn't officially exist anymore.
Or that had learned to hide.
"Show yourself," she said. "We know you're there. The blood on the floor is still wet."
Kael calculated odds. Four against one, but one of them was Ascendant-class and the others were at least Awakened-peak. He had Transcendent-stage power and Soul Reaver harvesting, but he didn't know these people or their capabilities. Ambushes worked both ways.
And there was the tablet behind him. If they saw it—
"I don't want trouble," he called back, stepping into the light. "I was following the same trail you were. The cultists led me here."
The woman's eyes narrowed. "Kael Ashborn. The Traitor Reaver." She said the name like it tasted bitter. "We've been watching you for weeks. You move through Thornwick like you own the city's secrets."
"I move through Thornwick like someone trying to stay alive. There's a difference."
"Most people trying to stay alive don't make enemies of every Order simultaneously." She didn't lower her crossbow. "What did you find?"
"Nothing that concerns you."
"Everything concerning the Abyss concerns us." Her companions shifted, tightening the formation. "The Seals are failing. The cult is mobilizing. And you—" She pointed at him with her free hand. "You're right in the middle of it. Coincidence stops being coincidence after the third encounter."
Kael studied her. The insignia, the bearing, the way she held herself like someone who'd lost everything and rebuilt from the wreckage. He'd seen that look before. In mirrors, sometimes.
"Who are you?" he asked. "Really."
"Someone who remembers what the world was like before the Seals. Someone who knows what happens when they fail." She lowered her crossbow an inch. "The Shadow Court isn't your enemy, Reaver. We have common ground."
"The Shadow Court serves whoever pays best. I've seen your work."
"We serve the mission. The mission predates the Orders. Predates the Concordat. Predates everything." Her voice dropped. "There was a time when six Orders didn't exist, when humanity faced the Abyss with nothing but hope and the ones who could touch the dead. The Reavers. The first and the last of their kind. We remember them. We remember what was lost."
The First Reaver. She was talking about the First Reaver.
Kael felt the tablet's presence behind him, a weight on the edge of perception. "And you think I can find what was lost."
"We think you might be what was lost." The woman's eyes held his. "A bridge to the past. A key to understanding what's coming."
The cavern seemed to contract around them. Kael's Reaver senses detected no additional hostiles approaching, but something felt wrong. A tension in the air. A sense of observation.
"You said you were watching me," he said slowly. "How did you know where to look?"
"We have sources everywhere."
"That's not an answer."
"No." She holstered her crossbow. "It's a test. And you're failing it."
One of her companions—a younger man with a scar across his jaw—raised his weapon. "Commander Dorian. The prisoner is gone. He must have slipped past us on the ladder."
The woman—Dorian—swore under her breath. "Fan out. Find him. He can't have gotten far."
"And if he got to the surface and raised the alarm?"
"Then we have bigger problems." She turned back to Kael. "The cult's timing has accelerated. They weren't supposed to move for another six months, but something changed. Something pushed them. And I think—" She hesitated. "I think it was you."
"Me?"
"Every major cult cell in Thornwick has shifted operations in the last two weeks. Every underground network has gone to war-footing. And every time we trace the source, we find your fingerprints. You killed someone important. Someone they couldn't afford to lose."
Kael thought back. Two weeks ago, he'd harvested a cultist lieutenant near the Docks. The man had carried information about a planned raid on an Order outpost. He'd used that information to expose a traitor within the Order of Dawn. Had that killed the cultist that was supposed to stay alive?
"The Lieutenant," he said. "By the old fish market."
"Yes."
"He was coordinating something. Supply lines, I thought. Weapons shipments."
"He was coordinating the Unsealing." Dorian's voice hardened. "He was one of only twelve people who knew the full timeline. Kill one, and the others go to ground. But they also accelerate. They assume we know. They assume we're coming."
"So this is my fault."
"This is the Abyss's fault. You're just the trigger they needed." She gestured to her team. "We're leaving. The cult will respond to the prisoner's alarm within the hour, and we can't be here when they arrive. You should leave too."
"And if I want to stay? If I want to see what comes out of that vault?"
"Then you'll die." No hesitation in her voice. "The cult has more assets in this city than we do. More than the Orders do. They've been building for decades. Centuries, maybe. And they're ready."
"Ready for what?"
Dorian looked at him for a long moment. Then she reached into her coat and pulled out a small leather folder. Inside, a single sheet of paper covered in handwriting that looked decades old.
"This was recovered from an abandoned cultist temple three years ago. We never knew what it meant." She handed it to him. "But I think you might."
Kael unfolded the paper. The text was fragmentary, incomplete, but the words he could read sent ice through his veins:
*...the Reaver returns, and the door shall open...*
*...seventy-two keys, seventy-two locks, and the first key was always meant to return...*
*...the Whisper King waits in the deep places, and when the last seal breaks, he will rise...*
The Whisper King. The most feared of the 72 Entities, supposedly imprisoned at the beginning of time in a seal that could never be broken.
Dorian took the paper back. "The cult doesn't want to destroy the Seals. They want to control them. And to do that, they need something they've been searching for since the Abyssal Wars ended."
"What?"
"The first Soul Reaver's tomb. The place where the True Names are hidden." She met his eyes. "The place where the Whisper King was first bound."
Kael's blood ran cold. The tomb. The original source of his ability. And somewhere in Thornwick—maybe in this very cavern—there was a clue.
"The Sepulcher Tablets," he said.
"You've seen them?"
"I know they exist. I know they're connected to the First Reaver." He didn't mention the tablet behind him. Couldn't. Not yet. "And I know that whoever finds them first controls the endgame."
Dorian nodded slowly. "The Shadow Court has been searching for the tomb for two hundred years. We've lost more operatives than I can count. And now you're here, with blood on your hands and cultists on your trail, and you might be the only person who can find it."
"Why me?"
"Because you're a Reaver. And Reavers don't just harvest the dead." She turned toward the ladder. "They remember them. Every soul you touch, every ability you take—it leaves traces. Fragments. Echoes of whoever came before." She paused. "The First Reaver harvested more souls than anyone in history. If any trace of that remains, it would be in someone like you."
The chamber fell silent. Rain drummed on the iron grate above. Somewhere in the distance, a steam-whistle screamed—the signal for the morning shift at the factories.
Kael looked at the vault one last time. The tablet waited, patient and unknowable. Locked until he was strong enough. Strong enough to know what the First Reaver had known. Strong enough to face what came next.
"I need time," he said. "To understand what I've found. To prepare."
"You have until the next new moon." Dorian began climbing the ladder. "After that, the cult moves regardless of whether we're ready. And when they do, everyone in Thornwick dies."
Her team followed, boots scraping against iron rungs. At the top, she turned back.
"One more thing, Ashborn. The cult knows about you. Not just your name—your ability. They're hunting Soul Reavers specifically. Anyone who can touch the dead and take what's inside."
"Why?"
"Because the last Soul Reaver disappeared three hundred years ago, and nobody knows why. Some people think he transcended—became something beyond human. Some people think he was killed by the Orders. And some people think—" She hesitated. "Some people think he made a deal."
"A deal with what?"
"With the thing in the Abyss that gave him his power in the first place." She vanished through the grate. Her voice drifted down like a final warning. "Be careful which deal you choose to keep."
The ladder clanged empty. The cavern settled back into silence.
Kael stood alone among the pillars, the bronze key in his hand, the tablet waiting behind him. Above, Thornwick woke to another gray morning. Below, the Abyss stirred in its ancient prison.
Somewhere in the city, cultists were mobilizing. Shadow Court agents were watching. The Orders were bickering over jurisdiction while the world tilted toward disaster.
And Kael—traitor, Reaver, survivor—had found the first real piece of the puzzle.
He took one last look at the vault. The Sepulcher Fragment pulsed with contained power, a secret waiting for the right key.
Soon.
Kael climbed the ladder into the gray morning light, the taste of copper on his tongue and the weight of destiny on his shoulders.
The cult had six days until the new moon.
He had six days to find the First Reaver's tomb.
Somewhere in Thornwick, the Whisper King waited.
And the door was already opening.
