Kael's boot splashed through a puddle of murky water as he rounded the corner into Dockside Alley. The gas lamps here guttered in the salt wind, casting long shadows that twisted and merged with the fog rolling in from the harbor. His hand rested on the knife at his belt—not that a blade would help much against what he suspected lurked in this part of Thornwick.
Three merchants had gone missing this week. The city's guard called it common thuggery. The Mortician's Guild called it a coincidence. Kael called it a pattern.
The warehouse ahead squatted between two condemned buildings, its windows boarded, its doors chained. Steam vents from the nearby engine works sent plumes of white into the night air, masking the building's edges. Kael had tracked the last missing merchant here—a fishmonger named Heston who owed money to the wrong people.
Or so the story went.
Kael's skin prickled. Spiritual pressure, faint but unmistakable, bled through the warehouse walls. The pressure signature was wrong—not the clean burn of Order magic, not the controlled pulse of regulated Awakened. This felt jagged, hungry, like teeth gnawing at the edge of perception.
Cult activity. Had to be.
He moved forward, keeping to the shadows. The warehouse's side door hung slightly ajar, the chain that should have secured it lying coiled on the ground like a dead snake. Someone had been careless. Or someone wanted company.
A muffled shout echoed from inside. Male, terrified.
Kael's hand tightened on his knife. He pushed through the door.
The interior was a cathedral of crates and shadow, lit by a circle of black candles that burned with flames that didn't flicker. Six figures in hooded robes stood around an altar constructed from stacked cargo boxes. Three more figures held a man flat against the altar's surface—the fishmonger, his face pale, his mouth gagged.
The cultists turned as one.
"Another stray," one of them said. The voice was feminine, amused. "The night provides."
Kael's eyes swept the room. Six robed cultists plus the three holding Heston. The altar was raised enough that reaching the fishmonger meant crossing the candle circle. The robed figures carried no visible weapons, which meant they planned to rely on their spiritual abilities.
"Put him down," Kael said. His voice came out flat, cold. "Walk away. Pretend this never happened."
Laughter rippled through the ranks. The lead cultist stepped forward, her hood falling back to reveal a face lined with ritual scars. "You don't understand, boy. We're not here for the fish. We're here for *you*."
The floor erupted.
Roots burst through the wooden planks, black and twisted, coiling around Kael's legs. He severed one with his knife only to have two more take its place. The candles' flames roared upward, changing from black to crimson in an instant.
A tendril whipped across his chest, knocking the knife from his grip.
"Take him alive," the scarred woman ordered. "The Reaver bloodline must be preserved for the Unsealing."
Kael's blood chilled. They knew what he was. The Soul Reaver—that title hadn't spread through Thornwick's underworld yet. Someone had told them.
The roots tightened, hauling him toward the altar. Heston's eyes met his, wide with terror, a vein pulsing visibly in the man's temple.
No time for clever.
Kael reached inward, finding that cold place where his Reaver ability coiled. The ability that let him touch death and steal its secrets. The ability that cost him nothing except the trust of everyone who learned what he was.
He pressed his palm against the nearest root and *pulled*.
The notification blazed across his vision, golden text burning through shadow.
**[Soul Harvest: You extracted vitality from the remains of a Veil-Touched Root. 5% of host abilities acquired. Minor degradation due to non-standard extraction vector.]**
The root withered, crumbling to ash. The other tendrils flinched, their growth stuttering. The cultists' eyes widened behind their hoods.
"What did you do?" the scarred woman hissed.
Kael tore free of the weakened vegetation, snatching his knife from the floor. "Insurance."
He moved.
The first cultist died with a sound like tearing silk. Kael's blade opened her throat before she could raise her hands. The second tried to fling a bolt of corrupted spiritual energy but caught a boot to the knee, collapsing with a scream. Kael harvested the first corpse without slowing—his fingers found her cooling neck, and the notification pulsed.
**[Soul Harvest: You extracted corrupted spiritual energy from the remains of a Veil-Touched cultist. 8% of host abilities acquired. Memories partially recovered.]**
Fragments of her final moments flooded his mind. A chamber beneath the warehouse. Chains. A man in silver robes being prepared for something called the "First Key." The memory shattered before he could grasp more.
The scarred woman raised her hands, and the remaining cultists formed a semicircle. "Hold! He's just one Reaver. We have five of us still standing."
Four, Kael corrected silently. The fifth was busy trying to stop bleeding out.
"Surrender now," the woman continued, "and we'll spare your friend. Resist, and we sacrifice you both to the Hungry Dark."
Kael wiped blood from his blade with his sleeve. "You already tried to sacrifice me to your roots. My answer hasn't changed."
He lunged.
The battle became a blur of motion and death. A cultist's spiritual strike grazed his shoulder, sending fire racing down his arm. Kael ignored it, driving his knife through the woman's guard and opening a gash across her forearm. She retaliated with a backhand that sent him staggering, her nails leaving trails of black corruption on his cheek.
They exchanged blows, fast and brutal. Kael's training under Lysara was evident—he used angles, leverage, the environment. He kicked a crate into another cultist's legs, swept the legs of a third. His knife found kidneys, throats, the soft flesh beneath ribs.
The scarred woman fought like a demon. Her spiritual attacks came in waves, each one pushing him back, eroding his defenses. But she was holding back—trying to take him alive. That hesitation would cost her.
Kael let her think she had the advantage. He let her drive him toward the altar, let her believe victory was moments away. When she raised both hands for a finishing strike, he dropped.
His fingers found the dead cultist's ankle behind him.
**[Soul Harvest: You extracted corrupted spiritual energy from the remains of a Veil-Touched cultist. 12% of host abilities acquired. Memories partially recovered.]**
Power surged through his veins. Not much—a fraction of what the original owner had possessed—but enough.
Kael's eyes blazed as he rose. "The First Key. Where?"
The woman's face contorted. "You dare take from our blessed—"
Kael moved. His knife cleared its sheath in a silver arc that ended at her hip. The blade bit deep, and he harvested her before she could scream.
**[Soul Harvest: You extracted corrupted spiritual energy from the remains of a Veil-Touched cultist Commander. 18% of host abilities acquired. Memories fully recovered. Soul Reaver specialization upgraded: Critical Extraction.]**
The Commander's memories flooded his consciousness. Plans. Faces. Names. A schedule of rifts being weakened across Thornwick. A meeting point three blocks from the harbor. A target—the leader of a major faction who would be attending a gathering tomorrow night.
And beneath it all, a name whispered with religious reverence: Prime Zarek.
Kael's jaw tightened. The same name that had appeared in every frame-up, every dead end, every false lead since his Awakening. The same name that kept surfacing whenever he scratched at the edges of the conspiracy that had put him on death row.
The survivors fled. Kael let them go—he needed witnesses, needed the fear to spread. Let the cult know that the Soul Reaver was coming for them.
He turned to the altar. Heston had rolled off during the chaos and was now crawling toward the door, tears streaming down his face.
"Wait," Kael said. "You're safe. It's over."
The fishmonger froze, looking back with eyes that expected death. When he saw Kael's expression—grim but not murderous—his shoulders sagged with relief.
"They said they needed me for blood," Heston gasped. "Said my bloodline was special. That I had to open the door for something big."
Kael knelt beside him. "Did they mention specific locations? Rifts, gates, anything like that?"
"There's a cellar beneath the warehouse. I saw stairs before they put the gag in." Heston grabbed Kael's sleeve with trembling hands. "Please. Get me out of here. I'll tell you everything I know about the people who sent me to this part of town. Just don't let them take me back."
"I'll get you out." Kael helped him to his feet. "But I need to see that cellar first. Can you walk?"
Heston nodded shakily.
They moved together through the warehouse, stepping over bodies. The candles had burned low, their flames now returning to normal colors. Kael found the stairs behind a stack of crates—wooden boards pried loose to reveal stone steps descending into darkness.
"Stay here," he told Heston. "If anyone comes, run."
The fishmonger nodded, pressing himself against the wall.
Kael descended.
The cellar was larger than expected, carved from the bedrock beneath the warehouse. Chains hung from the walls, their manacles empty but stained with old blood. Symbols covered every surface—not the clean geometries of Order magic but something older, messier, drawn by hands that understood power but not precision.
At the room's center stood a circle of obsidian mirrors, their surfaces swirling with oily darkness. The air here tasted of copper and rot. Kael's Reaver senses screamed warnings about the spiritual energy saturating this space—concentrated, refined, ready to be used.
A ritual chamber. They were preparing something.
Kael approached the mirrors. The swirling darkness seemed to pull at him, promising power, offering secrets. He recognized the sensation—the same hunger he'd felt when first Awakening, the same whisper that had told him death was just another door.
He stepped back.
The mirrors showed nothing useful—just reflections of himself, pale and bloodied, and something else. A shape in the darkness beyond the glass. Eyes that reflected no light. A mouth that opened too wide.
Kael's hand went to his knife. "Show yourself."
The shape flickered. For an instant, he saw a face—humanoid, gaunt, wearing robes that seemed woven from shadow. Then it was gone, and the mirrors showed only his own reflection.
**[Soul Sense Alert: Residual Entity presence detected. Origin: Unknown. Classification: Class-3 Abyssal Manifestation. Warning: Direct confrontation inadvisable at current power level.]**
Class-3. The Entities that had nearly broken the world during the Abyssal Wars. Sealed behind barriers maintained by the Six Orders, imprisoned until the end of time.
One of those barriers was weakening here.
Kael studied the symbols on the walls, committing them to memory. He'd need to report this—the Orders would want to know about the ritual chamber. But the Orders also wanted to capture him, experiment on him, use him as a weapon against his will.
He'd find another way. He always did.
He climbed the stairs, emerging into the warehouse. Heston was where he'd left him, though he'd found a crate to sit on. The fishmonger's color had improved slightly, though his hands still shook.
"The cellar has ritual work," Kael said. "They're preparing for something. I need to—"
A crash echoed from outside. Shouts. The clang of metal on metal.
Kael grabbed Heston and pulled him behind a stack of crates. Through the warehouse's broken windows, he saw figures in the alley—city watch, judging by the uniforms. Six of them, surrounding something.
No. Some*one*.
A woman stood in their center, her hands raised, blood dripping from a wound on her temple. She wore the gray coat of a Mortician's Guild operative, her dark hair pulled back in a severe bun.
"Lysara," Kael breathed.
His mentor fought like a storm made flesh. Even wounded, even surrounded, she moved with a grace that made the watchmen look like children playing at soldiers. Her spiritual attacks weren't lethal—she was holding back—but they were devastating. Two men went down with broken legs. A third clutched his chest, gasping.
"Stand down!" one of the watchmen bellowed. "By order of the Thornwick Council, you are under arrest for unlicensed spiritual activity and suspected association with known fugitive Kael Ashborn!"
Kael's blood turned to ice.
They knew Lysara was connected to him. They were using her to draw him out.
"Go," he hissed to Heston. "Out the back. Don't stop until you reach the public market."
"But—"
"*Go*."
The fishmonger needed no further encouragement. He vanished into the shadows, leaving Kael alone.
Through the window, Lysara fought with her back to the warehouse wall. She was losing—couldn't use her full power without killing, couldn't retreat without abandoning her position. The watchmen pressed in, their batons rising and falling.
Kael calculated odds. Six watchmen, all trained, all armed with spirit-reinforced weapons. Lysara was superior, but tired, wounded. He could tip the balance—but attacking city watch was a death sentence. Even with his new abilities, even with the harvest he'd taken, six-on-two was suicide.
Unless he wasn't seen.
Kael moved. He circled the warehouse, staying low, keeping to the shadows. The watchmen were focused on Lysara—none of them thought to watch their flank. He found an open window on the building's side, climbed through, and dropped silently to the floor.
Lysara saw him immediately. Her eyes widened—not with fear, but with something like resignation.
"You shouldn't have come," she said.
"Tried not to." Kael stepped up beside her. "Failing, apparently."
The lead watchman lowered his baton. "Kael Ashborn. You're wanted for questioning regarding multiple infractions of the Spiritual Regulation Act. Surrender now, and you'll receive a fair trial."
"And if I don't?"
"Then we use necessary force." The watchman's smile was thin. "Your friend here has already resisted arrest. Adding another count won't change anything."
Kael's mind raced. He could fight—six watchmen wouldn't be easy, but he had abilities they didn't know about. He could run—leave Lysara to her fate, save himself. Or he could negotiate.
"I'm willing to talk," he said slowly. "But not here. Not with weapons drawn. Take us to a neutral location. Let Lysara explain her side."
"Why would we—"
"Because I know things." Kael let his hand fall to his knife, not drawing, just resting. "Things about the cult activity in this district. Things about what's being prepared in these warehouses. Take me to your commander. Let me prove I'm worth more alive than dead."
The watchmen exchanged glances. The lead one hesitated.
A scream shattered the moment.
One of the watchmen—younger, newer—collapsed, clutching his head. His eyes rolled back, showing whites. Black veins crawled beneath his skin, spreading from his neck outward.
"Mind corruption," Lysara hissed. "Cult ambush. They're using our standoff as cover."
The remaining watchmen turned, searching the darkness. More screams echoed from the street—civilians, caught in the chaos. Kael saw shapes moving on the rooftops, heard the whistle of corrupted spiritual energy cutting through the air.
"This is a trap," the lead watchman said. "For all of us."
Kael grabbed Lysara's arm. "Back entrance. Now."
They ran.
The warehouse's rear exit opened onto a narrow alley choked with refuse and stagnant water. Kael led, Lysara close behind, the sounds of battle fading as they put distance between themselves and the ambush. The cult had planned this—used Lysara's arrest as bait to gather Awakened and city authorities in one place.
Somewhere in this city, something was happening. Something big. And he was running out of time to stop it.
They reached a safehouse three blocks away—a room above a tailor's shop that Lysara used for meetings. The space was sparse: a table, two chairs, a cot in the corner. Kael had stayed here before, after jobs gone wrong, when nowhere else was safe.
Lysara slumped into a chair, finally allowing herself to feel the exhaustion. Her wound had stopped bleeding, but the bruise on her face was spreading, darkening to purple.
"Tell me," she said. "Everything. Starting with how you knew where to find me."
Kael told her. The missing merchants. The warehouse. The ritual chamber with its obsidian mirrors and Class-3 presence. The ambush waiting for anyone who investigated too deeply.
When he finished, Lysara sat in silence for a long moment.
"The cult is accelerating," she finally said. "My contacts in the Shadow Court reported the same thing. They're not just weakening the seals anymore. They're preparing for mass unsealing."
"How long?"
"Days. Maybe hours." Lysara met his eyes. "The Six Orders know, but they're paralyzed. The Concordat prevents coordinated action. Every faction is waiting for someone else to move first."
"And the Soul Reaver. Why do they want me specifically?"
"Your ability." Lysara's voice was flat, clinical. "Soul Reavers can harvest from anything—corpses, yes, but also Entities. Spirits. The energy that powers the seals. If they could control you, they could use you to break the barriers without triggering the defense protocols."
Kael's stomach turned. "I'm a key."
"Worse. You're *the* key. The only Soul Reaver active in Thornwick." Lysara leaned forward. "You need to disappear. Leave the city. Go somewhere they can't find you."
"And watch the world burn?"
"The Orders will handle it."
"The Orders are paralyzed by treaties and politics." Kael stood, pacing. "The cult has been operating in this city for months, maybe years. They have assets everywhere—the watch, the guilds, probably the Council itself. And you want me to hide?"
"I want you to survive."
"Why?" The question came out sharper than he intended. "Because I'm useful? Because my ability makes me valuable?"
Lysara's expression didn't change. "Because you're not a monster yet. Because there's still something left worth saving." She paused. "Because I failed you once, and I won't do it again."
The words hung between them. Kael stopped pacing.
"Your sister," he said quietly. "She's in a Mortician's facility. I found the records last month."
Lysara's face went rigid. "You said nothing."
"Until I had proof. Until I knew what we were dealing with." Kael reached into his coat, produced a folded document. "Sera's location. Status report. She's alive, but barely. They're keeping her sedated, running tests."
Lysara took the paper with trembling hands. Her eyes moved across the text, reading, rereading. When she looked up, her expression had hardened into something dangerous.
"Who authorized this?"
"The Guild's Inner Council. But the orders came from above them. Someone in the Order of Dawn, or connected to them." Kael sat back down across from her. "This isn't about your sister. It's about you. About what you know. About who you're protecting."
"I protect my people."
"You protect the Shadow Court's interests." Kael met her gaze steadily. "I need to know who ordered Gwen's squad to die. I need to know who's been feeding information to the cult. I need to know if there are traitors in the Orders."
Lysara was silent for a long time. The lamp on the table flickered, shadows dancing across her features.
"Dorian," she finally said. "Mage-Adjunct Dorian. He served the Order of Dawn before transferring to the Mortician's Guild. He has access to both organizations' records. And he disappeared two weeks ago."
"The same Dorian who framed me?"
"The same."
Kael's hands curled into fists. "Then I need to find him. Before the cult does."
"The cult already has him." Lysara's voice was grim. "That's the only explanation for how they knew about my movements, about this safehouse, about everything. Dorian's been compromised, or he was never what he seemed."
A cold certainty settled over Kael. The threads of conspiracy were weaving together—the frame-up, the cult activity, the accelerated timeline. All of it connected to one man who'd vanished without a trace.
The door opened.
Kael spun, knife half-drawn, only to freeze.
A girl stood in the doorway. Young, maybe fifteen, wearing the patched clothes of a street urchin. Her eyes were too old for her face, and she clutched a leather satchel to her chest like a shield.
"You're Kael," she said. "The Soul Reaver."
"How did you—"
"There's a message for you." The girl stepped inside, dropped the satchel on the table. "From someone who says you owe them a debt."
Kael stared at the bag. Inside, he could feel spiritual energy pulsing—faint, contained, but unmistakable. Soul-essence. Enough to buy a small fortune.
"Who sent you?"
The girl's smile was brittle. "Someone who knows you're looking for Dorian. Someone who knows where he is."
She turned and walked out before either of them could respond. Her footsteps faded down the stairs, disappearing into the night.
Lysara moved to the window, scanning the street below. "We were followed."
"Or warned." Kael opened the satchel. Inside, nested in silk, lay three vials of soul-essence—top quality, worth more than a year of his old wages. And beneath them, a folded note.
He unfolded the paper. The handwriting was elegant, precise, each letter carved with mechanical precision.
*The Reaver walks in shadow.*
*The Cult moves at midnight.*
*The Seals break at dawn.*
*Dorian awaits in the place where the sun first touched stone.*
*Come alone, or watch the world end.*
*— A Friend*
Kael read the note twice. The message was clear: a challenge. A trap. Or possibly both.
"The place where the sun first touched stone," Lysara murmured, reading over his shoulder. "The Sunspire. The oldest monument in Thornwick, built before the city had walls. It commemorates the first dawn after the Abyssal Wars."
"Midnight," Kael said. "That gives us six hours."
"You can't go alone. It's a trap."
"Obviously." Kael tucked the note into his coat. "But if Dorian knows something about the cult's timeline, if he can tell us who's really pulling the strings..."
"We need him alive."
"We need him reaped." Kael's eyes glittered in the lamplight. "His memories. His contacts. Everything he knows about the conspiracy. If we harvest him, we won't need him to talk."
Lysara looked at him for a long moment. Something shifted in her expression—not disapproval, but a kind of weary recognition.
"You're changing," she said. "The power is changing you."
"I'm adapting." Kael stood, checked his knife, gathered the soul-essence vials. "The world's burning down around us. Change is the only option that isn't death."
He moved toward the door, then paused.
"Coming?"
Lysara rose slowly, testing her wounded side, wincing as the bruise protested. "I've come this far. Might as well see it through."
Outside, Thornwick churned with activity. Steam engines roared in the factories, casting orange light across streets choked with fog and smoke. Somewhere in the distance, a clock tower began to chime—ten bells, marking ten hours past midnight. Six hours until the cult made their move.
Six hours until Dorian told him everything, willing or otherwise.
Kael stepped into the night, Lysara at his side. The gas lamps flickered as they passed, casting long shadows that seemed to reach for them. The city's heartbeat—industrial, chaotic, alive—thrummed beneath his feet.
The Reaver walked in shadow.
The Cult moved at midnight.
The Seals broke at dawn.
And somewhere in the heart of Thornwick, Dorian was waiting for a reckoning six months in the making.
