Iron bars clanged shut behind Kael, the sound swallowed by the cavernous dark of Blackgate Prison. Torchlight guttered from sconces carved into living rock, painting the cell in shades of amber and shadow. A week since his miraculous survival of the gallows, and the Crown's bounty hunters had finally caught up in the border provinces.
His wrists burned where the manacles had been removed. Spiritual suppression cuffs—crude things that blocked his energy circulation without damaging his soul. Clever. Someone wanted him alive and weakened.
Kael pressed his spine against the damp wall. The stone leeched warmth from his body, spreading cold through his Awakened flesh. His senses spread outward despite the suppression, probing the prison's layout. Seven guards on this level. Two more at the entrance. A fourth-level barrier sealing the lower depths.
Nothing that could hold him permanently. But escape required patience, and patience required knowing what he was escaping into.
"Prisoner Ashborn." The voice came from beyond the bars—gravel and smoke, roughened by decades. Kael raised his head to find an old man standing in the corridor, unlit lantern dangling from one hand. Gaunt features, hollow cheeks, a face that had known violence and chosen to keep living anyway. Gray hair cropped close to the skull. Prisoner's garb, but this man moved with the ease of someone who had earned his stay through labor than crime.
Another inmate, then.
"Who's asking?" Kael's voice emerged steadier than he felt.
The old man smiled, teeth yellowed but intact. "Name's Mordecai. Mordecai Graves. Before your friends at the Crown kindly relocated me, I ran courier routes through the eastern provinces. Knew every smuggler's path, every forgotten tunnel, every blind spot in the border patrols." He leaned against the bars, lantern swaying. "I'm also the man who can get you out of this place."
"And why would you do that?"
"Because I know what you are, Soul Reaver." Mordecai's voice dropped to a whisper. "Saw the reports. Execution that wasn't. Bounty hunters dropping like flies. And that—" he tapped his own chest, over the heart "—feeling you get when someone dies nearby. The pull. The hunger."
Kael said nothing. The Soul Reaver stirred in his soul, acknowledging the truth of the accusation without confirming it.
"The Hollow Reach," Mordecai continued. "Three miles east of here, through the forest, past the dead town. That's where they execute the Order criminals. Awakened who've broken the Concordat." His eyes glittered in the torchlight. "Fresh corpses every moon cycle. Guards posted but sparse. And tonight—" he glanced over his shoulder "—tonight's the execution of a man called Brennan. Awakened. Third stage. Condemned for unsanctioned warfare against a rival sect."
"You want me to harvest him."
"I want you to use your gift. In exchange, I'll show you the way out of Blackgate entirely. Not just to Hollow Reach, but through it, past it, into territory where the Crown's writ runs thin and a man like you can disappear." Mordecai straightened. "I don't care about your soul-robbing ways. I'm dying anyway—three months, maybe four, before the cancer takes me. What I care about is making sure the men who put me here regret it. And you, prisoner Ashborn, are the perfect instrument for that regret."
Kael studied the old man. Cancer. He could see it now, the faint spiritual decay radiating from Mordecai's core—not infection, not corruption, but the slow collapse of a soul's housing. The man's body was failing, and he knew it.
"Why not just kill them yourself?"
"I have no gift for violence, boy. But you—" Mordecai's smile widened, revealing bloody gums. "You've got the real thing. And from what I hear, it costs you nothing but whatever moral coin you're still carrying."
The Soul Reaver pulsed in Kael's chest. Free energy. Free skills. The Law of Soul Conservation didn't apply to him, and harvesting was as natural as breathing. But accepting meant trusting a stranger, following him into unknown territory based on nothing but a convincing story.
Mordecai Graves had appeared exactly when Kael needed a way out. Too conveniently.
"Prove it," Kael said. "Prove you can get me out of here."
The old man's grin turned sharp. "Follow me in spirit, then. Just your awareness. The cuffs block energy, not consciousness." He pointed down the corridor. "Cell block D. Guard rotation leaves a four-minute gap at the third junction. Beyond that, the maintenance tunnels connect to the forest's edge. I mapped them myself over two years of careful observation."
Kael closed his eyes. His spiritual sense extended, passing through the suppression field like water through a net. He felt the old man ahead—diminished, to be sure, but present. Alive. No deception in his spiritual signature, only desperate determination.
And beyond him, something else. A faint resonance in the earth itself, pulling toward the east. Toward Hollow Reach.
"What's at Hollow Reach?" Kael asked, opening his eyes.
"Trouble," Mordecai answered. "The kind that makes prisons look like vacation homes. But also opportunity. The kind that can either kill you or make you untouchable." He extended a gnarled hand through the bars. "So. Do we have a deal?"
Kael took the hand and shook it. The grip was dry and firm, the grip of a man who had spent decades carrying secrets and burdens.
---
The cell door opened with a whisper of rusted hinges. Mordecai had picked the lock with a sliver of metal pinched between his fingernails—practiced, the motion. Kael followed, slipping into the corridor's darkness.
Three junctions. The first passed without incident, the second with only a distant snore from a sleeping guard. At the third, Mordecai pressed flat against the wall, and Kael mirrored him. Footsteps echoed from somewhere above—boots on stone, a casual patrol.
They waited. Sixty seconds. Ninety. The footsteps faded, and Mordecai moved.
The maintenance tunnel was a throat of ancient stone, barely wide enough for a man to crawl through. Pipes lined the walls—steam conduits, Kael realized, from the prison's furnace system. The metal was cold, sweating condensation that soaked his clothes.
"Fifteen minutes," Mordecai whispered. "Then they discover the empty cell. We'll need to be long gone."
They crawled in silence, the tunnel angling downward before leveling out. Fresh air began to mix with the stale furnace smell. Earth tones replaced gray stone. The tunnel opened into a root-choked drainage ditch, and beyond it, trees.
Blackwood Forest. Kael recognized the twisted oaks from his bounty hunter days—the bark blackened by some ancient blight, the branches reaching like skeletal fingers toward a moonless sky. The forest was said to be haunted, and while ghosts held no terror for a Soul Reaver, the things that lived between the trees might.
"Stay close," Mordecai said, pushing through the undergrowth. "The forest has its own rules. Travelers who wander off the path have a tendency to never return."
Kael followed, one hand on his cell-mate's shoulder. His spiritual sense spread outward, painting the forest in layers of spiritual light. Animals no reaction—simple creatures driven by instinct. But deeper in the wood, something flickered. Something vast and cold and hungry.
"What guards the Hollow Reach?" Kael asked.
"Nothing that can be killed with steel," Mordecai replied. "That's why they built the execution grounds there. The Crown learned long ago that some criminals require containment beyond what walls can provide." He paused at a fallen log, helping Kael over. "The Hollow Reach is a scar in the world. A place where the Veil wears thin. The old records call it a rift—but not one of the sealed ones. This one was deliberately torn during the Abyssal Wars, left open as a warning."
"A wound in reality."
"The kind that never heals." Mordecai's voice carried a weight that suggested personal knowledge. "My brother was executed there. Thirty years ago, for the crime of loving the wrong person. I came to pay respects. Found something else instead." He didn't elaborate.
The forest thinned. Trees gave way to scrub, scrub to bare earth, and finally they stood at the edge of a vast depression in the landscape. Hollow Reach.
It looked like a crater, maybe a mile across, descending in terraced layers toward a central point. But unlike a meteor strike, the edges were too regular, too geometric. Carved, Kael realized. This was a pit, excavated by hands or powers beyond mortal comprehension.
At the bottom, a faint luminescence pulsed. Purple and sickly, like light filtered through contaminated glass. And within that light, movement.
Bodies. Dozens of them, scattered across the crater floor.
"Execution site," Mordecai said. "They don't bury the dead. Don't clean up the remains. The Abyssal presence preserves them—makes them useful for study, or so the Arcane Spire claims. Every few weeks, a new corpse joins the collection."
Kael's Soul Reaver stirred, tasting the resonance in the air. Dozens of dead Awakened, their spiritual energy slowly bleeding into the rift. The pull was intoxicating. The temptation almost overwhelming.
But first, the living.
A wooden platform dominated the crater's center, raised on stone pillars. Torches burned at its corners, casting long shadows across the execution area. A man stood bound to a wooden frame at the platform's center—tall, broad-shouldered, with the calloused hands of a fighter. His spiritual signature blazed even from this distance. Third stage, just as Mordecai had promised. Awakened, but not for long.
Guards patrolled the platform's edges. Four of them, armed with the same suppression cuffs that had weakened Kael. Their formation was disciplined, their attention focused on the perimeter than their prisoner.
"The execution happens at moonrise," Mordecai whispered. "Another hour. But the prisoner will be sedated before the blade falls—standard procedure. When that happens, his spiritual defenses will drop, and you'll have your window."
"And if I want to take him before the execution?"
"Then you'll have four guards to kill instead of two. And these aren't common soldiers." Mordecai pointed at the nearest patrol. "See the sigils on their armor? Order of Dusk. They specialize in hunting Awakened criminals. Your average execution detail would run at the first sign of a Soul Reaver. These ones will fight until their hearts stop."
Kael assessed the situation. The guards were good—third stage, at least, with combat experience written into their stances. Together, they would be more than a match for his weakened state. But alone...
"The prisoner. Brennan. What did he do?"
"Massacre of a village. Killed forty-three civilians to eliminate a single target. The victim happened to be the nephew of an Order commander." Mordecai's tone suggested the full story was more complicated, but the essentials were clear enough. A killer, condemned by killers who wanted to save face.
The kind of death that wouldn't weigh on Kael's conscience.
"I'll need to get close," he said. "The harvesting requires physical contact."
"The prisoner will be unbound after sedation. They'll prop him against the frame for public viewing before the beheading. You can approach during the transition— the crowd of witnesses. When the blade falls and his head leaves his body, that's when you move. Touch him before his spirit fully departs, and the harvest will be clean."
"And if someone sees me?"
"Then you'll need to move fast." Mordecai pulled a hood from his coat—a beggar's disguise, worn and weathered. "The witnesses will be common folk from the nearby villages. They've come to watch justice served. They'll scatter the moment violence starts. Use the chaos."
Kael took the hood. The fabric smelled of tobacco and old sweat, but it would serve. He pulled it over his head, obscuring his distinctive features.
"One more thing." Mordecai's hand closed on his arm, grip surprisingly strong. "The rift. The Abyssal presence at the crater's heart. It'll sense you the moment you start harvesting. It won't be able to resist—the soul energy will call to it like blood in water. You need to be fast and be gone before it decides you're worth investigating."
"How long do I have?"
"Minutes. Maybe less. After that..." The old man shrugged. "Let's just say Hollow Reach earned its name for reasons beyond the executions."
Kael nodded. The plan was reckless, desperate, and exactly the kind of opportunity the Soul Reaver had been waiting for. Fresh corpses, weakened prey, and a path out of prison that didn't involve months of tunneling.
He descended into the crater.
---
The crowd had gathered by the time he reached the platform's base. Maybe thirty villagers, bundled against the night's chill, their breath misting in the cold air. Kael slipped among them, a shadow among shadows. His suppression cuffs had been removed by now—Mordecai had taken them during their escape, selling them to a fence in the forest for enough coin to fund his cancer treatments.
Without the cuffs, his spiritual energy flowed freely. He kept it contained, barely a whisper, but the Soul Reaver stirred with anticipation. The dead called to him, and soon he would answer.
The sky lightened as moonrise approached. The clouds parted, and silver light spilled into the crater, illuminating the platform and its prisoner. Brennan hung limp against his restraints, sedated as promised. His head drooped, chest rising and falling with mechanical regularity.
The executioner climbed the platform. A hulking figure in black, face hidden behind a featureless mask. He checked his blade—an actual sword, massive and single-edged, hanging from a hook on his belt. The edge gleamed in the moonlight.
"Sentence of death," a voice announced from somewhere behind Kael. A magistrate, maybe, or an Order official. "For crimes against humanity, against the sacred Compact, against the divine order of—"
The speech continued, but Kael stopped listening. His attention fixed on the prisoner, on the spiritual signature that flickered with the approaching death. The sedation was deep, the soul's defenses lowered. Perfect conditions for harvesting.
The blade rose.
The crowd inhaled as one.
Steel sang through the air.
Blood sprayed in a crimson arc, catching the moonlight like liquid fire. The head tumbled, bouncing twice before rolling to a stop at the platform's edge. Brennan's body slumped, held upright only by the restraints that still bound his wrists.
Kael moved.
He pushed through the crowd, shouldering aside a gasping woman, ducking under the flailing arm of a fainting man. The guards shouted, but their attention was split between the horrified witnesses and the ritual cleansing of the execution site. No one noticed one hooded figure darting toward the platform.
He climbed the steps two at a time. A guard turned, hand going to his weapon, mouth opening to shout a warning. Kael's palm struck the man's throat, not hard enough to kill but enough to silence. The guard crumpled, choking.
The others were turning now, seeing him, recognizing the threat. But Kael was already at Brennan's body, hands pressing against the cooling flesh of the dead man's chest.
The Soul Reaver woke fully.
Harvesting was not a pleasant experience. Others who possessed similar gifts—the true Soul Reavers of legend, the harvesters who served the cult—described it as communion, as merger, as becoming one with the departing spirit. Kael experienced it differently. He experienced it as consumption.
Energy flooded into him, raw and potent. Memories shattered against his consciousness—a childhood in the provinces, training in a mountain monastery, the faces of the forty-three people he had killed. Horror and justification and terrible, terrible certainty. Kael absorbed it all, let it flow through him, and reached for the skills that mattered.
The soul resisted. Even in death, even with defenses lowered, Brennan's spirit fought to protect its treasures. But Kael had done this too many times, had built his power through a dozen harvests, and the dead man's resistance felt feeble. Insufficient.
He reached deeper. Past the memories of blood and fire. Past the regrets and the rages. To the core.
Skill extracted: Executioner's Mark.
The knowledge settled into Kael's mind like a key turning in a lock. He understood, now, what it meant to be an executioner. Not just a killer, but a reader of souls. The ability to identify weakness in an opponent's spiritual defenses, to mark them for death with a simple touch, to strike at the precise moment when their soul was most vulnerable.
The System acknowledged the harvest with its characteristic bluntness.
Soul Harvest: You extracted the skill Executioner's Mark from the remains. Skill acquired: Executioner's Mark (Active). Mark a target's soul with your intent to kill. Your next attack against the marked target deals 200% damage and bypasses 50% of spiritual defenses. Duration: a few heartbeats. Cooldown: a few minutes.
Kael released the body and staggered back, gasping. The harvest had drained him—not spiritually, the Soul Reaver's gift remained cost-free, but emotionally. The weight of Brennan's final moments pressed against his mind. The terror. The acceptance. The strange peace of a killer finally meeting his end.
"Soul Reaver!" One of the guards had found his voice. "Kill him!"
Kael spun, raising his hands. The guard charging him was a blur of steel and spiritual energy, blade raised for a killing stroke. But Kael had seen the opening, the mark on the man's soul that whispered of a fractured ribcage and a heart that beat three times too fast.
Executioner's Mark activated.
His hand shot out, fingers closing around the guard's wrist. The touch was brief, violent, and final. Kael twisted, using the man's momentum against him, and felt the satisfying crack of bone as the guard's body struck the platform's edge. He fell, clutching his arm, out of the fight.
The remaining two guards hesitated. They had expected a desperate prisoner, a wounded fugitive. What they faced was something else entirely.
"You should run," Kael said. "This doesn't have to end with all of you dead."
The older guard—sergeant, by his insignia—made a decision. He drew his sword, the blade gleaming with spiritual enhancement. "Hold the line. He's one man. We are three."
Kael smiled without humor. "You counted wrong."
He lunged, and the Executioner's Mark guided his strike.
---
The fight lasted twelve seconds.
It would have lasted longer against opponents who weren't already compromised by the night's events—the chaos of the execution, the unexpected threat, the fading adrenaline. But Kael had the advantage of skill and desperation, and the guards had nothing but training and duty.
The sergeant fell first, a blade in his thigh that Kael twisted before ripping free. The second guard took an elbow to the temple and dropped, unconscious before he hit the ground. The third guard—the one Kael had choked earlier—ran.
Kael let him go. Dead guards meant investigation. A fleeing guard meant confusion, delay, time to escape.
He descended the platform as the crowd finally broke, villagers scattering into the night like startled birds. Mordecai was waiting at the crater's edge, face pale in the moonlight.
"You got it?"
"Got it." Kael showed his hands, still stained with Brennan's blood. "Skill called Executioner's Mark. The dead man's knowledge of spiritual combat is now mine."
"Then we need to move. The rift—" Mordecai pointed toward the crater's center, where the purple luminescence pulsed brighter than before. "It felt you. It's waking up."
Kael felt it too. The Abyssal presence pressing against his awareness, curious, hungry, drawn by the energy of his harvest. He had seconds at most.
"How do we get out?"
"Through the rift's shadow. There's a path that leads to the surface beyond the crater. The Abyssal presence can't follow there—too much residual energy from the old seals." Mordecai was already moving, scrambling down the terraced layers. "Stay close. Don't look at anything in the darkness. And whatever you do, don't respond to the voices."
Kael followed.
The path wound between corpses—hundreds of them, preserved by the Abyssal influence, arranged in patterns that suggested meaning he couldn't grasp. The dead stared at him with empty eyes, their mouths frozen in silent screams. Some of them had been Awakened once. He could still taste the residual energy in the air, old harvests that had never been claimed.
The luminescence grew brighter. The purple light began to pulse in rhythm, almost like a heartbeat.
"Don't stop," Mordecai gasped. He was flagging, his cancer-weakened body pushed beyond its limits. Kael grabbed his arm, hauling him forward.
A voice echoed from the crater's heart. Not sound, exactly, but meaning pressed directly into his mind. Words shaped from hunger and ancient loneliness.
*Soul Reaver.*
Kael's blood froze.
*We have not tasted your kind in an age.*
He ran. The path twisted, doubled back, threatened to swallow them in shadows. But Mordecai knew the way, calling out turns between ragged breaths, and Kael followed, and finally—finally—they burst from the crater onto open ground.
The Hollow Reach stretched behind them, a wound in the world, glowing with unholy light. But ahead, forest. Ahead, freedom.
Mordecai collapsed against a tree, clutching his chest. His face was gray, sweat-slicked, but his eyes were alive with triumph.
"You did it. You're out."
Kael looked back at the crater one final time. The Abyssal presence had stopped at the crater's edge, unable or unwilling to pursue. But it was still there, still watching, still hungry.
*Soul Reaver,* the voice whispered. *We will meet again.*
"Let me guess," Kael said quietly. "You weren't just a courier."
Mordecai laughed, the sound dissolving into a cough. "I was, once. Before they framed me. Before I found out what the Hollow Reach really was." He looked at Kael with eyes that held decades of pain. "My brother discovered the truth. That's why they killed him. And that's why I spent thirty years waiting for someone who could do something about it."
"What truth?"
"The rifts. The sealed rifts that hold the Entities at bay. Hollow Reach isn't just a wound in reality—it's a drain. The Abyssal presence here feeds on the death around it, channeling that energy into the seal network. The other seventy-one rifts are dying because Hollow Reach is feeding." Mordecai's voice dropped to a whisper. "Someone is using this place to kill the seals from the inside. Someone inside the Orders."
The revelation hit Kael like a physical blow. The Great Rift's seal degrading. Zarek's conspiracy. The cult's mass unsealing timeline. It all clicked into place.
"The Whisper King," he breathed.
Mordecai's eyes widened. "How do you know that name?"
"I don't." Kael straightened, his mind racing. "But I intend to find out."
A sound from the forest. Footsteps, dozens of them, approaching through the trees. Torches flickered between the blackened trunks. Voices shouted, coordinating, surrounding.
"The prison alarm," Mordecai groaned. "They've found our trail. Guards from Blackgate, probably. They won't stop until they have you back."
Kael assessed the situation. His newly acquired skill hummed in his mind, ready. The guards would be expecting a weakened prisoner, not a battle-hardened Soul Reaver with fresh abilities. And the forest offered no shortage of ways to even the odds.
"Get to the road," he told Mordecai. "Head east until you reach the Veiled Market. Tell them Kael Ashborn sent you. They'll give you shelter."
"And you?"
Kael drew his knife—a simple blade, no spiritual enhancement, but sharp enough. The forest's shadows embraced him like an old friend.
"I'm going to make them regret following me."
Mordecai nodded, understanding flickering in his eyes. "The old courier routes. They won't know them like I do. Take the northern fork, then the dried riverbed. It'll lead you to Thornwick by dawn."
"Thank you." The words felt strange in Kael's mouth, unpracticed. "For everything."
"Thank me when you've survived the week." Mordecai pushed himself upright, swaying but determined. "There's a woman in Thornwick. Lyra something. She runs a safehouse for people like us. Like you." He paused at the forest's edge. "Tell her Mordecai finally found someone worth betting on."
Then he was gone, swallowed by shadows and undergrowth.
Kael turned to face the approaching lights. Torchlight, maybe twenty torches, converging on his position. Voices called commands—capture, not kill, the Crown wants him alive. The guards were professionals, disciplined, coordinated.
But they were also human. And humans feared the dark.
Kael melted into the forest, letting the shadows swallow him whole.
---
The hunt lasted until dawn.
Kael struck three times—brief, brutal, efficient. The first engagement took out a flanking pair, their bodies found with marks on their throats and their souls already departed. The second ambush caught a squad of six as they crossed the dried riverbed, and Kael harvested another skill from their midst: Shadow Step, from a second-stage assassin who had made the mistake of approaching too closely.
Soul Harvest: You extracted the skill Shadow Step from the remains. Skill acquired: Shadow Step (Active). For a few heartbeats, become intangible and move at 300% speed. Cannot attack while active. Cooldown: a few minutes.
The third engagement was a mistake. The guards had called for reinforcements, and Kael walked into an ambush at the forest's edge. Six archers with suppression bolts, two Awakened officers, and a war hound with spiritual sensitivity.
He killed the hound first, its yelp silencing the night. Then the archers, their bolts deflected or dodged as he closed the distance. The Awakened officers put up more of a fight—one had a barrier skill that deflected his first strike—but Executioner's Mark did its work, and soon Kael stood alone in a field of groaning bodies.
He harvested what he needed. Left what he didn't.
By the time the sun rose over Thornwick's spires, he had accumulated three new skills, a significant boost to his soul energy reserves, and the undying hatred of the Crown's garrison. Not a bad night's work.
The city gates loomed ahead, massive iron doors flanked by statues of the Six Orders' founders. Guards checked travelers with bored efficiency, on the lookout for smugglers and criminals but not expecting the kind of criminal who had just escaped from Blackgate Prison.
Kael adjusted his cloak, covering the bloodstains and the torn prison garb. He fell into step with a merchant's caravan, just another worker heading to the city for the day's labor.
"Next!" The guard at the gate waved him forward.
Kael walked past, keeping his face down, his presence unremarkable. The guard's spiritual sense brushed against him—awareness without alarm, routine without suspicion. He was through.
The Veiled Market waited in Thornwick's lower quarter, a maze of alleys and hidden stalls where illegal goods changed hands under the guise of legitimate commerce. Kael navigated by memory, past the gambling dens and the alchemy shops and the brothels that catered to every conceivable taste.
Lyra's safehouse sat at the market's heart, disguised as a boarding house for traveling workers. Kael knocked the coded pattern on the back door, waited through the tense silence, and finally heard the lock click.
The woman who opened the door looked nothing like he expected. Young, maybe twenty-five, with sharp features and darker skin weathered by hard living. Her eyes were the color of winter ice, and they assessed Kael with a predator's calculation.
"Mordecai sent me," she said. Not a question.
"He did."
"And you're the one who walked out of Blackgate."
"News travels fast."
Lyra smiled, the expression sharp and dangerous. "News about a Soul Reaver always travels fast. The question is whether you're here to cause trouble or to make a deal."
Kael stepped inside, letting the door close behind him. The room beyond was cramped, cluttered with the debris of transient lives—unwashed dishes, rumpled bedding, weapons hidden in obvious places. But at its center stood a table, and at the table sat a man Kael had hoped never to see again.
Dorian Ashborn. His brother. Alive.
"Kael." Dorian rose, his face a mask of careful neutrality. "I see you survived the gallows after all."
Kael's hand went to his knife. The Soul Reaver surged with sudden, violent hunger, and for a moment he was tempted. Kill his brother here, harvest what he knew, and solve the mystery of the betrayal with a blade and a touch.
But Lyra was watching, and the room was full of people he didn't know, and killing his brother in a safehouse full of witnesses would cause exactly the kind of trouble that made his life difficult.
"You're supposed to be dead," Kael said instead.
"I'm supposed to be a lot of things." Dorian's smile held no warmth. "But right now, I'm the man who knows why Zarek framed you for murder. Why he ordered Gwen's squad to die. And why someone inside the Orders is working to destroy the seal network from within."
Kael's grip on the knife tightened. "Talk."
"Sit down, brother. This is going to take a while."
And as the morning light crept through the shutters, casting long shadows across the room, Kael listened to his brother's tale of conspiracy and betrayal. The story of how Zarek had risen through the Orders' ranks on a foundation of murder and manipulation. The truth about Gwen's squad, who had discovered something they shouldn't have and paid the price. The connection between Hollow Reach and the seventy-two sealed rifts.
The truth about the Whisper King.
"The cult isn't trying to unseal the rifts," Dorian concluded. "They're trying to redirect the seal network's energy. Hollow Reach is a drain, yes, but it's also a conduit. Someone is funneling power from the seals into something else. Something that will make them unstoppable when the time comes."
"Who?"
"That's what we need to find out." Dorian leaned forward, eyes burning with intensity. "And you're going to help us. The Soul Reaver ability—it's the key. You can harvest from the cultists, learn their secrets, uncover their plans. Together, we can stop them."
Kael considered. His brother had betrayed him once, had stood by while Zarek orchestrated his execution. Trusting him again was foolish. But Dorian had information, resources, connections. And the enemies they faced were the same enemies Kael had sworn to destroy.
"First," he said slowly, "you tell me everything about the Whisper King. Who he is. Where he came from. What he wants."
Dorian hesitated. The silence stretched, pregnant with unspoken fears.
"The Whisper King," he finally said, "isn't a cultist. He's something worse." Another pause. "He's a Soul Reaver. The first one. The original. And he's been manipulating the cult from behind the scenes for decades, maybe centuries."
The world tilted beneath Kael's feet. The first Soul Reaver. The man who had created the gift that now burned in Kael's chest. Alive. Active. Working toward goals that remained terrifyingly unclear.
"Where is he?"
"We don't know." Dorian's admission came reluctantly, the words dragged from him against his will. "But we know he's coming. The cult's operations have accelerated since your escape from the gallows. Something has changed. Something that has to do with you."
Kael absorbed this, mind racing through implications. The Soul Reaver's origin, finally within reach. The Whisper King, connected to everything. The seventy-two rifts, dying while Hollow Reach fed.
And somewhere out there, Zarek, still pulling strings, still orchestrating deaths.
"I want him," Kael said flatly. "Zarek. I want to be the one who kills him."
"You will be." Lyra's voice cut through the tension. "But first, we need to deal with the cult. Zarek is just a piece on the board. The Whisper King is the one who moves the pieces."
A knock at the door shattered the moment. Sharp, urgent, the pattern for genuine alarm.
Lyra moved, pulling the door open to reveal a young man gasping for breath. "Report."
"Cult activity," the messenger managed between gulps of air. "The Hollow Reach. Something's happening. The Abyssal presence is spreading. They're saying—" He looked at Kael with eyes full of fear. "They're saying it's because of him. Because he harvested from the execution ground."
Kael felt the color drain from his face. The rift had sensed him. Had tasted his harvest. And now, because of him, it was growing stronger.
"How long?" he demanded.
"Days. Maybe hours. The seal network is destabilizing. If Hollow Reach goes critical—"
"We need to move." Lyra was already grabbing weapons, packing supplies. "Everyone. Now. If the seal breaks at Hollow Reach, it could cascade into the entire network. Seventy-two rifts, all opening at once."
Seventy-two rifts. Seventy-two Entities. A world consumed by the Abyss.
