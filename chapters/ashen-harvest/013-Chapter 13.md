The Gutter Ghoul lunged, claws scraping stone where Kael's skull had been a heartbeat before.
Kael rolled left, his grabbing a rusted pipe from the debris. The creature's shriek split the air—a wet, bubbling sound that echoed through the collapsed tunnel. Three other shapes scrambled behind it, their eyes glowing sickly green in the darkness.
"You picked the wrong tunnel, corpse-roach," Kael spat, though his heart hammered against his ribs.
He'd been tracking the cultists for two days. Zarek's name burned in his mind like a brand. But this wasn't the ambush he'd expected. This was something older, hungrier, twisted by proximity to the Abyss.
The lead Ghoul crouched low, its spine bending at wrong angles. Dried blood caked its jaw. It remembered being human once. That made it worse.
"Stay focused," Kael muttered, forcing his breathing steady.
A notification pulsed at the edge of his vision:
[Soul Sense: 4 Abyssal-touched corpses detected. Distance: immediate. Threat level: Moderate-High.]
Four of them. Awakened-level threats, maybe stronger. The pipe felt flimsy in his grip.
Then footsteps echoed from deeper in the tunnel. Heavy boots on broken glass.
"Impressive reflexes, boy."
The voice came from shadows pooling near a collapsed archway. A figure emerged—tall, gaunt, dressed in a long coat patched with metal plates. His face was all angles and scar tissue, one eye replaced with a brass mechanism that clicked as it focused on Kael.
"Edmund Steelworth," the man said, as if introducing himself to a business partner. "I've been watching your work. The Ashford Warehouse. The Docks. Even that mess at the cathedral steps." He smiled, revealing teeth filed to points. "Magnificent, really. Most Reavers your age would've been caught by now."
Kael tightened his grip on the pipe. "Who are you?"
"I told you. Edmund Steelworth. Bone Pit master, soul trader, and—" he paused, savoring the words, "—your potential employer."
The Ghouls hadn't attacked. They crouched behind Steelworth like loyal hounds, waiting for a command. Kael understood now. These were his creatures. Tamed, or at least leased.
"The Bone Pit," Kael repeated. The name meant nothing to him yet, but Steelworth's presence here wasn't coincidence. He'd been herded.
"Follow me, and I'll explain everything." Steelworth turned toward the darkness. "Or stay here and die with your new friends. Your choice."
One of the Ghouls hissed. Kael's mind raced through options. Fight meant risking serious injury or death. Follow meant walking into unknown territory with a stranger who clearly knew too much about him.
Steelworth glanced back over his shoulder. "The Whisper King wants you dead, boy. I'm offering an alternative. Take it or don't."
The Whisper King. Kael's blood chilled. That name surfaced in fragments—the cult that killed Gwen's squad, the shadow organization pulling Zarek's strings, the whispered fear among even powerful Awakened.
"What do you want?" Kael asked.
"What everyone wants. Your talent." Steelworth's brass eye whirred. "The Bone Pit processes souls the Orders would destroy. We need someone who can extract abilities without the usual... Degradation. You'll be paid in soul-essence. Good soul-essence, not the watered-down garbage in the Veiled Market."
Kael had heard of the Bone Pit. Street-level rumors—bodies that went missing from the Mortician's Guild, Awakened who vanished after taking contracts they shouldn't. Some called it a slaughterhouse for the spiritually gifted. Others claimed it was a black-market bazaar where anything could be bought or sold.
"Why me?"
"Because you're a Reaver who hasn't been caught yet. That makes you either incredibly skilled or incredibly lucky." Steelworth spread his hands. "Either way, you're useful."
The Ghouls shifted restlessly. Kael could feel their hunger pressing against his senses, animal urges barely leashed. Steelworth controlled them —bound them with soul-essence, maybe, or some other dark contract.
"Five minutes," Kael said. "You explain. Then I decide."
Steelworth laughed—a dry, rattling sound. "Efficient. I like that."
He walked deeper into the tunnel, expecting Kael to follow. After a moment's hesitation, Kael did.
---
The Bone Pit sprawled beneath Thornwick's industrial district like a wound in the earth. Kael descended through a series of old mining shafts, each level revealing new horrors. Industrial furnaces burned with pale blue flame, consuming flesh and bone alike. Assembly lines of corpse-workers shuffled buckets of rendered remains to waiting vats. The air tasted of copper and smoke and something sweeter—soul-essence, freshly harvested.
"This way," Steelworth said, leading him past a row of iron doors. Each door had a viewing slot, and through them Kael glimpsed cells packed with people—prisoners, slaves, or customers, he couldn't tell. Some wore the ragged clothes of commoners. Others bore the tattoos of Awakened, branded with binding runes.
"Workers," Steelworth explained, noticing Kael's gaze. "The most valuable assets in this business. We harvest their labor, then their essence when they're depleted. Efficient."
"You're a monster," Kael said flatly.
"I'm practical. The world creates endless supply. We utilize it." Steelworth stopped before a heavy door marked with a stylized skull and crossed bones. "But you're not here to judge my operation. You're here because the alternative is death."
He opened the door.
Beyond lay a vast chamber, its ceiling lost in smoke and shadow. Piles of corpses stacked against the walls—hundreds, maybe thousands, in various states of decay. Most were fresh, hours old at most. Others were ancient, their bones bleached white and brittle.
"The Bone Pit's heart," Steelworth announced. "Every corpse in Thornwick eventually ends up here. The Orders think they control the soul trade, but they only get the scraps. We get the rest."
Kael stepped inside, his footsteps echoing in the sudden silence. The weight of so many deaths pressed against his chest. He'd harvested before—Gutters, cultists, enemies who'd tried to kill him—but never like this. Never in such quantities.
"You're impressed," Steelworth said. It wasn't a question.
"I'm horrified."
"Same thing, in this business."
He led Kael to a central platform where a single corpse lay on a stone slab. The body was fresh—a young woman, throat slit, eyes open and staring. Her spiritual energy still clung to her flesh, fading but detectable.
"Your test," Steelworth said. "Harvest her."
Kael hesitated. He'd never harvested in front of witnesses. The Soul Reaver was his secret, his weapon, his curse. Revealing it meant losing control over how others perceived him.
"Reave her," Steelworth repeated, "or walk back into that tunnel and face my Ghouls alone."
No choice. Kael knelt beside the slab and placed his palm on the corpse's forehead. The contact sent familiar shivers through his nerves—not pain, exactly, but a deep wrongness, as if his soul was reaching into territory it wasn't meant to touch.
He pulled.
The harvest took only seconds. Spiritual energy flooded through him, carrying fragments of the dead woman's essence—her final thoughts, her fading skills, her memories of pain and fear and desperate hope. Kael absorbed what he could use, let the rest dissipate.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 15% spiritual energy from the remains. Skill acquired: Basic Blade Training. Memory fragment acquired: Last moments of life.]
Steelworth watched with narrowed eyes. "Impressive. No ceremony, no ritual. Just raw extraction." He nodded slowly. "I've seen Order-trained harvesters take five minutes for the same yield. You did it in seconds."
Kael stood, wiping his hand on his coat. "Your point?"
"My point is that you're exactly what I need." Steelworth smiled that terrible smile again. "The Bone Pit needs a Reaver who can extract abilities without destroying the source. The Orders' harvesters are thorough but wasteful—they burn out the soul entirely. You preserve enough to keep the corpse... Functional."
Kael glanced at the woman on the slab. Her body still lay there, eyes still open. But something had changed. A faint spark of animation flickered in her chest—barely visible, easily missed.
"She's not dead?"
"Not entirely. Your harvest left enough essence to maintain basic functions. She'll be my worker now—obedient, tireless, and completely unaware of her own existence." Steelworth shrugged. "The Orders call it desecration. I call it efficiency."
Horror crawled up Kael's spine. This man animated corpses for labor, harvested them for profit, and disposed of the remains in industrial furnaces. And he'd just demonstrated that Kael's ability could be used for the same purposes.
"I won't help you," Kael said.
"You already have." Steelworth gestured at the woman's still-breathing body. "That harvest proved your capability. Now we negotiate terms."
"Negotiate?" Kael's laugh came out harsh. "You dragged me here at Ghoul-point. That's not negotiation."
"It's the beginning of one. You have leverage—I need your abilities. But I also have resources you need." Steelworth leaned against a pillar. "Information about the Whisper King. About Zarek's cult. About who really ordered Gwen's squad into that ambush."
Kael went still. "What do you know about that?"
"I know the official story is a lie. I know your friend Zarek wasn't acting alone—he's a puppet, same as the cultists who died in that fight. And I know the true mastermind has connections all the way to the Council of Six." Steelworth's brass eye gleamed. "Information like that doesn't come free. But it could be yours, if you work with me."
The chamber seemed to contract around Kael. Steelworth offered knowledge he desperately wanted—answers about Gwen's death, about Zarek's betrayal, about the conspiracy that had framed him for murder. But the price was becoming something Steelworth could use.
"What's the alternative?" Kael asked quietly.
Steelworth smiled. "I tell the Whisper King exactly where you are. Ghouls everywhere, bounty hunters on every street, and every Order very interested in your head." He spread his hands. "Your choice, Reaver. Work with me, or die hunted by everyone who matters."
Kael's mind worked through the options. Steelworth controlled this territory, had resources and information Kael needed. But working for him meant compromising everything—becoming complicit in horrors Kael had only glimpsed.
A commotion echoed from deeper in the Bone Pit. Shouting, the clash of metal, and then a scream that cut off abruptly.
Steelworth frowned. "That shouldn't be happening."
The Ghouls Kael had seen earlier scrambled past, heading toward the disturbance. Steelworth's face darkened.
"Stay here," he ordered, then disappeared into the smoke.
Kael didn't listen. He followed.
---
The source of the chaos lay in a secondary chamber filled with processing vats. Three of Steelworth's workers had been torn apart—their bodies ripped open, organs scattered across the floor. And standing in the center of the carnage was a creature unlike any Kael had seen.
It resembled a Ghoul at first glance—humanoid, hunched, limbs too long for its torso. But where the others had sickly green eyes, this one's eyes burned with pure black. Its skin wasn't gray but deep purple, mottled with lesions that pulsed with visible spiritual energy. And its presence... Kael felt it like a pressure against his soul, heavy and corrupting.
A Mutant Ghoul. Ghouls corrupted by prolonged exposure to concentrated soul-essence. Steelworth's efficient processing had created something far worse than natural Abyssal creatures.
More of Steelworth's workers arrived with weapons—cleavers, hammers, chains. They charged the Mutant with desperate courage.
The creature moved like liquid. One swipe of its arm sent three workers flying into the vats, their screams cut short. Another motion bisected a fourth, entrails splattering across the stone floor.
Kael hung back, assessing. The Mutant was strong—Awakened-level at minimum, maybe stronger. Its corrupted body absorbed damage that would kill normal creatures. And it was hungry, feeding on the essence of its victims even as it killed.
Steelworth appeared beside Kael, his expression grim. "My containment failed. This thing was supposed to be locked in the lower levels."
"Can you stop it?"
"Not without losing too many workers. It's beyond what my usual methods can handle." Steelworth glanced at Kael. "But you might."
"You want me to Reave it?"
"I want you to kill it. How you do that is your concern."
The Mutant finished consuming its latest victim. It turned toward the remaining workers, who scrambled backward in terror. In moments, it would slaughter them all.
Kael's mind raced. Reaving the Mutant would mean touching it, getting close enough to establish contact. That meant entering combat range with a creature that could tear him apart.
But if he didn't act, people would die.
Steelworth's voice echoed in his memory: "Every Reaver eventually faces this choice. Use your gift for survival, or watch others die because you refused."
Kael grabbed a fallen worker's cleaver and stepped forward.
The Mutant noticed. Its black eyes fixed on him, and it made a sound—a low, gurgling chuckle that raised every hair on Kael's body.
"Good," Kael muttered. "Look at me."
He didn't wait for it to charge. He ran toward it instead, cleaver raised.
The Mutant's arm swept horizontally. Kael ducked under it, felt the wind of its passage against his scalp. He swung the cleaver at its knee.
The blade bit deep. Corrupted blood sprayed across Kael's face. The creature shrieked—pain, or maybe surprise.
Kael pressed the attack. The Mutant was strong but uncoordinated, driven by hunger than skill. Each strike Kael landed drew more of its black blood, weakened its movements incrementally.
A claw raked his shoulder. Fire blazed through his nerves. Kael staggered but didn't fall. He drove the cleaver into the Mutant's thigh, twisting, tearing muscle from bone.
The creature collapsed on one knee.
Kael lunged for its throat. His free hand found purchase in the lesions covering its neck. The Mutant thrashed, trying to dislodge him. Its claws carved furrows in his back, but Kael held on, forcing his palm against the pulse of corrupted essence beneath its skin.
He Reaved.
The harvest was nothing like the woman on the slab. This was aggressive, hostile, consuming. The Mutant's soul fought back, trying to devour Kael in return. Dark images flooded his mind—endless hunger, the agony of transformation, memories of a human life erased by corruption.
Kael pushed through. His will asserted itself, tearing the Mutant's essence apart and pulling fragments into his own soul. The creature's resistance crumbled. Its body went limp.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 40% spiritual energy from the remains. Ability acquired: Abyssal Resilience (Passive). Soul fragment acquired: Echo of the Forgotten.]
Kael dropped from the Mutant's corpse, landing hard on the stone floor. His body screamed with accumulated pain—deep cuts on his back, his shoulder, his arms. Blood soaked his clothes.
But he was alive. And the Mutant was dead.
Steelworth's workers stared at him. Some knelt, whether in worship or terror Kael couldn't tell. Steelworth himself approached slowly, his brass eye clicking as it examined the Mutant's remains.
"Abyssal Resilience," he murmured. "That's a powerful passive. You'll take less damage from Abyssal sources—Ghouls, cultist magic, even minor Entities." He looked at Kael with something like respect. "And the Echo gives you a fragment of the original soul. Might contain useful information."
Kael tried to stand. His legs buckled. Steelworth caught him before he fell.
"Easy," the Bone Pit master said. "You're injured. Let my workers tend to you."
"What... What happens now?" Kael's voice came out hoarse.
"Now we talk terms." Steelworth guided him toward the door. "You just killed something that would've cost me dozens of workers. That's worth something. More importantly, you proved you can handle threats above your current level."
Kael's vision blurred. Exhaustion crashed over him—the cost of Reaving a corrupted creature, fighting while wounded, pushing his limits.
"The Echo," he managed. "I saw... Something. Memories."
"Tell me later. Rest first." Steelworth deposited him on a stone bench near the flowing vats. "You can refuse my offer when you're not bleeding out."
Workers swarmed around Kael, applying salves and bandages. The sting of their treatments barely registered against the deeper pain of his wounds.
As darkness crept into his vision, Kael replayed what he'd felt during the harvest. The Mutant's memories had included fragments of the woman it used to be—her name, her life, the day corruption claimed her. She'd been a factory worker before the Bone Pit processed her. Steelworth's own operation had created the creature that killed her.
And in the deeper layers of that fragmented soul, something else flickered. A sense of being watched. A whisper in a language that predated human speech.
The Whisper King. The Mutant had been near something—a chamber, a ritual, a presence. Something that connected it to the conspiracy Kael was only beginning to understand.
Steelworth knew more than he was saying. That much was clear. But Kael needed him alive, at least for now. The information about Gwen's squad, about Zarek, about the true enemy—it was worth tolerating this monster a little longer.
A worker finished wrapping Kael's torso. "You'll live," she said without emotion. "Master Steelworth wants to see you when you wake."
Kael's eyes closed. The last thing he heard was the distant sound of more workers arriving, more corpses being processed, more souls being fed into Steelworth's endless machine.
When he woke, the choices would return. Fight or flee. Compromise or die.
But for now, in the darkness between consciousness and oblivion, Kael allowed himself a moment of grim satisfaction. He'd survived. He'd harvested something powerful. And he'd taken the first step toward answers that had haunted him since Gwen's death.
The Bone Pit had been meant to break him. Instead, it had shown him how much stronger he'd become.
He drifted toward sleep, the Mutant's Echo pulsing faintly in his mind—fragments of a woman's final terror, a clue that might unravel everything.
Somewhere above, in Thornwick's gaslit streets, the conspiracy that had destroyed his life continued to move. Zarek plotted in shadow. The Whisper King whispered through cracked seals. The cult prepared its next atrocity.
But Kael Ashborn was no longer the frightened fugitive they'd framed for murder. He was something more—something the Bone Pit had forged in blood and desperation.
And when he woke, he would make them all pay.
Footsteps echoed in the darkness beyond his cell. Heavy, deliberate, accompanied by the clink of armored plates. The guards were changing shift.
Or someone else was coming.
Kael's eyes opened. His body ached, but the Abyssal Resilience pulsed through his veins, numbing the worst of his wounds. He sat up slowly, watching the iron door.
The footsteps stopped outside.
A key scraped in the lock.
Who had come for him now?
