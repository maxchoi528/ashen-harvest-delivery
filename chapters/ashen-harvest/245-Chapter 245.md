"Surrender the corpse or watch your throat open."
The voice came from the shadow behind the gaslight. Kael counted three heartbeats before answering, his fingers already tracing the cold edge of his blade's pommel. The fog rolling off the Thornwick road carried the stench of copper and wet ash. His thumb found the leather wrapping on the grip. Rough. Familiar. The damp air clung to his skin, seeping through his coat, settling into his bones with the kind of cold that had nothing to do with temperature.
"For a man threatening murder, you lot are remarkably confident," Kael called back. "Step into the light. Let me see who's brave enough to threaten a traveler on the Thornwick road."
Torchlight flared. Five figures materialized from the mist—cultists by their robes, marked with the inverted sigil of the Veil. The lead man carried a curved blade still wet with something dark. Kael's nostrils flared. Blood. Fresh enough that it hadn't begun to oxidize. The robes themselves were threadbare at the hems, stained with graveyard mud, and they moved with the stiff caution of men concealing additional weapons beneath the fabric.
"We're not interested in you, outsider." He gestured toward the crumpled form twenty paces ahead. "Only what you were doing with the body."
Kael's gaze flickered to the corpse. A woman, middle-aged, wearing the tattered remains of a Mortician's Guild uniform. The fabric had been torn at the collar, and one of her boots was missing—ripped away during whatever struggle had preceded her death. Her death couldn't have been more than an hour old. Rigor hadn't set in. The blood pooling beneath her was still spreading, dark and glossy in the torchlight. The cultists had followed him here, waiting for the moment of harvest.
"Private property," Kael said. "That corpse belongs to no one but me."
The cultist leader laughed. "Soul Reaver. We knew Thornwick had sent a hunter. The Shadow Court pays well for your kind—alive, preferably. Dead men can't harvest."
Kael drew his blade. The steel sang against the sheath, a thin note swallowed by wind. The fog curled around his legs like something alive, and somewhere in the distance, a gaslamp sputtered and died, its glass cracking in the cold.
"Only one of you is walking away from this," he said.
The first cultist lunged. Kael sidestepped, letting the blade whistle past his shoulder—close enough that he felt the displaced air against his neck—then drove his pommel into the man's temple. Bone cracked. The cultist dropped. His skull made a wet sound against the cobblestones, and his body twitched once before going still.
Two more came together, flanking. Kael spun low, cutting across the nearest man's hamstring. Blood sprayed in an arc that caught the torchlight, warm against Kael's knuckles. The cultist screamed and fell, clawing at the wound with both hands. His companion hesitated—just a breath—and Kael closed the distance, driving his blade through the man's sternum. Steel scraped bone. The cultist's mouth opened, but no sound came. Just a wet gurgle as blood filled his throat.
The leader stepped forward, his curved blade weaving patterns in the air. Spiritual energy gathered around him—stage two, maybe stage three. Strong enough to be dangerous. The air around him shimmered, warping the torchlight into halos that bent the wrong direction.
"The Shadow Court warned us you'd be skilled." He spat on the ground. "They also told us Soul Reavers have no spiritual reserves of their own. Everything you have comes from the dead."
He was right. Kael's reserves were empty after a week of running, hiding, and harvesting scraps from battlefield remnants. The dregs of those harvests sat in his veins like sediment—enough to keep his body functioning past human limits, but not enough to fuel anything that would matter against a stage-three opponent. He'd hoped to change that with the Mortician's corpse, but the cultists had arrived too soon.
"That means you can run forever," the cultist continued, "but fight until you're cornered. Then you die like any other man."
The remaining two cultists spread wider, cutting off escape routes. Kael backed toward the corpse, feeling its residual warmth through his boots. The woman's hand lay splayed on the cobblestones, fingers curled inward, and he could feel the faintest pulse of unspent spiritual energy radiating from her cooling flesh. A harvest waiting to happen.
"Three against one." He let his blade drop slightly. "I like those odds better than one against five."
The leader's confidence flickered. His jaw tightened. The two flanking cultists exchanged a glance.
"Kill him."
They charged.
Kael moved.
His body remembered what his mind hadn't consciously chosen. The Soul Reaver ability woke in his blood, reaching toward the cooling flesh behind him. Touch—that was all it required. Contact with the dead.
Kael dropped.
His hand slammed against the Mortician's wrist at the same moment steel whistled overhead. A blade shaved hair from his scalp. Another grazed his shoulder, parting fabric and drawing a thin line of fire across his skin. He ignored it. The harvest began.
Cold flooded through his palm. The woman's final moments played across his consciousness—terror, pain, a face leaning over her, hooded and speaking words in a language that hurt to remember. Each syllable pressed against his temples like a blade. He tasted copper at the back of his throat. Then nothing. The soul gone, but the skills remained, burned into the body's spiritual residue.
Kael's eyes snapped open. The woman's memories showed him what she'd seen before dying—a chamber beneath Thornwick's old cemetery, dozens of robed figures, and a rift in the Veil that pulsed with sickly light. The image burned behind his eyes. The rift. Not just a crack in reality, but something being prepared. Something being opened.
"Impossible," the leader breathed, seeing Kael rise with his blade already moving.
The first cultist's head left his shoulders before the man realized he'd been targeted. Blood fountained from the stump in rhythmic pulses. The second managed to raise his weapon; Kael deflected, swept his legs, and opened his throat in a single motion. Hot arterial blood splashed across Kael's forearm.
Two bodies hit the ground.
The leader ran.
Kael let him. The memory fragment was worth more than another kill. He watched the cultist disappear into the mist, his footsteps fading, then turned back to finish the harvest.
Kael knelt beside the Mortician's body, closing her eyes with his free hand. Her skin had cooled. The warmth he'd felt through his boots was already fading. Whoever she'd been, she'd died with knowledge worth killing for. He'd make sure that death meant something.
The underground passage descended in a spiral, carved from living rock and lit by phosphorescent fungi that cast everything in pale green. The walls wept moisture that tasted of mineral and old earth. Kael moved in silence, his newly restored reserves humming beneath his skin. Each step took him deeper. The air thickened. The green light shifted and pulsed, painting his hands in colors that had no name in the waking world.
The memory fragment showed the way. Three hundred paces past the old cemetery gates, a false wall hiding stairs that led to the cult's meeting place. The Mortician had discovered it by accident—following a supply shipment that never arrived at its official destination. She'd been curious. She'd been brave. She'd been killed for it.
Now she was dead, and Kael had her secrets.
He emerged into a corridor lined with iron doors. The doors were riveted, heavy, old—pre-Abyssal construction, the kind of craftsmanship that hadn't been practiced since the Wars. Voices echoed from somewhere ahead, chanting in that same horrible language. The air tasted of copper and ozone, the signature of a rift bleeding spiritual energy into the Material. His teeth ached with it. His reserves pulsed in response, reactive to the ambient energy like a wound reacting to salt.
The cult was more organized than he'd assumed. Not random worshippers dabbling in forbidden arts, but a structured operation with resources, planning, and purpose. The iron doors were labeled with symbols he didn't recognize. Supply caches. Living quarters. Training rooms. Each one sealed with wards that made his skin crawl.
The Six Orders needed to know. Even the ones who hunted him, the ones who'd executed him for crimes he hadn't committed. This was bigger than personal vendettas or territorial disputes. This was bigger than him.
The corridor opened into a vast chamber. Pillars carved with inverted seals supported a ceiling lost in shadow. At the center, a circular platform descended into a pit where reality itself seemed thin, a membrane stretched over something vast and hungry. The rift pulsed. Each pulse pushed against his spiritual senses like a physical blow, and the light it cast was wrong. It illuminated without warming. It revealed without brightening.
Rifts. Seventy-two of them, sealed after the Abyssal Wars, maintaining the boundary between worlds. The foundation of the entire defense network. Every child learned their significance. Every Awakened understood what they meant.
And this one was opening.
Figures in dark robes surrounded the platform, their chanting building toward a crescendo. Kael counted thirty, maybe forty. At the chamber's far end, a raised dais held a throne occupied by a shadow that moved wrong, that suggested shapes beyond the shape it wore. His eyes refused to focus on it. Each time he tried, the outline blurred, shifting between something humanoid and something that had never been human.
The Whisper King. The cult's leader, the one the Shadow Court feared and the Orders hunted. Kael had heard the name whispered in every port city, every back-alley market, every place where the desperate gathered to trade in forbidden knowledge.
"You came faster than expected."
The voice echoed despite the Whisper King not moving his lips. It came from everywhere, from the shadows themselves. It resonated in his chest cavity, in the spaces between his ribs.
"The Mortician's corpse was meant to be found. Her memory was meant to be harvested." The shadow tilted its head. "Soul Reavers always take the bait when a fresh death is involved. It's practically your defining weakness."
Kael's hand tightened on his blade. "You knew I'd come."
"We know many things." The Whisper King rose from the throne, and for a moment his outline shifted, showing something ancient and terrible beneath the human shape. The temperature dropped. Kael's breath crystallized. "We know the Shadow Court wants you dead. We know the Orders want you captured. We know you have no allies, no resources, and no reason to be here except curiosity and the hunger that drives your kind."
"I have plenty of reasons."
"Name one."
Kael stepped forward, letting his blade catch the phosphorescent light. "You're unsealing a rift. That affects everyone."
"Ah." The shadow seemed to smile. "Idealism. How quaint."
"It's practical. If that rift opens, the Entities come through. The Veil weakens. The whole seal network destabilizes. Every Awakened on the continent loses their advantage."
"And you think you're here to stop it?" The Whisper King laughed, a sound like wind through dead leaves. "You're one man, Soul Reaver. One stage-three Transcendent with no allies and a power that makes you a target for every faction on the continent. What exactly do you intend to do against an army of devoted cultists and a rift that's already sixty percent unsealed?"
Kael assessed the chamber. The cultists had stopped chanting, turning to face him. Thirty pairs of eyes, all showing the same flat devotion. Fanatics who'd given themselves to something beyond human comprehension. Their spiritual energy signatures blended together, a collective hum of submission and surrendered will.
"I'm not here to fight an army," he said. "I'm here to harvest a king."
The Whisper King hesitated. Just for a moment, but Kael caught it—the first genuine uncertainty he'd seen since entering the chamber. The shadow around the throne flickered. The ambient pressure in the room shifted.
"You don't know what I am."
"I know you're not human anymore. Whatever you were, the Abyss changed you. Made you something else." Kael's eyes swept the cultists again. "When you die, all that accumulated power has to go somewhere. Just like everyone else."
"You would have to kill me first."
"I've killed worse."
The cultists moved. Thirty fanatics charging across the chamber, weapons drawn, spiritual energy flaring in a dozen different colors. The Whisper King retreated to his throne, watching with that terrible patient amusement. The floor trembled beneath their collective momentum.
Kael met them in the center of the room.
His blade became a wheel of light, carving through the first wave. Bodies fell. Blood painted the ancient stones. The first cultist died with his weapon half-raised, Kael's steel parting his ribs. The second took a throat cut that sprayed warm crimson across Kael's face. He blinked it away. The cultists didn't retreat, didn't waver—death meant nothing to them when the promise of transcendence waited on the other side.
But they were still human. Still mortal. Still bound by bodies that could only take so much damage.
Kael harvested as he fought, pulling essence and skill fragments from every corpse that touched the ground. The power flowed through him like fire, warming muscles that had grown cold, sharpening senses that had dulled. A skill here—Blade Apprentice, barely worth the name. A memory there—fragments of training, of devotion, of the moment each cultist had surrendered their will to something wearing a king's shadow.
The harvest slowed the cultists' advance. Some fell to their knees, unable to process what they were witnessing. A Soul Reaver in full harvest mode was a horrifying thing to watch—the body moving, the soul departing, the green glow of harvested essence flowing into the predator's veins. One cultist screamed. Another dropped his weapon and ran. A third pressed his hands over his eyes, as if not seeing would make it stop.
But there were always more. Always the truly devoted, the ones who'd burned away enough humanity to feel no fear. They pressed forward, trading their lives for inches of ground. Kael's blade never stopped. Steel rang against steel. Bone splintered. Blood pooled in the carved channels of the ancient floor.
Kael's reserves swelled. Enough to fuel abilities he'd never tried, techniques he'd only read about in stolen texts.
Enough to fight a king.
He broke through the cultist line and ran for the throne.
The Whisper King rose to meet him.
Their clash shook the chamber. Spiritual energy detonated in waves, cracking pillars and scattering the remaining cultists. The Whisper King's form shifted between human and something else—a writhing mass of shadows and stolen faces, voices crying out in languages that predated human speech. Each face held an expression of frozen agony. Each voice carried words that scraped against Kael's consciousness.
"You should not have come here," the entity said. "This rift is the first. The beginning of the end for your world."
"The Orders will stop you."
"The Orders are already compromised." The Whisper King's blade—carved from crystallized shadow—parried Kael's steel. The impact sent vibrations up Kael's arm, into his shoulder, through his teeth. "Did you think the Shadow Court operates without their knowledge? Every major faction on this continent has representatives in our ranks. The conspiracy runs deeper than you can imagine."
Kael's mind raced. The Six Orders, the organization meant to protect humanity. The Council of Six, meeting annually in Thornwick. All of it potentially corrupted, potentially part of the plan to unseal the rifts.
But he couldn't think about that now. He had to survive the next thirty seconds.
"Then I'll stop you myself."
He let the Soul Reaver ability surge, drawing on every fragment he'd harvested. The woman's memories. The cultists' skills. The accumulated essence of thirty deaths.
Something clicked into place.
The world went pale. Kael's body became translucent, fading into the space between Material and Abyss. The Whisper King's blade passed through him without touching flesh. Cold washed through him. The kind of cold that existed between worlds.
"What—" The entity's confidence shattered. "That's not possible. You're stage three. That technique requires—"
"I'm a Soul Reaver." Kael's voice came from everywhere and nowhere. "Everything's possible when you're willing to take it from the dead."
He solidified behind the Whisper King, blade already descending.
The entity twisted, taking the blow on its shoulder instead of its neck. Shadow-blood sprayed across the ancient stones. The Whisper King screamed—a sound no human throat could produce—and teleported across the chamber. The shriek left Kael's ears ringing.
"You're not ready," it hissed. "This rift opens regardless of what you do. The timeline accelerates. By dawn, every major city on the continent will know that the Sealed Era has ended."
"Then I'll be ready by dawn."
Kael pursued, but the Whisper King was already fading, its form dissolving into the darkness that filled the chamber's corners. The cultists had fled or died. The rift continued its slow unsealing, the membrane between worlds growing thinner.
"The Soul Reaver who started this," the fading voice said. "Do you know what happened to him? Do you know why the first of your kind disappeared?"
Kael stopped. "Tell me."
But the shadow was gone, leaving only silence and the rhythmic pulse of the rift below.
He stood alone in the ruined chamber, surrounded by corpses and the fading echoes of spiritual warfare. The rift's light painted his face in sickly colors—the signature of something vast pressing against the boundary of reality. His muscles burned. A gash across his ribs wept blood into his shirt. His reserves churned, unstable from the volume of essence he'd processed in such a short window.
Kael lowered his blade. His hands trembled—not from exhaustion, but from something he'd heard in the Whisper King's final words. The first Soul Reaver. The origin of his power. The mystery that had haunted him since awakening on the execution platform. His jaw clenched. His knuckles whitened around the grip.
The cult knew something. The Shadow Court knew something. Every faction in this broken world had pieces of a puzzle he couldn't yet see.
But he had time now. Resources. A new ability that had surprised even him.
He approached the rift, studying its pulsing light. The seal was damaged but not destroyed. The cult's ritual had weakened it, but completion would require more than one night of chanting. Days. Weeks. The membrane throbbed. Each pulse pushed a wave of that copper-ozone taste across his tongue.
Weeks he could use.
Kael turned toward the exit, stepping over bodies without looking down. Above ground, the first hints of dawn touched the horizon. By the time he reached the surface, the sun would be rising over Thornwick—a city full of enemies and allies he couldn't tell apart, full of conspiracies he was only beginning to understand.
The Whisper King had been right about one thing: the Orders were compromised. The Shadow Court hunted him. The cult accelerated their timeline.
And somewhere in the shadows, someone was watching to see what he would do next.
Kael emerged from the passage into cold morning air. The cemetery stretched before him, ancient stones marking ancient graves. Dew had settled on the grass, soaking through his boots. The mist had thinned but not vanished, clinging to the headstones. He had one new ability, four thousand units of spiritual essence, and more questions than answers.
But he also had something else: a trail to follow, a conspiracy to unravel, and a timeline that wouldn't wait for him to be ready.
The rift opened in several hours. The cult would resume their ritual. The Whisper King would return with new power, new followers, new plans.
And somewhere in Thornwick, the Shadow Court was already moving.
Kael Ashborn walked into the morning light, alone and hunted and carrying secrets he'd harvested from the dead. The war for the Sealed Rifts had begun.
The question was whether he'd survive long enough to fight it.
Footsteps echoed behind him. Not one set—three. Maybe four. They moved with the careful precision of professionals, closing distance without announcing themselves. The gravel crunched beneath their boots. Measured. Deliberate. Unhurried.
Kael's hand drifted toward his blade.
They'd found him already.
