Kael pressed his back against the rusted iron pillar, the metal biting cold through his coat. Below, in the factory district's abandoned foundry, torchlight flickered against graffiti-scarred walls. Three figures in black robes knelt around a chalk circle, their chanting a low drone that set his teeth on edge.
He counted the bodies.
Seven cultists total. Two by the eastern exit. One on the catwalk above. Three in the circle. And their leader, the one they'd been tracking for three weeks, standing at the circle's center with arms raised toward a symbol that seemed to drink the light.
The Abyssal glyph pulsed with sickly violet radiance.
Kael's fingers curled around the dagger at his hip. His Soul Reaver senses stretched outward, tasting the spiritual residue in the air—corruption layered upon corruption, like rot beneath perfume. These cultists weren't amateurs. Their formation was precise. Their chanting hit frequencies that made his jaw ache.
He had maybe thirty seconds before the ritual completed.
"Lyris," he murmured into the communication crystal embedded in his collar. "They're here. Seven confirmed. Starting now."
Static crackled. Then her voice, tight with concern. "Kael, the Iron Vanguard patrol is four minutes out. You go in alone—"
"I'll be done in three."
He dropped from the ledge, landing in a crouch on the foundry floor. His movement shattered the chanting's rhythm. The robed figures spun toward him, hands flying to weapons—curved ceremonial blades that gleamed with the same violet corruption as the glyph.
The leader didn't flinch. She was older than he'd expected, gray streaking through her dark hair, her face a mask of absolute calm. "Soul Reaver." She spoke the word like a curse. "The Shadow Court warned us you might come."
Kael straightened, rolling his shoulders. "Then you know what I can do."
"We know what you *think* you can do."
He moved before she finished speaking.
The first cultist lunged with his ceremonial blade. Kael sidestepped, ramming his elbow into the man's throat. As the cultist crumpled, Kael's palm slapped against his chest, fingers splaying over the still-beating heart.
The harvest was instant. No cost. No strain. Just information flooding his mind like ice water.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted several hours of recent memories from the remains. Fragmentary combat training acquired. Spiritual corruption detected — source: Abyssal contact within last two days.]
The cultist collapsed, twitching. His companions hesitated—fear flickering behind their zealotry.
The leader's calm cracked, just slightly. "Kill him."
Two cultists rushed Kael simultaneously. He ducked under one's blade, drove his shoulder into the man's solar plexus, then pivoted to meet the second attacker. They exchanged blows—fast, brutal, muscle memory from the harvested fragment guiding his movements. Kael's fist connected with the cultist's temple. The man dropped.
Kael touched the body.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted tactical formation data from the remains. Enhanced spatial awareness acquired. Spiritual corruption moderate — extended Abyssal exposure.]
The fifth cultist came at him from the catwalk, dropping down with a barbed chain whip. Kael barely twisted away, the weapon's barbs tearing his coat sleeve. He grabbed the chain, pulled hard, and used the cultist's momentum to slam him into the nearest support beam. The impact cracked bone.
Another harvest.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted ritual components knowledge from the remains. Glyph recognition protocols updated. Spiritual corruption severe — direct Entity communion within last week.]
Six cultists down. One remained by the eastern exit, and the leader still stood at the circle's center, her chanting resuming with desperate intensity.
Kael wiped blood from a cut above his eye. "You should have run."
"And go where?" She laughed, a hollow sound. "The Court watches everywhere. My options were serve or die." Her hands moved faster, tracing complex patterns in the air. "But you—*you* have nowhere to hide either, Reaver. They know about you. They've always known."
The glyph beneath her blazed brighter.
Kael charged.
He crossed the distance in three heartbeats, ducking under a desperate slash of her blade, driving his shoulder into her midsection. They crashed against the chalk circle, scattering its precise lines. The chanting died in her throat as Kael's hand closed around her neck.
"Your memories," he said. "All of them."
Her eyes went wide. She tried to pull away, but he held her fast.
The harvest hit him like a physical blow.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 23 years of life memories from the remains. Full linguistic database acquired. Cultural knowledge expanded. Spiritual corruption critical — sustained communion with Faceless One's avatars.]
Information poured through him—decades of a life he'd never lived. Her childhood in the Thornwick slums. Her recruitment by the Shadow Court at age fourteen. The slow, patient process of corruption, each contact with the Abyss changing her a little more. Her assignment to this cell. Her instructions from handlers she'd never seen face-to-face. And beneath it all, a thread of genuine terror—the knowledge that she was a pawn in games so vast she couldn't see the board.
He saw the organization's structure. Not random cultists but a network, cells connected by dead drops and coded messages. He saw the names of other cells. He saw references to something called the "Sepulcher Initiative."
And he saw a map.
Kael's breath caught.
The map showed Thornwick's underground—the vast network of pre-war tunnels that ran beneath the city like arteries. And marked on that map, in violet ink, were seventeen locations. Seventeen points forming a pattern around the city's center.
Seventeen locations near the weakest points in the seal network.
"They're not random." His voice came out hoarse. "The attacks. The sacrifices. It's not chaos—it's mapping."
The leader slumped in his grip, her eyes glazed with the emptiness that followed complete extraction. Kael let her drop. She hit the floor and didn't move.
From the doorway came a gasp.
Kael spun. The seventh cultist—the one who'd stayed by the eastern exit—stared at him with undisguised horror. He was young, barely more than a boy, his ceremonial blade trembling in white-knuckled hands.
"Please," he whispered. "I didn't—I didn't want—"
"Run." Kael's voice was flat. "Tell whoever's listening that the Reaver knows. Tell them the pattern is broken. Tell them if they send more, I'll harvest every last one."
The boy fled. His footsteps echoed down the corridor, fading into the factory district's endless industrial groaning.
Kael stood alone in the ruined foundry, surrounded by bodies, the glyph beneath his feet still pulsing with dying light.
Lyris's voice crackled through the crystal. "Kael. The patrol just arrived. What did you find?"
He touched the communication device. "Seventeen points. They're mapping the seals. The Court is watching everything."
Silence. Then: "Get out of there. Now. I'll meet you at the safehouse."
Kael deactivated the crystal and moved toward the exit. He paused at the threshold, looking back at the symbol on the floor. The violet glow was fading, but he could still feel the Abyssal resonance—a fingerprint of something vast and hungry, pressed against the fabric of reality.
The Sealed Rifts. Seventy-two points where the membrane between worlds grew thin.
Seventeen of those points were now compromised.
Kael climbed through the window and dropped into the alley behind the foundry. Rain had started falling, cold and relentless, washing the factory district's chemical stench into the gutters. He walked quickly, pulling his coat tighter against the chill.
Thornwick stretched around him—a city of contradictions. Steam vents hissed from street grates. Gas lamps cast pools of amber light that fought against the darkness. Somewhere in the distance, a factory whistle screamed the changing of shifts.
And beneath it all, hidden from ordinary eyes, the Abyss pressed closer.

The safehouse was a basement apartment in the Iron Quarter, three streets from the Order of Dawn's local chapter house. Kael descended the narrow stairs, his boots splashing through water that had seeped through cracks in the foundation. The door at the bottom was reinforced steel—Lyris's work, she'd had it installed after the last time their location had been compromised.
He knocked. Three times, pause, twice more.
The door opened. Lyris stood in the gap, her silver hair pulled back in a severe knot, her amber eyes scanning the alley behind him before she stepped aside. She was tall, lean, dressed in the practical black clothing of someone who expected trouble.
"You're bleeding," she said.
"Scratches." He entered. The apartment was spartan—a table, two chairs, a narrow cot against the wall. A single gas lamp provided illumination. "The cultists weren't strong, but they were organized. That's new."
Lyris closed the door, engaging three separate locks. "Organized how?"
Kael moved to the table, pulling out a piece of paper and the charcoal pencil Lyris kept for exactly this purpose. He began sketching—crude but recognizable. The map from the leader's memories, simplified.
"Seventeen locations." He marked them in quick succession. "All near known seal weak points. All attacked in the last six months. Different cells, different methods, but coordinated timing." He looked up. "They're not testing the seals. They're mapping them. Learning which ones are weakest, which will fall first when they push."
Lyris stared at the map. Her expression didn't change, but Kael saw her jaw tighten. "This is the work of years. Decades, maybe. Someone has been planning this for a very long time."
"The Shadow Court."
"Maybe." She pulled out the second chair, sitting heavily. "Or maybe the Court is only one arm of something larger. The Six Orders have spent a thousand years maintaining the seals. Do you really think a secret society could coordinate something this extensive without—"
"Without inside help?" Kael finished. "No. I don't."
The implications hung between them, unspoken but understood. The Orders were supposed to be humanity's shield against the Abyss. If corruption had seeped into their ranks, if some within the Orders were actively working to weaken the seals—
The war would be over before it began.
Lyris leaned forward. "The information you extracted. How much can you trust it?"
Kael considered the question. Harvested memories were subjective. Emotions colored perception. The leader had genuinely believed what she'd told him, had believed in her cause, but that didn't mean her understanding was accurate.
"About the structure? Enough to act on." He tapped the map. "About the Court's internal operations? Less certain. About what she's seen of the Faceless One?" He paused. "I don't know what to make of that yet. She was communing with avatars. Direct contact. That's not typical cult behavior."
"It is if they're preparing for something specific."
"Agreed." Kael's mind raced through the implications. The Faceless One—one of the 72 Entities, sealed away during the Abyssal Wars, supposedly dormant. If the cultists were establishing direct communication, if they were preparing conduits for Entity manifestation...
"Three fragments," he said slowly. "That's what the Order's intelligence suggested. Three fragments of the Faceless One embedded in individuals. One of those individuals is me."
Lyris's eyes narrowed. "You believe you're being used."
"I know I'm being used. The question is whether the fragment is dormant, waiting for something, or active, guiding my actions without my knowledge." He met her gaze. "I need to understand what I'm carrying. Before it understands me."
A knock at the door. Three times, pause, twice more.
Lyris rose, hand moving to the blade at her hip. Kael stayed seated, his own hand resting on his dagger.
The door opened to reveal a young man—barely twenty, with the sallow complexion of someone who spent too much time in the city's lower levels. He wore the nondescript clothing of a messenger, but Kael recognized the subtle insignia on his collar: the Mortician's Guild, the organization responsible for harvesting soul-essence from the legally dead.
"Message for Lyris Vane," the messenger said. "Personal. Encrypted."
Lyris took the folded paper, dismissed the messenger with a nod. She waited until the door closed before unfolding it, her eyes scanning the contents.
Her expression shifted. Something cold moved behind her gaze.
"What is it?" Kael asked.
"The Council of Six has issued a summons." She looked up. "They want to meet with the Soul Reaver. Both of us."
Kael's stomach dropped. "The Council?"
"They know about you. They've always known, probably." She turned the paper over, as if checking for more text. "But this is the first time they've acknowledged it officially. The summons includes specific language—references to the Concordat, to collaborative defense against Abyssal incursion." Her laugh was bitter. "Bureaucratic code for 'we need your help, and we're pretending this is voluntary.'"
"The Orders hunt Soul Reavers. That's been the official position for centuries."
"Official positions change when the alternative is extinction." Lyris set the paper on the table. "The Great Rift is destabilizing. The seal is failing faster than anyone predicted. They're projecting complete failure within five years, maybe less."
Kael processed this. Five years. The Great Rift was the largest of the 72 sealed points—a wound in reality that, if it reopened fully, would allow unfettered passage between the Material and the Abyss. Everything the Six Orders had fought for, everything they'd sacrificed, would be meaningless.
"The cult activity," he said. "It's connected."
"Of course it's connected. The cultists are accelerating the decay. Someone is feeding them information about the seals' vulnerabilities. Someone inside the Orders." Lyris stood, pacing the small room. "The Council is fractured. Three Orders want to mobilize immediately against the cults. Two want to focus on sealing the Great Rift directly. One has gone silent—no communication for three weeks."
"Which Order?"
"The Order of the Veil. The one most closely associated with Soul Reavers historically."
Kael frowned. "I thought the Orders founded the suppression of Soul Reavers. Why would they maintain archives?"
"They didn't found the suppression. They inherited it." Lyris stopped pacing, facing him. "The original suppression came from the first Soul Reavers themselves. They understood what they'd created. They knew the power was too dangerous to pass down freely, so they sealed it away. Established the Orders as guardians of that seal." Her voice dropped. "But that was a thousand years ago. The knowledge of *why* has been lost. The Orders teach that Soul Reavers are abominations, that the power is Abyssal corruption. They don't teach the truth."
Kael's hands curled into fists. "Which is?"
"That the Soul Reaver ability was a gift. Not from the Abyss—from humanity's first allies in the war against the Entities. A weapon designed specifically to fight beings that couldn't be killed by ordinary means. The Entities are spiritual constructs. They can be wounded, weakened, but not destroyed—not permanently. Except by Soul Reavers. Except by someone who can unmake them at the soul level."
The words hung in the air between them.
"That's why the Court wants me," Kael said slowly. "Not because I'm dangerous to humanity. Because I'm dangerous to *them*."
"The Shadow Court is made up of those who've chosen to serve the Entities over humanity. Former Awakened who found a better offer. Humans who were promised power, immortality, dominion. They know what a Soul Reaver represents. They know you could do what no one else has done in a thousand years."
"Destroy an Entity permanently."
"Yes." Lyris returned to her seat. "And they know that until you understand your own power, until you master what you are, you're more of a threat to yourself than to them."
Kael stared at the map on the table. Seventeen points. Seventeen targets in a war that had been raging since before his birth. And somewhere in that web, the truth about his ability, about his purpose, about the fragment of the Faceless One coiled in the depths of his soul.
"When is the Council meeting?" he asked.
"Three days. In Thornwick, at the Concordat Hall." Lyris studied his face. "You're considering going."
"I'm considering what happens if I don't."
"If you don't, they might declare you an enemy of the Concordat. All six Orders would be obligated to hunt you down."
"And if I do go?"
"Then you walk into a room full of people who want to use you, control you, or destroy you—probably all three." She shrugged. "But you might also learn what you need to know. About the Soul Reaver legacy. About what you're carrying." She gestured to his chest. "About the fragment."
Kael stood, moving to the window. Rain streaked the glass, distorting the view of the alley below. Somewhere in this city, the cultists were regrouping. Somewhere, handlers were receiving reports of tonight's failure. And somewhere, at the center of it all, the Faceless One waited.
"What do you think I should do?" he asked.
Lyris was quiet for a long moment. When she spoke, her voice was soft. "I think you should go. I think you should walk into that hall and show them exactly what a Soul Reaver can do—not as a weapon, but as a warning. Let them see what they'll face if they try to control you. And then—" She paused. "And then, when they realize you're not controllable, they might tell you the truth. The Orders hoard information like dragons hoard gold. They'll only open their vaults if they believe the alternative is worse."
Kael turned from the window. "You've thought about this."
"I've been preparing for this. Since the moment you woke up after your execution, I've been preparing for the day you'd have to make this choice." She rose, crossing to him. "You're not alone in this, Kael. Whatever happens at the Council, whatever you decide—you're not alone."
He looked at her—this woman who had saved him from death row, who had taught him to control his abilities, who had fought beside him through horrors that would have broken lesser bonds. She was Shadow Court once. She carried her own secrets, her own guilt. But she had chosen a different path.
"Lyris. The Council knows about your past."
"Yes."
"They'll use it against you. Against us."
"Probably." Her smile was thin. "But they don't know the whole truth. They don't know what I was before the Court, or what I found when I left. They don't know about the original Soul Reaver's journal—how I obtained it, what it contains." She reached into her coat, withdrawing a small leather journal, its pages yellowed with age. "This is why I've been preparing you. This is why I've been waiting. The Council will never give you this. But I can."
Kael stared at the journal. His hands trembled—anticipation, fear, hunger, all tangled together.
"The original Soul Reaver," he breathed. "From the Abyssal Wars."
"His name was Aldric Vane. My ancestor." Lyris pressed the journal into his hands. "He created the Soul Reaver ability. He understood its cost, its potential, its danger. And he left instructions—how to master it, how to resist corruption, how to fight Entities at the soul level." Her grip tightened on his arm. "He also left warnings. The power is a gift, but it's also a trap. The Entities can sense Soul Reavers. They're drawn to the resonance, the way a predator is drawn to wounded prey. And the Faceless One—" She hesitated. "The Faceless One has a special interest in Soul Reavers. It was Aldric who helped seal it away. It was Aldric's power that wounded it most grievously."
"And now it wants revenge."
"Now it wants completion. The fragment in you—it's not just a piece of the Entity. It's a piece of Aldric. A fragment of his soul, captured when he delivered the final blow." Lyris stepped back. "He didn't destroy the Faceless One. He *merged* with it. Sealed them both away together. And over the centuries, that fusion has been passing from vessel to vessel, looking for a host strong enough to complete the destruction—or strong enough to take control."
Kael's blood ran cold. "You're telling me I'm carrying a hybrid. A fusion of a Soul Reaver and an Entity."
"I'm telling you that you have a choice. Aldric chose to bind himself to the Entity than let it escape. He turned a weapon into a cage. But cages can be opened, and the Faceless One is patient. It's been testing you since you awakened. Every harvest, every kill, every step you've taken—it's been watching. Learning. Waiting for the moment you become strong enough to be useful."
The journal felt heavier in his hands than any physical weight should account for.
"What happens if I read this?" he asked.
"Then you learn the truth. About Soul Reavers, about the Entities, about the war that's been raging beneath the surface for a thousand years. You learn how to master your abilities, how to resist the Faceless One's influence, how to finish what Aldric started." She moved to the door. "But you also become a target. Not just for the cults or the Shadow Court. For every faction that wants to control or destroy what's written in those pages."
Kael looked down at the journal. Aldric Vane. The original. The founder of everything he was.
The founder of everything he could become.
Outside, the rain intensified. Thunder rolled across Thornwick's sky, a low rumble that vibrated through the building's foundations.
Three days until the Council. Three days until he faced the leaders of the Six Orders.
Three days to decide who he really was.

Kael spent the night reading.
Aldric's journal was written in archaic script, but the language was close enough to modern Common that Lyris's tutoring had prepared him for most of it. He read about the Abyssal Wars—not as history books portrayed them, but as a soldier's firsthand account. He read about the Entities' true nature: not demons, not monsters, but something older, something that existed in the space between thought and matter. He read about the seals, how they worked, why they degraded over time.
And he read about Soul Reavers.
The ability was not a curse. It was not Abyssal corruption. It was humanity's answer to a question the Entities themselves had never anticipated: How do you fight something that exists on a different plane of reality?
The answer was resonance. Soul Reavers could perceive the spiritual dimension directly. They could touch the fabric of reality and manipulate it. They could unmake what the Entities had woven together.
But the ability came with costs.
Every harvest weakened the barrier between the Reaver and the Abyss. Every extraction left a trace—residual energy that accumulated over time, slowly shifting the Reaver's spiritual signature toward the Entities' dimension. Soul Reavers who harvested too frequently, too deeply, would eventually become something other than human.
Aldric had called it "The Drift."
Kael checked his own spiritual signature using the calibration exercises described in the journal. The results were... Concerning. His resonance was already shifted. Not dangerously—not yet—but measurably different from what it had been a year ago.
The Faceless One's fragment was accelerating the flow. It wanted him to drift. It wanted him to become something it could control.
But it had miscalculated one thing.
Aldric had discovered a countermeasure. A technique for purging the accumulated corruption, for stabilizing the spiritual signature. It required specific ritual components, specific timing, specific knowledge that the journal only hinted at.
Kael needed the rest of the tablets. The Sepulcher tablets.
He fell asleep as dawn crept through the safehouse's single window, the journal clutched to his chest like a talisman.

The next two days passed in a blur of preparation.
Lyris drilled him on Order protocols—who to acknowledge, who to avoid, which representatives could be reasoned with and which were fanatics. She shared intelligence about known Council members: their histories, their grudges, their likely positions on the Soul Reaver question.
Three Orders would support him: the Order of Dawn (historically opposed to persecution of unique abilities), the Order of Iron (practical, focused on results), and the Order of the Veil (who had been waiting for a Soul Reaver to appear for centuries).
Three Orders would oppose him: the Order of Flame (ideologically committed to destroying all Abyssal taint), the Order of Stone (tradition-bound, unwilling to revisit centuries of doctrine), and the Order of Whispers (who had the most to lose from revelations about the Shadow Court's true nature).
The Order of Whispers was the most dangerous. They controlled intelligence for the entire Council. They knew things.
Kael tried not to think about what they might know about him.
On the morning of the third day, he stood before a full-length mirror in the safehouse, adjusting the collar of a formal coat Lyris had procured from somewhere. The coat was black wool, cut in the old style, with silver threading at the cuffs. It made him look older, more official, more dangerous.
"You'll do," Lyris said from behind him.
"I feel like a fraud."
"That's probably healthy." She handed him a small device—a jade pendant on a silver chain. "Emergency beacon. If something goes wrong inside the hall, activate this. I've arranged a extraction team. They won't be able to enter Concordat territory, but they'll be close."
Kael took the pendant, tucking it beneath his collar. "And if everything goes right?"
"Then we learn what we need to know, and we leave before anyone can react." She studied his face. "Kael. Whatever happens in there—don't let them goad you into using your abilities publicly. The moment they see you harvest, they'll treat you as a threat instead of an asset. We need you to seem cooperative, not hungry."
"I know."
"Do you?" She stepped closer. "The journal said you could destroy Entities permanently. Did you understand what that means? If you master this power, you become the most valuable weapon in existence. Every faction will want to control you. Every faction will try to kill you before someone else controls you." Her voice softened. "This might be the last time you walk into a public space without someone trying to destroy you."
Kael met her gaze. "Then I'd better make it count."

Concordat Hall occupied a full block in Thornwick's governmental district—a sprawling complex of marble and iron, built during the height of the Abyssal Wars and maintained through every era since. The building's architecture was deliberately imposing: soaring columns, massive stone faces carved with the symbols of each Order, a central dome that rose like a clenched fist toward the sky.
Kael climbed the steps with Lyris at his side. Guards in ceremonial armor flanked the entrance, but they didn't challenge him. They had been briefed. They stepped aside with precise nods.
Inside, the hall was cavernous. The main chamber stretched before him—a circular space with tiered seating rising toward the vaulted ceiling. In the center, a raised platform held six high-backed chairs arranged in a semicircle.
Only four chairs were occupied.
The Order of Dawn's representative was a woman in her sixties with steel-gray hair and eyes that missed nothing. The Order of Iron's representative was a broad-shouldered man with a mechanical arm—field damage from some long-past battle. The Order of the Veil's representative was a figure in flowing robes, face hidden behind a silver mask. And the Order of Flame's representative was a young man with burn scars covering half his face, his expression one of barely contained hostility.
The empty chairs belonged to the Order of Stone and the Order of Whispers.
Kael stopped at the platform's edge, Lyris a step behind him. The silence was absolute.
"Kael Ashborn," the woman from the Order of Dawn spoke first, her voice carrying easily through the space. "Also known as the Soul Reaver. Also known as the Thornwick Butcher. Also known as the Blade That Walks Among the Dead."
Kael inclined his head. "I prefer Kael."
"Few would." The scarred young man from Order of Flame leaned forward. "A creature that feeds on the souls of the innocent—"
"A creature that feeds on cultists and criminals," Kael interrupted. "I've harvested seventeen hostile targets in the last month. All of them connected to Abyssal activity. All of them planning attacks on seal infrastructure."
The young man flushed. "Even so. The ability itself is—"
"Ancient," the masked figure from the Order of the Veil said. Its voice was genderless, ageless, echoing strangely. "The Soul Reaver ability is as old as the Orders themselves. Older, maybe. It was not created by the Abyss. It was created to fight the Abyss."
"The Veil speaks heresy," the scarred young man snapped.
"The Veil speaks truth." The mechanical-armed man from Order of Iron raised his flesh hand. "I move we hear the Soul Reaver's account of recent events before we proceed to judgment."
"A formal motion." The woman from Order of Dawn nodded. "All in favor?"
Three hands rose. The scarred young man and one other voted against. The masked figure abstained.
"Motion carries. Kael Ashborn, you are recognized to speak."
Kael stepped forward, his mind racing through everything he'd learned. The journal. The maps. The truth about the Soul Reaver legacy.
What do I tell them? How much do I reveal?
He looked at the faces watching him—the suspicion, the fear, the barely concealed hostility. He thought of the cultists in the foundry, the pattern of seventeen attacks, the evidence of deep-seated corruption.
And he made his choice.
"Three weeks ago," he began, "I discovered that the Shadow Court has been coordinating attacks on seal infrastructure across Thornwick. The attacks aren't random. They're systematic. They're mapping the seals, identifying weaknesses, preparing for a coordinated assault." He paused. "The Council should have known this. Your intelligence networks should have identified the pattern. But you didn't. Because someone inside the Orders has been feeding information to the Court."
The silence shattered.
The scarred young man rose from his seat. "This is slander. A convenient accusation from a creature that—"
"A creature that just handed you intelligence you didn't have five minutes ago." Kael's voice cut through the interruption. "I've provided specifics. Locations. Dates. Names extracted from cultist memories. Either you investigate and confirm the threat, or you ignore me and hope the seals hold when the Court decides to push." He turned to the woman from Order of Dawn. "I'm not here to be judged. I'm here because the Great Rift is failing, because the cult activity is accelerating, and because someone needs to do something before it's too late."
"And what would you have us do?" the masked figure asked.
Kael pulled out the map he'd sketched, holding it up. "Seventeen points. Seventeen opportunities to intercept cult operations, capture operatives, and trace the corruption back to its source. The Orders have the resources. You have the Awakened. You have the military capability." He looked around the circle. "You just need the will."
The mechanical-armed man spoke again. "If what you say is true—if there is corruption within the Orders—this could destabilize the Concordat entirely."
"The Concordat is already destabilized. The Great Rift is failing. The cult activity is coordinated. The Shadow Court is watching everything we do." Kael met each gaze in turn. "You can cling to the old structure and watch it collapse. Or you can adapt. Choose."
The woman from Order of Dawn studied him for a long moment. Then, slowly, she smiled.
"The Thornwick Butcher," she said. "Maybe you are more than the rumors suggest."
"I'm more than the rumors suggest because the rumors serve purposes I don't care about." Kael tucked the map away. "I'm here because I want the seals to hold. I'm here because I don't want to live in a world consumed by the Abyss. And I'm here because—" He hesitated.
The Faceless One stirred in his chest. A whisper of cold, of hunger, of promise.
"I'm here," he finished, "because someone has to be."
The masked figure from the Order of the Veil rose. "Then we have much to discuss. Details. Resources. Contingencies." It gestured to the seats below the platform. "Please. Sit. Let us begin the real work."
Kael descended the steps, taking the offered seat. As he did, he felt the weight of the pendant beneath his collar, the journal's knowledge in his mind, the fragment's cold presence in his soul.
Three days ago, he'd been a fugitive with a target on his back.
Now he was sitting in the heart of the Order's power structure, negotiating the terms of a war that had been raging for a thousand years.
The game had changed. The pieces were moving.
And somewhere in the shadows, the Faceless One watched, and waited, and smiled with Kael's face.
