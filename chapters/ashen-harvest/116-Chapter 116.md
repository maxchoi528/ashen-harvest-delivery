The blade found the mortar between stones three times before Kael heard the footsteps.
He dropped from the window ledge, boots hitting cobblestones in the narrow alley below. Rain hammered the gutters, masking the sound of his landing, but nothing would mask the six shadows detaching themselves from the building across the street. Their movements carried the unmistakable precision of trained killers—Awakened, all of them, their signatures bleeding through the downpour like ink through water.
"Soul Reaver." The tallest one spoke without inflection. Gray coat, silver insignia hidden at the collar. A Sunblade, or pretending to be. "You're a difficult man to locate."
Kael's fingers brushed the wall, feeling the ancient stonework. Old quarter. Plenty of corpses in the basements beneath these buildings, plenty of residual death to draw from if things went wrong. "You've got the wrong person."
"We watched you harvest from the Grayveil corpse." Another voice, female, shorter, circling to his left. "Three days ago. Warehouse district."
Kael's jaw tightened. He'd been careful. Scrubbed the warehouse clean, burned the wrappings, scattered ash across the drainage channels. Days of methodical work, undone by someone he hadn't seen.
The tall one raised his hand. "By order of the Shadow Court, you will accompany us for questioning. Resist, and we are authorized to use lethal force."
Shadow Court. The name turned Kael's stomach to ice. Rumors in the underworld spoke of them—deniable operations for Orders that wanted to violate the Concordat without leaving fingerprints. No official existence. No accountability. The kind of people who made bodies disappear and called it due process.
Kael backed toward the alley's mouth, buying seconds. "Questioning about what, exactly?"
"Your abilities. Their origin. Your connection to the sealed rifts." The tall one advanced, his companions forming a tightening crescent. "We know what you are, Soul Reaver. We know what you can do. The question is whether you're an asset or a threat."
Lightning split the sky, illuminating their faces for a single heartbeat. Kael counted: six Awakened, stage uncertain but likely Ascendant or lower. Survivable odds if he fought smart. Impossible odds if they brought reinforcements.
A carriage clattered into the square behind them, lanterns swinging. The horses steamed in the rain, hooves striking wet stone in a rhythm that made two of the operatives glance sideways. Distraction or trap? Either way, it bought him moments of their divided attention.
"I'm neither asset nor threat," Kael said, hand drifting toward the knife at his belt. "I'm just a man trying to survive in a city that wants him dead."
"Then die quietly." The tall one's spiritual pressure spiked, and he moved—
Kael was already running.
He dove through a gap between buildings, shoulder slamming against wet wood as he twisted sideways. Behind him, a blast of spiritual energy incinerated the corner of the alley, heat washing over his back, close enough to singe the hair on his neck. The tall one was strong. Stage three at least, maybe pushing four. Kael couldn't fight that head-on.
But he didn't need to.
The old quarter of Thornwick was a labyrinth of crumbling tenements and forgotten crypts. Kael navigated by memory and instinct, vaulting fences, sliding through drainage tunnels, circling back twice to shake pursuit before doubling toward his destination. His lungs burned. His legs screamed. Water streamed down his face, mixing with the salt-sting of sweat. He'd survived execution once already; he'd survive this.
The Mortician's Guildhall loomed ahead, its black spire cutting through the rain like a needle through flesh. Kael had no love for the Guild—they harvested the dead legally, with nominal family consent, and sold soul-essence to the highest bidder. But they also maintained extensive catacombs beneath their halls, and in those catacombs, bodies waited.
He slipped through a service entrance, nodding to a startled attendant who took one look at his rain-soaked coat and spiritual signature and decided he wasn't paid enough to intervene. The catacombs descended in spiraling stone, the air thick with the smell of preservation herbs and old death. Juniper and formaldehyde, layered over something older and sweeter. Kael breathed through his mouth.
He found a chamber lined with recent arrivals—bodies wrapped in cloth, waiting for processing. Most were commoners, their soul-essence weak and diluted. But two bore the faint traces of Awakened energy. Young ones, probably killed in some border skirmish or duel. Still fresh enough to yield decent results.
Footsteps echoed above. They were tracking him.
Kael knelt beside the first corpse, pressing his palm against the cold wrappings. The Soul Reaver ability stirred, that peculiar hunger that came with touching the dead. What the texts claimed about spiritual cost was wrong—or incomplete. The real cost was something else. Each harvest left more of the original owner's memories clinging to his consciousness, fragmenting his sense of self. Faces he'd never seen. Voices he'd never heard. Loves and losses that belonged to strangers, pressing against the walls of his identity like water against a dam.
The harvest was instant and invasive. Soul energy poured into him, not his own reserves but something borrowed, something taken. The system notification materialized in his vision, text burning gold against the darkness.
Soul Harvest complete. Minor combat proficiency acquired.
The proficiency was useless—basic swordsmanship he'd never use—but the essence would fuel his escape. Kael moved to the second Awakened corpse, pressing his hand against the chest. The wrappings were damp, clinging to cooling flesh beneath. This one had been dead longer. The energy tasted different, sharper, carrying an edge of violence that coated his throat like copper.
Soul Harvest complete. Active ability acquired: Dawn's Retribution.
A Sunblade technique. The stolen memory surfaced—a training hall, morning light through high windows, a bearded instructor correcting a young student's stance. Kael pushed it aside. Stolen lives weren't his to mourn.
The chamber's entrance exploded inward.
Kael threw himself sideways as spiritual energy screamed through the space, scorching the wall where his head had been. Stone chips peppered his cheek. The tall Shadow Court operative stood in the doorway, coat torn, face contorted with fury.
"You dare desecrate sacred ground—"
"Sacred?" Kael laughed, the sound harsh against the catacomb acoustics. "This is a meat locker. You're upset because I touched your inventory?"
The tall one raised his hand again, gathering power for a killing blow. Behind him, three more operatives crowded the entrance, blocking escape.
But Kael wasn't trying to escape anymore.
He activated Dawn's Retribution.
Golden light erupted from his palm, a concentrated beam of spiritual energy that had no business coming from someone with his signature. The tall one barely got his barrier up in time—the impact still hurled him backward, through the doorway, into his companions. Bodies crashed against stone.
Kael ran.
Not toward the exit—toward the deep catacombs, where older corpses waited. Degraded essence, possibly, but quantity had its own quality. The tunnels twisted and turned, rain-soaked boots splashing through puddles that had no business existing underground. Seepage from the city's drains. Or something worse. The walls changed as he descended—cut stone giving way to older construction, rougher, the mortar crumbling to dust at his touch. Carvings emerged from the gloom, worn smooth by centuries of seepage. Symbols he didn't recognize.
He emerged into a vast underground chamber, pillars supporting a ceiling lost in shadow. Ancient stonework, predating Thornwick by centuries. The original foundation, or something older still. Bones littered the floor—not recent, these were ancient remains, picked clean by time and vermin. The air tasted different here. Drier. Older. Carrying a weight that pressed against Kael's chest like a hand on his sternum.
And at the chamber's center, a door.
Not a normal door. Something about it made Kael's teeth ache, made the Soul Reaver ability pulse in his chest like a second heartbeat. Runes covered its surface, symbols he didn't recognize but knew. The language of seals. The language of the Abyss.
"Soul Reaver." A new voice, coming from everywhere and nowhere. "You shouldn't be here."
Kael spun, blade drawn. The chamber was empty except for shadows and the dead.
"I can see you, boy. Even if you can't see me." The voice carried an ancient weariness, like wind through an empty hall. "I've been watching your progress. Impressive, for someone so young in the power. Wasteful, but impressive."
"Who are you?"
"Someone who made the same choices you're making. Someone who walked the same path." A flicker of movement in Kael's peripheral vision, there and gone. "The Shadow Court knows about this place. They'll follow. You have a few minutes before they breach the catacombs' deeper wards."
Kael's grip tightened on his weapon. "Then help me. You're obviously powerful enough—"
"Powerful enough to destroy myself, yes. Powerful enough to help you?" A dry laugh echoed through the chamber. "That depends entirely on what you're willing to sacrifice."
The door pulsed. The runes brightened, then dimmed, as if breathing.
"I'm not making deals with shadows."
"Then die. It makes no difference to me." The voice was fading, retreating into whatever impossible space it occupied. "But know this, Soul Reaver—the door you're standing before leads somewhere the Shadow Court cannot follow. Somewhere even the Orders fear to tread. The original repository. The first seal. And the answer to every question you've ever asked about what you are."
Kael's heart hammered. The original repository. The first Soul Reaver's resting place. Everything he'd been searching for, hidden beneath Thornwick's streets, guarded by an entity that refused to show itself.
"Why tell me this?"
"Because you're running out of time." The voice returned, urgent now. "The Shadow Court didn't come for you because of the Grayveil corpse. They came because of what you didn't take. There's something different about you, Soul Reaver. Something the Abyss recognizes. The question is whether you'll survive long enough to find out what."
Footsteps. Distant but approaching. Torchlight flickering in the tunnels behind him, throwing long shadows against the ancient walls.
"The door will open once," the voice repeated. "Choose wisely."
Kael stood frozen between options, rain dripping from his coat, the weight of impossible decisions pressing against his chest. Behind him, enemies closed in. Before him, a door that promised answers—or oblivion.
He thought of Sera, waiting in the safehouse, unaware of the hunters descending. He thought of the Sepulcher tablets, the True Name hidden in their recorded memories. He thought of every step that had led him here, to this crossroads between the world of the living and whatever waited beyond the seal.
The door's runes pulsed brighter.
Kael made his choice.
He ran toward the door, not away. If the Shadow Court wanted to follow, let them try. If the entity behind the voice wanted to test him, he'd pass or he'd die. Either way, it would be his decision.
His palm pressed against the ancient wood.
The door opened.
Light—the wrong kind of light, Abyssal and hungry—poured from the threshold. Kael felt the pull immediately, felt his soul stretching toward that terrible brightness like iron toward a lodestone. The Soul Reaver ability thrashed in his chest, and for the first time since his awakening, he understood what the texts meant when they described corruption.
This wasn't harvesting. This was being harvested.
He wrenched his hand back, stumbling. The door remained open, a window into colors that had no names, geometries that hurt to perceive. And standing in that impossible space, a figure made of absence and hunger.
"Kael Ashborn." It spoke without a mouth, the words forming directly in his mind. "Soul Reaver. Breaker of seals. Friend of the Abyss."
The figure extended something that might have been a hand.
"Your presence is required."
Kael's knife was in his grip before conscious thought caught up. "Not interested."
"You misunderstand." The figure's non-presence shifted, and Kael caught a glimpse of something terrible beneath the void—knowledge, power, answers to questions he hadn't yet learned to ask. "I am not here to consume you. I am here because you called. Every time you harvest, every time you break what should be unbreakable, you sing a song that echoes through the Abyss. And I answered."
"I didn't call anyone."
"You didn't have to. Your existence is call enough." The figure drifted closer, and Kael's skin crawled with revulsion. "The Shadow Court knows what you are. The Orders suspect. But none of them understand what you could become—what you will become, if you survive long enough."
"And what's that?"
"The seventy-third."
The word hit Kael like a physical blow. Seventy-three. There were only seventy-two Entities sealed behind the rifts. Everyone knew that. Everyone had to know that, because the seal network required exact numbers to function.
"That's impossible."
"Impossible." The figure's non-voice carried something that might have been amusement. "You harvest the dead without cost. You carry abilities that should not exist. And you stand before a door that has been sealed since before the first Order rose." A ripple passed through the void-body. "Your understanding of possible requires adjustment."
Behind him, the tunnel torchlight grew brighter. Voices shouted, coordinating positions. The Shadow Court had found him.
"Time runs short, Soul Reaver." The figure retreated toward the impossible light. "You may enter and receive knowledge—partial, dangerous, but yours to use. Or you may return to your enemies and face a different kind of death. The choice, as always, remains yours."
Kael's hand trembled. Every instinct screamed to run, to hide, to survive another day by abandoning this opportunity. But survival had brought him here. Survival had led him to the Soul Reaver ability, to the Sepulcher tablets, to this moment. What was the point of surviving if he never learned what he was surviving for?
He stepped through the door.
The light swallowed him whole.
Information flooded his consciousness—not gradually, not gently, but all at once, a torrent of memory and power that drove him to his knees. Kael saw the first Soul Reaver, a figure of shadow and determination, standing before the seventy-three Entities as they tore reality apart. He saw the sealing ritual, the terrible cost, the sacrifice that bound the Abyss's children behind walls of light and will. He saw his own face, repeated across millennia, always a Soul Reaver, always walking the edge between humanity and the void.
The knowledge burned.
And at the center of it all, a name:
VAELITH. THE UNBOUND. THE SEVENTY-THIRD.
His name.
His true name.
The door slammed shut behind him.
Kael gasped, returning to consciousness on the ancient stone floor of the catacomb chamber. The door was closed, its runes dark, the Abyssal light gone. But the knowledge remained—fragmented, overwhelming, impossible to process all at once.
Footsteps. Torchlight. The Shadow Court operatives flooding into the chamber.
Kael forced himself upright, every muscle screaming in protest. The tall one stood at the front of the group, face pale with something that might have been fear.
"What did you do?" His voice cracked. "What did you just do?"
Kael smiled, blood on his teeth. "I became something you can't kill."
The tall one raised his hand, gathering power. Kael felt something stirring in his depths, answering a call he didn't understand. Power he couldn't control. A destiny written in blood and starlight.
Then the pain came.
It started in his chest—a searing, tearing pressure, as if something enormous was trying to crawl out through his ribs. Kael dropped to his knees, knife clattering against stone. His vision fractured: the catacomb chamber splitting into overlapping images, each one showing a different version of the same moment. The Shadow Court operatives frozen mid-stride. The torchlight stuttering. The ancient door behind him pulsing with light that shouldn't exist.
The tall one lowered his hand. "What's happening to him?"
Kael couldn't answer. His mouth was full of blood, copper and salt, and his body was no longer his own. Something moved beneath his skin, pressing against the inside of his wrist, his throat, the backs of his eyes. The stolen memories in his head—all of them, every harvest, every corpse—screamed at once.
And then silence.
Kael stood. But not because he chose to.
His body moved without his permission, straightening from the crouch with a fluidity that belonged to something else entirely. His hands—his hands that had harvested the dead for years, that had learned to steal without conscience—flexed and curled as if testing unfamiliar joints.
The Shadow Court operatives backed away. All of them, even the tall one, even the stage-three who had nearly killed him in the alley. Whatever they saw in his face, in the way he stood, in the way the torchlight bent wrong around his body—it was enough.
"What are you?" the tall one whispered.
Kael's mouth opened. The voice that came out was his, and not his. Deeper. Older. Carrying harmonics that made the stone walls tremble, that made dust sift from the ancient ceiling.
"Run."
The word rippled through the chamber. The operatives didn't need to be told twice. They turned and fled, torchlight bouncing wildly as they scrambled back through the tunnels. The tall one was the last to go, and even he stopped at the entrance, looking back with an expression Kael couldn't read.
Then he was gone.
Kael stood alone in the dark. His body was his own again—or at least, he thought it was. The power that had moved through him was retreating, sinking back into whatever depth it had risen from, leaving behind an ache that went deeper than muscle and bone.
His hands were shaking.
He looked down at them, at the familiar scars and calluses, at the blood drying beneath his nails. These were his hands. His body. His choices. He had walked through that door of his own free will, and whatever had come back—
The thought dissolved. He couldn't hold it. The knowledge from beyond the door was still there, but it was fragmented, slippery, refusing to coalesce into anything he could understand. Seventy-three Entities. The original seal. A name that was his and not his.
Vaelith.
He pressed his palms against his eyes until he saw stars. When he lowered his hands, the catacomb chamber was still there. The ancient bones. The sealed door. The silence.
He needed to move. Sera was waiting. The Shadow Court would regroup, bring reinforcements, come back with numbers he couldn't fight. He needed to get out of Thornwick, needed to—
His foot struck something on the floor.
Kael looked down. Among the ancient bones, half-buried in dust and debris, lay a tablet. Stone, cracked down one edge, covered in the same runic script as the door. He knelt, brushing dust from its surface. The symbols meant nothing to him. But the Soul Reaver ability pulsed in his chest, and when his fingers touched the stone, he felt something he had never felt before.
The tablet was warm.
Not residual warmth from the door's light—internal, alive, a steady heartbeat of heat that matched his own pulse. As if the stone itself was breathing.
Kael's fingers closed around its edges. The warmth spread up his arm, through his shoulder, into his chest. The ache from the power's retreat faded, replaced by something clearer. Sharper. A sensation like standing at the edge of a cliff and knowing, with absolute certainty, that the next step would either teach him to fly or teach him nothing at all.
He lifted the tablet from the bones. It was heavier than it looked, dense with something that wasn't stone, wasn't metal. The runes caught the distant torchlight from the tunnel and held it, glowing faintly from within.
Kael tucked it under his arm and turned toward the exit.
The catacombs were empty now. The Shadow Court's torches had retreated up the spiral, leaving him in darkness broken only by the faint luminescence of the tablet against his ribs. His boots splashed through the puddles he'd crossed before, but the sound was different now—hollower, as if the tunnels had grown longer while he wasn't looking.
He paused at the chamber where he'd harvested the two Awakened corpses. The wrappings lay scattered, disturbed by the fight. The bodies were still there, still and cold, their stolen energy already fading from his reserves. Dawn's Retribution had burned itself out—good for one shot, as expected. The minor swordsmanship lingered, useless and persistent, a ghost of skill he'd never asked for.
Kael stepped over the tall one's scorch mark on the floor and kept climbing.
The Mortician's Guildhall was quiet. The attendant who'd let him in was gone, the service entrance hanging open, rain sluicing through the gap. Kael slipped out into the storm, pulling his coat collar up against the downpour. Thornwick's gas lamps flickered in the distance, their light struggling against the rain.
He made it three streets from the Guildhall before the pain came back.
This time it started in his hand—the hand that had touched the door, the hand that now held the tablet. A burning sensation that climbed his arm like fire up a wick, settling in his chest with a weight that stole his breath. Kael staggered against a wall, pressing his forehead against wet brick, teeth clenched against the sound that wanted to escape his throat.
The tablet pulsed against his ribs. Once. Twice.
And then Kael heard it—not with his ears, but with something deeper, something the door had opened. A voice, thin and distant, threading through the spaces between heartbeats.
Not Vaelith. Not the entity from beyond the door.
Something else.
Something that was already inside him.
The voice said a word he didn't understand, in a language that predated the runes on the door, and Kael's vision went white.
When it cleared, he was on his knees in the gutter, rain hammering his back, the tablet clutched against his chest. His nose was bleeding—he could taste it, copper and salt mixing with rainwater on his lips. His hands were shaking so badly he could barely hold the stone.
But the voice was still there.
Faint. Patient. Waiting.
And Kael, for the first time since the door had opened, was afraid.
