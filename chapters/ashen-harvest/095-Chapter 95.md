Kael pressed his back against the crumbling brick wall, breath shallow, eyes tracing the symbols chalked across the alley's dead end. Three nights of surveillance. Three nights of watching hooded figures slip through the Fogmire shadows into this forgotten corner of Thornwick. Tonight, he'd finally followed close enough to see what they left behind.
A body.
Fresh. Still warm by the look of it.
He didn't need to check for a pulse. The way the corpse lay, limbs splayed at wrong angles, throat opened in a perfect crescent—someone had harvested what they needed and discarded the rest. But Kael wasn't here to avenge the dead. He was here to understand why Thornwick's streets had grown so much bloodier since the seals began failing.
The symbols on the wall pulsed faintly in his spiritual sight. Not Order magic. Not any sanctioned work he'd encountered in three months of scraping information from the city's underbelly. These markings reeked of something older, something that made his Soul Reaver senses itch with recognition he couldn't name.
A crow burst from a rooftop nearby, its cry swallowed by the city's ever-present steam-hiss and distant factory thunder. Below, in the alley's darkness, one of the hooded figures knelt beside the corpse. Its hands moved in patterns that hurt to watch directly—too many joints bending wrong ways.
Kael's fingers curled against the rough stone. His realm had stabilized months ago, reserves full, power itching for use. But he hadn't hunted like this since establishing the Fogmire base with Lyra. Hunted. The word fit better than 'worked' or 'gathered.' What he did out here was hunting, and the prey happened to be information as often as power.
The kneeling figure produced a vial from its robes. Soul-essence. The liquid inside glowed sickly green, different from the golden standard he'd seen traded in the Veiled Market. Different enough to make his stomach clench.
"Sacrifice complete," the figure said, voice distorted by what sounded like a resonance charm. "The Veil responds. Another thread loosened."
Kael's blood went cold. Another thread loosened. The Seals. The seventy-two locked gates between the world and the Abyss—the barriers humanity's ancestors had paid oceans of blood to construct. These cultists weren't just bleeding the city for power. They were dismantling the world's foundation, one murder at a time.
He needed more. He needed to follow, to track, to understand the scope of what Zarek's people had built in Thornwick. But the kneeling figure rose, tucking the glowing vial away, and gestured toward the alley's far end. Two more robed figures emerged from shadows that shouldn't have been deep enough to hide them.
They walked toward Kael's wall. Toward the street beyond.
He had seconds to choose.
---
The Howling Jungle sat at Thornwick's eastern edge, a district the city had never managed to claim. Steam-age progress pushed against its borders constantly—new rail lines, factory expansions, development plots surveyed and numbered—but the jungle itself refused to yield. Blackwood trees with bark like iron grew between abandoned tenements. Vines crushed brick walls into rubble. The air smelled of rot and copper and something sweeter underneath, like flowers blooming on mass graves.
Kael stood at the jungle's mouth now, three days after the alley sighting. Three days of following leads, bribing informants, and picking through the digital-archive records Lyra had access to through her Shadow Court contacts. The cultists had vanished after that ritual, their trail cold, but one name kept surfacing in connection with Thornwick's organized Abyssal activity.
The Howling Jungle. Supposedly empty. Supposedly picked clean after the last Order purge, twenty years ago.
Supposedly.
"You're certain about this?" Lyra stood beside him, her dark coat buttoned to the throat despite the summer humidity. She hadn't removed that coat since he'd known her—some scar she kept hidden, or some mark she couldn't remove. "The jungle's got a reputation even among Shadow Court operators."
"Because it's dangerous?"
"Because people who go in don't always come out the same." She met his eyes. "Or at all."
Kael watched the jungle's edge. Something moved between the trees—too large for a cat, too fluid for a human. The howler monkeys that gave the district its name kept up a constant chittering from the canopy, punctuated by occasional screams that sounded almost like voices.
"I've tracked cult activity for three weeks. Everything points here." He pulled out a crumpled paper—hand-drawn map, annotations in his own shorthand. "They meet at least twice weekly. Whatever they're doing to the seals, they do it from inside that mess."
"And if it's a trap?"
"Then I learn something about who's hunting me." He folded the map, tucked it into his coat. "You don't have to come."
Lyra's jaw tightened. "I know I don't have to. I choose to." She adjusted the blade at her hip—Shadow Court standard issue, nothing special, but she'd survived three years of deniable operations with that steel. "Someone needs to watch your back while you're busy robbing corpses."
He almost smiled. "Romantic."
"Pragmatic." But her lips twitched. "You harvest the skill, I'll watch for company. If it goes wrong, we run. If it goes right, we learn what we're fighting."
They stepped into the jungle together.
---
The canopy swallowed the sky within twenty paces. Light filtered down in green-gold shafts, fractured by leaves that seemed to move with intention. The ground squelched—soil too rich, too dark, fertilized by decades of accumulated death. Kael's boots sank slightly with each step.
"Movement," Lyra murmured, hand on her blade.
He saw it too. A shape between the trees, watching. Not human-shaped. Too many angles, joints bending in directions that made his eyes water.
"Don't engage unless forced," he said. "These things are territorial. We pass through, they should let us."
"Should?"
"I'm guessing."
The shape drifted closer. Kael's spiritual senses prickled—the creature wasn't Awakened, but it had absorbed enough ambient energy from the failed seals to develop something like instincts. Hunger without comprehension. It wanted, and what it wanted was warmth, life, essence.
The howler monkeys screamed louder.
The shape retreated.
Lyra exhaled slowly. "What the hell was that?"
"Echo-born. When the seals weaken, sometimes creatures from the Abyss leak through before the resonance fully manifests. They don't think, don't plan. Just... Consume." Kael kept walking, orienting on the compass in his pocket. The cult meeting point should be northwest, maybe half a mile. "The Orders call them echoes. They're why the purges happen—keep the jungle controlled so these things don't spill into the city."
"And yet." Lyra gestured at the thriving mess around them. "Clearly not controlled."
"Twenty years since the last purge. Seals failing faster now." He ducked under a vine thick as his arm. "The cult activity—I think they're accelerating the degradation. Not just using weak points, actively making new ones."
"That's insane. If the seals fail completely—"
"I know."
The undergrowth grew denser as they progressed. Old ruins emerged from the green—walls of temples dedicated to gods most people had forgotten, buildings that might have been mortuaries or research facilities before the jungle claimed them. Kael noted architectural details with growing unease. These structures matched designs from the pre-Orders era, the civilization that had built the seal network in the first place.
Someone had been here before. Someone who knew what they were looking at.
"Fresh tracks." Lyra crouched beside a muddy patch between collapsed pillars. Boot prints, modern style. Multiple sets. "Hours old, maybe less."
Kael knelt beside her. The tracks led deeper into the ruins, toward a structure that might once have been a library or archive. Its roof had partially collapsed, but the walls still stood, and he could see recent modifications—reinforced supports, new doorways cut into ancient stone.
"Found it," he breathed.
He reached out with his spiritual senses, trying to read the ambient energy in the area. His Ascendant-stage awareness could detect strong emotional residue, fear and desperation soaked into the ground. But the specific magical signatures remained frustratingly vague without a targeting ability.
He needed Detect Magic. The skill wouldn't unlock itself.
---
The cultists had cleared the central chamber of debris and installed rudimentary facilities—wooden tables covered in papers, lanterns fueled by something that burned with sickly green light, bedrolls for extended stays. Empty bedrolls. The place felt abandoned, but not for long.
"Still warm," Lyra said, touching one of the lanterns. "They were here today."
Kael moved through the space, cataloging details. The papers on the tables caught his attention—diagrams of seal structures, notes in cramped handwriting, charts tracking degradation rates across multiple locations. He recognized Thornwick's layout among the maps, with thirty-two points marked in red.
"Thirty-two active seal sites in the city," he said. "And they've identified all of them."
"That's not possible without access to Order archives."
"Unless someone with access is feeding them information."
Lyra went still. "You're saying there's a traitor in one of the Orders?"
"I'm saying thirty-two precise locations don't get guessed. They get delivered." He set the papers down carefully, aware of how fragile this evidence was. "Whoever's running this operation has inside help. Maybe multiple sources."
A sound echoed from deeper in the ruins. Metal on stone. Footsteps.
Kael grabbed Lyra's arm, pulled her behind a collapsed pillar. Through the gap, he watched a robed figure enter the chamber from a doorway he hadn't noticed—hidden passage, probably connecting to tunnels beneath the structure.
The cultist moved to one of the tables, rifling through documents, muttering to itself. Alone. Unaware.
His hand drifted to the corpse-bag at his hip. Standard equipment for his kind of work—a reinforced satchel containing tools for harvesting, preservation vessels for extracted essence, medical supplies for field repairs. In the bag's main compartment, three empty soul-containers waited.
The cultist's body would yield information no document could. Memories of meetings, names, hierarchies. Skills the cult had cultivated for this work. And if he was lucky—truly lucky—the specific ability that let them sense seal weaknesses.
"Target's alone," he breathed to Lyra. "I can take him quiet."
"You need fresh corpse for the harvest. This one's breathing."
" Won't stay that way."
Lyra's expression didn't change. Three years in the Shadow Court had prepared her for this calculus. "I'll watch the passage. You have maybe two minutes before someone else might come through."
Two minutes to neutralize a cultist, drag the body somewhere hidden, perform the harvest, and extract with whatever he'd gained.
Kael smiled without humor. "Plenty of time."
He moved.
---
The cultist died without understanding what happened. Kael's hand closed over its mouth while his other arm hooked around its neck, applying pressure to the carotid arteries. Unconsciousness in eight seconds, death in thirty. The body went limp, and Kael eased it to the ground with practiced care.
Lyra had already swept the chamber's other entrances. "Clear. For now."
He dragged the cultist's corpse behind the pillar, into shadow cast by the collapsed roof. Out of habit, he checked for traps—none—and then he knelt, placing both palms flat against the cooling flesh.
Soul Reaver activation came without ritual or cost. His spiritual senses expanded, pushing into the fading architecture of a human soul. Not departed—death was a flow, not an event—but close enough that the harvest could proceed.
The corpse's memories crashed through him. Not gently, never gently. He tasted the cultist's fear, the metallic tang of panic when he'd first encountered the cult's recruiters. He saw faces—other robed figures, some with names he caught, others with symbols he didn't recognize. He witnessed rituals, felt the wrongness of Abyssal energy seeping through weakened seals, understood the cultist's growing conviction that the old world deserved to burn.
Skill memories emerged next, sharper than emotional residue. Detect Magic. The cultist had learned it through months of practice, a minor Awakened talent cultivated for the specific purpose of finding seal weaknesses. Detect Magic allowed the user to sense magical auras within a limited range, to identify enchantments and track spiritual activity.
Kael pulled.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Detect Magic (Minor Awakened) from the remains. The skill requires 100 spiritual energy per use. Range: 30 feet. Duration: Concentration dependent.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted fragmented memories from the remains. Memory fragments acquired: cult meeting locations (3), superior's codename: "Nightbringer", Order infiltration confirmed.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted residual spiritual energy from the remains. 15 essence units acquired.]
The notifications pulsed through his consciousness, cold and efficient. He absorbed the skill directly—instinct now, after months of practice—feeling the Detect Magic ability settle into his spiritual architecture like a new room in a familiar house.
Lyra watched his face. "You got something good?"
"Nightbringer." Kael opened his eyes, still kneeling beside the corpse. "That's who runs this cell. And there's confirmation of Order involvement—infiltration, probably multiple levels."
"Infiltration or conspiracy?"
"Can't tell yet. The memories were fragmented." He stood, wiping his palms on his coat. The corpse would need to disappear, but that was a problem for later. Right now, they needed to leave before the cultist's absence was noted. "I can detect magic now. Might as well use it—see what else this place is hiding."
He reached inward, activating Detect Magic for the first time.
The chamber blazed with magical signatures. The green-burning lanterns carried faint enchantments—nothing sophisticated, just preservation wards to keep the fuel stable. The papers on the tables had been treated with detection-reveal ink, allowing the cultists to read seal layouts hidden by centuries of protective enchantments.
But beneath all of that, deeper, something pulsed.
Magic in the walls themselves.
Kael followed the sensation, Lyra covering his back, until he stood before the hidden doorway the cultist had used. His Detect Magic painted the passage in layers of spiritual energy—old enchantments predating the jungle's growth, newer workings layered on top, and at the foundation, something vast and terrible.
"Seal magic," he breathed.
"This was a seal site?"
"Or built on top of one." The recognition struck him like cold water. "The original seal network. They didn't just build barriers—they built facilities to monitor them. Outposts where guardians could track degradation and respond to breaches."
"And the cult's been using this place for months."
"Using it, and probably degrading it." He reached toward the passage, sensing the seal's weakening integrity. One thread loosened, the cultist had said. One thread of many. "Whatever's below, they've been working on it. Ritual sacrifice to weaken the barrier, maybe. Or something worse."
From the passage's depths, a sound drifted upward. Not footsteps. Not voices.
Breathing.
Something massive. Something that breathed with a rhythm older than humanity, vast and patient and hungry.
Lyra's blade cleared its sheath. "Kael."
He heard it too. Felt it through his Detect Magic—a presence below, vast and old and aware. The seal wasn't just weakened. It was being watched from the other side.
"The cult isn't just breaking the seals," Kael said, backing away from the passage. "They're communicating with what's behind them."
The breathing continued. Unhurried. Waiting.
"We need to leave." He grabbed Lyra's arm. "Now."
They ran.
---
The jungle fought their retreat. Vines seemed to reach for their legs. Shadows deepened around them, moving against the wind. The echo-born creature from earlier reappeared, watching with eyes that reflected no light, and this time it didn't retreat.
"Company," Lyra gasped.
Three more shapes materialized from the undergrowth. Echo-born, all of them, drawn by the spiritual energy Kael had expended activating Detect Magic. They surrounded the path ahead, blocking the route back to Thornwick.
Behind them, the passage to the seal chamber exhaled cold air that smelled of copper and ozone.
Kael's mind raced. Combat against four echo-born in jungle terrain would drain resources he couldn't afford to waste. His Soul Reaver ability had zero spiritual cost, but physical exhaustion still applied, and Lyra couldn't fight indefinitely.
The first echo-born lunged.
Kael twisted, driving his elbow into what might have been a jaw. The creature's form shifted, refusing solid impact, flowing around his strike like water around stone. Cold radiated from its touch, and when his fingers brushed its surface, he felt the void beneath—Abyssal emptiness, hunger without end.
It wanted his essence. If it touched him long enough, it would drain him dry.
"Fire!" Lyra's blade flashed, and one of the echo-born shrieked as silver burned through its substance. Shadow Court training—her steel carried consecration residues effective against creatures of the deep Abyss.
Kael drew his own weapon—a short blade, nothing special, but he'd treated it with essence-trapping compounds harvested from previous kills. The steel hummed as he swung, and the echo-born recoiled from contact.
Not enough. Not nearly enough.
He activated Detect Magic again, scanning for weaknesses. The creatures carried no physical organs to damage—they were manifestations of spiritual hunger given temporary form. But the magic sustaining them had a source, a connection to the leaking seal below.
He could sever that connection.
Kael dropped low, letting the nearest echo-born's limb sweep over him, and slammed his palm against its center mass. Soul Reaver activation—no harvesting involved, just raw spiritual contact. He grabbed the thread connecting this creature to its power source and pulled.
The echo-born screamed. Its form destabilized, edges fraying, the void at its core exposed to air. Lyra's blade found that gap, and the creature dissolved into ash and silence.
One down. Three remaining.
The others grew more cautious, circling, waiting for an opening.
"Kael." Lyra's voice carried a warning. "More coming."
She was right. Through the jungle's murk, more shapes assembled. Six, eight, a dozen—all echo-born, drawn by the spiritual disturbance of combat. The seal's degradation had created a beacon, and he'd lit it bright enough to attract every hungry thing in the district.
"We can't fight that many."
"Can't fight, can't run, can't hide." He spotted a structure through the trees—an old watchtower, maybe fifty yards distant, its upper floors still intact. "There. Higher ground."
They broke for the tower together. The echo-born pursued, their movements synchronized in ways that suggested a hive intelligence operating beneath individual hunger. Kael cut down another with essence-severing while Lyra covered their retreat, her blade singing a counterpoint to the creatures' inhuman shrieks.
The tower's door hung from one hinge. They piled inside, slammed it shut, and Lyra braced her shoulder against the wood while Kael searched for a way to barricade it properly. A collapsed shelf provided timber; he wedged it against the entrance as something massive struck from outside.
The door shuddered but held.
"For now," Lyra said. Blood ran from a gash on her forearm—minor, but bleeding freely. "We need a plan."
Kael climbed the interior stairs, checking each landing. The upper floors were empty but intact—someone had fortified this place years ago, then abandoned it. Arrow slits offered view of the jungle below. The echo-born had surrounded the tower, at least twenty now, their forms pressed against the walls like parasites on a host.
His Detect Magic swept the structure. The walls carried old protective enchantments—faded but not dead. The tower had been designed as a seal-monitoring station, one of dozens dotting the original network's perimeter. Whoever had built it understood what they were fighting.
"The wards are still active," he called down to Lyra. "Faded, but I can reinforce them with enough essence."
"You're talking about powering a centuries-old defense system using your own reserves."
"I'm talking about using the echo-born's reserves. They came from the seal—some of their energy should be compatible with the original enchantments."
A long pause. Then: "That's either brilliant or suicidal."
"Usually both."
He descended to find Lyra barricading the stairs behind the initial door. She'd shifted into crisis mode, movements economical and precise. The Shadow Court had trained her for extraction scenarios; this qualified.
"If you drain yourself powering those wards, you'll be useless for the fight after."
"I know."
"And if the wards fail—"
"I know."
She stared at him. In the dim light filtering through the arrow slits, her expression cycled through frustration, acceptance, and something that might have been resignation.
"Then do it," she said. "I'll watch your back."
Kael pressed both palms against the tower wall, reaching for the dormant enchantments beneath centuries of neglect. The wards recognized his spiritual signature—Ascendant stage, Soul Reaver lineage, close enough to the original architects' intent that they responded to his touch.
He reached out with his Soul Reaver senses, finding the echo-born outside, their Abyssal energy humming against the seal-magic in the walls. The frequencies didn't match perfectly, but they were close enough to transfer.
He pulled.
Energy flooded into the tower. The walls began to glow, ancient sigils flaring to life along their surface. Outside, the echo-born shrieked as their sustaining power ripped away. One by one, they dissolved, their forms collapsing into the hungry soil that had birthed them.
Twenty creatures fell in as many seconds.
Kael sagged against the wall, drained but alive. The notifications scrolled through his awareness:
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 847 essence units from echo-born sources. Energy converted to ward reinforcement.]
[Soul Harvest: Structural enchantments restored to 34% capacity. Tower defense active.]
[Soul Harvest: Detect Magic skill expenditure refunded. Current spiritual reserves: 73%.]
Lyra emerged from the stairwell, blade still ready, and stopped when she saw the walls glowing. "You did it."
"For now." He straightened with effort. "The wards won't hold if more creatures come. And the cult—"
Footsteps echoed from above.
Both of them froze. The tower shouldn't have had anyone inside. They'd cleared it on the way up. Unless—
A voice drifted down through the central shaft. Male, cultured, carrying the particular resonance of Awakened power.
"The Soul Reaver comes to my doorstep. How convenient."
Kael's blood turned to ice.
Lyra drew her blade fully. "Who—"
A figure descended from the upper floors, moving with the casual confidence of someone who had nothing to prove. Middle-aged, sharp-featured, wearing robes that blended with shadow until he chose to be seen. His eyes caught the ward-light and reflected it back, ancient and cold.
"I've been watching you for some time, Kael Ashborn." The man smiled, and there was no warmth in it. "Watching you take my creations, harvest my cultists, build a reputation in my city."
"Your city?"
"Thornwick stands on foundations older than your imagination. The seal beneath this tower, the network that chains the Abyss, the architecture that keeps humanity alive—all of it mine, or my predecessor's. You walk through my house and call it your discovery."
Kael reached for his blade. The man raised a hand, and invisible pressure locked his muscles in place.
"Nightbringer," Kael forced out. "You're—"
"I am what remains when the old orders fall. I am what grows in the cracks they leave behind." The man's gaze shifted to Lyra. "And you, daughter of the Shadow Court. Your masters sent you to watch him. Did they tell you what happens when a Soul Reaver fully awakens? Did they warn you that the first Reaver tore apart the seals and bathed the world in Abyssal fire?"
Lyra's face went pale. "You're lying."
"Am I?" The pressure on Kael eased slightly—not released, just loosened. A reminder of who held the power here. "The original Soul Reaver built this network to cage the Entities. Then he turned on his creators, broke the largest seal, and nearly ended everything. The Orders barely stopped him. Barely."
Kael's mind raced. The hidden line—information about the original Soul Reaver's fate. This man knew something. Or claimed to know something. Truth or manipulation, he couldn't tell yet.
"Why tell us this?"
"Because you need to understand what you are. What you could become." Nightbringer stepped closer, close enough that Kael could see the of old scars beneath his robes. "The cult you discovered, the rituals in the jungle—my people aren't breaking the seals. We're preparing them. The old barriers were designed to hold for a thousand years; we're ensuring they hold for ten thousand. The sacrifices, the ceremonies, the blood you saw them draw—"
"Was for sealing, not unsealing."
"Was for reinforcement. The Entities grow stronger as the Veil thins. My predecessor understood this. He built the network not to contain the Abyss forever, but to give humanity time to evolve, to grow strong enough to win. But the Orders forgot his vision. They turned the seals into monuments instead of tools."
Kael didn't believe him. Couldn't afford to believe him. But the information asymmetry gnawed at his thoughts—this man knew things about Soul Reavers, about the original network, about the seals that Kael desperately needed.
"How do I know you're not the one lying about the seals?"
Nightbringer's smile widened. "You don't. That's the beauty of faith, boy. You choose what to believe, and you live with the consequences." He stepped back, raising both hands. "Kill me if you like. My cult will continue. The work will persist. But consider this: the Order of Dawn knows about you. The Iron Vanguard has agents in Thornwick tracking your movements. The Arcane Spire has placed a kill order through the Veiled Market. You're three days from becoming the most wanted Soul Reaver on the continent."
The wards pulsed. Someone—or something—was approaching the tower from outside. Multiple presences, carrying the distinct signature of Awakened power.
"Company," Lyra whispered. "Coming fast."
"Then our conversation ends." Nightbringer turned toward the shadows at the tower's edge. "We'll meet again, Kael Ashborn. The game has only begun, and you have much to learn about what you carry in your blood."
He stepped into darkness and vanished.
Kael lunged after him, but his muscles still moved sluggishly, the residual effect of whatever power Nightbringer had used. By the time he'd cleared the stairs, the man was gone.
Outside, the new arrivals resolved into forms. Six Awakened, organized in a standard Order pursuit formation. Their leader carried the insignia of the Order of Dawn on her armor.
"Soul Reaver Kael Ashborn." Her voice rang with authority. "By order of the Council of Six, you are to surrender yourself for violations of the Concordat and crimes against the seal network."
Lyra appeared at his shoulder. "We're boxed in. Tower's surrounded, and whoever that was just made us public enemy number one."
Kael's mind worked through the options. Six Awakened, all probably higher-stage than his current Ascendant. Lyra, exhausted from the echo-born fight. The wards, holding but not invincible. And somewhere out there, Nightbringer, with his claims about seals and history and a vision of humanity's future that might be truth or might be poison.
He had one thing the Order agents didn't know about.
He could harvest from the living.
"Soul Reaver." The Dawn leader stepped forward. "Last chance. Come quietly, and you'll face tribunal. Resist, and—"
Kael smiled.
He'd always wondered if his ability worked on the living.
Time to find out.
"What happens if I refuse?"
