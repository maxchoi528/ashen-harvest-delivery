Blood painted the cobblestones in patterns that defied coincidence.
Kael dropped from the rooftop, landing in a crouch beside the latest corpse. Three cultists lay arranged in a triangle, their robes torn, their faces locked in silent screams. Whatever ritual they'd attempted had gone catastrophically wrong. Steam hissed from a broken pipe overhead, fogging the narrow alley in white wisps.
"Fourth site this week," he muttered. "Same configuration. Same failure."
The bodies were still warm. Kael reached down, pressing his palm flat against the chest of the nearest cultist. Cold flesh met his fingers, but the spiritual residue told a different story—fresh, potent, thrumming with borrowed power.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted residual essence from the remains. 3 Spiritual Essence Fragments acquired.]
Fragments. Not skills, not memories. Just scraps of power leaking from overloaded channels. These cultists had tried to draw from something beyond their capacity and burned themselves out in the flow.
Kael stood, scanning the rooftops. Lantern light flickered through the steam, casting jagged shadows across brick walls. Thornwick never slept, but it flinched at night. Always had, even before the seals started failing.
Footsteps echoed from the main thoroughfare—measured, deliberate. Not random citizens cutting through the alley. Someone expecting to find something. Someone who knew where to look.
Kael melted into the shadows between two warehouses, pressing his back against damp stone. His Divine senses stretched outward, reading the spiritual signature approaching. Strong. Trained. Awakened, definitely. But not hostile—not yet.
The figure emerged from the fog. Tall, angular, dressed in the charcoal garb of someone who preferred not to be remembered. A hood shadowed her features, but Kael caught the glint of silver at her collar—a pin shaped like a serpent eating its own tail.
Ouroboros. The symbol of the Shadow Court.
She stopped three paces from the bodies, surveying the scene with the detachment of someone cataloging inventory. Her head tilted, just slightly, and Kael felt the weight of spiritual perception brush against him like cold fingers.
"You're breathing loudly for someone trying to hide," she said.
Kael stepped out of the shadows. "You're obvious for someone who works for shadows."
A smile ghosted across her features. "Kael Ashborn. The Soul Reaver. We've been watching you."
"Flattering."
"Terrifying,." She reached into her cloak and produced a small leather folder. "My name is Seren. I represent interests that find your... Occupation... To be a valuable asset than a threat to be eliminated."
"Interests. You mean the Shadow Court."
"I mean those who recognize that the Orders are failing. That the Concordat is theater. That the 72 seals are cracking faster than anyone in power will admit." She extended the folder. "This contains information. Locations of cult activity. Ritual sites. Names of those who've made deals with entities beyond the Veil."
Kael didn't take it. "What's the price?"
"Information flows both ways. You encounter something, you share what you learn. We encounter something, we share with you. A partnership of mutual benefit."
"Why approach me? Why not just watch from a distance and steal whatever I harvest?"
"Because you're inefficient." Seren's voice carried no insult, only clinical assessment. "You harvest common cultists and walk away from the real prizes. The entities they're trying to summon. The power that's worth consuming."
Something cold settled in Kael's gut. "You're suggesting I harvest Abyssal entities."
"I'm suggesting you understand what you're dealing with." She set the folder on a nearby crate. "The cultists in Thornwick aren't random lunatics. They're organized. Funded. Directed by someone with genuine knowledge of the seal network. Someone who knows exactly which rifts are weakening and when."
She turned to leave, then paused. "The Order of Dawn's Thornwick chapter is compromised. The Iron Vanguard's garrison commander takes bribes to look away. The Arcane Spire lost three researchers last month—they've stopped reporting it. Read the file. Then decide if your pride is worth dying alone."
Seren vanished into the steam as completely as if she'd never existed.
Kael stared at the leather folder for a long moment. His fingers itched with the urge to take it, to tear it open and devour whatever secrets it contained. Information was currency in Thornwick. Information about cult activity, about weakening seals, about which rifts were about to blow—
Could change everything.
But accepting help meant accepting strings. And the Shadow Court didn't make charitable investments.
A scream shattered the night.
Not the theatrical shriek of a frightened citizen. This was raw, guttural, torn from lungs that had just encountered something incomprehensible. It came from the east—toward the merchant district, toward the crowds.
Kael moved.
He ran across rooftops, leaping gaps that would have killed a lesser Awakened. The scream cut off abruptly, replaced by a silence more terrifying than the sound itself. Below, people ran. Carriages overturned. Someone fired a pistol into the air for no reason Kael could discern.
Then he saw it.
A rift hung in the air above the Thornwick plaza like a wound that refused to close. Black corruption pulsed at its edges, tendrils of wrongness reaching downward. The seal—a massive sigil of interwoven symbols maintained by the Order of Dawn—still functioned, but cracks spider-webbed across its surface. Every few seconds, a fragment of the seal flaked away and dissolved into nothing.
Not natural degradation. Something was eating it.
Beneath the rift, cultists in blood-red robes formed a circle. Three of them held chains forged from something that wasn't metal, each link inscribed with symbols that hurt to perceive. The chains connected to a figure at the center of the circle—someone who had once been human.
Now the figure blazed with light that wasn't light. Shadows bent around it. The air tasted of ozone and copper and something older, something that predated language.
"Lord of the First Veil," one cultist chanted. "We offer this vessel. We offer this sacrifice. We offer the blood of your enemies. Break the chains that bind you. Return to the world that wronged you."
The vessel screamed again—that same raw sound, but filtered through a throat that no longer entirely belonged to a human throat.
Kael's Divine senses screamed warnings. The entity being summoned was old. Powerful. Hungry. If the cultists completed their ritual, that thing would tear through the seal like paper and drag half of Thornwick into the Abyss with it.
He needed to act. Now.
The cultists numbered seven. Three holding chains, four chanting. All Awakened, all channeling enough power to make the air vibrate. Kael was Divine—stronger than any of them individually—but taking on seven trained Awakened while trying to interrupt a ritual was suicide.
Unless he didn't fight them at all.
Kael dropped from the rooftop, landing among the chanting cultists. The nearest one turned, mouth open to shout an alarm. Kael's hand closed around her throat before she could make a sound. He wrenched her off her feet and slammed her into the cobblestones.
"Silence," he said.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Memory Shard from the remains. Knowledge acquired: Ritual Interruption Protocol.]
Not much. Fragments of training, mostly. But among the fragments was something useful—a cultist's understanding of how this specific ritual worked. The chant wasn't the binding element. The chains were. The vessel was. The chant was just... Noise. Aesthetic. The cultists believed they needed it, but the real work happened through the chains and the blood-soaked ground.
Kael released the cultist's body and spun toward the three holding chains. They froze, caught between maintaining the ritual and defending themselves.
"Stand down," Kael said. "Walk away. You'll live."
"You don't understand," one of them spat. "What we're summoning will—"
"I understand perfectly." Kael cracked his neck. "You're trying to rip open a hole in reality to let in something that's been imprisoned for a thousand years. Congratulations. You might succeed. And then you'll be the first things it eats, because it doesn't care about your ideology or your sacrifice or your noble cause. It's hungry. That's all."
"Faith protects us."
"Faith doesn't stop entropy." Kael gestured at the vessel, still thrashing at the end of the chains. "Look at your sacrifice. Does that look protected to you? Does that look like someone chosen for greatness?"
The cultist hesitated. Doubt flickered across his face.
The vessel exploded with light.
Kael threw himself backward as waves of corrupted energy swept outward. Two cultists nearest the center ceased to exist—not killed, not destroyed, just... Gone, their souls consumed in an instant. The remaining cultists screamed, their chains glowing cherry-red as the entity fought against its constraints.
The seal above cracked further. Major chunks flaked away, tumbling into the void below.
"NO!" The lead cultist grabbed for the chains, trying to maintain control. "Hold the binding! Hold—"
The entity laughed.
It wasn't a human sound. It was the sound of vast distances collapsing, of ancient pain finally finding release, of something vast and terrible remembering what it was like to be whole.
The chains shattered.
Kael felt the impact of raw spiritual force slam into his chest like a physical blow. He staggered, his Divine reserves absorbing the worst of it, but pain lanced through his skull nonetheless. Around him, the remaining cultists flew backward, crashing into market stalls and stone facades.
The entity turned toward him.
It had no fixed form—shifting constantly between shapes that almost made sense, almost resembled something from the human nightmares of a pre-linguistic era. Eyes opened and closed across its surface, each one focused on Kael with predatory intelligence.
"Soul Reaver," it said, and the voice resonated in frequencies that made Kael's teeth ache. "I taste your hunger. I taste what you take from the dead. How delicious. How... Familiar."
The entity moved faster than anything that size should be able to move. A tendril of shadow-wrapped-light whipped toward Kael's face.
He dodged. Barely. The tendril carved a furrow through the cobblestones where his head had been.
"Running won't help," the entity said. "I've waited centuries for this moment. I've learned patience in my cage. I know how you work, little reaver. I know your tricks. You touch the dead, you take what they carry. But I am not dead. I never was. I am beyond your reach."
Kael drew his blade—a simple steel sword, nothing special, purchased for six shillings from a street vendor. It wouldn't scratch an Abyssal entity. He knew that.
But the blade wasn't the weapon. The blade was the distraction.
He charged.
The entity laughed again and struck, tendrils whipping toward him in patterns designed to dismember. Kael twisted, turned, used every ounce of his Divine-enhanced reflexes to thread between attacks that should have killed him a dozen times over.
The blade arced toward the entity's shifting mass.
It passed through, of course. Steel meant nothing to something that existed partially outside the physical realm.
But Kael wasn't trying to cut it. He was trying to touch it.
His free hand shot out, grabbing a tendril of semi-solid shadow as it whipped past. His Soul Reaver ability activated—had to activate, had to work, because nothing else would—
Cold.
Absolute, agonizing cold flooded through Kael's palm. Not the cold of winter or of death. The cold of the void between stars, of the space between realities, of the moment before creation when nothing existed and nothing could exist.

[ERROR: Target does not meet corpse requirements.]
[ERROR: Target is not deceased.]
[ERROR: Target is not bound by conventional soul mechanics.]
[HARVEST FAILED]
Kael released the tendril and threw himself backward. His hand was numb, frost-blackened from fingertip to wrist. The entity watched him with what might have been amusement.
"Told you," it said. "Beyond your reach."
"I know," Kael said.
He hadn't expected it to work. He'd expected exactly this failure. But in the moment of contact, he'd felt something else. A thread of connection. A link between the entity and the physical world that the ritual had established.
The chains.
The chains had been forged from something that existed partially in the Material realm. The cultists had used them to drag the entity's presence through the Veil. Even broken, they still anchored parts of the entity to the physical plane.
Parts that could be touched. Parts that might—might—be harvestable.
The entity lunged.
Kael didn't dodge. He ran toward it, closing the distance instead of creating it. The tendril that should have taken his head off passed over his shoulder, close enough to slice his ear, drawing blood that steamed in the corrupted air.
He hit the ground, sliding between two broken chain links that lay sparking on the cobblestones.
His hands closed around the metal.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Fragment of Binding from the remains. The fragment contains: 1) Partial memory of the entity's imprisonment, 2) Residual power channeled through the binding during summoning, 3) Echo of the ritual's original intent.]
[Soul Harvest: WARNING. Entity essence detected in fragment. Extended contact may result in corruption.]
Kael ignored the warning.
Information flooded through him. Not skills or abilities—something better. Knowledge. The entity's knowledge. A thousand years of imprisonment, of planning, of waiting. The entity had watched through the Veil as its jailers grew complacent, as the Orders turned inward, as the seal network was maintained by people more interested in political advantage than cosmic responsibility.
The entity knew things. The entity knew where the other seals were weakening. The entity knew which of the 72 rifts would fall next, and the next, and the next. The entity knew—
An explosion of light interrupted the download.
Kael looked up to see the remaining cultists—one, two, three of them—throwing themselves at the entity. They weren't attacking. They were sacrificing. Each one that touched the entity's shifting mass was consumed instantly, feeding it more power, more presence, more ability to exist in the Material realm.
"FOOL!" the entity roared. "You cannot destroy me! I am eternal! I am beyond your—"
The entity's form shuddered. Contracted. For a moment, it looked almost solid, almost real, almost trapped in a shape that could be comprehended.
Kael saw his opening.
He surged to his feet and lunged, both hands grabbing the entity's mass. The cold hit him again, worse than before, driving ice shards into his skull and freezing his lungs from the inside. His vision blurred. His thoughts scattered.
But his Soul Reaver ability held.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted SHADOW VEIL from the remains. Entity ability acquired. Shadow Veil allows the user to temporarily phase partially out of the Material realm, becoming difficult to perceive and harder to hit. Duration: a few heartbeats. Cooldown: several minutes.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted FRAGMENT OF THE VOID from the remains. Spiritual essence equivalent: 47 soul-essence vials. WARNING: Purity level below standard. Purification recommended before use.]
Kael released his grip and fell backward as the entity screamed.
It was still alive—barely. Still present—enough. But the extraction had cost it. The cultists' sacrifices had given it enough power to break the chains, not enough to survive having that power ripped away.
The entity contracted further, its form destabilizing, edges flickering between presence and absence.
"This isn't over," it hissed. "The seals are failing. The 72 will rise. Your world will drown in the Abyss, and you—you will be the first to fall. I will remember your face, Soul Reaver. I will remember your taste."
It tore through the damaged seal and vanished into the void beyond.
The rift shuddered, groaned, and began to close. Cracks in the seal network sealed themselves—or tried to. The damage was done. This rift would be weaker now, easier to open again.
Kael lay on his back, staring at the smoke-filled sky. His hands wouldn't stop shaking. Frost still coated his fingers, slowly melting in the ambient heat of the burning market stalls around him.
People were screaming in the distance. Running. Fleeing toward the city gates. The Order of Dawn's bells would ring soon—they always did, after an entity incursion, no matter how minor. The political theater would begin anew. Accusations. Denials. Cover-ups.
And somewhere in the chaos, Seren was watching. The Shadow Court was always watching.
Kael sat up slowly, wincing as his ribs protested. The cultist file still waited on that crate in the alley. Seren had been right about one thing—the cultists weren't random. This ritual had required resources, knowledge, coordination. Someone was building toward something larger.
The entity's memories surfaced through the extraction fog. A name. A location. A timeline.
Varek. The Whisper King. The architect of everything.
Kael pushed himself to his feet. His Shadow Veil ability pulsed at the edge of his consciousness, a new tool waiting to be tested. His hands had stopped shaking—the frost had melted completely, leaving only pale skin that would bruise purple by morning.
He had work to do. Information to verify. Cultists to hunt.
The Shadow Court would have to wait.
Kael limped toward the alley where he'd left Seren, where the leather folder still waited on its crate. Behind him, the rift finished closing with a sound like reality exhaling. The entity was gone, but not destroyed.
They never were.
A figure stepped out of the shadows ahead.
Not Seren. Someone else. Someone wearing the insignia of the Order of Dawn—specifically, the Internal Purity Division. Her armor was scorched, her face splattered with blood that might have been her own.
"By authority of the Order," she said, blade drawn, "you are under arrest for unauthorized interference in seal maintenance protocols. Surrender your weapons and submit to—"
"Or what?" Kael asked.
The officer's jaw tightened. "Or we use force."
"You're the second person tonight to underestimate what that means." Kael raised his hand, letting the frost still clinging to his fingers sparkle in the lantern light. "I just harvested an Abyssal entity. I have abilities you've never seen. And I know for a fact that your Internal Purity Division has been taking bribes to look away from cult activity in the eastern district."
The officer's expression flickered. Just for a moment. But it was enough.
"Seren was right," Kael said. "You're compromised. So let's skip the part where we pretend otherwise. You can try to arrest me and die in the attempt. You can walk away and report that you found nothing. Or—" he reached into his coat and pulled out a handful of soul-essence vials, each one glowing faintly, "—you can take this payment and forget my face exists."
Soul-essence. Forty-seven vials' worth, according to the extraction notification. Worth more than most people saw in a lifetime.
The officer stared at the vials. Her blade trembled.
"Last chance," Kael said.
The blade lowered.
Kael tossed her two vials—one for the initial silence, one for any future inconvenience—and walked past. Behind him, he heard the officer's footsteps receding into the fog.
The leather folder waited where Seren had left it.
Kael picked it up, opened it, and began to read. Names. Locations. Dates. The organizational structure of cult activity in Thornwick, mapped in meticulous detail. This wasn't amateur work. This was intelligence gathering on a scale that required resources the Shadow Court didn't officially possess.
Someone powerful wanted the cultists stopped. Someone powerful enough to fund this operation without the Shadow Court's handlers knowing. Someone who had access to Order records, patrol schedules, sealed case files.
Someone who was playing a game Kael didn't yet understand.
The file's final page contained a single line of text, written in elegant script:
"The Whisper King moves his pieces. You are a piece now. Choose your role carefully."
Kael closed the file and tucked it inside his coat.
Outside the alley, Thornwick burned and bled and screamed. The entity was gone, but its presence had destabilized the local seal network. More cultists would move to exploit the weakness. More entities would test the barriers. More ordinary people would die in the crossfire between forces they couldn't perceive.
Kael would hunt them all. Harvest what he could. Leave the rest to rot.
But Seren had been right about something else. He couldn't do this alone. The cultists were organized. The Orders were compromised. The Shadow Court had its own agenda.
And somewhere in the background, the Whisper King moved his pieces.
Kael stepped out of the alley and into the firelit chaos of Thornwick's nightmare. His Shadow Veil ability hummed at the edge of his consciousness. His frost-burned hands ached with cold that would take days to fade.
The 72 Entities were stirring. The seals were failing. The world was ending, slowly, in increments too small for most people to notice.
But Kael noticed.
And now he had new abilities to test.
He vanished into the steam and shadows, hunting.
Somewhere in the distance, a child cried for a mother who would never answer.
