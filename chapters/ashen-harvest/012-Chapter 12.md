The blade missed Kael's throat by a finger's width.
He rolled left, boots skidding on wet cobblestones, and felt the assassin's second strike whistle past his ear. Three opponents. Professional. They moved like a single organism, covering angles, cutting off escape routes. Not street thugs. Someone had sent killers who knew what they were doing.
"Fall back to the alley," one of them hissed.
Kael didn't fall back. He lunged forward instead, using their surprise as a shield. His shoulder caught the nearest man in the chest, driving them both into the wall. The impact rattled teeth. The assassin's knife clattered away into darkness.
[Combat Detected — Engaging Soul Sight]
The world shifted. Kael's vision flooded with colors that shouldn't exist—life-energy blooming in the narrow alley like phosphorescent moss. The two standing attackers burned bright. The one pinned beneath Kael flickered, his reserves guttering toward extinction.
"Soul Harvest available," the system whispered. "Proceed?"
No time for hesitation. The pinned assassin convulsed beneath him, reaching for a secondary blade. Kael slammed his palm against the man's chest.
[System Alert] Target alive. Harvest aborting.
The realization struck like ice water. He couldn't harvest from the living. Only corpses. Only the dead held their secrets still.
The pinned assassin drove an elbow into Kael's ribs. Pain lanced through his side as he stumbled backward. The two standing killers closed in, their expressions cold beneath half-masks of black silk.
"Where did you learn to fight?" one asked.
Kael wiped blood from his split lip. "Didn't learn. Survived."
The remaining two assassins advanced in practiced formation. Behind them, the alley mouth gaped like a wound onto a lamplit street. Kael's gaze darted between the killers, calculating angles and odds. Three against one. Awakened stages unknown. His own reserves felt thin after the previous night's harvest—the fight against whatever had crawled out of that disturbed grave.
Then a whistle cut through the alley.
"Ho there! Thornwick Watch! We've had reports of disturbance!"
Torchlight flared at both ends of the narrow passage. Uniformed figures appeared, halberds raised, their faces hard with official suspicion. The three assassins exchanged glances. Silent communication passed between them.
"Retreat," one commanded.
They moved as water flowing backward, scaling walls with preternatural grace, vanishing over rooftop edges before the Watch could close distance. Kael started to breathe again.
The lead Watch officer, a thick-necked man with a scar bisecting his left eyebrow, approached with measured steps. His eyes swept the alley—the fallen knife, the scuffle marks, the blood on the cobblestones—before settling on Kael with uncomfortable intensity.
"You the cause of this ruckus, then?" His tone suggested he already knew the answer.
"I'm the victim." Kael held up his hands, palms out, showing his bloodied knuckles. "Three men jumped me. I'm just trying to get back to my lodgings."
"Mm." The officer didn't sound convinced. "Papers."
Kael's stomach dropped. His execution records had been sealed, but the documents that proved his citizenship before that unfortunate business had burned in the same fire that supposedly killed him. The forged papers Sera had provided were good, but not Watch-quality inspection good.
"Problem?" The officer's hand drifted toward his whistle.
"Problem?" A woman's voice, sharp as broken glass, cut through the tension. "Officer, this man was attacked in your precinct. Maybe you should focus on the criminals than the victim?"
Kael turned. A figure emerged from the watching crowd—tall, angular, dressed in practical traveling clothes that had seen better roads. Her face was striking than beautiful: sharp cheekbones, intelligent eyes the color of tarnished copper, and a scar tracing her jawline like a river finding its path to the sea. She carried herself with the easy confidence of someone accustomed to being listened to.
The officer's posture shifted almost imperceptibly. Not respect, but acknowledgment. "Doctor Penrose. We weren't aware you were in the city."
"And I wasn't aware Thornwick Watch had started harassing citizens in alleys." She produced a leather credential folio, flashed it with practiced speed. "I'm conducting research under Order of Dawn patronage. This man is my assistant. If you have questions about his documentation, direct them to the Order's registrar."
The threat implicit in those words landed like a hammer. Order patronage meant legal immunity from local interference. The officer's jaw worked silently.
"No questions, Doctor. My apologies for the inconvenience." He touched his cap and gestured to his men. "Move along, then. Nothing to see."
The crowd dispersed. Kael found himself standing alone with the woman—Adelaide Penrose, apparently—in the empty alley. Lamplight caught the dust in her dark hair, the calluses on her hands that spoke of more than book learning.
"You just saved me from an interrogation I couldn't survive," Kael said carefully.
"I secured an asset before it could be confiscated." She studied him with unsettling intensity. "You have the look, Kael Ashborn. The execution that wasn't. The hunger that doesn't fade. I've seen others like you."
He went very still. "What look is that?"
"The Soul Reaver look." She smiled, thin and humorless. "Don't worry. I'm not with the hunters. I have far more interesting motivations." She gestured down the lamplit street. "Walk with me. We have much to discuss, and standing in alleys makes me nervous. Especially alleys where people have recently tried to kill someone."
They walked. Adelaide set a pace that suggested familiarity with the city's maze-like layout, taking turns at random intervals, occasionally doubling back through shortcuts that existed only to those who knew where to look. Kael followed, alert for ambush, hyper-aware of every shadow and window.
"You're testing me," he observed.
"Observing. There's a difference." She didn't slow her pace. "You noticed the Watch's approach before I whistled. You identified the lead assassin's command voice. You tracked all three simultaneously despite chaos. These are Reaver instincts. The hunger that reads death in the air."
"I'm not—"
"Don't." She stopped abruptly at a narrow intersection where three streets converged. "I've spent twelve years studying Soul Reavers. The history, the abilities, the political implications. I know what they look like when they don't know what they are. You're at the awakening stage. Raw power without training. Dangerous to yourself and others."
The words stung because they carried truth. Kael thought of the grave-thing he'd destroyed last night. The way his hunger had pulled him toward the dying soldier. The combat system that had activated without his consent.
"What do you want?" he asked.
"Knowledge. Understanding." Adelaide resumed walking. "And maybe, if you're not entirely suicidal, a solution to a pressing problem."
"Explain."
"Not here. Too many ears." She turned down a side street where buildings leaned toward each other overhead, blocking the night sky. "We're almost there."
The Veiled Market existed in the spaces between spaces. Kael had heard rumors—underground bazaars where the forbidden changed hands, where stolen goods found buyers, where anything could be purchased for the right price. He'd never believed them. He'd been wrong.
The entrance was a condemned warehouse near the harbor district, its windows boarded, its doors chained. Adelaide led him around back, where a rusted loading dock concealed a pressure plate. The mechanism groaned. A section of wall swung inward on counterweights that had been oiled recently.
"Welcome to the Veiled Market," Adelaide said. "Don't stare at anything too long. Don't ask prices openly. And whatever you do, don't mention the Orders by name unless you want to start an incident."
The market sprawled through multiple connected chambers that had once been storage cellars and processing rooms. Hundreds of stalls lined pathways carved between stacked crates and hanging sheets that served as walls. Lanterns burned with colored flames—blue, green, violet—casting the scene in hues that made the eye ache.
Kael smelled incense and blood, ozone and something sweet-rotten. Goods arrayed on tables defied easy categorization: crystal vials glowing with trapped light, leather-bound tomes with covers that seemed to writhe, weapons that hummed with barely-contained energy, fabrics that shifted colors as they moved.
"Soul-essence," Adelaide murmured, nodding toward a stall where silver vials sat in rows. "Standard quality. Harvested from the dying with ritual assistance. Perfectly legal under Mortician's Guild charter."
"And that?" Kael pointed to another table where the vials burned with darker luminescence.
"Awakened-sourced. Significantly more valuable. Significantly more illegal." She steered him past. "Don't even think about it. Your face is too new here. You purchase anything, you'll be remembered."
They navigated deeper into the market's heart. Adelaide greeted several vendors by name, exchanged comments in what sounded like a trade cant, and eventually stopped at a stall near the back where an older woman sat surrounded by books and scrolls.
"Eliza." Adelaide's voice warmed slightly. "I've brought someone you should meet."
The woman—Eliza—looked up from a yellowed manuscript. Her face was weathered leather over sharp bones, her eyes the gray of winter storms. Gray hair pulled back severely. Hands that had clearly done hard work despite their current delicate occupation.
"This is him?" Eliza's gaze raked over Kael with unsettling thoroughness. "The one who burned down the execution platform?"
Kael's hand moved instinctively toward his belt, where a knife no longer hung. "How do you know about that?"
"I know many things, boy." Eliza gestured to a stool. "Sit. Answer questions. Then maybe we all get what we want."
He sat. Adelaide remained standing, arms crossed, watching.
"Tell me about the fire," Eliza said. "The night you were supposed to die."
The story came out in pieces. Kael spoke of waking in darkness, of flames consuming his bindings, of chaos and screaming and running through smoke-thick streets. He described the hunger, the pull toward the dying, the first harvest from a body he'd found in an alley. He didn't mention the system's messages by name—some instinct told him to keep that particular detail private—but he described the sensation of taking power from the dead.
Eliza listened without interruption. Her expression remained unreadable throughout.
"The original Soul Reaver," she said finally. "Have you heard of him?"
Kael shook his head.
"First recorded Soul Reaver appeared approximately four hundred years ago. No name survived, or if it did, it's been deliberately erased. He was a soldier in the Abyssal Wars. Fought against the Entities. Died at the Battle of Murkhallow." Eliza tapped one long finger against the manuscript. "Or so the official histories claim."
"You think there's more," Adelaide said.
"I think there's always more." Eliza's gray eyes fixed on Kael. "The first Reaver created something. A technique, a ritual, a way of harvesting death that shouldn't exist. The Orders hunted him. Killed him. Then spent the next four centuries hunting anyone who demonstrated similar abilities."
"The six Orders," Kael said slowly. "They're the organizations that maintain the seals against the Abyss."
Adelaide nodded. "The Order of Dawn, the Iron Vanguard, the Arcane Spire, the Verdant Circle, the Mortician's Guild, and the Shadow Court. Each controls access to spiritual power. Each maintains one of the six aspects of the seal network. Together, they hold the 72 rifts shut."
"72 rifts," Kael repeated. The number resonated strangely, like a half-remembered dream. "Each rift contains an Entity?"
"Each rift is a prison. The Entities are the original inhabitants of what lies beyond the Veil." Eliza closed her manuscript. "They want out. The Orders keep them in. It's been that way for centuries."
"Until recently," Adelaide added grimly. "The seals are weakening. Faster than projected. Faster than they should be."
Kael thought of the disturbance at the cemetery. The grave-thing that had crawled from disturbed earth. "Why?"
"That's what we're trying to determine." Eliza rose from her seat, joints cracking. "Cult activity has increased tenfold in the past year. The Whisper King's followers—you've heard of them?"
The name sent ice through Kael's veins. He'd heard whispers in the prison before his execution. Guards speaking of a shadow organization that sought to tear open the seals and release what waited beyond.
"I've heard the name," he admitted.
"The Whisper King wants to break the seals. Open all 72 rifts simultaneously. Let the Entities flood into our world." Eliza moved to a different stack of manuscripts, pulling one free. "We don't know why. We don't know how he plans to accomplish it. But we know he's been systematically targeting the seal locations. Several have already been damaged."
"This affects you how?" Kael asked. "Why do you care about the seals? Why help me?"
Eliza turned. In the colored lamplight, her weathered face looked ancient, tired, fierce.
"Because I'm dying, boy. Stage four soul depletion. I've got maybe two years before my reserves empty completely." She held up a hand that trembled slightly. "I've spent my life studying the old ways. The original techniques. If the seals break, if the Entities come through, everything I've worked for means nothing. The knowledge dies with this world."
"And me?" Kael gestured at himself. "What do you want from the Soul Reaver?"
This time, Adelaide answered. "Training. Control. The ability to harvest without destroying yourself or alerting the entire Order of Dawn." She stepped closer. "Soul Reavers are the only ones who can interact directly with the Entities. If the seals fail, if we need someone who can fight what's coming—"
"You want me as a weapon."
"We want you as an ally," Adelaide corrected. "Weapons are expendable. Allies are valuable."
Kael stood. His legs ached. His side throbbed where the assassin's blow had landed. He was exhausted, hunted, wanted for crimes he hadn't committed, carrying powers he didn't understand in a world that wanted him dead.
But standing in this hidden market, surrounded by forbidden knowledge and dangerous people, he felt something he hadn't experienced since waking from his own execution.
Hope.
"What would I need to do?" he asked.
Eliza smiled—the first genuine expression he'd seen from her. "First, you learn to control what you are. No more random harvests. No more feeding the hunger without purpose. Second, you help us investigate the seal degradation. Third—" She paused. "Third, we find out what really happened to the original Soul Reaver. Because I don't believe the Orders' story. Someone created this ability. Someone knows how it works. And that information might be the key to saving us all."
"Or dooming us," Adelaide added quietly.
"Everything has risk." Eliza handed Kael the manuscript she'd pulled. "Start with this. Basic soul theory. The Law of Conservation, spiritual circulation, the membrane between realms. Read it. Understand it. Come back tomorrow night. We'll begin actual training."
Kael accepted the manuscript. The pages felt heavy with secrets.
"One more thing," Adelaide said. "Those assassins tonight. They weren't random."
"Who sent them?"
"We don't know. But they were Awakened. Professional. Well-equipped." Her expression darkened. "Someone knows about you. Someone wants you dead before you can develop. That's information we need to factor into our planning."
"They'll try again," Kael said.
"Yes. They will." Adelaide met his eyes. "But now you have allies. That changes the calculation."
They left the Veiled Market through a different exit—a tunnel that emerged three streets away from the entrance, opening into a quiet residential district where gas lamps cast pools of amber light on empty sidewalks. Kael breathed the cold night air, feeling the manuscript's weight in his hands, Adelaide walking beside him.
"Can you trust them?" he asked.
"Eliza? Probably. She's dying and wants her life's work to mean something. Myself?" Adelaide laughed softly. "I trust that she and I want the same outcome. The seals staying closed. The Entities contained. Beyond that, we're all operating on incomplete information."
"That's not reassuring."
"It's not meant to be." She stopped at a corner where two streets diverged. "Your lodgings are east, I assume. The Order of Dawn's satellite chapter is west, but you don't want to go there yet. Too many questions about your documentation."
"Sera provided papers—"
"Sera's papers are good. But Order scrutiny is better. Best to avoid contact until we understand what resources they have." Adelaide produced a small brass token, pressed it into his palm. "If you're in genuine danger, if they come for you again, use this at any market stall. They'll get word to me."
Kael studied the token. Simple design. No identifying marks.
"Why help me?" he asked again. "You could have walked away. Let the Watch take me. Let the assassins finish the job. Why step in?"
Adelaide was quiet for a long moment. When she spoke, her voice had lost its sharp edges.
"Because I've seen what happens when Soul Reavers develop without guidance. They burn bright, then they burn out. The Orders hunt them down, or they die fighting, or they—" She stopped. Started again. "I made a promise once. To someone who didn't survive. That I'd help the next one."
"Who was it?"
"Another Reaver. Years ago." Adelaide's jaw tightened. "The Orders caught him. I wasn't fast enough. He died in a cell, soul depleted, before I could arrange escape. His name was Marcus."
Marcus. Kael filed the name away. Another Reaver. Another victim of the Orders' fear.
"I'm sorry," he said.
"Don't be sorry. Be careful." Adelaide turned west, her boots clicking against cobblestones. "Learn fast, Kael. Get strong. Because what's coming will make tonight's assassins look like children playing with toys."
She disappeared into the gaslit darkness.
Kael stood alone at the intersection, the manuscript heavy in his hands, the brass token warm in his palm. Somewhere in the city, assassins reported their failure to faceless employers. Somewhere, the seals continued their slow degradation. Somewhere, the Whisper King plotted to unmake the barrier between worlds.
And somewhere, in a cemetery that had burned and a grave that had been disturbed, something ancient stirred in the darkness.
He began walking toward his lodgings, footsteps echoing off shuttered storefronts. The night pressed close. The hunger simmered beneath his skin, patient and growing.
Tomorrow, training would begin. Tomorrow, he would learn what it meant to be a Soul Reaver in a world that hunted his kind.
But tonight, he had allies. Tonight, he had knowledge. Tonight, he would read the manuscript and begin understanding the power that had awakened in his blood.
Tomorrow, the real work would start.
He reached the boarding house as the church bells tolled midnight. Slipped inside through the back entrance. Climbed the narrow stairs to his rented room. Locked the door behind him, checked the window latch, ensured the curtains were tight.
Then Kael Ashborn sat on his bed, opened the manuscript to the first page, and began to read.
The words swam before his eyes at first—technical terminology from a discipline he barely understood. Soul-energy circulation. Membrane dynamics. The Law of Conservation applied to spiritual reserves. But slowly, pattern emerged from chaos.
He read about the six Orders and their shared responsibility. The seal network that maintained the barrier between realms. The 72 rifts, each containing an Entity that had been imprisoned during the Abyssal Wars. The slow, inevitable decay that threatened everything.
He read about Soul Reavers. Not much—the Orders had burned most records—but what remained painted a terrifying picture. Harvesters of death. Thieves of soul-energy. Users of power that should not exist.
But also, potential weapons. The only ones capable of interacting directly with Entities. The key to understanding what lay beyond the Veil.
By the time exhaustion claimed him, Kael had absorbed enough to know that everything he'd experienced since waking from execution had been leading here. The hunger. The system. The ability to harvest from the dead.
He wasn't a freak or an aberration. He was something old. Something the world had tried to destroy and failed. Something that might, if properly trained, save everyone.
Or doom them all.
He fell asleep with the manuscript open on his chest, dreaming of fire and sealed doors and voices whispering from the dark.
The knock came at dawn.
Three sharp raps against his door. Kael's eyes snapped open, hand reaching for a weapon before his mind fully woke. The manuscript tumbled to the floor.
"Message for Mr. Ashborn." A young voice, muffled by wood. "Urgent."
Kael crossed to the door, positioned himself to the side, opened it a crack. A boy in courier's livery stood in the hallway, cap in hand, holding a sealed envelope.
"From the Market?" Kael asked.
"Yes sir. Said you'd understand."
Kael accepted the envelope, tipped the boy a copper penny, closed and locked the door. The seal was unmarked. Inside, a single sheet of paper bore three lines in elegant script:
"SEAL SEVEN COMPROMISED.
ENTITIES ESCAPING.
COME IMMEDIATELY."
No signature. No address. No explanation.
Kael stared at the message, his blood turning to ice in his veins. Seal Seven. One of the 72. Already failing. Already releasing what should remain imprisoned.
The Assassins had been a distraction. A probe. Someone had been testing his abilities, measuring his responses, while the real threat grew unchecked.
He gathered the manuscript, hid it beneath a loose floorboard, checked his improvised weapons. A knife he'd sharpened himself. A length of chain that could serve as a flail. Nothing that would survive inspection, but enough to fight if necessary.
Then Kael Ashborn, last of the Soul Reavers, walked out into a Thornwick morning shrouded in fog, following a summons that might be rescue or might be the most elaborate trap he'd ever encountered.
The hunger stirred. Eager. Ready.
The Entities were waking.
And somewhere in the city's shadows, Eliza Finch and Adelaide Penrose were already moving toward the same terrible truth.
The seals were breaking.
The end had begun.
