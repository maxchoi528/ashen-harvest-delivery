The stone floor trembled beneath Kael's boots as the first seal cracked.
Three stories below the abandoned clocktower, crimson light bled through the cracks in the warehouse floor. The ritual had begun. Kael pressed his back against the rusted iron support beam, counting heartbeats. The cultists moved in perfect synchronization, their chanting a low vibration that resonated in his chest cavity.
A notification flickered across his vision, the familiar gold text of Soul Reaver's interface:
**[Soul Sense Active: 47 life signatures detected. 3 Awakened presences. Estimated threat level: High.]**
Good. The intelligence had been accurate. Someone had been feeding the Shadow Court information about this gathering for weeks, and Lyra's people had shared just enough to make it worth Kael's while. Not friendship—Kael had learned long ago that in Thornwick, friendship was just debt with better marketing. But mutual interest made for stable alliances.
He dropped from the beam, landing silent on a pile of rotting timber. The cultists below hadn't noticed. They couldn't sense him—his spiritual signature was deliberately suppressed, buried beneath layers of technique he'd harvested from a dead Inquisitor six months back. The man had been Sovereign stage, and his ability to mask spiritual presence had proven invaluable.
The chanting intensified. A woman in black robes stepped forward, her hands tracing symbols in the air that left trails of sickly green luminescence. Kael's eyes narrowed. That wasn't standard cult practice. That was Order technique, bastardized and corrupted, but unmistakable in its origin.
Which Order? The Arcane Spire kept their methods closely guarded. The Mortician's Guild dealt with death but rarely dealt in this kind of power. The Whisper Court's techniques were subtle, not theatrical.
Kael activated his harvest sense, the ability allowing him to read the residual energy signatures left by recent deaths. The warehouse reeked of endings. At least a dozen souls had departed this place in the past week, their spiritual energy harvested and stored in the obsidian vials scattered across the ritual circle.
He crept along the upper walkway, staying in the shadows cast by the massive clock mechanism above. The gears hadn't turned in decades, their teeth rusted into permanent stillness. From his vantage point, he could see the entire ritual floor—and more importantly, he could see the three Awakened directing the ceremony.
The largest was a man built like a siege weapon, his robes straining across shoulders that suggested heavy physical augmentation. Sovereign stage, if Kael had to guess. Probably Iron Vanguard trained, given the muscle distribution. A turncoat, then, or a plant from the beginning.
The second Awakened was harder to pin down. A woman with close-cropped silver hair and eyes that tracked too smoothly, as if she were watching multiple things at once. Her spiritual signature felt fragmented, wrong. Not corrupted exactly, but... Incomplete.
The third made Kael's stomach clench.
Zarek.
The man stood at the ritual's heart, arms raised, leading the chant. His robes were finer than the others, embroidered with sigils that pulsed in time with the breaking seals. Kael had been hunting this bastard for months, ever since Thornwick's gallows had failed to hold him.
They'd framed him for murders he hadn't committed. They'd handed him to the Order of Dawn's executioners with forged evidence and bought testimony. They'd watched him hang from the neck until his body went still.
They hadn't counted on Kael coming back.
**[Soul Harvest Available: 12 fresh corpses in ritual chamber. Quality: Degraded. Cause: Spiritual corruption. Recommendation: Harvest yields uncertain.]**
No good corpses here anyway. Kael dismissed the notification. His targets were alive.
The seal below groaned, a sound like a dying whale. Cracks raced across the warehouse floor, and through those fissures, something bled into the Material plane. Not fully, not yet—but the barrier between worlds was thinning. This ritual wasn't trying to summon an Entity directly. It was trying to weaken the seals that held the 72 rifts closed.
"Brothers and sisters of the True Path," Zarek's voice carried easily through the chamber, amplified by spiritual technique. "The moment approaches. Our sacrifices have weakened the Veil. Soon, the Nameless One shall walk among us once more, and the corrupt orders that betrayed humanity shall burn."
The cultists roared their approval. Kael counted at least forty people down there, most of them spiritually inert commoners who'd been promised salvation or wealth or power. The Awakened always found marks. The spiritually inert were desperate enough to believe.
Kael's hand found the hilt of his blade. Three Awakened meant risk. Even at Demigod stage, fighting three opponents simultaneously was dangerous, especially when one of them was Zarek, who Kael knew firsthand was cunning and ruthless.
But watching wasn't an option. If those seals broke completely, Thornwick would become ground zero for an Abyssal incursion. The city had two million people living in its steam-belching districts, most of them unaware that the world they walked on was nothing more than a thin membrane stretched over an ocean of hungry, alien power.
He needed more information. He needed to know how far along the ritual had progressed and whether interruption was still possible.
Movement at the warehouse's side entrance caught his attention. A figure slipped through the gap, hugging the shadows with practiced ease. Kael's breath caught in his throat.
Lyra.
She'd changed since their last meeting—her hair longer, her posture harder, her eyes carrying shadows that hadn't been there before. She moved with the fluid certainty of someone who'd killed recently and often. The Shadow Court had been using her well.
She wasn't here to stop the ritual. Kael could read that much from her body language. She was here to observe, same as him. The Shadow Court wanted to know what Zarek was really summoning—and whether it could be controlled.
Lyra paused at the edge of the ritual circle, her gaze sweeping the upper level. For a heartbeat, Kael thought she'd spotted him. Then her attention moved on, and she melted into the crowd of robed figures ringing the ceremonial space.
The seal cracked again. This time, the sound was accompanied by a rush of frigid air that raised goosebumps on Kael's arms. Through the widening fissures, he caught a glimpse of something on the other side. Not light, not color—something deeper. A presence that pressed against his mind like a thumb against a bruise.
**[WARNING: Unusual spiritual pressure detected. Source: Rift origin point. Classification: Entity-class presence. Recommendation: Avoid direct exposure.]**
No kidding. Kael pulled his awareness back from the edge, refusing to let the Entity's attention lock onto him. The first Soul Reaver had fought these things directly, had harvested power from their very essence. Kael wasn't that strong yet—might never be—but he understood the fundamental truth that made the Entities so dangerous.
They didn't think like humans. They didn't feel like humans. They were alien intelligences that perceived reality through spiritual frequencies that would shatter a mortal mind if contemplated too long.
The woman with silver hair raised her hands higher. "The third seal is breaking. Brother Zarek, the sacrifice must be prepared."
Zarek nodded, gesturing to two of the robed figures. "Bring forth the vessel."
They dragged a man into the ritual's center. He was young, barely past twenty, his face slack with terror and some kind of. Kael recognized the technique—an Arcane Spire method for rendering targets compliant without damaging their spiritual pathways.
This wasn't a random sacrifice. This was an Awakened, taken from one of the Orders.
Kael's jaw tightened. Zarek had crossed lines before, but kidnapping and ritual murder of fellow Awakened was a declaration of war against the entire system. Even the criminal underworld would be hunting him for that.
Unless Zarek wasn't planning to stay in Thornwick after this. Unless he was counting on the seals breaking to create enough chaos for him to disappear into the Abyssal incursion itself.
The thought chilled Kael more than the Entity-presence rising from the broken seals.
He had to act. Waiting for more information meant watching someone die for no reason other than Kael's caution. The old him—the man who'd been framed and hanged and resurrected—might have hesitated. That version of Kael Ashborn had still believed in rules, in proper channels, in the fiction that justice existed for those with enough coin to buy it.
That man was dead. Zarek's conspiracy had killed him, and Kael had climbed out of his own grave with Soul Reaver's power burning in his veins.
He dropped from the walkway, landing in the middle of the ritual circle.
The effect was instantaneous. The chanting stopped. The cultists scrambled backward, creating a ring of empty space around him. The three Awakened turned to face the intrusion, their spiritual signatures flaring to combat readiness.
Zarek's expression shifted from surprise to recognition to something that might have been fear before settling into cold calculation.
"Kael Ashborn." Zarek spoke the name like a curse. "They told me you died."
"They told me the same about you." Kael drew his blade, the steel singing as it cleared the scabbard. "Seems we both need to work on our reliability."
The massive man stepped forward, his fists clenched. "Brother Zarek, allow me to—"
"No." Zarek's voice cracked like a whip. "Stand down, Gorath. This one is mine."
Kael noted the name. Gorath. Another piece of intelligence for later, assuming there was a later.
"You shouldn't be here, Kael." Zarek's tone shifted, became almost conversational. "This doesn't concern you. Walk away now, and I'll let you leave. Consider it a professional courtesy between men who share an understanding of second chances."
"You framed me for murders you committed." Kael's voice carried without need for amplification. "You handed me to people who wanted me dead. You watched me hang and felt nothing."
"I felt satisfaction," Zarek corrected. "You were becoming inconvenient. Your abilities, your investigations, your inconvenient moral code. The Shadow Court wanted you eliminated, and I provided the solution."
The Shadow Court. Kael filed that away, adding it to his growing list of Zarek's associates. The organization operated in the gaps between Orders, conducting operations that no legitimate faction would sanction. They were supposed to be neutral information brokers, but Kael had learned long ago that neutrality was just another word for playing all sides.
"They must have paid well," Kael said.
"They paid in futures." Zarek smiled, thin and humorless. "The same currency I'm about to purchase with. This ritual will break the seals completely. When the Entities emerge, the old order dies with them. Every Order, every hierarchy, every rule that has kept humanity caged for a thousand years—all of it will burn. And in the ashes, something new will grow."
"Something like you, sitting on a pile of corpses?"
"Something like humanity, finally free to evolve." Zarek spread his arms wide. "You have no idea what the Entities really are, Kael. You've been told they're monsters, threats, the enemy. But the first Soul Reaver knew the truth. He knew that the Entities aren't invaders—they're inheritors. This world was theirs before humanity crawled out of the primordial soup. We're the invaders, and the seal network is just our defense mechanism against the inevitable."
Kael felt the words land somewhere in his chest, stirring something he didn't want to examine too closely. The first Soul Reaver. The origin of his power. Zarek spoke of that figure with familiarity, with something almost like reverence.
"You knew him," Kael said. "The first Soul Reaver."
"I knew what he became." Zarek's eyes glittered with fanatical light. "I knew what he chose. And I know what he left behind." His gaze dropped to Kael's hand, the one that wasn't holding his blade. "Soul Reaver's legacy, passed down through generations. Tell me, Kael—do you even understand what you carry? Do you know why the Orders fear your kind above all others?"
The woman with silver hair moved, placing herself between Kael and the sacrifice still trembling at the ritual's center. Her fragmented spiritual signature pulsed erratically, and Kael realized what was wrong with her.
She wasn't incomplete. She was composite. Someone had stitched multiple soul fragments together to create her, and the seams were showing.
"Brother Zarek," she said, her voice harmonizing with itself in disturbing ways. "The ritual requires completion. We don't have time for this."
"Patience, Vessel." Zarek never took his eyes off Kael. "I've waited years for this confrontation. The Whisper King himself will want to hear how I dealt with the latest Soul Reaver."
The Whisper King. Kael's mind raced through implications. The Whisper King was a legend, a figure whispered about in the darkest corners of the Awakened world. Some said he was the first Awakened, others claimed he was an Entity in human guise. No one knew for certain, but everyone agreed that crossing him was suicide.
Unless you were already planning to burn the world down. Then the Whisper King's threat was just another obstacle to overcome.
"I'm not here to listen to your sales pitch." Kael raised his blade. "I'm here to end you. Everything else is negotiable."
Zarek laughed, the sound sharp and genuine. "There's the man who murdered his way through Thornwick's underworld. I wondered if they hadn't broken you completely. Very well. Let's see what a Demigod can do against true conviction."
He moved, and the air around him ignited.
Kael had fought Awakened before. He'd harvested techniques from the dying and used their own power against them. He'd learned to read the subtle tells that preceded spiritual attacks, the micro-expressions and postural shifts that revealed intent.
Zarek had none of those tells. He existed in one position and then another, and between those moments, he struck like a viper. Kael barely deflected the first blow, his blade catching a surge of corrupted energy that screamed against the metal. The second blow grazed his shoulder, burning through fabric and searing flesh beneath.
He staggered, buying space, his mind processing threat assessment at supernatural speed. Zarek wasn't just Sovereign stage. He was something else, something that drew power from the weakening seals in ways that shouldn't have been possible. Every crack in the barrier between worlds fed him additional strength.
**[Soul Sense Alert: Target Zarek - Spiritual signature amplifying. Source: Rift proximity. Warning: Conventional combat may be insufficient.]**
Kael didn't need a system notification to tell him he was outmatched. He'd known it the moment he'd stepped into the circle. Zarek had been preparing this ritual for months, possibly years, anchoring himself to the very forces he claimed would remake humanity.
Gorath moved to flank, the massive man's fists wreathed in combat techniques that promised broken bones and pulverized organs. The silver-haired woman—Vessel, Zarek had called her—pulled the sacrifice backward, shielding him with her body as the ritual continued to pulse around them.
Kael's options narrowed with each heartbeat. Fight Zarek directly and lose. Focus on Gorath and leave himself open to Zarek's attacks. Try to disrupt the ritual and trigger exactly the catastrophe he was trying to prevent.
Then he saw Lyra.
She'd worked her way to the edge of the ritual circle, close enough to the broken seals that her presence registered on his harvest sense. Her spiritual signature was coiled tight, ready for action, but she wasn't moving. She was watching him, waiting to see what he would do.
The Shadow Court's observer. Ready to act if the situation favored intervention, but unwilling to commit resources until she understood the outcome.
Kael made a decision.
He charged Gorath instead of Zarek.
The massive man grunted in surprise, his guard dropping a fraction of a second as he processed the unexpected target. That was all Kael needed. He flowed around Gorath's haymaker, blade singing through the air, and opened a deep gash along the man's forearm. Blood sprayed, hot and real, and Gorath howled in pain.
But Kael wasn't trying to kill him. He was trying to touch him.
His free hand closed on Gorath's wound, fingers pressing against the flowing blood, and he activated Soul Reaver's harvest.
The sensation was always strange—cold spreading from his palm into his veins, the taste of copper and ozone flooding his mouth, the whispered echoes of someone else's final moments. Gorath's soul fragments tore free from the body, drawn through the connection like iron filings to a magnet.
**[Soul Harvest: Extracted Combat Technique - Iron Body Enhancement (Sovereign-grade). Stamina reserves acquired. Memory fragment extracted: Gorath's recruitment by Zarek.]**
Kael's body surged with borrowed power. His muscles densified, his bones hardened, his capacity for punishment multiplied. The Iron Body technique was crude but effective, transforming him into something closer to Gorath's natural armored state.
He turned to face Zarek.
The cult leader's expression had shifted from confidence to calculation. He hadn't expected Kael to harvest mid-combat, hadn't expected the sheer audacity of using an enemy's technique against them in the same breath as extracting it.
"You're full of surprises," Zarek said. "The reports said you were dangerous, but they didn't mention this kind of adaptability."
"Reports are often incomplete." Kael settled into his stance, the borrowed technique settling into his nervous system like it had always been there. "I've found that firsthand experience tends to be more reliable."
He lunged, and this time Zarek met him blow for blow.
Their exchange was brutal, a storm of steel and spiritual energy that drove the lesser cultists back against the warehouse walls. Kael's blade carved arcs through corrupted defenses, while Zarek countered with techniques that seemed to anticipate each movement half a second before it happened.
But Kael wasn't fighting to win. He was fighting to position.
Each exchange drove them closer to the broken seals, closer to the ritual's pulsing heart. Zarek noticed too late, his foot catching on one of the inscribed circles that powered the ceremony. He stumbled, just for a heartbeat, but it was enough.
Kael's blade found his shoulder.
Zarek screamed, spiritual energy erupting from the wound in a geyser of crimson light. He staggered backward, and his heel broke through the weakened seal on the floor.
The effect was immediate and catastrophic.
Reality buckled. The warehouse's walls seemed to stretch and compress simultaneously, geometry becoming suggestions than constants. The air filled with whispers that weren't whispers, communications from something vast and hungry pressing against the membrane of the world.
Kael felt it then—the attention of the Entity behind the seal. Not fully manifested, not yet, but aware. Interested. Reaching.
**[WARNING: Entity attention acquired. Defensive protocols recommended. Recommend immediate withdrawal from proximity.]**
He didn't withdraw. Instead, he pressed forward, driving his blade toward Zarek's heart.
The cult leader caught his wrist, their faces inches apart. Zarek's eyes had changed, the pupils dilated beyond normal parameters, the irises flickering with colors that didn't exist in the natural spectrum.
"You feel it now," Zarek rasped, blood flecking his lips. "The true power. The Entities aren't enemies—they're opportunities. They've offered me everything. Knowledge. Immortality. Purpose beyond the meaningless struggles of mortal politics."
"They've offered you a leash," Kael countered. "You're just too stupid to feel the collar."
Zarek smiled, and it was the smile of something wearing a man's face. "Then let me show you what the leashed can do."
Power surged through the connection between them—through the blade, through the grip, into Kael's very soul. The Entity was lending Zarek strength, pouring ancient energy into mortal channels, transforming the cult leader into something beyond Awakened, beyond anything Kael had faced before.
Kael's arm buckled. His blade shattered. And Zarek's hand closed around his throat.
"You could have been part of the new world," Zarek said, lifting Kael off the ground with casual strength. "Your abilities, your potential—imagine what we could have accomplished together. But you chose to be an obstacle. And obstacles exist to be removed."
Kael's vision darkened at the edges. Zarek's grip was unbreakable, his spiritual energy a crushing weight that threatened to collapse Kael's very essence.
Then Lyra hit him from behind.
She emerged from the crowd like a shadow given form, her blade driving through Zarek's back and out through his chest. The cult leader's eyes went wide, his grip loosening, and Kael dropped to the ground, gasping for breath.
"Shadow Court protocol," Lyra said, her voice flat and professional. "Unsanctioned Entity contact is grounds for immediate termination of investment."
Zarek laughed, even with a blade through his chest. Blood bubbled from his lips, but the sound was almost joyful.
"You think this ends anything? The ritual has already passed the point of no return. The seals are breaking. Not just here—across Thornwick, across the continent. Seventy-two rituals, seventy-two weak points, all weakening simultaneously. By dawn, the Veil will be nothing but memory, and the Entities will walk freely."
He reached up, grabbing Lyra's face with one blood-soaked hand. "And they'll find you first. All of you. Because you're marked. Every soul that has touched the Reaver's power is marked, and the Entities remember what was taken from them a thousand years ago."
His eyes found Kael's. "Run, little Reaver. Run back to your Orders and your rules and your desperate hope that the system can be saved. It can't. Nothing can. The Truth of Souls is coming, and when it arrives, you'll realize that everything you've fought for was just a prelude to the real war."
The light faded from his eyes. His hand dropped. And even as the cultists screamed and fled, even as Gorath and the Vessel tried to drag Zarek's body away, Kael felt the truth of those final words settling into his bones.
Seventy-two rituals. Seventy-two rifts. All weakening at once.
This wasn't a cult action. This was coordinated warfare against the seal network itself.
And somewhere, in the shadows of power he didn't yet understand, someone was pulling strings that stretched back a thousand years—to the first Soul Reaver, to the war that had created the current order, to secrets that the Orders had killed to protect.
Kael stood, his body aching, his stolen technique fading, his blade shattered and useless. Around him, the ritual continued its terrible work, the broken seals pulsing with light that wasn't light, calling to something vast and patient and hungry.
Lyra grabbed his arm. "We need to move. The Shadow Court has safe houses throughout the city. If what he said is true, we need to coordinate response."
"You're Shadow Court," Kael said. "Why help me?"
"Because I'm tired of being a tool." Her grip tightened. "Because the Shadow Court is fractured, and some of us want to fight for something instead of just observing. And because if the Entities break through, there won't be anyone left to pay for information. The market dies with the world."
It wasn't trust. It wasn't alliance. But it was enough.
Kael let her pull him toward the exit, toward the steam-fogged streets of Thornwick, toward whatever came next. Behind them, the warehouse groaned as reality continued to fray. Above them, the clocktower's frozen gears began to turn for the first time in decades, their rusted teeth grinding out a warning that no one alive could interpret.
The seals were breaking.
The war had begun.
And Kael Ashborn, Soul Reaver and death-row survivor, walked into a night that would change everything—carrying secrets he didn't understand, hunted by forces he couldn't see, armed with a power that might be the only thing standing between humanity and extinction.
Somewhere in the darkness, footsteps echoed.
Not his. Not Lyra's.
Someone—or something—else was following.
Kael's hand found the hilt of his broken blade, and he waited.
