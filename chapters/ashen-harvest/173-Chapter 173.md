The old woman's fingers closed around Kael's wrist like the grip of a corpse. "Five hundred sovereigns. Non-negotiable."
"You want me to march into a sealed tomb and pull out something that might kill me, and you're charging me for the privilege?" Kael kept his voice flat. "The Mortician's Guild already owns that site."
"The Guild owns the access tunnels. Not what's inside." The crone's milky eyes shifted toward the rain-slicked windows. "Three weeks ago, a team from the Arcane Spire tried to crack the outer wards. Two dead. One catatonic. Whatever's in that chamber doesn't want to be found."
Kael studied the floor, processing the implications. The Spire didn't send apprentices into dangerous ruins—they had resources, experience, protective talismans worth small fortunes. If they'd lost two Awakened to whatever guarded that artifact, the danger was real.
"I'll pay two hundred now. Four hundred when I return with the item."
"Three hundred now. You get the location of the third tunnel, the one the Guild doesn't know exists."
Kael extended his hand. The old woman clasped it, her grip surprisingly strong for someone who looked moments from the grave. Her skin was cold, bloodless, like touching parchment stretched over stone.
"The Sepulcher of Endings," she whispered. "Eastern quadrant of the undercity. The entrance is behind a statue of Saint Moravec—the one with the broken sword. The chamber you want is six levels down, past the bone gardens."
"And the artifact?"
"A box. Black iron. Warm to the touch even in the coldest chamber." Her grip tightened. "Don't open it until you've left the Sepulcher. Don't let it touch bare skin. And whatever you do—don't look directly at what emerges when you break the seal."
Kael released her hand. "That sounds like the kind of warning people give when they're about to describe exactly what happens to those who ignore it."
Her laugh was a dry rattle, like dead leaves scraping against stone. "Maybe it is. Maybe I'm tired of watching young fools die for curiosity." She shoved a rolled parchment across the table—a map, hand-drawn, marked with symbols Kael didn't recognize. "Midnight. The Guild changes shift at the eastern checkpoint at half-past. That's your window."
Kael counted out the coins, silver and copper clinking against the scarred wood. The old woman's eyes tracked the money but revealed nothing—no satisfaction, no greed. Just a flat, patient stare that reminded him too much of the dead he'd harvested.
"One more question." Kael paused at the door. "Who told you I was looking for sealed artifacts?"
For the first time, something flickered in her expression—fear, quickly suppressed. "Nobody tells me anything, boy. I remember things. Things that haven't happened yet." She turned away, dismissing him. "Now get out. I have customers who pay in soul-essence."
---
The rain had worsened by the time Kael left the tavern, turning the cobblestones into treacherous rivers of filth. Gas lamps flickered in their iron housings, casting pools of sickly yellow light that did more to obscure than illuminate. Above the rooftops, the great clock tower of Thornwick tolled the hour—nine bells. Three hours until his window opened.
He moved through the Arterium District with practiced anonymity, his coat buttoned high, his posture bent in the universal posture of a man who wanted to be ignored. Two hundred yards behind him, a figure in a gray cloak maintained the same pace, same distance, never closer, never falling further.
The Shadow Court's interest hadn't surprised him. Word of a Soul Reaver asking questions about sealed artifacts would reach every organization with a stake in the Underworld's secrets within hours. What did surprise him was the lack of direct approach—no confrontation, no offer of employment or threats. Just surveillance, patient and professional.
Kael turned down an alley between a pawnbroker's shop and a boarding house, then immediately ducked into a doorway alcove. He waited, counting heartbeats. Thirty seconds later, the gray cloak passed the alley's entrance without slowing, its wearer scanning ahead than checking behind.
Amateur.
Or a test. The Shadow Court didn't use amateurs.
Kael continued through the maze of the Arterium, his mind working through logistics. The Sepulcher of Endings wasn't on any public map—he'd checked the city's archives twice. Whatever the old woman had given him tonight was genuine knowledge, the kind that cost more than three hundred sovereigns. The question was why she'd sold it so cheaply.
Unless the price wasn't really about money. Unless she wanted someone to find what lay in that chamber, and she didn't care whether that someone lived or died afterward.
He reached the boarding house where Sera had secured them rooms, slipping through the rear entrance and climbing the narrow stairs to the third floor. The door to his room was locked, the ward intact—Sera's work, elegant and nearly invisible. He touched the sigil, felt the familiar resistance of her magic, and stepped inside.
The room was sparse: a bed, a washstand, a wardrobe, a small table where Kael spread out the old woman's map. The parchment was old, the ink faded in places, but the details were precise. Tunnel systems, chamber numbers, structural notations. The cartographer had known this place intimately.
Kael traced the route to the sixth level, where a small symbol marked the artifact's location. The same symbol appeared on the map's border, next to a single word: BELLOW.
Beneath it, in smaller script that Kael had to hold the parchment near the lamp to read: *The voice that never stops. The sound that unmakes. Guard well what you carry.*
A warning. Or a description.
Kael folded the map and began preparing for the night's work.
---
Midnight arrived with the consistency of dripping water.
The eastern checkpoint of the undercity hummed with activity—Guild workers in their gray uniforms, carrying lanterns and manifest tablets, checking crates that would be shipped through the city's underground canal network. Kael watched from the shadows of a drainage tunnel, timing the shift change.
At half-past, the outgoing supervisor handed off his ledger to a yawning replacement. The replacement spent thirty seconds complaining about the cold before herding her crew toward the warmth of the guard station. The incoming supervisor lit a pipe and leaned against the checkpoint wall, his attention fixed on the far end of the tunnel where a barge was approaching.
Kael moved.
He was through the checkpoint before the supervisor's pipe smoke finished curling upward, slipping past the customs barriers and into the maze of tunnels that honeycombed the rock beneath Thornwick. The air changed immediately—cool, damp, carrying the mineral tang of deep earth and something else beneath it. Something organic. Decay, but not animal. The stone itself seemed to be rotting.
The map proved accurate. First left, second right, down the spiral staircase marked with a cracked stone arch. Kael descended past the first level, where clusters of Guild workers loaded salvaged materials onto carts. The second level held storage chambers, their iron doors hanging open on rusted hinges. By the third level, the tunnels narrowed, the air grew thick, and the lamp light became sparse.
On the fourth level, Kael encountered the bone gardens.
They filled a vast chamber that might have been a natural cavern before the Guild carved it into a repository for the dead. Shelves of femurs and skulls lined the walls in neat rows, organized by date of deposit according to the metal tags still attached to some specimens. The floor was carpeted with smaller bones—finger bones, toe bones, the delicate architecture of ears and noses. Kael's boots crunched on them with every step.
He'd seen mass graves. He'd walked through battlefields where the dead outnumbered the living. But this was something different—this systematic accumulation of human remains, catalogued and stored like grain in a silo. The Guild didn't just harvest soul-essence from the dying. They preserved the evidence, waiting for families to pay retrieval fees, storing the remains of those who never got claimed.
Kael wondered how many of these bones had been harvested already. How many still carried traces of spiritual energy, slowly dissipating into the stone and soil.
Not his concern. The harvesting was legal, sanctioned, part of the world's machinery. Unlike what he did.
He passed through the bone gardens and continued downward. The fifth level held sealed chambers marked with hazard sigils—tomb robbers who'd triggered traps, Kael guessed, or Awakened who hadn't respected the dangers of the deep. The sixth level...
The sixth level was different.
The tunnel ended at an iron door, massive and ancient, covered in script that Kael couldn't read. The language was old—older than any he'd encountered, predating the Orders, predating the Abyssal Wars. Symbols that seemed to shift and writhe at the edges of his vision, never resolving into meaning.
Kael examined the door without touching it. No lock. No handle. Just those writhing symbols and a small aperture at chest height, barely large enough to admit a hand.
On the floor before the door lay a corpse.
The body was fresh—hours old, maybe less. Gray robes, hood fallen back to reveal a face frozen in an expression of absolute terror. The eyes were open, staring at nothing, and the mouth gaped as if the person had been screaming when they died. No wounds visible. No signs of violence. Just death, sudden and complete, in the moment of reaching for whatever lay beyond that iron door.
Kael crouched beside the body, studying the face. Young, maybe twenty. Features that suggested Northern ancestry—the pale skin, the light hair, the sharp cheekbones common to the mountain provinces. No identifying marks, no guild sigils, nothing to indicate affiliation.
An apprentice, Kael decided. Someone sent to test the door's defenses. Someone who hadn't known what waited on the other side.
He reached out, touched the corpse's hand, and felt the familiar pulse of his ability stir beneath his skin.
The harvest was quick, almost gentle. Kael closed his eyes as information flooded through the connection—fragmented memories, scattered thoughts, a jumble of impressions that took several seconds to sort. Most of it was useless: fear, confusion, the young Awakened's last desperate attempts to understand what was killing them. But buried in the noise, Kael found what he needed.
*The symbols were a ward. A test. The door opened for those who answered its question, sealed forever for those who failed.*
The apprentice had tried to read the script, to parse its meaning with logic and linguistic analysis. The symbols weren't meant to be read—they were meant to be *felt*. Understood through spiritual resonance than intellectual effort.
Kael stood, approached the iron door, and placed his palm against the cold metal. He didn't try to read the symbols. Instead, he opened his spiritual senses, reaching for the intent behind the ancient script.
The question came as pressure against his consciousness—not words, but meaning. A demand, ancient and implacable.
*What do you take from the dead?*
Kael answered without hesitation. "Everything they're willing to give."
The pressure intensified. The symbols on the door flared with pale light, and for a moment Kael felt something vast and unknowable pressing against his mind, examining him, weighing his soul on scales that had no human equivalent.
Then the door swung open, and the pressure vanished.
Beyond lay a small chamber, circular, carved from living rock. No decorations, no inscriptions, no apparent purpose except to hold what waited at the center: a pedestal of black iron, and on that pedestal, a box.
The box was exactly as the old woman had described—black iron, warm to the touch, radiating an energy that made Kael's teeth ache. It was the size of a prayer book, rectangular, covered in the same writhing symbols as the door. But where the door's symbols had been only strange, the box's markings seemed almost alive. They pulsed with a rhythm that matched Kael's heartbeat.
He approached slowly, every instinct screaming warning. The chamber's air was thick, hard to breathe, tasting of copper and ozone. The light from the doorway behind him seemed unable to penetrate the space around the pedestal—as if the box generated its own pocket of darkness.
Kael's hand hovered over the box's surface. He could feel the energy radiating from it, could sense the spiritual power coiled within like a serpent waiting to strike. The old woman's warning echoed in his mind: *Don't open it until you've left the Sepulcher. Don't let it touch bare skin.*
He pulled a length of cloth from his pack, wrapped it around the box, and lifted it from the pedestal. The weight was negligible, but the energy intensifies as he moved—the symbols flared brighter, and a sound emerged from somewhere beneath the iron.
Not a voice. Not. More like the memory of a voice, an echo preserved in the metal itself.
*Finally. Finally. Finally.*
Kael didn't waste time wondering why the artifact spoke or what it meant. He turned and walked out of the chamber, through the iron door, leaving the question of its nature for another day. The corridor beyond was silent, empty, unchanged by the presence of whatever power he'd just claimed.
The journey back to the surface passed without incident. No Guild patrols, no Shadow Court agents, no obstacles of any kind. Either his timing was perfect, or someone had cleared the path for him. Kael filed the suspicion away for later consideration.
Dawn was breaking by the time he emerged from the drainage tunnel into the Arterium District. The rain had stopped, leaving the streets slick with moisture that caught the first light of morning. Kael found a quiet corner, unwrapped the box, and finally allowed himself to examine it properly.
The iron was warm in his hands, almost hot. The symbols continued their restless movement, never repeating, never forming recognizable patterns. And beneath his spiritual senses, Kael could feel something inside—a presence, dormant, waiting.
He hadn't expected the artifact to contain a fragment of consciousness. The Sepulcher's original inhabitants must have imprisoned something inside the box, something dangerous enough to warrant centuries of isolation and forgotten knowledge.
Kael considered his options. He could bring the box to someone with more expertise—an Order archivist, a specialist in ancient relics, maybe even the Mortician's Guild. But that would mean explaining how he'd acquired it, revealing the location of the Sepulcher chamber, and surrendering control of whatever power the artifact contained.
Or he could open it.
The box wasn't sealed in any conventional sense—no lock, no latch, no obvious mechanism. It was the symbols themselves that kept it closed, holding the lid in place through spiritual force. Breaking that force would require more power than Kael currently possessed, or a specific counter-formula known only to those who'd created the ward.
Kael set the box on the ground, knelt before it, and pressed both palms against the iron surface. He pushed his spiritual senses into the symbols, analyzing their structure, their intent, their vulnerabilities.
The ward was sophisticated—layers of protective enchantments woven together with almost organic complexity. But it was also old, and age created gaps. Erosion. Weaknesses that the original creators couldn't have anticipated.
Kael found one such weakness—a point where two symbol sequences overlapped imperfectly, creating a small tear in the ward's fabric. He focused his energy on that point, applying pressure with the precision of a surgeon than the force of a battering ram.
The ward resisted for nearly a minute. Then it shattered.
The box's lid flew open with a sound like tearing metal, and light exploded from within—not visible light, but spiritual radiance that slammed into Kael's consciousness with physical force. He staggered backward, his head ringing, his vision swimming with afterimages that refused to fade.
When his sight cleared, the box lay open before him, empty except for a small object that hadn't been there a moment before.
A shard of crystal, black as obsidian, no larger than his thumbnail. It floated an inch above the box's interior, rotating slowly, catching and refracting light that didn't exist.
Kael reached for it, then stopped. The crystal was the source of the energy he'd felt—the imprisoned presence, now released from its prison but bound to this small physical form. Touching it would mean direct contact with whatever intelligence had been sealed inside.
"Choose," said a voice, emanating from the crystal. Not aloud—in his mind, bypassing his ears entirely. "Touch me, and learn what I know. Refuse, and walk away. But know this: the seals are breaking. The rifts will open. And what sleeps in the Abyss stirs toward waking."
Kael's jaw tightened. "Who are you?"
"I was a keeper of the Sepulcher. I guarded the boundary between worlds when the Orders were young and the Entities were many. I failed in my duty, and for my failure, I was imprisoned in iron and silence for three thousand years." The voice was calm, patient, ancient in a way that made Kael's skin prickle. "I do not have a name that your kind could pronounce. But the records of my people called me the Voice of the Unmade."
"The Abyssal Entities."
"Among other names. We are not the monsters your Orders teach. We are... Different. Older. Our existence predates the division between Material and Abyss. The separation was not a imprisonment—it was a negotiation. A treaty. And the treaty is dying."
Kael studied the floating crystal, weighing the implications. If the Voice spoke truth, the entire foundation of the Order's worldview was flawed. The Entities weren't invaders or demons—they were original inhabitants of reality, bound by ancient agreement than defeated enemies.
"How do I know you're not lying?"
"You don't. Trust is a currency I cannot spend." The crystal pulsed with dim light. "But consider: I was imprisoned by those who feared my knowledge. If I wished you harm, I would have deceived you into opening the box without warning. Instead, I gave you choice. That should tell you something about the nature of my kind."
Kael thought about the old woman, about her cryptic warnings and her desperate need to see the artifact found. She'd known what was imprisoned in that box. She'd wanted it released, or at least wanted someone else to make the decision.
He reached out and touched the crystal.
The contact lasted only a moment—a single heartbeat of connection—but within that heartbeat, information flooded through him like a dam breaking. Not memories, exactly, but knowledge. History. Truths that the Orders had buried beneath centuries of propaganda and deliberate forgetting.
He learned about the original Soul Reaver, a mortal who had touched the Abyss and emerged transformed. Not an artifact, not a weapon, but a person—one who had walked the boundary between worlds and found a way to harvest the energy of souls without destroying them. The first of his kind, and the template from which all other Soul Reavers descended.
He learned about the 72 rifts, each sealed not to imprison the Entities but to maintain the balance between two halves of a single reality. The seals were weakening, not from external attack but from internal decay—the Entities themselves were forgetting why the treaty existed, losing patience with limitations that had once seemed reasonable.
He learned about a conspiracy within the Orders, a faction that sought not to prevent the rifts from opening but to control the flow—choosing which entities would emerge, binding them to service, creating an army of Abyssal powers loyal to human masters than ancient agreement.
And he learned about the Shadow Court's true purpose. Not a deniable operations arm for Order factions, but an independent organization with its own agenda—monitoring the seals, identifying threats, eliminating those who got too close to truths that the world wasn't ready to face.
The contact ended. Kael found himself on his knees, gasping, blood trickling from his nose. The crystal lay inert in his palm, its energy spent.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted historical knowledge from the remains. Lore Acquired: The Treaty of Dividing, The Soul Reaver Origin, The 72 Rift Network, The Shadow Court's True Purpose.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted spiritual resonance from the remains. Ability Fragment Acquired: Resonance Sight (Passive) - You can perceive the spiritual signatures of objects and entities, including hidden wards, ancient artifacts, and dimensional boundaries.]
Kael closed his fist around the crystal, feeling its weight—heavier now, meaningful in a way it hadn't been before. The knowledge he'd gained would change everything. Not immediately, not obviously, but fundamentally. The world he'd been navigating was built on lies, and now he understood the shape of those lies.
The cult activity in Thornwick wasn't random heresy—it was sabotage, deliberate weakening of the seal network by a faction that wanted controlled release than natural decay. Zarek wasn't just a cult leader—he was an agent of that faction, working toward an outcome that would give certain Orders power over the Abyss itself.
And the Shadow Court was watching him, not because he was a threat but because he was an unknown quantity. A Soul Reaver asking questions about sealed artifacts was exactly the kind of variable they tracked.
Kael stood, pocketing the crystal, and walked back into the awakening city. The streets were filling with morning traffic—workers heading to factories, merchants opening shops, children running errands for parents who couldn't afford to pay others. Ordinary people living ordinary lives, unaware that the fabric of reality was slowly unraveling beneath their feet.
He needed to reach Sera. Needed to share what he'd learned, to plan their next moves with full knowledge of the stakes. But as he turned toward the boarding house, he felt it—a prickle at the back of his neck, the unmistakable sensation of being watched.
Not the Shadow Court this time. Something else.
Kael stopped in the middle of the street, turning slowly. The morning crowd flowed around him, oblivious to his sudden tension. There—at the corner of the Mercantile Exchange, half-hidden in the shadow of a steam-vent—a figure watching him with eyes that glowed faintly in the dim light.
Not human eyes. Not Entity eyes either. Something in between.
The figure raised one hand, a casual gesture that might have been a greeting or a warning, and then melted into the crowd, disappearing as if it had never been there at all.
Kael stood frozen, his mind racing through possibilities. Someone else who'd touched the Abyss. Someone else who'd changed. Someone who knew what the Soul Reaver was and wanted to make contact.
Or someone who wanted him to think that.
The crystal in his pocket pulsed once, warm against his thigh, and the Voice's final words echoed in his memory: *The seals are breaking. The rifts will open. And what sleeps in the Abyss stirs toward waking.*
The morning light fell across Thornwick's rooftops, golden and ordinary, and Kael understood that nothing would ever be ordinary again.
