"Contact front! They've got the alley flanked!" Gwen's voice cut through the smoke-choked air as her blade swept left, opening a cultist's throat in a spray of arterial crimson.
Kael dove behind a collapsed merchant's stall, splinters driving into his shoulder. The narrow street of Thornwick's Undercroft district—a maze of steam pipes and overflowing drainage channels—had become a killing floor. Three hours ago, they'd moved on what intelligence claimed was a minor cult stockpile. Now? A full engagement zone.
He counted the muzzle flashes. Six positions. Organized fire discipline. Not the usual desperate thralls waving ritual knives.
"Cull the rear!" Gwen barked, pivoting to meet a charging figure with a brutal axe. The cultist wore the gray robes of the initiated—no longer thrall, but not yet chosen. Gwen's counter-strike took him through the sternum.
Kael rolled, came up firing. His revolver barked twice. Two cultists crumpled, holes punching through their chests.
But they kept coming.
"Where are they getting reinforcements?" Maren's voice crackled from the communication crystal embedded in Kael's ear. She commanded their extraction vehicle three blocks north, watching through a scope.
"Does it matter?" Kael ejected a spent cylinder, slammed in a fresh one. "We move forward or die here."
The Undercroft smelled of coal ash and copper. Somewhere above, steam vents hissed their endless complaints into the night sky. Here, in the bowels where the city's underclass lived in corrugated metal shacks, the gaslights hadn't worked in years.
A figure dropped from a fire escape—twenty feet—and landed in a crouch before Kael. Not a cultist. The robes were wrong: black leather, silver clasps, the sigil of the Order of Dawn embroidered over the heart.
"Marshal Kael." The woman's voice carried the clipped precision of military training. "Commander Dorian sends his compliments. He requests you stand down and surrender the Mortician operative."
Kael's blood chilled. "Dorian knows where we are?"
"The Commander has eyes everywhere, Marshal. Your little raid was never secret." She smiled without warmth. "This is a courtesy. The alternative involves significant collateral damage."
Gwen appeared at his flank, sword dripping. Her scarred face twisted with recognition. "Order of Dawn Enforcer. Dawn's Hand Division."
"Commander Ashborn's memory serves." The woman inclined her head. "Lieutenant Vex, designation Silent Mercy. I'm authorized to offer terms."
"Terms." Kael laughed, the sound harsh in the confined space. "You surround us with cultists, force us into a position where our deaths look like another Undercroft massacre, then offer terms."
"The cultists are expendable. A regrettable necessity." Vex shrugged. "The Commander values your abilities, Marshal. A Soul Reaver of your caliber represents an investment worth protecting."
"I'm flattered."
"You should be. He wants you alive. The cult wants you dead. Choose your enemy carefully."
Maren's voice exploded through the crystal. "Kael! I've got movement on the rooftop line! Six—no, eight hostiles converging on your position! They're using the steam tunnels!"
The ventilation grate beside Kael burst open. A creature crawled through—not human, not anymore. The flesh had fused with what looked like rusted iron plating, and where eyes should have been, pits of burning amber glowed with hungry intelligence.
"Cult-constructs!" Gwen raised her sword.
Vex sighed. "That complicates matters."
The first construct lunged. Kael fired—point blank—the bullet punching through its skull. The creature kept coming, iron-plated hands closing around his throat.
Gwen's blade took its head off.
"Fall back to the drainage junction!" she shouted. "Use the water channel!"
The construct's head rolled across the cobblestones, still gnashing teeth. More crawled from the grate.
Kael grabbed the dropped head, felt the familiar cold surge as his Soul Reaver ability activated. No cost. Just contact.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Fragmented Combat Protocols from the remains. Memory shard acquired.]
Fragments of combat data flooded his mind—the construct's creators, their network patterns, the location of a central control node. Information the cult didn't know they'd lost.
"There are more coming," he said. "A lot more."
"Language," Vex muttered, drawing twin daggers that gleamed with dawnfire enchantment. "You were always theatrical."
"You know me?"
"Dorian does." She moved to flank, her stance suggesting she intended to fight beside them than against them. "He remembers everyone who's ever humiliated him."
The steam tunnels yawned before them, a concrete throat descending into darkness. Above, the rooftop positions opened fire—crossbow bolts and spell-bolts raining down.
Kael grabbed Gwen's arm. "The drainage junction. Now."
They ran.
---
The tunnel smelled of sewage and despair. Kael's boots splashed through ankle-deep water as he navigated by the faint phosphorescent moss clinging to the walls. Gwen moved beside him, her breathing heavy but controlled. Behind, the sounds of combat faded—Vex was buying them time, and he couldn't decide if that troubled him.
"Order of Dawn turned coat?" Gwen asked.
"Dorian's a pragmatist. If the cult is moving openly, the Orders won't be far behind." Kael checked his revolver. Four shots left. "The Concordat doesn't cover cultists."
"But targeting you specifically isn't about cult control."
"No."
The tunnel widened into a junction—three passages converging around a central pumping station. Rusty machinery groaned overhead. The only light came from Kael's palm, where he'd unconsciously manifested a wisp of spiritual energy.
Movement. Left passage.
Kael raised his gun.
"It's me," Maren's voice echoed. She emerged from the shadows, crossbow in hand, her face grimy with tunnel grime. "I moved to intercept. We need to—"
A crossbow bolt punched through her shoulder.
Maren spun, crashing against the wall. A second bolt followed, catching her in the thigh.
From the right passage, a figure stepped forward. Gray robes, silver mask. The cult's chosen representative.
"You should have stayed dead, Soul Reaver." The voice was distorted by the mask's resonance. "The Whisper King sends his regards."
Kael's mind raced. The Whisper King—a name that surfaced only in the most classified Order documents, associated with cult leadership that predated the current generation of operatives.
"You know who I am," Kael said. "That's interesting. Most cultists just want me dead."
"You are a resource that has been misallocated for far too long." The masked figure raised a hand. Spiritual pressure built in the tunnel—an almost tangible weight that made Kael's teeth ache. "The Abyss provides. It always provides. But a Soul Reaver's harvest is... More efficient."
"You're Abyssal-touched."
"I am enlightened."
The cultist's hand blazed with purple-black light. Gwen moved—fast, faster than human—her blade singing through the air.
The cultist caught it bare-handed. The blade stopped an inch from his throat.
"Fascinating reflexes," he said. "The Iron Vanguard's finest, if I'm not mistaken. Captain Gwen Steelborn. Your sister sends her regards."
Gwen's face contorted. "Mirren—"
"Alive. Cooperative. Serving the true cause with enthusiasm." The cultist smiled beneath his mask. "Everything the Iron Vanguard told you about her death was a lie. She saw the light years ago."
Kael saw Gwen's grip falter. Her weakness—her dead sister, supposedly killed in a border skirmish with cultists. A wound that never healed.
Now this.
"Gwen," Kael said sharply. "Focus."
The cultist released her blade and thrust forward. Purple fire erupted from his palm.
Kael tackled Gwen sideways. The fire scorched past, leaving a scar of melted stone on the tunnel wall.
"Maren!" Kael shouted.
She was already moving, ignoring the bolts through her shoulder and thigh, bringing her crossbow up. The first bolt flew wide. The second punched through the cultist's knee.
He screamed—a sound of genuine pain, not the theatrical endurance the chosen typically displayed.
"Harvest," Kael commanded, closing the distance. He grabbed the cultist's face as the man writhed.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Shadow Covenant Oath from the remains. Memory acquisition: HIGH QUALITY. Skill partially unlocked.]
Information flooded through him—the cult's organizational structure, cell designations, the existence of coordinated activity across Thornwick that suggested central leadership than independent madmen. And a name: Prime Zarek.
"Son of a bitch," Kael breathed.
Gwen grabbed his arm. "What?"
"Zarek. He's not just cult leadership—he's coordinating the cells. Someone is directing the entire operation." Kael's mind worked frantically. "The attacks across the city, the timing, the resources—they're not random. They're synchronized."
"That's impossible. No one has that kind of command structure."
"Someone does." Kael released the cultist's corpse—it slumped, already cold. "We need to move. There will be more."
"More what?" Maren limped toward them, leaving a trail of blood drops in the water. "I've got six bolts in me and we just lost our extraction point."
"Then we go on foot." Kael assessed the wounds. "Can you travel?"
"I can fight."
"Good enough." He looked at Gwen. "Your sister—"
"Is a conversation for later." Gwen's voice was hard as iron. "Right now, I want to find whoever's coordinating these cult cells and put them down permanently."
The tunnel ahead stretched into darkness. Above, the sounds of the ambush fading—Vex either dead, captured, or having chosen her own path.
Kael led them forward. His mind churned with the harvested information. The cult's structure was more sophisticated than anyone realized. Prime Zarek—a name that carried weight in the memories he'd stolen. Someone the cult feared. Someone they obeyed.
Someone who might know the location of the sealed rifts the Orders had supposedly kept secret.
"The main cell is in the Undercroft's northern district," he said, piecing together fragments. "Three levels down, beneath what used to be the old coal processing facility. That's where Zarek operates."
"And you know this how?" Maren asked.
"The construct's memories. And this cultist's." Kael touched his temple. "Soul harvesting. I extract skills, abilities, sometimes knowledge from the dead."
Maren stared at him. "That's—"
"Gruesome. Profane. Forbidden by every Order and law in existence." Kael met her gaze without flinching. "I know what I am. Question is, are you with me?"
The silence stretched. Water dripped somewhere in the darkness.
"I'm with you," Maren said finally. "Killed the Orders' way once. No reason to stop now."
Gwen nodded. "Same."
They moved.
---
Two hours later, they emerged into the coal processing facility's ruins. The building had been abandoned after a steam explosion decades ago—twisted metal and crumbling concrete forming a labyrinth of death traps and collapsed platforms.
Kael signaled halt. His spiritual senses reached out, probing the darkness.
"Six hostiles," he whispered. "Three Awakened. The rest are construct-swarmed."
"Suicide run," Gwen observed.
"Unless we use the element of surprise." Kael checked his revolver again. Three shots. Enough if he was precise.
"Surprise is gone. They know we're coming."
"Then we improvise." He looked at Maren. "You remember the Mortician facility in the northern quarter?"
"The one with the—"
"My sister. Yes." Kael saw the conflict in her eyes. "The cult is using the same location. Something there is important enough to establish a secondary command post."
"You're saying they're connected."
"I'm saying everything is connected. The cult's increased activity, the Order of Dawn's sudden interest in me, the constructs—it's all coordinated by someone."
The facility loomed before them, its broken windows like empty eye sockets staring at the smog-choked sky.
"Then we find out who," Maren said.
They entered.
The interior was a graveyard of industrial ambition—massive gears frozen mid-rotation, conveyor belts collapsed into heaps of rust, the skeletons of machines that once fed Thornwick's hunger for power. Light came from phosphorescent fungi growing on the walls, casting everything in pale green.
Kael led them down. His harvested memories guided them through traps, past guard posts that should have been invisible but fell to his perception.
The cultists died without raising alarms.
First level: two cultists, throats opened by Gwen's blade.
Second level: three constructs, destroyed by coordinated fire and Maren's explosive bolts.
Third level: a door.
Kael paused. His spiritual senses screamed warning.
"Powerful presence," he said. "Beyond the door. Awakened, but not standard progression."
"Entity-touched?" Maren asked.
"Maybe. Maybe something else." He reached for the handle. "Cover me."
The door opened onto a chamber that had once been a control room. Banks of dead machinery lined the walls. In the center, a figure sat in a rusted chair, facing away from them.
Prime Zarek.
"Welcome, Soul Reaver." The voice was calm, almost welcoming. "I've been expecting you since the ambush began."
Kael raised his revolver. "You knew we were coming."
"I arranged for you to come. The Order of Dawn, the cult cells, even the information leak that led you here—all pieces on a very large board." Zarek turned.
His face was gaunt, eyes burning with purple fire that should have been impossible for a human host. Scars covered every visible inch of flesh—ritual cuts that formed patterns Kael didn't recognize.
"You're not just cult leadership," Kael said. "You're something more."
"I am what the Abyss makes of those willing to serve." Zarek smiled. "The same thing that made you, Soul Reaver. The same thing that will consume this city when the seals finally break."
"The seals can't break."
"They are breaking. Has been breaking for years. The Orders know—their leadership, not the soldiers. They maintain the illusion of stability while preparing their own escapes." Zarek stood. "But you... You're different. You harvest without corruption. The Zero Cost." He shook his head in something like wonder. "I've studied the old texts. I know what the first Soul Reaver achieved. And you might be the only one capable of stopping what's coming."
"Why tell me this?"
"Because you're going to try, regardless. And because failure is more instructive than success." Zarek's hand moved, and purple fire erupted around them—not attacking, but forming barriers that sealed the exits. "I'm going to test you, Kael Ashborn. Show me what a Soul Reaver can do."
The barrier solidified.
Gwen charged. Her blade caught the flames and sizzled but didn't break.
Maren fired explosive bolts that dissipated harmlessly.
Kael stood still, watching.
"Interesting," Zarek said. "You don't waste energy on futile attacks."
"Neither do you." Kael holstered his gun. "This isn't a fight. It's a recruitment pitch."
"Perceptive." Zarek inclined his head. "The cult offers purpose, Soul Reaver. Power. The chance to shape what comes next instead of being shaped by it."
"And what comes next is the Abyss consuming everything?"
"What comes next is change. The entities will breach their prisons—there is no stopping that now. The only question is whether humanity survives in any meaningful form." Zarek stepped closer. "I choose to ensure our survival through adaptation. The Orders choose denial. The cult chooses worship. But you—you could harvest the entities themselves. Take their power. Become something unprecedented."
The words hung in the air.
"I harvest the dead," Kael said slowly. "Not cosmic horrors from beyond reality."
"You harvest soul-essence. Spiritual energy given form. The entities are nothing but spiritual energy given form—raw, corrupted, but still energy." Zarek's eyes gleamed. "Theoretically, a sufficiently powerful Soul Reaver could drain an entity. Imagine that power, Soul Reaver. The abilities of all seventy-two, harvested and yours."
The theory was insane. Dangerous. Possibly true.
"Why tell me this?" Kael asked again.
"Because the Abyss is coming, and I need the strongest Soul Reaver alive to be my ally, not my enemy." Zarek extended a hand. "Join me. Together, we can ensure humanity's survival."
The chamber fell silent.
Kael looked at Gwen, at Maren. Both wounded, both defiant. Both waiting for him to decide.
"Soul Reavers harvest the dead," he said finally. "You said the entities are spiritual energy. Which means—"
He moved.
Fast, faster than Zarek anticipated.
His hand closed around Zarek's throat, and the Soul Reaver ability activated without thought or hesitation.
[Soul Harvest: Contact initiated. Target possesses Abyssal Entity essence. Extraction... UNSTABLE. WARNING: Soul Reaver's harvest protocols not designed for entity-class entities. Proceed with caution. Y acquired: Fragment of Abyssal Knowledge (Corrupted).]
Zarek screamed—a sound of genuine agony as Kael tore something fundamental from the man's corrupted soul.
"You—" Zarek choked. "You don't understand what you're—"
"I understand you're not human anymore." Kael released him. Zarek stumbled back, clutching his chest. "Whatever you were before the Abyss took you, that's gone. You're just another monster wearing a man's face."
The barriers flickered.
Zarek laughed—broken, desperate. "Then you understand nothing. The Abyss doesn't take. It offers. Every choice, every corruption—it begins with us, not with them."
The room shook. The barriers collapsed.
"Run," Zarek gasped. "The facility—there's a secondary charge. If I die here, the entire Undercroft district comes down."
Gwen grabbed Kael's arm. "We need to move. Now."
Zarek slumped in his chair, the stolen fragment's absence leaving visible damage—his eyes flickering between purple fire and normal human pupils.
"Find the truth, Soul Reaver," he rasped. "The original Reaver's tomb. Everything is there. Everything."
Kael hesitated. The truth. The Soul Reaver's origin. The answers he'd been chasing since awakening in his own grave.
The ground shook again. Dust rained from the ceiling.
"He's lying," Gwen said. "Cultists always lie."
Maren was already moving toward the exit. "We can debate philosophy later. Move!"
Kael followed.
Behind them, Zarek's laughter echoed through the collapsing facility.

They ran through the third level as the structure groaned its death throes. Kael's mind churned with what he'd harvested—the fragment of corrupted knowledge, the implications of what Zarek had suggested.
The first Soul Reaver. The tomb. The truth about what he was becoming.
They reached the surface as the coal processing facility imploded behind them. The explosion sent a plume of debris and smoke rising against Thornwick's perpetual smog.
Kael collapsed against a wall, breathing hard.
"What did you get?" Gwen asked, crouching beside him. "From the harvest?"
"A piece of something." He touched his temple. "Knowledge. Instructions. Partial memories of rituals I don't understand." He looked at his hands. "And a warning. The entities are aware of Soul Reavers now. Aware of me."
"What does that mean?"
"It means I'm going to have enemies beyond the cult, beyond the Orders." He forced himself to stand. "And apparently, a potential recruitment pitch from the Abyss itself."
Maren leaned against a broken wall, her wounds still bleeding sluggishly. "This keeps getting better."
"We need to regroup," Kael said. "Medical attention. New intelligence on cult activity patterns. And—" He hesitated. "—someone needs to tell the Orders what Zarek revealed. The seals are failing faster than they admitted."
"The Orders won't listen to you," Gwen said. "You're a wanted criminal with a Soul Reaver bounty."
"Then we find someone who will listen." Kael met her eyes. "Your sister, the Iron Vanguard—any chance they—"
"Mirren is alive." Gwen's jaw tightened. "I need to find out if what that cultist said is true. If she's been compromised, if everything I believed about her death was a lie..."
"We'll find out," Kael said. "Together."
The Undercroft district stretched before them—a maze of poverty, desperation, and hidden dangers. Somewhere in this labyrinth, the cult operated. Somewhere, Prime Zarek was licking his wounds and planning his next move.
And somewhere in Thornwick, the seals continued to fail.
Kael touched his temple, feeling the edges of the corrupted knowledge he'd harvested. Fragments of entity lore. Hints about what the Abyss truly wanted.
He had enemies on all sides now. The cult wanted him dead. The Orders wanted him captured—or dead, depending on which faction. And now the Abyss itself had taken notice.
But he had something else: information. Power. The ability to harvest skills and knowledge from the dead.
The fight was just beginning.
A figure emerged from the shadows ahead. Order of Dawn robes, silver mask. Not Vex—a different enforcer.
"Marshal Ashborn." The voice was mechanical, distorted. "Commander Dorian requests your presence. Immediately."
Kael raised his revolver.
Behind him, Gwen and Maren prepared to fight.
The enforcer raised empty hands. "This is not a confrontation. This is an invitation. The Commander knows about your encounter with Prime Zarek. He has information you need."
"What information?"
"The location of the first Soul Reaver's tomb."
Kael's blood ran cold.
---
They stood in the ruins of a steam-powered factory, dawn's light filtering through broken skylights. Commander Dorian waited in the center, flanked by six Order of Dawn enforcers.
"Marshal Ashborn." Dorian was younger than Kael expected—mid-thirties, with the sharp features and calculating eyes of a born politician. "You look terrible."
"Occupational hazard." Kael kept his hand near his weapon. "You said you had information."
"I do. The location of the tomb where the first Soul Reaver was buried—and where the secrets of your power originated." Dorian smiled thinly. "I also know who ordered Gwen Steelborn's squad into the ambush that killed them. I know who betrayed the Mortician Guild's shipment last spring. I know about the Shadow Court's current operations in Thornwick."
Gwen stepped forward. "Who?"
"That's the price of cooperation." Dorian's eyes gleamed. "Work with me, Kael. The Order of Dawn can offer resources, intelligence, protection from the cult and the other factions hunting you. In return, you help me clean up this mess before it consumes the entire city."
"And if I refuse?"
"Then you continue running, bleeding, fighting enemies you don't understand with resources you don't have." Dorian spread his hands. "I'm offering partnership, not servitude. The Abyss is coming. The seals are failing. The cult is more organized than anyone realized. We need allies with unique capabilities—and you need someone who can provide context for what you're becoming."
Kael's mind raced. Dorian represented the Order of Dawn—a faction he'd had every reason to distrust. But Zarek's words echoed in his memory. The entities were coming. The seals were failing. And the truth about his power might be the only weapon that could save him.
"Tell me about the tomb," Kael said.
"Work with me, and I'll tell you everything."
Footsteps echoed from outside. More Order of Dawn enforcers appeared, sealing the exits.
Dorian's smile didn't waver. "The conversation is just beginning, Marshal. Shall we continue somewhere more... Private?"
The trap was obvious. Kael could feel it closing around him.
But the truth about his origins—the answers he'd been seeking since awakening in his own grave—was within reach.
And that was worth the risk.
