The blade caught the torchlight wrong, twisting the shadows into writhing shapes along the brick walls. Kael pressed himself against the alley's edge, breath shallow, fingers wrapped around the hilt of his father's sword. Three figures moved through the steam fog ahead, their robes bearing that symbol again—the circle with seven tears.
"The merchant's district," one of them hissed. "Prime Zarek wants the soul-essence harvested before the Iron Vanguard patrols."
Kael's stomach clenched. Soul-essence. They were harvesting people like cattle.
He had followed the trio for six blocks, ever since they'd dragged a shivering vagrant into the Underworld entrance behind the abandoned tannery. The trail led down, spiraling through maintenance tunnels that reeked of coal smoke and something older—copper, decay, ozone.
The leader paused at an iron door, producing a key that glowed faintly blue. Spiritual crafting. Expensive. Illicit.
"The Sepulcher tablets confirmed it," the speaker continued, voice dropping. "The seal on the forty-third rift is failing. We need more vessels."
Kael's grip tightened. The forty-third rift. One of the seventy-two sealed breaches between the world and the Abyss. The Orders kept detailed records of each one—locations, deterioration rates, containment protocols. He'd seen fragments of those records in the execution chamber, burned into his mind by the dying light of his own revival.
No citizen should know which rifts were failing. No cultist should possess that information.
The door groaned open. Stale air rushed outward, carrying screams.
Kael moved.
His body remembered the sword before his mind caught up—three years of arena combat, fighting for entertainment in the pit where the condemned made their final exhibition. The first cultist fell before he registered the swing, the blade opening his throat in a spray of arterial red. The second stumbled backward, hands rising with hastily-formed spiritual barriers that shattered like ice against Kael's furious assault.
"Please—" the man managed, before Kael's blade found his heart.
The third cultist fled into the darkness beyond the door.
Kael didn't pursue. Instead, he stood in the doorway, chest heaving, blood dripping from steel, and stared at the chamber beyond.
Rows of stone tables stretched into the gloom. Bodies lay upon them—some dead, some screaming, all connected by copper tubing that snaked toward a central mechanism resembling a massive brass heart. At the heart's core, a swirling mass of pale light pulsed with sickly luminescence.
Soul-essence. Dozens of vials surrounding the device, each containing fragments of human consciousness.
The harvested dead.
"Kael Ashborn." The voice echoed from the shadows near the far wall. "The Soul Reaver who should have died."
Kael spun, sword raised. A figure emerged from the darkness—tall, gaunt, wearing the black vestments of the Order of Twilight with silver trim. A Twilight Inquisitor. Stage three. At minimum.
"You're earlier than projected," the Inquisitor said. His face remained hidden beneath a ceremonial hood, but his voice carried the flat certainty of absolute confidence. "The intelligence suggested you would remain in the Upper Borough for another three days."
"Intelligence?" Kael kept his feet planted, weight balanced. The tunnel exit behind him lay twenty feet back. The Inquisitor blocked the only other path forward.
"The Shadow Court has been monitoring your progress since your resurrection." The Inquisitor's hand moved to his belt, where a weapon hung—not a sword, but a curved staff crackling with violet energy. "You've been remarkably resourceful. Learning to control your abilities without instruction. Navigating the Underworld without maps. Defeating opponents far above your stage." A pause. "Impressive. Irritating. Both things can be true."
Kael's mind raced. The Shadow Court. The hidden hand that moved pieces across the board of Thornwick's criminal underworld, supposedly disbanded centuries ago but whispered about in every back-alley negotiation. They were watching him.
"I killed your brothers in the alley," Kael said. "You're alone."
"The Mortician's Guild controls the morgue entrances. The Iron Vanguard patrols the surface. The Arcane Spire maintains no presence below the second Underworld tier." The Inquisitor took a step forward, staff trailing sparks. "You are standing in unclaimed territory. No one will hear you die."
"I'm not planning to die today."
"No. You're planning to harvest me."
The words hit like a physical blow. Kael's expression didn't change—three years in the arena had trained that reflex out of him—but internally, his thoughts spiraled. The Soul Reaver ability. The power that let him touch the dead and pull their skills, their memories, their very essence into himself. He had used it three times since his resurrection, always in secret, always on enemies who had attacked him first. Word had spread faster than he'd imagined.
"You're not afraid," Kael observed.
"I am an Inquisitor of the Order of Twilight. I have hunted seventeen Reavers in my career. Sixteen died screaming." The staff's crackling intensified. "You will be the seventeenth."
Kael lunged.
The Inquisitor moved with preternatural speed, sidestepping Kael's thrust and bringing the staff around in a vicious arc. Violet energy blazed against Kael's hastily-raised guard, sending agony lancing through his arms. The sword nearly flew from his grip.
Primary ability: Spiritual Amplification. The Inquisitor could channel ambient energy through physical contact, multiplying the force of his strikes exponentially.
Kael compensated by fighting dirty. He dropped low, sweeping the Inquisitor's legs, then drove his elbow into the man's throat as they crashed to the stone floor. The staff clattered away.
The Inquisitor's eyes widened—not with fear, but with calculation. His mouth opened, forming words of power, and Kael smashed his palm against the man's chest.
The Soul Reaver activation felt like plunging his hand into ice water and fire simultaneously.
[Soul Harvest: Initiating contact extraction.]
The Inquisitor's back arched. His screams filled the chamber, echoing off the walls, bouncing between the stone tables where the harvested victims lay in various states of consciousness. Kael felt the transfer beginning—spiritual energy flowing from the dying man into his own reserves, muscle memory flooding his nervous system, and fragments of memory burning themselves into his consciousness.
[Soul Harvest: 47 spiritual energy units extracted. Amplification Technique acquired. Temporal Fragment extracted.]
The Inquisitor went limp. Kael released the body, gasping, his body trembling from the intensity of the transfer. The notifications scrolled through his vision, appearing without his conscious thought—a gift from the entity that had branded him, the force that had pulled him back from true death.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 47 spiritual energy from the remains. Amplification Technique acquired. Temporal Fragment acquired.]
Amplification Technique. He could feel it nestled in his mind—a knowledge of how to channel external energy through physical contact, how to multiply the force behind his strikes. The same ability the Inquisitor had used against him.
Temporal Fragment. A memory, locked away until he had time to examine it.
The brass heart device continued its rhythmic pulsing. Around him, the harvested victims stirred on their stone tables, some groaning, others silent, all bearing the marks of spiritual extraction.
Kael sheathed his sword and moved to the nearest table. A woman, middle-aged, wearing the tattered remains of a servant's uniform. Her eyes fluttered open as he approached.
"The Order of Dusk," she whispered. "They came for us three days ago. The Hollowing, they called it. Said we were chosen."
"Chosen for what?"
"To feed the seal." Her voice cracked. "The Sun God's chosen, they said. Seventy-two souls to restore the forty-third rift. We weren't the only ones."
Seventy-two souls. The number matched the total rifts in the world. But the cultist had mentioned the forty-third specifically.
"How many others?" Kael demanded.
"I don't know. Maybe a hundred. Maybe more." Tears streaked her hollow cheeks. "They're moving us through tunnels. Taking us somewhere deeper. The place with the blue light."
Blue light. The color associated with freshly-formed soul-essence, before it degraded to pale white. Someone was conducting massive harvests underground, and the evidence was everywhere—dozens of vials, multiple tables, a device designed for bulk extraction.
The woman seized his wrist with surprising strength. "You have to tell the Orders. The Shadow Court knows. They've always known. That's why they sent you here."
"They sent me?"
"The Reaver was never meant to survive. But they made sure you'd wake up. They need someone to expose this. Someone beyond the law." Her grip loosened. "You understand? You can't go to the authorities. The authorities are compromised. You have to make it public. Make them see."
Her eyes rolled back. Kael checked her pulse—faint, thready, but present. Still alive. He moved to the next table, then the next, assessing the damage. Most were beyond saving without immediate medical attention. A few had already passed, their bodies cooling on the stone.
The freshest corpse lay nearest the brass heart device. A young man, maybe eighteen, with the calloused hands of a laborer and the hollow gaze of the recently harvested. His spiritual signature still lingered, not yet dissipated.
Kael knelt beside him and pressed his palm to the cooling flesh.
[Soul Harvest: 23 spiritual energy units extracted. Tunnel Mapping (partial) acquired. Laborer's Endurance acquired.]
Tunnel Mapping. A mental image exploded through Kael's consciousness—a three-dimensional representation of the Underworld's deeper levels, including routes he'd never seen and locations marked with symbols he didn't recognize. One symbol repeated at a dozen points: the circle with seven tears.
Laborer's Endurance. His muscles ached less. His breathing came easier. The body's conditioning transferred to his own physical limits.
He rose, examining the brass heart device with new understanding. The copper tubing connected to a central reservoir where soul-essence collected, then distributed to storage vials. Someone had engineered this operation for scale. Weeks of preparation. Months, possibly.
The Iron Vanguard would never allow this to continue if they knew. The Order of Dawn would burn the tunnels themselves if presented with evidence. But the Shadow Court—allegedly disbanded, supposedly a myth—had known all along and done nothing except send him.
Kael retrieved the Inquisitor's staff, testing its weight. Spiritual crafting above his current capacity, but functional. He could use it as a focus for the Amplification Technique.
The Temporal Fragment from the Inquisitor pulsed at the edge of his awareness. A memory, waiting to be examined. But not here. Not now.
He moved through the chamber, gathering the most coherent survivors. Three could walk with assistance. Two needed to be carried. The rest were too far gone, their souls drained beyond recovery.
"The tunnels," Kael said to the standing survivors. "Which way did they take the others?"
The woman who had spoken earlier pointed toward a passage at the chamber's far end. "That way. Toward the Foundry."
The Foundry. One of the Underworld's largest industrial districts, where steam engines powered by illegally-harvested soul-essence manufactured goods for the black market. Kael had passed through it twice since his resurrection, both times avoiding the notice of its brutal overseers.
If they were holding prisoners there, it meant the Foundry's leadership was complicit. Or controlled by the cult.
He couldn't save everyone. The mathematics of the situation were brutal but clear—three able-bodied survivors, two invalids, one Reaver with a sword and stolen staff against an unknown number of cultists operating in hostile territory.
But the woman had said seventy-two souls for the seal. And she had mentioned others—maybe a hundred, maybe more. If the cult had already harvested enough to begin the ritual, the window for intervention was closing.
Kael made his decision.
"You." He pointed to the strongest survivor. "Can you navigate to the Upper Borough? The Clockmaker's district?"
"I... Yes. I know it."
"Find a woman named Sera. Tell her Kael sent you. Tell her the Underworld is compromised and the cult has more prisoners than we thought." He pressed the stolen staff into the man's hands. "This will protect you. Don't let anyone take it."
"What about you?"
"I'm going to find the others."
He left them at the tunnel junction, directing them toward the surface exits while he descended into the depths. The passage narrowed as he traveled, the brick walls giving way to rough-hewn stone, then natural caverns where water dripped from stalactites and the air grew thick with sulfurous heat.
The blue light appeared ahead.
Kael extinguished his torch and moved by the dim glow of his own spiritual energy—a technique he'd discovered instinctively, channeling his reserves to produce bioluminescent light from his skin. Not bright, but sufficient for navigation.
The cavern opened into a vast underground cathedral. Pillars of carved obsidian rose toward a ceiling lost in darkness. At the center, a massive seal diagram blazed with azure light, its geometric patterns rotating slowly in violation of natural law. Around it, figures in ceremonial robes swayed in rhythmic chant, their voices creating harmonics that made Kael's teeth ache.
And on the seal itself, bound by chains of crystallized spiritual energy, dozens of people knelt in catatonic devotion. Their eyes were open but empty. Their mouths moved in unison with the cultists' chanting.
Seventy-two figures. Seventy-two souls, already partially drained, feeding their essence into the seal diagram.
The forty-third rift, Kael realized. They weren't trying to restore it. They were trying to open it.
A man stood at the seal's focal point—elderly, withered, wearing robes that had once been the white of the Order of Dawn but were now stained with decades of accumulated grime. His hands moved through complex gestures, directing the flow of harvested soul-essence toward a specific point in the diagram.
At that point, reality itself seemed to thin. A dark spot pulsed with hungry absence, the membrane between worlds growing weaker with each passing moment.
"The Reaver arrives." The elderly man's voice cut through the chanting. The cultists fell silent. "I wondered if you would resist the compulsion."
Kael froze. Compulsion? What compulsion?
"The Shadow Court inserted instructions into your resurrection," the man continued, his withered face splitting into a smile that held no warmth. "A trigger phrase. Did you not notice the obsession that drew you here? The certainty that you had to descend, had to investigate, had to find this place?"
Kael's blood ran cold. The woman in the chamber above—she had said the Shadow Court sent him. She had called him a tool. He had assumed she meant they had manipulated events from a distance, not that they had directly programmed his behavior.
"You've used the Reaver ability twice since entering this tunnel complex," the elderly man observed. "Once on Brother Aldric, once on the laborer. Your reserves should be depleted. Your body should be exhausted. Yet you stand before me, ready to fight." He laughed, a dry rattling sound. "The Soul Reaver was always the most useful of the Abyssal gifts. Zero cost. Maximum extraction. Do you know why the Orders fear you so much?"
Kael said nothing. His eyes swept the cavern, counting cultists—twenty at least, with weapons drawn, all of them wearing the same symbol. The circle with seven tears. But some bore additional markings: crossed bones, empty eyes, stylized flames.
Different factions. United in purpose.
"The Soul Reaver can drain the dead," the elderly man said. "But did anyone tell you it can drain the living as well? That the boundary between corpse and living tissue is only a matter of spiritual presence?" His smile widened. "I am Prime Zarek, leader of the Tears of the Veil. And I have been waiting for a Reaver to complete the ritual."
The Azure light pulsed. The dark spot at the seal's focal point began to expand.
"Forty-three rifts open," Zarek continued. "When this one joins them, the Abyss will pour through. Seventy-three Entities will emerge. The seal network will collapse within hours. And every living soul on this continent will become... Harvestable."
Kael understood then. The cult wasn't trying to restore a failing seal. They were trying to deliberately accelerate the apocalypse. Seventy-three Entities released simultaneously would tear the Veil apart. Reality would collapse. And these madmen believed they could control what emerged.
"You'll die with everyone else," Kael said.
"Some sacrifices are acceptable." Zarek's hands continued their gestures, accelerating the ritual. "The Entities respect power. When we emerge from the collapse as the strongest Awakened survivors, we will rebuild the world in our image. This is not destruction. This is renovation."
The cultists began to advance, forming a tightening circle around Kael.
He could run. The tunnel behind him remained clear. He could escape, warn the survivors, potentially reach the surface and alert the Orders before the ritual completed.
But the seventy-two people on the seal—bound, drained, Devoured. They would die if he fled. And if the rift opened, everyone on the surface would die as well.
Kael drew his father's sword. The blade gleamed with borrowed light.
"If I drain you," he said to Zarek, "does the ritual stop?"
"Touch me and discover the answer."
Kael charged.
The cultists met him in a wave of spiritual energy and sharpened steel. Kael's blade sang through the first strike, opening a throat, spinning to catch a staff aimed at his skull. The Amplification Technique flooded through his borrowed muscle memory, and his counter-blow shattered the attacking weapon and the arm that wielded it.
More came. Always more.
He harvested in motion—palms striking faces, fingers closing on throats, each contact pulling fragments of ability and memory from the dying cultists. Shadow Step from one. Pain Tolerance from another. A third gave him nothing but the man's final memory: Zarek binding the seal chains, speaking words of power that twisted the air itself.
[Soul Harvest: 34 spiritual energy units extracted. Shadow Step acquired. Pain Tolerance acquired.]
[Soul Harvest: 12 spiritual energy units extracted. Memory Fragment extracted.]
The notifications scrolled through his vision, but Kael had no time to examine them. Zarek stood at the seal's center, hands never pausing, the azure light growing brighter with each passing moment.
The rift pulsed. Reality rippled. Through the thinning membrane, Kael glimpsed something vast and hungry turning its attention toward the Material plane.
An Entity.
The pressure of its gaze alone drove cultists to their knees, their minds shattering from contact with pure Abyssal consciousness. Kael felt it too—a weight on his thoughts, a whisper in his blood—but the Soul Reaver's gift included resistance to such incursions. He walked forward, step by grinding step, toward the seal's focal point.
"Fascinating," Zarek murmured. "You should be dead. The Entity's presence alone should have destroyed your consciousness." His eyes narrowed. "The original Reaver's protections. They embedded fragments of their own soul in the creation of your ability."
Kael stopped three feet from the elderly cultist leader. The seal's chains wrapped around Zarek's wrists, anchoring him to the ritual's power source. Breaking those chains would require severing spiritual connections beyond Kael's current capacity.
But severing the connection between Zarek and the seal might be possible.
"If I drain you," Kael repeated, "does the ritual stop?"
"You would need to touch me. The chains will kill you before you reach me."
Kael looked at the crystallized spiritual energy binding Zarek's wrists. Then he looked at the seventy-two victims kneeling on the seal, their eyes empty, their souls nearly depleted.
He still had the Temporal Fragment from the Inquisitor. A memory, locked away until he examined it.
Kael focused on the fragment. The memory exploded through his consciousness—a scene of the Inquisitor standing before the Shadow Court's hidden council, receiving instructions about a Reaver who would soon awaken. The council's leader spoke words that now burned in Kael's mind with absolute clarity.
"The Reaver will serve as both key and sword. When the time comes, he will either seal the rift permanently or open it fully. There is no middle path."
No middle path. The Shadow Court hadn't just sent him here to witness the ritual. They had sent him knowing he would be forced to make a choice.
Seal the rift permanently—draining every ounce of his spiritual reserves and possibly his life force in the flow.
Or open it fully—becoming the weapon they had designed him to be.
Zarek's smile turned cruel. "You see it now. The trap closes. You cannot stop the ritual without sacrificing yourself. And if you sacrifice yourself, you become the final key that opens the door forever."
The Entity's presence pressed harder against the thinning membrane. The rift pulsed wider. Blood trickled from Kael's nose, his ears, his eyes as his body struggled to withstand the spiritual pressure.
He could feel the Seal Fragment pulsing in his chest—another gift from his resurrection, something he'd discovered but never fully understood. The fragment wanted him to act. It wanted him to pour everything into the seal and hope he survived.
But the Shadow Court's words echoed in his memory. Both key and sword. Either seal permanently or open fully.
Kael sheathed his sword. His hands moved through gestures he'd never learned, drawing on knowledge that belonged to someone else—the original Soul Reaver, the first bearer of this curse. The Seal Fragment responded, its warmth spreading through his limbs, and he understood what he could do.
Not seal. Not open. Sharpen.
The forty-third rift was failing because its seals were degrading. The cult had accelerated the flow by harvesting souls and feeding them into the mechanism. But Kael could reverse the flow—not by closing the rift, but by reinforcing the existing seal with harvested energy. It would take everything he had. It would leave him unconscious, possibly dying.
But it would save the seventy-two victims and buy the world time.
"You understand," Zarek breathed, his smile faltering. "You cannot possibly—"
Kael's palm struck the elderly cultist's chest.
The Soul Reaver activation screamed through his nerves as he pulled. Not abilities this time—spiritual reserves, accumulated power, the very essence that kept the man alive. Zarek's eyes bulged. His scream cut off as Kael drained him dry, feeding that stolen energy directly into the failing seal.
The azure light blazed white-hot.
The cultists' chanting resumed, but differently now—confusion, fear, the sound of zealots realizing their messiah had died.
Kael's vision darkened at the edges. His body wanted to collapse. He poured more of himself into the seal, reinforcing the geometric patterns, strengthening the membrane, pushing back against the Entity's hungry presence.
The rift pulsed once, twice—then began to contract.
"Impossible," someone shouted. "The ritual requires—"
Kael collapsed.
The last thing he heard before unconsciousness claimed him was the sound of footsteps approaching from the tunnel behind him. Multiple sets. Moving with coordinated precision.
The Shadow Court had sent a retrieval team.
Darkness swallowed everything.

"You're conscious." A woman's voice. Young, controlled, bearing the clipped accent of the Upper Borough aristocracy. "The medics said you would die within the hour."
Kael tried to sit up and discovered his wrists were bound to the bed frame with spiritual suppression cuffs. His spiritual reserves felt hollow—not empty, but depleted, like a well filled with silt.
"Where am I?"
"The Shadow Court's Thornwick chapter house." The woman stepped into view—dark hair, sharp features, wearing a tailored coat over practical combat attire. Her eyes held the flat assessment of someone who evaluated threats for a living. "I am Director Thorne. The organization has questions about your activities last night."
Kael's memories assembled themselves: the tunnel complex, the brass heart device, the harvested victims, the underground cathedral, Zarek's ritual. He had drained the cult leader and poured that stolen energy into the failing seal. The rift had contracted. The Entity had retreated.
And now he was here, in the Shadow Court's headquarters, bound like a prisoner.
"You sent me there," he said. "The compulsion. The obsession that drew me into the tunnels."
"We introduced a suggestion. Whether you acted on it was your choice." Director Thorne's expression didn't change. "You exceeded our projections. Draining Prime Zarek directly was... Unprecedented. The amount of energy you channeled should have destroyed your soul entirely."
"But it didn't."
"No. It did not." She leaned forward slightly. "The fragment you carry—the Seal Fragment—is not only a tool. It is a piece of the original Soul Reaver's consciousness. When you channeled that much power through yourself, you should have been overwritten. Absorbed into the Abyssal consciousness. Instead, you integrated the fragment's functions into your own being."
Kael processed this. The Seal Fragment—a piece of the first Reaver, embedded in his soul during resurrection. It had protected him, guided him, and now apparently it had merged with his consciousness in ways the Shadow Court hadn't anticipated.
"Why am I still alive?"

The monitoring equipment beeped steadily. Outside, Kael heard footsteps—guards, probably, patrolling the corridor.
"The cult's ritual failed," Director Thorne continued. "Zarek is dead. His followers are scattered. The forty-third rift is more stable than it has been in decades, thanks to your intervention." A pause. "But forty-two other rifts continue to degrade. And the cult has access to similar rituals at each location."
Forty-two other rifts. Similar rituals. Kael's stomach dropped.
"You're telling me this is just the beginning."

They have infiltrated the Orders at levels we are only beginning to understand." Director Thorne's voice remained flat, but something flickered in her eyes. "We need the Soul Reaver. Not as a weapon—many have tried to weaponize your kind and failed. We need you as a countermeasure. Someone who can drain cultists without killing them. Someone who can reinforce failing seals without sacrificing themselves. Someone who can walk the line between sealing and opening because that line is where the war will be won or lost."

"Then you remain bound in this room while the world burns. The Orders fall one by one. The rifts open. The Entities emerge. In approximately six months, reality collapses entirely." The monitoring equipment beeped. Kael's depleted reserves pulsed weakly in his chest.
"What do you want from me?"

"We want you to meet our Council. All of them. We want to show you what we have learned about the original Soul Reaver. And we want to offer you a choice."

She leaned close, her voice dropping to a whisper.
"Work with us, and we will teach you how to use your abilities without destroying yourself. We will provide intelligence on cult activities across the continent. We will shield you from the Orders' hunters—at least until you are strong enough to shield yourself."

Kael's mind raced. The Shadow Court—supposedly mythical, allegedly disbanded centuries ago—was real, present, and offering him survival in exchange for servitude. They had manipulated his resurrection, inserted compulsions into his thoughts, and now they wanted him to become their tool.
But they had also told him the truth about the cult's scope. About the failing seals. About the Entities waiting to emerge.
The woman in the tunnel chamber had said the Shadow Court needed someone to expose the conspiracy. But Director Thorne spoke of different needs entirely.

"When do I meet this Council?"

"A medic will visit to remove the cuffs once your reserves stabilize. I suggest you rest. The coming weeks will test you in ways the arena never could."
The door closed behind her. Kael lay in the bed, staring at the ceiling, his thoughts churning.
The Shadow Court wanted him as a countermeasure. The cult wanted to open every rift simultaneously. The Orders remained oblivious, their internal corruption preventing them from seeing the scope of the threat.
And somewhere in his depleted reserves, the Seal Fragment pulsed with borrowed memory—fragments of the original Soul Reaver's consciousness, waiting to be explored.
Seventy-two rifts. Forty-two actively degrading. One Reaver with zero spiritual cost and a gift for harvesting the dead.
The arithmetic was brutal. The timeline was impossible.
But Kael had survived execution. He had mastered abilities that should have destroyed him. He had drained a cult leader and reinforced a seal that decades of careful work had nearly broken.
He would not die in a Shadow Court holding cell.
The question was what he would become in the flow of surviving.
Outside his door, footsteps continued their patrol. Kael closed his eyes and began to plan.
