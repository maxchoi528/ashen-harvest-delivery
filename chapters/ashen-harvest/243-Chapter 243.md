"You again." The voice came from the alley's mouth, where a hooded figure leaned against damp brick. "Thought you'd have fled Thornwick by now."
Kael's hand stopped mid-reach for the bread he'd been considering. Three days hiding in this crumbling tenement, and someone had found him anyway. That meant either exceptional skill or exceptional stupidity. In Thornwick, those often looked identical.
"Depends who's asking." He kept his voice flat, his body angled away from the streetlamp's sickly glow. A steam-vent hissed somewhere behind him, masking footsteps that might—or might not—be approaching from the rear.
The figure laughed, low and mirthless. "No one worth lying to, Ashborn. I know what you are."
Cold trickled down Kael's spine. The Soul Reaver label had followed him from the gallows to this rat-infested corner, and it would likely follow him to whatever grave eventually claimed him. He turned, finally, to face the stranger.
A woman stepped into the light. Young, maybe twenty-five, with the pale complexion of someone who spent too many nights indoors. Her eyes caught the lamplight wrong, pupils contracted to pinpoints despite the gloom. Spiritual sensitivity—the middle tier, not Awakened, but enough to sense what others couldn't.
"The Shadow Court sent you," Kael said. Not a question.
"They sent me to talk. Whether you listen is your problem." She produced a folded paper from her cloak. "Word is you owe a debt to certain parties who dislike being owed nothing."
Kael hadn't moved. His fingers rested at his sides, close enough to draw the knife at his hip if this turned sour. It would, probably. Everything turned sour eventually in Thornwick.
"I'm listening."
The woman smiled, thin as a paper cut. "Good. Because what's on this paper will determine whether you leave this alley alive."

The paper contained names. Not many—six, scattered across Thornwick's districts like dropped coins. But the surnames attached to them made Kael's stomach clench. Industrialists, politicians, a magistrate from the Order of Dawn's civic liaison office. People with power, or access to power.
"Recognize any?" The woman watched him with those too-sharp eyes.
"Should I?"
"Three of these names appeared in Mortician's Guild shipping manifests two weeks ago. Containers marked 'surgical supplies' that weighed enough to hold bodies." She paused. "Or something worse."
Kael's jaw tightened. The Mortician's Guild controlled the legal soul-essence trade, harvesting with family consent, operating under Order oversight. If they were moving cargo alongside legitimate shipments, someone was skimming from the corpse pipeline. And corpse-skimming meant either black market soul-essence or something even uglier.
"Why tell me this?"
"Because you're useful." The woman's smile widened, showing teeth. "A Soul Reaver asking questions gets answers that a middle-tier like me could never extract. And because the Shadow Court wants those containers found before the Order of Dawn does."
"The Shadow Court." Kael let the words hang in the cold air. "The same organization that tried to have me executed three months ago."
"That was a different faction. Internal politics." She shrugged, the gesture rehearsed. "Current leadership sees value in your... Unique talents. The old accusations were politically convenient. They're not convenient anymore."
"And if I refuse?"
"Then you stay in this alley, eating stale bread, waiting for whoever comes next with less friendly intentions." She tilted her head, birdlike. "The cult activity in the Undercroft district has tripled in the past month. People are disappearing. The Orders know but they're paralyzed by protocol. The Shadow Court doesn't have those limitations."
Cult activity. Kael had noticed the signs—the way shadows pooled in corners that shouldn't cast them, the whispers that followed certain merchants through the market stalls. He'd assumed it was background noise, the usual spiritual contamination that plagued any city built atop old battlegrounds.
But organized cult activity meant people working together. Meant resources, planning, purpose.
"The cults aren't random," he said slowly. "They're coordinated."
"Now you're thinking like someone who survives." The woman pressed the paper into his hand. "The containers are being moved through the old steamworks tonight. Midnight shift change, when the guards are shortest-staffed. Whatever's in those crates, the cultists are terrified of it leaving Thornwick."
Kael looked at the paper, then back at the woman. "You're asking me to steal from the Mortician's Guild."
"I'm asking you to find the truth. What you do with it is your business." She stepped backward into the shadows. "One more thing. A gift, since you're considering cooperation."
She pulled something from her cloak—a small vial, glass catching dim light. Soul-essence. High-grade, by the color, enough to fuel several serious battles or buy information that would otherwise stay buried.
"From someone who appreciates what you do. Even if the world doesn't."
The vial landed in Kael's palm, cool against his skin. He closed his fingers around it before he could think better of the reflex.
"Who?"
"Someone who remembers the old ways. Before the Orders decided they knew better than everyone else." Her voice came from the darkness now, already retreating. "Someone who knows what the Soul Reaver originally meant. What it was meant to protect."
She was gone before he could respond, footsteps fading into the city's endless mechanical breath. Steam hissed from a hundred vents. Somewhere distant, a factory whistle shrieked the hour.

Midnight found Kael crouched behind a rusted steam-joint, watching the Thornwick Central Steamworks through a gap in corrugated iron. The facility was a cathedral of industry, all iron girders and spinning gears, its heartbeat the relentless throb of pistons driving water through pipes older than most living memory.
The shipping bay sat at the facility's eastern edge, a squat building connected to the main complex by an enclosed walkway. Lamplight painted yellow rectangles on concrete. Two guards flanked the loading doors, their postures suggesting boredom than alertness. Midnight shift, just like she'd promised.
But Kael had been in enough ambushes to recognize when something felt wrong. The shadows here pooled too deep, even accounting for the hour. The guards' boredom seemed rehearsed, performed for an audience that might be watching.
He waited. Watched. Counted the rhythm of steam releases until his internal clock said twenty minutes past midnight.
Movement. A cart emerged from the shipping bay's side door, pushed by two workers in Mortician's Guild gray. The crate on the cart was large, easily large enough to hold a body. Larger, maybe, than any single corpse would require.
The guards didn't react. That was the tell—professionals watched for exactly this kind of distraction, the deliberate withdrawal of attention while something valuable was moved. These guards weren't checking the cart. They weren't checking anything.
Kael slipped from cover as the cart rolled toward the facility's eastern gate. He kept to the shadows, bare feet silent on cold concrete, tracking the crate's slow progress. The workers didn't look back. The guards didn't look forward.
Everyone was playing their part. Everyone except the person who'd paid for this theater.
He caught up with the cart as it passed beneath a broken streetlamp, its glow too weak to reach the ground. One worker stumbled, cursed, and the other paused to help steady him. A minor delay, maybe thirty seconds. Enough time for Kael to slide beneath the cart, fingers finding the crate's underside.
Wood. Nails. And something else—a vibration, faint but unmistakable, like a second heartbeat running parallel to his own.
Spiritual energy. Concentrated, contained, pulsing with the slow rhythm of something alive.
Or something that wanted to be.
Kael hung there as the cart resumed motion, his mind racing through implications. The Mortician's Guild dealt in soul-essence and legally harvested remains. They didn't trade in living spiritual energy sources. That kind of concentration required something far more valuable.
Something the Orders would kill to possess. Or destroy.
The cart stopped at the eastern gate. A gate guard—different from the bay guards, older, with the weathered look of someone who'd seen too much—stepped forward with a clipboard. The workers produced documents. The guard examined them under lamplight, frowning at something Kael couldn't see.
"Problem?" one worker asked.
"Your authorization's been flagged." The guard's voice carried across the silent street. "New protocol. Supervisor wants to inspect all outgoing shipments personally."
The workers exchanged glances. Kael saw the tension in their shoulders, the way their hands drifted toward pockets that might contain weapons.
"This is a standard delivery. Same as last week."
"Last week there weren't cultist killings in three districts." The guard set down his clipboard. "Open the crate."
Silence stretched. The workers didn't move. The guard waited, hand resting on the truncheon at his hip.
Then the air split open.

The scream came from inside the crate—a human sound, torn between agony and something else, something that resonated in Kael's bones. The guards at the gate stumbled back. The workers bolted, abandoning the cart as the lid exploded upward in a spray of splinters.
Something emerged. Tall, wrong, its limbs too long and joints bending in directions that hurt to observe. Skin like wet leather stretched over nothing, draping bones that clicked and ground with each movement. Where a face should have been, there was only a smooth expanse that pulsed with inner light.
An Entity-touched. Someone transformed, or being transformed, by direct contact with Abyssal energy. Kael had seen the results before—on battlefields, in morgue reports, in the nightmares that followed failed seal repairs. He'd never seen one emerge from a shipping crate like livestock.
The creature's blank face turned toward the fleeing workers. Its mouth opened, impossibly wide, and the sound that emerged was not a scream, not a roar—something between that humans weren't meant to produce.
Kael dropped from the cart, landing in a crouch as the Entity-touched lunged. It moved fast, faster than anything that size should move, its elongated arms sweeping toward the gate guard. The old man had his truncheon up, spiritual energy flaring in a hastily-constructed barrier.
The barrier shattered. The guard went flying, body crumpling against brick with a sound like wet wood snapping.
Kael was already moving, circling wide, trying to find an angle. The creature's attention fixed on the workers now, pursuing them down the street with that horrible loping gait. Its prey screamed, stumbled, kept running.
Not his people. Not his problem. The smart play was to let the creature hunt, to slip away while it was distracted, to take the information he'd gathered and vanish into Thornwick's endless maze of alleys and steam-vents.
The Soul Reaver in him stirred, sensing the spiritual energy radiating from that twisted form. Fresh. Powerful. Everything a harvest should be, if harvesting didn't require touching the source.
And the source was moving away, chasing prey that wouldn't survive another block.
Kael ran.
He caught the creature as it cornered one of the workers against a dead-end wall. The man was sobbing, hands raised in useless defense, prayers tumbling from his lips in a steady stream. The Entity-touched loomed over him, that awful mouth stretching wider.
Kael hit it from behind, driving his knife into what passed for a shoulder joint. The blade sank deep, resistance grating against bone that felt older than it should. The creature screamed—that half-scream, half-roar—and spun, faster than Kael could track.
Claws raked across his chest. His coat shredded, fabric parting like paper. Pain blazed white-hot across his ribs, and he rolled away before the next strike could follow through.
The worker scrambled past, fleeing into darkness. One prey lost. The creature's attention shifted fully to Kael now, that eyeless face conveying rage.
"You should have stayed hidden." Kael's voice came out steadier than he felt. He kept the knife in his grip, blood dripping from the blade. "But you're not exactly known for rational decisions, are you? Transformed by some cult ritual, probably. Pulled through a weakened seal, most likely. You've got maybe an hour before whatever's keeping you coherent drains away completely."
The creature lunged.
Kael sidestepped, slashing at its arm as it passed. The blade bit deep, severing something that sparked with dying light. Black fluid—too dark, too thick—sprayed from the wound. The creature shrieked again, wheeling to face him.
"Here's what's going to happen." Kael backed toward the wall, positioning himself. "You're going to stop fighting. I'm going to touch you. And you're going to give me everything you remember about whoever did this to you."
The creature's laugh was a wet, bubbling sound. It didn't speak—couldn't, probably, whatever remained of its humanity drowned beneath Abyssal corruption. But it understood. Something in its posture shifted, recognition flickering through that alien mask.
It charged.
Kael let it come. Counted heartbeats. The creature's claws swept toward his face, and he dropped beneath them, driving forward into its torso. His free hand found the wound he'd created, fingers pressing against flesh that burned cold against his skin.
The Soul Reaver awakened.

The harvest flooded through him like ice water, like fire, like nothing he'd experienced before. The creature went rigid, that terrible animation draining away as Kael pulled at the threads of what remained. Fragments of memory tore free, not images exactly but impressions, sensations, the ghost of a voice speaking words that almost made sense.
A chamber lit by candles that burned black. Figures in robes chanting in patterns that hurt to perceive. The whisper that gave the cult its name, a voice that slid through the cracks in reality and promised things that couldn't be promised.
And beneath that whisper, something else. Something that recognized him. Something that said his name in a language older than the Abyss itself.
Kael wrenched his hand free and stumbled backward as the creature collapsed, whatever animation had held it draining away with the harvested essence. The body crumpled like a puppet with cut strings, falling into a heap of limbs that twitched once and went still.
He stood over it, chest heaving, blood seeping through his torn coat. The wound wasn't deep—claws had scored flesh than opened him fully—but it would need attention. Soon. The spiritual contamination alone would cause problems if left untended.
But his mind was elsewhere, processing the fragments he'd torn free.
The ritual chamber. A memory of stone walls covered in script, symbols that matched nothing in any Order archive he'd encountered. The cultists had been preparing something—or someone—when this creature had been... Created. Changed. Whatever word fit the monstrosity that had emerged from that crate.
And the voice. That whisper that gave them their name. The memory shard contained a fragment of it, and Kael could almost hear it again now, echoing through his skull like distant bells.

Not a threat. Not. Something more personal than that. Something that suggested history Kael had never lived.
His hand went to the pocket where the soul-essence vial rested, cool glass against his hip. The gift from the woman's benefactor. Someone who remembered the old ways. Someone who knew what the Soul Reaver originally meant.
The Soul Reaver.
Kael looked down at the corpse, at the empty shell of what had once been human. The harvest had taken something from it, yes. But it had also given something to him—not just the essence or the fragments, but the certainty that he was on the right path. The conspiracy was real. The cult was organized. The safehouse felt smaller in the pre-dawn gray, its walls pressing inward like the breath before a scream. Kael sat on the edge of a cot, shirt off, examining the wounds in a cracked mirror. Four parallel scores across his ribs, shallow but angry, skin around them already darkening with bruising.
He'd cleaned them with water from a pitcher and applied a poultice that burned worse than the injuries themselves. Spiritual contamination had a way of settling into flesh if left alone, turning minor wounds into gateways for worse things. The poultice wouldn't cure anything, but it would slow the corruption's progress.
The fragments from his harvest pulsed at the edge of his awareness, distinct presences among the chaos of his own thoughts. He could examine them now, use them, risk whatever truths they contained.
Or he could wait. Let them settle. Find someone who might know more about what he'd taken.

*I know what you were meant to become.*
The Soul Reaver hadn't always been his alone. Others had carried it before. And somewhere in Thornwick, the cult that had created his original power wanted him to join them.
A knock at the door. Sharp, deliberate. Not the hesitant tap of someone seeking shelter.

Footsteps. Retreating. The creak of old stairs under weight.

Then they were gone, swallowed by Thornwick's awakening streets.
He had until sunrise. Maybe less. The Order of Dawn's Inquisitors would piece together what they could from the corpse. The Shadow Court would report to their masters. The cult—whoever they really were—would know that someone had harvested their creature and taken its memories.

He dressed quickly, ignoring the pull of wounded flesh. The soul-essence vial went into his coat. The knife went into his boot. The fragments he'd harvested pulsed at the edge of his awareness, holding secrets he would need to unlock soon.
The Conspiracy had layers. He'd seen that much. The Shadow Court wanted to use him. The cult wanted something else. The Mortician's Guild was compromised, or complicit, or both. Kael pulled on his coat, wincing as fabric brushed wounds. The poultice was already cooling, its temporary protection fading. He needed supplies. Information. Somewhere safe to examine the fragments more carefully.
And he needed to find whoever had sent that woman with her warning and her gift. Someone who remembered the old ways. Someone who knew what the Soul Reaver originally meant.
Someone, maybe, who could explain why a cult of Abyssal fanatics seemed to think he belonged to them.
The sun crested Thornwick's skyline, painting iron and brick in shades of orange and gold. Steam vents roared their morning chorus. Somewhere in the city, factory bells began to ring.
Kael opened the door and stepped into the light, knowing that every choice from this moment forward would matter. Knowing, too, that walking away had never really been an option.
The game had begun. And he was done being a pawn.
