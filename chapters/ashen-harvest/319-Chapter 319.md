The rift tore open three hundred yards ahead, a wound in the world vomiting black smoke and the stench of burning copper.
Kael hit the frozen ground, rolled behind a shattered siege tower, and counted the shapes emerging from the portal. Six cultists first, moving in practiced formation. Then four hulking figures wrapped in chains that dragged furrows through the ash. Last came the avatar itself—a thing of angles and absence, seven feet of wrong geometry wearing the semblance of a man.
"Contact front!" someone screamed from the Iron Vanguard lines. The cry dissolved into the crack of spiritual discharge and the wet crack of bones.
Kael remained motionless, watching the avatar through a gap in the scorched timber. The entity's head swiveled, taking in the battlefield with eyes that held no whites, no iris—only depth. It raised one arm, and the chains on the hulking figures went taut. They charged toward the Vanguard's flank, drawn like hounds catching scent.
The Vanguard commander, a thick-necked man with silver pauldrons, redirected two Awakened to intercept. They met the chain-wrapped monstrosities in a collision of spiritual fire and arterial spray. One Awakened went down immediately, her skull crushed by a backhand that bent her helm into her neck. The other carved into his opponent with desperate fury, buying seconds.
The avatar observed, head tilted at an angle no human neck could achieve. It began walking toward the Vanguard commander's position with the unhurried confidence of a predator that knew its prey had nowhere to run.
Kael's fingers found the grip of his blade. Not a ceremonial piece—actual iron, heavy enough to break shields, with a edge that had tasted Awakened blood. He'd taken it from a dead Order member three weeks back during the retreat from Mirehollow. The man had been trying to execute a wounded scout, and Kael had decided the scout's life was worth more than the Order man's. No notification had come for that choice. Only consequences.
The Vanguard commander saw the avatar approaching. His face went the color of old parchment, but he held his ground, raising a shield inscribed with solar sigils. "Archers! Fire at the entity! Concentrate spiritual ammunition!"
A volley of arrows streaked across the ash-choked air. They struck the avatar's chest and dissolved into smoke before they could penetrate. The entity didn't slow. The sigils on the commander's shield began flickering, their light stuttering like a dying heartbeat.
Kael moved.
He circled wide, using the corpse-strewn terrain as cover. Bodies lay everywhere—the dead from this battle and the previous three. Ash fell in silence, blanketing the fallen in gray while the living screamed and bled. The Vanguard had pushed too far into the Ashenmoor Wastes, chasing cultist stragglers into territory where the Veil grew thin. Now they paid for that ambition in blood.
The avatar stopped ten paces from the commander. It spoke, and the words came from everywhere and nowhere, vibrating in Kael's teeth.
"Your order burns low. The Sun's light gutters. Soon the darkness comes for all."
The commander's hand trembled on his shield. "The Six Orders will never—"
"The Six Orders." The avatar's laugh scraped like rusted iron. "Seventy-two seals remain. How many will hold when we finish with you? How many when your champions fall one by one?"
Kael dropped to one knee beside a fallen Vanguard soldier. The man's eyes stared at nothing, his chest torn open by what looked like claws. Fresh—less than an hour dead. Kael pressed his palm flat against the cooling flesh and felt the familiar pull.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 847 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Memory fragment acquired: Tactical Retreat Formation - Iron Vanguard specialized defensive pattern.]
Not combat skills—the soldier had been too low-ranked for anything useful. But the memory fragment might matter. It might not. Kael filed it away and rose, tracking the avatar's position.
The entity had closed the distance. The commander's shield sigils guttered out entirely. The man backpedaled, shouting for reinforcements that couldn't arrive in time. Two cultists flanking the avatar broke away to intercept a trio of Vanguard soldiers trying to reach their commander.
Kael broke into a sprint, drawing on the harvested essence to push his speed beyond normal human limits. The Ashborn blade sang as it cleared the scabbard.
The avatar sensed him at the last moment—or maybe it had known all along. Those depthless eyes fixed on Kael as he closed, and the entity smiled with a mouth that held too many teeth.
"Ah. The Reaver walks among them."
Kael didn't answer. He lunged, driving the blade toward the avatar's chest.
The entity caught the sword between two fingers. The iron screamed, buckled, and shattered. Kael stumbled forward into a palm strike that caught him in the sternum and sent him flying backward. He hit a corpse pile and rolled, tasting copper and ash.
"The one who steals from the dead." The avatar's voice carried something almost like amusement. "The Orders fear you more than they fear us. Do you know why?"
Kael spat blood and pushed himself upright. His ribs screamed protest, but nothing cracked. Sovereign stage resilience—he'd taken worse hits and kept moving. He drew a second blade from his belt, this one a short sword with a serrated edge.
"Because they know death should be final," he rasped.
"Because you remind them what they fight to deny." The avatar resumed its advance toward the commander, dismissing Kael as a concern. "The truth you carry would unmake their faith. Better to hunt you than face reality."
The commander used the distraction to retreat, signaling his remaining soldiers to fall back. A wise choice. A survivable choice. It cost him his position, but positions could be retaken. Dead commanders couldn't issue orders.
Kael circled again, blade ready. The avatar's disregard was useful, but it wouldn't last. Entities learned. Adaptations came fast.
A Vanguard soldier died fifteen feet to Kael's left, bisected by a cultist's curved blade. The body collapsed in a spray of arterial mist, and Kael moved toward it without conscious thought. The cultist saw him coming and raised her weapon—a heavy cleaver etched with corruption sigils.
She swung. Kael ducked, buried the short sword in her thigh, twisted, and wrenched the leg out from under her. She fell hard, and he stomped on her wrist to send the cleaver skittering away. She snarled curses in a language that hurt to hear, thrashing against his weight.
"The avatar—" she spat. "You can't—"
Kael drove the blade through her throat. The words stopped. He wrenched it free, dropped beside the Vanguard soldier's corpse, and harvested while the cultist's body still twitched.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 1,247 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Skill acquired: Corruption Sigil Carving - Intermediate. Memory fragment acquired: Thornwick Cultist Meeting Point - Warehouse District, 14 Bellmore Street.]
Information. The cultists weren't scattered lunatics—they had infrastructure. Meeting points. Communication networks. This aligned with what Kael had suspected since Mirehollow: the attacks across Ashenmoor weren't random violence. Someone coordinated them.
Someone with resources and intelligence.
Kael filed the warehouse address and returned his attention to the battlefield.
The avatar had reached the Vanguard's fallback position. It moved through the defensive line like a fog, and soldiers stopped fighting when it passed. Not dead—Kael could see them breathing, twitching, trying to move. But paralyzed. Contained.
"A gift for my favorite hunter." The avatar's voice carried across the battlefield. "Run back to your masters. Tell them what you saw. Tell them the darkness remembers their names."
The entity turned and walked back toward the rift. The cultists and chain-wrapped horrors fell in behind it, a grotesque procession retreating into the wound in reality. None of the Vanguard soldiers pursued.
Kael watched them go, his grip tight on the serrated blade. He could try to pursue. Harry the avatar. Strike at its back and hope to wound it. Sovereign stage gave him a chance—not a good one, but a chance.
He stayed where he was.
The avatar glanced over its shoulder as it reached the rift's edge. Those empty eyes found Kael across the distance, and the entity nodded once—a gesture of acknowledgment, maybe even respect—before stepping through the portal. The rift collapsed behind it with a sound like reality exhaling.
Silence fell over the battlefield. The ash continued to drift down, covering the dead in gray.
Kael lowered his blade. His chest ached where the avatar had struck him, and his hands trembled with spent adrenaline. He catalogued his injuries: cracked ribs, probable bruising beneath the bruising, a splitting headache that suggested minor spiritual contamination. Survivable. He'd had worse.
The Vanguard commander limped toward him, supported by two soldiers. Blood soaked the man's side— he'd been wounded during the retreat. His face held the hollow look of someone who'd seen their beliefs crumble.
"You fought it," the commander said. Not a question.
"Tried to." Kael wiped his blade on his coat. "Didn't accomplish much."
"You bought time. Enough for us to fall back." The commander studied Kael with something between gratitude and revulsion. "The reports said you were in Ashenmoor. I thought they meant you were fighting the cultists. Fighting alongside us."
"I fight for myself."
"Then why intervene at all?"
Kael sheathed the blade. "Because watching that thing walk through your lines unopposed would have been bad for everyone. Including me."
The commander absorbed this. His soldiers shifted uneasily, hands near their weapons, but they didn't draw. Killing a Sovereign-stage Awakened required numbers they didn't have, and everyone on this battlefield had seen what Kael could do when pressed.
"The Six Orders need to know about this," the commander said finally. "An avatar bold enough to walk through a military formation. The seals must be weakening faster than—"
"Seventy-two seals." Kael cut him off. "The entity mentioned seventy-two. That's all of them. Every rift between here and the Abyss."
Understanding dawned slowly on the commander's face, then horror. "They're attacking all of them? Simultaneously?"
"The coordinated strikes across Ashenmoor. The cult activity in Thornwick. It's not random persecution—it's preparation." Kael gestured at the battlefield, at the dead and dying scattered across ash-gray ground. "They're testing responses. Identifying weak points. Planning something larger."
The commander's face went gray. "The Council of Six needs to—"
"Will the Council act in time?" Kael's tone carried no faith. "You've seen how the Orders squabble. How long since they mediated a real dispute? How long since they acknowledged a threat that required cooperation?"
"We have the Concordat—"
"The Concordat covers inter-Order conflict. It doesn't cover coordinated Abyssal assault." Kael turned away, scanning the battlefield for harvestable corpses. The Vanguard soldiers gave him space, watching him with the wariness of men who'd heard stories about Soul Reavers. "The entity was right about one thing. The Orders are fighting the wrong enemy. Spending more effort hunting me than the cultists tearing holes in reality."
"That's not—" the commander started.
"Prove me wrong." Kael walked toward the nearest corpse. "The Thornwick warehouse district, fourteen Bellmore Street. Cultist meeting point. You could raid it, capture intelligence, maybe find whoever's coordinating the attacks."
He didn't wait for a response. The body at his feet belonged to a cultist—the same woman he'd killed minutes ago, though her corpse had already begun to cool. Kael crouched, pressed his palm to her forehead, and harvested.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 412 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Memory fragment acquired: Ritual Preparation Sequence - Location: Ashenmoor North Ridge. Duration: Three days. Objective: ???]
Not much. Fragments of devotion to something Kael couldn't see. But the reference to a north ridge location matched terrain features he'd observed during the approach to this battlefield. The cultists had been building something there. Ritual platforms, maybe. Nexus points for the coordinated assault.
He processed the information and moved to the next corpse.
The Vanguard soldiers watched him work. Some muttered prayers or curses. None intervened. The commander had given no order to stop him, and no one wanted to be the first to try.
Kael harvested systematically, working through the battlefield in efficient sweeps. Cultists and Vanguard soldiers alike—dead was dead, and death left behind energy that would dissipate within twenty-four hours if not harvested. The Orders called it desecration. Kael called it practical resource management.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 634 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Skill acquired: Iron Vanguard Shield Wall - Basic. Memory fragment acquired: Commander Aldric Thorne - Tactical Preferences.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 298 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Memory fragment acquired: Ashenmoor Topography - Sector Seven. Elevation changes, defensible positions.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 1,089 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Skill acquired: Corruption Resistance - Tolerance. Memory fragment acquired: Cult Hierarchies - Names and Roles.]
The last one mattered. Kael paused over the body of a cultist whose sigil-covered robes suggested higher rank. The memory fragments painted a picture of organization: cells, reporting structures, compartmentalized communication. The Thornwick network answered to someone called the Pale Architect. The northern ridge operation fell under a different authority—the Broken Covenant, whoever that was.
Two distinct command structures. Possibly more.
The cult wasn't a single movement. It was multiple factions pursuing related goals.
Kael straightened, processing implications. Coordination between factions meant shared intelligence, pooled resources. It also meant potential fractures—different groups with different priorities might not support each other when pressure came.
He noted the possibilities and filed them away.
"Reaver."
The commander's voice. Kael turned to find the man approaching alone, his soldiers holding position at a cautious distance. Blood still soaked the commander's side, though someone had applied a pressure bandage. His face had settled into grim determination.
"You mentioned a warehouse address. Fourteen Bellmore Street." The commander kept his voice low. "Why share that?"
"Because watching cultists murder civilians annoys me. Because coordinated attacks could destabilize the entire region. Because—" Kael paused, considering how much truth to offer. "Because I need the Orders focused on the real threat. Wasting resources hunting me while rifts open across Ashenmoor benefits no one."
"And when the threat is contained? When there's no more use for cooperation?"
"Then we go back to being enemies." Kael met the commander's gaze without flinching. "I know how this works. I'm not naive."
The commander studied him for a long moment. "The Shadow Court has been asking about you. In Thornwick. That's what my intelligence indicates."
Kael's expression didn't change. "Shadow Court monitors everyone. It's what they do."
"They've flagged you as a priority target. Interest level: significant." The commander's tone carried warning. "That means resources. Attention. Someone powerful wants information about the Soul Reaver who walks Ashenmoor."
"I've attracted attention since Thornwick. Not news."
"It's news when the attention comes with assassination authorization." The commander reached into his coat and withdrew a folded paper. "My scouts intercepted this courier three days ago. Addressed to someone called the Pale Architect. Instructions regarding you."
He held out the paper. Kael took it, unfolded it, read.
*Priority acquisition. Alive if possible. Dead if necessary. The Soul Reaver's cooperation cannot be assumed. Prepare for resistance. The asset has demonstrated capability beyond initial assessment. Recommend tier-three containment protocols for engagement.*
Kael refolded the paper and slipped it into his coat. "When did your scouts intercept this?"
"Three days before the current engagement. I would have warned you sooner, but—" the commander gestured at the battlefield, the dead, the ash still settling. "Circumstances intervened."
"Useful information. Thank you."
"Don't thank me yet." The commander's jaw tightened. "I have my own reasons for wanting the cult disrupted. My daughter serves in the Arcane Spire's Thornwick chapter. If the coordinated attacks succeed, if the seals fail—" He stopped, composing himself. "The enemy of my enemy."
"Doesn't make us friends."
"No. But it makes us temporarily aligned." The commander stepped back, swaying slightly. His wound was worse than he'd allowed. "Fourteen Bellmore Street. I'll have a team investigate within the week."
"I'll be interested to see what they find."
"You're not planning to wait that long."
Kael's silence served as confirmation.
The commander nodded, as if he'd expected nothing else. "Then you'll be facing whatever's there without support. The Vanguard can't spare resources for a warehouse raid while the northern ridge operation remains active."
"I work alone."
"So I've heard." The commander turned to leave, then paused. "The entity called you its favorite hunter. That seems like something you should know."
"I've been called worse."
The commander limped away, rejoining his soldiers. They began the grim work of gathering wounded, counting dead, establishing a perimeter against further cultist incursion.
Kael stood alone in the ash, reviewing what he'd learned.
The Six Orders existed and fought the Abyss. This battlefield proved it—the Vanguard had engaged cultists and an avatar without hesitation, despite the cost. The coordinated attacks suggested organization, but organization could be disrupted.
The 72 sealed rifts. The avatar's mention confirmed what Kael had suspected: all the seals faced simultaneous pressure. Not random degradation. Deliberate targeting.
Cult activity in Thornwick was organized. The warehouse address, the hierarchy names, the compartmentalized cells—all evidence of structured planning, not scattered fanaticism.
The Shadow Court monitored him. The intercepted message proved it. Someone called the Pale Architect wanted him captured or killed, and that someone had resources to deploy tier-three containment protocols.
Kael checked his harvested essence reserves. The battlefield had provided well—over four thousand units accumulated, plus skill fragments and memory data. Enough to fuel advanced techniques if needed. Enough to pursue the intelligence leads he'd gathered.
The north ridge operation. Three days until whatever they planned executed. The warehouse in Thornwick. Evidence of the Pale Architect's network. Two targets, limited time.
He could pursue both. Difficult but possible if he moved immediately.
Kael sheathed his blade and turned toward the western ridge, where a narrow path descended toward Ashenmoor's interior. Thornwick lay four days' hard travel to the south. The north ridge, closer. Two days if he pushed through the ash-fields without rest.
He started walking.
Behind him, the Vanguard soldiers continued their work. The dead would be buried or burned—the Mortician's Guild had standards, even in wartime. The wounded would receive treatment if enough essence remained in the medical supplies. The commander would file reports that would eventually reach the Council of Six, where they would be debated, dissected, and filed away for future consideration.
Kael didn't look back. The battlefield's dead would keep for twenty-four hours, and he had harvested what mattered. The living could manage their politics and their grief. He had his own priorities.
The ash fell thicker as he descended into the wasteland's interior, swallowing the sounds of the retreating army until only his footsteps remained.
* * *
The camp sat in a shallow valley, hidden by ridge lines on three sides and a ring of watch-fires on the fourth.
Kael reached it after nightfall, moving through terrain he'd memorized from harvested memory fragments. Three tents, canvas stretched over wooden frames. A cook-fire burning low. Two figures hunched over maps spread across a makeshift table.
He approached openly, letting them hear his footsteps.
The first figure to rise held a crossbow leveled at his chest. The bolt gleamed with spiritual discharge—a potent round designed to punch through Sovereign-stage resistance. The wielder's face was weathered, scarred, professional. An assassin, not a soldier.
"Wrong camp," she said. "Move along."
"I'm looking for the Pale Architect's network." Kael stopped ten feet away, hands visible. "I have information they want."
The crossbow didn't waver. "No one knows that name."
"Fourteen Bellmore Street. Tier-three containment protocols. Priority acquisition of a Soul Reaver." Kael watched her reaction. "The message your courier carried. The one the Vanguard intercepted."
The second figure looked up from the maps. Younger, sharper-featured, with the pale coloring common to those who'd spent too long away from sunlight. His expression shifted from irritation to calculation.
"You killed the courier."
"I harvested him." Kael let the correction carry its weight. "He wasn't carrying anything else useful. His memories suggested this camp existed. Here I am."
The crossbow remained steady. The second figure rose slowly, studying Kael with the focus of a collector examining a rare specimen.
"The Soul Reaver. We heard you'd entered the wastes." He gestured, and the crossbow lowered—not put away, but no longer aimed at vital organs. "Most would have stayed clear of cult activity after what happened at Mirehollow."
"Most didn't watch an avatar walk through a military formation unopposed."
"You witnessed the engagement?" Interest sharpened in the younger man's voice. "The Iron Vanguard forces near the southern approach?"
"I killed one of your people during that engagement. Harvested her corpse. Got fragments about north ridge operations and hierarchy names." Kael's gaze moved between the two cultists. "I know about the Broken Covenant. I know about compartmentalized cells. I know the Pale Architect wants me acquired."
Silence stretched. The cook-fire crackled. Somewhere beyond the watch-fires, something howled—a predator in the ash-lands, hunting by the light of a moon that never fully emerged from cloud cover.
The younger cultist smiled. It wasn't a pleasant expression.
"You're here to make a deal."
"I'm here to understand the game board." Kael planted his feet, settling into a stance that allowed quick movement in any direction. "The Six Orders are fighting the wrong battle. Wasting resources on internal conflicts while you tear holes in reality. That's inefficient. I'd prefer efficiency."
"Efficiency." The older cultist's voice carried skepticism. "The Reaver lectures us about efficiency."
"The Reaver has survived this long by being useful to people who matter." Kael met her gaze directly. "Right now, your operations face interference. The north ridge ritual requires resources you haven't secured. Thornwick's network is compromised—the Vanguard has your warehouse address."
The younger cultist's expression flickered. "Our Thornwick operation—"
"Has been identified. The commander I spoke with plans to raid within the week." Kael watched both cultists process this. "I'm offering to delay that raid. Buy you time to relocate assets and personnel."
"For what price?"
"Information. The Pale Architect's identity. The scope of the north ridge operation. The endgame behind the coordinated attacks." Kael spread his hands, palms up. "I'm not asking for alliance. I'm asking for transparency. Tell me what you're building, and I'll help you build it."
The older cultist laughed—a harsh, bitter sound. "The Reaver offers cooperation. The creature who steals souls wants to join our cause."
"I don't want to join anything." Kael's tone hardened. "I want to survive. The Orders will hunt me regardless of what I do. The cult wants to acquire me for reasons I don't yet understand. Between those two options, I'd deal with people who'll eventually need my abilities."
The younger cultist stepped closer, circling to Kael's flank. The older one adjusted her aim, maintaining coverage. They worked well together—coordinated, practiced.
"You're testing us," he said. "Seeing if we'll reveal information that could be used against us."
"I'm testing whether you're smart enough to recognize mutual benefit." Kael didn't turn to track the younger cultist's movement. "The alternative is continued antagonism. You send people to capture or kill me. I harvest them and use what I learn against you. Eventually, one of us destroys the other, and the Orders benefit from our weakness."
"You assume we can't kill you now."
"You could try." Kael's hand drifted toward his blade. "Two against one, here, in your camp. But what about the next time? And the time after that? I'm difficult to kill. You're difficult to replace."
The younger cultist stopped circling. His expression settled into something resembling respect.
"The Pale Architect isn't one person," he said. "It's a title. The network's public face—designed to be identified, tracked, opposed. A distraction."
"Then who coordinates your operations?"
"Three voices. The Broken Covenant leads the northern offensive. The Threshold Assembly manages the southern disruption. The Veil Collective handles Thornwick and the western territories." The younger cultist glanced at his companion, who nodded slightly. "Information has a price."
"Name it."
"Your next harvest. Full extraction from a target of our choosing. We provide the corpse, you provide the results."
Kael considered the terms. A single harvest meant minimal risk—he'd done worse for less. But it established precedent. Created obligation. The cult would expect continued cooperation.
"Your people tried to acquire me with tier-three containment protocols."
"Someone authorized that message. Not us." The younger cultist's tone carried genuine annoyance. "The Shadow Court operates independently. They have their own agenda, their own methods. The three voices didn't sanction priority acquisition of a Soul Reaver."
"Then who did?"
"Someone who believes controlling you benefits their position within the broader network. We don't know who." He spread his hands. "The cult isn't unified. Factions pursue different goals. The Broken Covenant wants rifts opened. The Threshold Assembly wants the Orders weakened. The Veil Collective wants—" He paused. "We don't know what the Veil Collective wants. They're the most secretive. But they watch everything."
The Shadow Court. Kael filed the connection. The intercepted message, the independent authorization, the faction without clear purpose—something didn't align. The Shadow Court had always operated as a deniable operations arm for the Orders. Now they seemed embedded within the cult structure itself.
Or the cult had infiltrated the Shadow Court. Or both organizations had been compromised by a third party.
Complexity layered on complexity. Kael filed the puzzle away for future examination.
"The north ridge operation. What's the objective?"
The younger cultist hesitated, then answered. "Seal degradation. The north ridge sits above a minor rift—one of the seventy-two. We're not trying to open it fully. Just weaken the containment enough to let an avatar manifest for an extended period. Long enough to gather intelligence on Order response patterns."
"You sacrificed an avatar for reconnaissance?"
"We sacrificed a projection. Avatars can be reconstituted. The information gained about Order coordination—and the Reaver's involvement—far exceeds the cost."
Kael absorbed this. The cult had willingly exposed an avatar to learn how the Orders would respond. And how he'd responded. They'd watched him engage the entity, noted his intervention, and drawn conclusions about his capabilities and priorities.
"The raid on the warehouse. How much time do I buy you?"
"If the commander acts within a week, we need forty-eight hours to relocate critical assets. Personnel, documents, essence reserves. The rest can be sacrificed."
"Twenty-four hours. That's what I can guarantee."
The younger cultist's eyes narrowed. "You're overpromising."
"I'm not. The commander wants cooperation. He's willing to move fast if I provide intelligence to justify immediate action. I control what he learns." Kael let the implication hang. "Twenty-four hours. Take it or find your own solution."
The older cultist shifted her crossbow, finger tightening on the trigger. "You assume we need your help."
"I assume you'd prefer not to lose Thornwick entirely. The Veil Collective's western operations hinge on that network. Lose it, and you lose eyes and ears across three territories." Kael held his ground, refusing to flinch from the crossbow. "I can prevent that loss. The price is reasonable."
Silence stretched. The cook-fire burned lower, shadows lengthening across the valley floor.
"Twenty-four hours," the younger cultist said finally. "And you provide the warehouse intelligence personally. No intermediaries. No delays."
"Agreed. And you provide the corpse for my next harvest."
"Tomorrow night. We'll have a target prepared." The younger cultist extended his hand. "Do we have an accord?"
Kael looked at the offered hand. Cultists operated outside normal moral frameworks. Allying with them—temporarily, conditionally—violated everything the Orders taught about the proper response to Abyssal corruption.
The Orders had also abandoned him to die in Thornwick. Had hunted him across Ashenmoor. Had prioritized political convenience over actual threat response.
He took the hand.
"Accord."
The grip was firm, brief, professional. When they released, both cultists stepped back, some of the tension leaving their postures. The older woman finally lowered her crossbow entirely.
"The corpse will be waiting at the old watchtower, three miles east of the ridge where we engaged the Vanguard. Midnight tomorrow." The younger cultist gathered his maps, rolling them into a cylinder. "The target is a deserter from the Threshold Assembly. He knows things he shouldn't. Things that could compromise operations in the southern territories."
"Why not harvest him yourselves?"
"Threshold Assembly members resist standard extraction techniques. Spiritual contamination, trained mental defenses." The younger cultist's smile returned, sharper this time. "But you're a Soul Reaver. His defenses won't matter."
Kael filed this information. The cult had desecrators with spiritual resistance. Useful to know. Also useful to know that the cult wanted to test his abilities by putting him against protected targets.
Another layer of complexity. Another potential betrayal waiting to happen.
He turned toward the valley's eastern edge, where the ridge lines rose against the star-scattered sky. "I'll be at the watchtower by midnight. Your target better be fresh."
"We understand the principles of your... Occupation." The younger cultist's tone carried amusement. "Freshness matters."
Kael walked away without looking back. He heard the cultists resume their conversation once he'd moved beyond easy earshot—low voices, rapid exchanges, the sounds of people adjusting plans based on new information.
The watch-fires burned at his back as he climbed the ridge. The ash fell thicker with each passing hour, blanketing the wasteland in gray silence. Somewhere to the north, the rift operation continued. Somewhere to the south, the Vanguard prepared reports that would be read, debated, and largely ignored.
And somewhere in the darkness, Kael felt eyes watching him. Not the cultists—he'd left them behind. Something else. Something that had observed the entire exchange from a distance.
The Shadow Court, maybe. Or another faction he hadn't yet identified.
He kept walking.
The eyes remained.
Kael's hand found his blade, fingers resting on the worn leather grip. He didn't draw. Drawing invited escalation, and he wasn't ready for that confrontation. Not yet.
But he noted the sensation. Filed it away with all the other fragments of information he'd gathered today.
The game board grew more complex with every move. The cult wanted his abilities. The Shadow Court wanted him controlled or eliminated. The Orders wanted him dead or imprisoned. And Kael wanted only to survive long enough to find answers about what he'd become.
His feet carried him east, toward the watchtower, toward the promised harvest, toward the next piece of a puzzle that grew more dangerous with each revealed fragment.
Behind him, the eyes in the darkness continued to watch.
And somewhere in the depths of his soul, the Soul Reaver stirred—hungry, patient, waiting for the next corpse to feed its endless hunger.
