"The thing moved wrong. No—things never moved right when they were dead, but this one twisted like smoke through a keyhole."
Kael pressed his back against the crumbling brick of the alley wall. Rain hammered the cobblestones, filling the narrow passage with white noise that couldn't drown out the wet shuffling ahead. He had come to Fogmire District looking for easy pickings—gang members killed in last night's territory dispute, bodies left to rot while the Thornwick Constabulary pretended not to notice. What he'd found instead was worse.
A figure stood at the alley's end, illuminated by a sputtering gaslight that shouldn't have been burning. The fixture hung from a corroded bracket, its flame an unnatural blue-green that cast no warmth. The figure beneath it wore the tattered remnants of a constable's uniform, but the badge had been torn away, leaving only rust stains on the chest. Its head lolled at an angle that made Kael's stomach clench.
"Come on, then," he muttered, more to himself than the corpse. "Let's see what you've got left to give."
He took a step forward, and the constable's head snapped toward him. Not turned—snapped, like a puppet on strings. Hollow eye sockets gleaming with that same sickly light reflected his own pale face back at him.
The thing that had been a constable opened its mouth, and a voice like grinding glass scraped through the rain.
"Reaver."
Kael's blood froze. He had been called that word before—whispered in the underground, screamed by victims, spat by Order Inquisitors who caught his scent. But this corpse-thing spoke it like a title. Like a name.
"You are marked." The constable's body straightened, joints cracking. "The Court watches. The Court waits. But you are not ready."
"Lyra?" Kael called out. She had been right behind him. Now the alley behind him held only silence and the drumming rain. "Lyra, we have a problem."
The constable took a step forward. Then another. Its movements were jerky, mechanical, each stride accompanied by the squelch of wet flesh against stone. Gaslight Wraith, Kael's mind supplied—the street gangs called them the Bluejackals, spirits bound to corrupted streetlights, but this was something older. Something that remembered being human.
"Run, little Reaver." The voice came from everywhere now, echoing off the walls. "Run far. Run fast. The Court sends its regards, and the Wraith hungers."
Kael drew his blade. A simple weapon—a length of reinforced steel with a notched edge that had seen better days. It wouldn't do much against a spirit, but it was better than nothing. Better than standing empty-handed while death walked toward him.
"I'm not running," he said. "Not until I get answers."
The Gaslight Wraith laughed—a sound like shattering porcelain. "Answers? The dead do not give answers. We give only what remains. And what remains of you, Reaver, will be delicious."
It lunged.
Kael threw himself sideways as spectral fingers—translucent, crackling with pale fire—swiped through the space where his throat had been. The gaslight flared, blinding him for a heartbeat, and in that heartbeat the Wraith moved again.
A blow caught his shoulder, spinning him into the wall. Pain bloomed, sharp and electric. He tasted copper. His blade clattered away into the darkness, spinning end over end before clanging against something metal.
"You fight like a mortal," the Wraith observed, circling. Its form flickered between solid and smoke, making it impossible to track. "Maybe you are still mortal. Maybe the Reaver blood hasn't fully awakened."
"Talk," Kael gasped, pushing himself upright. His shoulder screamed in protest. "You know something. You mentioned a Court—"
"The Shadow Court knows many things." The Wraith's head tilted, considering him like a bird examining a worm. "They have watched you since the gaslight districts. They have questions about what you carry. About what sleeps inside your chest."
Kael's hand went to his sternum, where the Soul Reaver's mark had burned itself into his bones. A gift, or a curse—the power to harvest skills, memories, and fragments of fate from the dying. The Orders called it an abomination. The cultists called it a treasure. Kael called it survival.
"If they have questions," he said carefully, "why send a monster instead of a messenger?"
The Wraith's form solidified, and for a moment Kael saw the face beneath the decay—a young woman, pretty once, her features twisted by whatever ritual had bound her soul to this cursed light. Her eyes held a flicker of something almost human. Almost pleading.
"Because messengers can be intercepted." Her voice changed, becoming the grinding glass again. "Because the Court wishes to see what you do under pressure. Because—"
The alley behind Kael exploded with light.
Not gaslight—true light, brilliant and searing, accompanied by a voice that rang like struck iron. "Abyssal remnant! Release your hold!"
Lyra stepped into the street, her staff blazing with contained lightning. She looked like war incarnate—dark hair plastered to her skull by the rain, eyes blazing with power that made the air itself tremble. Behind her came a second figure, someone Kael didn't recognize: a man in gray robes, his hands weaving patterns that left trails of silver light in the air.
The Gaslight Wraith shrieked and recoiled, its form scattering like ash in a gale. But it didn't flee. Instead, it drew together again, condensing into something denser, more solid, more dangerous.
"Order dogs." The Wraith's voice dripped venom. "The Court warned me you would come. But they did not warn me—" Its hollow gaze fixed on Kael. "—that they would send only two."
"What are you talking about?" Lyra demanded, advancing with her staff raised. "Kael, get behind me. This thing is beyond your—"
"I'm not hiding behind you."
He reached for his belt, where the small vials glinted in the lightning-flash. Not soul-essence—he wasn't that stupid, not in front of an Order operative—but flashpowder, a crude alchemical that produced a blinding burst when shattered. He had bought it from a Veiled Market dealer two weeks ago, telling himself it was for emergencies.
This qualified.
Kael hurled the vial at the Wraith's feet. The world went white.
When his vision cleared, the gray-robed man was on his knees, blood streaming from his nose, his silver-light patterns shattered. Lyra stood between the Wraith and Kael, her staff raised, but her arms were trembling. The spirit had gotten close—too close—and whatever it had done to her, it had cost her.
"The corruption spreads." The gray-robed man's voice was weak, but his eyes were sharp. "I've never seen a Wraith channel that much residual energy. Someone is feeding it."
"Someone is always feeding something," Lyra snarled. "Help me seal it. Now."
The man's hands moved, drawing patterns in the air that Kael couldn't follow. Lyra's staff blazed again, but dimmer this time, flickering like a candle in a storm. The Wraith laughed, its form expanding, filling the narrow alley with smoke and fire and the smell of burning meat.
"You cannot banish what has already been given freely!" It roared. "I am claimed! I am chosen! I am—"
The gray-robed man spoke a word that hurt to hear. The air tore, and something grabbed the Wraith from the other side of reality—a hook of pure light that dragged it screaming into a gap that hadn't existed a moment before. The gap sealed. The rain fell. The silence was deafening.
Lyra dropped her staff and spun to face Kael. Her eyes were wide, pupils dilated, and her skin had taken on a grayish tinge. "You—" She staggered. "What did you—that vial—"
"Flashpowder." Kael caught her as she fell. She weighed almost nothing, her body rigid in his arms. "It's just flashpowder. Lyra, stay with me."
"The ritual." The gray-robed man was crawling toward them, leaving a trail of blood on the wet stones. "It requires contact. Physical contact with the Wraith. Kael, you have to—"
"Have to what?"
But Kael already understood. The Soul Reaver mark burned hot against his chest, and he looked at the space where the Wraith had been torn away. A scorch mark remained on the cobblestones, and within it, a hand.
The Wraith's hand. Pale, translucent, still crackling with dying light.
The mark pulsed again, hungry.
"Kael." Lyra's voice was barely a whisper. "Whatever you're thinking—don't."
He knelt beside the hand. Touched it.
The world inverted.

He stood in a chamber of black glass, surrounded by mirrors that reflected nothing. Before him hung the Gaslight Wraith—not the decaying constable, but the woman she had been. Young. Beautiful. Terrified.
"You came." Her voice echoed strangely. "No one ever comes."
"Where am I?"
"Inside. Between. The place where the dead speak, if anyone bothers to listen." She turned to face him, and her eyes held centuries of grief. "You carry something old, Reaver. Something that remembers me."
"I don't understand."
"You will." She reached out, and her fingers passed through his chest, finding the mark. "It remembers all of us. Every soul ever reaved, every fragment ever taken. We are written on your bones. We are inked in your blood."
The chamber trembled. Cracks spread across the black glass mirrors, and through them Kael glimpsed images—flashes, fragments, a tapestry of moments he had never lived. A battlefield soaked in moonlight. A child crying in an endless night. A voice screaming a name he almost recognized.
"The Court sends these things deliberately," the woman said. "They test you. They measure your power against their needs. And when the time comes—when the seals crack and the Entities stir—they will come for you themselves."
"Who is the Court?"
"Shadows wearing faces. Foes pretending to be friends. You have already met one of them, Reaver. You have broken bread with another." Her form began to fade. "Find the tablets. The first Reaver left a map. The truth lies beneath the—"
The chamber exploded.

The man's face went pale. "Who told you about the first?"
"No one. Something else did."
Lyra made a sound—half laugh, half sob. "Of course. Of course this is how it happens. My first real mission in Thornwick, and my partner summons a soul-eating monster and then has a spiritual conversation with its corpse." She looked up at Kael, and her expression was unreadable. "I should report you. I should let the Order take you."
"But you won't."
"No." She shook her head. "Because the Order isn't what it claims to be, and you—" She paused. "You're something else. Something I haven't seen before."

"I am Brother Aldric of the Order of the Gilded Dawn. I came to Thornwick seeking corruption, and I found—" He looked at Kael. "I found a young man who harvests the dead without corruption. Who stands against the Abyss without Orders. Who carries a mark that should not exist."
"I didn't choose it."

Kael took the offered hand and let himself be pulled to his feet. His shoulder throbbed, his head ached, and his palm still burned with stolen power. Behind them, the gaslight flickered and died, plunging the alley into darkness.
"Where?" he asked.
"There is a place. Below the city, where the old seals still hold. It is there that the truth about the Court—and about you—will be revealed."
"And if I say no?"
Aldric smiled, but it didn't reach his eyes. "Then you will continue alone, hunted by the Orders, targeted by the Court, your power growing without direction until something consumes you. The choice is yours, Reaver. It always has been."
Kael looked at Lyra. She nodded once—a small, tight gesture that said more than words. Then he looked at the scorched cobblestones, where the Wraith's ashes were already being washed away by the rain.
"Lead the way," he said.

The passage descended through layers of Thornwick's foundations—first the old sewer systems, then the ruins of a pre-Concordat civilization that had been buried and forgotten. Aldric navigated by touch and memory, his fingers tracing symbols carved into the stone that Kael couldn't see. Lyra brought up the rear, her staff casting a dim light that barely held back the pressing dark.
"How far down does this go?" Kael asked.
"To the roots." Aldric's voice echoed strangely in the confined space. "Thornwick was built on a nexus. Six ley lines converge beneath the city, which is why the Orders established their presence here. It is also why the Court chose it."
"The Court again. Who are they?"
"Agents of the Abyss," Lyra cut in. "Cultists. Fanatics who want to break the seals and bring the Entities into the Material."
"Partially correct." Aldric stopped at a junction where three tunnels met. "But the Court is more than that. They are former members of the Orders who became disillusioned. Exiles. Heretics who discovered that the truth about the Entities—and about the seals—was deliberately hidden from the Awakened masses."
"What truth?"
Aldric turned to face him. In the dim light, his features looked ancient, weathered by more years than a single lifetime could hold. "That the seals are not permanent. That the 72 Entities are not imprisoned—they are waiting. That the Six Orders fight an eternal war not to protect humanity, but to maintain a balance that serves their own purposes."
"Serving their purposes how?"
"By keeping the Entities weak enough to control. By harvesting spiritual essence from the dying to fuel their own advancement. By telling us that death is final, when in truth—" He paused. "In truth, the dead speak, if we know how to listen."
Kael's hand went to his chest, where the mark pulsed.
"The Soul Reaver," he said slowly. "It lets me hear them. The dead."
"Yes." Aldric's eyes gleamed. "You are not the first. There have been others throughout history—soul-eaters, deathwalkers, by whatever name they were called. Most were hunted down by the Orders and executed. A few survived long enough to leave records. The tablets I mentioned—those records are real. They are hidden beneath Thornwick, in a place where no Order has set foot for three hundred years."
"And you want me to find them."
"I want you to understand what you carry." Aldric raised his hand, and a soft glow emanated from his palm—not aggressive, but warm, welcoming. "The Soul Reaver is not a curse, Kael. It is a gift. A key. The Entities fear it because it can unmake them. The Orders fear it because it can reveal their lies. And the Court fears it because—" He hesitated. "Because there is a prophecy."

"That a Reaver will rise when the seals break. That this Reaver will choose—truly choose—whether to save the world or consume it. That the Entities, the Orders, and the Court all seek to control that choice."
The tunnel ahead was silent. Rain dripped somewhere in the distance, a steady metronome counting down to something Kael couldn't see.
"What happens if I refuse?" he asked. "What happens if I just—walk away?"
"Then the choice will be made for you." Aldric's voice was gentle, almost kind. "The Court will not accept refusal. They will send more Wraiths, more cultists, more agents disguised as friends. They will use everyone you care about as leverage. And eventually, they will break you—or you will break them."
Lyra stepped forward, putting herself between Kael and the monk. "You're manipulating him. Both of you."

Kael looked at the tunnel ahead. At the darkness that seemed to breathe, waiting. At the man who claimed to have answers and the woman who had risked everything to stand beside him.
"Teach me," he said.

The chamber they entered was vast—larger than any underground space had a right to be. Pillars of black stone rose toward a ceiling lost in shadow, and at the chamber's center stood an altar of white marble, untouched by the dust of centuries. Upon it rested a single object: a clay tablet, no larger than a man's palm, covered in script that shifted and changed as Kael watched.
"The First Tablet," Aldric whispered. "Written by the original Soul Reaver. It contains everything he learned about his power—and about the Entities that created it."
Kael approached the altar. The script resolved into words he could almost understand, letters that seemed to crawl across the surface like insects. His mark burned hot, resonating with something ancient and powerful.
"Be careful," Lyra warned. "The last man who touched this tablet lost his mind."
"What did he see?"
"Everything." Aldric's voice was soft. "The truth about the seals. The nature of the Entities. The reason the Orders suppress knowledge of the Soul Reaver. He saw it all, and it drove him mad."
"And you think I'm strong enough?"
"I think you have no choice." Aldric stepped back, giving Kael space. "The mark has already accepted you. The tablet will show you what that means."
Kael reached out. His fingers hovered over the clay surface, trembling.
"What if I don't like what I see?"
The chamber lights flickered. Somewhere above them, distantly, something screamed—human or inhuman, he couldn't tell.
"Then you will have to decide what to do about it."
Kael touched the tablet.
The world exploded into memory.
He saw the Abyss—not as a place of darkness, but as a place of light, blinding and absolute. He saw Entities beyond counting, vast intelligences that made the sun itself seem small. He saw the war that had birthed the seals, the betrayal that had split the Orders, the secret history that had been buried for a thousand years.
He saw the first Soul Reaver, a man who looked like him, who carried the same mark, who had faced the same choice.
And he saw the words carved into reality itself, glowing with terrible truth:
**THE SEALS WILL BREAK. THE ENTITIES WILL RISE. AND THE REAVER WILL DECIDE WHAT COMES AFTER.**
Kael wrenched his hand back, gasping. The chamber swam around him, the pillars tilting like ships in a storm.
"Breath," Lyra commanded, grabbing his shoulders. "Look at me. Breathe."
He breathed. The world steadied.
"What did you see?" Aldric asked.
Kael opened his mouth to answer—and froze.
There was a sound at the chamber's entrance. Footsteps. Multiple sets, approaching quietly but purposefully.
"Someone else is here," Lyra hissed, raising her staff. "They must have followed us."
Aldric's face went pale. "That's impossible. No one knows about this place."
"Apparently someone does."
The footsteps stopped. A figure stepped into the light—a man in black armor, his face hidden behind a mask of polished bone. Behind him stood a dozen others, similarly armed, their weapons drawn.
"Brother Aldric." The masked figure's voice was flat, emotionless. "The Court thanks you for leading us to the Reaver. Your service is no longer required."
The man in gray robes—Brother Aldric—raised his hands slowly. "Lord Whisper. I can explain—"
"You can die." The masked figure raised his hand. Light gathered at his fingertips, hot and hungry. "All of you can die."
Kael grabbed Lyra's arm and pulled her toward a gap between two pillars. "Run. Now."
They ran.
Behind them, Aldric screamed.
Kael didn't look back.
The footsteps came closer. Then closer still. The masked figure's voice echoed through the chamber, promising death, promising answers, promising pain.
Kael ran faster.
The sound of pursuit grew louder. Kael's lungs burned, his legs ached, but he forced them forward. Lyra kept pace beside him, her face set in grim determination.
"There!" She pointed to a narrow passage between two pillars. "That way!"
They dove into the passage as a bolt of energy exploded against the stone where they'd stood. The passage twisted and turned, dropping sharply, and soon they were scrambling down a slope that felt like descending into the earth's own throat.
Light faded. Sound faded. The only thing that remained was the pounding of Kael's heart and the mark burning cold against his chest.
They burst into a cavern filled with water—black water, still and deep, stretching into darkness that their weak light couldn't penetrate. Behind them, the passage continued, but they could hear the sounds of pursuit growing closer.
"The water," Lyra gasped. "It might lead somewhere. Or it might lead nowhere."

"Together?" he asked.
She took his hand. Her grip was cold, firm, unyielding.
"Together."
They plunged into the water.
Above them, the masked figure known as Lord Whisper arrived at the cavern's edge. He stood motionless, watching the ripples spread and fade, listening to the silence that followed.
"Find them," he said. "The Reaver will not escape."
In the darkness beneath the water, Kael kicked toward an exit he couldn't see, dragging Lyra with him, the mark on his chest pulsing with stolen power and borrowed time.
Somewhere ahead, the water might lead to freedom.
Or it might lead to something worse.
The question hung in the water, unanswered, as they sank deeper into the black.
