The creature wore the face of a dead man.
Kael's blade stopped an inch from its throat, suspended by a force he couldn't see. The avatar's stolen features twisted into something that might have been amusement—or hunger. Behind him, the ruined cathedral groaned. Stone dust filtered through fractured stained glass, painting the fog in cathedral colors.
"You've learned to taste death," the thing said. Its voice scraped like rusted iron. "The first Reaver could do that too."
"Get out of that body." Kael adjusted his grip. The sword hummed with suppressed energy, his Sovereign-stage reserves barely containing the killing intent coiled inside. "Or I'll cut you out."
The avatar laughed. The sound belonged to a man who'd died screaming three days ago—a Thornwick dockside informant who'd known too much about Zarek's operations. Kael had harvested him already, pulled seventeen skills and a map of the cult's safe houses from cooling flesh.
But this thing had claimed the corpse anyway.
"Touching, how you reave the fallen." The avatar's borrowed eyes rolled toward the heaps of cultists Kael had carved through to reach this moment. "Twelve bodies in one hour. You're building the arsenal."
"Eighteen." Kael took a measured step sideways, testing the entity's reaction. "The ones in the vestries didn't have lungs anymore."
"Soul Reavers always were hungry." The word dripped with contempt that predated human language. "Though yours runs deeper than most. Tell me, little sovereign—do you know what you truly carry?"
The question landed like a trap spring. Kael had learned to distrust Entity interest. They wanted something. They always wanted something.
"My blade wants your head," he said. "That's all the knowing I need."
The avatar's mouth stretched too wide. Joints cracked. The informant's face sagged sideways, revealing a second mouth beneath the first—an opening into absolute darkness that breathed cold and hunger.
"Then take it."
The thing lunged.
Kael moved. Three years of combat had taught him that Entities were fast, stronger than any Awakened, and guided by instincts that didn't map to human patterns. He'd also learned their weaknesses—fragmented consciousness, reliance on stolen vessels, the fundamental alienness that made them misjudge mortal reflexes.
His sword carved a horizontal arc. The avatar twisted impossibly, its spine bending backward, and the blade scored a burning line across its chest. Black ichor sprayed. The thing shrieked—not pain, exactly, but something like surprise.
It landed three paces back. The wound closed, but the movement was sluggish. Slower than before.
"You're damaged," Kael observed. The cathedral had been sealed for six hours. Whatever avatar this thing wore, the host body was failing. "How long can you hold that meat together?"
"Long enough." The avatar's second mouth gaped wider. "Long enough to ask my question again. What do you carry, Soul Reaver?"
"I told you—"
"You carry a splinter." The thing's voice dropped to a whisper that filled the ruined space. "A splinter of something vast. Something that was here before the seals. Before the Orders. Before your kind learned to fear the dark."
Kael's hand tightened on his sword. A splinter. He thought of the dreams—the ones he didn't talk about, the black space beneath his soul where something waited. The way his harvesting felt different from other Reavers, cleaner, deeper, more complete.
"How do you know that?"
"Because I was there." The avatar smiled with too many teeth. "When the first Reaver walked the Material. When the Abyss bled and wept and screamed at what it had birthed. When seventy-two children were caged and one was—" The thing paused, tasting the word. "Cast out."
The air changed. Something fundamental shifted in the cathedral's broken geometry. Kael felt it in his marrow—a resonance that bypassed his Sovereign senses entirely and spoke directly to whatever lived in his darkness.
"You didn't come here to kill me," he said slowly. "You came to talk."
"I came to watch." The avatar's borrowed face arranged itself into an expression of ancient satisfaction. "The cultists were convenient. The seal near here grows thin. Soon the Great Rift will crack and all your Orders' little wars will mean nothing."
Kael sheathed his sword. The motion was deliberate, a calculated risk. "If you wanted me dead, I'd be dead."
"You're not wrong."
"Then what do you want?"
The avatar's form flickered. The informant's corpse was failing faster now, skin graying, eyes sinking into their sockets. Whatever powered this manifestation was burning through its vessel like oil through a wick.
"The same thing I always want." The voice lost its iron scrape, becoming something older, colder. "I want the splinter returned. I want my lost brother to come home."
Brother.
The word detonated in Kael's chest. He thought of the Sepulcher tablets, the fragmented records, the first Reaver whose name had been erased from every archive. A figure who had walked between Material and Abyss, who had created the harvesting technique, who had—
Who had vanished. No body. No trace. Just absence where history should have been.
"You're lying."
"I cannot." The avatar's form collapsed an inch, its knees buckling. "We do not lie. We do not need to. We are."
"Then prove it." Kael stepped forward, close enough to touch. The corpse reeked of decay and something chemical—the substance the cultists used to preserve their vessels. "Show me what I carry. Show me what you claim I am."
The avatar looked up. The informant's eyes had gone fully black, twin voids that reflected nothing.
"Touch me, then. Reave me. You know how."
Kael hesitated. His hand hovered over the corpse's shoulder. Every instinct screamed warning—this was exactly the kind of trap Entity avatars set, the that turned Reavers into something worse than their quarry.
But his darkness stirred. Something deep in his soul that recognized this presence. Something that remembered.
He touched the corpse.

"Now you know," the thing rasped. "The harvesting was never yours. The power you carry was lent, not given. And one day—"
The corpse collapsed. What remained was a husk, skin stretched over bone, eyes empty sockets. The Entity was gone. Whatever manifestation had worn this body had burned itself out.
Kael stood over the remains, hand still tingling with resonance. A splinter. Borrowed power. The first Reaver hadn't died—he'd become something else, something bound to the Abyss itself, and a piece of that binding had passed to every Soul Reaver who came after.
Including him.
The cathedral groaned again. Kael looked up, instincts screaming danger. Cracks spiderwebbed across the vaulted ceiling. The seal—he'd forgotten about the seal. The cultists had been performing a ritual when he'd crashed their gathering, and all this time they'd been weakening the barrier between here and whatever waited beyond.
Light blazed through the fractures. Not sunlight—something colder, older, the pale luminescence of the Abyss bleeding through inadequate barriers. The seal was failing. The rift beneath the cathedral was widening.
Kael ran.
The ceiling collapsed behind him, tons of stone crashing down in a cascade that shook the entire district. He burst through the cathedral's main doors into a Thornwick night thick with coal-smoke and panic. People filled the streets, their faces turned toward the ruins, their mouths open in screams he couldn't hear over the thunder of structural failure.
The ground shuddered. A crack split the street, racing outward like a living thing, and from that fissure came light the color of drowned stars. The rift wasn't just in the cathedral—it was spreading.
"Evacuate the district!" Kael shouted, his voice carrying the resonance of Sovereign-stage authority. "Everyone move north! Now!"
Some people obeyed. Most just screamed. This was what panic looked like in a city that had lived under the threat of Abyssal incursion for generations—the animal terror of prey that knew predators existed but had never truly believed they'd be eaten.
Kael found a relatively stable position on a pile of rubble and let his senses expand. The Soul Reaver gift included sensitivity to spiritual residue, the ability to track souls and their afterimages. The entire district was thick with fading echoes—the cultists he'd killed, yes, but also the residents who'd been caught in the collapse, the workers who'd died defending this sector when the first tremors hit.
Dozens of corpses. Maybe hundreds.
The ground beneath him pulsed. Another crack split the cobblestones, and the drowned-star light intensified. The rift wasn't just opening—it was feeding. Feeding on the death he'd created, drawing power from the bodies he'd reaved, converting spiritual residue into kinetic force for its expansion.
"Damn it."
Kael dropped from the rubble and ran toward the nearest body. A woman, middle-aged, dressed in worker's clothes. She'd been crushed by falling masonry. Her eyes stared at nothing. She was still warm.

He needed to move. Needed to get clear, warn the Orders, prepare for whatever came next. The avatar's revelation still echoed in his mind—the first Reaver hadn't died but had become part of the prison, and Kael carried a splinter of that sacrifice. The power he'd built his survival on, the harvesting that had kept him alive through three years of impossible odds, was borrowed from something vast and bound to something cosmic.
But that was a problem for later. Right now, the immediate threat demanded attention.
Kael turned to run—and stopped.
A figure stood at the edge of the light-column. Human-shaped but wrong in proportions, limbs too long, joints bending in directions that hurt to observe. It moved with a flowing grace that suggested it had never learned to walk on human legs.
The thing turned toward him.
Its face was beautiful. Terribly, perfectly beautiful, features shifting like water, each expression more compelling than the last. Kael found himself staring, his legs heavy, his sword arm slow to respond.
"Hunger," the thing said. Its voice was music and screaming, harmony and cacophony, two sounds that should have clashed but instead created something addictive. "Such hunger. You taste like the one who built the cage."
Kael's darkness stirred. Something in him recognized this presence—not as enemy, but as... Kin? The splinter pulsed, responding to the Entity's attention with something like curiosity.
