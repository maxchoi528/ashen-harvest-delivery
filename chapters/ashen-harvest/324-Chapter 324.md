The door to the abandoned textile warehouse exploded inward, shards of rotting timber spraying across the floor like broken teeth. Kael rolled beneath a falling beam, heat from the burning rooftop licking at his coat. Three cultists rushed through the breach, their robes smoking, chanting something in that awful tongue that made his teeth ache.
"Stay down, Ashborn!" Sera's voice cut through the chaos. She stood on a collapsed catwalk above, her bone-white hair catching the firelight as she hurled a vial of silver liquid at the nearest cultist. The splash hissed against the man's chest, and he collapsed screaming, steam rising from his flesh.
Kael scrambled behind a overturned loom, counting his options. The warehouse sat on the northern edge of Thornwick's industrial quarter, far enough from the Order garrison that nobody would hear gunfire. He'd followed the tip about cult activity here for three hours, expecting a small gathering. Instead, he'd stumbled into something massive.
Another cultist appeared at his flank, knife raised. The man was young, maybe twenty, with the wild eyes of a true believer. Kael caught the blade on his forearm, felt the edge bite through leather into flesh. Pain flared white-hot. He headbutted the cultist, used the stagger to twist the knife away, then drove his palm into the man's sternum with enough force to crack ribs.
The cultist crumpled. Kael dropped beside him, pressed his palm against the still-warm chest, and pulled.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted basic fire resistance (two days) from the remains. Physical conditioning acquired.]
The fire resistance settled into his bones like a familiar coat. His burned shoulder stopped hurting. The smoke no longer choked him. Small mercies from a corpse that still trembled with residual terror.
"Sera! Report!"
"Four down, three more on the second floor!" She dropped a second vial, blinding another cultist. "This is bigger than intel suggested. They're preparing something in the basement."
Kael pulled his knife from the dead man's ribs and wiped it clean on the corpse's robe. The fire had spread to the eastern wall now, eating through ancient wooden supports. Minutes before the roof collapsed. Minutes before the whole structure came down.
"Then we move fast."
He climbed through a hole in the floor where the catwalk had given way, dropping into darkness thick with smoke and the copper smell of blood. The basement stairs were stone, old construction from when this building had been a guild hall. Cultists had cleared the lower level entirely—heavy crates pushed against walls, creating a wide central space that reeked of incense and something chemical.
Twenty robed figures stood in concentric circles around a massive summoning circle drawn in salt and sulfur. Candles guttered in iron holders, casting dancing shadows across faces that held the slack expressions of deep trance. At the circle's center, a rift-tear hung suspended in the air like a wound that refused to close.
Kael counted the cultists. Twenty against two. Bad odds even with Awakened abilities.
"The seal!" Sera materialized beside him, breathing hard. Her coat was torn, blood running from a gash on her temple. "They're not summoning anything. They're weakening a seal."
"How many?"
"The basement circle. If this completes, they'll crack one of the minor rifts in the ward-network. Probably the Ashfeld gate." She spat blood. "The Six Orders maintain seventy-two primary seals across the continent. Crack enough of them, and the Veil falls apart."
Twenty cultists. A mass ritual. An active rift-tear growing in size with each passing second.
Kael's mind raced through possibilities. Direct assault meant dying in seconds—the cultists were armed, aware, and ready to kill. Retreat meant letting the ritual complete, which meant one more seal failure in a system that could barely hold. Neither option worked.
"The people in the circles," he said. "Are they willing participants or victims?"
"Mixed. Inner ring chose this. Outer ring are probably thralls—mind-controlled or drugged." Sera readied another vial. "The inner ring maintains the ritual. Take them out, and the whole thing collapses."
"Take them out how?"
"Get close enough to disrupt their focus. Break their chant. They'll wake up furious and probably kill us, but the magic will fail." She met his eyes. "I can blind them with flash-powder. You hit the inner ring."
"You've worked with Reavers before."
"I've survived Reavers before." She smiled without humor. "Different thing."
The fire above groaned. The building had minutes at most before structural failure. The rift-tear pulsed, hungry and growing. Kael watched the inner ring cultists—five figures in darker robes, standing at cardinal points around the tear, their voices rising in that awful harmonic chant.
"Sera. Can you keep the outer ring busy?"
"For about thirty seconds if I'm throwing everything I have." She cracked her knuckles. "What are you planning?"
"I'm going to harvest one of them."
"You're insane."
"Probably." Kael moved to the edge of the circle, staying in the shadows where the candlelight didn't reach. The outer ring cultists had been swaying in trance, but several now turned toward the stairs, alert to sounds of struggle from above. Good. Focus split was useful. "The inner ring leader. You see that one, black robes, gold trim?"
"Old man with the silver beard?"
"He's the anchor. The others are following his rhythm. Disrupt him, and they all stumble." Kael's palm itched with anticipation. He'd harvested abilities from dozens of corpses in the past months. Skills, memories, sometimes something stranger. The Soul Reaver had never told him exactly what he could take from the living.
Now he'd find out.
Sera's flash-powder detonated in a sphere of blinding white light. Cultists screamed, clawing at eyes. Kael moved.
He crossed the circle in three seconds, hitting the old man at full sprint. The cult leader stumbled back, hands rising to cast some defensive spell. Kael caught both wrists, held on, and pulled.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Void Resistance (several hours) from the living. Cult Ritual Knowledge (fragmented) acquired.]
The old man's eyes went wide. His mouth opened in a silent scream as Kael drank knowledge directly from his soul—not memories exactly, but understanding. The ritual. Its purpose. The pattern of syllables needed to maintain a rift-tear. Information that should have cost years to learn, delivered in a heartbeat of contact.
The cult leader collapsed. His body hit the salt circle and the pattern shattered like glass, sulfur and incense erupting in a cloud of noxious smoke. The rift-tear shuddered, stabilized, then began to collapse in on itself with an unholy shriek.
"Retreat!" one of the remaining inner ring cultists screamed. "The anchor is down! Abort, abort!"
They fled. Not toward the stairs—toward a hidden exit at the back of the basement that Kael hadn't noticed. Sensible. The building was dying around them, and victory had become impossible.
Kael let them run. He had the information he needed.
The rift-tear sealed itself with a final pop of displaced air. The outer ring cultists had scattered into the smoke, some still blind, others confused. Sera moved among them with brutal efficiency, dropping anyone who tried to maintain the ritual or attack her.
Kael stood in the ruined circle, tasting the aftereffects of his harvest. Void Resistance settled into his awareness like a second skin—the ability to withstand the corrosive touch of Abyssal energy. Useful for what came next.
The Cult Ritual Knowledge was stranger. Fragments of understanding that didn't fit his own experience. He knew,, that the ritual had been designed to weaken a specific seal—the Ashfeld gate, just as Sera said. He knew it had taken six months of preparation. He knew the cultists had branches in six other cities, all preparing similar rituals for tonight.
Tonight. Not a random attack. A coordinated strike.
"Sera!" He found her dragging an unconscious cultist toward the stairs. "The other cities. We need to warn someone."
"The Orders already know. Probably." She kicked the cultist into a pile with others. "The Shadow Court has been tracking cult movements for weeks. They know something big is coming."
"Then why aren't they stopping it?"
She paused, breathing hard. The fire above them had reached critical mass. The ceiling groaned, ancient beams preparing to give way. "Because sometimes the Orders want things to happen. Controlled damage. If the cults weaken a few minor seals, the Orders get emergency powers, increased funding, public support."
"That's insane."
"That's politics." She grabbed his arm and hauled him toward the stairs. "Move. Now."
They climbed through smoke and collapsing wood, emerging into the alley behind the warehouse just as the roof gave way entirely. Flame erupted upward, a pillar of fire visible across the industrial quarter. People would see it. People would ask questions.
Kael didn't care about questions. He cared about the knowledge burning in his skull.
Seventy-two seals. Six cities. Coordinated attacks. The cult wasn't random terrorism—it was surgical strikes designed to cripple the ward-network without destroying it entirely. Someone wanted the Veil weakened but not fallen. Someone wanted controlled chaos.
"The Ashfeld gate," he said as they walked quickly away from the burning building. "How important is it?"
"Minor seal. Contains a Class-3 Entity. If it broke, the Entity would emerge, kill everyone in a five-mile radius, then probably die itself when the remaining seals reinforce." Sera wiped blood from her face with her sleeve. "Bad, but not apocalyptic."
"And if they crack multiple seals at once?"
She stopped walking. Her expression shifted from exhaustion to something colder. "Then the Veil starts failing across the entire network. The Entities don't need to break free individually. They just wait for the structure to collapse, then walk through together."
"That's the plan?"
"That's the fear." She resumed walking, faster now. "The Abyssal cults have talked about it for centuries. The Great Unsealing. They believe that if all seventy-two seals fail simultaneously, the Material and Abyss merge into a single realm. They call it paradise."
"What do the Orders call it?"
"The end of everything."
They reached the edge of the industrial quarter, where cobblestone gave way to the paved streets of Thornwick proper. Steam-carts rumbled past, their passengers oblivious to the fire visible on the horizon. The city breathed on, unaware of how close it had come to catastrophe.
Kael thought about the cult leader's memories. Fragmented, yes, but clear enough. The ritual patterns. The timing sequences. The way the summoning circle had been designed specifically to exploit weaknesses in the ward-network. This hadn't been built by amateurs following ancient texts. Someone with deep knowledge of the seal structure had designed this attack.
Someone who understood the system well enough to break it.
"The Orders," he said slowly. "Which one maintains the Ashfeld seal?"
"Order of Dawn, mostly. Some overlap with the Iron Vanguard for the physical wards." Sera's eyes narrowed. "Why?"
"Because someone fed the cults information about those wards. Detailed information. The ritual tonight knew exactly where to strike."
She didn't respond immediately. They walked in silence through streets that grew progressively wealtheir, from industrial slums to merchant district brownstones. Finally, she spoke.
"You think someone inside an Order is working with the cults."
"I think someone with access to ward-maintenance schedules gave the cults a roadmap." Kael turned the knowledge over in his mind, examining it from different angles. "The ritual required six months of preparation. That's a lot of time for someone to notice cult activity and report it."
"But nobody reported anything."
"Or somebody reported it, and the report got buried." He stopped at a corner where gas-lamps cast pools of amber light. "Sera. The Shadow Court has been tracking cult movements. How much do they know?"
Her expression flickered—uncomfortable, guarded. "Enough to be useful. Enough to be dangerous."
"Does the Shadow Court have moles inside the Orders?"
"I can't answer that."
"That's not a no."
She laughed, harsh and short. "Kael. The Shadow Court exists because the Orders need deniable operations. Blackmail, assassination, information warfare—things that can't be tied back to official institutions. If we didn't have sources inside the Orders, we'd be useless."
"So you do have sources."
"I didn't say that." But her tone said everything. "Look. Whatever you're thinking, forget it. Tonight was a victory. We stopped the ritual. We got information. The cult's plan failed."
"The plan failed tonight. They have six other cities. They have people inside the system. And you just told me the Orders already know something big is coming and aren't stopping it." Kael felt frustration boiling beneath his surface calm. "How is that a victory?"
"Because we survived. Because we have leverage now." Sera pointed at him. "You harvested a cult leader. That means you have ritual knowledge they think is safe inside a dead man's head. Knowledge about their network, their communication patterns, maybe even their leadership structure. That's worth more than a thousand burned warehouses."
The Soul Reaver hummed in his awareness, satisfied with the acquisition. She wasn't wrong. The fragments of Cult Ritual Knowledge gave him more than just tonight's failed summoning. He understood the framework now, the underlying principles that made the rituals work. If he encountered another cult ritual, he'd recognize the structure. He'd know how to disrupt it.
More importantly, he understood why they'd chosen the Ashfeld gate specifically.
"The seal network," he said. "It's not random. The seventy-two rifts are positioned in a pattern."
Sera went very still. "What do you mean?"
"The cult leader knew. He understood the geometry of the system. The seals aren't scattered across the continent—they're arranged in a formation. Like..." He struggled for words that matched the knowledge burning in his brain. "Like a lock. The seventy-two rifts aren't prisons. They're pins in a mechanism that holds the Veil together. The cults aren't trying to break individual locks. They're trying to break the right combination of pins to make the whole mechanism fail."
Silence stretched between them. A steam-cart rumbled past, its passengers laughing at some private joke. Somewhere in the distance, fire bells rang.
"Where did you get that information?" Sera's voice had changed—harder, more careful.
"From the cult leader. His memories." Kael met her gaze. "Does the Shadow Court know about the lock mechanism?"
"If they do, they haven't shared it with me."
"But you suspect."
She turned away, looking toward the fire still visible on the horizon. "The Shadow Court has access to ancient texts. Pre-Orders texts from before the Abyssal Wars. Some of them describe the seal system as a 'great working'—a single massive enchantment than seventy-two separate bindings." She paused. "I always thought it was metaphor. Religious language for a political structure."

"Then everything we know about the war is wrong." She turned back to face him. "The Orders were founded to maintain individual seals. If those seals are components of a unified system, then the Orders have been treating symptoms instead of understanding the disease. And if the cults understand the system better than the Orders do..."

"I don't know. I need to report to my superiors. They'll decide what to do with the information." She hesitated. "Kael. What you did tonight—harvesting from a living person—that's not something the Orders tolerate. If they find out, they'll hunt you."

Complete destruction of your soul so you can't come back or be resurrected." She stepped closer, voice dropping. "The original Soul Reaver was destroyed for exactly this. The Orders spent centuries making sure nothing like him could exist again. And you just proved the technique still works."
Kael considered her words. The memory of harvesting from the cult leader still burned in his mind—the man's terror, his soul's resistance, the moment of connection when knowledge flowed between them like water through a broken dam. It hadn't felt like murder. It had felt like theft.
But the man had died. Maybe not from the harvest itself, but from its effects. The soul couldn't survive being emptied like a cup, and Kael had taken more than simple abilities.

And when something threatens their control, they burn everything to preserve it."
"You don't trust them."
"I trust them to protect their own interests. Those interests don't always align with justice." She pulled her coat tighter. "The cult attack tonight was a test. They wanted to see how the Orders would respond, how quickly they'd mobilize, what resources they'd commit. Now they have data. They'll adjust and attack again, probably within the month."

If it's real, the cults aren't just trying to free the Entities. They're trying to take apart the machine entirely. That means they understand how it works. That means someone designed it with foreknowledge of how it would be dismantled."

"I think nothing survives forever. The seal network is old. Older than the Orders. Older than the Concordat. Maybe the designers built in a termination sequence. A way for the system to reset if it became corrupted."

He could see it now, if he focused. A web of energy stretching across the continent, seventy-two nodes connected by lines of force that pulsed with each heartbeat of the Veil. The Ashfeld gate sat at one end of a chain, a specific pin in a specific position. Remove it, and nothing happened immediately. But remove the right combination, and the whole structure would cascade into failure.
"The cults don't have a complete map," he said.
Sera glanced at him. "What?"
"The cult leader. He knew the Ashfeld gate was part of a chain, but he didn't know which gates connected to which. The ritual tonight was designed to break one pin. If they break random pins, they might weaken the system, but they won't collapse it." Kael felt certainty growing in his chest. "They're guessing. Testing. Trying to figure out the complete pattern by trial and error."

"They have time. The seals weaken naturally. Each year, the cults get stronger while the system gets weaker. They don't need to rush. They just need to keep testing until they find the right combination."
They'd reached the edge of the merchant quarter, where expensive shops gave way to the elegant townhouses of minor nobility. Sera stopped beneath a gas-lamp, shadows cutting across her face.
"You've changed since the execution," she said quietly. "The man I met six months ago wouldn't have thought any of this through. He would have fought the cultists, harvested some corpses for power, and called it a victory."

"That's the question, isn't it?" Sera turned to leave, then stopped. "The records say he was destroyed by the combined might of all six Orders, at the height of the Second Abyssal War. They say he betrayed humanity, tried to use the seal system for his own purposes. But the records also say he died defending the Thornwick gate during the first major cult uprising."

"Most history is." She walked into the darkness between gas-lamps. "My superiors will want to talk to you. I'll send word through the usual channels."
Then she was gone, footsteps fading into the ambient noise of the city.
Kael stood alone beneath the light, turning the vial of Veil-draught over in his hands. The knowledge from the cult leader still churned in his mind, fragments of understanding that didn't fit together. He knew the ritual pattern. He knew the geometry of the seal network. He knew the cults had people inside the Orders.
But he didn't know who. Didn't know why. Didn't know the ultimate goal beyond 'collapse the system.'
The fire in the industrial quarter had been contained. Smoke still rose from the ruins, a gray smear against the night sky. Somewhere in that wreckage, cultists were dead or captured, their plan ruined for tonight.
But six other cities still had rituals prepared. Six other strikes waiting to happen. And somewhere in the web of Orders and shadows that governed the world, a traitor worked to ensure those strikes succeeded.
Kael pocketed the Veil-draught and started walking toward his lodgings. He needed to organize his thoughts, plan his next moves, figure out how to use his new knowledge without getting himself killed.
Behind him, in the ruins of the warehouse, something stirred. A body that shouldn't have been alive. A cultist who had played dead when Sera went through, who had waited for the chaos to pass.
The figure rose slowly, bones cracking back into place, skin knitting together where vials had burned it. The man's eyes opened—not human eyes anymore, but pits of swirling darkness that reflected no light.
"Reaver," he whispered, voice like grinding stone. "The Court sends regards."
He began walking toward the city center, following the path Kael had taken.
The night was far from over.
