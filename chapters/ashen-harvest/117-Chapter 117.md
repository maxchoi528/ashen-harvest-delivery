The Emberfall Golem moved like a thunderclap given flesh.
Kael dove left as stone fists cratered the cobblestones where he'd stood a heartbeat before. Heat radiated from the construct's molten joints, and the smell of scorched iron filled his nostrils. He rolled to his feet, Bloodthorn already singing in his grip, and fired three times into the creature's chest. The bullets punched craters in basalt flesh but did nothing to slow its advance.
Two figures lingered in the tavern doorway across the street—cultists in tattered red robes, watching with the casual interest of men at a dogfight. One leaned against the frame, arms folded. The other turned his head and spat into the gutter. They'd been trailing him for two blocks before the golem dropped from a rooftop.
No time to worry about them.
The construct swung again. Kael ducked under the arc, feeling the displaced air slice at his hair. He darted closer, driving Bloodthorn's blade into a gap between the golem's shoulder plates. Molten material bubbled around the steel, but the weapon held.
Not yet. The golem wasn't dead.
It grabbed him. Massive fingers closed around Kael's torso, lifting him off the ground. Pain lanced through his ribs as stone crushed against bone. The golem raised him to eye level—or, to the two pits of amber light that served as its gaze.
The cultists had begun a low chant. Kael couldn't make out the words, but he recognized the rhythm of a summoning. The golem's grip tightened. Heat spread across Kael's chest, blistering his shirt, and then the pain became a white-hot roar that drove everything else from his mind.
He needed to get free. He needed to get free now.
Kael stabbed downward, driving Bloodthorn into the golem's forearm. The blade sank deep, and for a moment the construct's grip loosened. Kael wrenched himself sideways, feeling stone scrape against skin, and dropped five feet to the cobblestones. His left arm screamed in protest—a fracture, maybe worse. His ribs shrieked louder, a grinding agony that stole his breath and buckled his knees.
The golem reached for him again.
Kael pressed his palm against the construct's ankle where it met the ground.
The world inverted. Kael found himself standing inside the golem's core, a spherical chamber of cooling magma and ancient runestones. A figure knelt in the center—a man in Order robes, his skin fused with the stone, his eyes empty sockets leaking embers. The Seventh Rift Binder. Dead centuries ago, but his soul had been harvested and compressed into fuel.
Kael stretched out his hand.
The dead man looked up. "You shouldn't be here."
The soul resisted for only a moment before fragmenting, its essence flowing into Kael's palm.
Kael gasped as he returned to his body. The golem stood motionless, its amber eyes flickering. Without the binding, it was just stone—beautiful, terrifying stone, but stone nonetheless.
One cultist swore and bolted. The other followed a half-step later, their robes snapping in the cold air as they vanished into the smog.
Kael didn't chase. He couldn't. His ribs ground together with every breath, and his left arm hung at an angle that made his vision swim when he tried to lift it. He limped into the nearest alley, each step sending fresh agony through his chest. The fracture wouldn't kill him. The ribs might, if a splintered edge shifted wrong and found his lung.
He found a doorway in the alley's depths, half-hidden behind a collapsed rain barrel. He lowered himself to the ground, back against rotting wood, and waited for the pain to become something he could think around.
It took a long time.
The Emberfall Binding sat coiled in his chest like a second heartbeat. He could feel it—heat and knowledge and centuries of combat experience compressed into a single, waiting ember. But his body was a wreck. His arm throbbed. His ribs turned every breath into a negotiation with pain. The Soul Reaver's gifts didn't extend to instant healing. They kept him alive, kept him functional, but the damage remained.
He sat in that doorway for what felt like hours, breathing in shallow sips, cataloging what he'd learned. Soul Reaver didn't just work on corpses. It worked on anything that contained harvested souls. Constructs, wards, bindings. Any cage built from stolen essence could be opened.
This was new. This was dangerous.
When he could finally stand without his vision going black at the edges, he pulled himself upright and began to move.
Three blocks away, a figure in gray robes watched him disappear into Thornwick's eternal smog. Inquisitor Vane noted the location, then melted back into the fog.
The Underworld entrance lay beneath a condemned tannery in the Drowning Ward. Kael descended via a rope ladder that crumbled the moment he touched bottom, forcing him to drop the final stretch into darkness. He landed hard, rolled, and came up with Bloodthorn drawn. The impact sent a fresh spike of pain through his ribs, and he bit down on a curse.
Smells hit him first: sulfur, decay, and something older. Something that predated the city's foundations.
"Halt," a voice commanded.
Torchlight flared, revealing six figures in mismatched armor. The Thornwick Underworld Syndicate—the criminal organization that controlled access to the tunnels below. They'd been fighting a shadow war with the cultists for months, and Kael had made himself useful enough to earn a meeting.
"I'm here to see the Warden," he said.
"The Warden doesn't meet with—"
"Tell them I know about the Seventh Rift. Tell them I know what's really down there."
The guards exchanged glances. One left, returning later with a strip of black cloth.
"Blindfold," the guard said. "Standard procedure."
Kael complied. The walk wound through passages that felt less like tunnels and more like the throat of some massive creature. The air grew warmer, then colder, then warm again. He heard distant sounds—machinery, voices, something that might have been singing.
The blindfold came off in a chamber carved from living rock. A woman sat behind a desk made from salvaged ship timbers, her silver hair cropped close to her skull, her eyes the pale blue of winter ice. The Warden. Kael had never learned her real name.
"Sit," she said, gesturing to a chair that looked less comfortable than it probably was.
Kael sat. The movement cost him. He kept his injured arm pressed against his torso and his breathing shallow.
"You've been busy tonight." She poured two cups of something that smelled like burnt leather and alcohol. "Killed an Emberfall golem. That's not easy."
"I didn't kill it. I unbundled it."
"Even worse." She slid a cup across the desk. "Soul Reaver tricks. The Orders would pay a fortune for your head."
"The Orders can line up behind everyone else."
The Warden smiled—a thin expression that didn't reach her eyes. "I like you, Ashborn. You've got teeth. But teeth don't mean much down here. What do you want?"
"Information. The Seventh Rift—someone's been working on it. The cultists. They mentioned feeding constructs."
The Warden's expression didn't change, but something flickered behind her eyes. "The 72 Rifts. Sealed after the Abyssal Wars. Everyone knows about them."
"Everyone knows they're sealed. Nobody knows they're being worked." Kael leaned forward, then wished he hadn't as his ribs protested. "I've seen cultists in Thornwick for months. Thought they were isolated nutjobs. They're not. They're organized. And they're targeting specific rifts."
"Which ones?"
"Seventh. Twenty-Third. Forty-One. Maybe more."
Silence stretched between them. The Warden took a long drink from her cup, then set it down with excessive care.
"You're either very brave or very stupid," she said. "Come with me."
She led him through a door he hadn't noticed, into a chamber that stole his breath.
Maps covered every wall. Not just Thornwick—maps of the entire continent, marked with symbols he didn't recognize. Red threads connected certain points, forming patterns that seemed almost organic. In the center stood a massive table covered in documents, ledgers, and what looked disturbingly like severed fingers.
"The Six Orders," the Warden said, pointing to the symbols. "The Iron Vanguard, Arcane Spire, Order of Dawn, Silver Covenant, Crimson Circle, and the Veiled Monastery. Each maintains certain rifts. Each has its own reasons for wanting them sealed—or open."
"And the cultists?"
"Astute." She pulled a document from the table. "The Shadow Court. Formerly an intelligence arm of the Orders, now operating independently. They've been monitoring rifts for decades, waiting for opportunities."
"Opportunities to unseal?"
"Opportunities to control." The Warden met his gaze. "The Shadow Court doesn't want the rifts open. They want the keys. Every seal has a corresponding key—sometimes physical, sometimes spiritual. Control the key, control the Entity."
Kael's stomach turned. "They're trying to enslave Abyssal Entities."
"The Whisper King did it once. Why not others?"
The name hit Kael like a physical blow. The Whisper King—a name from half-forgotten legends, the Entity that had nearly broken the world during the Second Rift War. Supposedly destroyed. Supposedly.
"You know something about that," the Warden observed. "Your face does interesting things when I mention him."
"I know legends."
"Legends come from somewhere." She returned to the table, rifling through documents. "Here's what I can offer you, Ashborn. The Shadow Court is watching you. They've been watching since you first used your abilities. You're interesting to them."
"Why?"
"Because you're a Soul Reaver. The first in over a century." She pulled out a small case and slid it across the table. "The Shadow Court wants to recruit you. The cultists want to kill you. The Orders want to dissect you. And the Underworld Syndicate wants to make you an offer."
Kael opened the case. Inside lay a silver medallion, inscribed with symbols he didn't recognize.
"Associate status," the Warden explained. "You'll have access to Underworld resources—safe houses, information networks, supplies. In exchange, you provide services as needed."
"What kind of services?"
"The kind that keep people like me awake at night." She smiled again, that thin, cold expression. "The kind that keep the rifts sealed. Or controlled."
Kael weighed the medallion in his hand. Joining the Underworld Syndicate meant painting an even larger target on his back. It meant becoming part of an organization that existed in the cracks of the established order.
It also meant resources. Information. Allies.
"What do you know about the Emberfall Binding I just harvested?" he asked.
The Warden's eyebrows rose. "You harvested an Emberfall Binding? From a golem?"
"Yes."
"That's..." She trailed off, her composure cracking for the first time. "That's not supposed to be possible. Soul Reavers harvest from the dead, not from constructs."
"I touched the golem. I was inside its core. There was a soul in there—a Binder, bound to fuel the construct."
"That's a binding. Not a soul. Unless..." She trailed off again, staring at him with new eyes. "Unless you can harvest the bindings themselves. The chains that hold other souls captive."
Kael hadn't thought of it that way, but it made sense. Soul Reaver didn't just extract from corpses—it extracted essence. And essence was essence, whether bound to flesh or stone.
"You're dangerous," the Warden said quietly. "More dangerous than I thought. The Shadow Court was right to watch you."
"And the Underworld Syndicate? What do they want?"
"Survival. The same as everyone else." She retrieved another document, this one covered in hand-drawn diagrams. "The rifts are weakening. Every year, the seals require more maintenance. The cultists aren't trying to speed up the flow—they're trying to control it. To be ready when the seals finally fail."
"How long?"
"Decades. Maybe a century if we're lucky." She set the document down. "But luck isn't something I rely on, Ashborn. The Shadows are already moving. The cultists are already working. And you—you've just announced yourself to everyone who matters by killing an Emberfall golem in the middle of Thornwick."
"Anyone decent was already watching."
"Not this many." She pulled a small crystal from her pocket and held it up to the light. Inside, something dark writhed. "This is a Shadow Echo. Recorded in the Underworld's deepest passages. It's been following you for three days."
Kael's hand moved to Bloodthorn. "Following me?"
"Monitoring you. Someone wants to know everything about you—what you eat, who you speak to, what you dream." She set the crystal down. "The Shadow Court has files on every Soul Reaver in history. They know how you think. They know what you want. And they know how to break you."
"What do they want?"
"The same thing they always want. Power. Control." She paused. "The same thing you want, Ashborn, if we're being honest. You're not hunting cultists because it's the right thing to do. You're hunting them because they have something you need."
Kael didn't argue. She was right. Every kill, every harvest, every risk—all of it driven by the need to grow stronger. To survive long enough to find what he was really looking for.
The truth about his past. The truth about the Soul Reaver. The truth about why he'd been executed—and why he'd come back.
"One more thing," the Warden said, returning to the map table. She pointed to a location in the northern mountains. "The Seventh Rift. Where your Emberfall golem came from. The cultists have been excavating there for months."
"Is there a base?"
"A temple. Old. Pre-Orders. Built on the ruins of something older still." She traced a line on the map. "If you want to know what they're planning, that's where you'll find answers."
Kael memorized the location. "And if I find something the Underworld Syndicate wants?"
"We negotiate. Everyone wins." She extended her hand. "Do we have an accord, Ashborn?"
Kael looked at her hand, then at the silver medallion, then at the maps covering the walls. He thought about the cultists in the streets, the Shadow Court in the shadows, the Orders in their towers. He thought about Vane—the hunter who was surely tracking him at this very moment.
He thought about the Emberfall Binding burning in his chest, waiting to be used.
He took the medallion. He didn't take her hand.
"Associates," he said. "Not allies. I don't owe you loyalty."
The Warden smiled—a real smile this time, cold but approving. "Acceptable. The Underworld Syndicate has its own codes."
Kael left the Underworld through a different tunnel, emerging in a drainage culvert near the river docks. Dawn was breaking, painting the sky in shades of copper and ash. His arm throbbed with dull pain. His ribs ached with every breath. The Emberfall Binding sat heavy in his chest, alien and hot.
He found a safehouse—a collapsed tenement in the Merchant Quarter—and slipped inside. The room was small, dark, and smelled like mildew, but it had a bed and a door that locked from the inside.
Kael sat down, pulled out the silver medallion, and examined it in the dim light. The symbols were unfamiliar, but he could feel something radiating from them—a faint warmth, like standing near a fire.
A knock at the door.
Kael was on his feet instantly, Bloodthorn drawn, pressed against the wall beside the frame. The movement cost him. Pain flared through his ribs, and he had to clamp his jaw to keep from crying out.
"Who's there?"
"A friend," a voice replied. Familiar. Kael recognized the accent. "Aldric. From the Order of Dawn. I believe we have mutual interests."
Kael opened the door. A young man stood in the hallway, dressed in traveling clothes that couldn't hide the Dawn insignia on his collar. He had kind eyes and a nervous smile, and he was carrying a leather satchel that bulged with documents.
"Inquisitor Vane is two blocks east," Aldric said, slipping inside and closing the door behind him. "We don't have much time."
"What do you want?"
"To help you. And to ask for help in return." He opened the satchel, revealing stacks of papers covered in official seals. "I'm investigating corruption within the Order of Dawn. Someone's been selling information to the Shadow Court. I need to know who, and I need to prove it."
"Why come to me?"
"Because you're the only one they haven't been able to control." Aldric met his gaze. "You're outside the system, Ashborn. You can do things we can't. See things we can't."
Kael considered. Aldric was naive—he could see it in the man's eyes. But he was also offering something valuable: inside information. Access to Order resources. A potential ally who wasn't controlled by the same forces hunting him.
"What do you know about Soul Reavers?" Kael asked.
Aldric hesitated. "Not much. The Orders don't talk about them. They're taboo. Forbidden. But I've read the old records. The first Soul Reaver was a woman named—" He paused, checking a document. "Her name was Eriadne. She discovered her abilities during the Third Rift War."
"And?"
"She disappeared. Somewhere around the Thirty-Second Rift. The Orders searched for years, but they never found her body." Aldric looked up. "Or her weapon."
Kael's hand tightened on Bloodthorn. "You think I can find her?"
"I think you might be the only one who can." Aldric pulled out a map, this one showing the northern regions. "The Thirty-Second Rift. It's been sealed since the war, but the seal has degraded faster than any other. The cultists have been working on it for decades."
"What's down there?"
"According to the oldest records? A tomb. Built by the first Soul Reaver. Containing her research, her weapons, her victims."
Kael stared at the map. A tomb. Containing secrets about the Soul Reaver. About where this power came from. About why he could do what he did.
"I'll think about it," he said.
Aldric nodded, gathering his documents. "I have to go. Vane is getting closer, and if he finds me here, we're both dead." He paused at the door. "One more thing. The Shadow Court—their leader. We don't know who they are. But we know what they want."
"What?"
"Another Soul Reaver. Someone loyal to them. Someone they can control." Aldric's eyes met Kael's. "They've been hunting Reavers for decades. You're the last one alive. Whatever they're planning, you're at the center of it."
The door closed. Kael stood alone in the darkness, processing everything he'd learned.
The Shadow Court wanted to control him. The cultists were working the rifts. The Underworld Syndicate was offering resources. The Orders were corrupt. And somewhere in the mountains, a tomb held secrets that could change everything.
His arm throbbed. His ribs ached. And in his chest, the Emberfall Binding waited to be tested.
Kael closed his eyes and reached for the ability. It unfurled like a flower of fire, spreading through his limbs with liquid heat. He could feel the power now—not just the ability itself, but the knowledge of how to use it. The Binders had encoded centuries of combat experience into every binding.
Emberfall. Binding: Construct.
He opened his eyes and looked at the room around him. The walls were stone. The floor was wood. The bed was—
The bed was burning. Flames licked along the wooden frame, spreading to the mattress and the curtains. Kael stumbled backward, releasing the ability. The flames died, leaving behind nothing but smoke and the smell of char.
He looked at his hands. They were trembling—not from pain, but from power. The Emberfall Binding gave him control over fire and constructs. Not much, not yet, but enough to matter.
Enough to survive.
Kael moved to the window and peered through the grimy glass. Thornwick stretched before him, a maze of rooftops and alleyways. Somewhere out there, Vane was hunting. The cultists were scheming. The Shadow Court was watching.
And in the northern mountains, a tomb waited.
He pulled on his coat, checked his weapons, and slipped out into the dawn.
Later, Kael found himself at the gates of the northern cemetery, staring at a fresh grave.
The headstone bore a name he recognized: Commander Elara Vance. Order of Dawn. Killed in action—or so the official report claimed.
But the body in this grave was missing.
Kael knelt beside the disturbed earth, feeling the spiritual residue. Someone had been here. Recently. And they'd taken the corpse.
He followed the trail, moving through the cemetery toward the old ossuary at its edge. The building was abandoned, its doors hanging off rusted hinges, its windows dark and empty. But inside, he could hear voices.
Kael pressed himself against the exterior wall, controlling his breathing. His ribs protested the crouch, a grinding reminder that his body hadn't forgiven the golem.
"—the Ashborn boy." A man's voice, clipped and impatient. "He unbundled an Emberfall construct."
A second voice, deeper, resonant with authority. "The Shadow Court wants him alive. For now."
"Then we use something stronger. The Hollow Knight from the Thirty-First Rift. It's been bound for decades. One command and—"
"You overreach. The Knight is a weapon of last resort. The Court's timetable hasn't reached that point."
"The timetable is accelerating. The Reaver is growing faster than projected. If he reaches the Thirty-Second Rift before—"
"Then we report to the Court. Not act on our own initiative. Is that understood?"
Silence. Then, grudgingly: "Understood."
Kael moved before they could finish the conversation. He burst through the ossuary door, Bloodthorn blazing with stolen fire, and slammed into the first cultist before the man could react. The cultist's neck broke cleanly. Kael harvested before the body hit the ground.
The second cultist threw a bolt of shadow magic. Kael dodged, feeling the cold pass inches from his face, and retaliated with Emberfall fire. Flames engulfed the cultist, who screamed and writhed until nothing remained but ash.
The third cultist was already running.
Kael chased him through the ossuary, out into the cemetery, and into the streets beyond. The cultist was fast, but Kael was faster. He tackled the man in an alley, drove Bloodthorn through his shoulder, and held the blade there while he harvested.
"Why?" Kael demanded, pressing harder. "What's in the Thirty-Second Rift?"
The cultist laughed—blood bubbling at his lips. "You'll never stop us. The seals are already failing. The Entities are waking. And when they rise, the old world dies with it."
"Where's the Commander's body?"
"What body?" The cultist's eyes went glassy. "She's been dead for weeks. We just borrowed her face."
Kael's blood ran cold. "The Shadow Court. They have someone using her appearance?"
"Everyone has secrets, Reaver. Even your precious Order of Dawn." The cultist coughed, crimson flecking his lips. "Look for her at the temple. The one they built on the ruins. She's waiting for you."
The man died. Kael let him fall.
He stood in the alley, mind racing. The Shadow Court had someone impersonating a dead commander. They were working the rifts. They were hunting him. And they were building toward something—a deadline he couldn't see yet.
He had to find the temple. He had to find answers.
But first, he had to deal with the footsteps approaching from the alley's entrance.
Kael turned. Three figures stood in the morning light—two men in gray armor, one woman in black robes. Inquisitor Vane's hunters.
"Kael Ashborn," the woman said. "You're under arrest for crimes against the Orders."
Kael backed toward the alley's far end, Bloodthorn raised. "The Orders don't have jurisdiction over me."
"The Council of Six disagrees." She stepped forward. "Come quietly and you'll be given a trial. Resist and we'll take you in chains."
Behind him, shadows moved. More hunters, blocking his escape route.
Kael looked at the hunters, at the weapons pointed at his chest, at the inevitable confrontation ahead. He thought about running. He thought about fighting. He thought about the Emberfall Binding burning in his veins.
Then he smiled.
"Chains won't hold me," he said. "But I'll make you a deal. Help me find the temple in the northern mountains—the one the cultists are using as a base. Help me stop whatever they're planning. And I'll come willingly."
The hunters exchanged glances.
"Why should we trust you?" the woman asked.
Kael reached into his pocket and pulled out the silver medallion, letting them see it clearly. "Because the Underworld Syndicate is already moving. Because the Shadow Court is playing all of us. And because if the rifts unseal, it won't matter what Orders we serve."
Silence stretched between them.
"The Inquisitor won't like this," one of the men said.
"The Inquisitor," Kael replied, "is being played. Just like the rest of you."
The woman studied him for a long moment. Then she lowered her weapon.
"Tell us everything," she said.
The temple ruins rose from the mountainside like broken teeth, jagged spires of black stone reaching toward a sky that had turned the color of bruises. Kael stood at the base, flanked by two of Vane's hunters—the woman named Sera and the man named Torvin—while the third kept watch on the path below.
"You're sure about this?" Sera asked.
"No," Kael admitted. "But I don't see another choice."
He could feel the temple's presence—a weight in his mind, like standing too close to a fire. Ancient power, bound and waiting. The cultists had been working here for months, maybe years. Whatever they were doing, it was close to completion.
"You go in alone," Torvin said. "That's suicide."
"I go in with you, and the whole Order of Dawn knows my business." Kael checked his weapons—Bloodthorn, the Emberfall Binding, and two abilities he'd harvested in the past day. Enough to survive. Maybe enough to win.
"I'll mark your position," Sera said, tapping something at her wrist. A tracking sigil. "If you're not out in a few hours, we come in after you."
Kael nodded.
He started up the path.
The temple's entrance was an archway carved with symbols that seemed to shift when he wasn't looking directly at them. Kael stepped through, feeling the air thicken around him, and found himself in a chamber that defied geometry. Walls stretched in impossible directions. Stairs led both up and down. Light came from nowhere and everywhere.
"Impressive," a voice said.
Kael spun. A figure stood in the center of the chamber—tall, gaunt, wrapped in shadows that seemed to breathe. The Shadow Court's representative.
"Elara?" Kael asked, recognizing the face even though he knew it wasn't her.
"Clever boy." The figure smiled. "No, I'm not Commander Vance. But I am wearing her. Her soul was delicious."
"You're the one who's been impersonating her."
"And many others. The Shadow Court has many faces." The figure stepped closer. "We've been watching you for so long, Kael Ashborn. Waiting for you to grow powerful enough to be useful."
"Useful for what?"
"The unsealing. The rifts need keys to open—keys made from harvested souls. Soul Reavers are the only ones who can create them." The figure spread its arms. "Join us. Take your place as the vessel of the new world."
Kael laughed. "You're insane."
"I'm patient. The Entities will rise. The old gods will fall. And in the chaos, the Shadow Court will rule." The figure tilted its head. "Unless you stop us. But you can't. You're just one man, no matter how many souls you've harvested."
Kael's grip tightened on Bloodthorn. "Maybe. But I'm also the only one who can unbundled your bindings. Strip your constructs. Free the souls you've stolen."
For the first time, something flickered in the figure's eyes. Uncertainty.
"You wouldn't dare."
"Watch me."
Kael lunged forward, Emberfall fire blazing, Bloodthorn singing through the air.
The temple erupted into chaos.
They fought through chambers that defied understanding, past traps that turned space against itself, through halls lined with the remains of those who had tried before him. Kael harvested everything he could touch—abilities, knowledge, fragments of power that added to his arsenal.
Each harvest pushed him closer to the temple's heart, where something massive waited.
The Hollow Knight.
It stood thirty feet tall, a suit of armor forged from shadow and bone, its helm empty save for two pits of consuming darkness. The cultists had bound it here centuries ago, fueling it with stolen souls. It was a weapon of last resort—and it was awake.
"Soul Reaver," it rumbled, voice like grinding stone. "You smell like death."
"I am death," Kael replied, and he realized he meant it. "I'm the thing that comes for people like you."
He activated everything at once. Emberfall fire wrapped around Bloodthorn, turning the blade into a baton of liquid flame. Shadow Veil cloaked him in darkness. Temporal Stutter sent his movements out of sync with reality.
He attacked.
The Hollow Knight moved with terrible speed, its blade carving through stone where Kael had been a moment before. But Temporal Stutter let him dodge in ways that shouldn't have been possible, his body flickering between moments. He carved chunks from the construct's armor with every strike, Emberfall fire eating into its shadow-flesh.
But it wasn't enough.
The Hollow Knight caught him with a backhand blow, sending him crashing through three pillars. Kael felt ribs crack—fresh breaks, layered over the old ones. Blood filled his mouth. The construct loomed over him, raising its blade for the killing stroke.
Kael reached up and touched the Hollow Knight's ankle.
He dove into the construct's core, into a space that existed between existence and oblivion. The souls bound within screamed as he passed—thousands of them, centuries of stolen essence. And at the center, a single point of light that was the binding itself.
Kael grabbed it.
The binding came free, hot and screaming and alive. It flowed into him, filling spaces he didn't know he had. He felt the Hollow Knight collapse behind him, its armor crumpling into ash as the power that held it together vanished.
Kael staggered out of the cloud of ash, gasping for breath. His body was broken in a dozen places. His mind felt like it had been scoured with sand. But he was alive.
And he was stronger.
He found the chamber at the temple's heart—a room lined with mirrors that reflected not his image but his possibilities. Different versions of himself, doing different things. Some heroic. Some monstrous. Some dead.
In the center stood a pedestal, and on the pedestal lay a book bound in leather that seemed to be made from skin. The Sepulcher Tablets—the records of the first Soul Reaver.
Kael reached for it.
"Stop," a voice commanded.
He turned. A woman stood in the doorway—old, with eyes that had seen too much and a bearing that spoke of battles long past. She wore robes that had once been white, now stained with the dust of ages.
"You shouldn't take that," she said.
"Who are you?"
"Someone who made the same choice you're about to make." She walked toward him, moving with the careful grace of the ancient. "I am Eriadne. The first Soul Reaver. And you're standing in my tomb."
Kael stared at her. The first Soul Reaver. The woman who'd discovered this power, who had written the tablets, who had disappeared centuries ago.
"You're dead," he said.
"I'm what's left of her." Eriadne gestured to the mirrors. "Echoes. Memories. Anchored to this place by the tablets themselves. When you take that book, you'll learn everything—how the Soul Reaver was created, what it truly is, what it can become."
"And?"
"And you'll carry it. Forever. The weight of every soul I've harvested. Every choice I've made." Her eyes met his. "Are you ready for that?"
Kael looked at the book. At the mirrors. At the woman who had started everything.
"Yes," he said. "I am."
He took the book.
Power flooded through him—not Emberfall fire, not Shadow Veil, but something older. Something that felt like destiny. He saw flashes of his own future, fragments of possibilities, and at the center of it all, a single truth:
The Soul Reaver was never meant to be a weapon.
It was meant to be a key.
Not for opening rifts, but for closing them. For undoing the damage of millennia. For giving the souls trapped in the Abyss a chance to rest.
"Learn well," Eriadne whispered, her form beginning to fade. "And remember—the power you carry was stolen from the dead. Every harvest is a choice. Choose wisely."
She vanished.
Kael stood alone in the chamber, holding the tablets, feeling their weight settle into his bones. Outside, he could hear the hunters arriving. Inside, he could feel his own power growing.
And in the mirrors, his reflections watched him—every version of himself, every path not taken, every choice yet to come. One by one, they turned their backs and walked away, leaving him alone with the book and the silence and the weight of what he'd become.
