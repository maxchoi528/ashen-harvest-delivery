The cell door groaned open, iron scraping against stone. Kael rolled to his feet, muscles coiled, expecting guards.
Three figures stood in the corridor instead. Hollow cheeks. Ragged uniforms. Eyes that held the particular emptiness of men who'd stopped hoping. Prisoners. A fourth man hung back, his fingers working through a sequence of subtle gestures—sign language, Kael realized. A language the guards couldn't read.
"New blood," the largest prisoner rasped. His forearm bore a tattoo: interlocking circles surrounding a six-pointed star. "Heard you wereExecution Day's miracle. Came back from the dead."
Kael said nothing. The cell behind him measured six paces by four, and the walls still held the memory of his screaming. Three days he'd spent in that darkness, convinced he'd lost his mind. The resurrection hadn't healed him. His shoulder ached where the noose had bit. His throat felt permanently scoured.
The big man stepped closer. "The Warden's people will come asking questions. We can make those questions stop."
"Can't help you with Warden Thorne's inquiries," the signaller said aloud, his voice a dry whisper. "But I can tell you about the ones who came before you. The ones who died wrong."
Kael's eyes swept across the three men. The signaller appeared barely past thirty, but his gaze carried decades. The woman beside him had shorn hair and burn scars climbing her neck. The third man—older, thinner—kept one hand hidden in his sleeve.
"What's the price?" Kael asked.
"Information goes both ways," the woman said. "You give, we give. That's how things work in Blackgate."
A bell clanged somewhere deep in the prison. Distant footsteps echoed through stone corridors. The big man's attention snapped toward the sound, then back to Kael.
"Warden's runners. They'll reach this block in six minutes." He glanced at the signaller. "Mira's choice."
Mira studied Kael with unsettling intensity. "Soul Reaver. That's what the rumors say. You took something from the dead before they hanged you. That's why you came back."
The claim landed like a thrown blade. Kael kept his expression neutral, but his pulse spiked. He'd harvested from one guard during his escape—the man had been dying from a crossbow bolt when Kael found him, and the extraction had been violent, desperate. Word had already spread through the prison's rumor network.
"Soul Reavers are dead," the old man said for the first time. His hidden hand emerged from his sleeve, and Kael saw the silver ring on his index finger—a sigil he didn't recognize. "The Orders made sure of that, centuries ago. Killed every last one."
"Not every last one," Mira replied. "Just the ones they caught."
Footsteps growing louder. Kael calculated distances, angles, the weight distribution of the three prisoners. The signaller had positioned himself to block the corridor junction. Deliberate formation. They'd approached his cell with a plan.
"You want something specific," Kael said. "You wouldn't risk Warden Thorne's attention otherwise."
"Warden's nephew," the big man confirmed. "Garrett Thorne. He's been feeding prisoners to something in the lower levels for months. The Mortician's Guild doesn't ask questions when bodies go missing from Blackgate. We need proof."
"You need a witness who won't be believed," Kael said. "An executed man, fresh from the grave. They'd assume I was mad."
The signaller smiled without warmth. "They already think you're mad. Might as well be useful."
Another bell. Closer now. The footsteps resolved into multiple sets—boots, heavy and purposeful.
Kael moved. No warning, no telegraph. He seized the big man's wrist and twisted, using the man's own momentum to send him sprawling into the corridor wall. The signaller lunged; Kael sidestepped and drove his palm into the man's sternum, dropping him to his knees. Mira froze mid-motion, her hand dropping from a concealed blade.
"Next time, ask first," Kael said. "I don't appreciate being cornered."
The big man pushed himself upright, cradling his shoulder. "Fair enough. But you heard the bells. Warden's people—"
"I know." Kael stepped over the signaller's groaning form. "Where's the lower level access?"
Mira's eyes widened. "You're going to—"
"Proof requires evidence. Evidence requires investigation." Kael grabbed the old man by his collar and pulled him close enough to smell the fear-sweat soaking through his rags. "The thing feeding on prisoners. Describe it."
The old man's silver ring caught the dim torchlight. Up close, Kael could see the sigil more clearly—an eye bisected by a vertical line, surrounded by thorns. The symbol meant nothing to him, but the man's terror was genuine.
"Aberration," the old man whispered. "Entity-touched. Garrett Thorne found a way to summon fragments through the old seals. It's growing. Every prisoner it consumes, it becomes stronger."
Kael released him. The information aligned with fragments he'd gathered before his execution—whispers about seal degradation, cult activity in Thornwick's underground. The Six Orders maintained the world's defenses against the Abyss, but those defenses were crumbling. Seventy-two sealed rifts, each containing an Entity. Some of those seals were failing.
"The access point," Kael repeated.
Mira recovered faster than the others. She pointed toward the corridor's far end. "Maintenance stairs. Third door past the iron maiden. Leads down to the old cisterns. No guards past that point—the Warden doesn't want witnesses."
"Garrett Thorne goes there personally," the big man added, still rubbing his shoulder. "Every third night. Always with two jars of soul-essence."
Soul-essence. The currency of Awakened society, worth a hundred gold sovereigns per vial. The Mortician's Guild controlled the legal supply, harvesting from the deceased with family consent. But illegal trade flourished in places like Blackgate, where desperate men sold whatever remained of their spiritual essence.
The footsteps reached the block entrance. Voices barked orders.
"Go," Mira said. "We'll tell them you threatened us. Standard procedure for new arrivals."
Kael didn't waste breath on thanks. He moved.
The maintenance stairs descended through increasingly ancient stonework. Newer construction gave way to medieval foundations, then older still—pre-Concordat architecture, when the Orders had first established their grip on spiritual power. Kael counted the levels as he descended. Four below ground. Five. Six.
At the seventh level, the air changed. Moisture and decay, but underneath it, something else—a pressure against his spiritual senses. His Ascendant-stage perception registered the wrongness immediately. This wasn't natural corruption. Something was bleeding through from the other side.
A distant splash. Then silence.
Kael drew from his reserves, coating his hands in spiritual energy. Soul Reaver harvesting was free—no cost to his own essence—but manipulation required expenditure. He shaped the energy into a thin barrier across his palms and continued forward.
The cistern opened before him, vast and dark. Broken pillars rose from stagnant water like drowned fingers. Along the walls, chains hung from ancient hooks. Old prison cells, Kael realized. Forgotten architecture repurposed for something uglier.
Fresh blood stained the nearest pillar. A body slumped against the wall—young, male, throat opened in a pattern too precise for suicide. The corpse couldn't be more than an hour old. Still warm.
Soul Reaver instincts stirred. Kael knelt beside the body, pressed his palm against the cooling flesh.
The harvest interface erupted behind his eyes.
**[Soul Harvest: You extracted a few hours of residual memory from the remains. Fragment acquired: "Garrett Thorne... The ritual circle... It demands more than we anticipated..."]**
Kael absorbed the fragments. The dead man's final hours unfolded in disjointed flashes—pain, fear, a chamber deeper in the cisterns, candles arranged around symbols that hurt to perceive. Garrett Thorne stood at the center, arms raised, speaking words in a language that predated the Orders.
A shape coiled in the shadows behind him. Not human. Too many angles. Eyes that existed in dimensions the mortal mind wasn't built to process.
The memory ended with the corpse's own scream.
Kael stood. The chamber stretched in three directions, and the fresh blood trail led east, toward a section of wall that looked solid but wasn't. His perception showed him the truth—the wall existed, but it was also a passage. Layered reality. He'd need the signaller's gift for that kind of visual deception.
Someone else had been here recently. The footprints in the dried mud told that story. One set entering. None leaving. Either the corpse had been carried out after death, or whoever killed this man had abilities Kael couldn't see.
A grinding sound echoed from the passage. Stone against stone.
Kael moved toward it, keeping his spiritual senses extended. The pressure intensified as he approached the hidden doorway. Whatever lived down here was aware of his presence. It wanted him to come closer.
He entered anyway.
The chamber beyond defied architecture. Walls curved in impossible directions, meeting a ceiling that seemed simultaneously ten feet and a thousand feet above. Candles burned without wax, flames casting shadows that moved independently of their sources. At the center, a circle of salt and iron surrounded a depression in the floor—shaped, Kael realized with cold certainty, like a grave.
Garrett Thorne stood beside it. Young, handsome, wearing the uniform of a prison administrator with medals Kael didn't recognize. His eyes glowed faintly in the candlelight.
"The returned dead," Thorne said. His voice carried an undertone of wrongness, harmonic frequencies that grated against Kael's spiritual senses. "I wondered when you'd find your way here."
"Where are the jars?" Kael asked. "The soul-essence you're feeding to that thing."
Thorne smiled. "The Aberration doesn't consume essence. It consumes souls. Whole. Complete. The energy returns to the Abyss, strengthening the seal from the other side."
"That's not how seals work."
"No. It's how they used to work, before the Orders rewrote the history books." Thorne gestured, and the shadows behind him shifted. The shape Kael had seen in the memory resolved into something concrete—a serpentine form wrapped around a pillar, scales like polished obsidian, eyes that burned with cold fire. "The Abyssal Entities aren't prisoners. They're the world itself. The seals don't protect us from them. They protect the Entities from us."
The serpent uncoiled. It moved without sound, descending from the pillar with liquid grace.
Kael prepared his spiritual barrier. The Soul Reaver ability churned beneath his skin, hungry for contact. He could feel the Aberration's presence—vast, alien, but not mindless. It watched him with something that might have been curiosity.
"The cult activity in Thornwick," Kael said. "You didn't organize it alone."
"Organized?" Thorne laughed. "I only provide opportunity. The true believers do the rest. They've waited centuries for the seals to weaken enough for this. Every night, more cracks appear. The Great Rift's degradation accelerates. Soon—"
The serpent struck.
Kael rolled left, feeling air part where his head had been. His barrier absorbed the tail that followed, spiritual energy flaring bright. The impact drove him backward three steps.
The creature didn't pursue immediately. It circled, keeping the pillar between them, watching.
"The Soul Reaver's gift," Thorne observed. "My sources told me you could harvest abilities from the dead. But can you harvest from something that was never alive?"
Kael's mind raced. Soul Reaver rules required contact with a corpse. The Aberration wasn't a corpse. It was something else entirely—a fragment of a greater Entity, summoned through a cracked seal.
"Interesting," Thorne continued. "The Orders would pay fortunes for that answer. Even fragments of Entity-touched essence are priceless on the black market. But you're not here for money, are you?"
The serpent lunged again. Kael ducked under its jaw and drove his palm against its flank. The contact sent harvest feedback screaming through his nerves—alien, incomprehensible, nothing like harvesting from human remains.
**[Soul Harvest: Partial contact. Aberration essence resists extraction. Y acquired: 0.3ml Entity-touched residue. WARNING: Unstable integration.]**
The residue burned. Kael felt it seeping into his own spiritual reserves, foreign and aggressive. His body tried to reject it. His body was losing.
"Your ancestors made the same mistake," Thorne said. He hadn't moved from his position, arms folded, watching with clinical interest. "The first Soul Reaver tried to harvest from the Entities themselves. The result wasn't death. Death would have been mercy."
Kael forced himself upright. The serpent circled again, slower now, conserving energy. It knew it had wounded him. It was waiting for the poison to finish the work.
"Tell me about the first Soul Reaver," Kael said. His voice came out steadier than he felt.
"Records say they died sealing the Great Rift." Thorne's smile widened. "But records lie. The first Soul Reaver didn't die. They became something else. Something that serves."
The serpent struck a third time. Kael caught its fangs on his barrier, felt bone-deep pressure as the creature tried to force its way through. His vision blurred. His spiritual reserves depleted faster than they should have.
Then the chamber's far wall exploded inward.
Stone and debris scattered across the salt circle, breaking its pattern. A figure strode through the dust—woman, armored in leather and steel, carrying a blade that glowed with contained fire.
"Garrett Thorne." Her voice rang off the walls. "By order of the Council of Six, you are charged with violation of the Concordat, unlawful summoning of Abyssal fragments, and the murder of civilians under warrant."
Thorne's expression shifted from confidence to fury. "The Shadow Court. You're not supposed to—"
"We're not supposed to exist." The woman advanced, blade raised. "Yet here we are."
The serpent abandoned Kael. It turned toward the new threat, scales rippling with reflected firelight.
Kael used the distraction. He grabbed a chunk of broken stone and hurled it at Thorne's head. The man dodged, but his attention fractured for one crucial second. Kael closed the distance, drove his harvest-charged palm against Thorne's chest.
The contact overwhelmed sensory input. Thorne's soul wasn't like normal human essence—it was hollowed out, replaced with something that resonated at Abyssal frequencies.
**[Soul Harvest: You extracted several hours of ritual knowledge from the remains. Fragment acquired: "The Whisper King's covenant... Sealed at the source... Only blood of the Reaver can—"]**
The notification cut off. Kael staggered backward, clutching his head.
Thorne collapsed. His eyes had gone white, mouth working soundlessly. The serpent shrieked—a sound like tearing metal—and lunged at Kael with renewed fury.
The armored woman intercepted it. Her blade carved a blazing arc through the air, striking the creature's neck. Black ichor sprayed. The serpent thrashed, weakened but not defeated.
"Run!" she shouted at Kael. "This thing will regenerate if I don't finish it properly!"
Kael didn't argue. He dragged Thorne's unconscious form toward the passage, every step an exercise in agony. The Entity-touched residue still burned through his spiritual channels, and the harvested knowledge sat uneasily in his mind—a jumble of ritual patterns and names that meant nothing yet.
Behind him, the woman's battle-cry echoed off impossible walls. Steel met scaled flesh. Fire met shadow.
Kael reached the passage entrance and pulled Thorne through. The layered reality resisted his passage, reality folding around him like wet cloth. He emerged into the cistern, gasping.
The water had risen. Dark and cold, lapping against the ancient pillars. In the distance, other figures moved—guards, maybe, responding to the explosion.
Kael dropped Thorne and checked his vitals. Alive, barely. The hollowed soul would make him a vegetable without specialized treatment. The Mortician's Guild might help, if he could get Thorne to them with the right price.
His hands trembled as he examined his own spiritual reserves. Depleted by nearly forty percent from the Entity residue. The 0.3ml sat in his channels like broken glass, slowly being filtered out by his body's natural defenses.
A new notification appeared, unbidden.
**[SYSTEM UPDATE: Entity-touched residue detected. Soul Reaver integration incomplete. Side effects may include: spiritual perception fluctuations, temporary alignment with Abyssal frequencies, enhanced awareness of seal degradation. Full integration requires: complete Entity fragment or Transcendent-stage capacity.]**
Kael swore under his breath. He'd touched something he shouldn't have. The first Soul Reaver had made the same mistake—reached for power beyond their grasp.
But he'd also gotten what he came for. Proof of Thorne's activities. Knowledge of cult organization. A fragment of information about the Whisper King that made no sense yet but mattered.
The footsteps reached the cistern entrance. Torches appeared around the corner, casting dancing shadows.
Kael grabbed Thorne's collar and dragged him toward the eastern wall. He remembered Mira's directions—maintenance access, third door past the iron maiden. If he could reach it before the guards secured the exits...
A new voice echoed through the chamber. Female, cold, commanding.
"Garrett Thorne. By the authority of the Order of Dawn, you are under arrest for crimes against the seal network."
The armored woman from the ritual chamber. She must have escaped the serpent.
Kael's grip tightened on Thorne's collar. Two factions now. The Shadow Court had claimed him first, but the Order of Dawn had authority he couldn't easily dispute. Both wanted Thorne for different reasons.
Neither would let him walk away with their prize.
He made a decision. Kicked Thorne's legs out from under him, letting the unconscious man drop into the shadow of a broken pillar. Then he moved, fast and silent, toward the western passage—the one the guards hadn't reached yet.
Behind him, voices clashed in confrontation. The Shadow Court operative demanding Thorne's release. The guards demanding answers. Thorne's body lying unattended in the dark.
Kael didn't look back. He found the passage, climbed the maintenance stairs, and emerged into Blackgate's upper corridors. Dawn light filtered through high windows. The prison was waking—bells, shouts, the organized chaos of shift changes.
He slipped through it like a ghost, blending with the flow of prisoners being led to morning labor. No one looked twice at the gaunt man in bloodstained rags. In Blackgate, looking twice at anything could get you killed.
By the time he reached the outer yard, his mind had already shifted to logistics. He needed shelter. Information. A way to process what he'd harvested from Thorne's hollowed soul without losing himself to Entity contamination.
The notification pulsed again.
**[Soul Harvest: several hours of ritual knowledge acquired. Access to this data requires decryption. Estimated processing time: 6-several hours. WARNING: Certain fragments contain classified information. Premature disclosure may trigger spiritual wards.]**
Kael ignored it for now. He had more immediate concerns.
A figure intercepted him near the yard's main gate. Mira, wearing a guard's discarded coat, her burn scars hidden beneath a hastily wrapped scarf.
"You survived," she said. "And you brought back something."
"Garrett Thorne's knowledge. It'll take time to decode." Kael scanned the yard, noting guard positions, gate mechanisms, the distance to the outer wall. "The serpent?"
"Killed the Shadow Court operative. Injured two guards before it retreated through a seal-crack." Mira's voice carried a tremor. "The Warden will blame everyone except himself. They'll lock down the entire prison."
"Good. Lockdowns mean fewer eyes." Kael turned toward the eastern wall. "I need a way out of Blackgate. Then I need to contact someone who deals in restricted information. Soul-essence traders. Anyone who knows about the cult activity in Thornwick."
Mira grabbed his arm. "The cult isn't random. That's what everyone learns eventually. Something's coordinating them—someone with access to old records, old seals, old power." She paused. "There's a broker in Fogmire District. Calls herself the Green Mother. She trades in exactly what you're looking for."
Green Mother. One of the Entities that had aligned with humanity during the Abyssal Wars. Unusual name for a broker, but the criminal underworld loved irony.
Kael nodded once. "Coordinates."
Mira gave him an address, a contact phrase, and a warning: the Green Mother didn't deal with amateurs. Anyone who wasted her time disappeared into the prison system—permanently.
"Thanks for the vote of confidence."
"I'm not voting. I'm betting." Mira stepped back, melting into the crowd of prisoners. "You came back from the dead once. Maybe you can do it again."
Kael found an unguarded section of wall, scaled it using ledges invisible to casual observation, and dropped into the alley beyond. Blackgate's outer perimeter stretched before him—then the industrial quarter, smoke stacks belching against the morning sky, steam engines roaring through streets still slick with night fog.
Thornwick. The gaslit city where ancient seals cracked and criminal enterprises flourished in the gaps between Order jurisdictions. Kael had been executed here three days ago. He'd been resurrected in a prison cell two nights later.
Now he walked free, carrying stolen knowledge and Entity contamination in equal measure.
The Six Orders maintained the world's defenses. The 72 sealed rifts held the Entities at bay. Cults worked to undermine those seals, feeding fragments to something called the Whisper King.
Kael had pieces of a puzzle he couldn't yet see. The Soul Reaver's gift had let him steal those pieces from a man who'd sold his own soul to something inhuman.
He touched the hollow where his left kidney should have been. The Entity residue still burned, a reminder that some harvests cost more than others.
A child's scream echoed from a nearby alley. Kael tensed, then forced himself to relax. The scream cut off abruptly—not killed, he realized, just frightened. Probably a street urchin startled by his passage.
He couldn't help everyone. That wasn't his function. The Soul Reaver took from the dead, and the living had to solve their own problems.
But he could follow threads. Find answers. Unravel the conspiracy that had framed him and turned a city into a feeding ground for Abyssal fragments.
Kael walked deeper into Thornwick's fog-shrouded streets.
Behind him, unnoticed, a shadow detached itself from a rooftop and began to follow.
