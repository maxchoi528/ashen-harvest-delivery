"The old cathedral," she said, wrapping his purchases in oilcloth. "You're not the first to go digging there."
"Other people have tried?"
"Couple of Iron Vanguard Inquisitors, six months back. They came out three days later, screaming about shadows that moved wrong." Dresh accepted his payment in old coin. "One of them cut his own throat in the temple courtyard. The other one resigned from the Order and joined a monastery up north."
Kael tucked the bundle under his arm. "Did they find anything?"
"They found the door. After that, I guess they found their faith tested."
Not encouraging. But the Iron Vanguard's failure meant the cult remained operational. And if the cult was operational, they were either feeding the Entities or widening the rifts. Either outcome demanded intervention.
"Corvin," he said when he returned to the safehouse. "The cult members who guard the crypts. Are they volunteers or conscripts?"

"True believers are dangerous. They don't retreat."

Lyra's jaw tightened, but she didn't argue. She'd already made her position clear—she was coming whether he authorized it or not.
"I'll be ready," Corvin said.

The third bell rang across Thornwick's waterlogged rooftops.
Kael moved through the fog like a blade through tissue. Lyra flanked him, her dark clothing blending with the shadows. They approached the cathedral from the east, where the sacristy door waited behind a crumbling buttress.
The lock was mechanical, not spiritual. Two heavy bolts and a crossbar from the inside. Kael produced his picks—thin strips of tempered steel—and went to work. The mechanism resisted, corroded by decades of disuse, then gave way with a soft click.
"Two people," he murmured. "They said two to open."
Lyra understood. She pressed her shoulder against the door's edge while Kael did the same on the opposite side. Together, they pushed.
The door screamed on ancient hinges. The sound echoed through the empty nave, bouncing off pillars and empty pews. Kael froze, counting heartbeats.
Nothing stirred.
They slipped inside. The cathedral's interior smelled of mildew and faded incense. Moonlight filtered through shattered stained glass, painting the floor in fragments of blue and red. Pews lay overturned, some broken for firewood. Someone had spray-painted slogans across the altar—anti-Order rhetoric, mostly, calling for the burning of Awakened.
Not the work of the cult. Too crude. This was general unrest, the underclass expressing its frustration.
The sacristy door opened onto a narrow corridor. Kael led, one hand trailing the wall, feeling for spiritual signatures. The pressure increased as he descended—faint at first, then stronger. Lyra sensed it too; her breathing grew shallow.
"Something's wrong here," she whispered.
"Everything's wrong here."
The stairs ended at a landing. Above, a ladder descended into darkness. Kael activated his alchemical vial—the liquid inside glowed pale green, intensifying near spiritual energy.
The ladder was old but solid. He descended first, Lyra covering above. The lower crypt opened around them—a vaulted chamber lined with stone coffins, their lids carved with saints and serpents. The air tasted of copper and ozone.
Faint chanting echoed from deeper within.
Kael moved toward the sound, each step deliberate. The coffins' lids were sealed—no fresh harvests here, then. The cult kept their sacrifices elsewhere.
A corridor branched left. He followed it, the chanting growing louder, the words now recognizable as bastardized Old Tongue. Not pure Abyssal dialect—cultists rarely mastered true Entity speech—but close enough to raise bumps along his arms.
The corridor opened into a circular chamber. Kael flattened himself against the wall, gesturing for Lyra to do the same.
Through a gap in the stonework, he saw them.
Twenty cultists, kneeling in concentric rings. Their robes were black, unmarked. Their faces bore painted symbols—spirals and crescents that pulsed faintly with spiritual energy. At the chamber's center, a boy hung from iron hooks, his chest split open, blood pooling into a carved channel that led toward a dark circle on the floor.
The circle radiated cold.
Inside the circle, something waited. Kael couldn't see it clearly—his perception slid away from its edges—but he felt its attention. Vast. Hungry. Patient.
The cult leader stood at the circle's edge, arms raised. She was older than the others, her white hair cropped short, her eyes milky with recent scarring. An Awakened, probably Ascendant class. Her voice rang out, commanding.
"...the Veil thins. The Great Devourer stirs. Soon, the 72 shall walk free, and flesh will be only clothing for our true selves."
The cultists responded as one. "Clothing. Clothing. Clothing."
Kael's hands clenched. The boy on the hooks—he was still alive, barely. His eyes rolled, seeing nothing. Blood dripped from his opened chest, and something dark moved within the wound.
Entity corruption. The thing in the circle was feeding.
"Lyra," he breathed. "The one hanging. Can we save him?"
"Not a chance. Look at him."
She was right. Even if Kael cut him down, the corruption had spread too far. His soul was being devoured in real-time, transferred to whatever waited inside the circle. Minutes remained, maybe less.
If Kael moved now, the cult would respond. Twenty against two, with an Ascendant leader and whatever malevolence presided over the ritual. Lyra could fight—her skills were formidable—but they were outnumbered and outpositioned.
But if they didn't move, the boy died. And something else happened. The ritual completed. The Entity grew stronger.

A hybrid. A soul-eater who worked with Abyssal forces.
