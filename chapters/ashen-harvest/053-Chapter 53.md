The cultist leader's body had gone cold. Kael pressed his palm flat against the corpse's chest, feeling the skin tighten as his Soul Reaver senses reached inward.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Soul Essence (Corrupted) from the remains. Memory Fragment acquired.]
The leader's memories crashed through Kael's mind in fragmented images. Ritual circles drawn in blood. Sealed rifts waiting to be torn open. A face obscured by shadow giving orders that made even fanatics flinch.
"The compound," the dead man had thought, just before Kael's blade found his throat. "Everything leads to the compound."
Kael withdrew his hand and stood. The tunnel stretched in both directions, slick walls glistening in the light from his cracked lantern. Behind him, sounds of pursuit echoed off stone—the rest of the cult squad had finally found their way through the collapsed passage.
"Which way?" Lyra appeared at his elbow, her breathing shallow. Blood from a gash above her eye had dried in a dark streak down her cheek.
"Forward. There's an exit ahead."
"You hope."
"I know." Kael didn't elaborate. The leader's memories had included a mental map of these tunnels—maintenance passages used by the Mortician's Guild to transport the recently deceased to processing facilities in the lower districts. The cult had been using them for months, moving supplies and personnel between safe houses scattered across Thornwick.
Lyra wiped blood from her eye. "Those were Order-trained fighters back there. You saw their formations, their blade work. Cultists don't train like that."
"I saw." Kael checked his sword, noting the chips in the blade where it had met another steel. "Someone's funding this operation with serious resources. Military-grade equipment, tactical training, coordination."
"And they're hunting you specifically. That fireball was meant to kill you, not slow you down."
The fireball. Kael had dove sideways a heartbeat before the flames consumed the space he'd occupied. The cult leader had aimed to incinerate, not incapacitate. They wanted him dead, not captured.
"Why would a cult want a dead Reaver?" Lyra asked, voicing the question neither of them could answer.
Kael started moving toward the distant flicker of daylight. "Maybe they know something I don't."
The tunnel narrowed as it descended, forcing them to walk single file. Kael led, sword drawn, senses straining for any sound that didn't belong. The air grew thick with the smell of old stone and older secrets.
"You're quiet," Lyra observed.
"I'm thinking."
"About what?"
"The journal we found in the safe house. The one with the ritual circles."
Lyra's footsteps faltered for just a moment. "What about it?"
"The circles weren't random. They were positioned at specific points throughout the city. Bridges, guild halls, steam engine stations." Kael ducked beneath a support beam that had partially collapsed. "I think they're planning something big."
"Big how?"
"I don't know yet. But the leader's memories mentioned a timeline. Weeks, not months."
They reached a junction where three tunnels converged. Kael consulted the mental map, choosing the leftmost passage. This one sloped upward, and the air carried the faint taste of rain and coal smoke.
Thornwick. The city waited above them.
---
The safe house wasn't safe anymore.
Kael stood in the doorway, surveying the damage. Someone had torn through the small apartment with methodical precision. Furniture overturned, drawers emptied, the thin mattress slashed open. Whatever they'd been looking for, they'd been willing to destroy everything to find it.
Lyra pushed past him, her eyes scanning the chaos. "My things..."
"Sorry." Kael didn't offer false comfort. They'd escaped with the essentials—his sword, her knives, the satchel containing the journal and their meager funds. Everything else was replaceable. "How long ago?"
"Hours, maybe. The residue is still fresh." Lyra knelt beside a dark stain on the floor. "This is from one of them. He was hurt."
"You can tell?"
"The blood's still tacky." She stood, wiping her hands on her coat. "Someone knew where to find us. Which means either they've been following us, or..."
"Or we were set up from the beginning."
The words hung in the stale air. Lyra's jaw tightened, but she didn't deny it.
"Maybe," she admitted. "Or maybe Zarek's people have resources we don't understand. Tracking talismans, scrying methods. This city has layers, Kael. Thornwick wasn't built on the surface—it was built on secrets."
Kael moved to the window, peering through the grimy glass. The street below showed normal activity: workers returning from late shifts, a vendor pulling his cart, children playing some elaborate game in the fading light. Nothing overtly threatening, but that meant little.
"The journal," he said. "We need to decode it."
"You know cipher work?"
"I know patterns. The cult uses standard Order numbering for their hierarchy. Three-seven-two corresponds to something—maybe a rank, maybe a location, maybe a target."
Lyra moved to stand beside him, close enough that he could smell the copper tang of blood still clinging to her hair. "The Order of Twilight uses three-seven-two. It's a command structure designation. Third echelon, seventy-second active cell."
Kael turned to look at her. "You're certain?"
"I worked with them once. Before." Lyra's voice went flat. "The Twilight Order guards the old seals. They maintain the barriers between here and... There. If they're involved in this, it means the corruption goes deeper than some fanatics playing with powers they don't understand."
The implications settled over them both. If the Order of Twilight had been compromised, then the institution meant to protect the world from Abyssal incursion had become a vehicle for invasion.
"What do the numbers mean when combined?" Kael asked. "Three-seven-two as a single figure."
Lyra closed her eyes, calculating. "Seventy-two. The number of sealed rifts."
The temperature in the room seemed to drop. Seventy-two sealed rifts, each containing an Abyssal Entity too powerful to destroy. The seal network was humanity's only defense against a second Cataclysm.
"They're not just targeting one rift," Kael said. "They're targeting all of them."
---
The rain came just before midnight, washing Thornwick in a grey curtain that turned the gaslit streets into rivers of reflected light and shadow. Kael stood on a rooftop overlooking the merchant district, watching the city through the downpour.
The journal lay open before him, its pages spread across the tar paper covering. He'd been studying the cipher for hours, cross-referencing symbols with the fragmentary memories extracted from the cult leader.
His conclusions painted a picture that grew darker with each discovery.
The ritual circles weren't random placement—they were a network. Each location connected to others through ley lines that pulsed beneath the city's foundations. The cult was building something, assembling pieces of a mechanism that, once complete, would do what the leader's memories had hinted at.
Unseal the rifts. All of them.
"How bad is it?" Lyra's voice came from the shadows. She'd been keeping watch while he worked, patrolling the rooftop perimeter with the restless energy of someone who couldn't sit still.
"They've already activated three circles," Kael said. "The damage to the seals is progressing. Each active circle accelerates the degradation."
"Can we stop them?"
"Maybe. If we understand how the network functions." He tapped a symbol in the journal. "This one appears at every location. It's the keystone—the element that ties the system together."
"Can you harvest enough from one of the circles to understand it?"
"The circles are guarded. Heavy security, probably reinforced with traps. Attacking one alerts the others." Kael had seen that much in the memories. The cult operated in coordinated cells, each unaware of the others until activation.
Lyra moved closer, rain plastering her dark hair to her skull. "There might be another way. The Shadow Court has information on Abyssal workings. If I contact my old handlers..."
"Your old handlers sold you out to Zarek."
"I don't know that for certain."
"You suspect it."
Silence stretched between them. The rain drummed against the rooftop, filling the space where words failed.
"There might be someone else," Lyra finally said. "A broker I knew before. He deals in forbidden knowledge—ritual designs, Order secrets, things that shouldn't be for sale. If anyone understands this cipher, it's him."
"What's his price?"
"Information. Favors. Sometimes..." She hesitated. "Sometimes souls."
Kael's expression didn't change. "You're suggesting I harvest someone for him."
"I'm suggesting we use every resource available. You're already a monster in the eyes of the world. Might as well act like one when it serves your survival."
The words cut deeper than the rain. Kael turned them over in his mind, examining their edges.
"I'm not interested in becoming what they fear I am," he said.
"Even if it means dying?"
"Even then."
Lyra laughed—a bitter, exhausted sound. "You're going to get us both killed with that attitude."
"Maybe. But I'd die knowing I stayed myself than live having become something else."
She stared at him for a long moment. Then she shook her head, rain scattering from her hair. "Fine. We'll find another way. But if it comes down to harvesting someone or watching the world burn—"
"We'll cross that bridge when we reach it."
---
The meeting place was a bombed-out chapel in the lower quarter, one of dozens of structures damaged during the riots three years prior. No one came here anymore. The locals said it was haunted.
Kael had his doubts about ghosts, but the darkness that filled the broken sanctuary felt heavy with more than just absence of light. Something had died here. Something that hadn't fully let go.
Lyra had insisted on coming despite his protests. "You don't know this broker," she'd said. "He's dangerous, and dangerous people respond better to familiar faces."
The broker's name was Corvus. That was all Lyra would tell him, except that he'd been Excommunicated from the Order of Twilight decades ago for practices deemed too controversial, even for an organization built on secrets.
They waited.
Footsteps echoed from somewhere in the ruins—slow, deliberate, accompanied by the tap of a cane against stone. A figure emerged from the shadows, hooded and cloaked against the rain that dripped through the shattered roof.
"Lyra." The voice was a rasp, like dry leaves scraping against each other. "You've brought company."
"He's the one asking questions."
The hood turned toward Kael. Pale eyes caught the dim light, ancient and cold. "A Soul Reaver. How delightful. I haven't seen one of your kind in... Oh, twenty years? They burned the last one at the stake in Karth."
"I'm not here to discuss history."
"No, you're here about the cult activity. The circles. The accelerated seal degradation." Corvus smiled, revealing teeth filed to points. "Everyone's talking about it. The Orders are scrambling, the Shadow Court is taking positions, and somewhere in the depths, the Entities are stirring. It's exciting, really. End times always are."
Kael produced the journal. "I need this deciphered."
"May I?"
Corvus took the journal with gloved hands, turning pages with surprising delicacy. His eyes moved rapidly, reading symbols that seemed to shift and change when Kael tried to focus on them.
"Interesting," the broker murmured. "Very interesting. This is Twilight work, but corrupted. Someone's taken Order methodology and twisted it toward... Ambitious purposes."
"Can you read it?"
"Most of it. This cipher is based on astronomical positioning—planetary alignments from the pre-Cataclysm era. Whoever designed this knew what they were doing." Corvus looked up. "The question is, what do you know, Reaver? Information flows in both directions."
"What do you want?"
"A sample. Not a harvest—you keep your abilities to yourself. I want a demonstration of what you can do. Show me a soul extraction, and I'll give you everything I know about these circles."
Lyra stepped forward. "That's too high a price."
"Is it?" Corvus turned to face her. "Your friend here can take memories, skills, even personality fragments from the dead. Think of what that means. Think of the secrets that die with people every day, lost forever because no one was there to capture them."
"You're asking him to become a thief of souls."
"I'm asking him to be what he already is, just more efficiently." The broker smiled again. "Come now, Lyra. We both know you've done worse for lesser reasons. The difference here is that he gets to choose what he takes. The dead can't consent either way."
Kael's hand rested on his sword hilt. Every instinct told him this was wrong—not the harvesting itself, but the implication. Corvus wanted to see him work, to understand his capabilities, to evaluate whether Kael was worth recruiting or eliminating.
"I have a counter-offer," Kael said. "You decipher the journal. I pay you in coin—triple your usual rate. And I answer one question about myself, truthfully."
"Only one?"
"That's all you get."
Corvus considered, his pale eyes never leaving Kael's face. The silence stretched, broken only by the rain drumming through the roof.
"Two questions," he finally said. "And I want the coin regardless of answers."
"Done."
The broker's smile widened. "Then we have a bargain. Come—let's find somewhere dry to work. This cipher will take hours to fully decode, and I'd not catch my death. Again."
---
The deciphered journal revealed everything.
The cult—known only as the Unbound—was planning a synchronized ritual that would shatter all seventy-two seals simultaneously. The timing corresponded to a planetary alignment that occurred once every three hundred years, when the barriers between realms grew thin.
They'd already placed operatives within the major Orders. The corruption wasn't just institutional—it was systemic. Some of the most trusted guardians of the seal network had been replaced by fanatics committed to releasing the Entities.
"Why?" Lyra asked, reading over Kael's shoulder. "What do they gain from this? The Entities will consume everything—including them."
"Because they're not planning to be consumed." Corvus traced a finger along a diagram in the journal. "This is a summoning matrix. The Unbound aren't trying to free the Entities—they're trying to control them. Channel their power through human vessels."
"That's suicide."
"It would be, for ordinary Awakened. But there are stories..." The broker hesitated. "Legends of humans who merged with Entities during the original Cataclysm. They became something neither fully human nor fully Abyssal. Something powerful."
"The first Soul Reaver," Kael said quietly.
Corvus nodded. "The Soul Reaver wasn't just a title. It was a being that emerged from the chaos of the sealing ritual. A human soul fused with Entity essence, capable of harvesting the power of death itself. The Unbound believes they can recreate that process—and they're using the seal network as a catalyst."
Kael stared at the journal, his mind racing. The Soul Reaver. He'd always known his ability was impossible—death magic that cost nothing, that violated the fundamental laws of spiritual conservation. Now he understood why.
He wasn't just a Reaver. He was a remnant of something that had been created three hundred years ago. A mistake. A weapon that had never been meant to exist.
"How do we stop them?" Lyra demanded.
"The ritual requires all seventy-two circles to be active simultaneously. Destroy even one, and the timing fails." Corvus gathered the papers. "Of course, the Unbound has prepared for that possibility. The circles are guarded, trapped, and linked to each other. Destroying one triggers the others."
"But not immediately," Kael said, reading the relevant passage. "There's a delay. Minutes, maybe less, but enough time to escape the immediate blast radius."
"Enough time to escape, yes. But the shockwave would still propagate. You'd need to be far underground to survive."
"The tunnels," Lyra said. "The Mortician's Guild passages extend throughout the entire city. If we could get below the blast zone—"
"It's possible." Corvus folded his hands. "It's also suicide. You'd have one chance. No preparation, no second attempts. You'd need to hit every circle in sequence, destroying them before the Unbound realizes what's happening and triggers the ritual early."
Kael looked at the list of locations. Seventeen sites, scattered across Thornwick and its surroundings. Seventeen targets, each heavily defended.
"I can do it," he said.
"Kael—" Lyra started.
"Not alone. I'll need help. Someone who knows the city, knows the Order's tactics, can handle the guards while I work."
"That's a lot of conditions."
"That's what it takes."
Corvus studied him with those ancient eyes. "You understand what you're proposing? Even if you succeed, the Unbound will know who to hunt. Your face, your abilities, your location—all of it will become known. You'll have the Orders, the cult, and whoever else is pulling strings coming after you."
"I know."
"And you're willing to become the most wanted man in Thornwick?"
Kael closed the journal. "I was already on my way there. At least this way, I get to choose why."
---
The safe house had been abandoned for hours by the time they returned. Fresh footprints in the dust. Disturbed furniture. Whoever had searched the place had been thorough.
"Someone's been here," Lyra said, drawing her knives.
"I see it." Kael moved through the apartment, senses extended, searching for the presence that had invaded their space. The Soul Reaver ability wasn't limited to corpses—he could sense residual spiritual signatures, echoes left by strong emotions or abilities.
There. A thread of cold that didn't belong.
He followed it to the window, where he found what the intruder had left behind: a single black feather, resting on the sill.
"Shadow Court calling card," Lyra said when she saw it. Her face had gone pale. "They know I'm here."
"What does it mean?"
"It means I need to leave. Tonight. If they traced me to this location, they'll have traced everything else. My contacts, my safe houses, my—" She stopped, swallowing hard. "My sister."
Kael caught her arm before she could bolt for the door. "Wait. Think. Running now just confirms their suspicions. If you disappear, they'll assume you're compromised and act accordingly."
"And if I stay?"
"They might hesitate. Might think you're gathering intelligence instead of running."
Lyra laughed, but there was no humor in it. "You don't know the Shadow Court. They're not the hesitating type."
"Then we give them something else to focus on." Kael held up the feather. "A common enemy. The Unbound is threatening everything—including whatever plans the Shadow Court has for this city. Point them at the cult instead of each other."
"And how do we do that?"
"The journal. We release what we've learned—selectively. Make it seem like the Shadow Court uncovered the conspiracy. They'll get credit, they'll get resources, and in the confusion, you'll have room to move."
Lyra stared at him. "You'd give up our only advantage?"
"I'm trading information for time. There's a difference." He tucked the feather into his coat. "The cult's compound—where is it?"
"I don't know. That's not my territory."
"But someone knows."
"Probably." Lyra's expression shifted, calculation replacing panic. "If we release the journal through the right channels, the Unbound will panic. They'll move to protect their assets, which means revealing their network. And if the Shadow Court is watching that network..."
"Then we find the compound."
"And walk right into a trap."
"Maybe." Kael moved to the window, scanning the street below. Normal traffic. Normal people. But the feeling of being watched hadn't left him since they'd entered the apartment. "Or maybe we set the trap ourselves."
He could feel Lyra thinking, weighing risks against options. The Shadow Court had made her, trained her, turned her into a weapon. Now they wanted to destroy her because she might have seen something she shouldn't have, or because her usefulness had expired.
The same thing that had happened to him. The same game played by people who believed they controlled the board.
"The Mortician's Guild," Lyra said finally. "They keep records of everything that moves through their facilities. If the Unbound is using the tunnels, there'll be discrepancies in the manifests. Goods moving that shouldn't be there."
"Can you access those records?"
"Not directly. But I know someone who can—for the right price."
Kael nodded. "Then we have a plan."
"We have the beginning of a plan. The Unbound, the Shadow Court, the Orders—they're all moving pieces on a board we can't see. We're trying to play a game we don't understand."
"Sounds like every day since I woke up."
Lyra almost smiled. "At least you're used to it."
The rain had stopped sometime during their conversation. Kael looked out at the city emerging from the storm, gaslights flickering through the mist, steam vents releasing clouds of white into the night air.
Seventy-two rifts. Seventy-two seals. And somewhere in the darkness, forces were moving to tear them all open.
He would stop them. Or he would die trying.
Either way, the game was about to change.
---
The footsteps came from the alley behind them.
Kael spun, blade drawn, positioning himself between the sound and Lyra. Shadows moved in the darkness between buildings—more than two, less than a dozen. The whisper of steel being drawn echoed off wet brick.
"Company," he muttered.
"Looks like the compound isn't the only trap in town." Lyra's knives appeared in her hands, held low and ready. "Could be Shadow Court. Could be Unbound. Could be both."
"Neither option excites me."
"Me neither."
The footsteps stopped. Silence filled the alley, broken only by the drip of water from the rooftops and the distant rumble of steam engines.
Then a voice, calm and measured, cutting through the darkness: "Kael Ashborn. The Soul Reaver of Thornwick."
Kael's grip tightened on his sword. The voice belonged to someone educated, confident, used to commanding attention. Someone who had been waiting for this moment.
"We've been looking for you," the voice continued. "The cult speaks of you with fear. The Orders speak of you with hatred. Even the Abyssal Entities seem to recognize your name."
"Who are you?"
Footsteps again—but this time, approaching. A figure emerged from the shadows, tall and lean, wearing a coat that blended perfectly with the darkness. A scar ran down one side of his face, from temple to jaw, pulling his lips into a permanent half-smile.
"My name is Dorian Vex. I represent certain... Interests that wish to see the seal network maintained." The man stopped ten feet away, hands visible at his sides. "The Unbound wants to open the rifts. We want to keep them closed. It seems we have common cause."
"And if I refuse your offer?"
Dorian's half-smile widened. "Then the Unbound wins, the world burns, and everyone you care about dies in the chaos. Including the woman beside you."
Kael felt Lyra tense. Behind them, the shadows shifted—their backup, still waiting.
"Talk fast," Kael said. "What exactly are you proposing?"
Dorian reached into his coat and withdrew a sheaf of papers—more documents, different from the journal but bearing the same cipher. "The Twilight Order has been compromised, but not completely. There are those of us who still remember our duty. We have intelligence on the Unbound's operations, including the location of their compound."
"In exchange for what?"
"Your abilities. We need a Reaver who can harvest information from the cult's prisoners—people we can't access without triggering alarms. You take what we need, we provide the targets, and together we dismantle the conspiracy before it's too late."
Kael studied the man, searching for deception. Dorian's spiritual signature was masked, deliberately suppressed, but there was a presence beneath the concealment that felt... Familiar. Like something he'd encountered before.
"The cult's compound," Kael said slowly. "What happens when we hit it?"
"Chaos. The Unbound's leadership scatters, their communication network breaks down, their timing fails. The planetary alignment passes without the ritual completing, and the seals remain intact."
"For now."
Dorian inclined his head. "For now. But the Unbound will have been dealt a blow they won't soon recover from. And you'll have earned the gratitude of people who can protect you from the consequences of being what you are."
Kael glanced at Lyra. Her expression was unreadable, but her stance had shifted slightly—less defensive, more curious.
"The Shadow Court knows I'm here," Lyra said to Dorian. "Your people set them on me."
"The Shadow Court was a necessary inconvenience. They knew too much, moved too aggressively. We needed them distracted while we focused on the larger threat."
"And my sister?"
The question hung in the air. Dorian's expression didn't change, but something flickered in his eyes—calculation, or maybe regret.
"Your sister is safe. For now. Help us stop the Unbound, and I can guarantee her continued safety."
Lyra's knives lowered slightly. "You can't guarantee that."
"No. But I can guarantee that if the Unbound succeeds, no one survives—including her. The Entity that tears through the rift won't distinguish between Shadow Court operatives and common citizens. It will consume everything."
The logic was sound, even if Kael didn't trust the source. The Unbound needed to be stopped, and Dorian's organization had resources they lacked. Working together made tactical sense.
But cooperation meant exposure. It meant trusting people who had already demonstrated willingness to sacrifice others for their goals.
"Show me the documents," Kael said finally. "The intelligence on the compound. If it checks out, we talk terms."
Dorian smiled—a cold expression that didn't reach his eyes. "I knew you'd be reasonable. This way. We have a safe house nearby."
Kael followed, Lyra at his side, the shadows trailing behind. His hand never left his sword.
The game had changed. New pieces had entered the board.
But the objective remained the same: survive, stop the Unbound, and protect what little remained of his humanity.
The compound waited.
The ritual countdown continued.
And somewhere in the depths of Thornwick, seventy-two seals trembled on the edge of failure.
