The blood had barely dried on his palms when Kael Ashborn heard the whistles.
Three of them. The shrill, piercing shriek of Awakened-enforcement flares splitting the Thornwick night. He counted the beats between each blast. Long intervals meant distance. Short intervals meant they were close. The third whistle sang its dying note, and silence swallowed the alley.
That silence pressed against his eardrums, thick and suffocating. The copper tang of spilled blood still coated the back of his throat, mingling with the rotting-fish stench of the Thames drifting through the damp air.
Kael wiped his hands on his coat, the fabric already stiffening with drying gore. The coarse material scraped against his raw skin, sending a shiver up his forearms. The body at his feet had stopped being useful the moment the last spark of soul-essence drained into his palms. Some Cultist lackey. Stage-two, maybe stage-three. Not worth the risk of lingering.
He moved.
Cobblestones slick with evening dew echoed beneath his boots. The gas lamps along Fester Alley cast pools of sickly yellow, punctuated by stretches of impenetrable shadow. The glass casings hissed and popped, burning off residual moisture from the evening fog. Kael had learned to love those shadows. They didn't ask questions. They didn't care that he reeked of harvested death.
Another flare screamed to life somewhere behind him, this time accompanied by the rhythmic thunder of armored boots. The Iron Vanguard's enforcers. They'd gotten faster since the last time he'd slipped their net.
The narrow passage between the tannery and the old grain warehouse loomed ahead. The rancid stench of animal fat and lye from the tannery slapped him across the face, stinging his eyes. Kael hit the corner at a sprint, shoulder brushing the rough brick, and launched himself into the gap. He had four seconds before they rounded the same turn.
He used three of them.
The passage emptied onto Drowning Wharf, where the river stretched black and oily beneath a moonless sky. The docks stretched before him, their wooden planks groaning under the weight of midnight commerce. Smugglers, fishermen, the occasional corpse floating downstream. Perfect cover for a man who harvested the dead.
Kael slowed to a fast walk, letting his breathing settle. His heart hammered against his ribs, but his hands had stopped shaking three alleys back. That was the gift of practice. The body's panic faded when the mind knew exactly what came next.
He found the rowboat where the message had promised. A simple wooden craft, unremarkable, tied to a rotting post with fraying rope. The hull glistened with river scum, and a single oar lay across the thwarts. No note. No sender. Coordinates and a time.
The Seal-Breakers had been leaving him gifts for two months now. Dead Cultists on his doorstep. Locations of supply caches. Once, a whole cart of soul-essence vials, the genuine article, not the synthetic garbage the Veiled Market peddled. Someone wanted him equipped. Someone wanted him hunting.
Kael didn't know why. He didn't care to ask.
He cut the rope and pushed off, letting the current take him downstream. The river churned black and cold beneath him, carrying the city's filth toward the sea. The boat rocked, and freezing water sloshed over the gunwales, soaking through his boots and biting at his ankles. Above, the stars burned indifferent behind a veil of coal smoke.
For the first time in weeks, the sirens fell silent.
He drifted for an hour before beaching on the southern bank, somewhere in the marshlands that marked the city's edge. The boat scraped against muddy stone, and Kael stepped out, boots sinking into the soft earth. The mud squelched, gripping his heels with cold, greedy fingers.
The marsh stretched in all directions, broken by the silver ribbons of tidal channels and the skeletal fingers of dead trees. No gas lamps here. No enforcers. Croaks of frogs and the distant cry of night herons punctuated the silence.
Kael sat on the boat's stern, letting his legs dangle in the water. The cold seeped through his boots, sharp and clarifying. He needed to think.
The Cultist he'd harvested tonight had been a messenger. He'd carried documents in a leather satchel, the kind couriers used for short-range communication. Kael had taken both the satchel and the man's final memories before the soul-fragment dissolved.
The memories came in fragments. Impressions than narratives. A chamber lit by green fire. Six figures hooded and kneeling. A voice like grinding stone speaking coordinates—latitude and longitude that Kael didn't recognize—and a date.
Three days from now. The old temple ruins near the Greymoor Pass.
The Six Orders meet in secret, the Cultist's memory whispered. The seals weaken faster than they admit.
Kael had known the seals were failing. He'd felt them, deep in his chest, a vibration that never disappeared. But confirmation from the other side? That was new.
He reached into the satchel and withdrew the documents. The leather was cracked and water-stained, but the ink remained legible. Names. Dates. Locations of other Cult cells throughout the kingdom.
This was the network. This was organization.
Someone had spent years building a coordinated effort to breach the seals, and they were close. Closer than the Orders realized. The Great Rift wasn't just cracking—it was being actively dismantled.
Kael read through the documents twice, memorizing every detail. The parchment crinkled beneath his fingertips, smelling of mildew and old ink. Then he tore them into pieces and scattered them in the current.
Let the enforcers chase ghosts. Let the Orders scramble to plug holes that had already been widened. Kael had washed his hands of their war the moment they'd signed his death warrant.
He stood, joints popping, and surveyed the marsh. Dawn was still hours away. The Seal-Breakers' gifts had served their purpose—Kael had learned more about the Cult's operations in two months than the Orders had uncovered in two years. He knew where they trained, where they sourced their soul-essence, which corrupt officials took their coin.
Knowledge was power. Power was leverage. And leverage was the only currency Kael trusted.
But knowledge without action was trivia. The Cult would continue their work whether he intervened or not. The seals would continue to crack. The Entities would continue to press against their prisons, whispering promises to anyone desperate enough to listen.
None of it required his participation. The world had executed him once. He saw no reason to save it now.
He found the abandoned cottage as the first gray light crept over the horizon. A simple structure, stone walls half-collapsed, roof long rotted away. Perfect.
Kael entered through a gap where the door had been, stepping over shattered glass and decades of accumulated debris. The glass crunched beneath his boots, loud as breaking bones in the stillness. The hearth was cold but dry. He gathered what remained of the old furniture—wooden chairs, a broken table, split logs that had escaped the rot—and struck a flame from his palm.
Soul-fire. A gift from a Herald he'd reaved six months ago. The flames burned blue-green and cold, casting no heat but enough light to see by. The fire crackled, releasing a faint scent of ozone and something sweeter, like crushed lilacs.
He settled against the wall, letting the fire illuminate his hands. They were scarred across every palm, the telltale marks of a Soul Reaver. Every harvest left its signature, a web of silver lines that never faded. He'd stopped counting the marks after the first hundred.
The hunger came with sunset.
Not physical hunger—this was something deeper, something that coiled in his chest and whispered in a language older than words. The Reaver's thirst. The need to touch death and draw out its secrets.
Most Reavers fed this hunger with the dying. The desperate, the criminal, the convenient. They harvested from the weak because the weak couldn't fight back. Kael had done the same, once. In the early days, when he hadn't known better, when he'd thought the ability was a gift than a curse.
Now he understood what the Reaver truly was. A tool. A weapon. Nothing more.
The thirst didn't care about philosophy. It demanded satisfaction.
Kael stood and walked to the cottage's rear wall, where a corpse lay waiting. He'd placed it there the previous evening, before his hunt. A Cultist. Stage-three. The fresher the source, the cleaner the harvest.
He knelt and placed both palms against the cooling flesh.
The contact sparked immediate response. The soul-fragment activated, recognizing kindred essence, and Kael felt the familiar sensation of falling inward. His awareness split, half anchored in the physical world, half drowning in someone else's final moments.
The Cultist had been named Marrick. Thirty-two years old. Awakened at seventeen, passed the trials for the Order of Iron before washing out at Stage four. Discharged for "spiritual instability"—the polite term for breaking under the weight of what the Abyss showed you.
Marrick had spent eight years drinking himself toward oblivion before the Cult found him. They'd offered purpose. Acceptance. A cause greater than his own failures.
Kael felt the man's desperation like a physical weight. The need to belong. The hunger for significance. The Cult had fed both with skillful precision.
Then the memory shifted, and Kael saw what Marrick had seen three days before his death.
A chamber beneath Thornwick's old cathedral. Six hooded figures standing in a circle, each representing one of the Orders. Not Cultists—Order members. High-ranking. The kind who attended Council meetings and pretended to hate each other.
The Whisper King stood at the circle's center.
Kael's breath caught.
He couldn't see the figure's face—the hood obscured everything—but he could feel the presence. Ancient. Patient. Impossibly powerful.
The Whisper King spoke, and Marrick's memory captured every word.
"The seals will not hold another year. When they break, the Entities will pour through, and we will be ready. The Six Orders will fall. The old powers will rise. And from the ashes, a new world will be born."
One of the hooded figures—a woman, her robes trimmed in gold—stepped forward. "The Soul Reaver complicates matters. His interference has cost us three cells in the past quarter."
"The Reaver is a tool," the Whisper King replied. "Like all tools, it can be used or discarded. The Faceless One has already begun the flow of alignment. When the time comes, he will serve our purposes whether he wills it or not."
Kael's hands spasmed against the corpse. His knuckles went white, tendons straining against the skin. The harvest sputtered, threatening to collapse, but he forced himself to hold on. There had to be more. There had to be—
The memory fragmented. Marrick's final moments flooded through—terror, confusion, the sensation of being watched. Someone had discovered him. Someone had silenced him before he could report what he'd seen.
The harvest ended. Kael slumped backward, palms burning, head pounding with borrowed pain. The cottage floor pressed cold against his back, rough stone biting through his coat.
He stared at the cottage ceiling, counting his breaths until the world stopped spinning.
The Faceless One. The second Soul Reaver. The paradox that haunted every moment of his existence since the power had first awakened.
The Whisper King knew about him. Knew his nature. And apparently, considered him a future asset.
That changed things.
Kael had spent the past months convinced he was hunting the Cult. A lone wolf picking off isolated prey while the Orders slept and the seals crumbled. He'd felt righteous in his isolation. Special, even. A necessary evil in a world too broken to save itself.
Now he knew better. He wasn't hunting anyone. He was being managed.
The Cult—the real Cult, not the expendable cells he'd been butchering—was led by someone who understood his power better than he did. Someone who had a second Reaver waiting in the wings. Someone who expected him to align, willingly or otherwise.
The hunger coiled tighter in his chest. Another harvest. Soon.
Kael pushed himself upright, ignoring the tremor in his limbs. The sun had risen while he'd been under, light streaming through the gaps in the ruined walls. Dust motes swirled in the pale beams, dancing their slow, oblivious waltz. He'd lost hours to that memory-dive.
He needed to move. The cottage wasn't safe—they'd find him eventually, the Cult or the Orders or both. His pattern was becoming predictable. The Seal-Breakers' gifts had drawn attention.
But where? The roads out of Thornwick were watched. The river crossings had checkpoints. Even the old smuggler routes were compromised, crawling with enforcers looking for deserters and criminals.
Kael pulled himself together, gathered the remains of his fire, and stepped outside.
The marsh stretched quiet and gray. Somewhere in that vast nothing, the old temple ruins waited. Three days. The Orders meeting in secret. The Cult planning their final assault on the seal network.
He should walk away. Let them destroy each other. Find some forgotten corner of the kingdom and wait for the world to end. That was the smart play. The safe play.
The hunger disagreed.
It wanted more. More harvests. More memories. More power to feed the endless void where his soul should be.
Kael started walking.
The Greymoor Pass took three days on foot. Kael covered the distance in two, traveling only at night, sleeping in shifts during the day. The hunger drove him forward like a beast on a leash, through forests and across rivers and up rocky slopes that left his lungs burning.
He didn't eat. Didn't rest properly. The Reaver's thirst had evolved into something closer to obsession, and every step brought him closer to answers he wasn't sure he wanted.
Pine needles crunched beneath his boots, releasing their sharp, clean scent into air that grew thinner with every upward step. Wind cut through the pass, keening between the peaks, numbing his cheeks and gnawing at his fingertips. Below, the valley floors disappeared into mist, swallowed whole by gray nothing.
The temple ruins appeared at dusk on the second day, perched on a ridge overlooking the pass. Ancient stones, weathered by centuries of wind and rain, arranged in patterns that predated the Orders by a thousand years. This had been a holy site before the Abyssal Wars. A place where the barriers between worlds ran thin.
Now it was a meeting place for traitors.
Kael arrived an hour after sunset, scaling the cliff face on the ruins' eastern side. His fingers found purchase in cracks and crevices, muscles screaming as he pulled himself up hand over hand. The rock face scraped his knuckles raw, leaving smears of blood on cold stone. The Demigod stage gave him strength beyond mortal limits, but even that had boundaries.
He crested the ridge and pressed himself flat against cold stone. His breath plumed white in the frigid air. Below, the ruins exhaled dampness and decay, the scent of moss and crumbling mortar rising through the gloom.
In the courtyard at the temple's heart, figures gathered. Twelve of them, arranged in a circle around a central altar. Six wore the robes of the Six Orders—Dawn, Iron, Arcane, Veil, Blood, and Shadow. The other six were hooded, their faces hidden behind masks of white porcelain.
The Faceless Ones. Kael's skin crawled.
The meeting had already begun. Voices rose and fell, words carrying on the night wind, but Kael was too far away to hear clearly. He needed to get closer.
He moved along the ridge, finding a path carved into the rock in ages past. The steps were uneven, designed for feet far smaller than his, but they served their purpose. He descended until he was directly above the circle, thirty feet up, clinging to an outcropping that gave him a clear view of the altar.
The Whisper King stood at the circle's center.
This time, the hood fell back.
Kael's breath stopped.
The face beneath was familiar. Impossibly, undeniably familiar. He'd seen that face in mirrors for twenty-eight years. The same sharp jaw. The same hollow cheeks. The same scar running from temple to chin, though this version was older, deeper, weathered by time.
"My children," the Whisper King said, his voice carrying without amplification, "the hour approaches. The seal at Rift Seventeen will fall within the month. When it does, the Faceless One will manifest, and the new age will begin."
Kael's mind reeled. His pulse roared in his ears, drowning out thought.
The Whisper King wore his face. Or, he wore an older version of it, aged by decades of corruption and power. The same genetic structure. The same bone structure.
He was looking at his future. Or his past. Or something far worse.
"The Soul Reaver suspects," one of the masked figures said. "He has been disrupting our cells for months."
"Let him." The Whisper King smiled, and the expression transformed his features into something alien. Something hungry. "Every action he takes serves our design. Every death he causes weakens the seal network further. When the time comes, his power will be exactly what we need."
Kael's hands clenched on the stone. His nails drew blood, the sharp pain grounding him in the moment. The revelation burned through him like acid, dissolving everything he thought he knew.
The Cult didn't want to control him. They wanted to use him. Everything he'd done—the harvests, the kills, the disruption—had been feeding their plan. Every corpse he'd left behind had weakened the seals. Every Cultist he'd murdered had been a sacrifice, willingly or not.
He'd been their weapon all along.
The realization drove him forward. He stepped onto the outcropping's edge, balance precarious, and opened his mouth to shout—to expose them, to shatter the meeting, to do something—
The stone crumbled beneath his foot.
Kael fell.
The world compressed into a series of frozen moments: the rush of wind tearing at his coat, the shouts of alarm from below, his own scream ripping at his throat. He twisted in midair, seeking any purchase, any edge—
His fingers caught the altar's edge.
The impact nearly tore his arms from their sockets. His body swung like a pendulum, feet dangling over the circle, and a dozen faces turned upward to stare.
The Whisper King looked up.
Their eyes met.
"Ah," the ancient figure said. "You've come to join us."
Kael hung there, suspended between salvation and damnation, and understood at last what it meant to walk away from the world.
The world had already walked away from him.
Below, the Faceless Ones began to climb.
