"Forget the perimeter! They're inside the circle!"
Kael's blade caught the gaslight as he spun, steel screaming against a curved cultist blade. Three shapes materialized from the steam fog, moving with the fluid coordination of a rehearsed dance. Not random cultists. Soldiers.
His left hand shot out, catching the throat of the nearest corpse still twitching on the cobblestones. The Soul Reaver pulsed hungry and eager beneath his skin.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 3.2 spiritual units from the remains. Memory Fragment acquired: Cell Coordination Pattern Delta-7.]
Not enough. He needed more.
"Flank formation!" Sera's voice cut through the chaos. She stood at the alley's mouth, twin daggers gleaming with arterial red, her Mortician's Guild coat torn at the shoulder where a blade had kissed too close. Blood seeped from the wound, but her stance remained steady. "We walked into a prepared killbox."
Kael ducked a thrusting spear, felt the weapon's tip part the air where his skull had been a heartbeat before. The Transcendent stage sang through his meridians, his perception expanded to capture every micro-movement of his attackers. They were fast. Enhanced. A rank of Abyssal-touched warriors wearing stolen Iron Vanguard armor.
The second attacker came in low with a serrated hatchet. Kael pivoted, letting the blow whistle past his hip, then drove his elbow into the man's floating ribs. Cartilage cracked. The cultist folded. Kael dropped beside him, palm pressing against cooling flesh.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 7.8 spiritual units from the remains. Combat Memory acquired: Iron Vanguard Grappling Counter.]
Better. Much better.
"Kael!" Sera's warning came half a second before the rooftop exploded upward in a shower of tiles and screaming metal. A figure plummeted through the gap, black robes spreading like crow wings, a corrupted seal burning. The Whisper's Apostle had found them.
Not just any Apostle. This one bore the Mark of the Severing—a tattooed sigil that Kael recognized from his own fragmented memories of the execution grounds. One of the men who had watched him die.
"You were supposed to be a corpse," the Apostle said, voice layered with harmonic distortion. "The reports clearly stated you were a corpse."
Kael rose from the corpse, his new memory slotting into place like a key finding its lock. Iron Vanguard grappling techniques, the muscle memory of a hundred training sessions burning through his nervous system. He settled into a fighting stance, weight balanced, center low.
"Reports can be wrong."
"Can they?" The Apostle raised one hand. Shadow pooled at the fingertips, thickening into a blade of condensed darkness. "I suppose we'll find out."
The cultists pressed forward, tightening the noose. Kael counted six on the ground, two on the adjacent rooftops, and the Apostle making nine total. Nine against three. Bad odds even for Awakened fighters, and two of his team weren't Awakened at all—just skilled mortals with more courage than sense.
"Sera. Thorne. Back to back."
They moved without question, three points of a collapsing triangle holding against the rising tide. Sera's daggers sang their lethal aria, finding throats and eyes with mechanical precision. Thorne—built like a steam engine, scarred like a veteran—swung his mechanical arm with devastating force, each punch backed by hydraulic pressure that crushed bone and bent armor.
The Apostle waited. Watching. Reading.
Kael understood the tactic. The cultists weren't meant to kill them directly. They were meant to tire, to bleed, to open wounds that the Apostle would then exploit. A coordinated weakening before the killing blow.
"Cell Coordination Pattern Delta-7," Kael muttered, sifting through his harvested memory. "They're rotating in threes. Fresh attackers every thirty seconds to maintain pressure."
"Useful intel," Sera grunted, sidestepping a thrust and opening the attacker's femoral artery. "How did you—"
"Later. Thorne, when they next rotate, hit the gap on your left. Sera, slide right. I need thirty seconds."
"With what?" Sera demanded.
Kael didn't answer. He was already moving toward the nearest corpse, a cultist with a shattered skull leaking brain matter onto the ancient cobblestones. Not fresh—maybe an hour old—but still within the window. His palm found the cooling forehead.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 1.4 spiritual units from the remains. Degraded Memory acquired: Whisper Protocol Alpha.]
Fragments of communication passed through his mind. Fragments of orders. "The Soul Reaver must not reach the lower archives. Contain and eliminate. The Shadow Court has expressed particular interest in this asset. Repeat: particular interest."
The Shadow Court. Kael's blood chilled despite the heat of combat. That name shouldn't appear in cultist planning. The Shadow Court was supposed to be a deniable operations arm, funding conflicts through proxies, never showing its face. But here they were, mentioned in the same breath as kill orders for Kael specifically.
"Enough!" The Apostle swept forward, darkness-blade carving through the air in a horizontal arc that would have taken Kael's head if he hadn't already been moving. The blade carved a line of shadow through the gaslight, leaving an afterimage that burned in Kael's retinas.
Kael countered with an elbow strike that connected with the Apostle's wrist, hyperextending the joint with a sound like a snapped branch. The darkness-blade flickered, dissipated, reformed. The Apostle hissed—a sound like steam escaping through a cracked pipe—and kicked Kael in the chest.
The impact sent him staggering back three steps. His lungs burned. His ribs complained, at least one cracked. The Transcendent body was resilient, but it wasn't invincible. The Apostle had access to Abyssal power, and that power hurt.
"Impressive resistance," the Apostle said. "But you're still just Transcendent. I can taste the limit of your soul from here. One more exchange. Maybe two. Then you'll break."
Kael wiped blood from the corner of his mouth. "You've been watching too many theater performances. The villain monologue is getting old."
"Maybe." The Apostle smiled, revealing teeth filed to points. "But you'll still die."
The cultists surged forward again, a fresh wave replacing the exhausted first rank. Sera was bleeding from three separate wounds now, her movements growing sluggish. Thorne had taken a spear through the meat of his thigh, the weapon still embedded, limiting his mobility. They were losing. Every second that passed saw them closer to the edge of the cliff.
Kael made a decision.
He stopped defending.
The cultist's blade descended toward his shoulder, aimed at severing the tendon. Kael rotated into the strike, letting the weapon slice through cloth and skin, opening a wound that painted the cobblestones crimson. At the same moment, his left hand seized the cultist's weapon arm, twisted, and drove his blade up through the man's chin.
The corpse dropped. Kael dropped with it, both hands pressing against the fresh wound, against the blood still pumping from the severed vessels.
[Kritical Wound Taken: -12% physical functionality. Soul Reaver harvesting initiated.]
He didn't care about the wound. He cared about the harvest.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 15.2 spiritual units from the remains. Combat Memory acquired: Abyssal Enhancement Protocol.]
Information flooded through him. Training regimes. Enhancement rituals. The cult's understanding of the Abyssal energies that powered their abilities. This cultist had been an enforcer, one of the inner circle, and his memory contained details that the Apostle would never willingly share.
"The cells aren't independent," Kael said, rising from the corpse. Blood soaked his shirt, but the wound was already closing, the Transcendent body knitting itself back together with unnatural speed. "They're networked. Someone is coordinating your movements in real-time."
The Apostle's expression flickered. Just for a moment. But Kael caught it.
"I can taste your fear," Kael continued, advancing. "Someone told you I was dangerous. Someone told you I might know things. But you don't know what I know, do you? You're just another puppet dancing on strings."
"You're bluffing."
"Am I?" Kael raised one hand, and the Soul Reaver responded, a tendril of spectral energy coiling around his forearm like a hungry serpent. "I've harvested your people. I know about the Shadow Court's interest. I know about the coordinated network. I know you're not fighting to win—you're fighting to delay me until someone else arrives."
The Apostle's poise cracked, just slightly. "You're lying."
"Maybe." Kael smiled, and felt no warmth in it. "But if I'm not, you have approximately ten seconds to decide whether you want to be standing here when I start taking apart this network. Because I will take it apart. Every cell. Every coordinator. Every link in the chain. And I will harvest every single one of them."
Silence fell over the alley. The cultists hesitated, weapons half-raised, looking to their leader for guidance. Sera and Thorne used the respite to catch their breath, pressing back-to-back, watching the standoff with expressions caught between hope and horror.
Finally, the Apostle spoke. "The Shadow Court will hear of this."
"Good." Kael's smile widened, sharp and predatory. "Tell them I'm coming for them next."
For a long moment, nothing happened. Then the Apostle barked a single word in a language that predated the Orders, and the cultists dissolved into the steam and shadows like smoke dispersing in wind. The Apostle lingered for a heartbeat longer, eyes locked on Kael's, before melting into the darkness between two buildings.
Gone. All of them.
Kael stood in the sudden silence, chest heaving, blood dripping from his shoulder onto the cobblestones. The wound had mostly healed, but the pain remained—a reminder that even Transcendent flesh had limits.
"Kael." Sera's voice trembled. "What did you do?"
"I scared them." He turned to face his team, his expression carefully neutral. "The Apostle was feeding information to someone. A coordinator. They were running a planned ambush, which means this isn't random cult activity—it's organized resistance. Someone knew we were coming. Someone knew our route."
"The Shadow Court mentioned your name specifically," Sera said. "That's not random."
"No. It's not." Kael looked at her, then at Thorne, weighing what he could safely share. "We need to change our approach. The cells are networked, which means they're communicating. If we hit one, the others will know. We need to be faster, more decisive. Hit them before they can coordinate."
Thorne spat blood onto the cobblestones. "And how exactly do we manage that when they're expecting us?"
"We use what I harvested." Kael rolled his shoulder, testing the joint. The pain was manageable. "I have their enhancement protocols. Their coordination patterns. Their understanding of how the Abyssal energy flows through their network. I can predict their movements now, at least partially."
"And you can trust what you harvested?" Sera asked. "Memories can be planted. False information can be embedded in cultivation techniques."
"Everything can be lies." Kael met her gaze. "But some lies are more useful than others. And right now, I have a choice between trusting fragments from the dead or trusting nothing at all. I'll take my chances with the dead."
The sounds of distant shouts echoed from the main thoroughfare. City Watch responding to the noise, probably. They had minutes at best before the area flooded with authorities who would ask uncomfortable questions about the corpses.
"We need to move," Sera said. "There are three more cells on our list, but if they know we're coming—"
"They don't know we're coming." Kael picked up his blade, wiped the blood on his trouser leg. "They know we escaped an ambush. They'll be expecting us to regroup, lick our wounds, change tactics. They won't expect us to press forward immediately."
"That's insane."
"Yes." Kael started walking toward the alley's mouth. "But so is walking into Thornwick in the first place. We're already committed to insane. Might as well lean into it."
Thorne laughed—a rough, broken sound that turned into a cough. "Boss, you might be the craziest person I've ever followed."
"Probably." Kael glanced back. "Question is—are you still following?"
Thorne limped forward, mechanical arm hanging at his side, hydraulic fluid leaking from a ruptured joint. "Until the bitter end. Or until the next ambush. Whichever comes first."
Sera hesitated, then followed. "My sister. If the Shadow Court is involved, she might be in more danger than we thought. I need to find her before they realize she's connected to this investigation."

Kael led them through the winding streets of Thornwick's industrial district, past steam vents and coal furnaces, past the rattling trams that connected the city's disparate quarters. "But first, we finish what we started. The cult cells need to be neutralized before they can coordinate a response. Once we're sure they can't mount a second ambush, we can focus on the personal stakes."
"And if there is no safe moment?" Sera asked. "If the Shadow Court keeps throwing resources at us until we're dead?"
Kael didn't answer immediately. He was too busy sorting through the harvested memories, searching for patterns, connections, weaknesses. The Abyssal Enhancement Protocol contained details about the cult's spiritual architecture—a network of linked souls feeding power to a central coordinator. Sever the link, and the network would collapse.
"There will be a moment," he finally said. "There always is. We just have to survive long enough to find it."
They turned a corner into a narrow side street, one of dozens identical in the industrial maze. Warehouses lined both sides, their facades blank, their windows shuttered. Perfect places to hide. Perfect places to set another ambush.
Kael raised his hand, signaling a halt.
"Movement ahead," he said quietly. "Two figures. Watching the warehouse on the left."
Sera and Thorne froze, eyes scanning the shadows. "Where?"
"There." Kael pointed. "Third floor. Corner window. They're using some kind of concealment technique—I can barely see them. But they're definitely there."
"A rear guard?" Thorne guessed. "Watching to see if we regroup?"
"Possibly." Kael studied the figures, reaching for more harvested memories, trying to place the silhouette. "Or a second team. Sent to follow us after the first ambush failed."
"The cult has multiple teams operating in the same area?"

"I'm saying they sent people after what I can do." Kael touched his chest, where the Soul Reaver hummed beneath his skin. "They want the Reaver. Or they want it controlled. Either way, I'm valuable to them. Valuable enough to commit resources. Valuable enough to coordinate with cult cells to create killboxes."

"Can you do that?" Thorne asked. "Can you let them think they're winning?"
Kael considered the question. The harvested memories painted a picture of enemies who underestimated him. They knew he was dangerous, but they didn't know the full extent of his abilities. They didn't know about the Soul Reaver's zero-cost harvesting, or the way he could pull combat memories from the fallen and make them his own.
They thought he was just another Awakened. Powerful, yes. Threatening, certainly. But ultimately limited by the same rules that governed everyone else.
They were wrong.

The performance began. Kael lowered himself to one knee, pressing his hand against his mostly-healed shoulder wound. The blood was still fresh enough to leave a convincing stain on the cobblestones. He let his head bow, let his breathing sound ragged, let every detail scream weakness.
The figures in the window watched. He felt their attention like insects crawling across his skin.
Minutes passed. An eternity compressed into seconds.
Then one of the figures moved. Slid down from the window. Circled around the building's corner with footsteps that made no sound at all.
Professional. Trained. Patient.
The figure entered the alley from the opposite end, and Kael saw them clearly for the first time: a woman in gray leathers, face half-hidden by a hood, hands empty but ready to draw weapons that weren't visible. She moved like water, like smoke, like something that had never been fully human.
"Kael Ashborn." Her voice carried the soft accent of the southern provinces. "The Resurrected Butcher. The Soul Reaver. So many names for one man."
"Just one man," Kael replied, not rising from his knee. "You must be disappointed."

Kael rose slowly to his feet. "You ambushed my team."
"Standard operating procedure. We needed to assess your combat capabilities firsthand. The cultists were expendable."
"They're still people."

The cult sacrifices volunteers who believe they're serving a higher purpose. The Shadow Court sacrifices assets who know exactly what they're signing up for. Everyone uses people. We admit it."

"Your freedom, partially. Your loyalty, entirely. But in exchange, you'd have answers. About your power. About where it came from. About the people who created it." She tilted her head. "Don't you want to know? The Soul Reaver didn't appear from nowhere. Someone made it. Someone chose you. And that someone is still out there, watching, waiting. Wouldn't you like to know why?"
The words struck like a physical blow. Kael's hands clenched at his sides, the Soul Reaver surging beneath his skin, hungry for something it couldn't name. The Whisper King's cryptic warnings, the fragmented memories of his own execution, the mystery of his impossible resurrection—all of it pointed to a truth just beyond his reach.
"You're baiting me," he said.
"Every negotiation involves bait." She didn't flinch. "The question is whether you take it."
Kael considered the woman before him. Shadow Court operative. Professional. Probably lying about most of what she'd said. But the core offer—information about the Soul Reaver's origin—that was a hook he couldn't ignore. Not yet. Not until he understood what he was and why he existed.
"Tell me one thing," he said. "Something true. Something I can verify. Prove that the Shadow Court has what you're claiming."
"Very well." She reached into her coat and withdrew a small leather journal, bound in something that looked unsettlingly like skin. "This belonged to an Apostle who died forty years ago. He was investigating the origins of the Soul Reaver phenomenon—trying to understand where the first Reaver came from and why certain individuals were chosen to carry the ability. He compiled his findings here."
She held the journal out. "This is yours if you agree to the proposal."
Kael stared at the journal. Old. Genuine. Smelling faintly of dust and something older, something that made the Soul Reaver in his chest pulse with recognition.
"How do I know it's real?"
"You don't. But you can read the first three pages. If they're convincing, we continue the conversation. If not, you walk away, and we part as strangers."
The woman extended the journal further, arm steady, expression neutral. Behind her, in the window, the second figure watched—another operative, hands visible but ready, covering their associate.
Kael's mind raced through possibilities. Accept the journal, and he committed to a negotiation he didn't fully understand. Refuse it, and he walked away from potentially valuable information about his own nature.
The choice, like all choices in Thornwick, came down to risk and reward.
He reached for the journal.
