The thing wearing Lord Ashworth's face smiled with too many teeth.
Kael stumbled backward, boots scraping against the wet cobblestones. Rain hammered the broken awning above him, turning the narrow alley into a sluice of filth and moonlight. Behind him, the collapsed wall offered no escape—a pile of rubble blocked the passage, and ahead, the thing that had been Thornwick's senior magistrate took another step forward.
"Lord Ashworth sent me," Kael said, keeping his voice level. His hand rested on the knife at his belt, though steel meant nothing against what stood before him. "You know this. You were there when he gave the order."
The Ashworth-thing tilted its head at an angle that human vertebrae could not accommodate. Its eyes—pale blue in life, dulled by decades of bureaucratic tedium—now blazed with something that made Kael's stomach clench. Not rage. Not hunger. Recognition.
"The Ashworth boy is dead," it said. The voice layered over itself, harmonics building beneath the familiar tone like distant screaming. "Has been for six hours. The cult took him during the purge." A pause. "They took many during your purge, Soul Reaver."
Kael's blood froze. The entity wore its stolen memories like a costume, probing his reactions through borrowed expressions. Ashworth's face twisted into something like sympathy.
"Did you think the Shadow Court moved against you on their own initiative? No. They positioned assets. They fed intelligence. They let your little vendetta burn through Thornwick's defenses." The thing laughed, a wet gurgling sound. "And now the seal network bleeds while you chase phantoms through the Dockside."
"I'm not here for a lecture." Kael dropped into a combat stance, weight shifting to the balls of his feet. His spiritual senses unfurled, testing the entity's presence. The reading came back wrong—fragmented, like trying to grasp smoke. "You're an avatar. A fragment. The real entity is still bound."
"Bound." The thing savored the word. "For now. The membrane weakens with every purge, every 'righteous' crusade the Orders mount against their own shadows. How many seals did your war with the Shadow Court damage, Kael Ashborn? How many souls did you harvest that might have reinforced the network?"
The accusation landed like a blow. Kael's hands trembled—not with fear, but with the effort of holding back. Every word from that borrowed mouth confirmed what he'd suspected for weeks. The conflict consuming Thornwick served something larger. Something patient.
"I came here to kill cultists," he said. "Not listen to sermons from Abyssal trash."
"You're killing everyone," the entity replied. "The distinction stopped mattering centuries ago."
It moved.
The Ashworth-thing lunged, arm extending far beyond human range. Kael twisted aside, feeling the wind of corrupted fingers rake through the air where his throat had been. He countered with a blade strike aimed at the thing's wrist—but steel passed through the limb as if cutting fog. The entity's form wasn't fully solid. It existed somewhere between corpse and concept, anchored to the Material by stolen essence and dark will.
Kael retreated three steps, buying space to think. His Soul Sight painted the creature in sickly luminescence, revealing the threads of spiritual energy binding its borrowed flesh. The avatar drew power from two sources: the Abyss below, and the dead magistrate's soul fragments still clinging to the vessel.
"I can't destroy you directly," Kael said, more to himself than his opponent. "But I can starve you."
He reached inward, toward the cold reservoir of harvested power coiled in his spirit. Two months of kills—Shadow Court agents, cultist fanatics, the occasional Order operative who'd proven too persistent—all of it catalogued and waiting. The Soul Reaver's curse was also its gift. Where other Awakened burned through limited reserves, Kael could draw from an ocean of stolen essence.
He chose carefully. Not the combat skills—those he'd need for the cult's higher servants. Instead, he activated a lesser harvest: the binding knowledge extracted from a dying cultist acolyte three weeks ago. Binding rituals. Soul tethering. The theoretical framework that let fanatics anchor entities to mortal vessels.
The knowledge flooded through him, foreign and invasive. His fingers moved in patterns he had never learned, tracing sigils in the rain-soaked air. Spiritual energy—his own reserves, supplemented by the harvested essence—poured into the working.
The Ashworth-thing recoiled. Its borrowed face contorted with something approaching respect.
"You've been studying," it hissed. "The first Reaver's texts, no doubt. Stolen from some desperate archivist or murdered scholar."
"The knowledge exists. Someone preserved it." Kael's voice came out steadier than he felt. The binding ritual demanded concentration, each sigil requiring precise spiritual input. Too little and the working would fail. Too much and he'd burn through reserves he couldn't afford to waste. "That someone wasn't you."
"Foolish boy." The entity's form began to shift, flesh rippling as it tried to abandon the magistrate's corpse. "You think you understand what you're wielding? The Soul Reaver power was given to humanity as a weapon. A necessary evil during the Abyssal Wars when the Entities threatened to consume everything. But weapons rust. Covenants expire." The voice dropped to a whisper that filled the entire alley. "The original Reaver understood that. He chose to seal the power away than let it corrupt future generations. And yet here you are, dragging that same corruption back into the world."
Kael's hands stuttered. The mention of the original Reaver struck something deep in his chest—a hollow ache he couldn't name. The Sepulcher tablets mentioned a founder, a legend, a warning. But the entity spoke as though it had known the man personally.
"You knew him," Kael said. Not a question.
"I watched him die." The Ashworth-thing's face smoothed into an expression of profound melancholy. "Not killed—died. Chose to die than face what he'd become. The power consumed him, Kael. Every soul he harvested, every ability he claimed, added weight to a burden no mortal could bear indefinitely. In the end, he sacrificed himself to seal Soul Reaver away, hoping humanity had learned to defend itself without that particular crutch." A bitter smile. "It hadn't. And now you stand here, repeating his mistakes, convinced you're different."
"Different enough." Kael completed the final sigil. The binding formation snapped into place, a cage of spiritual energy that would prevent the avatar from drawing additional power from the Abyss. The creature screamed—a sound that came from everywhere and nowhere—and its form began to destabilize.
But it wasn't finished. The entity concentrated what remained of its essence into one final act. Ashworth's corpse collapsed, the stolen flesh unable to maintain coherence without spiritual support. From the heap rose a wisp of concentrated darkness,ing upward like smoke caught in reverse.
"Run back to your Order masters," the entity's voice came from the dissipating shadow. "Tell them the Faceless One sends his regards. Tell them the seventh seal has cracked. And tell them—" The voice rose to a shriek. "—the Reaver's heir will fall just as the first fell, because the Abyss always collects its debts!"
The wisp shattered against the invisible barrier of Kael's binding, fragmenting into a thousand motes of dying light. The alley fell silent except for the rain.
Kael stood motionless, breathing hard. His spiritual reserves had taken a significant hit from the binding ritual, and the harvested knowledge now sat uncomfortably in his mind—useful but not yet fully integrated. He would need time to process those stolen memories, to separate useful technique from the acolyte's corrupted worldview.
But first, he needed to deal with the corpse.
The magistrate's body lay crumpled on the cobblestones, rain washing away the unnatural pallor that had marked the entity's possession. Lord Ashworth—or what remained of him—would need a proper burial. The soul fragments had been consumed by the avatar's destruction, leaving behind only empty flesh.
Kael approached, kneeling beside the remains. The Soul Reaver's curse demanded tribute. Even in death, even with nothing left to give, the power within him reached out instinctively.
[System Notification] Soul Harvest initiated. Target: Lord Ashworth (deceased, several hours post-mortem). Corpse freshness: Degraded. Harvest quality: Minimal.
Kael placed his palm against the wet fabric of Ashworth's ruined coat. The harvesting sensation washed over him—not painful, exactly, but deeply invasive. He felt the boundaries of his consciousness expand to encompass the remains, rifling through whatever residue remained.
[System Notification] Soul Harvest: You extracted minor administrative memory (1) from the remains. Spiritual essence acquired: 2 units.
A trickle of information flowed into his awareness. Administrative details, mostly—schedules, budgets, the tedium of running a city's legal apparatus. Ashworth had been a bureaucrat to his core, even his final moments consumed by paperwork concerns. The harvest yielded nothing of combat value, nothing that would help against the cult's remaining forces.
But the flow itself revealed something else.
Kael's eyes widened. The binding ritual had worked better than expected because Ashworth's soul had already been partially consumed by the entity. The avatar had burned through the magistrate's spiritual reserves to maintain coherence, leaving behind a husk that was paradoxically easier to harvest. The entity had needed Ashworth's memories to impersonate him effectively. It had needed his social connections, his authority, his access to Thornwick's administrative secrets.
Which meant the cult had planned this ambush for weeks. Possibly months.
Kael withdrew his hand from the corpse, mind racing. The entity's final words echoed through his thoughts. The seventh seal. The original Reaver. The confirmation that his power had a history—a lineage of misuse and corruption that the Orders had tried desperately to bury.
He needed answers. The Sepulcher tablets might provide them, but those were locked away in Order vaults, protected by oaths and wards that even his abilities couldn't easily circumvent. The harvested memories from the acolyte might contain fragments of cult knowledge—locations, rituals, identities. But accessing that information would require time and focus that Kael currently lacked.
The rain intensified, drumming against the collapsed awning and turning the alley into a maze of shadows. Kael rose, sheathing his knife. His spiritual senses swept the surrounding area, searching for additional threats. The cult had known he would come. They had positioned an avatar here specifically to deliver a message—or to buy time for a more important operation elsewhere.
Where was Zarek? The thought surfaced unbidden. The man who had framed Kael for murder, who had orchestrated the Shadow Court's war against him, who had escaped every attempt at confrontation. The entity's words suggested a deeper connection between Zarek and the Abyssal cults than simple political alliance.
"Ashborn." The voice came from the alley's entrance. Kael spun, hand dropping to his weapon, only to find himself facing a familiar figure. Mira Thornwood stood at the edge of the lamplight, rain plastering her dark hair to her skull. She wore the mud-spattered leathers of the Order of Dawn's enforcement cadre, and the sigil on her shoulder blazed with contained power. "You're alive. Good."
"Disappointed?" Kael's tone carried no warmth. Mira had been useful in the past, but her Order's involvement in the Shadow Court's persecution of him remained an open wound.
"Not particularly." She stepped into the alley, eyes scanning the scene with professional detachment. Her gaze lingered on Ashworth's corpse before returning to Kael. "The entity's avatar?"
"Destroyed. The real one is still bound to whatever seal it's attached to." Kael watched her carefully. The Order of Dawn had increased its presence in Thornwick over the past month, ostensibly to combat the cult resurgence. Officially, they were allies. Unofficially, Kael didn't trust anyone wearing an Order uniform as far as he could throw them. "How did you find me?"
"We tracked the entity's spiritual signature when it manifested. Thought it was one of the lower manifestations at first, easily handled. Then the readings spiked." Mira's expression tightened. "An avatar-class manifestation in the middle of Thornwick's residential district. That shouldn't be possible. The seals should have contained it."
"The seals are failing." Kael stated it flatly. "The entity told me as much. Said the seventh seal has cracked."
Mira's composure cracked slightly. "The seventh seal? That's—" She stopped herself, jaw tightening. "That information is classified at the highest levels. How would an avatar know?"
"Because it's been planning this for a long time. Longer than your Order's purge operations. Longer than my war with the Shadow Court." Kael stepped closer, watching her reaction. "The cult didn't start this conflict. They accelerated an existing process. Someone—or something—has been working to destabilize the seal network for years, maybe decades. And your Orders have been too busy fighting each other to notice."
"That's not—" Mira began.
"Enough." Kael cut her off. "I don't have time for political maneuvering. If the seventh seal is failing, we need to know which rift it protects and how much time we have before it opens fully. Those are facts I need, and you're going to provide them."
Mira stared at him for a long moment. The rain continued to fall, filling the silence between them. Finally, she exhaled—a sound of reluctant surrender.
"The seventh seal protects the Thornwick Rift," she said quietly. "The one beneath the city's old cathedral. The one that's been showing stress fractures for the past century." She paused, something like fear crossing her features. "The one your Soul Reaver abilities were designed to monitor, before the Orders decided the knowledge was too dangerous to leave in anyone's hands."
The revelation struck Kael like a physical blow. His power—the ability to harvest souls, to extract knowledge from the dead, to walk the boundary between existence and oblivion—hadn't been created as a weapon against humanity. It had been designed as a failsafe. A way to detect and respond to seal degradation before catastrophe occurred.
And the Orders had buried that knowledge because they feared what a Soul Reaver could become.
"The original Reaver," Kael said slowly. "He didn't seal the power away because it was dangerous. He sealed it away because the Orders asked him to. Because they couldn't control him."
"The Council of Six deemed it a necessary sacrifice." Mira's voice carried no conviction. "One man's power against the stability of the entire seal network. They thought they could maintain the system without him. They were wrong."
"So wrong," Kael agreed, "that they've spent the last millennium pretending the whole thing never happened. Burning records. Executing anyone who showed potential. Building their precious Concordat on a foundation of lies."
"The Concordat keeps the peace," Mira insisted. "The Orders have maintained the seals through conventional means. It worked for a thousand years."
"Until it didn't." Kael turned away, looking toward the collapsed wall at the alley's end. Beyond it, he could see the distant glow of Thornwick's industrial district—furnaces burning through the night, steam engines driving the city's endless production cycles. All of it built on the assumption that reality would hold. That the membrane between worlds would remain intact. "The cult knows something we don't. They've been preparing for this moment specifically. And they used my conflict with the Shadow Court to weaken defenses that should have contained them."
"Which is why the Order of Dawn needs your help." Mira moved to stand beside him, her presence less threatening now that the confrontation had shifted to pragmatism. "Not as an enemy. Not as a tool. As a partner, if you're willing to accept the risk."
Kael considered the offer. Every instinct screamed caution. The Orders had hunted him, framed him, tried to execute him. Working with them meant trusting people who had proven themselves untrustworthy. It meant exposing himself to political manipulation and possible betrayal.
But the alternative was fighting alone against forces that had been preparing for this confrontation far longer than he had.
"What do you need?" he asked.
Mira reached into her coat, producing a small leather folder. She handed it to him without ceremony. Inside, Kael found documents bearing the seals of multiple Orders—information that should have been impossible to obtain through legitimate channels.
"Cultist movements across the continent," Mira explained. "Not random attacks. Not opportunistic raids. Coordinated strikes against specific seal maintenance facilities. They've been degrading the network's infrastructure for years, and we only noticed the pattern six months ago." She pointed to a section of the documents. "Three of the targeted facilities are already compromised. Two more show signs of sabotage. And Thornwick—" She hesitated. "Thornwick is next on their list. Not because of you. Because of what's beneath it."
Kael studied the documents, his harvested knowledge from the acolyte providing context that Mira couldn't have expected. The pattern was clear. The cult wasn't trying to free a single Entity—they were attempting a cascading failure. Damage enough seals simultaneously, and the entire network would collapse. The Entities would pour through the gaps before anyone could mount a defense.
"The Sealed Rifts," he said. "There are seventy-two of them, correct? Seventy-two Entities, seventy-two seals."
"Seventy-two prisons," Mira corrected. "And seventy-two potential catastrophes if they open simultaneously."
"How long do we have?"
Mira's expression darkened. "The seventh seal is already compromised. Our best estimates give us two weeks before it fails completely. The entity you destroyed tonight was testing our response time, measuring how quickly Order forces mobilize." She met his gaze directly. "They know we're coming now. Whatever they're planning for Thornwick will happen within days."
Kael absorbed this information, his mind already calculating possibilities. Two weeks. That was both longer and shorter than he'd expected. Long enough to prepare, if he could gather the right resources. Short enough that conventional approaches would almost certainly fail.
"The harvested memories I extracted from a cultist," he said. "They contain fragments of their operational structure. Names, locations, ritual components. If I can access that information properly, I might be able to identify their key targets before they move."
"That would require someone with the appropriate knowledge to help you interpret—"
"I know exactly who to ask." Kael's mind went to Lyra, whose Shadow Court connections might provide additional context, and to the scattered scholars who had survived the Orders' historical purges. But another option existed, one that carried its own dangers. "There's a tomb beneath Thornwick's old quarter. Sealed for centuries, supposedly containing nothing of value. But the cult has been seen operating in that area for months."
Mira's eyes narrowed. "You're suggesting the tomb might contain cultist infrastructure."
"I'm suggesting it might contain answers. About the original Reaver. About why the Orders were so desperate to bury this knowledge." Kael tucked the documents into his coat. "I'm going to find out what happened to the man who came before me. And when I do, I'll know how to finish what he started."
"And if what you find suggests the original Reaver failed? That his solution didn't work?"
Kael turned to face her fully, rain streaming down his face. "Then I'll find a better one."
Mira studied him for a long moment, then nodded slowly. "The Order of Dawn will provide what support we can. Resources, intelligence, tactical assistance. But Kael—" She caught his arm as he turned to leave. "—be careful. The cult wants you alive. The entity tonight wasn't trying to kill you. It was trying to talk. To plant seeds of doubt, to make you question everything you think you know."
"I know exactly what it's doing." Kael pulled free from her grip. "The question is whether I'm strong enough to harvest the truth without losing myself in the flow."
He walked away without looking back. The rain swallowed him within seconds, leaving Mira alone with the magistrate's corpse and the weight of secrets that had been buried for a thousand years.

The tomb's entrance lay beneath a collapsed tenement in Thornwick's oldest quarter, hidden by decades of debris and deliberate neglect. Kael had spent the better part of an hour clearing the passage, his harvested strength making the manual labor bearable. Now he stood at the threshold of a chamber that should have been empty.
It wasn't.
The walls bore inscriptions in a language that predated the Orders' founding—angular symbols that seemed to shift when viewed directly. At the chamber's center stood a pedestal, and upon it rested a book bound in something that was decidedly not leather. The cover displayed a single word in the old script: SEPULCHER.
Kael's breath caught. The Sepulcher tablets he'd heard mentioned in fragments—the recorded wisdom of the first Soul Reaver—existed in multiple forms. The Order archives contained sanitized versions, stripped of practical knowledge and heavy with warnings. But this book, this original text, radiated power that made his Soul Reaver abilities resonate in recognition.
He approached slowly, aware that traps might protect the chamber. Nothing activated as he crossed the distance. The book remained still, waiting.
Kael reached for it—
The temperature dropped thirty degrees. Kael's hand stopped inches from the cover, frozen by a presence that hadn't been there a moment before. He spun, knife drawn, only to find the chamber's entrance now blocked by a figure.
Tall. Gaunt. Wearing robes that seemed woven from shadow itself. The figure's face remained obscured by a hood that cast the features into impenetrable darkness, but Kael could feel its attention like pressure against his skull.
"You shouldn't be here," the figure said. Its voice carried the layered harmonics of the entity, but the words remained singular. Focused. Present in a way that avatar speech never achieved. "This place is not for the living."
"You're not dead either." Kael held his ground, though his instincts screamed at him to run. This wasn't an avatar. This was something far worse. "Are you?"
The figure tilted its head, a gesture horribly reminiscent of the Ashworth-thing's inhuman movement. "I am what remains when the living choose to become more. The first Reaver made a pact with the Abyss, Kael Ashborn. He thought he could control the terms. He thought mortality's end would grant him eternity to pursue his goals." A sound emerged from beneath the hood—laughter, maybe, or a sob. "He was wrong."
"You're him." The realization struck like a physical blow. "The original Reaver. You're still here."
"In a manner of speaking." The figure stepped forward, and Kael found himself backing against the pedestal. The book waited behind him, tantalizingly close. "My body died two centuries ago. My soul fragmented, scattered across the seal network as a failsafe I never intended to become necessary. But pieces of me remain. Watching. Waiting." The figure's hood turned toward the book. "For someone worthy to claim what I left behind."
"I'm not here to claim anything." Kael's voice came out steadier than he felt. "I'm here to understand. The cult is moving against the seventh seal. The Orders are compromised. The entire seal network is failing, and I need to know how to stop it."
"Stop it?" The figure's amusement was palpable. "You cannot stop entropy, child. You can only redirect it. The question is whether you're willing to become what I became to preserve what remains."
Kael's hand trembled against his knife. Every word from this remnant confirmed his worst fears. The original Reaver hadn't sealed away the Soul Reaver power out of wisdom or restraint. He had done it because the burden had broken him. Because immortality—or whatever approximation of it the Abyss offered—came with costs that no mortal should bear.
"How did you survive?" Kael asked. "For two centuries, trapped between worlds, watching everything you built crumble—what kept you from surrendering to the Abyss?"
The figure's response came after a long pause. When it spoke, the layered harmonics had faded, leaving only a single voice. Tired. Ancient. Desperately human.
"Spite, mostly."
The chamber shook. Dust fell from ancient ceilings. The figure's form began to flicker, destabilizing at the edges.
"The cult has breached the outer wards," it said, urgency replacing its earlier languor. "They've been tracking your progress since you entered the quarter. I bought you time with what remained of my influence, but it won't last." It thrust one shadow-wreathed hand toward the book. "Take it. Learn what you can. The rest—" Another tremor. "The rest you'll have to discover for yourself."
"How do I know this isn't a trap?" Kael demanded. "How do I know you're really what you claim?"
"You don't." The figure's form was nearly transparent now. "But you know the soul you're carrying. The resonance it creates when true danger approaches. Does it feel like a trap to you?"
Kael reached inward, toward the cold reservoir of harvested power. The Soul Reaver's curse pulsed in response—not with warning, but with recognition. As if the abilities he carried had encountered something familiar. Something that shared their fundamental nature.
He grabbed the book.
The world exploded into light. Knowledge flooded through him—not harvested, not stolen, but freely given. The original Reaver's lifetime of research, condensed into a single overwhelming moment of transfer. Kael's knees buckled. His vision blurred. Through the chaos, he heard the figure's final words:
"The seventh seal won't hold. But you have something I never did—people who might stand with you when the end comes. Don't waste that advantage. Don't become what I became."
The presence vanished. The chamber stopped shaking. Kael lay on the cold stone floor, clutching the book to his chest, gasping for breath that wouldn't come easily.
When he finally rose, the tomb was empty except for the pedestal and the weight of revelation pressing against his mind. He had answers now—partial, incomplete, but more than he'd possessed an hour ago. The original Reaver had been neither hero nor villain. He had been a man who had made impossible choices and lived (or existed) with the consequences.
The cult would come for him. The Orders would demand explanations he wasn't prepared to give. The seventh seal continued its inexorable degradation, and somewhere in the Abyss, the Faceless One prepared to claim what it believed it was owed.
Kael opened the book. The first page contained a single line of inscription, written in the original Reaver's hand:
*To whoever comes after: the power is a tool. The user determines its purpose. Don't let them convince you otherwise.*
He readied his knife, checked his spiritual reserves, and moved toward the chamber's exit. The cultists would arrive soon. He would be ready.
The book burned against his chest, full of knowledge he needed time to understand.
And somewhere above, through layers of stone and earth and collapsing tenements, Kael heard footsteps. Dozens of them. Moving with purpose.
They had found him.
The question was whether he could find a way out before they claimed what the original Reaver had left behind.
