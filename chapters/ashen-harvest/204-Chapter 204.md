The blade found the cultist's throat before the man could finish his prayer. Kael wrenched the steel free, arterial spray painting the cobblestones in a dark arc, and dropped the body without ceremony. Two more figures emerged from the gaslit fog of Miller's Alley, their robes marked with the jagged sigil of the Rift-Blessed.
"You're making a scene," Vane called from the rooftop above, his silhouette sharp against the belching smokestacks. "The Wardens will be here within minutes."
"Let them come."
Kael's hand closed around the dying cultist's wrist. The flesh was still warm, the spiritual residue potent. He reached with that other sense—the one that tasted death—and pulled.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted flickering prayer-memory from the remains. Fragment of devotional practice acquired. Cult combat techniques: basic. Quality: degraded.]
The fragments meant nothing alone. A handful of mumbled invocations, a broken gesture toward the sky. But strung together with others harvested from the bodies littering the alleyway, they painted a picture.
"Prayers to Rift-Zero," Kael said, straightening. "Not the usual catalogue. Someone's consolidating."
Vane dropped from the rooftop, landing with the fluid grace of someone who'd spent a lifetime treating gravity as a suggestion. His coat—expensive, undamaged despite the chaos—fluttered behind him like a banner. "The Wardens—"
"Will find bodies and blood. They won't find me."
"Because I'm here." Vane's tone carried no gratitude. "Again. You're burning through favors faster than I can generate them."
Kael counted the dead. Five cultists, each marked with the same sigil. In the three weeks since he'd arrived in Thornwick, the Rift-Blessed had grown bolder—attacks in broad daylight, ritual circles drawn in public squares, followers preaching about the "cleansing fire" in marketplace squares. The Orders called it isolated extremism. Kael called it something else.
"The rally in Ashworth Square," he said. "Yesterday. You were there."
Vane's expression didn't change, but something behind his eyes sharpened. "I attend many rallies."
"Seventeen thousand attendees. Fourteen preaching stations. Coordinated sermon schedule across all of them." Kael stepped over a corpse, approaching the younger man. "That's not a movement. That's an army."
The fog rolled thicker between the buildings, carrying the acrid bite of coal smoke and something else beneath it—ozone, maybe, or the metallic tang that preceded spiritual surges. Distant bells began to toll. The Wardens, responding to reports of violence.
"Walk with me," Vane said, and turned without waiting for an answer.
---
They moved through Thornwick's underbelly, where the gaslight gave way to lantern-dim passages and the sound of the city faded to distant echoes. Vane led with the confidence of someone who knew every shadow, every shortcut, every blind spot in the Wardens' patrol patterns.
"The Six Orders have maintained the seals for three hundred years," Vane said, his voice low. "You understand what that means?"
"It means they've had three centuries to get comfortable."
"It means they're entrenched. Ossified. The current generation of Wardens has never faced a true Abyssal incursion. Most of them believe the Entities are legend, kept alive by superstitious peasants and Order propaganda." Vane ducked beneath a low archway, emerging into a wider tunnel that smelled of stagnant water. "They don't understand that the seals are weakening."
Kael kept pace, his boots splashing through puddles that reflected the dim light. "How weak?"
"The projections say sixty years until the first major failure. The optimistic projections." Vane glanced back at him. "My projections say twelve."
"That's not optimism."
"No." The word hung between them. "It's accounting for the cult activity."
They emerged into a larger chamber—a natural cavern, maybe, or a forgotten basement beneath the old city. The walls were lined with shelves, and on those shelves sat dozens of glass vials, each containing something that glowed with soft, pulsing light. Soul-essence. Enough to fund a small war.
"You've been busy," Kael said.
"Protecting assets." Vane moved to a table at the chamber's center, covered with papers, maps, and a wooden box that hummed with faint spiritual energy. "The cult doesn't operate randomly. Every attack, every rally, every conversion—they're coordinated. Someone is feeding them information about the seal network's vulnerabilities."
"The Shadow Court."
Vane's hands stilled on the papers. "You know about them?"
"I know they're watching." Kael met the other man's gaze without flinching. "I know they've been tracking my movements since I entered the city. I know one of their agents approached me last week, offering information about 'mutual interests.'" He let the silence stretch. "I declined. They didn't like that."
"Declined." Vane's laugh was humorless. "The Shadow Court doesn't accept declines."
"I noticed."
The younger man studied him for a long moment, then reached for the wooden box. "This was recovered from a cultist compound three days ago. The Orders have it classified, but I have... Sources."
He opened the box. Inside, resting on velvet, was a small metal disc covered in script that seemed to shift when Kael looked at it directly. The symbols weren't any language he recognized—too angular, too hungry.
"A map," Vane said. "Partial, but legible to those with the proper... Access."
Kael leaned closer, and the symbols resolved into something that made his blood chill. Lines connecting points across the city. Circles marking locations. And at the center, a single glyph that pulsed with dark light.
"Seventy-two points," he said slowly. "This is a map of the seal network."
"More than that." Vane's voice dropped. "It's a map of where the seals are weakest. Where the rifts can be... Enlarged. The cult isn't trying to break the seals completely. They're trying to create passages. Small ones. Enough to let things through."
Kael straightened, his mind racing. The implications unfolded like a corpse on an autopsy table. "If multiple rifts opened simultaneously—"
"The Orders couldn't respond to all of them. They'd have to choose which threats to address, which populations to abandon." Vane closed the box with careful precision. "It's not a single apocalyptic strike. It's death by a thousand cuts. Overwhelm the defenses, exhaust the response, let the Abyss seep back into the world one village at a time."
"Who's leading them?"
"Prime Zarek." The name landed like a blade between them. "You know that name."
Kael's hand drifted to the sword at his hip. "I know he framed me for murder. I know he stood in the public square and watched my execution. I know he's connected to the Shadow Court, and I know—" He stopped, the memory clawing at something raw. "I know I'll kill him eventually."
"Eventually isn't good enough." Vane returned to the table, shuffling papers with renewed urgency. "Zarek has been moving for years. Maybe decades. The cult isn't his tool—it's his foundation. He's building something, Kael. Something that requires multiple rifts open at once."
"Requires?"
"The rituals." Vane pulled out a sheaf of documents, covered in cramped handwriting. "Recovered from a cult scribe. Zarek isn't just opening rifts. He's using them. Channeling Abyssal energy into a specific purpose."
Kael scanned the pages, his Soul Reaver senses catching fragments of the spiritual residue clinging to the ink. These weren't copied by rote—they were composed by someone who understood what they were writing. Someone with power.
"What purpose?"
"I don't know." The admission seemed to cost Vane. "The final pages were missing. Destroyed, probably, when the compound was discovered. But the earlier rituals mention a 'vessel.' A 'seat of the hollow throne.' Terms that don't appear in any Order archives."
A vessel. A throne. Kael thought of the Sepulcher tablets, the fragments of the first Soul Reaver's writings he'd glimpsed in his visions. The True Name that could reshape reality. The power to control the boundary between worlds.
"Soul Reaver," he said quietly.
Vane went still. "What?"
"The cult uses Soul Reaver terminology. I've seen it in their prayers, their ritual scripts. They talk about 'the first harvest' and 'the throne of the hollow king.'" Kael met the younger man's eyes. "Someone is trying to recreate the Soul Reaver's power. Or take it."
"You're saying the cult wants to make more of you?"
"I'm saying they want what I have. Maybe they want to control it. Maybe they want to destroy it." He thought of the Shadow Court's interest, their careful surveillance, their "mutual interests." "Either way, I'm a target."
The bells in the distance had stopped. Thornwick held its breath, waiting for the Wardens to finish their investigation, to file their reports, to pretend that five dead cultists meant something in a city where the Abyss was slowly reclaiming ground.
"There's something else," Vane said. He hesitated—a rare display, from what Kael had seen. "The Sepulcher tablets. The ones you've been seeking."
Kael's attention sharpened. "What about them?"
"Three months ago, a fragment surfaced in the Veiled Market. Sold to a collector in the Iron Quarter before the Order could intercept it." Vane reached into his coat, producing a small cloth bundle. "This was inside the collector's safe when I... Visited."
He unwrapped the cloth. A small piece of stone, no larger than Kael's palm, covered in the same shifting script. But this one was different. Older. The symbols seemed to move with purpose, forming patterns that tugged at something deep in Kael's awareness.
"The tablet talks about the Soul Reaver's origin," Vane said. "About where the power comes from. About what it truly means to harvest."
Kael's fingers itched to touch the stone, to pull at its secrets. But something held him back—an instinct developed through months of dealing with powers he didn't fully understand.
"Why are you giving this to me?"
"Because I can't read it." Vane's expression flickered with something that might have been frustration, or fear. "I've tried. The Orders have tried. The script resists translation. But you—" He gestured at Kael. "You carry the Soul Reaver's legacy. Maybe the power recognizes its own history."
"And if it does?"
"Then you learn what you're truly capable of. And maybe—" Vane's jaw tightened. "Maybe you find a way to stop Zarek before his ritual completes."
Kael reached for the stone. His fingers brushed the surface, and for a moment the world shifted—the cavern fading, replaced by darkness absolute and cold. He stood at the edge of an abyss that stretched beyond comprehension, and something vast was looking back at him.
Then he was in the chamber again, gasping, the stone clutched in his hand.
"Soul Harvest: You touched an ancient artifact. Fragmentary inscription detected. Memory of the First Reaver: partial extraction. Warning: origin-memory may trigger involuntary visions. Cult techniques: advanced Rift-Prayer acquired. Quality: significant."
The information flooded his mind—not neatly packaged like previous harvests, but raw and overwhelming. Images. Sounds. A voice speaking words he couldn't understand, but felt in his bones.
The first Soul Reaver hadn't created their power. They'd taken it. From something that already existed. Something imprisoned in the Abyss, something that had clawed its way into the Material and been trapped there, its essence scattered across the seal network.
Kael looked at the stone in his hand, and for the first time since arriving in Thornwick, he understood what he was truly dealing with.
The seals weren't just keeping the Entities out. They were keeping something else in. Something that wanted free.
---
Night had fallen by the time they emerged from the tunnels. The gaslight cast long shadows across the streets, and the fog had thickened to a gray curtain that swallowed sound. Kael carried the stone in his coat pocket, close to his chest, feeling its faint pulse like a second heartbeat.
Vane walked beside him, silent for once. Whatever the younger man had expected from giving Kael the tablet, the intensity of his reaction had clearly surprised him.
"The Shadow Court," Kael said finally. "You know more than you're telling me."
"I know enough to stay alive." Vane's tone was careful. "The Shadow Court operates across all six Orders. They have agents everywhere—Wardens, priests, even some who claim to be fighting them. They're not an organization so much as a... Consensus. People who believe the old ways must return, regardless of cost."
"And Zarek?"
"Connected. Deeply. But I don't know how. The threads go back too far, cross too many boundaries." Vane stopped at a corner, the fog swirling around them. "What I do know is that the Shadow Court wants the seals broken. Not all at once—that's not their style. They want managed collapse. A slow infusion of Abyssal power into the Material, until the boundary dissolves entirely."
"That's suicide. The Entities would consume everything."
"They believe they can control it. They believe there's something in the Abyss that will save them—or reward them—when the Veil falls." Vane's laugh was bitter. "Fanatics. Zealots. The same type that have plagued every civilization since the first Awakened drew power from the dark."
Kael thought of the cultists in Miller's Alley, their prayers cut short by his blade. Fanatics, yes. But also believers. People who'd found meaning in the coming apocalypse, who'd chosen their side and were willing to die for it.
"The collector you visited," he said. "The one whose safe held the tablet."
"Yes?"
"Was he a cultist?"
"No. Collector. Wealthy, connected, obsessed with rare artifacts. He bought the tablet because it was valuable, not because he understood what it meant." Vane paused. "Why?"
"Because someone told him to buy it. Someone wanted that piece of history out of the Order's reach." Kael turned to face the other man fully. "The Shadow Court is feeding artifacts into the market. Guiding them to specific buyers. Why?"
"I don't—"
"They're creating a network." The pieces clicked together with terrible clarity. "Not just cultists. Collectors, merchants, smugglers—anyone who handles rare goods. People with access to places the Orders don't watch. People who can be pointed toward specific targets."
Vane's face had gone pale in the gaslight. "You're saying the Shadow Court has built an infrastructure. A parallel market that moves more than soul-essence."
"I'm saying Zarek's ritual isn't just spiritual. It's logistical." Kael's hand found the stone in his pocket, its pulse synchronizing with his own heartbeat. "He's not just opening rifts. He's building a supply chain. Getting materials, information, and people into position. And the Shadow Court is making sure the Orders don't see it coming."
The fog seemed to press closer, as if listening. Somewhere in the distance, a dog barked twice and fell silent.
"Kael." Vane's voice was tight. "There's something I haven't told you."
"Tell me now."
"The Shadow Court approached me six months ago. Before you arrived. Before any of this accelerated." The younger man's composure cracked, revealing something beneath—fear, maybe, or guilt. "They offered me a position. Information, resources, protection. All I had to do was report on specific individuals. People they believed were threats to their... Project."
"And?"
"I refused. At first." Vane's hands clenched at his sides. "Then they showed me what they could do. My family's holdings. My contacts. Every secret I'd accumulated in years of dealing in the shadows." He swallowed. "They gave me a choice. Report, or watch everything I'd built burn. So I report. Small things. Movements, purchases, conversations. Enough to satisfy them without..." He trailed off.
"Without helping them."
"Yes." The word was barely audible.
Kael studied the man before him—this smugglers' prince, this dealer in secrets, this creature of compromise and survival. He should have felt disgust. Maybe he did. But beneath that, something else stirred. Recognition.
"How many?"
"What?"
"How many Shadow Court agents have you reported to? How deep does this go?"
Vane shook his head. "I don't know their full structure. I report to a handler—a woman named Mira who I've never met face-to-face. She gives me targets, I provide information. Sometimes the targets disappear. Sometimes they don't. I've learned not to ask questions."
Mira. The name echoed in Kael's memory. The Shadow Court operative who'd approached him in the market, speaking of mutual interests. Coincidence, probably. The Court had dozens of agents, maybe hundreds.
But the coincidence bothered him.
"Next time your handler contacts you," Kael said slowly, "tell her you have information about my movements. Tell her I'm getting close to something—a lead on Zarek's ritual location, maybe. Make it vague but tempting."
"You want me to feed you to them."
"I want them to make a mistake." Kael's smile held no warmth. "The Shadow Court operates in shadows. They watch, they wait, they manipulate from a distance. But they don't expect their pawns to bite back. When they come for me, they'll be confident. And confident people make errors."
Vane was silent for a long moment. The fog curled between them, carrying the distant sounds of Thornwick's nightlife—music from a tavern, the clatter of a late carriage, the ever-present rumble of steam engines.
"This is dangerous."
"Everything is dangerous. At least this way, we choose which dangers to face."
A sound broke the stillness—a footstep, deliberate and close. Kael spun, his blade clearing its sheath in a motion that had become instinct. The fog parted, revealing a figure in a dark coat, face obscured by a wide-brimmed hat.
"You're a hard man to find, Kael Ashborn." The voice was feminine, low, amused. "I've been following your trail all night."
"Handler Mira?" Vane's voice cracked slightly.
The figure laughed—a soft, melodic sound that seemed wrong in the fog-choked street. "My name is Lyra. And I'm not here on Court business." She raised one hand, palm outward, showing a mark burned into the flesh—a sigil that made Kael's Soul Reaver senses scream in recognition. "I'm here because the Shadow Court made a promise to someone I care about. A promise they've broken."
Lyra. The name hit Kael like a physical blow. Lyra, who he'd known in another life—before his execution, before the Soul Reaver, before any of this. Lyra, who had her own secrets, her own agenda, her own reasons to want Zarek dead.
"What promise?" he demanded.
"The same one everyone makes." Lyra's eyes gleamed in the gaslight. "The promise to tell the truth when the time comes. And the time is running out, Kael. Zarek's ritual is accelerating. The cult will move on the first rift within the week."
She stepped closer, and Kael saw the exhaustion in her face, the desperation that mirrored his own.
"You want to know what I know? Follow me. But understand—" Her gaze flicked to Vane, sharp as a blade. "—some truths have costs. And not everyone survives paying them."
She turned and walked into the fog, not waiting to see if they followed.
Kael looked at Vane. Vane looked at Kael.
"Well," the smuggler said dryly. "This night is getting interesting."
Kael sheathed his sword and moved after Lyra's vanishing figure. The stone pulsed against his chest, warm now, almost hot. The First Reaver's memories stirred in his mind, fragments of power and purpose that he was only beginning to understand.
Ahead, in the fog, Lyra's footsteps echoed.
Behind, in the shadows, something else was watching.
And somewhere in Thornwick, Prime Zarek was completing a ritual that would tear the world apart.
Kael's hand closed around the stone, feeling its ancient pulse.
The conspiracy had revealed its first true face. Now came the reckoning.
