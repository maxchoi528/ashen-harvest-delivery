"Until it collapses all at once."
Kael stood, mind racing through the implications. The war against the Abyss depended on the seal network. Seventy-two rifts, each containing an Entity, each sealed by ancient working. If the seals failed in sequence, humanity would face a coordinated incursion it couldn't repel.
This wasn't random cult activity. This was an assault on civilization's foundation.

"Orders intelligence?"
"Possible. The Shadow Court sells to anyone." She pulled a journal from beneath the plinth, flipping through pages of cramped script. "Ritual schedules. Anchor point designations. This is operational planning, not inspired madness."

"They're not hitting all seventy-two at once," he realized. "They're targeting clusters. Concentrated damage in specific areas, then moving on."
"Testing responses. Refining techniques." Lyra's voice went flat. "Learning how to kill the seals that keep the world intact."
A sound echoed from above—footsteps on the cathedral's upper floor. Multiple sets, moving with purpose.
"We need to leave," Kael said.
"Not yet." Lyra was photographing the maps with a crystalline device, its flash painting the chamber in brief light. "I need to document this. The Orders need to see—"
"Lyra."
"—the scale of what we're dealing with—"
"Lyra." He grabbed her arm. "We can come back. Right now, we're about to be surrounded."
The footsteps multiplied. Voices murmured in the Abyssal tongue, the same broken language Kael had harvested minutes ago. More cultists, converging on the cathedral.
Lyra finished her photograph and pocketed the device. "Back exit?"
"Through the ritual chamber. There should be an old priest's passage."
They moved through the darkness, Kael leading by the memory of the cultist's final moments. The passage existed—he'd seen it in the dying vision, a narrow corridor behind a false wall. His fingers found the seam in the stone, and he pushed.
The wall swung inward on silent hinges.
Cold air breathed from the tunnel beyond, carrying the scent of earth and something older. They slipped through, pulling the wall closed behind them. In the darkness, Kael heard Lyra's breathing, the distant chanting from above, and beneath it all, a vibration that seemed to come from the stones themselves.
"The seals," he whispered. "Can you feel them?"
"A little. They're always there, if you know how to listen." Lyra's voice carried a strange tension. "This district's network is old. Pre-Concordat. Built when the first Orders understood what they were fighting."
"The first Soul Reaver helped design it."

Lyra went still. "What did you just say?"
"Nothing."
"You said the first Soul Reaver. How do you know about—"

"They'll also find the maps. The journal. They'll know about the anchor points."
"And they'll mobilize to defend them." Lyra's tone sharpened with grim satisfaction. "Which means Zarek's people will have to move faster than planned. Mistakes happen when you rush."
Kael slowed, letting the implications settle. They'd disrupted one operation, but the Thornwick network was larger than a single cathedral basement. Zarek had multiple cells, multiple objectives, months of preparation.
"This changes the timeline," he said. "Whatever they planned for next week—it's happening tonight now. They can't afford to wait while the Orders scramble."
"Then we find where they're converging. Hit them before they can regroup."
"The ritual site from the first cultist's memory. The cathedral basement was secondary—the main gathering is elsewhere."

"You knew about this?"
"I suspected. The Guild hall connects to the old sewer network—easy movement, hidden from prying eyes." Her expression flickered with something Kael couldn't read. "I had a source inside. She went silent three weeks ago."
"I'm sorry."
"Don't be. She knew the risks." Lyra squared her shoulders. "Tonight, we finish what she started."
The Dockside sprawled along Thornwick's industrial waterfront, its warehouses and foundries working ceaselessly. Even at this hour, cargo moved by torchlight—soul-essence shipments from the Mortician's Guild, steel plates for the Order war machines, crates of supplies that would never see a manifest.
The Mortician's Guild hall stood at the district's edge, a building of black stone and iron grates. Its windows were boarded, its doors chained. To normal eyes, it was abandoned.
To Kael's sight, it blazed with spiritual energy.
"Cultists inside," he murmured. "More than a dozen. And something else. Something bigger."
"Something else?"

"Fragments," he said finally. "Fragments of a larger whole."

Soul fragments. Pieces of harvested souls, woven into something monstrous.

"For what?"

"He felt the cathedral breach. He knows we're coming."
"Or something else drew him. Something we don't understand yet."
The implication settled cold in Kael's stomach. Zarek had been framed, setting events in motion that led to Kael's execution and awakening. Now, years later, they were finally approaching confrontation. But the board had changed. The stakes were higher than personal revenge.
Kael's fingers brushed the Soul Reaver's presence in his chest. The weapon had chosen him—or maybe he'd been born to it, the way the ancient texts described. A lineage of Reavers stretching back through centuries, each one carrying fragments of the original, each one walking the same path.
The first Soul Reaver had been a weapon against the Abyss. Then the Orders had turned on them, fearing what they represented. The knowledge had been buried, the Reavers hunted, until only legends remained.
Kael was the latest link in that chain. He carried pieces of the first Reaver's power, harvested unconsciously from the corpse of a man who had died without knowing his own legacy.

"I died once already. It's hard to stay scared after that."

Soul fragments. Pieces of power drawn from the dead.

"Soul Harvest. You extracted 847 fragments of former Reaver essence. Knowledge acquired: Location of the Sepulcher Tablets. Skill acquired: Soul Tether (Origin)."

Kael's hand found the Soul Reaver's presence in his chest. It pulsed with something that might have been hunger, or anticipation, or the accumulated weight of centuries.

The war for Thornwick was far from over. It was just beginning.
And in the shadows of a city built on steam and secrets, the true players were finally revealing their hands.
