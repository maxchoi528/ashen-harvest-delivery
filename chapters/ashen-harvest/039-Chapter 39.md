Kael's fingers tightened around his drink. Bodies. In Thornwick, bodies went to the Mortician's Guild for processing, or they went to the undercity where the Veiled Market traded in less legal merchandise. But the docks? The docks meant water. The docks meant disposal.
Or shipment.
The woman hesitated. Kael saw calculation in her eyes—the cost of fifteen sovereigns against whatever information she sought. Soul-essence vials dominated high-value exchanges, but common coin still ruled everyday transactions.
"Twelve," she countered. "And you tell no one else."
"Done." The gaunt man pocketed the purse. "The old tannery on Silt Street. Basement's been converted. They bring 'em in through the water gate at night, process 'em before dawn."
Kael's stomach dropped. Processing. The word hung in the air, carrying weight it shouldn't.
Processing could mean a dozen things. Rendering for fertilizer. Extracting soul-essence for the illegal trade. Or something worse—the rumors that had been circulating for months about the cult activity stirring beneath Thornwick's streets.
The woman stood, adjusting her gloves. "If I discover you've sold that address to anyone else—"
"You won't." The man's grin showed gaps where teeth should be. "I know when to keep my mouth shut."
She swept from the tavern without looking back. The door swung shut behind her, and Kael rose from his seat.

The streets of Thornwick wound like entrails through the city's core. Gas lamps cast pools of sickly yellow light at intersections, but the alleys between them drowned in shadow. Fog rolled in from the harbor, carrying the stench of brine and rotting fish and something else—something chemical and wrong.
Kael moved through the fog with his hood raised, letting the city swallow his outline. His boots splashed through puddles of water that had pooled in the cobblestone dips. Somewhere above, a steam whistle shrieked, marking the shift change at the ironworks.
He was three blocks from Silt Street when he felt it.
The sensation crawled up his spine before his eyes could confirm it—a pressure, a presence, a wrongness that had nothing to do with the darkness and everything to do with what lurked within it. His skin prickled. His breath misted before his lips.
Something was hunting him.
Kael's hand moved to his belt, finding the knife he kept there. A simple blade, nothing special—no spiritual enhancement, no Order runes. Just steel and edge and the promise of violence.
The fog thickened ahead. Shapes moved within it—too fluid, too quick, following his path without sound.
"Easy," Kael muttered to himself. Talking helped. Kept him present. "Just a stroll. Nothing to see."
He turned down an alley he hadn't intended to take—a shortcut that would put him two blocks from Silt Street. The walls here pressed close, brick rising on either side like the sides of a grave. His shoulders brushed stone slick with moisture and something darker.
The footsteps behind him stopped.
Kael froze.
Silence filled the alley, broken only by the distant clang of machinery and the drip of water from somewhere overhead. His heart beat steady in his chest. Fear was a luxury. Fear cost seconds he couldn't afford.
"I know you're there," he said, keeping his voice level. "Might as well show yourself."
The fog shifted. A shape detached itself from the darkness ahead, blocking the alley's exit. Tall. Thin. Wearing clothes that hung like rags on a scarecrow. But the proportions were wrong—arms too long, legs bent at angles that suggested joints where joints shouldn't bend.
And the face. What might have been a face once, before the Abyss had taken hold and twisted the humanity into something else entirely.
"The Whisper King's paid good coin for your head."
The voice came from behind him. Kael spun, and another figure stood at the alley's entrance, identical in its wrongness. Then another at the fire escape above. Then another at the gap between buildings to his left.
Four of them. Surrounding him.
"The Whisper King," Kael repeated, tasting the name. He'd heard it before—in whispers, in rumors, in the way other criminals spoke of a power that moved through Thornwick's underworld like smoke through a keyhole. "Never heard of him."
"Liar." The one ahead stepped closer. Its neck cracked like old timber. "The Soul Reaver. We know what you are. We know what you took."
Kael's grip tightened on his knife. "Then you know what I can do."
"The Order will pay double for what we bring back." The creature's mouth opened too wide when it smiled. "Dead or alive. The Master didn't specify."
They moved as one—not running, not walking, something between that made Kael's eyes ache to follow. Four of them, closing the circle, and he had one knife and no way out.
No way out except through.

The first one lunged.
Kael sidestepped, letting claws rake through the air where his torso had been. The creature's momentum carried it past him, and he buried his knife in its back, driving deep until the hilt kissed leather and cloth. The thing screamed—a sound like steam escaping through a broken valve—and collapsed.
No soul-extraction notification. The creature wasn't alive enough to harvest.
The second one came from his left. Kael ripped his blade free and spun, catching its arm before claws could find his throat. Hot blood sprayed across his face as he drove the knife into its chest, twisting. It crumpled.
Two left. They circled now, wary.
"What are you?" Kael asked, breathing hard. "Not Awakened. Not human. Not dead."
"Does it matter?" The larger one crouched, preparing to spring. "You die tonight. That's what matters."
Kael threw his knife.
The blade spinning end over end, catching lamplight for a single frozen instant before burying itself in the creature's eye socket. It staggered back, clawing at the steel protruding from its skull.
The fourth one bolted—toward the wall, up the brick, onto the fire escape in three impossible bounds. Kael watched it flee into the fog above, heard its footsteps clanging against metal until distance swallowed the sound.
He stood alone in the alley, surrounded by bodies that weren't bodies, blood pooling at his feet. His hands shook as he retrieved his knife from the ruined face of the second creature, wiping the black ichor on his coat.
"Whisper King," he muttered. "Paid for my head."
The implications crashed over him like a wave. Someone knew about him. Someone with resources, with reach, with enough interest in Kael Ashborn to hire these things—whatever these things were—to eliminate him.
He knelt beside the first creature he'd killed, examining it in the dim light. The flesh was cold. Too cold, like meat from a cellar. And beneath the skin, he saw the flicker of something else—residual energy, fading but present.
He touched the corpse.

The notification pulsed in his vision, familiar and foreign at once. Since his awakening, since his execution and return, these messages had become part of his reality. A gift. A curse. A tool that marked him as something the world feared and hunted.
Three more creatures to harvest. Each one gave him fragments of energy, weak but cumulative. His reserves grew by small measures.
When he finished, Kael stood and sheathed his knife. The fog had begun to thin, the distant steam whistles marking another hour passing. He had wasted time. The woman from the tavern would be at Silt Street by now, or close enough.
He moved toward the alley's exit, then stopped.
A document lay on the ground near the wall—a scrap of paper that hadn't been there before, or maybe had been obscured by one of the creatures when it fell. He picked it up, angling it toward the light.
Symbols covered the page. Not any language he recognized—not the Common Tongue of Thornwick, not the old High Speech used by the Orders, not even the runic shorthand of the underground markets. These were something older, something that made his eyes water when he tried to focus on them.
And beneath the symbols, a diagram. A map, maybe, or a schematic. Lines connecting circles, circles labeled with numbers that made no sense.
Except one.
72.
Kael's blood ran cold.
The number lodged in his memory like a splinter—the number from the rumors he'd half-ignored, the warnings he'd dismissed as superstition. Seventy-two seals. Seventy-two rifts. Seventy-two entities waiting behind the Veil for the chance to break through.
He folded the paper and slipped it into his coat, next to his heart.

Silt Street stretched before him like a wound in the city's flesh.
The buildings here had seen better centuries. Windows gaped like empty eye sockets, their glass long since smashed or never installed. Walls bore the marks of fire, of neglect, of whatever violence had driven the previous tenants away. A single lamppost stood at the street's entrance, its gaslight flickering with an irregular heartbeat.
The old tannery dominated the block's center—a squat building of stone and timber, its smokestack cold for decades. Water Gate, the sign above the side entrance read, letters half-obliterated by time.
Kael approached from the shadows, circling the perimeter. The woman's footprints from the tavern had led here—he tracked them through the grime, noting where they'd entered through the water gate and disappeared inside.
No guards. No watchers. That concerned him more than sentries would have.
He pressed his ear to the door. Silence. Then, faintly, the sound of voices. More than one. More than two.
He eased the door open and stepped into darkness.
The tannery's interior had been gutted. Machinery stripped away, vats drained and filled with something that smelled of copper and decay. Scaffolding rose toward a ceiling lost in shadow, and along the walls, he saw them—
Bodies.
Dozens of them, laid out on tables that stretched into the gloom. Some covered with sheets. Others naked, open, their contents exposed to examination. And among the bodies, moving figures in gray robes, their faces hidden behind masks of polished bone.
Not Mortician's Guild. Something else. Something that made his skin crawl and his soul cry out in recognition of the wrongness saturating the air.
One of the robed figures looked up. "More inventory," it said, voice muffled by the mask. "Put him with the others."
Kael didn't hesitate.
He threw himself sideways as spiritual energy crackled through the space where he'd stood. The attack missed—barely—scorching the doorframe and leaving black marks on the stone beyond. He rolled behind a table, feeling the wood splinter as another bolt of energy punched through.
"Mistake," another voice said. "He's not inventory. He's the Soul Reaver."
"The one the Master wants?"
"Alive if possible. But is acceptable."
Kael rose from cover, knife in hand. Three robed figures faced him now, hands crackling with power he could sense even through the Veil—the spiritual energy they manipulated, the abilities they wielded. Awakened, then. At least three of them, and possibly more hidden in the darkness.
"I want to see your leader," Kael said. "The Whisper King."
The figures exchanged glances behind their bone masks.
"You'll see him soon enough." The largest one stepped forward, arms raised. "Right before you die screaming his name."
Lightning erupted from its palms.
---
Kael moved.
Not away—toward. Closing the distance before the second strike could form, before the other two could flank him. The knife flashed in the dim light, catching glow from the spiritual attacks that illuminated the room in strobing blue and white.
The first robed figure fell with Kael's blade in its throat, a wet gurgle escaping the mask before the body crumpled. Kael wrenched the knife free and spun, deflecting a strike that would have taken his head off. Steel screamed against steel—or against whatever material reinforced those bone masks.
"Corrupted!" someone shouted. "The blade is corrupted!"
Kael didn't know what that meant, but he used the distraction. He drove his elbow into the second figure's solar plexus, felt the impact shudder through his arm, then brought the knife around in a backhand arc that opened the mask and the face beneath it.
Two down. One left.
The final figure had stopped attacking. It stood motionless amid the carnage, head tilted, watching Kael with an attention that felt like insects crawling across his skin.
"You killed them," it said. "Two of our brothers. In seconds."
"They tried to kill me first."
"They were Awakened. You are Awakened. The Master will be... Intrigued." The figure's voice had changed—no longer the flat tones of a zealot reciting doctrine, but something almost conversational. "Tell me, Soul Reaver. Do you know what you are?"
Kael's grip tightened. "I'm a man with a knife and a grudge. That's all."
"Wrong." The figure spread its arms, and the spiritual energy around it pulsed outward in waves Kael could feel against his skin. "You are a key. The only key that can open the doors that must remain closed. Or—" It paused, savoring the moment. "—the doors that must be opened."
"The seventy-two."
The word hung in the air between them.
"You've heard of them." The figure seemed pleased. "Then you understand. The seals weaken. The Veil frays. The old enemy stirs in its prison, and soon—" A gesture toward the bodies on the tables. "—there will be enough. Enough essence. Enough power. Enough sacrifice to bring seventy-two dawns of chaos upon this world."
"You're cultists."
"We are the faithful. We are the chosen. We serve the one who will lead humanity into its true inheritance—not behind the Veil, but through it. The Abyss is not a prison. It is a throne room, waiting for its rightful sovereign."
Kael advanced, knife raised. "And the Whisper King sits on that throne?"
"The Whisper King serves the throne. As we all do. As you will—" The figure's hands came up, spiritual energy gathering between them. "—or you will die like all the others who stood against the coming tide."
Kael lunged.
The figure unleashed its attack—but Kael was already moving, already inside the range of those devastating bolts of energy, already driving his knife through cloth and flesh and whatever passed for a heart beneath the bone mask.
The body fell. Kael stood over it, breathing hard, surrounded by the dead and the dying.
Then he began to harvest.

The harbor spread before him like a mirror of black glass, reflecting gaslights and stars in equal measure. Fog had rolled in thicker now, shrouding the docks and the ships and the dark water beneath. Kael crouched behind a stack of crates, catching his breath, watching the tannery's water gate for pursuit.

"Spread out," she ordered. "He can't have gone far."
The others split, disappearing into the fog. The woman herself walked toward the harbor's edge, pausing at the water line.
"Kael Ashborn." Her voice carried across the distance without effort. "I know you can hear me. I'm not here to fight."
Kael stayed hidden. Words meant nothing. Actions meant everything.
"The Order of Dawn has been investigating the cult for months," she continued. "We knew about the body shipments. We knew about the rituals. What we didn't know was that they had a Soul Reaver in their ranks—a real one, not just the stolen abilities they cultivate from harvested essence."

Seventy-two seals. Seventy-two rifts. Seventy-two entities waiting to break through.
The Whisper King. The cult. The woman who claimed to serve the Order of Dawn.
