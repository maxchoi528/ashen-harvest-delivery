"Soul Harvest: You extracted 847 essence from the remains. Memory Fragment acquired."
Kael's fingers peeled away from the cooling forehead. The notification faded from his vision, replaced by the familiar sting of information that tasted like copper and regret. Another dead cultist. Another piece of the puzzle he hadn't known he was assembling.
He stood in the ruins of what had once been a respectable tavern, its name—*The Drowning Eel*—still visible above the shattered doorframe despite the fire that had gutted the interior three days ago. Rain drummed against the broken roof tiles, filling the space with a hollow percussion. Around him, the bodies of a dozen cultists lay in positions that spoke of a swift, violent end. Their robes bore the sigil of the Bleeding Moon—three crescents arranged in a triangle, each one dripping crimson thread.
That sigil meant nothing to the authorities. The Watch had written this off as a gang dispute, probably involving the Veiled Market's territorial realignments. They'd moved on after taking a few statements from trembling survivors in the neighboring buildings.
Kael knew better. He'd seen those survivors' eyes. Not fear of violence—fear of recognition.
He crouched beside the nearest body, pushing aside the robe to expose a symbol branded into the flesh below the collarbone. Not a cult marking. Something older. A series of angular lines forming a pattern that his newly acquired Memory Fragment identified as proto-Seal script, predating the Orders' formation by at least three centuries.
"Interesting."
The voice came from behind him. Kael spun, one hand already reaching for the short blade at his hip—a precaution that proved unnecessary when he recognized the speaker.
Vane stepped through the ruined doorway, rain streaming from the brim of her wide-brimmed hat. The assassin wore her characteristic gray coat, daggers catching the weak light filtering through the broken roof. Her expression held the same calculated neutrality she'd shown when they'd first crossed paths in the Ashwick Docks six months ago.
"If you're here to kill me," Kael said, "you'll have to wait in line."
"Noted." She didn't draw her weapons. "I'm here because you accessed a restricted archive in the Order of Dawn's secondary library three nights ago. The one containing pre-Concordat seal maintenance logs."
Kael felt ice crystallize in his stomach. The archive had been a dead end—a frustrated attempt to understand why his Soul Reaver abilities functioned the way they did. The texts had referenced the original Soul Reaver, the first bearer of this cursed gift, but had offered nothing concrete.
"I'm also here," Vane continued, moving deeper into the ruined tavern with the careful grace of someone accustomed to hostile environments, "because you've killed eleven cultists in the past week without reporting any of them to the relevant authorities. Cultists who, I might add, were operating under Shadow Court warrant."
The words hit like a physical blow. Kael's mind raced through the implications.
The Shadow Court wasn't an organization—it was a shadow. A network of deniable assets operating across all six Orders, conducting operations that official channels couldn't sanction. He'd known they existed. Every Awakened above the Ascendant stage had heard whispers about the Court's involvement in various disappearances and political maneuverings.
But Shadow Court warrant? That meant these cultists had been sanctioned operatives. Protected, in some fashion, by the highest levels of the established power structure.
"You're either here to recruit me or silence me," Kael said. "Which is it?"
Vane's lips curved into something that wasn't a smile. "The Court has been monitoring your progress for some time, Kael Ashborn. Your ability to harvest soul fragments without the usual... Degradation... Made you a person of interest. We've lost three operatives to the same cult network you've been dismantling. The difference is, they never came back."
She reached into her coat and withdrew a small leather folder, holding it at arm's length. "The Shadow Court would like to offer you a formal contract. Information sharing, resource access, and official sanction for your harvesting activities in exchange for your cooperation."
Kael didn't take the folder. "And if I refuse?"
"Then the eleven bodies around us become a problem for the Order of Dawn's disciplinary committee. You have no alibi for their deaths, and several witnesses have already been... Encouraged to provide testimony that doesn't favor your position."
The rain intensified, drumming harder against the ruins. Kael assessed his options with the cold clarity that had kept him alive through the Ashwick purges, the prison sentences, and his own execution.
"You mentioned losing three operatives," he said finally. "What were they investigating?"
Vane's expression flickered—the first genuine emotion he'd seen cross her features. "A cell operating in Thornwick's Underbelly district. They were tracking ritual components—specific metals, spiritual conduits, and something called the 'Seamaker's Chalk.' Our people got close to identifying their leadership structure before they went dark."
The Memory Fragment Kael had just harvested stirred in his consciousness, offering connections he hadn't known to look for. The proto-Seal script, the ritual components, the organized nature of the attacks— they formed a pattern that made his skin prickle with wrongness.
"The Bleeding Moon cult isn't random," he said slowly. "They're not recruiting from the desperate and the devout. They're targeting Awakened with specific abilities. People who can manipulate spiritual energy in precise ways."
Vane's eyes narrowed. "How do you know that?"
"Because I just pulled it from one of their corpses." Kael knelt beside the body he'd been examining, pointing to the angular brand. "This is a resonance marker. It identifies someone as a potential... Component. The cultists were harvesting Awakened who fit specific profiles."
"And what profile do you fit?"
The question hung in the damp air between them.
Kael stood, wiping rain from his face. "The only one they can't harvest. I'm not spiritual-sensitive or Awakened in the conventional sense. I'm something worse."
Vane studied him for a long moment. "The Soul Reaver anomaly. We know. The question is whether you understand what you're capable of, or if you're just a blind man swinging a hammer."
"Give me the folder."
She passed it over without hesitation. Inside, Kael found a single sheet of parchment covered in neat handwriting—a list of names, dates, and locations. Three names had been crossed out in red ink. The rest were concentrated in three districts: Underbelly, Ashwick, and something called the Seams.
"This is your operative network?"
"What remains of it. The cult has been systematically eliminating our surveillance points for the past month. They've become bolder—or more desperate. We can't determine which."
Kael's eyes moved to the remaining names. Most were marked with simple designations: *Informant*, *Watcher*, *Relay*. But three names bore different markers. *Deep Cover Asset*. Those three were clustered in the Underbelly district, all marked with the same date: the upcoming Council of Six convocation.
"The cult is planning something for the convocation."
"Obviously." Vane's tone carried a note of frustration. "The Council meets in Thornwick in nine days. Every major faction sends representatives. If the cult wanted maximum impact—"
"No." Kael cut her off, his mind racing through the implications of the Memory Fragment. "They're not targeting the convocation. They're using it as cover. Every Awakened worth noticing will be focused on political maneuvering and territorial disputes. Security will be concentrated on the diplomatic zones."
Leaving other parts of the city exposed. Vulnerable. Undermanned.
"Where would you move assets if you wanted to destabilize the entire seal network?" he asked.
Vane's expression went carefully blank—the look of someone who had just realized they were out of their depth. "You're suggesting they're not targeting the Council at all."
"I'm suggesting the Council is the distraction. Whatever they're planning, it's happening somewhere else. Somewhere that won't get proper attention until it's too late."
The rain stopped as as it had started, leaving the ruins in an oppressive silence. Kael became aware of other sounds—distant traffic from the main thoroughfares, the creak of unstable timbers, and underneath it all, a low vibration that resonated in his bones. Not sound, exactly. More like the world itself was holding its breath.
"We need to move," Vane said. Her hand had drifted toward her daggers. "The resonance from those bodies—"
"Won't attract attention from civilians." Kael finished. He could feel it too—a spiritual signature that hadn't been there before. Fresh. Intentional. Someone had been monitoring his harvest.
"How many?" he asked.
"At least three. Possibly more." Vane had drawn her weapons now, twin daggers that gleamed with etched sigils. "They're positioning around the exits. Did you trigger an alert when you harvested?"
Kael reviewed the Memory Fragment's contents with growing unease. The answer came back negative—the alert hadn't been his doing. The cult had been tracking these specific cultists, waiting for someone to find their bodies.
Waiting for him.
"They knew I'd come here," he said. "This whole thing—the bodies, the archive access, all of it. It's bait."
"Clever." A new voice echoed from the upper floor, followed by the scrape of boots on rotted wood. "The Soul Reaver pieces together the puzzle, just a few moments too late."
Kael looked up as a figure descended through the gap in the ceiling. The cultist wore robes similar to the corpses below, but newer, cleaner, and marked with additional sigils that pulsed with faint spiritual light. The resonance he'd felt came from the figure's hands—palms that seemed to absorb the available light.
"You should have stayed in the shadows," the cultist continued, dropping the final distance to land without sound. "Where your kind belongs."
Vane moved to flank, her daggers up. "You're the leadership. The one running the Thornwick cell."
"I coordinate resources." The cultist's tone carried the bored precision of someone delivering a rehearsed presentation. "Resources include Awakened harvested for specific purposes. You, Soul Reaver, were a secondary objective. The Shadow Court's disruption of our operations made you... Useful. A way to draw out their remaining assets."
"So I was bait," Kael said. "And now that you've drawn out the Shadow Court operative—"
"Now I complete the primary objective." The cultist raised one hand, and the spiritual resonance spiked. From outside, Kael heard screams—civilians caught in whatever was happening on the streets beyond.
"The convocation draws near," the cultist continued. "The Orders grow complacent, believing their seals will hold forever. They don't understand that the membrane between worlds was never meant to be permanent. It was a temporary solution to an eternal problem."
"You're trying to unseal the rifts."
"Not trying." The cultist smiled. "Succeeding. The ritual begins tonight, at midnight. By dawn, the first of the 72 will walk freely among you. The Orders will burn. The Awakened hierarchy will collapse. And from the ashes, a new world will emerge."
Vane lunged without warning, her daggers flashing toward the cultist's throat. The attack should have been lethal—Kael had seen her kill Awakened at the Ascendant stage with similar strikes.
The cultist caught both blades in one hand, the metal shrieking against spiritual-hardened skin. "Predictable. The Shadow Court always responds to provocation."
The ground heaved. Kael stumbled, grabbing a burned beam for support as the ruins shifted around them. Through gaps in the walls, he saw other figures approaching—cultists in the same distinctive robes, moving with coordinated precision.
"Run," Vane spat. "Get the information to the Council."
"Too late for that." The cultist released her daggers, sending her staggering backward. "The Soul Reaver stays. We have questions about the original bearer. Questions that require... Direct extraction."
Kael felt the spiritual pressure intensify, pressing against his consciousness like a physical weight. The cultist was powerful—Awakened at the Transcendent stage, possibly higher. More concerning was the resonance emanating from the brand on the cultist's chest. Not a normal spiritual signature. Something that belonged to the Abyss.
The cultist raised both hands, and reality folded around Kael like a shroud.
When he opened his eyes, he was no longer in the ruined tavern. The new location defied easy description—a space that seemed to exist between spaces, lit by sourceless gray light that cast no shadows. The floor beneath his feet felt solid but looked like smoke frozen mid-dispersal.
Vane was gone. He was alone with the cultist, and three other figures who had materialized from nowhere. All four wore the same unsettling brand, and all four radiated the same wrongness that marked them as something other than human.
"The Soul Reaver ability doesn't occur naturally," the cultist said, as if continuing a lecture. "It was granted. Stolen from something that existed before the seal network, before the Orders, before humanity understood what it was fighting."
Kael's mind raced through implications. The original Soul Reaver. The mystery that had haunted him since his Awakening. If this cultist knew something—
"The original bearer made a bargain," the cultist continued. "Gave up something precious to obtain power over death itself. That bargain was never completed. The original soul fragments remain... Somewhere. Waiting."
"And you want them."
"We want the knowledge of how to replicate the flow. Your ability to harvest without corruption, without the usual degradation—that's the key. We need to understand why you can do what no one else can."
The cultist stepped closer, and Kael felt the spiritual pressure spike again. The space around him seemed to compress, threatening to crush him through sheer spiritual weight.
"The choice is simple, Soul Reaver. Submit to examination, and we'll make it quick. Resist, and we'll take what we need regardless."
Kael met the cultist's eyes—eyes that held no light, no humanity, only the cold calculation of something that had forgotten what warmth meant.
He didn't answer. Instead, he lunged.
His blade found the cultist's throat, severing something vital. Black fluid sprayed from the wound, evaporating before it touched the ground. The cultist's expression shifted from confidence to shock as Kael twisted the blade, widening the wound.
"The primary difference between us," Kael said, "is that I've already died once. Nothing you do can scare me more than that."
The other cultists attacked. Kael felt the impact of spiritual attacks battering his defenses, felt the edge of his consciousness fray under the assault. But his Soul Reaver ability stirred in response—not attacking, but pulling.
The cultist he'd stabbed had stopped moving, and Kael grabbed the falling body by the collar, pressing his palm against the still-warm flesh.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted 1,247 essence from the remains. Combat Ability acquired: Void Step. Soul Fragment acquired."
The knowledge flooded through him—not just the ability, but context. The Void Step allowed short-distance translocation through spaces where reality was thin. He could use it to escape.
But first, he needed to escape the other three.
They came at him with coordinated precision, their attacks designed to trap him in one place while they closed in for the killing blow. Kael dodged the first strike, blocked the second with his blade, and let the third graze his shoulder—minor damage, acceptable risk.
The Void Step activated on instinct. Reality folded around him, and for an instant he existed in a space between spaces, seeing the gray-lit prison from angles that human eyes weren't meant to process.
Then he was outside, falling through rain-slicked streets toward the cobblestones below.
Kael twisted mid-fall, landing in a controlled crouch on a rooftop three buildings away from the ruined tavern. Behind him, he heard the sounds of pursuit—cultists emerging from the nowhere-space, searching for their escaped prey.
He didn't have much time. The information in his hands, combined with what he'd learned from the Memory Fragment and his conversation with Vane, painted a picture that terrified him.
Seventy-two sealed rifts. Seventy-two entities waiting beyond the Veil. And a cult that had learned to harness the power of those entities, using stolen Awakened as components in a ritual designed to accelerate the seal degradation.
The convocation was nine days away. The cult's ritual began tonight. And he was the only one who knew how extensive the conspiracy truly was.
Kael moved across the rooftops, his newly acquired Void Step making the jumps easier, faster, less predictable. Behind him, the cultists' pursuit grew more frantic. Ahead, the city sprawled in its nighttime chaos—taverns emptying, brothels opening, the legitimate and illegitimate economies of Thornwick churning through their nocturnal routines.
He needed to reach the Council's advance team. He needed to warn them about the cult's true target. He needed to understand what the cult meant by "the original soul fragments" and why they believed his ability held the key.
But most urgently, he needed to survive the next hour.
The sound of pursuit grew closer. Kael risked a glance backward and saw shapes moving through the rain—not the cultists he'd escaped, but something larger. Something that moved wrong, limbs bending at angles that suggested the bone structure had been fundamentally altered.
The cult hadn't just recruited Awakened. They'd created something new. Something that drew on Abyssal energy in ways that violated every principle the Orders taught.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted 1,890 essence from the remains. Combat Ability acquired: Void Step. Soul Fragment acquired."
Kael froze. The notification had appeared without his triggering it, suggesting he was in proximity to a corpse he hadn't yet encountered. He scanned the rooftop ahead, searching for the source.
The body lay in a drainage gutter, half-hidden by debris. A figure in gray—the same shade as Vane's coat—bent at an angle that spoke of a fall from considerable height. Kael approached carefully, aware that this could be another trap.
But when he turned the body over, he recognized the face.
Not Vane. Someone else. Someone whose features matched the description in the Shadow Court's file—the deep cover asset in the Underbelly district. The operative who had been positioned to monitor the cult's leadership structure.
Dead. Recently. And still warm enough to harvest.
Kael placed his hand on the cooling flesh and pulled.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted 1,456 essence from the remains. Intelligence Report acquired."
The information that flooded through him nearly drove him to his knees. Not abilities this time. Intelligence. Detailed records of the cult's organizational structure, their supply chains, their timeline.
And their target.
The cult wasn't trying to destabilize the entire seal network. They were focusing on a single rift—a minor one, barely worth mentioning in the Orders' strategic assessments. Rift Alpha-7, located beneath a forgotten cemetery in the Ashwick district. The seal had been degrading for decades, but the maintenance logs suggested it would hold for at least another century.
The cult disagreed. According to this intelligence report, the seal had been compromised six months ago. A small team operating under direct orders from someone called "Prime Zarek" had begun preparations for a partial unsealing—a ritual that would allow one of the 72 to extend a tendril of influence into the Material realm without fully crossing over.
Not a full invasion. A test. A proof of concept.
And if it worked, the cult would have found a way to exploit the barrier between worlds in ways the Orders had never anticipated.
The footsteps behind him grew louder. Kael turned to face the approaching threats—two of the wrong-limbed creatures, their movements grotesquely fluid, their eyes holding the same empty light as the cultist he'd killed.
He had the information he needed. The question now was whether he could survive long enough to use it.
"Zarek," he said aloud, tasting the name. "The Whisper King."
The creatures didn't react to the words. They didn't need to. They were just tools—the visible instruments of a conspiracy that stretched far beyond what he'd imagined.
Kael backed toward the edge of the rooftop, calculating angles and distances. The Void Step would carry him across the gap to the next building, but it would also leave him vulnerable during the transition. If the creatures timed their attack correctly...
One of them lunged.
Kael activated the ability, feeling reality fold around him. The transition took less than a second, but in that moment of vulnerability, he felt something brush against his consciousness—not an attack, but a presence. Something old. Something waiting.
*Little Reaver*, a voice whispered through the void between spaces. *You carry my fragments. You will bring them back to me.*
The landing on the far rooftop sent pain lancing through his ankle—he'd miscalculated the distance—but Kael kept moving, ignoring the injury as he vaulted toward the next building and the next, putting distance between himself and the pursuing horrors.
Behind him, the creatures didn't follow. They'd received new orders, or lost interest. Either way, Kael didn't slow until he reached the border of the Ashwick district, where the narrow streets and cramped buildings gave way to the wider avenues and stone facades of the more respectable neighborhoods.
He found shelter in an abandoned workshop, its previous occupant long since departed for debtor's prison or worse. The space smelled of old grease and sawdust, and the equipment lining the walls had been stripped of anything valuable. But the roof held against the rain, and the single entrance provided defensible ground.
Kael settled against the wall, letting the adrenaline drain from his system. His injuries were minor—the shoulder wound from the cultist, the twisted ankle from the landing. Nothing that would slow him down for long.
But the real damage was internal. The voice in the void. The fragments it mentioned. The implication that his Soul Reaver ability wasn't just a gift or a curse, but something deliberately placed within him by an entity that had been waiting for the right vessel.
He had nine days until the convocation. One night until the ritual at Rift Alpha-7. And no allies he could trust—the Shadow Court operative was dead, Vane's fate remained uncertain, and the Orders would view him with suspicion at best.
Kael pulled out the leather folder Vane had given him, spreading the documents across the dusty floor. Names. Dates. Locations. The scattered pieces of a conspiracy that had been hiding in plain sight for decades.
One name stood out from the rest, circled in ink that had faded almost to invisibility. *Seraphine Duskmantle. Mortician's Guild. Ashwick Chapter. Status: Missing.*
Below it, in different handwriting, a note: *Contact if investigating Sepulcher connection.*
The Sepulcher. Where the original Soul Reaver had supposedly left their records. Where the truth about the ability that haunted him might finally be found.
Kael's hands trembled as he studied the documents. The conspiracy was larger than he'd imagined. The cult's reach extended into every Order, every major city, every level of the Awakened hierarchy. And somewhere in the center of it all, pulling strings that had been tangled for generations, sat a figure known only as Prime Zarek.
The Whisper King.
The rain continued to fall, drumming against the workshop's roof with the same hollow percussion as the ruined tavern. Somewhere in the city, cultists prepared for a ritual that would tear a hole in reality. Somewhere else, the Shadow Court mourned their fallen operative and reassessed their position.
And somewhere, in the space between spaces, something ancient waited for the fragments of itself to be reunited.
Kael closed his eyes, feeling the weight of it all settle on his shoulders. Nine days. One night. And the fate of seventy-two seals hanging in the balance.
The question wasn't whether he could stop the ritual. He'd find a way—he always did. The question was whether he could survive long enough to matter.
A sound at the workshop door interrupted his thoughts. Footsteps. Too quiet to be the pursuing creatures, too deliberate to be a random vagrant.
Kael rose silently, blade in hand, positioning himself beside the entrance. His new Void Step ability pulsed at the edge of his consciousness, ready for use.
The door opened.
Vane stood in the frame, rain streaming from her clothes, blood staining her gray coat in patterns that suggested wounds than slaughter. Her daggers were still in her hands, though her grip had loosened.
"You survived," she said.
"Barely." Kael lowered his blade but didn't sheathe it. "The cultist—the one who took me to that gray space—"
"Dead. Or close enough." Vane stepped inside, moving with the careful precision of someone trying not to attract attention to their injuries. "I have new information. The cult's ritual isn't at the cemetery. It's beneath the cemetery. In the catacombs that predate Thornwick's founding."
Kael's mind raced through the implications. "The seal is underground."
"Which means conventional assault won't work. The entrance is protected by wards that only respond to specific spiritual signatures. Awakened signatures."
"Their operatives."
"Our operatives, once." Vane's expression hardened. "The Shadow Court placed agents in that cult decades ago. Long-term infiltration. The assets are compromised now, but their access codes might still function."
"And you want to use them."
"I want to stop that ritual before dawn. After that, the seal will be weakened beyond recovery. The tendril will extend. And whatever entity waits on the other side will begin feeding."
The horror of it settled over Kael like a physical weight. Seventy-two entities beyond the Veil, each one a threat to reality itself. And this cult—this conspiracy—had found a way to invite them through one at a time.
"The intelligence I harvested," he said slowly. "It mentioned something called the 'Seamaker's Chalk.' Do you know what that is?"
Vane's eyes widened almost imperceptibly. "Where did you access that?"
"One of your operatives. The deep cover asset in the Underbelly. I found the body on a rooftop."
"She's dead?" Vane's composure cracked for just a moment. "We had no confirmation—"
"Her body's still there. I harvested what I could before the cult's pursuit drove me off." Kael paused, weighing the cost of what he was about to say. "The Seamaker's Chalk is a spiritual conduit. It allows entities from the Abyss to manifest partial presence in the Material without fully crossing over."
"And you know this how?"
"Because I pulled it from her memories." Kael met Vane's gaze. "The ritual tonight isn't about unsealing the rift. It's about creating a bridge. A permanent connection that lets something feed without risking full manifestation."
Vane absorbed this information with the cold professionalism of someone who had seen too much to be surprised by anything. "Then we need to destroy the chalk before midnight."
"Or use it to lure out whoever's running the operation." Kael's mind had already begun constructing possibilities. "The ritual requires specific conditions. Specific timing. If we can identify what's needed and deny it to them—"
"We might be able to force them to reveal themselves." Vane finished. "It's risky."
"Everything I do is risky." Kael sheathed his blade. "But I have access to something the cult wants. Something they think will help them complete their process."
"Your Soul Reaver ability."
"My connection to whatever created it. The cultist mentioned an original bearer. Fragments that need to be returned." Kael felt the words forming as he spoke them, truth emerging from instinct than planning. "If I offer myself as bait—"
" They'll try to capture you." Vane's objection was immediate. "Use you as a component in whatever they're planning."
"Which gives us the opportunity to find their leadership. Take out Prime Zarek, whoever that is. Destroy the ritual."
Vane studied him for a long moment. Rain continued to fall outside, the sound creating a barrier of white noise around their conversation. "You're asking me to help you walk into a cultist trap using yourself as the lure."
"I'm asking you to help me stop something that could end the world as we know it." Kael held her gaze. "The Shadow Court lost three operatives to this conspiracy. The Orders don't know what's coming. I'm the only one with the information and the ability to do anything about it."
"And if you fail?"
"Then at least I'll have given you enough time to evacuate whatever assets matter most." Kael's tone carried no bravado, no false confidence. Just the flat assessment of someone who had learned to measure risk against necessity. "But I don't intend to fail."
Vane was silent for several heartbeats. Outside, the rain intensified, hammering against the workshop's roof with renewed fury. Somewhere in the distance, thunder rolled across the sky—the mundane kind, not the spiritual disturbance that accompanied Abyssal manifestation.
"The Shadow Court has resources," she said finally. "Information networks. Safe houses. Limited tactical support." She paused, and Kael saw something shift in her expression—something that might have been respect, or might have been resignation. "I can give you a six-hour window. After that, if you haven't achieved results, I'm pulling out. The Court's priorities don't include martyrdom."
"Six hours is more than enough."
"We'll see." Vane moved toward the door, then paused. "The cemetery entrance is on Harlow Street. Third grave from the eastern wall—the one marked with a broken angel. The catacombs beneath are extensive. If the cult has reinforcements, they'll have positioned them to defend the ritual chamber."
"And if I can't get close enough to trigger their trap?"
"Then you're dead, and this conversation becomes irrelevant." Vane's tone held no sympathy, but no cruelty either. Just the honest assessment of someone who understood exactly what they were risking. "I need to report to my superiors. Coordinate with whatever assets we have left in the city. I'll send word when the window opens."
She disappeared into the rain, leaving Kael alone in the abandoned workshop with documents spread across the floor and a plan that balanced precariously between desperate and insane.
The night stretched ahead. Midnight approached. And beneath the streets of Thornwick, in catacombs that predated human civilization, something ancient stirred in anticipation of a bridge that would finally let it reach across the void.
Kael gathered the documents, securing them in a hidden pocket sewn into his coat. He checked his blade, confirmed the Void Step ability remained accessible, and took stock of his injuries. The shoulder wound had stopped bleeding. The ankle would hold.
It would have to.
He stepped out into the rain, letting the cold water wash away the last traces of exhaustion and doubt. Ahead of him, the city churned through its nocturnal routines—unaware that the barrier between worlds was about to be tested in ways the Orders had never imagined.
The conspiracy's true scope had finally become clear. Now he needed to prove it didn't matter.
Harlow Street waited. The cemetery waited. And somewhere in the darkness beneath the broken angel's grave, Prime Zarek waited for a sacrifice that would never arrive.
Kael walked into the rain, carrying the weight of seventy-two sealed rifts on his shoulders and the fragmented knowledge of an original Soul Reaver in his mind. The night had only just begun.
And it was already too late to turn back.
