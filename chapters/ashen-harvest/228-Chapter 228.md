"Move it, Ashborn. The Sepulcher doesn't excavate itself."
Zarek's voice cut through the pre-dawn mist like a blade through silk. The High Inquisitor stood at the expedition's lead wagon, his ceremonial armor catching the first grey light of Thornwick's winter morning. Steam hissed from the vehicle's auxiliary engines, mixing with the fog rolling off the Ironhallow Canal.
Kael adjusted the straps of his pack and fell into step with the other Awakened prisoners. Seven of them, chained at the wrists with cuffs — spiritual dampeners that would keep their abilities neutralized until they reached the work site. The cuffs were a formality for most. For Kael, they were a joke.
The cuffs couldn't suppress what Soul Reaver was.
"Two miles through the Ashlands," Zarek continued, his tone carrying the practiced authority of a man who'd never questioned his own righteousness. "The Sepulcher lies beneath the old battlefield. Our scholars believe it predates the Orders by three centuries."
Thornwick's gaslamps flickered in the distance, their flames struggling against the damp air. Kael counted seven members of the Iron Vanguard escorting them, their armored boots splashing through puddles that reflected the ashen sky. Too few for a prisoner detail. Too many for a casual expedition.
The Whisper King's voice echoed in his memory: *The cult moves faster than the Orders realize. Your 'ally' serves two masters.*
The High Inquisitor wasn't only gathering cheap labor for archaeological work. Zarek wanted something from the Sepulcher, and he'd chosen expendable Awakened to retrieve it.
Kael's gaze swept the fog. Somewhere in that grey veil, shadows moved that didn't belong to the Vanguard soldiers.
---
The Ashlands earned their name from the bone-white soil and the skeletal trees that dotted the landscape. Three hundred years ago, the First Battle of the Rift had raged here — the conflict that had birthed the seal network and nearly destroyed civilization. Now, wind whistled through the dead branches like mournful voices, carrying the faint scent of sulfur and old death.
The expedition stopped at a cleft in the earth. Stone steps, worn smooth by countless feet, descended into absolute darkness.
"Ashborn." Zarek's command drew Kael forward. The Inquisitor's pale eyes studied him with cold calculation. "You've worked the Sepulcher before. Three years ago, before your... Indiscretion."
"I remember."
"Then you'll lead the advance team. Five others with you." Zarek gestured to a cluster of prisoners. "Mercer. Thorn. The twins. You and one more."
Kael scanned the remaining prisoners. A woman with a shaved head and ritual scars covering her arms caught his attention. Her spiritual signature was muted, carefully controlled — the mark of someone who'd learned to hide their true strength.
"Lyra," he said.
She inclined her head. No words. Her eyes held the flat emptiness of someone who'd seen too much and decided to stop reacting.
The chains rattled as the advance team assembled. A Vanguard sergeant distributed glowstones — crystalline reagents that emitted pale light when activated. Kael's cuffs clinked against the stone as he accepted his share.
"The rest of you dig at the secondary chamber," Zarek instructed. "My preliminary scans indicate structural integrity issues. We need to reach the central archive before the passage collapses entirely."
Kael descended first, one hand trailing along the cold stone wall. The glowstone's light revealed hieroglyphs carved into the rock — early script, predating the modern Order languages by centuries. His fingers traced a symbol that made his stomach clench.
The Soul Reaver mark. Not the modern version the Orders used to identify practitioners. The original sigil, the one that appeared in no official records.
"Problem?" Lyra's voice came from behind him, quiet enough that the others couldn't hear.
"The Orders know what this place is."
"They know enough to send a High Inquisitor and seven armed guards for 'archaeological work.'" Her tone carried bitter amusement. "They know exactly what they're looking for."
The staircase ended in a vast chamber. Dust motes swirled in the glowstones' light, illuminated like golden snow. Shelves carved into the walls held pottery, metal tools, wrapped bundles that might have been documents or human remains. In the center, a circular depression suggested where an altar once stood.
One of the twins — Kael couldn't tell them apart — whistled low. "That's not normal dust. That's spiritual residue."
Kael knelt, touching the floor. His fingers came away tingling. Ancient power had saturated this chamber, and even after centuries, the echo remained. The Orders would pay fortunes for this kind of concentrated essence.
A trap, then. Or bait.
"The main archive is through there." Mercer pointed to a passage on the far side. Unlike the main chamber, no dust covered the floor ahead. Boot prints marked the stone — dozens of them, layered over each other, some fresh enough to still show detail.
"Someone's been here recently," Thorn muttered, his hand drifting toward his concealed weapon. The cuffs prevented them from using abilities, but physical training remained accessible.
Kael counted the tracks. "Six sets. Different sizes. Military boots, not civilian."
"Cult activity," Lyra said flatly. "The Shadow Court has been monitoring this location for weeks. I didn't expect they'd moved this fast."
"Why tell me?" Kael asked.
"Because you're standing in their way." Her eyes met his, and for a moment, the emptiness cracked. Something flickered there — warning, maybe, or calculation. "The cult doesn't negotiate. They don't capture. They consume."
The temperature dropped ten degrees. Kael felt it before he saw it — a wrongness in the air, a pressure against his senses that made his teeth ache.
The glowstones flickered.
Then went dark.
---
Kael's hand closed around the nearest shelf, pulling himself forward as absolute blackness swallowed the chamber. His eyes adjusted slowly, revealing faint shapes against slightly darker shapes. The others moved similarly, trained survivors who'd learned that panic killed faster than enemies.
"Don't," Mercer's whisper barely carried.
A sound echoed from the passage. Wet. Crunching. Like someone eating something that shouldn't be eaten.
Then voices. Not speaking — screaming. Dozens of them, layered over each other, a cacophony of agony that seemed to come from everywhere and nowhere.
The Cult of the Unbound. The Abyssal fanatics who believed the 72 Entities should be freed from their seals, reality be damned.
Kael felt Lyra's hand close on his arm. Her grip was iron. "Secondary exit. Northwest corner. I mapped the chamber before we descended."
"You had time to map—"
"I was told to watch you. Monitoring duty." Her whisper turned sharp. "Now shut up and move."
They retreated through the shadows, navigating by memory and the faint luminescence of spiritual residue. Behind them, the screaming voices converged, coalescing into something that walked on too many legs and breathed with too many mouths.
The cultists had been waiting. The expedition wasn't an excavation — it was a sacrifice. Zarek had delivered them as offerings.
Kael's blood boiled. The High Inquisitor's game crystallized in his mind: use the prisoners to trigger whatever defenses remained in the Sepulcher, weaken the ancient protections, then sweep in with clean hands and take what he wanted. The cult was the cleanup crew, the anonymous violence that would leave no evidence connecting Zarek to the crimes.
Unless Kael killed him first.
The northwest corner held a narrow crack in the stone, barely wide enough for a man to squeeze through. Thorn went first, then the twins, then Mercer. Kael was about to follow when Lyra stopped him.
"The cult brought something up from the deep chamber. I saw them carrying it." Her voice was barely audible over the approaching horror sounds. "It's a corpse. Ancient. But not human."
Kael's mind raced. A corpse meant Soul Reaver potential. Physical contact would let him harvest abilities, memories, information. But getting to that corpse meant going deeper into the Sepulcher, deeper into the cult's grasp.
"I'll draw them," Lyra said. "You get to the corpse."
"Why?"
"Because if you die, my handlers consider the mission failed. And because—" she paused, something shifting in her expression "—the original Soul Reaver is buried somewhere in this complex. The cult is looking for the same thing I am. If they find it first, everything changes."
She didn't wait for his response. Lyra stepped into the chamber's center, her spiritual pressure blazing like a signal fire.
"Kael Ashborn!" Her voice rang through the darkness, addressed to the approaching nightmare. "I am Lyra Vane, agent of the Shadow Court! Your prey stands before you!"
The screaming voices changed pitch, focusing on her. The weight in the air shifted, drawing away from the passage where Kael stood hidden.
He ran.
---
The deep chamber lay three levels below the main archive. Kael navigated by the spiritual residue embedded in the stone, following traces of ancient power that whispered of battles fought and prices paid. His cuffs were useless against this — Soul Reaver wasn't about manipulating spiritual energy. It was about touching what was dead and taking what remained.
The cultists had carved their own path through the rock, using tools that left black scorch marks along the edges. Their work was fast, desperate, driven by prophecy or madness or both.
Kael found the corpse in a reliquary at the chamber's heart.
The body was humanoid but wrong. Six arms, each ending in a different weapon fused to the bone. A face that was almost human, almost beautiful, almost screaming. Spiritual energy radiated from the remains like heat from a forge, concentrated and pure.
A Soul Reaver. The original, maybe, or one of the early practitioners who had served as templates for the Orders' current theories.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted memories of battle from the remains. Abyssal Combat Integration acquired.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted knowledge of the Seal Network's original structure. +15% Understanding.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted fragmented intention. Purpose: ???]
Information flooded through Kael's mind — not his own memories, but borrowed knowledge. He saw the Sepulcher as it had been three centuries ago: a fortress-temple where the first Soul Reavers had trained, their abilities still wild and undefined. He saw the seal network being constructed, stone by stone, using harvested power from fallen Entities. He saw the betrayal that had ended the original Reavers, driven into hiding or killed by the newly-formed Orders who feared what they'd created.
Most critically, he saw the map.
The 72 sealed rifts weren't random. They were arranged in a pattern, a key or a lock or a circuit that maintained the Veil's integrity. Damage one seal, and the stress distributed across the network. Damage enough seals, and the entire system failed simultaneously.
The cult's plan wasn't to free one Entity. They wanted to collapse the network entirely.
And they needed something specific to do it — something that only a Soul Reaver could provide.
The answer hit Kael like a physical blow. The cult wasn't just collecting power. They were collecting souls, harvesting from anyone willing or unwilling, building a reserve of spiritual essence that could be used to break the strongest seals. They needed a Reaver to do the harvesting efficiently, someone whose ability had zero cost and no practical limit.
The Whisper King.
The thought rose unbidden, shaped by the ancient corpse's dying intention. The cult's leader was searching for something that could make their plan succeed. A tool. A weapon. A Reaver under their control.
Kael's blood went cold.
The surface expedition. Zarek. The convenient prisoners delivered as "sacrifice."
It wasn't just Kael they wanted. They wanted every Soul Reaver the Orders had captured or identified. The expedition was a sorting mechanism, a test to see who could survive the initial trap. Survivors would be valuable enough to harvest.
"Kael." Lyra's voice, ragged and fading, echoed down the passage behind him. "They're coming. I can't hold them—"
The screaming voices swelled, closer now, multiplied. The cultists had seen through her distraction.
Kael touched the ancient corpse again, pushing his ability deeper despite the discomfort of harvesting from remains centuries old.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted technique. Soul Anchor: Preserve a fragment of your essence in the material world, allowing limited function even when the body is suppressed.]
The technique slotted into his mind like a key into a lock. Physical touch required, but the effect was subtle — a backup system, a way to maintain presence even when spiritual abilities were blocked.
He gathered the ancient corpse's remaining essence into a vial he'd been carrying for exactly this purpose. The spiritual energy condensed, glowing faintly, taking up barely any space in his pack. The Orders would kill for this kind of concentrated essence. The cult would kill for it too.
Then he moved toward Lyra's voice.
---
He found her in the main archive chamber, surrounded by six cultists in black robes. Her ceremonial blade had shattered, and she was fighting with the fragments, buying time against opponents who seemed more interested in capturing than killing.
Two cultists broke away as Kael emerged, their eyes lighting with recognition.
"The Reaver," one hissed. "The Whisper King will be pleased."
Kael didn't waste breath on response. He moved like water through the shadows, his borrowed Abyssal Combat Integration guiding his limbs through forms that shouldn't have been possible for a human body. The first cultist's strike missed by inches. Kael's counter-move didn't.
His hand closed around the cultist's throat. Physical contact. Enough.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted tactical knowledge from the remains. Enemy formations: understood.]
The cultist collapsed, but the others were already adapting, spreading out to prevent another easy touch. Their coordination spoke of training, of preparation, of someone who'd studied how Soul Reavers operated.
The Shadow Court had shared their intelligence. Lyra's handlers had given the cult information about Reaver capabilities.
Lyra drove a shard of her broken blade into the nearest cultist's eye. Blood sprayed. She twisted the weapon, disabling him. Her movements were efficient, brutal, the technique of someone who'd killed before and would kill again without hesitation.
"The secondary passage," she gasped. "It's not blocked yet."
Two cultists remained standing. Kael engaged the first while Lyra retreated, covering their escape. The enemy's formation dissolved as their numbers dropped, desperation replacing discipline.
Zarek's voice echoed from somewhere above, muffled by stone but recognizable. The High Inquisitor was calling for his soldiers, demanding reports, his careful plans crumbling as the cult revealed its true nature.
The prisoners weren't supposed to survive. The expedition's official records would show everyone killed by Abyssal fanatics. Zarek would mourn publicly and collect whatever the cult left behind.
Kael drove his elbow into the last cultist's solar plexus, following with an open palm strike that connected with the man's sternum. The heart stopped. The body fell.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted spiritual resonance marker. You can sense cult members within 50 meters.]
"Sera found something," Lyra said as they ran. "The Mortician's Guild has been selling soul-essence to the cult. Large quantities. Your investigator friend is trying to trace the supply chain."
"She found your sister," Kael replied. "The one you thought was dead."
Lyra's stride faltered for half a step. She recovered instantly, but Kael saw the impact. Her mask cracked further.
"Where?"
"A facility outside Thornwick. The Guild is holding her for 'spiritual rehabilitation.' It's a harvesting operation. They take people, extract their essence, and discard what's left."
They emerged into grey daylight, the Ashlands spreading before them in all their skeletal misery. The expedition's wagons sat abandoned, the Vanguard guards either dead or fled. In the distance, smoke rose from the direction of Thornwick — the city was under attack.
The cult's offensive had begun.
Zarek stood beside the lead wagon, his ceremonial armor dented and his face pale. Three Vanguard soldiers lay dead at his feet. A fourth knelt, clutching a wound in his side, while the Inquisitor himself held a blade that dripped with blood.
"Ashborn." Zarek's voice shook. "I can explain—"
Kael crossed the distance between them in three strides. His hand closed around the Inquisitor's collar, lifting him off the ground despite the armor's weight.
"You delivered us to the cult. Prisoners as sacrifice." Kael's voice was flat, controlled, dangerous. "How many others? How many expeditions ended like this?"
"The cult has infiltrators everywhere." Zarek's composure cracked, the righteous authority crumbling into raw fear. "They took my family. My daughter. They said they'd kill her if I didn't cooperate. What was I supposed to do?"
"A real question deserves a real answer." Kael's grip tightened. "You chose your daughter over strangers. Over your duty. Over every principle the Orders supposedly stand for."
Zarek's eyes widened. "The Shadow Court sent me. They said they wanted information about Reavers, about the cult's capabilities. I thought it was legitimate intelligence gathering. I didn't know—"
"Didn't know or didn't ask?" Lyra's voice cut through the exchange. She stood five paces away, her broken blade still bloody, her expression carved from ice. "Your handlers in the Shadow Court. Who specifically gave you orders?"
The Inquisitor hesitated. His gaze flickered toward Lyra, then toward Kael, calculating survival odds.
"I never saw faces," he said finally. "Communications were dropped at dead drops. But the seal on the instructions was the Whisper King's personal mark. I recognized it from old files."
Kael's stomach dropped.
The Whisper King wasn't just monitoring the cult. He was directing it.
"Why?" Kael asked. "What's the goal? Why collapse the seal network now?"
"I don't know." Zarek's voice cracked. "I swear on my daughter's life, I don't know. But the cult talks about a convergence. They say the Veil is already failing, that we don't have decades anymore. We have months. Maybe weeks."
The smoke over Thornwick thickened. In the grey sky above, something vast and dark moved against the clouds — a shape too large to be a bird, too wrong to be natural.
The Entities were stirring.
"We need to get back to the city," Lyra said. "The Orders will mobilize. The Council will demand answers. Everything changes now."
Kael released Zarek. The Inquisitor crumpled to his knees, his armor clanking against the stone. The High Inquisitor who had orchestrated murders and sacrifices now looked like what he was: a broken man who'd made terrible choices for understandable reasons.
That didn't make him innocent.
"The Shadow Court knew," Kael said to Lyra. "They sent you to monitor me, but they already had eyes on the cult. They already knew what was happening."
"My handlers are compartmentalized. I only know what they tell me." Her mask had slipped entirely now, revealing exhaustion and something that might have been guilt. "But yes. Someone in the Court has been playing both sides. Feeding information to the cult while pretending to fight them."
"Why?"
"Because they want the same thing the cult wants. They're just not insane about it." She met his gaze, her eyes holding secrets he couldn't read. "The Soul Reaver who founded our abilities wasn't trying to help humanity. He was trying to free himself from the cycle. The Abyssal Entities aren't prisoners — they're fragments of a broken god. The seal network isn't protecting reality. It's keeping the pieces apart."
Kael's mind reeled. Everything the Orders taught, everything the world believed, was built on a lie. The Abyssal Wars hadn't been a defensive action against demonic invasion. They had been a civil war within a shattered divinity, and humanity had been caught in the crossfire.
The original Soul Reaver had discovered this truth. He had tried to use his harvesting abilities to reunite the fragments, to restore what was broken. The Orders had stopped him — and then built their entire religion on the suppression of that knowledge.
The cult wasn't trying to free monsters. They were trying to complete a ritual that had been interrupted three centuries ago.
"The Sepulcher," Kael said slowly. "The first Reaver is buried here. I harvested from his remains. There was something else — a purpose I couldn't read. An intention buried deeper than the memories."
"What kind of intention?"
"I don't know yet. But the cult is looking for it too. The corpse I took — they wanted it more than they wanted me." Kael touched the vial in his pack, feeling the ancient essence pulse against his awareness. "Whatever the original Reaver was trying to do, it's still possible. Maybe more possible now than ever."
Lyra's expression shifted, calculating. "If the cult finds the rest of him — the parts they didn't bring to the surface—"
"Then they complete the ritual themselves. And reality comes apart at the seams."
A horn sounded in the distance. Not Thornwick's alarm bells — the resonant call of the Order of Dawn's emergency mobilization. The Six Orders were responding to the attack, gathering their forces, preparing for a conflict that would dwarf anything the Concordat had ever envisioned.
Zarek struggled to his feet. "The Council will need testimony. I'll tell them everything. The cult, the Shadow Court, the infiltration—" His voice caught. "Everything I did."
"They'll execute you," Lyra said.
"Probably." The Inquisitor straightened, and for a moment, his old authority returned. "But my daughter might live. If the cult loses its leverage, they have no reason to keep her. The Orders can rescue her."
Kael studied the man who had tried to sacrifice him. Hatred was easy. Forgiveness was harder. But Zarek was offering something valuable: testimony that would expose the Shadow Court's role in the conspiracy, that would force the Council to acknowledge what was really happening.
The High Inquisitor wanted redemption. Or at least a chance to save what remained of his family.
"Come with us," Kael said. "You can make your confession to the Council directly. But understand this: if you're lying, if this is another manipulation—"
"Then you'll harvest what's left of me." Zarek's laugh was hollow. "I know what Soul Reavers do, Ashborn. I helped write the classification criteria."
They began walking toward Thornwick, three unlikely allies in a world that was rapidly falling apart. Behind them, the Sepulcher loomed like a wound in the earth, its secrets half-revealed and more dangerous than ever.
The attack on Thornwick was just the beginning. The cult's real offensive had never been military. It was theological, metaphysical, a challenge to everything the Orders had built on foundations of lies and desperation.
And Kael, the death-row prisoner who had become something more, stood at the center of a convergence that would determine whether humanity survived the truth about its own origins.
The vial in his pack pulsed with ancient power. The memories he'd harvested whispered of purposes older than nations. The knowledge of the seal network's structure burned in his mind like a map to catastrophe.
Somewhere in the shadows, the Whisper King was watching. Waiting. Planning the next move in a game that had started three hundred years before Kael's birth.
The fight for reality had just begun.

Kael.

Who are you? he asked silently.

I am what you will become. The first. The last. The Reaver who understood.

The cult's ritual would reunite those fragments without control, without purpose, tearing reality apart in the flow. But if a Soul Reaver harvested them willingly — if someone with the ability to touch the dead gathered each piece deliberately—
The ritual could be completed correctly.
Or it could be stopped entirely.
Kael looked at Lyra, at Zarek, at the burning city ahead. The choice was his. The burden was his.
The 72 Entities stirred in their prisons. The Veil cracked. The convergence approached.
And the Reaver who carried the first Reaver's purpose walked toward a war that would determine everything.
