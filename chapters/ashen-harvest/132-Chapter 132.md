The knife took Kael in the shoulder instead of the throat.
He twisted mid-fall, boots scraping cobblestones slick with evening fog, and drove his elbow into the ribs of his attacker. Bone cracked. The figure staggered back—a woman in a hooded cloak, her face a mask of pale scar tissue.
"Target deflected," she hissed into some unseen device. "Reaver's mobile. Deploying secondary."
Kael yanked the blade free. Blood welled, dark against his black coat, but the wound burned with more than pain—a faint spiritual pressure, the kind that came from poisoned steel. His Ascendant physiology fought it, but the toxin pushed back.
Three more figures materialized from the alley mouth. They moved in formation, professional and patient, surrounding him with a precision that spoke of training. Not common thugs. Not cultists either—those rushed in with religious fervor. These killers worked for someone who valued results over spectacle.
"Shadow Court," Kael said. His voice came out steadier than he felt. "You're a long way from your master's shadow."
The scarred woman pressed a hand to her broken ribs. "The Court has many shadows, Reaver. You should know—you've been living inside one for months."
Kael's hand drifted toward his belt, where a small vial of water rested. Water wouldn't help against these killers. Nothing liquid would. But his mind raced through options, calculating angles, exits, the distance to the nearest ley line junction.
The woman raised her hand. Her people stopped. Waiting.
"The Court doesn't send assassins for stage-two Awakened," she continued, something like respect coloring her voice. "You should feel honored. Prime Zarek himself approved your designation."
Zarek. The name landed like a hammer blow. Kael had been hunting that bastard for weeks, following threads of conspiracy that led nowhere fast. And now the Shadow Court—Zarek's weapon—had found him first.
"Why tell me this?" Kael kept his stance loose, ready to move. "Seems like poor operational security."
"Because you'll die tonight regardless. And the Court prefers its enemies to understand why." The woman's hand dropped. "Kill him cleanly. He earned that much."
They moved.
The first attacker came high with a curved blade. Kael sidestepped, caught the man's wrist, and redirected the momentum into the second attacker. They collided in a tangle of limbs and steel. Kael didn't waste the moment—he seized the opportunity to run.
The scarred woman screamed orders. Boots thundered behind him.
Kael vaulted a stack of crates, hit the ground rolling, and came up facing a dead end. Brick walls rose three stories on either side, slick with condensation and decades of grime. A rusted iron ladder clung to one wall, leading up into darkness.
Behind him, footsteps approached. Two sets, maybe three.
He could fight. Ascendant stage gave him advantages—enhanced reaction time, spiritual energy reserves that burned brighter than anything below. But four-on-one with poisoned steel in his shoulder and fog muffling his senses? Those weren't odds that favored survival.
Kael climbed.
Hand over hand, ignoring the way the ladder groaned and shifted under his weight. The fog thickened as he rose, swallowing the street below until only vague shapes remained—the attackers reaching the base of the wall, looking up.
The scarred woman stood motionless. Her face tilted toward him, eyes catching what little light filtered through the murk.
"Ceiling," she called. "He's going over the rooftops."
Kael hauled himself onto a flat roof, gasping. The shoulder wound sent pulses of cold through his arm, the poison spreading despite his spiritual resistance. He needed to move, needed to find somewhere defensible, needed to—
A figure stepped from the darkness of a rooftop water tank.
Kael froze.
This one was different. Where the others had moved with trained efficiency, this man radiated something else—a pressure that made Kael's teeth ache. Stage three. Maybe higher. The Shadow Court had sent a stage-three killer to handle a stage-two Reaver.
The absurdity of it nearly made him laugh.
"Kael Ashborn." The voice came flat and measured, utterly calm. "The Reaver who walks among the dead. The man Zarek fears more than any Inquisitor."
"Zarek doesn't fear me." Kael planted his feet, ignoring the tremor in his wounded arm. "He fears what I might remember."
The figure tilted his head. A thin smile crossed his face.
"Then maybe it's time you remembered."
He moved. No warning, no stance—just speed that blurred the distance between them. Kael threw himself sideways as a palm swept through the space where his throat had been. The spiritual pressure hit like a physical force, knocking him off balance.
Kael hit the roof hard. Slates cracked under his impact. He rolled, came up with his fists raised, and caught the assassin's knee to his midsection.
The blow should have shattered bone. Instead, it glanced off something invisible—a barrier of concentrated spiritual energy that surrounded the stage-three like armor.
Ascendant versus Transcendent. Kael was outclassed, outmatched, and running out of time.
The assassin seized his throat and lifted. Kael's feet dangled above the rooftop, the man's grip unbreakable. Dark spots crept into his vision.
"The Soul Reaver's origin," the assassin said, almost conversationally. "Zarek believes you carry the weapon that destroyed the original. He's half right. You carry something far more valuable—the knowledge of where the original lies dormant."
Kael clawed at the hand around his throat. His spiritual energy flared, pushing against the man's defenses, but he might as well have tried to move mountains. The assassin tightened his grip.
"The Court will find what you hide, Reaver. One way or another."
Darkness closed in from the edges. Kael's lungs burned. The poison in his shoulder had spread to his chest now, turning his blood sluggish and cold. He was going to die here, on this rooftop, far from anyone who might mourn him.
Something cracked inside his mind.
Not pain—something stranger. A resonance that hummed through his bones, down through the rooftop, into the building below. Kael felt it then: the death echo of this place, layered memories of people who had lived and worked and died within these walls. A factory, fifty years abandoned. Workers crushed by machinery, suicides from the poverty, one woman who'd fallen from this very roof.
Their residual energy called to him. Not from corpses—he needed corpses—but from the spiritual residue that lingered in places saturated with death.
The Soul Reaver's touch extended further than living flesh.
Kael's hand shot out, pressing flat against the assassin's chest. The man's eyes widened. His spiritual armor held for a heartbeat—then shattered.
"Soul Harvest: Transcendent Essence extracted. Abyssal Resonance acquired."
The notification blazed through Kael's consciousness. Power flooded his limbs, borrowed strength from a man who had killed dozens of Awakened without ever expecting to become prey himself.
Kael's foot connected with the assassin's knee. The joint bent backward. The man screamed—a sound of pure shock than pain—and collapsed.
Kael dropped to the rooftop, gasping. His shoulder burned where the poison had spread, but the new energy fought it back, burning through the toxins like fire through oil. He wouldn't have long. The Transcendent essence would fade within minutes if he didn't use it, but he didn't need long.
Below, the scarred woman had climbed the ladder. She crested the roof's edge, blade drawn, and froze when she saw her partner writhing on the ground.
"Retreat," Kael said. His voice came out strange—deeper, resonating with borrowed power. "Tell Zarek the Reaver remembers. Tell him the Court should have sent more than four."
She ran.
Kael let her go. His legs shook as he crossed to the fallen assassin, kneeling beside the man whose spiritual energy now fueled his own temporary ascension. The assassin's eyes were glazed, his breathing shallow. The harvest had taken more than power—it had taken life force.
Would have killed him if Kael had pushed harder. The realization sat cold in his stomach, but he pushed it down. The man had been sent to murder him. Whatever death he'd found, it was kinder than the Shadow Court's usual methods.
"Kill me," the assassin whispered. Blood flecked his lips. "Quick. The Court will... They will know I spoke."
"I didn't take anything you weren't willing to give." Kael stood. "Get out of here. If you survive, tell your masters that Kael Ashborn is no longer hiding. Tell them the Reaver walks openly now, and anyone they send will feed me just like you did."
The man's eyes rolled back. His breathing steadied—not recovery, just the body accepting its new limitations. He'd live, but his spiritual core was fractured. He'd never reach stage three again.
Kael moved to the roof's edge. The fog had thinned, revealing the maze of Thornwick's industrial district spreading below. Smoke rose from a dozen chimneys, orange light spilling from factory windows where the night shift labored for pennies. Somewhere out there, Zarek plotted. Somewhere, the Shadow Court gathered its shadows.
And somewhere, the original Soul Reaver's tomb waited—holding secrets that Zarek would kill to possess and Kael needed to survive.
He climbed down.
The streets had emptied for the night, most workers retreating to cramped apartments or taverns that never saw real light. Kael moved through the shadows, staying off the main thoroughfares, letting the borrowed Transcendent energy burn through his system. His shoulder ached where the poison had been, but the worst had passed. The Abyssal Resonance he'd harvested—that was something new. Something he didn't yet understand.
[Soul Harvest: Transcendent Essence extracted. Abyssal Resonance acquired.]
[New Ability Unlocked: Veil Walker (Passive) — You can perceive spiritual residue in locations of death. Range: 50 meters.]
The notification settled into his awareness like a new piece of furniture in an unfamiliar room. Kael tested it, focusing on the world around him. At first, nothing changed. Then—
Colors bled through the visible spectrum. Where the street intersected ahead, a faint red glow marked the place where someone had died violently. Old blood had been scrubbed away decades ago, but the spiritual imprint remained. In the alley to his left, a tangle of green threads wove through the air—the lingering presence of a natural death, illness, age. Nothing malicious, just the slow fading of a life that had ended peacefully.
And there: a thread of purple so dark it approached black, stretching from a warehouse two blocks away, climbing into the sky like a beacon. Something waited in that warehouse. Something that had died recently and violently.
Kael followed it.
The building was a textile factory, its massive smokestacks cold and dark. A chain-link fence surrounded the property, but the lock had been cut recently—metal twisted by spiritual force than bolt cutters. Fresh footprints marked the mud near the main entrance.
Inside, the darkness was absolute. Kael's enhanced vision cut through it, revealing a cavernous space filled with dormant machinery, massive looms that hadn't turned in years, and stacks of rotting cloth that filled the air with the sweet rot of mold. At the far end, near what had once been a foreman's office, a circle had been drawn on the floor.
Not paint. Something darker. Something that seemed to drink the light.
Four bodies lay at the circle's cardinal points. Cultists—Kael recognized the robes, the branded symbols on their foreheads. They'd died on their knees, hands clasped in prayer, faces frozen in expressions of absolute ecstasy.
At the circle's center, a rift pulsed. Small, maybe a meter across, but growing. The purple light he'd seen from the street originated here—this was a fragment of the Abyss bleeding through into the Material.
"They move faster than I expected."
Kael spun, hand raised. A figure emerged from the shadows of the old office, hands raised in a gesture of peace. Tall, gaunt, wearing the robes of an Order healer—but the insignia had been torn away.
"You're the Reaver." The man stopped ten feet away, keeping distance. "I heard you were in Thornwick. The Inquisitors have been hunting you for months."
"And you're what? A defector?" Kael's voice came out hard. "One of Zarek's puppets?"
"Former Arcane Spire. Current status: hunted by everyone with a stake in these seals." The man gestured at the bodies around the rift. "I've been tracking this cell for three weeks. They performed the ritual six hours ago. That rift wasn't more than a crack when I arrived."
Kael studied him. The man's spiritual signature was muted, suppressed through some technique Kael didn't recognize, but the core burned bright enough to mark him as Awakened. Stage two, maybe stage three—he couldn't tell through the concealment.
"What do they want?" Kael nodded toward the rift. "Why crack a seal when there are seventy-two to choose from?"
"They're not trying to open a rift. They're trying to weaken the membrane." The ex-Spire mage moved closer, his voice dropping. "The cult's been running these micro-rituals for months. Different locations, different seals, always small. The Orders dismiss them as vandalism. Extremist posturing."
"But they're not."
"The cumulative effect is measurable. Each micro-ritual weakens the surrounding seal network by a fraction of a percent. Multiply that by hundreds of locations across the continent, and you get..." He pointed at the pulsing rift. "This. A naturally occurring breach where no seal exists."
The implications hit Kael like a physical blow. The cult wasn't targeting a single rift—they were trying to unmake the entire seal network. Seventy-two Entities, freed simultaneously. The Abyssal Wars would look like a skirmish by comparison.
"Who's leading them?" Kael asked. "Zarek?"
"Zarek's a tool. A puppet dancing on strings he doesn't even know exist." The mage shook his head. "The real power is someone else. Someone who knows the original Soul Reaver's work well enough to reverse-engineer it. The micro-rituals use harvesting techniques that shouldn't exist outside of—"
He stopped. His eyes met Kael's.
"Outside of what?" Kael stepped closer. "Tell me."
"The original Soul Reaver's notes. The Sepulcher Tablets." The mage's voice dropped to barely a whisper. "Someone's been reading them. Someone's been using the techniques they describe to accelerate the seal degradation."
Kael's mind raced. The Sepulcher Tablets—recorded by the first Soul Reaver, containing secrets about the nature of death and the Abyss. He'd seen fragments in his visions, but never the whole. And now someone else had found them, was using their knowledge to tear the world apart.
"Who?" he demanded. "Who has access to the Tablets?"
"I don't know. But I know where they're being studied." The mage reached into his robes and produced a folded paper. "The Shadow Court's main compound in Thornwick. Basement level, warded against spiritual detection. That's where the cult's inner circle meets. That's where the answers are."
Kael took the paper. A map, hand-drawn, marking a location in the merchant district. Legitimate businesses above, shadow operations below. A perfect cover for monsters.
"Why give this to me?" he asked.
"Because you're the only one who can get inside." The mage's face hardened. "Soul Reavers can harvest from the dead—even the spiritually inert. Every guard in that compound was once a person. Get close enough to touch them, and you can take what you need. Skills. Memories. Access codes."
"You want me to infiltrate the Shadow Court."
"I want you to stop what's coming. The Orders won't act without proof, and the Shadow Court destroys evidence better than anyone. But a Soul Reaver who can harvest the truth from their corpses?" He smiled grimly. "That's evidence the Council of Six can't ignore."
He turned to leave, then paused.
"One more thing. The Rift Resurgence—the cult's been accelerating it. Something's changed in the last month. They moved up their timeline." His voice tightened. "Weeks, maybe. Not months."
Then he was gone, slipping back into the shadows as if he'd never existed.
Kael stood alone in the factory, surrounded by the dead, watching the rift pulse with stolen light. The Abyssal Resonance he'd harvested thrummed in his blood, responding to the proximity of Abyssal energy. He could feel the seal network now—not just the broken membrane here, but the distant echoes of the true rifts, each one straining against containment.
The world was ending. Not in a dramatic apocalypse with fire and screaming, but through patient erosion, centuries of decay accelerated by madmen who thought they could control what they'd unleashed.
He had weeks. Maybe less.
Footsteps echoed outside the factory. Multiple sets, approaching with purpose.
Kael moved toward the rear exit, mind already calculating. He needed supplies. Information. Allies, if any could be trusted. The map in his hand marked a starting point, but infiltration meant preparation, and preparation meant time he might not have.
The door at the factory's front burst open. Light flooded in—spiritually-charged lanterns held by figures in the uniforms of the Mortician's Guild. Behind them, moving with cold efficiency, came members of the Order of Dawn.
"In the name of the Concordat," a woman's voice rang out, "you are under arrest for unauthorized spiritual activity and suspected affiliation with Abyssal cults."
Kael recognized that voice. Inquisitor Vane's second, the woman who'd interrogated him three weeks ago.
Smart timing on their part. They'd tracked the cultists, sensed the rift breach, and arrived to find Kael standing in the middle of their ritual circle. Nothing about the scene suggested innocence. A Reaver with stolen power, surrounded by cultist corpses, standing before an active Abyssal rift.
Guilty until proven guilty.
"Stand down, Reaver." The woman stepped forward, her blade drawn. "Make this easier on yourself."
Kael looked at the rift. At the approaching guards. At the woman whose name he didn't know, whose face promised no mercy.
The door behind him was thirty feet away. The guards between him and that door numbered eight, plus the Inquisitor's second. Breaking through would cost lives—theirs or his. The rift behind him pulsed, hungry for spiritual energy, ready to consume anyone foolish enough to touch its edges.
He had seconds to decide.
"If I surrender," Kael said, "what happens to the rift?"
The woman's eyes narrowed. "The Order will seal it. That's not your concern."
"It is if you're just going to plug the hole and leave. This rift formed because the cult's been weakening seals across the city. Across the continent. You seal one breach, ten more open elsewhere."
"Again. Not your concern."
"It is when Zarek's people are laughing at you from whatever hole they're hiding in." Kael raised his hands slowly, showing he wasn't reaching for a weapon. "I know where the Shadow Court's main compound is. I know where they keep the Sepulcher Tablets. I know more about their operation than anyone the Orders have captured alive."
The woman's blade didn't lower. "A likely story. Reavers lie. It's what they do."
"Some do. I don't." Kael held her gaze. "Take me to Inquisitor Vane. Let her decide if I'm useful enough to keep breathing. If I'm lying, you'll know soon enough."
Silence stretched. The guards shifted uneasily, lanterns casting long shadows across the factory floor.
The woman stepped closer. Her blade pressed against Kael's throat, cold steel kissing skin.
"One chance," she whispered. "You betray us, you die screaming. You help us, you die anyway when the Council finds out what you are. The Orders don't negotiate with Soul Reavers. Remember that."
"I remember."
The blade withdrew. Two guards seized Kael's arms, binding his wrists with spiritually-warded chains. Another pressed a cloth over his face—drugged, he realized a moment before darkness took him.
When he woke, he sat in a cell.
Stone walls. No windows. A single spirit-lamp provided dim illumination. His wrists ached where the chains had been, but the poison had fully burned away, replaced by a lingering exhaustion that suggested he'd been out for hours.
The cell door opened. A tray slid across the floor—water, bread, a chunk of dried meat. Kael grabbed the water first, drinking deep. Dehydration was a slower killer than poison, but it still killed.
A figure entered the cell. Tall, angular, wearing the formal robes of an Inquisitor. Gray hair cropped close to the skull, eyes like chips of flint, a face that had seen everything and judged it all wanting.
Inquisitor Vane.
"So," she said, her voice dry as old paper. "The Reaver of Thornwick. I've read your file three times. It keeps changing."
"I get that a lot."
"I imagine you do." She sat on a stool across from his cell, producing a leather notebook. "You claimed knowledge of the Shadow Court's operations. Their compound location. Access to the Sepulcher Tablets. That you understand the true scope of the cult's plans."
"I did."
"I had your claims verified while you slept." Vane flipped a page. "The compound location matches independent intelligence gathered by three separate Order sources. The Sepulcher Tablets match descriptions from the Arcane Spire's restricted archives. Your understanding of the micro-ritual pattern matches observations made by our own investigators."
Kael waited.
"Either you're telling the truth," Vane continued, "or someone fed you information to make you appear credible. Someone who wanted the Orders to believe you were an asset than a threat."
"Could be both."
That earned him a thin smile. "Honest, at least." She leaned forward. "Here's what I know. The cult's been accelerating their timeline. The seal network is degrading faster than our models predicted. Within weeks—maybe less—the cumulative damage will reach a critical threshold. Seventy-two rifts opening simultaneously. The Entities freed. The world as we know it ending."
"Forever."
"Yes." No hesitation, no softening. Vane understood exactly what she was describing. "The Orders will fall. The Concordat will shatter. Everything humanity has built will burn, and what rises from the ashes won't be human at all."
Kael absorbed this. The same information the ex-Spire mage had given him, but from someone who had the authority to act on it.
"What do you want from me?"
Vane stood, circling his cell like a predator assessing prey.
"Soul Reavers harvest memories from the dead. You've used that ability to survive—killing your way through enemies, stealing power, becoming what the world fears." She stopped before him. "But that same ability could save us. If you could harvest information from cultist corpses, you might learn things we cannot. Locations. Names. The identity of whoever's truly leading the Shadow Court."
"You want me to work for the Order of Dawn."
"I want you to work *with* the Order of Dawn. There's a difference." Vane's voice hardened. "You surrender your freedom. We provide resources, access, protection from other factions. You use your abilities to further our goals. At the end of this, the Council will decide your fate."
"And if I refuse?"
"Then you rot in this cell until the rifts open and consume us all. Or I execute you now and save the world the trouble of dealing with a Soul Reaver turned loose."
Kael considered. The chains on his wrists felt heavier than they should. Vane's offer was a collar with better branding—freedom traded for the illusion of purpose. But it also meant access. Resources. A chance to find Zarek, to learn what the Shadow Court knew, to maybe—maybe—survive the catastrophe coming whether he helped or not.
"The compound," he said. "I want access. Full infiltration, not just a peek at the outer guards."
"Granted. With support."
"And I want immunity for any harvesting I do during operations. No questions about methods, no interference from Inquisitors who think they're morally superior."
Vane's jaw tightened. "You want to reave Order personnel if necessary?"
"If necessary. Only if necessary. I won't waste good soldiers for convenience."
The silence stretched. Kael watched Vane's face for any crack in the mask, any sign of what she was truly thinking.
"Done," she said finally. "You get your infiltration. Your immunity. Your resources. In exchange, you deliver information. Real information, not theories and implications. And if you betray us—if you use this access to harm the Order or advance your own agenda—"
"You'll do what you promised. Kill me screaming."
"I'll do worse." Vane extended her hand. "Do we have an accord?"
Kael clasped her hand. The grip was firm, professional, and utterly without warmth. Two predators agreeing to hunt the same prey. What happened after the hunt ended would be a problem for later.
"Welcome to the Order of Dawn's auxiliary operations," Vane said. "Try not to die before you're useful."
She left. The cell door closed. The spirit-lamp dimmed to a faint glow.
Kael sat in the darkness, tasting the metal of chains he'd agreed to wear. The world was ending. The cult was accelerating toward apocalypse. The Orders would tear themselves apart fighting over scraps while the Abyss rose.
And somewhere in the heart of Thornwick, in a compound he'd soon be infiltrating, answers waited. Answers about the Sepulcher Tablets. About the original Soul Reaver. About why he could do what no other Awakened could.
His shoulder still ached where the knife had struck. His wrists bore red marks from the chains. But beneath the pain, something else stirred—the resonance of Transcendent essence he'd harvested, slowly fading but leaving traces that might become useful.
Footsteps echoed in the corridor outside. Heavy, deliberate. More than one set.
Kael rose to his feet. The footsteps stopped before his cell. A face appeared in the spy-hole—a guard he didn't recognize, eyes flat and empty.
Then the face vanished, replaced by the sound of retreating footsteps. Whoever had looked in was gone before Kael could see more.
The cell felt smaller. The walls closer.
Outside, somewhere in the compound, a bell tolled midnight. The night shift changed. Guards rotated. Routines continued.
But someone had come to look. Someone had wanted to see the Reaver in his cage.
And that someone hadn't been Inquisitor Vane.
Kael settled against the wall, watching the spy-hole, waiting for whatever came next. Sleep wouldn't come—not with shadows moving at the edges of his awareness, not with questions multiplying faster than answers.
The Shadow Court knew he was here. The Shadow Court was watching.
And in a compound not three miles from this cell, Zarek planned the end of everything.
The footsteps faded. The cell fell silent. Kael waited.
Outside, in the darkness of Thornwick's industrial district, a rift pulsed with stolen light, growing inch by inch toward a breaking point no one could predict.
