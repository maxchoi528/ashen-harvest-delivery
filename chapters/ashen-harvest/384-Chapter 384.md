The cultist's head separated from his shoulders before he could scream. Kael twisted the blade free, already moving toward the second attacker lunging from the smoke.
Three hooded figures had ambushed him in the collapsed warehouse district, a trap that reeked of desperation and preparation. They wore no sigils, carried no weapons that caught the gaslight, but their coordinated assault spoke of training the common street thug could never possess.
Kael ducked beneath a sweeping leg, drove his elbow into the man's kidney, and felt ribs crack under the impact. The cultist crumpled. The third one hesitated—a fraction of a second, but enough.
"You know what I am," Kael said. "You knew where I'd be."
The cultist's hood fell back, revealing a young face streaked with sweat and conviction. "We know what you're becoming. The Court wants you alive. The Orders want you dead. You have nowhere left to run, Soul Reaver."
Kael's grip tightened on his blade. "The Shadow Court sent you?"
"Questions won't save you." The cultist's hand darted toward his belt. "But they might save—"
A vial shattered against the floor. White smoke erupted, filling the warehouse with choking fog that burned Kael's eyes and scraped his throat. He heard footsteps retreating through the chaos.
Then the building groaned.
Timber cracked somewhere above him. Kael spun toward the exit as the ceiling began to collapse, a cascade of rotted wood and rusted metal raining down. He dove forward, throwing himself through a shattered window, and hit the cobblestones hard enough to drive the breath from his lungs.
The warehouse came down behind him in a thunder of destruction. Dust billowed outward, carrying with it the smell of old smoke and something else—something organic and wrong, like meat left too long in the sun.
Kael rolled onto his back, gasping. His ears rang. His shoulder throbbed where he'd landed wrong. But his mind was already working, cataloging what he'd learned.
The Shadow Court wanted him alive. The cultists had military-grade smoke grenades. They'd known his patrol route with precision that suggested either surveillance or information from someone inside the resistance network he'd been cultivating.
A cough wracked his chest. He sat up slowly, scanning the rubble. The third cultist had vanished—escaped in the chaos or slipped away before the collapse. Either way, the message was clear.
He wasn't safe anywhere in Thornwick.
The smoke began to dissipate, revealing figures emerging from the surrounding shadows. Not cultists this time. Three men in the practical leather armor of city watch, their batons crackling with faint spiritual energy. Leading them was a face Kael recognized—Captain Marren, the watch commander who'd been hunting him since the night of his execution.
"There he is!" Marren pointed. "The dead man walks! Take him down—Orders' bounty says alive if possible!"
Kael pushed himself upright. His blade had fallen a few feet away, half-buried in debris. The watch advanced cautiously, forming a loose perimeter. They knew what he could do, at least in general terms. The bounty notices had been vague but terrifying enough to make professionals cautious.
"The bounty says alive," Kael called out, keeping his voice steady. "Your prisoner just tried to collapse a building on my head. If you'd arrived thirty seconds earlier, you'd be scraping him off the cobblestones."
Marren's jaw tightened. "Save your excuses, Ashborn. The Order of Dawn's Inquisitors are en route. Whatever game you're playing, it ends tonight."
The Order of Dawn. Kael's mind raced through implications. Of all the Six Orders, the Dawn focused most heavily on hunting practitioners of forbidden arts. Soul Reavers topped their list of priorities—right alongside necromancers and anyone dabbling in Entity corruption.
The Inquisitors would have methods. Questions. Tools that made the watch's batons look like children's toys.
Kael's gaze swept the scene, calculating. Three watchmen with basic spiritual augmentation. One exit blocked by Marren. Two other alleys leading into the warehouse district's maze of collapsed buildings and blind corners. If he ran, they'd pursue and eventually corner him. If he fought, he'd leave bodies—more fuel for the fire that was already burning around him.
The Faceless One's fragment stirred somewhere behind his sternum, a cold presence that never fully slept anymore. A resource, if he was desperate enough to use it. A complication he hadn't fully understood.
"Not tonight," Kael muttered.
He dove left, rolling behind a stack of fallen beams, and came up running. Behind him, Marren shouted orders. Footsteps gave chase. Kael vaulted a collapsed wall, landed hard, and kept moving through the darkness.
The district had been abandoned after last month's Entity incursion—a failed breach attempt that the Orders had sealed, but not before the surrounding blocks had burned. Now it served as a no-man's-land between the industrial quarter and the richer districts, populated by those who had nowhere else to go and those who preferred to conduct business away from official eyes.
Kael knew these streets from months of moving through them, finding safe houses and alternate routes. He ducked through a collapsed doorway, squeezed beneath a fallen beam, and emerged into a narrow courtyard choked with dead weeds and garbage.
A body lay in the center of the courtyard.
Fresh. Blood still glistened on the cobblestones, dark and wet in the gaslight filtering through gaps in the surrounding walls. The cultist who'd escaped the warehouse—the one who'd thrown the smoke grenade—lay face-down with a crossbow bolt buried in his back.
Dead before he could report.
Someone had cleaned up the loose end.
Kael approached cautiously, blade ready. But the cultist was truly gone, his spiritual signature fading toward the void. Around him lay evidence of a brief, violent confrontation—scorch marks on the walls, a second crossbow bolt embedded in wood, drag marks suggesting the fight had occurred elsewhere before the cultist had crawled here to die.
The killer was gone. The courtyard held only the dead, the dying light, and Kael's own heartbeat pounding in his ears.
He crouched beside the corpse, examining the wound. The crossbow bolt had entered between the shoulder blades, piercing the heart from behind. Professional work. Single shot, no wasted movement. Whoever had done this had been waiting for the cultist, positioned perfectly to intercept his escape.
The Shadow Court, Kael realized. They'd killed their own agent to prevent information from reaching the Orders. A message to Kael, maybe. Or containment—eliminating anyone who knew too much about the night's events.
The thought should have been cold comfort. Instead, it opened possibilities. The Shadow Court wanted him alive. They were cleaning up cultist failures. Which meant they might have resources, information, a network that could help him survive what was coming.
It also meant he owed them. And debts to the Shadow Court had a way of compounding.
Kael placed his hand on the cooling flesh of the dead cultist, feeling the residual spiritual energy clinging to the corpse like morning dew. The Soul Reaver's hunger stirred, familiar and terrible.
"Soul Harvest," he whispered. "Let's see what you were hiding."
The extraction hit him like ice water flooding his veins. Memories fragmented and reassembled—a flash of a basement ritual chamber, symbols drawn in blood, a voice chanting words that made Kael's ears ring. The cultist's skills came next: a basic proficiency in concealment magic, the kind that helped him slip through shadows and avoid notice. Fragments of knowledge crystallized into understanding.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 14 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Concealment Magic (Basic) acquired.]
The spiritual essence felt thin, degraded—hours-old death reduced the quality significantly. But the skill slotted into Kael's growing arsenal, another tool for survival. Basic concealment wouldn't hide him from trained Awakened, but it might buy him seconds of invisibility against mundane threats.
More valuable were the memories. Kael sifted through them, pulling threads that might prove useful.
The ritual chamber. The symbols. The chanting. It all pointed toward an organized operation—multiple cultists working in concert, following protocols and hierarchies that suggested planning beyond simple fanaticism. This wasn't random worship of the Abyss. This was something structured, funded, directed.
And the voice. The chanting voice in his memory felt familiar, though Kael couldn't place it. Male, commanding, accustomed to obedience. Someone who had orchestrated tonight's ambush with precision.
Zarek. The name surfaced unbidden. Kael hadn't confronted him yet—the framing, the execution, the trail of destruction that had led Kael to this moment. The debt between them remained unpaid. But Zarek's involvement in the cult activity suggested the reckoning might come sooner than expected.
Kael released the corpse, letting the remaining spiritual residue dissipate into the air. The body would be worthless for further harvesting within hours. He should move.
But his attention caught on something else—a leather pouch tied to the cultist's belt, partially hidden beneath the body's prone form. Kael rifled through it with practiced efficiency.
Three vials of soul-essence. High-quality, judging by the density of energy within the glass. The kind that could fuel weeks of spiritual advancement for an Awakened, or purchase significant favors in the right markets.
And a folded piece of paper, sealed with wax that bore no sigil.
Kael broke the seal and unfolded the document. Handwritten orders, concise and brutal in their language:
*The Reaver moves through the eastern district. Eliminate the Order agents pursuing him, but keep the Reaver breathing. The Faceless One's vessel must remain intact. Deliver him to the Thornwick waystation within three days. Failure is not an option.*
The Faceless One's vessel. Kael's blood chilled.
They knew about the fragment embedded in his soul. They wanted to use him—use whatever the Faceless One had planted in him as a child, before his awakening, before any of this had begun. The Soul Reaver's power wasn't the only reason the Shadow Court wanted him alive.
Footsteps echoed from the alley he'd entered through. The watch, still searching. Kael tucked the documents into his coat and moved.
He climbed to the roof of the courtyard's collapsed building, using the pile of debris as a ladder. From this vantage point, he could see the gaslit streets below, the distant fires of the industrial quarter, the spires of the Order compounds rising against the night sky. Thornwick stretched before him like a map of complications, each district hiding its own threats and opportunities.
The documents in his coat changed his calculations significantly. The Shadow Court had been tracking him, orchestrating events around him. The cultists answered to someone higher than local fanatics. And the mention of the Faceless One connected threads he'd been following since discovering his own contamination.
The Faceless One had planted fragments of itself in three individuals. Kael was one. If he could identify the other two, he might understand the Entity's purpose—and maybe find a way to remove the piece that had taken root inside him.
But that was a problem for later. Right now, the Order of Dawn's Inquisitors were approaching. Captain Marren and his watch would soon be joined by genuinely dangerous opponents.
Kael moved across the rooftops, staying low, using the basic concealment he'd just acquired to blur his outline against the night sky. It wouldn't fool a trained Awakened, but it might keep him invisible to the watch searching at street level.
The waystation mentioned in the documents—a Shadow Court operation within Thornwick itself, a place where they kept assets until needed. If he could find it first, he might turn the Shadow Court's own operation against them. Or at least learn more about what they wanted with him.
The cultists were organized. The Shadow Court was watching. The Orders were hunting. Everyone in Thornwick seemed to know something about his movements except him.
Kael paused on a rooftop overlooking the canal district, letting the cold night air clear his head. Below, boats moved through the dark water, carrying cargoes he couldn't identify. The city's heartbeat continued regardless of his personal crises.
"The Six Orders fight the Abyss," he murmured to himself, recalling fragments from his harvest. The cultist's memories had included institutional knowledge—information about the world's structure that even common members were taught. "Seventy-two sealed rifts. Each one containing an Entity that would consume everything if it broke free."
The cultists didn't want the Entities released. They wanted something else. Something that required a Soul Reaver as a component.
The documents mentioned a waystation within three days. If Kael was supposed to be delivered there, he could use that timeline to his advantage. Let them think their trap was closing while he prepared to spring his own.
First, though, he needed to disappear. The Order of Dawn's Inquisitors would sweep the district soon, and their methods of finding a target were far more sophisticated than Marren's watch. Kael needed shelter, time to analyze what he'd learned, and a plan for the coming confrontation.
The Veiled Market. Thornwick's hidden bazaar operated in the spaces between legitimate commerce, accessible only to those who knew the signs and spoke the passwords. Kael had visited before, trading information and occasionally soul-essence for supplies his survival required. The Market's keeper—a woman known only as the Broker—had no loyalty to anyone except profit. But profit could be cultivated.
He dropped down from the rooftop, landing in an alley that smelled of rotting vegetables and industrial runoff. The Veiled Market operated from midnight until dawn, moving locations each night to avoid the watch's attention. Tonight's location, if his information was current, would be in the hollowed-out shell of a church in the Drowned Ward.
Kael made his way through the darkness, letting the concealment magic blur his passage. The streets grew narrower, the buildings more decrepit, the gaslight more sparse. Eventually, he reached a church whose steeple had collapsed inward, the doors hanging open to reveal only darkness within.
He spoke the counter-sign—three words in a language the Orders had tried to exterminate centuries ago. The darkness rippled, and the church interior was visible: lanterns strung between broken pews, stalls selling goods that shimmered with spiritual significance, figures moving through the space with the careful nonchalance of those who dealt in illegal commerce.
The Broker's stall stood at the center, marked by a banner of shifting colors that defied the eye's attempts to focus. She sat behind a table laden with vials, documents, and devices Kael couldn't identify—a woman whose age was impossible to determine, whose face seemed to shift slightly each time he looked directly at it.
"Ashborn." Her voice carried no particular emotion. "You're early for our next appointment. And you're bleeding."
Kael looked down. A shard of wood from the collapsing warehouse had opened a gash along his forearm, blood seeping through his torn sleeve. He'd been so focused on the revelations that he'd ignored the physical damage.
"I need a safe house," he said. "Information on the Order of Dawn's movements tonight. And anything you have on Shadow Court operations in Thornwick."
The Broker's lips curved—not a smile. "That's a significant order. For someone with your particular reputation, the price would be steep."
Kael reached into his coat and produced the three vials of soul-essence. "From a cultist who won't be needing them anymore. High-grade, Entity-touched, worth at least three hundred sovereigns in the right market."
Her eyes—were they black or gold? Kael could never tell—flicked to the vials and back to his face. "You killed cultists tonight. Caught in an ambush you survived. The watch is hunting you. The Orders will be hunting you by dawn. And you want information on the Shadow Court."
"They want something from me," Kael said. "I want to know what."
"Curious." The Broker reached beneath her table and produced a small leather case. "I happen to have exactly what you need. A safe house in the Ash Quarter—defensible, hidden, connected to no one who would sell you out. Details on tonight's Order sweep, including Inquisitor deployment patterns. And whispers about a Shadow Court facility that's been active lately, receiving visitors who don't always leave."
She slid the case across the table. "Three hundred sovereigns worth of soul-essence, as you said. Or something more valuable."
"Name it."
"The cultist whose essence you harvested. I want the memory of his last day. Everything he experienced before you found him."
Kael's hand moved instinctively toward his chest, where the harvested skills and memories resided. The Soul Reaver's power allowed him to retain what he extracted—permanently, as far as he could tell. Giving up those memories meant giving up control of information that might prove crucial.
But the Broker's offer included a Shadow Court facility. A location. Something concrete that could help him understand what he was walking into.
"Twenty percent of the memories," Kael countered. "The cultist's knowledge of Shadow Court operations and ritual procedures. Not his personal observations, not anything that connects directly to tonight's ambush."
The Broker's non-smile widened fractionally. "Seventy percent. You keep personal memories of fear and desperation—I have no use for those. But the operational knowledge is worth more than your soul-essence."
"Thirty percent. You get the ritual knowledge and tactical information. I keep the emotional context, which might be relevant for understanding cultist psychology."
They bargained in the language of the Market—half-words and significant pauses, implications layered upon implications. Eventually, they settled on forty percent of the harvested memories, transferred through a device the Broker provided that would copy the information without removing it from Kael's possession.
The transaction completed, Kael found himself with a safe house location, detailed maps of Order movements, and the address of what the Broker called "the Thornwick waystation"—a warehouse in the industrial quarter that had changed hands three times in the past year without ever officially appearing in any property records.
"One more thing," the Broker said as Kael turned to leave. "The Shadow Court doesn't just want you alive, Ashborn. They want you compliant. Whatever they're planning, it requires your cooperation—or at least your inability to resist. I'd suggest making yourself difficult to capture."
"Any other friendly advice?"
"Friendly?" Her laugh was like breaking glass. "I don't do friendly. But I'll tell you this for free: the man who led tonight's ambush. The voice in the cultist's memory. His name is Varen. He was Order of Dawn before he was Shadow Court. Now he hunts people like you for people worse than me."
Varen. Kael filed the name with the other pieces of the puzzle. An ex-Inquisitor working for the Shadow Court, leading operations against both the Orders and their enemies. Someone with inside knowledge of how the hunters operated—and how to evade them.
Kael left the Veiled Market as the first gray light of dawn touched the eastern sky. The safe house waited in the Ash Quarter, a derelict tenement that had once housed factory workers before the spiritual contamination had made the entire district uninhabitable to all but the desperate and the dead.
Inside, the space was spartan but functional. A cot, a table, a few supplies cached in hidden compartments. Kael barricaded the door, checked the windows for sight lines, and finally allowed himself to rest.
The documents from the cultist's pouch spread across the table. Kael studied them in the dim light filtering through boarded windows, piecing together implications. Three days until the waystation delivery. Order of Dawn Inquisitors sweeping the city tonight. Shadow Court operations coordinating in the background. The Faceless One's fragment stirring beneath his conscious awareness.
The Soul Reaver ability pulsed within him, a cold fire that demanded nothing but offered everything. He could harvest the dead, extract their skills and knowledge, build his power through consumption. The cost wasn't spiritual—it was social, political, the growing reputation that made him a monster in the eyes of every Order and a prize in the eyes of every enemy.
His hand moved to the documents, tracing the seal that bore no sigil. The Shadow Court operated in shadows, never showing their full hand. But they'd shown enough tonight to give Kael a target.
The waystation. Whatever they wanted to do to him, they'd need to bring him there. Which meant preparation, security, resources. A facility that active would have vulnerabilities.
Kael's mind worked through possibilities. He could infiltrate before the deadline, gather intelligence on Shadow Court operations, maybe even disrupt their plans before they came for him. Or he could let them come, spring a trap within a trap, turn their ambush into an opportunity.
The Soul Reaver's hunger whispered approval. The Faceless One's fragment pulsed in response, cold and alien and patient.
Kael gathered the documents and began planning. Three days until the waystation. Three days to prepare. Three days until he confronted whatever the Shadow Court had planned for the Faceless One's vessel.
Outside, Thornwick woke to another gray morning, its streets filling with workers and merchants and those who moved through crowds looking for opportunities. The Orders maintained their eternal vigil over the sealed rifts. The cultists plotted in their hidden chambers. The Shadow Court pulled strings that connected to everything and revealed nothing.
And somewhere in the city, a man named Varen prepared for the next phase of an operation that had been years in the making.
Kael didn't know what waited for him at the waystation. He didn't know what the Shadow Court wanted with the Faceless One's fragment or why they needed a Soul Reaver for their plans. The information asymmetry left him operating on fragments and implications, building a strategy from incomplete data.
But he knew one thing with absolute certainty: they expected him to come willingly or be brought by force. They expected a prisoner, a tool, a vessel for purposes he hadn't chosen.
They didn't expect him to walk into their trap with his eyes open and his blade ready.
Kael smiled, cold and humorless. Let them come. Let them bring their Inquisitors and their cultists and their carefully laid plans. The Soul Reaver had eaten his way across a war-torn continent. He'd survived execution, betrayal, and the endless hunger that came with his curse.
He would not go quietly into whatever darkness they had prepared.
The first harvester of the new day worked through the city's refuse, collecting bodies for the Mortician's Guild. He approached the Ash Quarter's borders cautiously—the contamination kept most people away, but those who lived in the shadows sometimes sheltered in contaminated zones, believing spiritual corruption preferable to starvation.
He found the cultist's corpse in the courtyard where Kael had harvested it, cold and stiff and already beginning to smell. The harvester checked for valuables, found the belt pouch empty, and shrugged. Someone had gotten there first. That was common enough in his line of work.
He reported the find to his supervisor, who noted it in the guild's records. A dead cultist in the Ash Quarter, likely from the night's disturbances. The Orders would be informed, an investigation would be opened, and eventually the matter would be filed away with all the other unsolved deaths that accumulated in a city like Thornwick.
But the harvester's report mentioned one detail that seemed insignificant at the time: the corpse's spiritual signature was almost entirely depleted. Someone had harvested the body with such thoroughness that barely any essence remained.
The Mortician's Guild passed this information to the Order of Dawn's observers, who filed it under "concerning but not actionable." The Shadow Court's agents received the same report through their network of informants, and this time the information moved up the chain of command to someone who understood its implications.
The Soul Reaver had been active in Thornwick. He'd harvested a cultist, gaining whatever knowledge the dead man possessed. Which meant his next moves might be unpredictable, based on information they hadn't expected him to receive.
A message went out through channels that didn't officially exist, reaching a man named Varen as he prepared for another night of hunting. The Reaver knew about the waystation. The Reaver had learned about the Shadow Court's plans. The Reaver would be coming—and he would not be alone.
Varen read the message twice, then burned it. His hand moved to the blade at his side, a weapon that had tasted the blood of a dozen Soul Reavers before Kael Ashborn had ever awakened.
"Alright," he said to the empty room. "Let's see what you're really made of, little corpse-eater."
In the safe house, Kael finished his preparations and lay down to rest. Sleep came reluctantly, populated by dreams of chanting voices and symbols that rearranged themselves into faces he almost recognized.
When he woke, the sun had set and his internal clock told him night had begun. Eighteen hours until the waystation deadline. Eighteen hours to position himself for whatever came next.
He gathered his weapons, checked his supplies, and moved toward the door. The Soul Reaver's hunger walked with him, patient and eternal, promising nothing except the certainty of what it could provide.
The city waited. The Shadow Court waited. The Faceless One's fragment waited, stirring with anticipation that might have been its own or might have been something else entirely.
Kael stepped out into the darkness, ready for whatever came next.
Behind him, unnoticed in the shadows of the safe house, a symbol glowed faintly on the wall—a mark that hadn't been there when he'd arrived, drawn by hands that moved without sound or presence. It pulsed once, twice, then faded to invisibility.
Somewhere in the city, Varen received confirmation that the Reaver had taken the bait.
