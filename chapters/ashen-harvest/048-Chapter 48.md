The Whispering Shade moved like oil across water, a silhouette of darkness threading between the crumbling pillars of the old cathedral. Kael pressed his back against a fallen statue, breath shallow, fingers wrapped around the hilt of his blade. Three days he'd survived in these ruins. Three days without food, without water, without any sign of the extraction point his allies were supposed to establish.
The Shade paused. Its head tilted at an angle no human neck could achieve.
"Kael Ashborn." The voice came from everywhere and nowhere, a chorus of whispers layered over each other. "The Orders want you dead. The cult wants you captured. Even your own Shadow Court has abandoned you." A sound like laughter, sharp and brittle. "Why do you keep running?"
Kael's jaw tightened. Running implied fear. Fear implied weakness. He hadn't survived the gallows, the execution, the impossible resurrection that left him carrying a power that shouldn't exist—just to die in some forgotten church while a monster in the dark asked rhetorical questions.
"Talk is cheap," he said. His voice came out steadier than his heartbeat. "You here to kill me or bore me to death first?"
The Shade flowed forward, and Kael moved.
He threw himself sideways as a tendril of shadow carved through the stone where his head had been. Sparks erupted. The statue's face—some forgotten saint, now faceless—shattered into a cascade of debris. Kael hit the ground rolling, came up with his blade raised, and saw the creature had already repositioned.
It looked different now. More solid. Almost human-shaped, if humans were made of compressed night and had too many joints in their limbs.
"Impressive reflexes," the Shade acknowledged. "The reports, but they undersold your instincts."
Kael's eyes narrowed at the flash of another language. "Who sent you?"
"Does it matter?" The Shade flickered, reappearing three paces closer. "Every hunter in Thornwick knows your name. The Soul Reaver who walks among the dead. The heretic who defies the natural order." Another tilt of that impossible head. "The Orders have posted a bounty that could buy a small fleet. The cult offers something far more... Personal."
Kael's grip on his blade tightened until the leather wrapping creaked. His mind raced through the environment—crumbling pillars, collapsed pews, dust motes drifting through shafts of pale light from the broken windows. No exits he hadn't already mapped. No weapons beyond the steel in his hand and the thing coiled inside his chest that made him different.
The Soul Reaver.
He couldn't see the Shade's soul, not really. But he could feel it—that cold, wrong presence that marked the creature as something that had stepped too far across the boundary between living and dead. A spirit given form through stolen essence. Dangerous, but not invincible.
The Shade struck again, faster this time, tendrils of shadow erupting from its back like a corona. Kael deflected the first, ducked the second, felt the third rake across his shoulder and screamed as frost burned through cloth and skin. He stumbled backward, blood dripping onto the ancient flagstones.

The pillars. The statues. The architecture of a church built to withstand siege.
He feinted left, then burst right, using a fallen pew as a springboard to launch himself toward the central nave. Behind him, he heard the Shade's pursuit—a sound like tearing silk and grinding bones. Kael didn't look back. He was already calculating angles, distances, the structural integrity of pillars that had stood for three centuries and might not survive concentrated assault.
The Shade caught up faster than he'd anticipated. A tendril wrapped around his ankle and yanked. Kael hit the ground hard, teeth cracking together, and felt the creature dragging him backward across the stone. He hacked at the tendril with his blade and severed it—black ichor sprayed across his face, smelling of copper and rot—but two more had already replaced it.
"You can't kill me," the Shade said, almost apologetic. "I'm not truly alive. Your little tricks don't work on me."
Kael's fingers found a shard of broken stone. He drove it into one of the tendrils, channeling not spiritual energy—his reserves were dangerously low after three days of running—but pure, stubborn spite. The Shade shrieked, the sound splitting Kael's ears, and the grip loosened for just an instant.
He twisted free, rolled, came up in a crouch with his blade ready.
"Maybe I'm not trying to kill you," he said, breathing hard. "Maybe I'm just trying to make you angry enough to be stupid."
The Shade reformed its tendrils, slower now, the wounds knitting shut with visible effort. "You're stalling. Waiting for what? No one is coming. Your allies have abandoned this sector. The cult has the extraction routes compromised. You're alone."
"Am I?"
Kael pointed toward the eastern wall, where afternoon light streamed through a gap in the ancient mortar. The Shade glanced—instinct, nothing more—and in that fraction of a second, Kael threw the shard in his hand.
Not at the creature. At the pillar behind it.
Stone screamed. The column, already weakened by age and neglect, gave way with a sound like thunder. The statue on top—a winged figure with a sword raised toward heaven—tumbled forward directly into the Shade's path. The creature tried to flow around it, but the massive stone wings caught its form and disrupted its cohesion for one vital heartbeat.
Kael didn't hesitate. He charged.
His blade found the Shade's core—that dense knot of darkness where stolen essence condensed into something resembling a heart. Steel met shadow and met resistance, the metal shrieking as it tried to cut through spiritual matter. Kael put every ounce of strength into his arms and felt the edge bite deeper.
The Shade screamed again, but this time the sound carried something new. Pain. Real pain.
"How—" The creature's voice fractured. "Your blade isn't Awakened. How are you—"
Kael twisted the blade, feeling the resistance shift as the shadow-matter around the core began to tear. "Told you. Talking is cheap."
The Shade collapsed inward, its form unraveling like smoke in a gale. Kael kept his grip on the sword, kept the edge buried in the creature's center, and felt the last threads of its existence shred apart. The creature made a sound that might have been a word—might have been a name—but the wind took it before Kael could parse the meaning.
Then it was gone. Just a smear of darkness on ancient stone, already fading.
Kael fell to his knees, chest heaving. The wound on his shoulder throbbed with cold fire, and his hands were shaking—not from fear, but from exhaustion. Three days of running. Three days of hiding. And now this.

He forced himself to stand, one hand pressed against his bleeding shoulder. The cold notification persisted—bleeding wasn't going to stop on its own, and he had no medical supplies, no bandages, nothing but his own blood and the stubborn refusal to die that had kept him alive this long.
Movement in the shadows at the far end of the nave.
Kael's blade came up, muscles screaming in protest, and he saw three figures emerge from behind a collapsed section of wall. Two of them wore the gray coats of the Mortician's Guild—collectors, not fighters, but dangerous enough in their own way. The third was someone else. Taller. Sharper. Vesper's smile widened. "The Whispering Shade mentioned your blade isn't Awakened. That was true three minutes ago. But you channeled spiritual energy through it when you struck the creature's core. Kael looked at his sword—worn, unremarkable, the kind of weapon any soldier might carry. He'd taken it from a dead man in the chaos following his resurrection. He'd never thought to question where it came from.
