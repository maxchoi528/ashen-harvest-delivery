The blade found the cultist's throat before the man's scream could form. Kael wrenched steel free, dark spray painting the brick wall behind. A second attacker lunged from the alley's mouth, ceremonial dagger raised. Kael sidestepped, drove his elbow into the man's solar plexus, followed with a pommel strike to the crown of the skull. The cultist crumpled.
"Clear," he breathed, though nothing in Thornwick's underbelly ever stayed clear for long.
Three bodies lay scattered across the cobblestones. Their robes bore the sigil Kael had learned to recognize over the past months — an eye weeping tears of void. The Shadow Tear cult. They numbered in the dozens across the city, scratching at seals they didn't understand, convinced they could bend the Abyss to mortal hands.
Kael crouched beside the nearest corpse. The man's face was frozen mid-yell, mouth still open, eyes staring at nothing. Recent death — within minutes. The harvest would be potent.
His palm pressed flat against cooling flesh.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted combat instinct from the remains. Frenzy acquired.]
The ability slotted into his consciousness like a key finding its lock. Kael felt the ghost of the man's battle-rage flicker through his nerves, then settle into dormancy. Ready when needed.
"Get away from them."
The voice came from everywhere and nowhere. Kael spun, blade raised, scanning the rooftops, the shadows pooling between gas lamps, the mouths of intersecting alleys. Nothing moved.
"We see you, Reaver." The words slithered through the air itself. "We have always seen you."
A figure detached from the darkness ahead. Not a man — not anymore. The thing wore the shape of something that had once been human, stretched wrong, limbs too long, joints bending in directions that hurt to witness. Its skin was absence given form, a hole in the world that still moved.
The Unmaking. Kael had heard whispers of them — what remained when cultists succeeded in their rituals, when they pulled too much from the Abyss and the Abyss pulled back. Living voids. Hungry things that consumed spiritual energy and left nothing but emptiness.
"You carry the mark." The Unmaking's voice was a wound speaking. "The first one left his brand on you. Did you know? Did you understand what he made you?"
Kael's grip tightened on his blade. "I'm not here for philosophy."
"You are here because we allow it." The creature tilted its head at an angle that made Kael's stomach clench. "Every corpse you have touched. Every soul you have devoured. We watched. We measured. You are almost ready."
"Ready for what?"
The Unmaking smiled. The expression split its face open like a wound, revealing nothing behind but deeper darkness.
"For the tomb."
Before Kael could respond, the creature lunged. Faster than anything that broken shape should move. Kael barely deflected the strike, felt the unnatural cold radiate from the limb that scraped his blade. His sword numbed to the touch.
More of them emerged from the shadows. Three, five, a dozen shapes that might once have been human. The Unmaking had brought a pack.
Kael backpedaled, counting exits. Two alleys, one rooftop access, a sewer grate twenty feet to his left. Not good odds against this many.
The nearest Unmaking swiped at his face. Kael ducked, drove his blade through its chest. The creature kept coming, impaled on steel, claws raking his shoulder. Frost exploded across his collarbone. Pain lanced through muscle and bone.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted void-touched resilience from the remains. Cold Resistance acquired.]
The notification burned across his vision even as he ripped his sword free. His shoulder burned with cold fire. He couldn't feel his left arm from elbow to fingertips.
"Fight or die, Reaver." The first Unmaking circled behind him, cutting off the sewer route. "Make your choice."
Kael understood then. They weren't here to kill him — not yet. They were testing. Measuring. For what purpose, he couldn't guess.
That uncertainty was its own kind of prison.
He chose to fight.
The frenzy he'd harvested surged through his blood. Kael let it loose, felt his vision sharpen, his reactions accelerate. The next Unmaking came in high; he deflected low, opened its belly with a backhand cut, pivoted to gut the one trying to flank him.
But they kept coming. Cut one down, two more took its place. Their claws carried cold that sapped his strength, darkness that drained the energy from his limbs. The Law of Soul Conservation meant he couldn't generate power from nothing — every dodge, every block, every strike cost something from his own reserves.
And the Unmakings weren't. They didn't tire. They didn't bleed.
A clawed hand caught his wounded shoulder. Kael screamed as frost spread across his chest, stealing breath, stealing thought. His sword clattered from numb fingers. He dropped to one knee.
The pack leader stood over him.
"Now you see." The creature's voice carried genuine satisfaction. "You are powerful for a mortal. Gifted beyond your peers. But you are still mortal. Still bound by laws you do not understand."
Kael's gaze swept the carnage around him. More cultists dead. More opportunities for harvest. The knowledge of what his ability could do — what it demanded — crystallized in his mind like ice forming on still water.
Soul Reaver. The name others used with fear, with hatred, with desperate hope. He had thought himself its master. Understood now that the relationship ran in only one direction.
"You want me to take something from these things." His voice came out raw, scraped hollow by cold. "That's why you're here. Not to kill me. To show me what I need."
The Unmaking's smile widened. "The first Reaver learned the same lesson. Power demands payment. The dead are currency. Will you spend them, or will you let them spend you?"
Kael's hand closed around his fallen blade. The metal burned with residual cold, but his grip held.
"I'm not interested in your lessons."
"Then you will die ignorant." The creature raised its hand, darkness gathering like storm clouds at its fingertips. "As the first Reaver should have."
Kael dove sideways as the void-bolt shrieked past, melting brick where it struck. Rolled to his feet, grabbed a cultist corpse, dragged it into the path of the next attack. The blast tore through dead flesh instead of living bone.
His palm pressed against cooling meat.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Sacred Flame technique from the remains. Holy Fire I acquired.]
The ability blazed into his awareness — not a memory, not a skill, but understanding itself. Kael felt the fundamental truth of sacred fire unfold within him, the way righteous fury could be shaped into cleansing flame. The Sun God's gift, filtered through a thousand generations of believers, now his to wield.
Power flooded through him. Not the borrowed strength of frenzy or the stolen resistance to cold, but something older, purer. The flame of the faithful, born from conviction given form.
He released it.
Sacred fire erupted from his outstretched hand. Golden light tore through the alley, reducing the nearest Unmakings to screaming ash. The pack leader recoiled, its form beginning to unravel where the flames touched absence.
"Impossible." The creature's voice cracked with something that might have been fear. "You cannot command the Sun's fire. You are a thief, a corpse-robber, an—"
"I'm whatever I need to be." Kael advanced through the conflagration, flames parting around him like water around stone. "You wanted to show me what I am? Congratulations. Now I know."
The Unmaking lunged. Kael met its charge with a blade wreathed in golden fire. The collision threw shadows in every direction. Steel carved through void-flesh, and sacred flame cauterized the wound before darkness could spread.
The creature screamed — a sound like reality tearing — and collapsed into a puddle of absence that quickly evaporated.
Silence fell. The alley reeked of burned flesh, melted stone, ozone. Kael stood in a circle of scorched pavement, breath ragged, shoulder screaming with restored sensation as blood returned to frozen tissue.
One Unmaking remained. It fled into the shadows, moving faster than anything should.
Kael let it go.
The implications crashed over him like a wave breaking against cliffs. Sacred Flame. A skill that belonged to the faithful, to those who worshipped the Sun God, who had devoted their lives to fighting the Abyss. He had stolen it from a corpse and made it his own.
The Six Orders would call this heresy. The Concordat would name this violation. Every principle of spiritual law insisted that abilities flowed from devotion, from study, from the slow accumulation of personal power.
Soul Reaving broke all of it.
Kael sheathed his blade. His hands wouldn't stop shaking.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted memory fragment from the remains. Coordinates acquired — Thornwick catacombs, Sector 7, Vault of Echoes.]
Another notification. More stolen goods, catalogued and sorted within his consciousness. The corpse at his feet had been a cultist True Believer — someone who had spent years climbing the ranks of the Shadow Tear, accumulating secrets, preparing for whatever ritual their masters had planned.
Now Kael owned those secrets.
The memory fragment painted pictures behind his eyes: endless corridors lit by phosphorescent moss, doors sealed with locks that responded only to blood, and at the center of it all, a vault that shouldn't exist. The Vault of Echoes. Where cultists stored the abilities they harvested from their victims.
Where they kept their most dangerous acquisitions.
Kael had been hunting Zarek for months. The man who had framed him, who had painted a target on his back, who had vanished into Thornwick's underworld the moment Kael's execution ended in failure. Every thread of investigation led to dead ends, to cultists who knew nothing, to evidence that evaporated like morning fog.
But this memory fragment — this stolen coordinates — changed everything.
The Vault of Echoes. If Zarek was planning something with the Shadow Tear cult, if the cult's organized activity tied back to the seals, to the Great Rift resurgence that everyone pretended wasn't happening, then the vault would hold answers.
Kael moved through the burned alley, stepping over bodies, checking for survivors. Two cultists breathed still — barely. He made it quick, merciful, pressed his palm to cooling flesh.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted ritual knowledge from the remains. Abyssal Binding Basics acquired.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted contact information from the remains. Shadow Court operative designation: Whisper. Location: Iron Quarter, The Broken Compass.]
The notifications stacked in his vision. Kael absorbed the knowledge without pleasure, felt the weight of stolen lives pressing against his conscience.
The first Soul Reaver had walked this path. The creature — the Unmaking leader — had spoken of the first one as if they knew each other. As if the first Reaver's story was incomplete.
The tomb. The brand. The truth that waited in whatever grave the original Soul Reaver had been buried in.
But that was a question for later. Right now, Kael had a lead worth following.
He found a street lamp, used its light to examine his shoulder. The cold damage had scarred over, pink flesh where frost had killed the tissue. Not permanent disability, but a reminder. The Unmakings could have killed him. They had chosen not to.
The first Reaver had learned the same lesson. What did that mean? What had the original learned that shaped whatever came after?
Kael pushed the questions aside. Pondering mysteries wouldn't keep him alive.
The Broken Compass waited in the Iron Quarter. A Shadow Court operative with the designation Whisper. If the cult was organizing, if the Shadow Court was involved, if Zarek was part of something larger than personal vengeance, then the Broken Compass might hold pieces of that puzzle.
Or it might be a trap designed to kill him. Either way, Kael needed to know.
He walked.
Thornwick at night was a creature of contrasts. Gas lamps painted pools of yellow light across cobblestones slick with moisture. Steam vents hissed from grates, releasing clouds that caught the light and scattered it into ghost images. The wealthy districts slept behind warded walls, while the poor crowded into boarding houses that never fully darkness.
Kael moved through the spaces between. The skills he'd harvested — frenzy, cold resistance, sacred flame, abyssal binding, ritual knowledge — sat in his consciousness like weapons in an armory. Ready if needed. Dangerous if mishandled.
The Iron Quarter announced itself through sound before sight. Hammers rang against metal, a constant percussion that never stopped no matter the hour. Foundries belched smoke that turned the sky a permanent shade of grey. Workers trudged through streets lined with workshops, their faces exhausted, their bodies bent from labor.
The Broken Compass occupied a corner lot, its sign a shattered wheel hanging at an angle that suggested the establishment had seen better centuries. Kael pushed through the door into a common room thick with pipe smoke and cheap whiskey.
Conversations died. A dozen pairs of eyes turned toward him, assessing threat level, calculating whether he was worth the trouble of killing. Kael let his hand rest on his sword hilt.
"Looking for Whisper," he said.
Silence stretched. Then a woman at the bar — grey-haired, scarred, built like a blacksmith's anvil — laughed.
"You found Whisper. Congratulations. Whisper didn't find you." She raised her glass in a mocking salute. "That's how it works, boy. You don't hunt the Court. The Court hunts you."
Kael didn't move. "I'm not hunting. I'm asking questions."
"Everyone asks questions. Few people get answers." The woman drained her glass, set it down with deliberate care. "What makes you special?"
"I have information the Court might want."
"Everyone has information. Most of it isn't worth the air used to speak it."
Kael considered his options. Negotiation wasn't working. Violence would bring the whole establishment down on him, and while he might survive, the chaos would destroy any chance of finding Whisper tonight.
He pulled his hand from his sword. Instead, he let sacred flame flicker at his fingertips — a demonstration, a promise, a threat.
The room's temperature spiked. Gas lamps flared brighter.
"Information about the Shadow Tear cult's Vault of Echoes," he said. "Coordinates I didn't get from you. Skills I didn't buy from anyone. What I have is worth more than anything Whisper could learn from killing me."
The woman's expression shifted. Not fear — Kael had learned to recognize the fear response, and this wasn't it. This was calculation. Interest.
"Touch flame to wood," she said quietly. "Any wood. Show me what you carry."
Kael extended his hand, let a controlled stream of sacred fire lick across a nearby table. The surface blackened, smoked, caught flame. He extinguished it a moment later.
The woman stared at the scorch mark. When she looked back at Kael, something had changed in her eyes.
"You shouldn't have that." Her voice dropped to barely a whisper. "The Sun's fire doesn't acknowledge the unworthy. Doesn't answer to those who haven't sworn the oaths."
"I'm not Sworn to anything."
"That's what makes this interesting." She stood, and Kael realized she was taller than he'd thought, nearly his own height. "Whisper will want to see you. I'll take you. But understand something, boy."
She leaned close. Her breath smelled of tobacco and something sharper.
"The Court doesn't recruit. The Court collects. You walk through that door, you're becoming property. The only way out is death — theirs or yours."
Kael met her gaze. "I'm not looking to join anything."
"No one ever is." She moved toward the back of the establishment. "Follow. Don't speak. Don't touch anything. And for the love of whatever gods you don't believe in, don't try anything stupid."
He followed.
The corridors behind the Broken Compass twisted deeper into the Iron Quarter's underbelly. Kael lost track of turns, realized the woman was leading him in circles designed to confuse any follower. Eventually they reached a door made of black iron, unmarked except for a symbol Kael recognized from his recent harvest.
The Abyssal Binding sigil.
"Whisper," the woman said, knocking a specific pattern. "Guest for you."
"Enter."
The voice came from everywhere and nowhere, similar to the Unmaking but warmer, more human. Kael stepped through the door into a chamber lit by phosphorescent moss, its walls covered in maps, documents, diagrams of spiritual formations he didn't recognize.
And standing at the center, examining a tablet that pulsed with contained power, was a man.
Not old. Not young. The kind of face that had stopped counting years decades ago. Grey eyes that held depths no normal person should possess. Hands that moved with precise grace as he set the tablet down and turned to face Kael.
"So." Whisper's voice carried the weight of someone accustomed to being obeyed. "The Reaver walks into my domain. Tell me, did you come seeking rescue or revenge?"
Kael kept his stance loose, ready. "I'm looking for answers."
"Everyone wants answers." Whisper gestured to a chair. "Sit. We have much to discuss, and I suspect neither of us wants to be standing when the conversation turns."
"Why would it turn?"
"Because you don't understand what you are." Whisper's grey eyes held no mockery, no deception — only a terrible certainty. "You think Soul Reaving makes you powerful. In truth, it makes you a target. The first Reaver learned this lesson. The last Reaver must learn it again."
"The first Reaver." Kael didn't take the offered chair. "You knew them."
"I watched them rise. Watched them fall." Whisper's expression grew distant. "The original Soul Reaver was not a monster, whatever the Orders teach. They were a scholar. A researcher who stumbled onto truths that should have stayed buried."
"And what happened to them?"
"That is the question, isn't it?" Whisper moved to one of the maps on the wall, tapped a location in the Thornwick catacombs. "The original Reaver discovered the Vault of Echoes. Discovered what the cult truly planned. And then they vanished, leaving behind only fragments. A tomb that no one can find. A legacy that haunts everyone who touches it."
Kael's pulse quickened. The Tomb. The Unmaking had mentioned it. This conversation was circling the same mystery.
"You're telling me the original Soul Reaver is dead."
"I'm telling you what the records claim." Whisper turned back to face him. "The truth is more complicated. The original Reaver didn't die. They transcended. And in transcending, they became something the world is not prepared to face."
"Transcended to what?"
"That, my young friend, is what you must discover for yourself." Whisper's smile held no warmth. "You stand at a crossroads. The path you've walked — harvesting the dead, taking what isn't yours, growing stronger with every corpse you touch — leads somewhere. The question is whether you reach that destination as master or servant."
"You're speaking in riddles."
"I'm speaking in truths you lack the context to understand." Whisper reached into his coat, produced a small vial filled with something that swirled like liquid shadow. "This is a fragment of the original Reaver's soul. Harvested from their tomb before it was sealed. I have kept it for decades, waiting for someone worthy to claim it."
Kael stared at the vial. The liquid shadow pulsed in response, and he felt something resonate within him — a recognition, an echo of shared origin.
"What do you want for it?"
"What everyone wants. Information. Service. A future where the Abyss remains sealed and the world continues to exist." Whisper extended the vial. "The cult is moving. The seals are weakening. In less than a month, the Great Rift will open fully, and if we have not prepared, everything burns."
"The cult's mass unsealing timeline." Kael took the vial, felt its cold weight against his palm. "This is what you've been waiting for. Someone who could walk into the Vault of Echoes, take what the cult has gathered, and use it against them."
"The original Reaver would have done exactly that. Before they became what they became." Whisper stepped back. "The choice is yours. Take the soul fragment, gain knowledge that no living being possesses. Or walk away, continue your current path, and face whatever comes unprepared."
Kael looked at the vial. The shadow within seemed to move, reaching toward him with invisible tendrils.
"The Vault of Echoes. Coordinates in my memory. I could go now."
"You could. But you would die. The defenses are designed specifically to kill Soul Reavers." Whisper's expression softened slightly. "The soul fragment contains the keys. The patterns. The understanding you need to survive what waits in those corridors. Without it, you are walking into a slaughterhouse with a butter knife."
"And if I take this? If I gain what the original Reaver left behind?"
"Then you become what they were. Powerful enough to change the world. Dangerous enough that everyone will try to stop you." Whisper spread his hands. "Welcome to the burden of the Soul Reaver. It is not a gift. It is a weight that crushes all who carry it."
Kael weighed the vial in his palm. The shadow within pulsed again, and this time he heard something — a whisper of voice, fragments of memory, the echo of someone who had walked this path before him.
The original Reaver. Whoever they had been. Whatever they had become.
Kael had spent months running from what he was. Hiding his harvests. Avoiding the questions that would lead him back to his own origins. He had told himself he was different, that he could control what the Soul Reaver made him.
Standing here, with a piece of that original darkness in his hand, Kael understood something he had been refusing to accept.
There was no difference. There was no controlling it. The Soul Reaver was what it was, and he was what he was, and the two were already inseparable.
"Tell me about the cult's mass unsealing timeline."
Whisper smiled. It was not a pleasant expression.
"Soon. Very soon. And when it begins, the world will learn what the Abyssal Entities truly are." Whisper moved to the door, opened it. "Take the fragment. Study it. When you are ready to move on the Vault, find me. Until then, know that you are not alone in this fight."
Kael pocketed the vial. The weight of it sat against his chest like a second heartbeat.
"One more question."
"Ask."
"The original Reaver. The one who discovered all this. What was their name?"
Whisper paused at the door. His expression flickered — something old and painful crossing those grey eyes.
"Everyone calls them the First Soul Reaver. A title, not a name." He stepped through the doorway. "But they had a name once. A real one. The tomb holds that secret, and I suspect you will find it there."
The door closed behind him. Kael stood alone in the chamber, surrounded by maps and documents, holding the piece of a mystery that had waited decades for someone to solve it.
He had come to Thornwick looking for answers about Zarek. Found instead a labyrinth of conspiracies, cults, ancient powers, and a legacy he had never asked to inherit.
The Soul Reaver had not been a gift. Whisper was right about that.
It was a chain. And Kael was only beginning to understand how tightly it had wrapped around him.
He needed to move. The Vault of Echoes called to him, coordinates burning in his memory. The soul fragment pulsed with secrets waiting to be learned. And somewhere in this city, Zarek was planning something that might end the world.
Kael left the chamber, navigated the corridors back to the Broken Compass, emerged into the smoke-filled common room. The grey-haired woman watched him go with an expression he couldn't read.
Outside, the Iron Quarter hammers still rang. The smoke still choked the sky. And somewhere beneath his feet, the catacombs waited with their vaults and their secrets and their traps designed to kill whatever approached.
He walked toward the nearest catacomb entrance, the vial warm against his chest, the coordinates clear in his mind.
The original Soul Reaver had walked this path.
Kael intended to survive where they had fallen.
The shadow-touched woman from the bar emerged from the Broken Compass as Kael passed. She fell into step beside him without asking permission.
"The boss said you'd need backup." She checked a knife at her belt, a brutal-looking thing designed for close work. "Name's Greta. I'll keep the rats off your back while you do whatever stupid thing you're planning."
"I don't need—"
"Everyone needs someone." Greta spat to the side. "Especially Reavers. The dead don't watch your back. The living do."
Kael glanced at her. "You know what I am."
"I know what you're becoming." Greta's scarred face hardened. "The question is whether that becomes something useful or something I have to put down."
They reached the catacomb entrance — a yawning mouth of stone descending into darkness. Phosphorescent moss painted the steps in pale green, barely enough light to see the next step ahead.
Greta struck a match, held it up. The flame danced in the subterranean air.
"Sector 7 is three levels down, about two miles east." She started descending. "The Vault of Echoes sits at the end of a corridor that's trapped every way a corridor can be trapped. Oh, and the cult keeps guards there. Real guards, not the shuffled corpses you killed tonight."
Kael followed her into the dark. The vial pulsed against his chest.
"I have the coordinates."
"You have a map. The cult changes the traps every week. And they know someone is coming." Greta's match burned lower. "They've been preparing. Waiting."
"For what?"
"That's the question, isn't it?" She laughed, though nothing about the situation was funny. "Maybe for a Reaver stupid enough to walk into their den. Maybe for the right moment in their timeline. Maybe for something else entirely."
The stairs descended deeper. Air grew colder, damper, carrying the mineral smell of deep earth and the sharper scent of old death.
Greta changed the match. Her face was all shadows and scar tissue in the flickering light.
"Last chance to turn back."
Kael kept walking.
The Vault of Echoes waited below. And whatever answers it held, Kael would face them.
Even if those answers changed everything he thought he knew about himself.
