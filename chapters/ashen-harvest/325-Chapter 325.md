This cultist had been Awakened. Low stage, barely Transcendent, but the soul remnants sang with combat training. Kael's mind filled with half-formed techniques: a defensive stance, a thrust optimized for armor gaps, the muscle memory of someone who'd trained for fifteen years to kill things that shouldn't exist.

The Ashenmoor Wastes stretched before him in all directions—a blasted plain of grey ash and where nothing had grown in living memory. Behind him, the siege engines of three Orders hammered at the cultist fortifications. Ahead, the Abyssal threshold pulsed with sickly light, a wound in reality that leaked wrongness into the world.
The war had reached this place three weeks ago. Before that, it had been a monastery—something about the first Soul Reaver, a tomb or a library, Kael hadn't bothered to learn the details. Now it was a battlefield. The cultists had fortified the old ruins, and the Orders had decided they needed it back.
Kael hadn't cared about the monastery or the ruins or whatever history slept beneath the ash. He'd cared about the corpses.
Three weeks of fighting had produced thousands of them. Cultist bodies, mostly—the Abyssal worshippers threw themselves at the Orders with suicidal fervor, their Entity-touched flesh burning under disciplined spiritual attacks. But enough Awakened fell on both sides to keep Kael fed, growing, accumulating power that would take normal cultivators decades to achieve.
He crouched beside a fallen Iron Vanguard soldier, pressing his palm to the cooling chest. Not yet stiff. Hours old, maybe less.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted 8 points of spiritual essence from the remains. Basic Resilience Technique acquired."
Lesser quality. The man's soul had been plain, soldier-trained, nothing remarkable. But Kael took what he could get. The notification system had become as natural as breathing—a constant stream of data confirming what his body already knew. He was getting stronger. Not fast enough, maybe, but faster than anyone else on this field.
The ground trembled.
Kael's head snapped up, every sense straining. The tremor came again, rhythmic, purposeful—and wrong. Not artillery. Not the psychic thunder of clashing Awakened. Something heavier. Something that didn't belong on a battlefield of men and women wielding spiritual power.
"Second squad, hold position!" Voss's voice crackled through a communication array. "We've got movement at grid reference seven-seven. Big signature. Awakened, high stage, approaching from the northeast."
High stage. Kael's pulse quickened. Cultist leadership rarely exposed themselves to battle, preferring to direct their minions from safe distances. When they did venture forth...
A shape resolved through the smoke. Tall, impossibly tall, walking with the measured steps of something that had forgotten how to hurry. Ash swirled around its legs. Its body was human in outline but wrong in proportion—arms too long, torso stretched like a marionette, face obscured by a hood woven from something that wasn't cloth.
[Warning] Entity-Class Spiritual Pressure detected. Source: Unknown. Recommended action: retreat or engage with caution.
Kael's vision darkened at the edges. The pressure rolled over him like a physical weight, testing his reserves, probing for weakness. He knew this sensation. He'd felt it when fighting the Abyssal Remnant that had nearly killed him in Thornwick, when battling the Order champions who'd tried to execute him for crimes he hadn't committed.
But this felt different. Older. More *patient*.
The figure stopped fifty yards away. The hood turned toward him, and beneath the fabric, Kael glimpsed movement—not eyes, but the suggestion of them. Hollow spaces that watched without seeing.
"You carry the blade."
The voice came from everywhere and nowhere, vibrating through Kael's bones than his ears. It wasn't a question.
"Identify yourself," Kael called back, his hand drifting to the sword at his hip. Soul Reaver. The weapon that had been with him since his execution, the tool that had returned him to life and set him on this path of power and blood.
"You carry the blade." The figure drifted closer, ash rising in its wake. "The First made that weapon. The First walked this ground. The First died here, so that others might live."
Kael's blood went cold.
The First. The original Soul Reaver. The legend he'd been chasing since his Awakening, the mystery that had driven him across continents and through countless battles. He'd heard fragments—a warrior who'd wielded death as a tool, who'd fought the Abyss before the Orders even existed, who'd bound their power into a weapon that passed from hand to hand across generations.
And that warrior had died here.
"You lie," Kael said, but his voice wavered. The spiritual pressure emanating from the figure was immense, beyond anything he'd encountered in mortal combat. If it wanted to kill him, he'd be dead already. "The First Soul Reaver is a myth. A story to frighten children."
"Stories are true in the ways that matter." The figure reached up, pulling back the hood.
Kael's breath caught. The face beneath was human once, he thought—features that might have been handsome before whatever this was had hollowed them out. Eyes that had become windows into nothing. A mouth that moved without sound, shaping words that appeared directly in Kael's mind.
*You seek the tomb. You seek the knowledge. You seek the power that was taken from you.*
"I don't know what you're—"
*The seal remembers. The seal always remembers.*
The ground beneath Kael's feet shifted. Not physically—the earth remained solid, the ash unmoved—but something deeper. A resonance. His connection to Soul Reaver flared white-hot, the blade at his hip singing with recognition.
*Here*, the voice whispered. *Here, the First fell. Here, the blade was forged. Here, the truth waits.*
The figure raised one elongated arm, pointing toward the ruined monastery beyond the cultist fortifications. Toward rubble and ash and graves.
*The tomb lies beneath. The tomb waits.*
Then the moment broke. The spiritual pressure vanished as if it had never existed. The figure crumpled, collapsing into ash that scattered on a wind Kael couldn't feel. Where it had stood, nothing remained but a scorched patch of earth and the lingering taste of Abyssal corruption.
Kael stood frozen, his heart hammering against his ribs. The notification system had gone silent—no harvest, no system message, nothing. Just the echo of words that shouldn't have existed.
*The First died here.*
*Here, the blade was forged.*
*Here, the truth waits.*
"Kael!" Commander Voss's voice cut through his paralysis. "Kael, what happened? The entity—where did it—"
"Gone," Kael managed. His voice sounded strange to his own ears. "It left."
Voss appeared at his side, face grimy with smoke and blood. The man had lost his left arm three days ago and refused evacuation. Iron Vanguard to the bone. "We saw it. Whole squad saw it. What was that thing?"
"A ghost." The word tasted bitter. "A remnant of something old."
"You need to get to the medics. That pressure could have—"
"I'm fine." Kael turned toward the ruins. The monastery rose against the grey sky like a broken tooth, its walls pocked with cannon damage, its towers crumbled into rubble. Somewhere beneath that destruction lay a tomb. Somewhere in that tomb lay answers.
The First had walked this ground. The First had died here. And, impossibly, Kael carried the weapon that the First had forged.
"Kael." Voss's hand caught his arm. "The assault begins in ten minutes. You're with me. We need you—"
"No." Kael pulled free. "I need to go to the ruins. Now."
"That's insane. The cultists have fortified—"
"Then I'll cut through them." Kael met the commander's eyes. "Voss, listen to me. That thing—the remnant—it told me something. Something about the war, about the seals, about how to end this."
Voss's expression shifted. The man had seen too much to dismiss the supernatural entirely. "What did it tell you?"
"That the First Soul Reaver died here. That this place holds answers we can't get anywhere else." Kael drew Soul Reaver, watching light play along its edge. "And that I'm the first to carry the blade in three hundred years."
Silence stretched between them.
"You can't be serious," Voss said finally.
"Watch me."
Kael turned toward the fortifications and began to walk.

They didn't expect a single figure moving through the smoke like a ghost, killing with surgical precision, leaving bodies that twitched and went still in his wake.
Kael harvested as he moved. A sentry's hands around his throat—crushed, throat opened, palm pressed to cooling flesh. Two more in a trench, their Entity-touched bodies burning with residual corruption that he stripped away like dead skin.

The First had walked here. The First had died here.
And Kael would find out why.
The courtyard was empty. Bodies lay scattered across the flagstones—cultists killed in the initial bombardment, their blood long since dried to rust. Kael stepped over them without looking down. He'd learned long ago not to look too closely at the faces. They started to blur together after a while. Man, woman, child, soldier, civilian—it didn't matter. Dead was dead, and dead bodies were resources.
The monastery had been old before the Orders existed. Kael could see it in the architecture—the massive stone blocks fitted together without mortar, the narrow windows designed to withstand siege, the walls thick enough to swallow a man whole. This place had been built for war, for faith, for something that predated the modern understanding of spiritual power.
He found the entrance to the crypts behind the altar.
A stone door, easily ten feet tall, covered in script that hurt to look at directly. The characters seemed to shift when observed from the corner of his eye—now letters, now symbols, now something that his brain refused to process. Kael pressed his palm against the surface, feeling for the mechanism.
The stone flared with light.
Kael yanked his hand back, but the illumination held steady—not hostile, but expectant. The script resolved into something almost readable: a language he didn't know but understood.
*Only the blade may open the way.*
He drew Soul Reaver. The weapon sang as it left the scabbard, a note of pure recognition that echoed through the empty chamber. Kael raised the blade and pressed its edge to the stone.
The door didn't open so much as *cease to exist*. One moment, solid rock blocked his path; the next, a passage yawned before him, stairs descending into darkness that his eyes couldn't penetrate.
[System Notification] Soul Anchor detected. First Blade resonance confirmed. Accessing restricted archive.
Kael hesitated at the threshold. The darkness below breathed with something that might have been air or might have been the presence of death so old it had forgotten how to rot. His spiritual senses screamed warnings—too much residual energy, too many fragments of power clinging to the walls, a concentration of soul-stuff that made his teeth ache.
But beneath the warnings, something else. A pull. An invitation.
He descended.
The stairs went down farther than any basement should go. Kael counted steps—fifty, a hundred, two hundred—until the numbers lost meaning and he walked. The darkness remained absolute, but he could feel the walls close around him, could sense the weight of stone and earth pressing down from above.
Finally, the stairs ended.
Light bloomed ahead—not torchlight, not spiritual fire, but something gentler. A soft, steady glow that revealed a chamber carved from living rock. The walls were covered in the same shifting script as the door above, but here the symbols organized themselves into images: a figure holding a blade, standing against shadows that had no right to exist, surrounded by bodies that lay in patterns of sacrifice.
The First Soul Reaver. The history Kael had been chasing, written in stone.
And at the center of the chamber, on a plinth of white marble that seemed to glow from within, lay a skeleton.
Not decomposed—*preserved*. Bone bleached to impossible whiteness, arranged in a seated position that suggested the person had died mid-meditation. The skull tilted forward, chin resting on a chest cavity that had been carved open and never closed. Inside, where organs should have been, there was only darkness. A void. An absence so complete it drew the eye like a wound.
Kael approached the skeleton slowly, every instinct screaming that this was wrong. The spiritual pressure here was different from the entity outside—not hostile, not demanding, but *heavy*. The weight of three centuries of waiting.
He stopped three feet from the plinth. Close enough to see the details: the cracks in the skull, the yellowing of teeth that had survived the ages, the position of hands folded in the lap. Whoever this had been, they'd died consciously. Deliberately. In a pose of peace that belied the violence of their passing.
*You found me.*
The voice came from everywhere and nowhere, resonating through the chamber like a struck bell. Kael spun, Soul Reaver raised, but there was no one else in the room. The skeleton remained motionless on its plinth.

The skeleton. The First Soul Reaver. Speaking from beyond death, from beyond whatever existed after the soul departed.

But they are locked behind seals that even I could not break.* The voice dropped lower, intimate as a whisper. *There is a door beneath my bones. Kael's stomach turned. "You want me to murder someone."

You take from the dead, yes, but you give in return. Every harvest weakens you—not spiritually, but something deeper. The blade takes a piece of your soul with each use. Eventually, there will be nothing left but hunger and the weapon.*
"That's not—" Kael's protest died in his throat. He thought of his harvests. The thousands of corpses he'd drained, the skills and memories he'd claimed, the power that had elevated him from death row to Sovereign in a matter of months. Had he felt different lately? Emptier? "You're lying."

I harvested and grew and became the most powerful Awakened of my age. And when the Abyss came, when the seals failed and the Entities walked free, I gave everything I had to seal them again. My soul. Kael's mind raced. The First Soul Reaver—his predecessor—had committed suicide to forge a weapon capable of containing the Abyss. Every Soul Reaver since had harvested power, grown strong, then eventually sacrificed themselves to maintain the seals. The blade wasn't just a tool. It was a chain. A burden passed from corpse to corpse across centuries.
And he was next.

Zarek. The name hit Kael like a physical blow. The same Zarek who had framed him for murder in Thornwick, who had orchestrated his execution, who had set in motion the chain of events that had led him here. His oldest enemy. The architect of his death.

Someone whose death will fuel the blade long enough for you to face what waits inside.*

And then there was Zarek.
The man who had framed him. The man who had murdered him. The man who was even now gathering power, preparing to claim the blade that Kael carried. Their confrontation had been inevitable from the moment Kael had risen from his own grave.
