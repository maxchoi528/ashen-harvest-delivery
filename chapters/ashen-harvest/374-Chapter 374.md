Blood splattered across the cobblestones as Kael drove his palm through the cultist's chest cavity. The body convulsed once, then went still—heart stopped, lungs empty, soul departing for whatever oblivion awaited. He pressed his hand deeper, fingers closing around the fading ember of spiritual energy anchored to the corpse's sternum.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted 847 units of spiritual essence from the remains. 1 fragmentary memory acquired."
The notification pulsed in his mind like a brand. Seven hundred years ago, a Soul Reaver had carved the same ability into humanity's collective soul. The knowledge should have died with him. Instead, it had propagated through some twisted miracle of blood or circumstance, surfacing in death-row prisoners and desperate outcasts across generations.
Kael wrenched his hand free and wiped the gore on his coat. The alley stank of coal smoke and copper. Above him, Thornwick's gas lamps flickered through the evening mist, casting orange halos that did nothing to push back the darkness gathering in the city's corners.
He checked the pouch at his hip. Three soul-essence vials rattled inside—black market currency worth three hundred gold sovereigns if he found the right buyer. The Mortician's Guild would pay half that and ask questions. The Veiled Market would pay premium and ask no questions at all.
A single question burned through his thoughts: why would an organized cell of Entity-worshippers target a warehouse full of grain shipments bound for the Iron Vanguard's northern garrison?
Footsteps echoed from the alley's far end. Kael spun, spiritual energy coiling around his forearms like serpents preparing to strike. Three figures emerged from the gloom—two wearing the grey coats of the Order of Dawn's enforcers, one dressed in civilian clothes that fit too well to be accidental.
"Ashborn." The civilian's voice carried the clipped precision of someone accustomed to being obeyed. "The Order requires your testimony."
Kael's eyes narrowed. "Testimony about what?"
"The Grain Riots. The Order of Dawn has opened an official inquiry into the recent disturbances in the merchant quarter."
"The riots happened two weeks ago. Your enforcers investigated, found cultist involvement, and closed the case." Kael gestured toward the cooling corpse at his feet. "I was handling the cleanup. Seems your official inquiry missed some threads."
The civilian's expression didn't change, but something shifted behind his eyes—a calculation, a reassessment. "The Order's inquiry has... Expanded. New evidence suggests the cultist involvement was more extensive than initially determined."
"New evidence." Kael tasted the words like poison. "Or new convenient facts?"
One of the enforcers—a broad-shouldered woman with the silver insignia of a Dawn Marshal on her collar—stepped forward. Her hand rested on her sword hilt with practiced casualness. "Master Ashborn, the Order requests your cooperation. As a registered Awakened operating within Thornwick's jurisdiction, you are obligated to submit to inquiry when summoned."
"Registered?" Kael's laugh came out harsh. "I'm a death-row escapee who was supposed to hang three months ago. The only registration I have is a warrant with my name on it."
"Registration granted by the Order of Dawn's Archpriest after your... Unique contribution to the Thornwick Sealing Initiative." The civilian smiled thinly. "You were made an exception. Denying this inquiry would invalidate that exception. Permanently."
The threat hung in the air like smoke from a recently extinguished candle. Kael understood perfectly—the Order had given him legitimacy to keep him leashed. Revoke that legitimacy, and every bounty hunter, half-trained militia member, and ambitious enforcer in Thornwick would have legal grounds to cut him down.
Or he could walk away and prove he didn't need their leash in the first place.
He looked at the cultist corpse again. The body's residual energy still tingled against his senses—faint traces of Entity corruption woven through the spiritual fabric like rot through diseased wood. These weren't random cultists. They'd been organized, equipped, positioned. Someone had fed them information about the grain shipments, the patrol routes, the timing of the Iron Vanguard's resupply convoys.
The question crystallized with terrible clarity: who in Thornwick had access to that kind of intelligence?
"I'll accompany you," Kael said slowly. "But I'm keeping the body."
The Dawn Marshal's hand tightened on her sword. "That's not—"
"The body comes with me. Non-negotiable." Kael stepped forward until he stood over his kill, then crouched and heaved the corpse over his shoulder. The weight was substantial—muscle and bone and the lingering warmth of recently departed life. "You want my testimony about cult activity? Here's your evidence. An organized cell, active in Thornwick, targeting Order supply lines. I'll tell you everything I know. But this body doesn't leave my possession until I've harvested everything useful from it."
The civilian traded glances with the Marshal. Some silent communication passed between them—an argument conducted entirely through eyebrow positions and subtle posture shifts. Finally, the civilian nodded.
"Acceptable. The Order values cooperation." He turned and gestured toward the alley's exit. "Our transport awaits on Merchant Street. Shall we?"
Kael followed, the cultist's corpse growing heavier with each step. The evening fog had thickened into genuine mist, rolling through the narrow streets like the breath of something vast and hungry. Somewhere in the distance, a factory whistle screamed its change-of-shift signal—a sound Kael had heard a hundred times during his months in Thornwick's prison, a reminder that the world continued spinning even when a man's own life had stopped.
They walked for twenty minutes through increasingly upscale neighborhoods. The cramped warrens of the merchant quarter gave way to cobblestone avenues lined with townhouses sporting brass fixtures and ivy-covered walls. The homes of Thornwick's wealthy Awakened, the nobility of a spiritual hierarchy that had replaced bloodline with power as the measure of a man's worth.
The transport was a closed carriage bearing the Order of Dawn's sunburst insignia. Two more enforcers waited inside, their presence making the confined space feel even smaller. Kael seated himself across from the civilian and the Dawn Marshal, the cultist's corpse arranged carefully at his feet.
"So," he said as the carriage lurched into motion, "who's asking the questions?"
"Investigator Corvin Ashworth." The civilian inclined his head slightly. "Special Affairs Division. I've been assigned to examine recent discrepancies in Thornwick's anti-cult operations."
"Discrepancies." Kael let the word roll around his mouth. "Meaning?"
"Meaning that over the past six months, cultist activity in Thornwick has increased by three hundred percent, but Order of Dawn intercepts have decreased by forty percent. Meaning that the Shadow Court's known operatives have been spotted near cultist gathering points without initiating the hostility our records indicate they should. Meaning—" Ashworth paused, studying Kael's face with unsettling intensity, "—that someone with inside knowledge of our operations has been feeding information to cultist cells."
The carriage hit a rut, and the corpse slid sideways. Kael caught it with his foot, propping it back into position. "You think I have this inside knowledge."
"I think you're the only person in Thornwick who has killed cultists, harvested their souls, and lived to discuss it. I think whatever you extracted from those corpses contains information that would be... Valuable to someone running a conspiracy from within the Orders."
Kael felt the hair rise on the back of his neck. Ashworth wasn't asking about the Grain Riots. This was something else entirely—a fishing expedition, testing to see what Kael knew, what he might reveal.
"Three questions," Kael said. "First: why me? Second: why now? Third: who else knows you're having this conversation?"
Ashworth's smile didn't reach his eyes. "First: because you're unknown quantity operating in known quantities. The Orders have spent decades mapping their enemies, and you've emerged from nowhere with abilities that shouldn't exist. Second: because the Great Rift's seal is failing faster than projected, and every cultist action we've observed in the past year points toward accelerating that failure. Third: only the Archpriest and myself. This carriage is warded against spiritual eavesdropping."
The Great Rift. Kael had heard whispers about it during his time in prison—the largest of the seventy-two sealed rifts, the wound in reality where the most powerful Entities had been imprisoned a thousand years ago. If that seal failed...
"Show me what you want to know," Kael said finally.
Ashworth reached into his coat and produced a small leather journal, its pages covered in cramped handwriting. He flipped to a marked section and extended it toward Kael.
"This is a list of names. Cultist operatives we've identified but never successfully captured. Note the pattern."
Kael took the journal. The names meant nothing to him, but the dates beside them told a story—operations planned, Order responses coordinated, cultist raids executed with precision. A perfect record of interceptions that should have disrupted every attack but instead had allowed the cultists to slip away each time.
"They're ghosting your operations," Kael said slowly. "Every time your people move, the cultists already know. Either your people are compromised, or—"
"Or someone is impersonating Order of Dawn officers and feeding false intelligence." Ashworth's voice had dropped to barely above a whisper. "We've discovered three cases in the past month where enforcers reported to duty but were later found dead, their identities apparently assumed by others."
The implications settled into Kael's mind like stones dropped into dark water. If cultists could impersonate Order enforcers, they could access sealed intelligence, manipulate patrol routes, coordinate attacks that appeared to come from inside the Orders themselves.
"Why tell me this?" Kael asked. "I'm a fugitive. I have no loyalty to your organization."
"No. But you have loyalty to your own survival." Ashworth leaned forward, his eyes boring into Kael's. "The cultists have been recruiting. Not random desperation—targeted recruitment of Awakened with specific abilities. Your name appeared on three separate intelligence reports as a potential recruitment target."
"They tried." Kael thought of the cultist cell he'd dismantled in the Ashford district, the leader who'd offered him power in exchange for service to something called the Faceless One. "I declined."
"Declined publicly. But we've received information suggesting the Shadow Court has taken renewed interest in your... Unique talents. The Soul Reaver ability is rare. Maybe one in ten million Awakened manifests it naturally. The cultists would value you as a weapon, and the Shadow Court would value you as a threat to be neutralized."
The carriage slowed. Through the small window, Kael could see they had entered a courtyard surrounded by high walls topped with iron spikes. The Order of Dawn's compound, he realized—the central administrative hub where the Thornwick Chapter conducted its endless bureaucratic war against the forces of darkness.
"Soul Reaver," he said quietly. "You keep using that term. Where did you learn it?"
Ashworth's expression flickered—a momentary crack in his professional mask. "The Order maintains extensive archives. Historical records from the Abyssal Wars. The term appears in contexts we've only begun to understand."
"And you're not going to tell me what those contexts are."
"Not until I've determined whether you're an asset or a threat." Ashworth opened the carriage door and stepped out into the courtyard. Dawn enforcers formed a loose perimeter around the vehicle, their hands resting on weapons. "Come. The Archpriest wishes to meet you."
Kael followed, leaving the cultist's corpse in the carriage. Whatever harvesting remained could wait. Right now, he needed information more than power.
The compound's interior was a maze of stone corridors lit by magelight crystals set into brass fixtures. Enforcers saluted as Ashworth passed, their eyes lingering on Kael with undisguised curiosity. A death-row prisoner walking freely through the Order of Dawn's headquarters was clearly an unusual occurrence.
They descended three levels below ground. The air grew colder, damper, tinged with the mineral scent of ancient stone. Finally, Ashworth stopped before a massive door carved with symbols that seemed to writhe at the edges of vision.
"The Archpriest is waiting." Ashworth stepped aside. "A word of advice: she knows more about the Soul Reaver ability than anyone alive. What she tells you may change how you understand yourself."
The door swung open.
The chamber beyond was circular, maybe fifty feet across, its walls covered in the same shifting symbols that adorned the entrance. At the center stood a woman in the white robes of a Dawn priestess, her silver hair bound in elaborate braids, her eyes the pale blue of winter ice.
"Ashborn." Her voice resonated with power that made Kael's teeth ache. "Step forward. I would look upon the one who carries a dead god's gift."
Kael approached, stopping ten feet from the woman. Spiritual pressure rolled off her in waves—the unmistakable signature of someone who had transcended the normal boundaries of Awakened power. A Sovereign, maybe even higher.
"The gift of Soul Reaving," he said carefully, "wasn't given by any god. It was harvested from the dead."
"Both statements are true." The Archpriest smiled faintly. "The Soul Reaver ability originated during the Abyssal Wars, when humanity desperately needed weapons against the Entities. The first Soul Reaver was a woman named Mira Coldhollow. She died creating the ability, her soul torn apart to forge a tool that could harvest power from the dying." The smile faded. "She did not die cleanly. Neither did the seventy-two Awakened who followed her example over the next century."
"Seventy-two." The number struck Kael like a physical blow. "The seventy-two sealed rifts."
"The Entities imprisoned in those rifts were not conquered through military might. They were sealed using power harvested from seventy-two Soul Reavers who sacrificed themselves to create the binding. The Soul Reaver ability was supposed to die with them." The Archpriest's eyes glittered. "It did not. The ability bred true in certain bloodlines, surfacing sporadically across generations. Most carriers were found and eliminated by the Orders before they could reproduce. But some... Some evaded capture. Some passed the ability to their children in secret. And some—" she fixed Kael with an intensity that pinned him in place, "—some appear in places like death rows, where no one thinks to look for them."
"You knew." The realization crystallized in Kael's mind. "The Order granted me legitimacy not to keep me leashed. To keep me hidden."
"We kept you hidden because the Shadow Court has been hunting Soul Reavers for a century. Their current leader, a woman known only as the Whisper Queen, has executed eleven confirmed Soul Reavers in the past fifty years. She believes that destroying all carriers of the ability will prevent the Entities from ever breaking free of their prisons." The Archpriest paused. "She is wrong. The Entities will break free regardless. But she is not wrong that a Soul Reaver in the wrong hands could accelerate that breaking free by centuries."
"And whose hands are the right ones?"
"Mine. The Order of Dawn's. The coalition of Orders that maintained the seals for a thousand years." The Archpriest extended her hand. "Join us, Ashborn. Not as a leashed asset, but as an ally. The corruption within our ranks is real—we've lost three chapters to cultist infiltration in the past decade. But we are not your enemy. We are the only thing standing between Thornwick and annihilation."
Kael studied the offered hand. Spiritual power pulsed beneath the aged skin, vast reserves accumulated across decades of advancement. This woman could kill him with a thought if she chose. The fact that she was asking than demanding suggested either genuine desperation or an elaborate trap.
"I'm going to harvest that cultist corpse," he said finally. "Whatever memory fragments I extract will be shared with your investigators. In exchange, I want access to your archives. Everything you have about Soul Reavers, the Abyssal Wars, the original seal network."
"That is... A significant request."
"You want my cooperation. I want to understand what I'm carrying." Kael met her gaze without flinching. "Seventy-two Soul Reavers died creating the seals. I'm supposed to believe I'm the only one who's manifested the ability since? What happened to the others? Why did the Shadow Court start hunting them?"
The Archpriest was silent for a long moment. Finally, she nodded.
"Granted. Ashworth will escort you to the archives after your harvest. But understand—" her voice hardened, "—the information you find there may change how you view the world. The truth about the Abyssal Wars is not the heroic narrative the Orders teach. And the truth about Soul Reavers is darker still."
Kael left the chamber with more questions than answers. The cultist's corpse still waited in the carriage above, its residual energy slowly dissipating. Every hour that passed reduced the harvest quality, but the Archpriest's revelations had planted seeds of urgency in his mind.
Seventy-two Soul Reavers. Seventy-two seals. One ability that had shaped the fate of the world and then been systematically erased from history.
He found the carriage where they'd left it, the enforcers maintaining a respectful distance from the driver's seat. Kael climbed inside and pulled the corpse back into position, then pressed his palm against the cultist's cold forehead.
The harvest took longer this time. The body's spiritual energy had degraded significantly since the initial extraction, requiring more effort to pull coherent fragments from the dissipating soul-stuff. But eventually, the notifications began appearing.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted 412 units of spiritual essence from the remains. 3 memory fragments acquired. 1 skill fragment acquired."
The skill fragment resolved in his mind as a basic Entity-touched combat technique—useful but not exceptional. The memory fragments were another matter entirely. Kael sifted through them with growing unease.
The first showed the cultist's recruitment, years ago, in a cellar beneath a abandoned brewery. A figure in grey robes had explained the coming of the Faceless One, the liberation that awaited those who served the Entities willingly. The figure's face remained shadowed, but the voice carried a cultured accent that Kael associated with Order of Dawn officers.
The second showed a meeting. Six hooded figures in a chamber he didn't recognize, discussing the timeline for seal degradation. One voice—older, female, commanding—insisted that the flow could not be accelerated further without alerting the coalition. Another voice—male, impatient—argued for a more aggressive approach.
The third showed a name. Not a cultist code designation, but a genuine name, written in official documentation bearing the Order of Dawn's seal. The name belonged to a Dawn Marshal—one of the enforcers currently standing guard outside the carriage.
Kael looked up sharply. Through the carriage window, he could see the enforcers maintaining their positions. One of them—the broad-shouldered woman from the initial encounter—caught his eye and smiled thinly.
She knew he was looking. She had to know. The evidence was right there in the memories she'd never realized she'd shared.
The carriage door opened. The Dawn Marshal stood blocking the entrance, her sword now drawn, her expression calm and professional.
"Master Ashborn. I'm afraid there's been a change of plans."
"I imagine there has." Kael kept his voice level. "Marshal...?"
"Veyra. And before you ask, no, I don't know why that memory was in the cultist's head. Yes, I understand how bad that looks. And no—" her smile sharpened, "—I'm not going to give you the chance to harvest anything else before we have a longer conversation."
Two more enforcers appeared behind her, weapons ready. Kael counted exits: carriage door, carriage roof, windows too small to squeeze through. Not good odds, especially with four Awakened enforcers between him and freedom.
"Ashworth sent you," he said. "The Archpriest's offer was never real."
"Ashworth is dead." Veyra's voice carried no emotion. "He was compromised eighteen months ago. The cultists have been using his credentials to identify and eliminate threats to their operation. You were supposed to be the next target—walk into the compound, get surrounded, eliminated as a hostile. The Archpriest was never informed about the real plan."
"Then why tell me all this?"
"Because you already know too much. And because—" Veyra paused, something flickering across her face, "—I'm not working for the cultists."
Kael felt the spiritual pressure shift around him. Veyra's power was rising, coiling, preparing to strike. But the direction of that strike was no longer aimed at him.
"I work for the Shadow Court," she continued. "We've been tracking the cultist infiltration for two years, waiting for the right moment to expose it. You just provided that moment. Congratulations."
The enforcers behind her moved. Spiritual techniques flared in the courtyard—combat arts, defensive wards, coordinated strikes that sent shockwaves rippling through the mist. Veyra stepped into the carriage, sword flashing, and Kael understood: this was not an attack.
This was an extraction.
He moved to meet her blade, deflecting with the back of his forearm, feeling the kiss of sharpened steel across his skin without breaking flesh. She pressed forward, driving him back, her movements precise and choreographed to look like genuine combat to any observer.
"Play along," she hissed. "There are witnesses I can't eliminate."
Kael understood. He threw an elbow that connected with her shoulder, generating enough impact to satisfy the illusion of resistance. She stumbled back, her sword arm dropping, and he surged forward with a palm-strike that sent her sprawling across the carriage floor.
The door behind her exploded inward. More enforcers flooded in, their weapons drawn, their faces twisted with manufactured rage. Kael let them drag him out of the carriage, let them force him to his knees on the wet cobblestones, let them bind his wrists with spiritual suppression manacles that hummed with contained power.
He kept his eyes on Veyra. She remained on the ground where he'd knocked her, one hand pressed to her shoulder, her expression a perfect mask of humiliated fury. Their eyes met for a fraction of a second.
The message was clear: the Shadow Court had agents embedded everywhere. The cultists had compromised the Orders from within. And somewhere in Thornwick, the seventy-two seals continued their slow decay toward catastrophic failure.
An enforcer hauled Kael to his feet. "You're going to the dungeon. Whatever you extracted from that corpse doesn't matter. By morning, you'll be transferred to the Order of Dawn's secure containment facility. The Archpriest won't be informed."
Kael said nothing. The memory fragments burned in his mind—the cultist's leader with the Order of Dawn seal, the meeting of six hooded figures, the name of a Marshal who had apparently been feeding intelligence to the enemy for years.
The enforcers marched him toward the compound's lower levels. The same stone corridors, the same magelight crystals, the same oppressive weight of ancient architecture pressing down from above. But this time, Kael noticed details he'd missed before: hairline fractures in the walls that pulsed with sickly light, symbols that seemed to shift when he wasn't looking directly at them, the faint hum of spiritual energy that carried a note of wrongness.
The seals. He could feel them here, threaded through the compound's foundations like veins through flesh. Seventy-two seals, maintaining the barrier between worlds, and somewhere in this building, someone had been systematically weakening them.
They reached a heavy iron door. One of the enforcers produced a key inscribed with Order of Dawn glyphs and pressed it against the lock. The door swung open to reveal a small chamber—a holding cell, furnished with nothing but a wooden bench and a bucket.
"Sit. Don't move. Don't try anything." The enforcer shoved Kael inside and slammed the door. The lock's mechanism engaged with a resonant click.
Kael waited until their footsteps faded, then examined the suppression manacles. Standard Order of Dawn design—effective against spiritual manipulation, nearly impossible to remove without the corresponding key. But the lock mechanism was flawed. He could feel the spiritual resonance of the glyphs, identify the frequency they operated on.
His fingers found the release sequence. Not a key, but a precise application of spiritual energy mimicking the key's frequency. The manacles clicked open and fell to the floor.
He stood, rubbing circulation back into his wrists, and studied the cell's interior. The walls were solid stone, too thick to break through. The door was warded against spiritual escape. The ventilation shaft was too small to crawl through.
But the floor beneath his feet thrummed with power. The seal network ran directly beneath this chamber.
Kael knelt and pressed his palm flat against the stone. Spiritual energy flowed through him—not his own power, but the ambient resonance of the ancient binding keeping the Entities imprisoned. He could feel the individual threads of the network, seventy-two distinct signatures woven together into a tapestry of desperate sacrifice.
And he could feel where those threads had been cut.
Not from inside the compound. From somewhere else—distant, hidden, connected through channels that shouldn't exist. The cultists had found a way to bleed power from the seals without alerting the Order's monitoring systems.
He stood and faced the door. Whatever happened next, he needed to find that connection. The seventy-two seals were failing faster than projected, and the cultists were accelerating the flow using methods no one had anticipated.
The Archpriest wanted him as an ally. Veyra and the Shadow Court wanted him dead or controlled. The cultists wanted his ability as a weapon.
Kael wanted answers.
He reached for the door's warding and began to unravel it, thread by careful thread. Whatever awaited him beyond this cell, he refused to be caged while the world crumbled around him.
The ward collapsed. The door swung open.
The corridor beyond was empty. The enforcers had retreated to more comfortable positions, leaving only magical sentries to guard the prisoner they assumed was securely contained.
Kael stepped through the doorway and began to walk, following the thread of damaged seals toward whatever horror awaited at its source.
Behind him, deep beneath the compound, something vast and hungry stirred in its prison.
And in a shadowed chamber across the city, six hooded figures gathered around a map marked with seventy-two points of light, watching one of those lights flicker and dim.
"Soul Reaver detected in Thornwick," the female voice announced. "He knows too much."
"Then he dies," another voice replied. "As they all do."
The vote was unanimous.
