Kael dropped the last torch into the canal. The flames guttered, hissed, and died beneath the black water.
Behind him, the Thornwick Council House burned. Smoke billowed through the shattered windows, carrying with it the screams of clerks who had tried to bar the doors. Kael didn't look back. He'd spent three years in that building's cells, waiting for an execution that never came, listening to the muffled sounds of justice being bought and sold by men in silk robes.
Now the building burned, and he walked away.
The cobblestones were slick with rain that had stopped an hour ago. Kael's boots echoed in the empty street. His cloak—the same one he'd worn since escaping the gallows, now patched in seven places—dragged through puddles without care. The gas lamps overhead had been shuttered by the Watch hours ago, when the first reports of trouble reached the upper districts.
Nobody stopped him.
A Divine-stage Awakened, even one marked as a Soul Reaver, could walk through the lower quarters without challenge. The spiritually inert commoners pressed themselves against walls as he passed, eyes downcast. They could feel his presence even if they couldn't name it—a pressure in the chest, a wrongness in the air. Kael had stopped trying to hide it years ago. Hiding meant nothing when the Shadow Court's hounds could smell his soul from three districts away.
He turned onto Farrow Street, where the cobblestones gave way to older stones worn smooth by centuries of cart wheels. The buildings here leaned toward each other, their upper stories nearly touching and blocking what little moonlight made it through the perpetual haze. The smell of fish and cheap tallow hung heavy.
"Soul Reaver."
The voice came from an alley to his left. Kael didn't stop walking. He didn't reach for the knife at his hip or the longer blade strapped across his back. He waited.
A figure detached from the shadows. A woman, young, wearing the patched leathers of a dockworker but moving with the careful precision of someone trained in combat. She had a sword at her hip and a crossbow held loosely at her side. Her hair was cut short, practical, and her eyes held the flat look of someone who had stopped being surprised by the world years ago.
"You're the one they call Kael Ashborn," she said. Not a question.
"I'm the one they tried to hang three years ago." Kael kept his hands visible, away from his weapons. "Close enough."
"Zarek sent word you were in the city. Said you'd be looking for answers about the seals." She tilted her head, studying him. "Said you might be useful."
Kael stopped walking. The name landed like a stone in still water, sending ripples through the careful blankness he'd built over the past months. Zarek. The man who had framed him, who had testified at his trial with perfect lies delivered in a voice of wounded righteousness, who had walked away with Kael's execution warrant in his pocket while Kael rotted in a cell waiting for the hangman's noose.
"Where is he?"
The woman's lips curved, not a smile. "Not here. Not yet. But he's watching the northern approach to the Ashfall Gate. He said you'd come when you were ready." She reached into her coat and produced a small object, holding it out. A brass token, stamped with an unfamiliar seal—a circle bisected by a vertical line.
"He wants you to have this. Proof of good faith."
Kael stared at the token for a long moment. The metal gleamed dully in the faint light filtering through the clouds. He could feel something radiating from it—a faint spiritual resonance, the kind left by Order-crafted items.
"I'm not interested in good faith," he said finally. "I'm interested in answers."
"Zarek has those too." The woman stepped back into the shadows. "The Ashfall Gate. Tonight. Don't be late, Soul Reaver. The world doesn't have much time left, and neither do we."
She was gone before Kael could respond, her footsteps fading into the maze of alleys that made up the lower quarters. He looked down at the token in his hand. The brass was warm, as if it had been held close to someone's body. The seal on its face meant nothing to him—he didn't recognize the symbol, didn't know what faction it represented.
But Zarek had framed him three years ago, had watched him dragged to the gallows with a smile that never reached his eyes. And now Zarek was reaching out, offering tokens and promises of answers.
Kael closed his fingers around the brass. He didn't trust it. He didn't trust Zarek, didn't trust the woman, didn't trust anyone who moved in the shadows as if shadows were home. But he had spent three years in a cell, waiting for a justice that would never come, and he had learned something in that time: the people who wronged you rarely did so without reason.
Zarek had framed him for a reason. The question was whether that reason still mattered.
He pocketed the token and continued walking. The Ashfall Gate was on the northern edge of the city, where the walls met the cliffs that dropped away into the harbor. It was a two-hour walk through the lower quarters, longer if he wanted to avoid the Watch checkpoints that had been set up after the Council House fire. Kael didn't particularly care about avoiding checkpoints—a Divine-stage Awakened could walk through checkpoints the same way a man walks through rain, getting wet but not stopping—but he did care about avoiding attention.
The Shadow Court had eyes everywhere. They'd been tracking him since he escaped the gallows, watching from rooftops and alley mouths, never approaching but always present. He didn't know why they were watching, didn't know what they wanted from him. The Soul Reaver ability made him valuable to anyone who understood what he could do, but it also made him dangerous. A man who could harvest skills and memories from the dead was a man who could learn secrets that were meant to stay buried.
Kael had used that ability sparingly. Each harvest was a violation, a theft of something that should have been left in peace. The Souls he took screamed when they were reaped—not with sound, but with something deeper, a vibration that resonated in his bones for hours afterward. He had learned to filter them out, to take what he needed and leave the rest, but the cost was never zero. The cost was never zero even when the system said it was.
The system only measured spiritual expenditure. It couldn't measure the dreams.
He passed through Millers' Square, where the night market was winding down. Stalls stood empty, their canvas awnings rolled up and tied. A few vendors remained, packing away the last of their goods by lamplight. One of them—a woman selling roasted chestnuts from a cart—looked up as Kael approached, and her face went pale. She grabbed her cart handle and pulled it into an alley without a word.
Kael didn't blame her. Soul Reavers had a reputation, and it wasn't a good one. The Orders called them parasites, thieves of the dead, abominations that violated the natural order. The common folk called them worse, when they dared to speak at all. In the three years since his execution, Kael had learned to live with the fear he inspired. It was, in its own way, useful. Fear kept people at a distance. Distance kept people safe from him.
He reached the northern wall an hour before dawn. The Ashfall Gate was a massive construction of iron and stone, built during the First Abyssal War to control the flow of traffic between the city and the northern territories. It was closed now, sealed by Order magic after the last major rift breach had nearly destroyed the harbor district. Nothing moved through the Ashfall Gate without permission from the Council of Six, and the Council hadn't given permission in forty years.
But there were ways around sealed gates. Kael had learned most of them in his years as a wanted man.
He found the maintenance passage behind a section of wall that had been damaged in a rockslide three months ago. The rubble had never been cleared, leaving a gap that was just wide enough for a man to squeeze through if he didn't mind scraping his shoulders against the rough stone. Kael squeezed through, feeling the cold bite of the wind as he emerged on the other side.
The northern cliffs stretched before him, jagged teeth of rock dropping away into the churning gray waters of the harbor. The sky was lightening in the east, the first hints of dawn bleeding through the clouds. In the distance, he could see the lights of ships in the outer anchorage, their lanterns bobbing gently as the swells passed beneath them.
And closer, maybe fifty yards away, a figure stood at the edge of the cliff.
Kael recognized him immediately. Zarek hadn't changed in three years—still tall, still lean, still wearing the formal robes of an Order magistrate with the casual elegance of a man who had never doubted his own right to wear them. His silver hair was tied back in a queue, and his face held the same expression of benevolent authority that had convinced the jury to sentence Kael to death.
"Soul Reaver." Zarek's voice carried across the distance without effort. "You came."
"You framed me." Kael's hand found the hilt of his knife. "You testified that I murdered Lord Ashworth. You watched them put the rope around my neck. And now you want to talk."
Zarek didn't flinch. "I want to explain. There's a difference."
"Explain what? How you lied? How you knew I was innocent and condemned me anyway?"
"I knew you were guilty." Zarek's expression didn't change. "Guilty of being a Soul Reaver, of possessing a power that threatens the balance of the world. The murder charge was... Incidental. A convenient fiction."
Kael's blood went cold. He had suspected, of course. Suspected that the murder accusation was fabricated, that Zarek had manufactured evidence to ensure a conviction. But hearing it spoken aloud, so casually, was something else.
"You're proud of that," he said. Not a question.
"I'm proud of nothing. I'm satisfied." Zarek began walking toward him, each step deliberate. "Do you know what you are, Kael Ashborn? Do you understand what your power means?"
"I'm a Soul Reaver. I harvest abilities from the dead. That's all."
"That's all?" Zarek laughed, a short, humorless sound. "A Soul Reaver is an abomination. You take what should be sacred—souls, memories, the accumulated experience of a lifetime—and you convert it into fuel for your own advancement. The Orders have hunted your kind for centuries because you cannot be trusted. You are outside the natural order."
"And yet here you are, talking to me. Not hunting me."
" Circumstances change." Zarek stopped ten feet away, close enough to speak without shouting but far enough to be out of immediate reach. "The seals are failing, Soul Reaver. All seventy-two of them, weakening faster than we can repair them. Within a year, maybe two, the barriers between realms will collapse entirely."
Kael felt something twist in his chest. The seals. The 72 rifts that separated the Material from the Abyss, each one containing an Entity that had nearly ended the world during the First War. The Orders maintained them, reinforced them, sacrificed resources and lives to keep them intact. And now Zarek was saying they were failing.
"The cultists," Kael said slowly. "The ones who've been attacking the city. They know about the seals."
"They've known for decades. They've been working toward this moment since the end of the Abyssal War." Zarek's expression hardened. "The Faceless One has agents in every Order, every major city, every position of influence. They've been preparing for a final assault on the seal network, and they're closer to success than anyone realizes."
"And you need me to stop them."
"I need you to understand what you're capable of." Zarek reached into his robes and withdrew a small glass vial, holding it up so Kael could see the contents. Inside, something luminous swirled—a pale, ghostly light that seemed to move against the laws of physics. "This is soul-essence. Harvested from the body of an Awakened who died defending a rift in the eastern territories. One hundred gold sovereigns' worth, maybe more."
Kael stared at the vial. He had seen soul-essence before, had handled it in the illegal markets that catered to Awakened clientele. But this was different. The light inside seemed to pulse with awareness, with something that might have been memory or emotion or pure spiritual force.
"Soul-essence is the foundation of the economy," Zarek continued. "It's what fuels the Orders' efforts to maintain the seals. It's what powers the rituals that keep the Entities imprisoned. And it's what you can harvest, for free, without cost or consequence—while the rest of us must purchase it at ruinous expense or sacrifice our own spiritual reserves."
"You're saying I could help."
"I'm saying you already have." Zarek pocketed the vial. "Three months ago, a cultist cell in the harbor district was destroyed. The Watch reported three casualties among the attackers, but when they examined the bodies, they found nothing. No souls, no residual spiritual energy. As if the bodies had been empty for years."
Kael said nothing. The harbor district. He remembered that night—a confrontation with cultists who had been trying to breach a minor seal near the fishing docks. He had killed two of them, harvested their abilities without thinking, and left the bodies where they fell. He hadn't considered the implications.
"Soul Reavers are feared for good reason," Zarek said. "Your kind can drain a man's soul in seconds, leaving nothing but an empty shell. But that same ability could be used against the Entities. The cultists draw their power from the Abyss, from the Entities themselves. If you could harvest that power, sever their connection to the source..."
"You want me to fight them."
"I want you to consider the possibilities." Zarek turned to look out over the cliffs, toward the lightening horizon. "The Six Orders are divided. They've been divided for decades, squabbling over resources and influence while the seals crumble. The Concordat that binds them prevents open conflict, but it also prevents decisive action. When the final breach comes, and it will come, the Orders will be too busy fighting each other to mount an effective defense."
"And the Shadow Court?" Kael asked. "They've been watching me for months. Why?"
Zarek glanced back at him. "The Shadow Court has been monitoring all Soul Reavers for centuries. We're not sure why—our records from that period are incomplete—but the pattern is consistent. Every Soul Reaver who emerges is tracked, observed, and eventually... Acquired."
"Acquired."
"Recruited. Conscripted. Removed." Zarek's expression didn't change. "The Shadow Court serves the Orders in ways that cannot be acknowledged publicly. We're the blade that strikes in darkness, the hand that moves when others must remain clean. Soul Reavers are valuable tools, and the Court has developed methods to control them."
Kael's grip tightened on his knife. "You're warning me."
"I'm informing you. The woman who gave you the token tonight was a Court operative. She reported your location to her superiors the moment the exchange was complete. By now, a team is being assembled. They'll intercept you before you leave the city."
"Then why tell me this? Why warn me about something you clearly want to happen?"
Zarek smiled, the first genuine expression Kael had seen on his face. It didn't reach his eyes. "Because I'm not the Shadow Court. I'm not part of their plans or their schemes. I told you the truth because the truth serves my purposes. The Court wants to control you. I want you to understand what control means, so you can make an informed choice about whether to resist."
"And what do you want me to choose?"
"Survival." Zarek turned to face him fully. "The world is ending, Soul Reaver. The seals are failing, the cultists are massing, and the Entities are stirring in their prisons. In six months, maybe less, the Abyss will tear through the Veil and consume everything. The Orders will fall. The cities will burn. The spiritually inert will die by the millions, and the Awakened will be hunted down like animals by things that make your worst nightmares look like children's stories."
Kael felt the weight of those words settle onto his shoulders. The world ending. He had always known it was possible—the seal network was ancient, maintained by methods that even the Orders didn't fully understand. But hearing it spoken so plainly, so matter-of-factly, was different.
"What do you want me to do about it?"
"What you were made to do." Zarek stepped closer, close enough that Kael could see the lines of exhaustion around his eyes, the tremor in his hands that he'd hidden until now. "Harvest. Take the power from the cultists, from the Entities, from anyone who stands in your way. Build yourself into something that can survive what's coming. And when the Veil finally breaks, be ready to fight."
"I don't take orders from you."
"No. You don't." Zarek reached into his robes again, this time producing a folded document, sealed with wax. "But you might take this. It's a list of cultist strongholds in the northern territories, compiled by Order intelligence over the past decade. Locations, defenses, leadership. Everything you'd need to begin a campaign of... Selective harvesting."
Kael took the document without thinking. The wax seal bore the mark of the Order of Dawn, the most militant of the Six Orders. That Zarek had access to such materials meant he was more than a simple magistrate—he was connected, powerful, dangerous in ways that went beyond spiritual advancement.
"Why?" Kael asked. "Why help me? You framed me, you condemned me, and now you're giving me weapons. What do you gain?"
Zarek's smile returned, colder this time. "I'm a realist, Soul Reaver. I've spent forty years serving the Orders, watching them squabble and scheme while the real threat grows stronger. I believe in the seal network. I believe in the mission. But I don't believe in the people who claim to protect it. When the collapse comes, I want to be on the winning side. And right now, you're the only card that hasn't been played."
He turned and began walking toward the cliff's edge. Kael watched him go, the document heavy in his hands, the brass token burning a hole in his pocket.
"Zarek."
The man stopped but didn't turn.
"The murder you framed me for. Lord Ashworth. Did you kill him?"
A long pause. The wind howled across the cliffs, carrying with it the salt-smell of the sea.
"Someone did," Zarek said finally. "And someone needed to pay for it. You were... Convenient."
He stepped off the cliff and was gone.
Kael stood at the edge for a long time, watching the waves crash against the rocks below. Dawn broke over the harbor, painting the sky in shades of orange and gold, and somewhere in the city below, bells began to ring for the morning watch. The world continued as if nothing had changed, as if the conversation on the cliff had never happened, as if the seals weren't failing and the cultists weren't massing and the Entities weren't stirring in their prisons.
He unfolded the document and began to read. Names, locations, dates. The architecture of a conspiracy that stretched back centuries, tied together by faith and fear and the desperate belief that the world could be saved if only enough people were willing to die for it.
Kael read until the sun cleared the horizon, then folded the document and tucked it into his coat. He had what he came for—answers, or at least the beginning of them. The seals were failing. The cultists were organized. The Shadow Court was hunting him. And somewhere in the northern territories, there were cultists who had never heard of Soul Reavers, who had no idea what was coming for them.
He began walking north, away from the city, toward the territories where the seals were weakest and the cultists were thickest. The woman with the crossbow had been right about one thing: the world didn't have much time left.
But Kael Ashborn had never been the type to wait for the world to end.
He would meet it halfway.

Three days later, Kael found the cultist camp in a dried riverbed two miles from the village of Millhaven. Seven tents, twenty-three souls, and a freshly dug pit that smelled of sulfur and something older—something that resonated in his bones like a half-remembered scream.
He approached at dusk, when the light was failing and the cultists were gathered around their central fire. They wore robes of black and red, the colors of the Faceless One, and they chanted in a language that hurt to hear. Kael stood at the edge of the trees and watched, cataloging positions, estimating capabilities.
The leader stood at the fire's edge, arms raised, voice rising in cadences that made the air itself feel wrong. Kael could see the spiritual energy radiating from him—not the clean light of Awakened power, but something murkier, corrupted by contact with the Abyss. The Entity-touched. The ones who had gone too far and couldn't go back.
They were stronger than the harbor cultists, more disciplined. But they were still human, still mortal, still capable of dying.
Kael drew his knife and stepped into the firelight.
The chanting stopped. Twenty-three heads turned toward him, and for a moment, the only sound was the crackle of flames and the distant call of night birds. Then the leader smiled, a terrible expression that split his face like a wound.
"A Soul Reaver." His voice was deeper than Kael expected, roughened by decades of speaking to things that shouldn't be heard. "We felt you coming. The Master warned us you might find this place."
"Your Master can feel whatever he likes." Kael's voice was flat, empty. "He can't stop what's going to happen next."
He moved.
The first cultist died without understanding why—one moment standing, the next falling, Kael's knife buried in his throat. The second tried to raise a ward, hands weaving patterns that flickered with sickly light, but Kael was already past it, blade slashing across the man's chest and opening a wound that steamed in the cold air. The third and fourth came at him together, short swords and brutal efficiency, but they'd never faced a Divine-stage Awakened before. Kael caught the first sword on his own blade, twisted, and felt bone snap as he drove his elbow into the man's face. The second he ignored, letting the strike land on his shoulder while his knife found the cultist's kidney.
The leader watched the carnage with something like approval. "The Master said you would come. He said you would try to stop us."
Kael wiped blood from his eyes and advanced. "I'm not trying to stop you. I'm harvesting you."
He could feel it now—the connection forming between himself and the bodies on the ground. The souls that had fled their physical shells, hovering just beyond reach, waiting to be claimed. The Soul Reaver ability activated without conscious thought, drawing them in, pulling them through the membrane between life and death and into the space where Kael could use them.

The notifications scrolled through his vision, words and numbers that had become as familiar as breathing. Kael had learned to filter them, to focus on the relevant information and let the rest fade into background noise. The spiritual energy was useful—it strengthened his reserves without depleting his own soul. The skill fragment was interesting, a piece of knowledge that might prove valuable later. And the location data...
"The northern stronghold." Kael looked up at the leader. "That's where your Master is?"
The man's smile widened. "The Master is everywhere. The stronghold is where He's most... Present."
"Then I know where to go next."
"You won't make it." The leader's hands began to move, weaving patterns that made the air around him shimmer and distort. "The Master's servants are already moving. The Shadow Court's hunters are three days behind you. And the seals..." He laughed, a sound like breaking glass. "The seals will break whether you fight or not. You've chosen a side, Soul Reaver. Now live with the consequences."
He raised his hands, and the fire exploded outward in a wave of corrupted energy.
Kael threw himself aside, feeling the heat wash over him, the spiritual force battering against his defenses. The world went white, then black, then he was lying on his back in the riverbed, staring up at a sky full of stars.
The cultists were gone. The tents were burning. And somewhere in the distance, he could hear footsteps approaching—not one set, but many, converging from multiple directions.
He pushed himself upright, ignoring the pain in his ribs, and scanned the darkness. The footsteps were getting closer. He could see movement between the trees, shapes that moved with purpose and coordination. Not cultists. Something else.
The Shadow Court had found him.
Kael gathered his weapons and began to run, heading north, toward the stronghold and whatever waited there. Behind him, the footsteps followed, patient and relentless. Ahead of him, the darkness grew thicker, heavier, as if the world itself was holding its breath.
He didn't look back. He didn't slow down.
The game had changed. The pieces were in motion. And somewhere in the darkness, the Faceless One was watching, waiting, smiling a smile that no human face could contain.
The hunt had begun.
