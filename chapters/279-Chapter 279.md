[Soul Harvest: You extracted 3 Skill Fragments from the remains. Memory Echo acquired. Spiritual Essence recovered.]
"Sixteen years in the business and you still flinch when you harvest." Vane's voice cut through the alley's fetid air, accompanied by the click of boots on wet cobblestones. "Some things never change."
Kael yanked his hand back, wiping residue on his coat. The body belonged to a cultist—one of three he'd tracked through Thornwick's underbelly over the past week. All bearing the same mark: a stylized rift, mouth gaping wide. All dead before he could extract anything useful. Someone was always one step ahead.
"You're not supposed to be here," Kael said, turning to face the newcomer.
Vane stood framed by the alley's mouth, gaslight catching the silver threading through his dark hair. The Order of Dawn insignia gleamed on his collar—a sunburst, radiating blades. His expression carried that particular blend of amusement and calculation that made Kael's skin prickle.
"The Shadow Court has been tracking cult activity in Thornwick for six months." Vane stepped closer, boots squelching in gore. "Massive coordinated movements. Resources diverted from legitimate operations. Someone is planning something significant."
"The Shadow Court monitors everything," Kael said. "Why tell me?"
"Because you're already involved." Vane produced a small leather journal, its pages yellowed and cracked. "This belonged to a cultist operative named Dresh. Found in his quarters after he died three days ago. His notes reference a 'Sovereign-class anomaly' that the cult has been attempting to neutralize."
Kael's blood ran cold. Sovereign. His current stage.
"Don't look so concerned." Vane tossed the journal. Kael caught it reflexively, thumbing through pages filled with cramped handwriting. "The cult doesn't know your name. But they know someone at your power level has been interfering with their operations. They've dispatched a specialized team."
"A team of what?"
"Expendables, mostly. Three Ascendants, five Transcendents, and one unknown entity they call 'The Witness.'" Vane's jaw tightened almost imperceptibly. "The Witness is the problem. Whatever it is, it's not human. The cult has been conducting rituals for months to create it."
The weight of the journal felt considerable. Kael flipped to a page marked with a inked symbol—a circle within a circle, containing seven smaller marks. Beneath it, someone had written in shaking script: 'SEAL INTEGRITY ASSESSMENT - THORNWICK CLUSTER - 72 TOTAL.'
"Seventy-two," Kael murmured.
"The sealed rifts." Vane confirmed. "Each containing an Abyssal Entity. The seals are weakening, but no faster than projections suggest. Except in Thornwick."
Thunder rolled across the sky, though no clouds gathered. The gaslights flickered in unison, their flames bending toward some invisible source. In the distance, a clock tower began to chime midnight.
"We need to move," Vane said. "The cultist you just harvested—his death would have triggered an alert. They know someone is in this sector."
"Alerting whom?"
A scream tore through the night, high and piercing, followed by the crash of breaking glass. Then another. Then three more, overlapping in discordant harmony.
"Not whom." Vane drew a blade from his coat—steel etched with prayers, humming with stored spiritual energy. "What."
Kael moved before thought could catch up, muscle memory and instinct propelling him toward the alley's exit. The street beyond had transformed into chaos. A carriage lay overturned, its horse screaming with inhuman terror. Figures in dark robes streamed from doorways and shadows, their movements too fluid, too coordinated.
"The Witness doesn't command them," Vane explained, cutting down a cultist who rushed too close. "It amplifies them. Takes control of anyone nearby with the cult's mark."
"Then why aren't you affected?"
Vane's smile was thin and humorless. "Because I removed my mark years ago. The pain was... Memorable."
Kael grabbed a cultist's reaching arm, twisted, and felt the satisfying snap of bone. A harvest notification flickered across his vision as the body crumpled.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 1 Skill Fragment from the remains. Spiritual Essence recovered.]
"The Witness is controlling them through spiritual resonance," Vane continued, parrying a strike that came from an impossible angle. "It's somewhere in the crowd. Find it, break its concentration, and these puppets lose their strings."
"How do I find something I can't identify?"
"Everyone else runs away from the Witnesses. You run toward them." Vane slammed his palm against his chest, and light erupted from his body—a barrier of golden energy that threw back the nearest cluster of puppets. "Go. I'll hold them."
Kael didn't argue. He dove into the chaos, shouldering through fleeing civilians and vaulting over overturned market stalls. The screams followed him like a tide, but beneath them, he heard something else—a droning hum, discordant and wrong.
The smell hit him next: ozone and copper, the scent of lightning meeting blood.
He found it in the market square, standing atop a fountain that had begun spouting black water. The Witness wore a robe like the others, but its form seemed to shift and blur at the edges, as if reality itself struggled to define the shape. Where its face should have been, a smooth expanse of flesh bore seven wounds, each leaking pale light.
The seven marks. Seven entities. Seven rifts.
Kael understood then, with sudden terrible clarity. The cult wasn't trying to weaken the seals. They were trying to create something that could control what emerged when the seals finally broke.
The Witness turned toward him. Its voice came from everywhere and nowhere, vibrating through Kael's chest.
"Sovereign-class. Finally."
It raised one hand, and the nearest civilians—those too slow to flee—stumbled toward Kael with empty eyes and grasping fingers. Not killing him, he realized. Trying to restrain him.
The crowd control approach. The cult wanted him alive.
Kael smiled grimly and reached for the nearest puppet. If he couldn't harvest from distance, he could at least harvest what was close.
His fingers found flesh, and the familiar sensation of soul extraction flooded through him.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 2 Skill Fragments from the remains. Memory Echo acquired. Spiritual Essence recovered.]
The puppet collapsed, and Kael felt new knowledge bloom in his mind—the muscle memory of swordsmanship, decades of training compressed into seconds. He drew his blade, borrowed skills flooding his limbs with foreign precision.
"That won't be enough," the Witness said, drifting closer without seeming to move. "I am seven souls woven into one vessel. I have consumed the dying breaths of seven Transcendents, three Sovereigns, and one—"
It stopped abruptly. The smooth expanse of its face rippled.
"Interesting," it said, voice carrying new weight. "Your soul signature. It's familiar. Like a melody I've heard before, long ago."
Kael's grip tightened on his blade. The Soul Reaver origin—the mystery he'd been chasing since the beginning. Could this creature know something?
"Tell me," he said, pressing forward. "Tell me about souls like mine."
The Witness laughed—a sound like breaking glass. "Souls like yours don't exist anymore. The last one died two centuries past, in a tomb that no one can find. Unless—"
It lunged, faster than thought, faster than muscle memory. Kael twisted aside, but not fast enough. Pain lanced across his side as claws raked through fabric and flesh.
Blood dripped onto the cobblestones. The crowd of puppets surged forward.
Kael rolled, came up slashing, and severed the arm of a puppet that had gotten too close. Harvest notification. Another skill fragment. The borrowed swordsmanship fused with his own desperate instincts, creating something brutal and effective.
"You're wounded," the Witness observed, circling. "Your soul energy should be depleting rapidly at Sovereign stage. Yet you continue to fight without hesitation. Without conservation."
"Maybe I'm just stubborn."
"Stubborn doesn't explain this." The creature's voice dropped to something almost like respect. "You harvest. Like the old one did. But you harvest without cost, without corruption. How?"
Kael answered by attacking, blade singing through the air toward the Witness's throat. The creature caught his wrist with inhuman strength, and for a moment they stood locked in combat, spiritual energy crackling where their bodies connected.
"You could tell me," Kael said through gritted teeth. "Tell me about the original Soul Reaver. Tell me about this tomb."
"I could." The Witness leaned closer, its breath cold against Kael's face. "But then you would seek it. And that path leads only to madness and death. The original Reaver chose it anyway. They all choose it."
The puppet crowd tightened around them, and Kael felt his options narrowing. Too many enemies. Not enough time. Not enough spiritual reserves, despite what the creature assumed about his abilities.
He needed another approach.
The harvest notifications in his vision seemed brighter, more numerous. Six skill fragments from the puppets he'd cut down. Three memory echoes. Spiritual essence pooled from a dozen sources.
If he could harvest the Witness itself...
"You're considering it," the creature said, delighted. "Harvesting me. Do you understand what I am? Seven souls. Seven fragments of power that would tear your mind apart before you could process them."
"Old news," Kael said. "I already harvest from those who carry pieces of Abyssal Entities. The corruption doesn't take. The cost doesn't exist."
"You don't understand. I am not a fragment. I am seven complete souls, bound by blood and ritual, and each one remembers what it meant to be sovereign, to be—"
Kael's free hand shot out and gripped the Witness's face. Not the mask of wounds—the area beneath it, where a jaw might have been. Where flesh met whatever passed for bone in this monstrosity.
The creature's words dissolved into a scream.
Soul energy flooded through Kael's palm, and for one terrible instant, he felt them—all seven souls, all seven lives, all seven centuries of accumulated memory and power pressing against his consciousness like a mountain trying to fit inside a teacup.
[Soul Harvest: ATTEMPTING TO HARVEST UNSTABLE ENTITY. WARNING: TARGET RESISTANCE EXCEEDS NORMAL PARAMETERS. SOUL CONSERVATION LAW SUSPENDED FOR REAVER-CLASS HARVEST. YIELD: UNKNOWN. CONSEQUENCES: UNKNOWN.]
The cost was nothing. The cost was also everything.
Kael felt his mind stretch, splinter, reform. Seven lifetimes of experience crashed through his awareness—a woman who had loved the sea, a man who had betrayed his Order, a child who had died in the first Abyssal War, an elder who had sealed the rifts, a warrior who had sought immortality, a scholar who had discovered the truth about the afterlife, and beneath them all, something older, something that had been there before the first harvest, before the first Reaver, before the first seal.
The original.
Kael gasped and released his grip. The Witness stumbled backward, its form flickering wildly between states of cohesion. The puppets surrounding them collapsed as one, puppet strings severed.
"Impossible," the Witness whispered, its voice fractured across seven tones. "You touched the source. You touched the memory of—"
A blade of golden light punched through the creature's chest from behind.
Vane stood in the fountain's spray, his weapon blazing with stored prayers, his face pale with exertion. "Time to leave. Now."
The Witness looked down at the blade, then up at Kael, then laughed—a sound of genuine wonder.
"The tomb. The original Reaver. It's not a place you go." Each word came from a different voice, different soul. "It's a choice you make. A door that opens only for those who have already paid the price."
"What price?" Kael demanded.
But Vane grabbed his arm and pulled him away, and the Witness began to dissolve into pale light, seven souls departing seven different directions, carrying their secrets with them into the Thornwick night.
They ran through empty streets, past the wreckage of the market, past bodies both harvested and unharvested. Vane led them to a safehouse—basement of an abandoned church, walls lined with prayers and warding symbols.
"That was stupid," Vane said, collapsing onto a crate. "You nearly got yourself killed trying to harvest something that could have consumed you."
"But I didn't."
"No. You didn't." Vane studied him with an expression Kael couldn't read. "The cult will know now. They will know what you are. What you can do."
"What am I?"
The question hung in the air, heavy with implications.
Vane rose and moved to a table covered in maps and documents. He spread his hands across the surface, fingers tracing lines of ink that might have been trade routes or battle fronts or something else entirely.
"The first Soul Reaver was a man named Aldric Voss. He discovered the ability to harvest souls without cost during the first Abyssal War, when death surrounded him on every side. He became the most powerful Awakened of his generation—and the most feared. The Orders hunted him. The cult wanted him. Everyone wanted what he had."
"What happened to him?"
"He went looking for answers. About his power, about the seals, about the Entities themselves." Vane pulled a document from beneath the maps—a sketch, old and faded, showing a figure standing before a massive door covered in symbols. "He found a tomb. Inside, he found a choice. He walked through the door, and he never came back."
Kael stared at the sketch, at the figure's familiar silhouette. "And his power? The Soul Reaver ability?"
"Never appeared again. Until you." Vane met his eyes. "The cult has been trying to recreate it for two hundred years. You've succeeded without their rituals, without their sacrifices. You're either the answer to their prayers or the destruction of their plans."
"Which do you think?"
Vane's smile returned, thin and sharp. "I think you're standing in the middle of a conspiracy that spans multiple Orders, several hundred cult cells, and at least three different factions all trying to use the same global catastrophe for their own purposes." He gestured to the maps. "The seals are weakening. Not evenly, not randomly—strategically. Someone is choosing which rifts to weaken, which Entities to free."
"Why?"
"That's the question, isn't it?" Vane sat back down, looking every one of his years. "The cult says they're preparing humanity for a new age. The Orders say they're preventing apocalypse. The Shadow Court says they're maintaining balance. Everyone has an excuse. Everyone has a plan."
"And the Soul Reaver? The original one?"
"Legend says Aldric Voss discovered something in that tomb. Something that would change everything. A weapon, a truth, a choice that could rewrite the rules of this world." Vane leaned forward. "The cult wants that. The Orders want that. If you follow this path, you'll become the most valuable—and most hunted—person on this continent."
Kael considered this. Outside, dawn was breaking over Thornwick, painting the sky in shades of amber and rose. Somewhere in the city, the cult would be regrouping, planning their response. Somewhere, the Orders would be receiving reports of last night's chaos. The conspiracy was moving, pieces being positioned, traps being laid.
But he also had something they didn't expect. Seven souls' worth of memories, fragmentary but illuminating. The Witness's dissolution had left him with more than he'd expected—not complete knowledge, but fragments. Pieces of a puzzle he was only beginning to assemble.
He knew now that the original Soul Reaver had not been destroyed. He had been transformed. He had walked through a door that existed in a place between places, a threshold that only Reavers could perceive.
And that door, according to the seventh memory—the oldest, the deepest—was still open. Waiting.
The conspiracy was about more than seals and rifts. It was about what lay beyond them. What had been imprisoned there. What wanted to be free.
And Kael, the new Reaver, stood at the center of it all, with a choice to make that would echo across centuries.
"Tell me about the cult's operations in Thornwick," he said finally. "Everything you know. Everything the Shadow Court has observed."
Vane raised an eyebrow. "You're not going to try to stop me? Turn me in to the Orders?"
"The Orders want to control the Reaver. The cult wants to control the Reaver. The Shadow Court wants to control the Reaver." Kael smiled, and there was nothing pleasant in it. "Everyone wants to use me. Fine. Let them try. But first, I need to understand what game they're playing."
He pulled out the journal he'd taken from the cultist's body, flipping to a page filled with coordinates and dates. Then he added the documents Vane had shared, spreading them across the table like cards in a hand.
"If the cult is strategically weakening seals, then Thornwick is not their target. It's their staging ground." His finger traced a pattern on the map. "They're using this city's proximity to three major rifts as cover. All their movements look like attacks on the seals, but they're —"
"Training exercises," Vane finished, eyes widening. "They're practicing large-scale rituals, testing their ability to coordinate across multiple sites, preparing for something bigger."
"Exactly." Kael looked up. "And somewhere in all of this, there's a timeline. When do they plan to unseal the major rifts? How many Entities do they want to release?"
"The journal might have that information."
"Or it might not. Dresh was low-level. He knew his role, not the whole picture." Kael closed the journal. "We need someone higher in the organization. Someone who knows the endgame."
Vane stood, moving to a cabinet in the corner. He returned with a bottle and two glasses, pouring amber liquid into each. "The Shadow Court has been infiltrated the cult for decades. We have assets in place, eyes and ears throughout their structure. But reaching them is dangerous. The cult screens everyone, tests loyalty constantly."
"Then I'll give them a reason to test me." Kael accepted the glass but didn't drink. "Yesterday, I killed three of their operatives. Tonight, I fought their Witness. By tomorrow, they'll know a Sovereign-level Soul Reaver is operating in Thornwick. They'll want to know who I am. They'll want to control me."
"You want to bait them."
"I want to find out what they're planning before they finish planning it." Kael finally took a drink, the liquid burning pleasantly down his throat. "And I want to find that tomb. Whatever Aldric Voss discovered, whatever choice he made—it's relevant. It might be the only thing that's relevant."
Vane studied him for a long moment. "You're either very brave or very stupid."
"Probably both." Kael set down the glass. "But I have something they don't expect. I've already harvested part of the Witness. I have memories, fragments of knowledge about their operations and their goals. If I can find a way to process those memories fully, extract the useful information—"
"You could become the cult's worst nightmare." Vane's smile turned predatory. "Or their greatest asset. Depending on perspective."
"Depends on who you ask."
Outside, the city was waking. Carriages rattled through streets. Merchants opened shops. Children ran to lessons, oblivious to the forces gathering in shadows and sealed tombs. The normal world continued its normal patterns, unaware that decisions made in basements and council chambers would reshape everything they knew.
Vane moved to the window, peering through a gap in the boarded planks. "There's something else you should know. About the Shadow Court."
"I'm listening."
"We didn't always operate as we do now. Fifty years ago, we were something different. A secret Order, dedicated to protecting humanity from the Abyss than maintaining the balance between Orders." His voice carried a weight of old grief. "Then the Whisper King rose to power within our ranks. He changed our purpose, our methods, our very nature. The Shadow Court became what it is now because of him."
"And where is the Whisper King now?"
"Gone. Vanished twenty years ago, after a catastrophic operation that cost us half our senior members." Vane turned from the window. "But his influence remains. The conspiracy you're uncovering? It has roots in decisions he made decades ago. The cult's structure, the Orders' internal corruption, even the state of the seals—all of it traces back to choices made by a man who saw the Abyssal Entities not as threats but as tools."
Kael's mind raced through the implications. The Whisper King—a figure of shadows and manipulation, shaping events from behind the scenes. If his influence still permeated the current conflict...
"You think he's still alive?"
"I think he's too dangerous to die." Vane returned to the table, spreading his hands across the documents. "The cult's operations, the weakening seals, the creation of Witnesses—none of it makes sense unless someone is coordinating from a position of absolute power. Someone who understands the original Soul Reaver's research. Someone who's been planning this for longer than either of us has been alive."
The pieces clicked together in Kael's mind, forming a picture both terrifying and clarifying. The conspiracy wasn't just about controlling the seals or releasing the Entities. It was about controlling what came after. Whoever emerged from that global catastrophe with power intact would reshape the entire world.
And Kael, with his impossible abilities and fragmentary memories, stood at the center of that struggle.
"One more thing," Vane said, reaching into his coat. He produced a small object—a badge, silver and black, bearing the symbol of a crown within shadows. "This is a Shadow Court insignia. Not an official one—the kind we give to assets we're cultivating. With this, you can access certain resources, enter certain places. It won't grant you full membership, but it will open doors."
Kael took the badge, feeling its weight. "Why give this to me?"
"Because everything I've seen tells me you're the key to everything. The cult wants to use you. The Orders want to control you. The Shadow Court wants to manipulate you." Vane's eyes glittered in the dim light. "But I'm not interested in any of those approaches. I want to help you. Not because it serves the Court, but because I think you might be the only one capable of stopping what's coming."
"And if I can't stop it?"
Vane shrugged. "Then the world ends, and none of this matters anyway. But I'd bet on someone who harvests souls without cost and survives encounters with Witnesses than on any organized faction I've ever served."
Kael weighed the badge in his palm. Such a small thing to carry such weight. A key to secrets, a mark of potential alliance, a symbol of trust from someone who had every reason to doubt him.
"I'll consider it," he said finally, pocketing the insignia.
"Consider quickly." Vane moved toward the door. "The cult will regroup within hours. The Orders will have investigators in Thornwick by nightfall. And somewhere, the Whisper King is watching, waiting to see what move you make next."
He paused at the threshold, glancing back. "There's a girl. Young, Awakened, works at the Thornwick Archives. She has access to historical records that even the Orders don't know exist. If you're looking for information about Aldric Voss, about the tomb, about the original Reaver's choice—she's your best lead."
"What's her name?"
"Maren." Vane smiled slightly. "She doesn't know it yet, but she's been expecting someone like you for a very long time."
Then he was gone, leaving Kael alone with the documents, the maps, and the dawn light creeping through boarded windows.
He gathered the materials, organizing them by priority and relevance. The journal from the cultist. The documents from Vane. The fragments of memory from the Witness. The knowledge that somewhere in this city, a conspiracy was preparing to reshape the world.
And he had a choice to make. The path forward wasn't clear, but the destination was becoming visible through the fog—a tomb that wasn't a tomb, a door that wasn't a door, a choice that had been waiting for two hundred years for someone brave or stupid enough to make it.
Kael stood, stretched muscles still sore from last night's battle, and moved toward the door. Outside, Thornwick waited—its secrets, its dangers, its opportunities.
The game was just beginning.
Footsteps echoed in the corridor outside. Heavy. Deliberate. More than one set, approaching slowly.
The cult had found him already. Or the Orders. Or someone else entirely.
Kael drew his blade and moved toward the shadows, ready to face whatever came next.
