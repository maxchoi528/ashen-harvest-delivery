The warehouse door groaned as Kael forced it open, rust biting into his palms. The corroded iron left orange traces on his skin, the scent of oxidized metal and old blood mixing with the damp air spilling from the dark interior. Lantern light flickered across stacked crates bearing the Mortician's Guild seal—a skull pierced by a scalpel, stamped in black wax. His newly enhanced perception flared, the ability he'd harvested moments ago bleeding into his vision. He could see the residual essence clinging to the wood, dark smudges of spiritual energy that clung to the grain. The warehouse was cold, breath pluming in front of his face.
"Wrong move, Ashborn." The voice came from the shadows between shelves. A figure stepped forward—a woman in black leather, silver sigils glowing at her collar. "The Court warned us you'd come sniffing."
Kael's fingers curled around the blade at his hip. The wrapped leather grip dug into his palm, callused skin fitting into familiar grooves. "Shadow Court?"
"The one you should've joined when they offered." She gestured, and six cultists emerged from the darkness, their eyes wrong—glazed, hungry. The pupils were dilated too wide, swallowing the iris, chemical-stimulated or spirit-touched. "But you had to play hero. Now you die in a nowhere warehouse, and nobody will remember."
One cultist lunged. Kael sidestepped, driving his elbow into the man's throat. Cartilage crunched under the strike, a wet crack that echoed off the low ceiling. He dropped, and Kael felt the familiar pull—Soul Reaver singing to life in his blood. The ability pulsed behind his sternum, that cold heat that never felt like his own. He pressed his palm against the falling body.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Lethal Precision from the remains.]
The notification flared behind his eyes. Skills flooded in: the exact angle to sever a carotid, the muscle memory of a hundred kills performed with clinical efficiency. His hands twitched as the knowledge embedded itself in his tendons, his joints, his spine. The dead man's expertise became his. The weight of the theft settled in his chest, familiar and hollow.
"Two down, four to go," Kael said, drawing his blade fully. Steel whispered against leather.
The woman laughed. "We're not here to kill you. We're here to deliver a message."
She threw something at his feet—a leather satchel. Kael's foot kicked it open. Inside: documents covered in sigils, sketches of rift locations, names. Dozens of names.
"The Court knows what you are, Reaver. What you can do." She backed toward a side door. "They know about the Sepulcher. About what you're really searching for."
The cultists advanced, forcing Kael to engage. He moved through them like water through stones—blade opening wrists, knees, non-lethal but crippling. Each touch yielded another harvest. Perception Enhancement. Network Analysis. Spiritual Barrier Weakening. He fed on their dying skills, their spent knowledge, their trained instincts. Each harvest carved something out of the dead and slotted it into his living flesh.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Spirit Sight (Partial) from the remains.]
Colors shifted. The warehouse walls became translucent, revealing ley lines beneath the floorboards—bright threads converging on a point three streets away. The world peeled back in layers, mortar and timber and stone giving way to the pulsing veins of spiritual energy that threaded beneath the city. The sensation was vertigo wrapped in clarity.
The woman vanished through the door.
Kael retrieved the satchel, scanning the documents with his newly enhanced perception. The sketches weren't random. They mapped the 72 sealed rifts—all of them—and noted degradation rates. Dates were circled in red. One location bore a fresh annotation: *Thornwick. Alpha-7. 14 days.*
Fourteen days until something happened at the rift beneath the city. The ink was still fresh, the parchment still carried the faint chemical scent of the preservative solution used by guild scribes. These documents had been copied recently. Prepared for him specifically.
A commotion outside. Boots on cobblestone—heavy, coordinated. Kael moved to a window and looked down into the street. Men in grey coats with the Order of Dawn's sunburst emblem poured from carriages, surrounding the warehouse. The carriages were unmarked, the horses dark. Whoever had arranged this didn't want the Dawn's involvement obvious to casual observers.
"Reaver Kael Ashborn!" A voice boomed through a speaking-trumpet. "By authority of the Council of Six, you are ordered to surrender. The Shadow Court has been designated a terrorist organization. All affiliated individuals are subject to immediate apprehension."
Kael's jaw tightened. The Shadow Court—affiliated with which Order? The documents in his hands suggested connections to all of them. His grip on the satchel tightened, leather creaking. He'd walked into a setup. The Court's ambush, the documents, the timing—someone had orchestrated this entire encounter.
He spotted movement on the rooftops. Snipers in positions that suggested planning, not reaction. Someone had known he would be here. The woman hadn't been running—she'd been buying time.
The warehouse door exploded inward.
Splinters sprayed across the floor. Brother Aldric Vane strode through the smoke, his golden armor gleaming despite the grime of the street. Two Dawn soldiers flanked him, weapons drawn. Vane's boots crunched on debris. The spiritual energy radiating from him pressed against Kael's skin like static before a storm.
"Ashborn." Vane's voice carried genuine disappointment. "I hoped you would be smarter than this."
"The Shadow Court left me a gift." Kael held up the satchel. "Seems someone wanted me to find this. Convenient timing for the Dawn, wouldn't you say?"
Vane's expression flickered—loss of composure, quickly masked. "Those documents are forgeries. The Shadow Court specializes in deception."
"Are they?" Kael pulled out a sheet covered in sigil-work. "This is written in the Order of Dawn's internal cipher. The one only senior brothers learn." He met Vane's eyes. "Someone in your Order is feeding information to the Shadow Court. Or is the Shadow Court inside the Order?"
The soldiers shifted uneasily. Their grips adjusted on their weapons, knuckles whitening.
"You twist words." Vane drew his blade—a standard weapon, not spiritual. "The Council has evidence linking you to cult activity. Your... Harvesting... Has been tracked for months."
"By whom?" Kael asked. "The Court that tried to kill me twenty minutes ago? Or the Order that sent someone to watch me die?"
Vane's silence stretched too long.
Kael made his decision. He threw smoke-powder at the ground—a common street technique, nothing spiritual—and dove for the back entrance. The powder ignited on contact with air, belching thick grey clouds that smelled of sulfur and rendered salt. Bullets whined past his head, the cracks of spiritual firearms deafening in the enclosed space. One round sparked off the wall beside his ear, leaving a scorch mark. He hit the door at full speed, splintering it outward into a narrow alley.
Rooftops. Always the rooftops in Thornwick.
He ran, leaping gaps between buildings with harvested agility guiding his limbs. The skill wasn't his—had belonged to someone else, someone dead—but it moved his body with precision his own training could never match. His feet found purchase on slate tiles, boots scraping against moss-slick surfaces. Behind him, Vane's voice ordered pursuit, but the soldiers were slower, less agile. Kael gained distance.
The city blurred beneath him. Chimney smoke stung his eyes. The sounds of the street rose up—shouting, the clatter of hooves, the hiss of gas lamps. Thornwick's nightlife churned oblivious to the chase playing out above. His lungs burned. The harvested agility was precise but demanding, expecting a body conditioned to its rhythms. His muscles strained to match the skill's expectations.
Three blocks away, he dropped into a basement through a broken window. Glass scraped his forearm, leaving a thin line of blood. Darkness swallowed him. He waited, breathing controlled, until the footsteps above faded.
The documents told a story he wasn't ready to believe.
Seventy-two rifts. All weakening simultaneously—not randomly, but on a schedule. The annotations suggested deliberate acceleration, as if someone wanted all the seals to fail at once. His Spirit Sight overlay showed him the ley line disturbances mapped in the documents. The patterns matched. This was real. Someone was systematically destabilizing the barriers between the Material and the Abyss.
And the names. Dozens of names, each marked with a location corresponding to a rift. Some names were crossed out—dead or captured, presumably. Others bore dates within the next month.
One name caught his attention. Sera Vance. Her location: Mortician's Guild Facility Seven, Thornwick Dock District.
The same facility Kael had been planning to investigate for unrelated reasons.
His throat tightened. Coincidence? In his experience, never.
He needed to move. The Dawn would have trackers—spiritual sensors that could follow his essence signature. He had a sliver of time before they narrowed his location. The Soul Reaver ability left traces. Every harvest deposited residual energy in his aura, invisible to normal sight but blazing like a beacon to anyone with the right training.
The satchel contained one more item: a black card stamped with a sigil Kael didn't recognize. The sigil seemed to shift when he looked at it directly, the lines rearranging themselves at the periphery of his vision. On the back, handwritten text: *When you're ready to stop pretending. The Court always welcomes the broken.*
The broken. That was how they saw him.
Kael tucked the card away. He had no intention of joining the Shadow Court, but he also wasn't foolish enough to destroy potential leverage.
Movement outside. Kael froze.
Footsteps—light, deliberate. Someone approaching the basement entrance. Not heavy boots. Not soldiers.
A woman's silhouette appeared in the doorway, backlit by distant streetlamps.
"Calm down, Reaver." Her voice carried an accent he couldn't place—something eastern, from beyond the mountain passes. "If I wanted you dead, you'd already be dead."
"Who are you?"
"Someone who watched you kill six men in under two minutes and still walked toward you instead of away." She stepped inside. Her face emerged from shadow—middle-aged, scarred, one eye replaced with something metallic that gleamed faintly. The artificial eye clicked and whirred as it adjusted to the basement's darkness. "My name is Vex. I represent the Iron Vanguard's interests."
"The Vanguard sent one agent?"
"The Vanguard sent me because we know what the Dawn is planning." Vex moved to a crate and sat, casual as if this were a social call. The wood creaked under her weight. "The Shadow Court isn't a terrorist organization. It's a creation. The Dawn built it forty years ago as a deniable operations arm. When the other Orders found out, they forced the Dawn to 'dismantle' it. The dismantling was a cover. The Court went underground, kept operating, and eventually grew beyond anyone's control."
Kael kept his blade ready. "And you're telling me this because?"
"Because the current Shadow Court leadership wants to break all 72 seals simultaneously. They believe humanity is better off living with the Entities than under Order rule." Vex's metal eye whirred, focusing on him. "The Dawn wants to use this chaos to consolidate power. The other Orders want to stop them. And you, Reaver, have become a wildcard that nobody can predict."
"Wildcard." He let the word sit.
"Your ability is unique. You can harvest skills from the dead—skills that took others decades to develop. You're not bound by the Law of Conservation because you take from others. The Seals were built using soul-energy from the first Awakened. If you could harvest from the right corpse..." She leaned forward. "You could potentially strengthen a seal instead of breaking it."
Kael's mind raced. The Sepulcher Tablets mentioned something about this—about using harvested essence to reinforce than destroy. He'd dismissed it as theoretical.
"Where's the proof?"
Vex reached into her coat and produced a leather tube. She tossed it to him. He caught it, the tube's weight surprising him. Inside: a single sheet of ancient paper, covered in script that made his eyes water. The characters writhed on the parchment, refusing to resolve into readable text. His Spirit Sight flared, and the script steadied. Old language. Pre-Consolidation. Older than the Orders themselves.
"From the Sepulcher itself. One of the original tablets, removed before the site was sealed." She watched his face carefully. "The Vanguard recovered it from a Dawn excavation team three years ago. We've been waiting for someone who could use the information."
"And you think that's me."
"The Soul Reaver chose you. That wasn't random. The first Reaver built the seal network. The second Reaver—the one they call the Whisper King—spent centuries trying to break it. You're the third. Which side are you on?"
Kael studied the tablet. The script was fragmented, but he recognized patterns. Reinforcement sigils. Stabilization sequences. And at the bottom, a phrase that made his blood run cold: *The Reaver that binds may also unbind. Choose wisely.*
His fingers traced the faded ink. The parchment was warm despite the basement's chill. Spiritual energy hummed through it. This was real. Genuine. Not a forgery.
"Fourteen days," he said. "That's when Alpha-7 fails."
"Eleven now. The timeline accelerated last week. We don't know why." Vex stood. "You have until then to decide. Help us reinforce the seals, or watch the world burn."
"What do you need from me?"
"Information. The Shadow Court is using cult networks to prepare for the unsealing. We know where some of those networks operate. We need someone who can infiltrate, harvest intelligence, and not leave witnesses." Her metal eye gleamed. "Someone like you."
"And if I refuse?"
"Then you die in some alley within the week. The Dawn wants you captured. The Shadow Court wants you dead. The cultists want to use you as a sacrifice. Even the Morticians have put a price on your head—apparently you've been disrupting their soul trade." She moved toward the door. "The Vanguard offers protection. Resources. Purpose. The others offer only death."
She paused at the threshold.
"The Mortician's Guild facility. The one with Sera Vance. The Shadow Court plans to use her in a ritual at Alpha-7. If you care about your ally, you'll want to move fast."
Then she was gone, melting into the darkness like she'd never existed.
Kael sat in the silence, the weight of choices pressing down on him. The basement smelled of mold and old stone. Water dripped somewhere in the darkness, a rhythmic patter that matched his pulse.
The satchel. The tablet. The black card. The names and dates and locations. A conspiracy that stretched across every Order, every faction, every power structure in the world.
And at the center of it: 72 rifts, 72 Entities, and a clock counting down to catastrophe.
His Soul Reaver ability pulsed in his chest—not with hunger, but with something that felt almost like anticipation. The first Reaver had built the seals. The second had tried to break them. The third...
Kael looked at the black card again. The Court's offer.
*When you're ready to stop pretending.*
He wasn't pretending. He was surviving. That had always been enough.
Until now.
A sound from above—scraping, like boots on the basement stairs. Too many to be Vex returning.
Kael moved, silent as death, toward a drainage grate he'd spotted earlier. His Spirit Sight tracked the spiritual signatures above—Dawn soldiers, their essence flaring with the rigid patterns of Order-trained Awakened. Above, the door burst open. Grey coats flooded in, Dawn soldiers spreading through the space with weapons raised.
"Clear!" one shouted.
"He's not here." Another voice—familiar. Kael pressed against the wall, barely breathing. The cold stone bit into his shoulder blades. Brother Aldric Vane descended the stairs, his golden armor dulled by the darkness. "The Reaver slipped us again."
"Orders, sir?"
Vane's response came slowly, weighted with something Kael couldn't identify.
"We continue surveillance. The Council wants him alive."
"And if he refuses capture?"
Vane didn't answer. He pulled a small device from his belt—some kind of spiritual tracker—and scanned the room. The device beeped twice. The sound made Kael's skin crawl. That pulse of spiritual energy had passed right through the wall, right through him. They were tracking his residual signature. His harvests. The dead cultists' skills he'd absorbed.
"He was here. Recently." Vane's boots turned toward the stairs. "Seal the area. I want every drain, every tunnel, every rat hole checked."
The soldiers moved out. Vane lingered, his back to Kael's hiding spot.
"Ashborn," he murmured, so quiet that Kael almost missed it. "You're either the answer to everything or the end of us. I haven't decided which."
Then he left, and the basement fell silent.
Kael counted to one hundred before moving. The drainage grate opened onto a tunnel that smelled of rot and stagnant water. The iron rungs were slick with moisture, cold against his palms. He climbed in, pulling the grate closed behind him. The sound of metal against stone was swallowed by the tunnel's depth.
Above, Thornwick churned with chaos—soldiers searching, cultists plotting, Orders maneuvering for advantage. Somewhere in that chaos, Sera was trapped in a facility that the Shadow Court planned to use for a ritual. Somewhere, Zarek was preparing to finish what he'd started. Somewhere, the 72 seals were cracking, and eleven days wasn't enough time to stop the inevitable.
But Kael had something nobody else had: the ability to take what he needed from the dead.
The tunnel led him upward, toward street level. He emerged in a narrow alley behind a tavern, the sounds of the city swallowing him—vendors shouting, wheels clattering, a baby crying somewhere in a tenement window. Coal smoke hung thick in the air, mixing with the salt-rot smell of the nearby docks. Gas lamps flickered at the alley's mouth, casting orange light across wet cobblestones. The world continued as if nothing had changed. For most people, nothing had.
Normal life. For how much longer?
He checked the documents again, mapping routes to the Mortician's Guild facility. Three guards on the perimeter. Two Awakened inside, likely Ascendant stage based on the energy signatures he'd need to avoid. A small price.
Kael's fingers brushed the black card in his pocket.
The Court wanted him broken. Wanted him desperate enough to join.
They didn't understand. Desperation wasn't his weakness. It was his fuel.
The night pressed in around him, thick with coal smoke and the distant rumble of steam engines. Somewhere, the cultists were gathering. Somewhere, the Orders were preparing for war. The city seethed with tensions it didn't understand, factions maneuvering in shadows while ordinary people slept.
And somewhere, in a facility guarded by the Mortician's Guild, Sera Vance waited for rescue that might never come.
Unless a Soul Reaver decided to change the equation.
Kael stepped into the street, letting the crowd carry him toward the docks. The press of bodies—dockworkers, prostitutes, drunks, beggars—swallowed him. He became one of a thousand shadows moving through Thornwick's arteries. Anonymous. For now.
Eleven days. Seventy-two seals. One chance to tip the scales.
The game had changed. The conspiracy had revealed its scope, and it was vaster than he'd imagined. This wasn't about one city or one Order. This was about everything—the fundamental structure of the world, the balance between the Material and the Abyss, the future of every soul that would ever live.
No pressure.
A figure fell into step beside him—young, nervous, clutching a folded paper. The boy's hands trembled. Sweat glistened on his upper lip despite the cold.
"Kael Ashborn?" The messenger's voice cracked. "I was told to give you this. Payment on delivery."
He handed over the note and fled before Kael could respond. The boy's footsteps receded into the crowd. Kael's hand closed around the paper. The weight of it was wrong—too heavy for a single sheet. Something was embedded in the fold. A token of some kind.
Kael unfolded the paper under a streetlamp. A single line, written in a hand he didn't recognize:
*The Tomb of the First Reaver lies beneath the Sepulcher. Third level. Enter through the bone gate at midnight. Come alone, or don't come.*
Below the text: a rough sketch of an entrance, marked with directional arrows. The lines were precise. The sketcher had been there, had seen the gate themselves.
Kael's heart hammered against his ribs. His pulse throbbed in his throat. The Soul Reaver ability stirred in his chest, resonating with something in the paper. The ink gleamed faintly under his Spirit Sight. The letter had been written with spiritual-infused pigment, invisible to normal eyes.
The original Soul Reaver's tomb. The source of his power. The answer to questions he'd carried since his Awakening.
It could be a trap. Almost certainly was. The warehouse ambush had been coordinated. The Dawn's arrival had been timed. Someone was pulling strings from a distance, arranging pieces on a board he couldn't see.
But if the documents were real, if the tablet was genuine, if any of this was true...
Then the first Reaver had left something behind. Something that could change everything.
A choice, then. The safe path—rescue Sera, gather intelligence, play the factions against each other. Or the dangerous path—descend into the Sepulcher, face whatever waited in the First Reaver's tomb, and potentially unlock knowledge that could save the world or doom it.
His jaw clenched. His knuckles whitened around the paper.
Footsteps echoed behind him. Multiple sets, moving with purpose. Not the aimless shuffle of dockworkers. These were coordinated, measured. The pace of hunters closing on prey.
Kael quickened his pace, ducking into another alley. The footsteps followed. He wove between crates and refuse, his boots silent on the slick stone. The alley narrowed. His Spirit Sight flared, scanning for spiritual signatures. Three behind him, maybe four. Their essence patterns were shielded, hidden behind something that felt like interference.
He wasn't alone tonight. He hadn't been alone since the warehouse. Someone was tracking him, coordinating with multiple factions, feeding information to everyone at once. The realization settled in his gut. He was a piece on someone's board. Had been since before the warehouse.
The conspiracy's true scope had just become terrifyingly clear.
And he was standing at its center.
The footsteps grew closer. Shadows shifted at the alley's mouth. Three figures. Four. Their silhouettes resolved from the darkness, blocking the exit.
Kael pressed himself against the wall, blade ready, and waited to see what the night would throw at him next.
