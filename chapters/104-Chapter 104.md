Fire erupted from the drake's throat, painting the alley in hues of amber and ruin. Kael rolled beneath a sweep of talons, felt the heat sear the edge of his coat, and came up running. Smoke coiled through the passage like a living thing, catching on brick and iron, filling his mouth with the taste of sulfur and char.
The Cindermaw Drake crashed through the wooden stalls behind him. Its obsidian scales scraped brick walls into dust, leaving pale gouges in the stone like scars carved by a giant's blade. Three tons of muscle and fury, with a maw that could melt steel. Kael could hear it breathing—a deep, bellows-like rasp that vibrated in his chest. He had seconds before it caught him, and he had no idea where he was.
Thornwick's Undercity. The gas-lamps down here flickered with a sickly green tint, their mantles cracked and weeping something that smelled of copper and rot. The light caught graffitied warnings scratched into the stone walls: SEAL WEAKENING. END COMES. The cultists' handiwork, carved deep with obsessive precision, the letters gouged over and over as if the carver couldn't stop. Kael's boots splashed through standing water—black, thick, reeking of something older than sewage. He darted left, using the narrow passages between collapsed buildings where the drake's bulk would work against it. The beast roared and rammed through a load-bearing pillar instead of going around. Half the alley collapsed behind it, sealing the route in a cascade of crumbling brick and choking dust.
"Clever beast," Kael muttered. His throat burned. He had no weapon, no spiritual reserves, nothing but the weight of his own lungs screaming for air and the cold certainty that the drake was already finding another path.
The Soul Reaver stirred beneath his skin. Not a thought. Not a voice. A pressure, like something turning over in sleep, aware that blood was nearby. It wanted. It always wanted. Kael pushed it down. Not now. Not like this.
Two streets ahead, a rusted grate led down into darkness. Kael did not hesitate. He grabbed the metal, felt corrosion bite into his palms, and wrenched it free with strength that made his joints ache in that wrong, borrowed way. The grate came loose in a shower of flakes and stagnant water. He dropped into the void below.
The drake's jaws snapped shut on empty air. The sound was a thunderclap—metal striking metal, teeth grinding against the stone edge of the grate opening. Then silence. Then the scraping began.
Kael landed hard on wet cobblestones, the impact jarring through his knees and up into his spine. He stayed low, one hand flat against the slick ground, breathing through his mouth. The air down here tasted of calcium and old earth. Above, claws scraped against stone in long, deliberate strokes. The creature was digging. He needed to move.
"You picked a poor hiding spot, Reaver."
The voice came from the darkness ahead. Kael's hand shot out, fingers closing around a loose brick. The clay was damp and cold against his palm. He readied himself, weight shifting to his back foot.
A figure stepped into the faint light filtering from the grate above. Male, middle-aged, wearing the gray coat and brass insignia of a Mortician's Guild operative. The coat was too clean—brushed, pressed, buttoned to the collar. Nobody dressed like that in the Undercity unless they wanted to be noticed. Aldric Vane. Kael had seen that face before, staring out from wanted posters in every district. Five hundred gold sovereigns for information leading to his capture. The ink on those posters was barely dry. Not a hunter. Not an Order member. Something else entirely. The kind of something that made Kael's neck prickle.
"Peace," Aldric said, raising empty hands. The palms were pale, uncalloused. "I'm not here to turn you in."
"Funny. That insignia says otherwise." Kael kept the brick at his side, ready to swing.
"The insignia says many things." Aldric's gaze flicked upward as the scraping above stopped. The silence was worse than the noise. "The drake will find another entrance. We have a few minutes at most. I'm offering you a choice."
Kael kept the brick in his hand. "Talk fast."
"In the last month, seven Abyssal creatures have breached the Undercity. This is the third drake. The seals are failing, Kael, and someone is accelerating the flow." Aldric spoke the way men do when they've rehearsed their lines. Measured. Controlled. Each word placed like a stone in a wall.
"You know my name."
"I know many things. Including the fact that you can harvest spiritual energy from corpses without depleting your own reserves." Aldric's eyes tracked to Kael's hands, then back to his face. "That's why the cult wants you dead, and why the Orders want you in chains. You're a walking violation of the Law of Soul Conservation."
Kael's grip tightened. The brick was warm in his palm now, heated by his own skin. The Law of Soul Conservation was the foundation of the Six Orders' power—the principle that spiritual energy could not be created or destroyed, only transferred at cost. Every guild, every academy, every throne in the civilized world had been built on that law. And Kael broke it every time he touched a corpse.
"What do you want?" Kael asked.
"Help me stop whoever is sabotaging the seals. In exchange, I'll give you what you came here for—the location of the last known Soul Reaver grave site in Thornwick." Aldric paused. Let the silence do its work. "Your kind wasn't extinguished by accident, Ashborn. Someone hunted them down. Systematically. To the last."
The scraping above resumed. Closer now. Kael could hear stone cracking, falling in chunks. The drake was patient. It could afford to be.
He weighed his options. Aldric was too calm, too informed. The man's pulse hadn't quickened once. His hands hadn't trembled. This reeked of manipulation—the kind where the trap was the offer itself. But the alternative was facing a Cindermaw Drake alone in the dark, and even the Soul Reaver had limits.
"Lead the way," Kael said.
Aldric turned and disappeared into the tunnel. Kael followed, keeping three paces behind, the brick still in his hand. He didn't trust the man. But he trusted the sound of the drake digging closer.
The passage twisted downward, cutting through layers of brick and older stone. Kael counted turns, memorizing the route. Left. Right. Down a half-flight of eroded steps. Left again. His eyes adjusted to the dark, revealing alcoves filled with old bones—centuries of the dead stacked like cordwood, femurs and skulls arranged in patterns that suggested ritual than storage. The Mortician's Guild maintained catacombs beneath every major city. This was one of theirs. The air grew colder as they descended, carrying the smell of calcium dust and something faintly sweet, like decayed flowers.
"The Abyssal Wars ended centuries ago," Aldric said, his voice low. The tunnel amplified every sound, turning his words into whispers that seemed to come from the walls themselves. "The Six Orders formed afterward to maintain the seals. Thornwick sits directly atop one of the seventy-two rifts. Seal Forty-Seven. The cult isn't random, Kael. Someone is orchestrating attacks across the city to weaken that specific seal. I need your help to find out who."
"I don't work for free," Kael replied. His voice bounced off the bone-lined walls. Somewhere deeper in the catacombs, water dripped in a slow, irregular rhythm.
Aldric's expression shifted. A tightening around the eyes. "The Mortician's Guild has resources. Soul-essence vials. Information networks. We want the cult stopped as much as you do."
Kael considered the offer. The Mortician's Guild operated in the shadows of legitimate business, harvesting the dead for power. They had no reason to care about cultists or seals—unless there was profit in it. And there was always profit in it for the Guild.
"What aren't you telling me?"
Aldric stopped walking. The tunnel opened into a wider chamber, its ceiling lost in darkness. Gas-lamps hung from iron hooks, their green light catching the brass buttons of Aldric's coat. He turned to face Kael, and for the first time, something moved behind his eyes. Not fear. Calculation.
"The cult has something. A fragment of a soul, taken from one of the original Soul Reavers." Aldric's voice dropped. "We believe it's being used to create more like you."
Kael's blood went cold. The brick in his hand felt heavier. The Soul Reaver beneath his skin stirred again—not with hunger this time, but with recognition. Like hearing a name you'd forgotten you knew.
The Mortician's Guild wanted to harvest the cult's knowledge and create more Soul Reavers. Or control the ones that already existed. Aldric needed Kael to infiltrate the cult's compound and retrieve the soul fragment before the cultists could use it to break the seal. The logic was clean. The motives were not.
"Show me," Kael said. "And you have a deal."
Aldric nodded once. He reached into his coat and produced a folded scrap of oilcloth, handing it to Kael without a word. Inside was a charcoal sketch of a building—warehouse proportions, with loading doors and a single entrance marked with the symbol of a textile guild. The eastern district. Kael recognized the street layout from the canals sketched in the margins.
They parted ways at the next junction. Aldric disappeared into a side passage without looking back. Kael stood alone in the dark, listening to the sound of his own breathing and the distant drip of water on stone. The drake had gone silent. That worried him more than the noise.
The cult's compound sat in the eastern district, disguised as a textile warehouse. Kael moved through the narrow streets, Aldric's instructions echoing in his mind. The building hunched at the end of a dead-end alley, its windows dark, its loading doors chained shut. From the outside, it looked abandoned. But Kael could feel it—a low hum in his bones, like standing too close to a furnace. Spiritual energy. A lot of it, concentrated and wrong.
He found a side entrance. The lock was new, the hinges oiled. Someone wanted this door to open quietly. Kael slipped inside.
The interior smelled of incense and copper. Candlelight bled through cracks in the floorboards above, casting thin lines of gold across the dusty ground floor. Kael moved along the wall, boots silent on the packed earth, and found the stairs. Each step groaned under his weight. He took them one at a time, breathing slow, listening.
Voices. Chanting. A language he didn't recognize—guttural, rhythmic, each syllable landing like a hammer on an anvil. The words pressed against his ears with a weight that wasn't physical. Old words. Words that remembered being spoken by mouths that no longer existed.
Kael reached the upper floor and pressed himself against a support beam. The warehouse's main floor had been cleared of machinery and looms. In their place, cultists in dark robes knelt in concentric circles around a pit dug through the concrete. The pit glowed with a faint, sickly light—the color of infected wounds. Seal Forty-Seven. Kael could feel it pulling at him, a gravity that had nothing to do with mass.
The cultists chanted. Their leader stood at the pit's edge—a woman, tall, her hair cropped short, her eyes burning with reflected light from below. Not candlelight. Something deeper. She raised her hands, and the chanting intensified. The floor vibrated. Dust rose from the boards in thin spirals.
Kael counted. Twelve cultists. One leader. The pit between them. He waited.
The ritual reached a crescendo. The woman's voice rose above the others, sharp and commanding. The glow from the pit brightened, and Kael felt the Soul Reaver surge beneath his skin—not waiting, not stirring, but pulling. Toward the pit. Toward whatever was down there.
He moved.
The first cultist died without a sound. Kael's hand closed around the man's throat from behind, and the Soul Reaver did what it did—pulled, drank, harvested. The body went limp. Energy flooded Kael's veins, hot and electric, and his vision sharpened. The second cultist turned too late. Kael caught the robe, twisted, and the man's weight carried him over Kael's hip and into the floor. The impact cracked the board. The Soul Reaver fed again.
The chanting broke. Cultists scrambled to their feet. Kael was already moving through them, his body faster than it should have been, fueled by stolen vitality. He caught a third by the arm, felt the spiritual energy rip free, and used the momentum to spin into a fourth. His elbow connected with a jaw. Bone cracked. The Soul Reaver drank.
But the woman was fast.
She moved like smoke, her robes billowing as she crossed the distance between them. Her hand came up, and Kael felt something hit him in the chest—not a fist, not a weapon, but a force that bypassed his ribs and squeezed his lungs from the inside. He staggered. The stolen energy scattered like sparks from a struck flint.
"You reek of the dead," the woman said. Her voice was calm, her burning eyes fixed on his. "What are you?"
Kael didn't answer. He lunged. She sidestepped, her hand tracing a sigil in the air that left a trail of black light. The sigil struck Kael's shoulder and numbed his entire arm. The brick—he'd been carrying it this whole time, he realized—clattered to the floor. She pressed forward, another sigil forming between her fingers.
Kael grabbed the nearest corpse—the cultist he'd killed first—and the Soul Reaver responded. Energy surged through the dead man's flesh, and for a moment, the body moved. It lurched upward, intercepting the woman's attack. The sigil struck dead flesh and dissipated. Kael used the distraction to put distance between them.
His arm hung useless at his side. The numbness was spreading. He could feel the Soul Reaver working beneath his skin, trying to metabolize the damage, but it was slow. Too slow. The woman advanced, and the remaining cultists closed ranks behind her.
"Kill him," she said.
They rushed him together. Kael met the first with a kick that caved the man's knee. The second swung a knife—Kael caught the blade in his good hand, felt it slice through his palm, and used the cultist's own grip to pull him into the third. They went down in a tangle. The Soul Reaver fed on contact, draining the life from the man beneath him in a rush that left Kael dizzy.
But there were too many. A club caught him across the back of the skull. The warehouse tilted. He tasted blood. Another blow landed on his ribs, and something cracked inside him with a wet sound. He went to one knee.
The woman stood over him. Her burning eyes studied him the way a surgeon studies a tumor—with clinical interest.
"Fascinating," she said. "The dead feed you. And you feed the dead in return." She looked at the pit. At the seal beneath. "You'll make excellent fuel."
She raised her hand. The sigil formed between her fingers, larger this time, the black light pulsing with a rhythm that matched Kael's heartbeat.
Kael's good hand found the floor. He pushed. His vision swam. The Soul Reaver screamed beneath his skin—not in words, not in language, but in a frequency that made his teeth ache and his bones hum. The woman flinched. Just for a moment.
It was enough.
Kael threw himself sideways, off the edge of the pit. He fell. The sickly light swallowed him, and for a moment, there was nothing but cold and the sound of something vast breathing in the dark.
Then stone. He hit the ground hard, his broken ribs screaming, and rolled into a narrow passage that branched off from the pit's base. The walls were slick with moisture that smelled of iron and age. Above, the woman's voice echoed down, distorted by distance.
"Find him. He cannot leave this place alive."
Kael crawled. The passage narrowed, then widened, then narrowed again. His arm was coming back—feeling returning in pins and needles as the Soul Reaver metabolized the damage. His ribs still ground together with each breath. His hand was still bleeding. But he could move.
He found a drainage grate at the end of the passage. The metal was rusted, the stone around it crumbling. He kicked it once, twice, and it gave way. Night air rushed in—cold, clean, tasting of coal smoke and canal water. He pulled himself through the opening and collapsed on the bank of a drainage canal, staring up at the gas-lamps of the eastern district flickering above him.
The warehouse was behind him. The cult. The woman with burning eyes. The pit and the seal and whatever lay beneath it. All of it, behind him.
For now.
Kael lay in the mud and breathed. His hand had stopped bleeding. His ribs had settled into a dull, persistent ache. The Soul Reaver was quiet again, sated on the energy he'd harvested, coiled beneath his skin like something sleeping.
He thought about Aldric's offer. The soul fragment. The grave site. The woman's words echoed in his skull: You'll make excellent fuel.
She'd recognized what he was. She'd known. And she hadn't been afraid.
Kael sat up. His body protested. He ignored it. The canal stretched ahead, cutting through the eastern district toward the river. He could follow it. Disappear. Find the grave site on his own terms, without the Mortician's Guild's strings attached.
But the seal. The pit. The thing breathing in the dark beneath the warehouse floor. He'd felt it. The Soul Reaver had felt it. And whatever it was, it was getting closer to the surface.
Kael stood. His knees buckled, then held. He started walking along the canal, keeping to the shadows. The gas-lamps flickered overhead, their green light catching the ripples in the water.
Behind him, in the distance, something roared. Not the drake. Something else. Something deeper.
The sound rolled through the streets like a wave, rattling windows and setting dogs to howling. Kael stopped. Listened. The roar faded, leaving a silence that pressed against his ears like cotton.
Then the Soul Reaver stirred. Not with hunger. Not with recognition.
With fear.
Kael's hand went to the wall beside him. The stone was vibrating. Faintly, steadily, like a heartbeat buried in the foundations of the city. He could feel it through his boots, through his palms, through the marrow of his bones.
The seal was cracking.
And in the darkness beneath Thornwick, something vast turned over in its sleep and opened one eye.
