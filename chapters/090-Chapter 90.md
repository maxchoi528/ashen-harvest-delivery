The Last Light Lighthouse stood three miles outside the city's official boundary, perched on a granite outcropping that jutted from the sea like a broken tooth. Once it had guided ships through Thornwick's treacherous harbor. Now its beacon had been dark for eleven years, and the building itself had become a landmark of wrongness—locals crossed themselves when they passed, priests from the Order of Dawn refused to even acknowledge its existence, and the fishing boats that once clustered in its shadow had long since found safer waters.
Kael approached through the tidal caves beneath the cliff, following a path worn into the rock by countless feet. The air here tasted different—salt and copper and something else beneath, something that prickled against his spiritual senses like static before a storm.
The cave opened into a natural chamber, lit by phosphorescent moss clinging to the walls. Water dripped from stalactites above, counting seconds in the darkness. And there, sitting on a flat stone as if he'd been waiting for hours, was the scavenger from the alley.
"You're punctual." The hooded figure hadn't moved. "I wasn't certain you'd come."
"You said cultists. Cultists mean Zarek." Kael scanned the chamber, cataloging exits—two, not counting the way he'd come in. "Talk fast. Dawn breaks in five hours, and I don't want to be caught in the open when the Wardens start their sweeps."
The figure reached up and pushed back the hood.
A woman's face emerged from the shadow. Middle-aged, sharp-featured, with the gray-streaked hair and calloused hands of someone who'd spent decades working with her hands. Her eyes, though—her eyes were old. Old and tired and furious about it.
"My name is Magistrate Elara Vance. I served on Thornwick's city council for fifteen years before the cult drove me out. Now I survive by knowing which alleys to hide in and which faces to forget." She stood, brushing rock dust from her coat. "The lighthouse is their center of operations. Not the building itself—the tunnels beneath it. The old naval armory, sealed after the Abyssal Wars ended. They've been excavating for months."
"Excavating what?"
Vance's expression tightened. "A rift. One of the seventy-two. The records say it was sealed during the original binding, but the seals are weakening. The cult knows this. They've been preparing—not just to witness the unsealing, but to guide it. To harness the Entity that emerges and bend it to their will."
Kael felt ice crystallize in his chest. Seventy-two rifts. Seventy-two Entities. If even one broke free at the wrong moment, with cultists ready to control it—
"The Council of Six knows about this?"
"The Council argues about jurisdiction while the seals crumble." Vance's voice dripped contempt. "The Order of Dawn wants to claim credit for stopping the cult. The Iron Vanguard wants to seize the lighthouse as a military asset. The Arcane Spire studies the phenomenon from a safe distance. The Veiled Circle profits from the chaos. The Thornguard protects their aristocratic masters, and the Silent Monks have disappeared entirely. Six Orders, and not one of them can agree on who should stop a knife aimed at the world's throat."
She reached into her coat and withdrew a rolled parchment, extending it toward Kael.
"Patrol schedules. Entry points. The names of cultists I've identified so far. Zarek's lieutenants—the ones who run operations while he plays prophet in the shadows. Take it. Use it. Or throw it away. I don't care anymore. I just want to see those bastards burn."
Kael took the parchment. The paper felt old, brittle, like it had been hidden for years. "Why me? Why not take this to the Orders yourself?"
"Because the Orders are compromised." Vance met his gaze, and the exhaustion in her eyes hardened into something colder. "Not all of them. Not officially. But there are cultists inside the Council, inside the military, inside the institutions that are supposed to protect us. I've seen them. I've watched them move. And every time I try to report through proper channels, my evidence disappears and my allies turn up dead."
She gestured toward the cave entrance. "You have no institutional loyalty. No master to betray you for advancement. You're a weapon they've already disowned. That makes you dangerous to the cult in ways that a magistrate or a Warden could never be."
"And what do you want in return?"
Vance's smile held no warmth. "I want you to walk into that lighthouse. I want you to burn their operation to the ground. And if you happen to find the man who ordered my daughter's execution—her name was Iris, she was a Warden, she died because she found the wrong evidence at the wrong time—tell him that Elara Vance remembers. Tell him the Reaver is coming for him next."
Kael tucked the parchment into his pack. "I'll find out what I can."
"Do more than that." Vance moved toward the far exit, pausing at the threshold. "The cult isn't just opening the rift for power. They're trying to summon something specific. A voice from the Abyss that promises to teach them how to break all seventy-two seals at once. If they succeed, this city won't survive the week. Thornwick will become a gateway, and everything behind it will pour through."
She vanished into the darkness.
Kael stood alone in the phosphorescent glow, the weight of her words settling over him like a burial shroud. Seventy-two rifts. Seventy-two Entities. A cult trying to break the world open.
And somewhere in the lighthouse tunnels, Zarek was waiting.
He checked his knife. Counted his soul-essence vials. Felt the new Rune of Warding humming in his soul, ready to activate if needed. The cave exit stretched ahead, leading up toward the lighthouse's foundations.
