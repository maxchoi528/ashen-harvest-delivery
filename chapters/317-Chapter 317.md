The blade found the cultist's throat before he could scream. Kael let the body drop, already moving toward the next target emerging from the fog. Three more figures in black robes, their movements coordinated, professional. Not random cultists. Organized cells.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted 12 spiritual units from the remains. Minor coordination acquired."
The notification pulsed in his vision as his foot connected with the second cultist's chest, sending him sprawling. The third lunged with a ritual dagger etched with Abyssal script. Kael pivoted, using the man's momentum to throw him into the fourth attacker.
"Contact front!" Sera's voice cut through the steam-hiss of Thornwick's underground district. She emerged from behind a rusted pipe manifold, twin daggers gleaming with spiritual reinforcement. "Six more coming down the eastern tunnel."
"Western passage?"
"Blocked. Collapsed when the first cultist blew the charges."
Kael counted heartbeats. Three attackers still struggling to rise. Two minutes before reinforcements arrived. If they could even call them that—the Order of Dawn's response teams were stretched thin across the city, handling at least a dozen simultaneous cultist strikes tonight.
The Abyssal cults were acting with unprecedented coordination.
"Change of plans," he said. "We push through. Whatever they're protecting down here matters enough to sacrifice soldiers."
Sera's eyes narrowed. She didn't ask how he knew. Three months of working together had taught her to trust his instincts, even when he couldn't explain them.
They ran.
---
Thornwick's industrial sector churned beneath the city's skin. Steam vents exhaled white clouds that mixed with coal smoke, turning the narrow passages between processing plants into a maze of corroded metal and shadows. The Mortician's Guild had abandoned these tunnels decades ago, leaving behind the skeletal remains of soul-extraction facilities that had once served the city's legal trade in spiritual essence.
Now something else operated down here.
Kael's Sovereign-stage senses traced the spiritual residue through the labyrinth. It formed a pattern—converging lines leading toward a central point beneath what the old maps called the Ashborn Refinery. The name struck him as he ran. Ashborn. His own surname. Coincidence, probably. Thornwick had thousands of residents with that lineage.
But the pattern of cultist movements wasn't coincidence.
"Here." Sera skidded to a halt before a section of wall that looked identical to every other corroded surface. She pressed her palm against what appeared to be a ventilation grate. Spiritual energy pulsed through hidden channels, and the wall shivered before sliding aside.
Kael studied her. "You knew about this passage."
"My sister was processed through a Mortician's facility. I've traced every route they use." Her jaw tightened. "Somewhere in this maze, the Guild holds prisoners they claim are 'volunteers.' I've found evidence that suggests otherwise."
The confession hung in the air between them. Sera had never discussed her sister's disappearance directly. Kael had assumed the topic was off-limits.
"Your sister—"
"Not tonight." She stepped through the hidden entrance. "Tonight we find out why the cults are converging on this location. After that, we can discuss my family drama."
He followed her into darkness.
---
The passage sloped downward, each step taking them deeper beneath Thornwick's industrial foundation. Spiritual residue grew thicker as they descended, coating the walls with an iridescent film that Kael's enhanced perception could barely parse. This wasn't residual energy from the dead. This was something older. Something sealed.
"Do you feel that?" Sera's whisper barely carried over their footsteps.
"The pressure? Like something's watching?"
"Like something's waiting."
Kael agreed. The sensation crawled along his spine, raising hackles he hadn't known he possessed. His Soul Reaver ability stirred in response, not from hunger but from recognition. Whatever waited below shared some fundamental connection to his power.
They emerged into a vaulted chamber that stopped his breath.
The space stretched a hundred meters in every direction, its ceiling lost in shadow. Pillars of black stone rose from a floor covered in geometric patterns—sigils that pulsed with sickly green luminescence. At the chamber's center, a massive stone coffin dominated the space, its surface covered in the same script that had adorned the cultists' weapons.
And around the coffin, arranged in careful circles, lay dozens of bodies.
Kael moved closer, his boots echoing against stone worn smooth by centuries of footsteps. The corpses were fresh. Hours old, maybe less. Their robes marked them as cultists, but their poses suggested sacrifice than combat. They'd been arranged deliberately, their bodies positioned to form patterns that mirrored the sigils on the floor.
"Gods below," Sera breathed. "They've created a spiritual array."
"A battery." Kael understood now. "They're using these cultists as fuel. Drawing their life essence into the coffin."
"For what purpose?"
He had no answer. The coffin radiated power that made his teeth ache, a frequency that resonated with his Soul Reaver ability like a struck tuning fork. The souls of the dead cultists flowed toward the stone container in invisible streams, their energy feeding something that slept within.
"Soul Harvest: Spiritual residue detected. 34 corpses available for processing. Estimated yield: 156 spiritual units."
The notification highlighted the bodies in his vision, their outlines glowing with potential. But Kael hesitated. Harvesting here meant touching each corpse, one by one. The flow would take time. Time the cults wouldn't give them.
"We need to leave." He grabbed Sera's arm. "Report what we've found to the Orders. This is beyond—"
"Wait." She pointed toward the coffin's lid. "There's writing."
They approached together, Kael positioning himself between Sera and the stone surface. If something rose from that coffin, he wanted to be the first target.
The inscription covered every visible inch of the container's exterior. Not Abyssal script—older. A language that predated the Orders, that predated the Abyssal Wars themselves. Kael couldn't read a single character, but the Soul Reaver ability translated the meaning directly into his mind.
*The First Reaver sleeps here. The hunger sleeps with Him. Wake not the tomb, lest you wake the hunger. It has no master. It has no limit. It will consume all.*
"Soul Reaver," Sera read aloud. "They mean you. There's a Soul Reaver in this coffin."
"A predecessor." Kael's hand hovered over the stone, feeling the thrum of power beneath. "The original. Or what's left of them."
His ability pulsed in response, stronger than he'd ever felt it. Not demanding harvest. Something else. Something almost like recognition.
"We need to report this immediately." Sera pulled at his shoulder. "If the cults are trying to resurrect a previous Soul Reaver—"
"They're not trying to resurrect anything." Kael's voice came out hollow. "Look at the sigils. Look at the arrangement of the bodies. This isn't resurrection. This is imprisonment. Someone sealed this Soul Reaver inside their own tomb."
"Then why are the cults feeding the coffin?"
The question hung between them as a sound echoed through the chamber—stone grinding against stone. The coffin's lid shifted, rising by inches before settling. Green light pulsed from the gap, synchronized with the rhythm of a heartbeat.
Something inside the coffin was awake.
Kael's hand closed around his blade. "Back. Now."
Too late.
The lid exploded upward, striking Sera's shoulder and sending her sprawling. Kael moved on instinct, blade arcing toward the figure rising from the stone container. His weapon clashed against a forearm that shouldn't have been able to block steel, met flesh that had turned to something harder than iron.
The face that looked down at him was human. Mostly. Sunken eyes that burned with emerald fire. Skin stretched tight over a skull that seemed to shift and reform with each breath. A mouth that opened too wide, revealing teeth that tapered to points.
"You carry my blade." The voice came from everywhere—from the walls, from the floor, from inside Kael's own chest. "You taste of my hunger."
"Soul Harvest: Warning. Progenitor Soul Reaver detected. Extraction not recommended. Danger level: Extreme."
Kael stepped backward, keeping his weapon between them. The thing that had been human regarded him with something that might have been curiosity. Its body continued to rise from the coffin, revealing robes that had fused with its flesh centuries ago.
"You want to take from me," it said. "I can feel it. The same hunger that drove me to consume the world. But you are young. Still tethered to mercy. Still believing you control the hunger."
"Control is everything." Kael found his voice. "The hunger serves me. Not the other way around."
Laughter filled the chamber, rattling loose stones from the ceiling. "Then prove it." The progenitor's hand shot forward, passing through the air an inch from Kael's face. "Kill your ally. Prove your devotion to the hunger. Feed me her soul."
"Absolutely not."
"Then you have already chosen your death."
The progenitor moved faster than Kael could track. Spiritual pressure crashed over him like a wave, pinning him against the floor. His Sovereign-stage power blazed in response, flooding his limbs with energy, but it wasn't enough. Nothing he had would be enough against this creature.
This thing that had been the first Soul Reaver.
Sera's blade flashed from the side, striking the progenitor's neck. The weapon shattered on contact, fragments scattering across the stone floor. The creature didn't even look at her.
"Insignificant," it pronounced. "You are all insignificant. The hunger made me a god. It will make you nothing."
Kael felt his ribs creak under the pressure. One more second and they'd crack. Two more and his lungs would collapse.
Three seconds.
The Soul Reaver ability detonated inside him.
Not in response to his will. In response to proximity. The progenitor's power crashed against his own, and the collision created a vacuum that pulled at both of them. The creature hissed, its burning eyes widening.
"You dare—" it started.
The ability didn't ask permission. It reached into the progenitor's form and pulled.
What emerged was not a harvest. It was a contest. Two Soul Reavers fighting over the same resource, their abilities interlocked in a struggle that made the air around them scream. Kael felt the progenitor's essence—fractured, ancient, corrupted beyond anything he'd ever touched—and found an edge. A point where the creature's power had been sealed. A weakness born from centuries of imprisonment.
He pushed.
"Soul Harvest: Progenitor resistance detected. Extraction in progress. Spiritual units draining... 89... 94... 102..."
The progenitor screamed, a sound that shook the chamber's foundations. Its grip on Kael weakened as his ability stole pieces of its power. Not much. Maybe a tenth of what the creature possessed. But enough to break the stalemate.
Enough to run.
Kael threw himself sideways, rolling across the floor to Sera's side. He grabbed her arm, hauling her upright as the progenitor thrashed against its own coffin.
"You cannot escape!" The creature's voice had lost its resonance, becoming thin and reedy. "The hunger knows no walls! I will find you! I will—"
Kael didn't hear the rest. He was already running, dragging Sera toward the passage they'd entered through. Behind them, the chamber collapsed as the progenitor's rage tore through the structural supports. Stone fell. Sigils shattered. The spiritual array that had powered the prison for centuries died in a cascade of dying light.
They emerged into the industrial tunnels just as the ceiling above them gave way.

"Report." Commander Vex of the Order of Dawn stood in the emergency response station, surrounded by maps and tactical displays. His scarred face betrayed nothing as Kael and Sera delivered their account.
"An ancient Soul Reaver," he repeated. "Sealed beneath Thornwick's industrial district. The cults were feeding it."
"Reviving it, more likely." Kael's voice rasped from the smoke they'd inhaled during their escape. "The seal was weakening. Another few days and it would have broken free."
"And you engaged this entity."
"It attacked us."
Vex studied him for a long moment. "You touched it. Your ability reacted to it. I can see the resonance in your spiritual signature."
No point denying it. Kael nodded.
"The original Soul Reaver," Vex continued. "We've known about the legend for centuries. Most assumed it was myth. The First Hunger, they called it. A monster that nearly ended the world before the Orders united to stop it."
"Did they stop it?"
"They sealed it. The cost was catastrophic. Half the Awakened who participated in the binding died in the ritual." Vex's jaw tightened. "The Orders have spent centuries ensuring no one rediscovered the tomb's location. Clearly, our security measures failed."
Sera stepped forward. "The cults knew exactly what they were doing. The bodies we found—the sacrifice array. Someone gave them the information."
"Someone inside the Orders," Vex agreed. "Or someone who has studied our histories long enough to piece it together themselves. Either way, this changes everything."
Kael understood what the commander wasn't saying. The progenitor's awakening wasn't just a local threat. If the First Hunger broke free, it would consume everything. Not just Thornwick. Not just the Orders. The entire world would become its feeding ground.
"We need to return to that chamber," he said. "Finish what the cults started. Seal it again before—"
"You've done enough." Vex raised a hand. "Your report has been logged. The Order of Dawn will handle containment from here. You and Sera are to stand down."
"Stand down?" Kael's temper flared. "That thing is still active. The seal you mentioned is compromised. If we don't reinforce it—"
"You'll do as ordered." Vex's voice hardened. "The original Soul Reaver is beyond your capabilities. Engaging it would mean your death. The Order cannot afford to lose another Awakened at this stage of the conflict."
The words carried weight beyond their surface meaning. Kael heard the implication. The Orders were losing the war against the Abyssal cults. Every Awakened mattered. Every death widened the cracks in the seal network.
But walking away felt like surrender.
"One question," he said. "The tomb I found. The inscription called the entity the 'First Reaver.' But there was something else. A warning. It mentioned hunger and consumption. What happened to the original Soul Reaver? How did it become that... Thing?"
Vex's expression flickered—something that might have been grief or guilt. "The first Soul Reaver discovered their abilities three thousand years ago. For a century, they protected humanity from the Abyss. But the ability came with a cost. Every harvest fed the hunger. Every consumption made it stronger. Eventually, the hunger consumed its host."
"The Soul Reaver became the monster it was meant to fight," Sera said quietly.
"Exactly." Vex turned away. "Now you understand why the Orders fear your kind. The hunger is not a tool. It is a predator wearing the mask of power. Eventually, every Soul Reaver faces the same choice the First faced: master the hunger or become it."
Kael absorbed the words, feeling them settle into his understanding of his own ability. The Soul Reaver wasn't just a gift. It was a parasite that would eventually try to consume him.
Unless he found a way to stop it.
"Commander." He kept his voice steady. "I appreciate the briefing. But I won't stand down. That thing in the tomb—it knows about me now. It felt my ability when we clashed. It's not going to stay sealed forever."
"Then we will handle it when the time comes."
"And if that time comes tonight?"
Vex met his gaze. Something passed between them—respect, maybe. Or recognition of a stubbornness that matched his own.
"Then I suggest you stay close to the response team," the commander said. "And pray the seal holds."

Outside the response station, Thornwick's night pressed down like a closing fist. The distant rumble of explosions echoed from the eastern district where another cultist cell had engaged Order forces. Steam vents hissed their endless breath. Somewhere in the darkness, a bell tolled for the dead.
Kael stood on the station's roof, watching the city burn in increments.
Sera joined him, her torn sleeve exposing a shoulder mottled with bruises. "You're not going to follow orders, are you?"
"I followed them for three months. Stayed within the Concordat's limits. Did everything the Orders asked." He watched another explosion bloom orange against the clouds. "It didn't stop the cults from finding that tomb. It didn't stop them from nearly releasing the First Hunger."
"So what now?"
Good question. The progenitor's warning echoed in his memory: *You are young. Still tethered to mercy. Still believing you control the hunger.*
But the hunger hadn't controlled him in that chamber. He'd pushed back against a creature that should have crushed him like an insect. His ability had responded to the threat, drawing power from the very entity that had birthed the gift in the first place.
He wasn't mastering the hunger. He was becoming something else.
"Research," he said finally. "The original Soul Reaver had three thousand years to learn about their ability. There's probably documentation somewhere—records from the Orders' founding. They must have learned something about the hunger during that first war."
"The Archive of Seals," Sera offered. "The Order of Dawn keeps their oldest records there. Centuries of documentation on Abyssal entities and how to contain them."
"Can you get us access?"
She smiled grimly. "I can get us in. Whether we get out again depends on how good your explanations are."
"I'll work on my bedside manner."
A new explosion rocked the district nearest the river. The sound carried a different quality than the others—deeper, more resonant. Kael felt the spiritual disturbance it created, a ripple in the world's fabric that made his teeth ache.
"What was that?"
Sera's face had gone pale. "That came from the harbor. From the direction of the—"
A second explosion answered, brighter and closer. Green light speared into the sky from somewhere near the docks, a pillar of sickly luminescence that painted the clouds in colors that shouldn't exist.
"The Third Rift," Sera whispered. "The cultists are opening the Third Rift."
Kael's blood ran cold. The 72 sealed rifts. The prisons holding the Abyssal Entities. If the cults could open one, they could open more. They could crack the seal network wide enough to let the Abyss bleed through.
"The Orders—"
"Spread thin across the city. Handling at least a dozen different attacks." Sera's voice carried an edge of despair. "This is what they wanted. Chaos everywhere at once. The response teams can't be everywhere."
Kael looked from the harbor's glow to the eastern district's fires. Two crises. Not enough resources to handle both. The Order of Dawn would have to choose which catastrophe to address first.
And somewhere beneath Thornwick's industrial sector, an ancient horror waited in its broken tomb, aware that its prison had finally cracked.
"You said you could get us into the Archive of Seals," he said.
"Yes, but—"
"Take me there. Now."
"The rift—"
"Is someone else's problem for the next hour. If we don't understand what we're dealing with, we can't stop it. The Orders have been fighting the Abyss for three thousand years and they still don't know how to kill an Abyssal Entity. That information exists somewhere. It has to exist."
Sera hesitated, then nodded. "This way."
They descended from the roof into Thornwick's maze of alleys and passages. Behind them, the harbor burned. Ahead, the Archive waited—ancient secrets that might hold the key to everything.
Kael's hands trembled as they ran. Not from fear. From anticipation.
The hunger stirred, tasting the fear that saturated the air. So many souls in peril. So many deaths approaching. His ability hummed with potential, ready to feed if he let it.
But he controlled the hunger. He reminded himself of that with every step.
*Still tethered to mercy,* the progenitor had said.
Maybe mercy wasn't weakness. Maybe mercy was the only thing that separated him from the monster in the tomb.
They turned a corner into darkness, and Kael felt eyes tracking them from the shadows. Multiple presences. Professional. Patient.
The Shadow Court had found them.
A figure stepped into the alley's narrow light—a woman with silver hair and eyes that glowed with the same emerald fire as the progenitor's. She smiled without warmth.
"Kael Ashborn," she said. "The newest Soul Reaver. We've been watching you for some time."
Behind him, other figures emerged from doorways and shadows. A dozen at least. Maybe more.
"Shadow Court," Sera breathed.
"Kael." The woman's smile widened. "My name is Vera. I knew your predecessor. The one in the tomb. We were friends once, before the hunger consumed him."
She extended a hand, palm upward.
"The Shadow Court would like to make you an offer. Help us release the First Hunger from its prison, and in return, we will teach you how to master the hunger. You will never become the monster he became. You will become something greater."
The harbor exploded again, closer this time. The ground trembled beneath Kael's feet.
He had seconds to decide.
"Soul Harvest: Hostile intent detected. 14 targets in range. Spiritual units available for emergency extraction."
The notification pulsed in his vision, waiting for input. His ability prepared to feed.
But Vera wasn't attacking. She was offering. And somewhere beneath the city, the entity that had started everything waited for rescue.
Kael's grip tightened on his blade.
"Tell me about the First Hunger," he said. "Tell me what really happened three thousand years ago."
Vera's smile turned predatory.
"Oh, child. That's a story that requires more than words." She gestured toward the shadows behind her. "Come with us. See the truth for yourself. Then decide whether you want to be the hunter or the hunted."
The city's screams continued around them. The Third Rift bled its corruption into the sky. The Shadow Court waited for his answer.
And in the distance, barely audible over the chaos, came a sound that made Kael's blood freeze—a low, resonant laugh echoing from somewhere beneath Thornwick's foundations.
The progenitor was awake.
And it knew he was here.
