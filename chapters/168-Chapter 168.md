The Drowned King's crown rose from the black water like a island of barnacles and bone. Kael stood on a slick stone pier, boots soaking through, and watched the thing that called itself sovereign of this rotting underworld reach for him with fingers made of drowned sailors.
"Another thief," the King rasped. Saltwater poured from a mouth that hadn't closed in centuries. "Another fool thinking the deep owes them answers."
Kael's hand found the hilt of his blade. "I didn't come for answers. I came for what's buried here."
The King's laughter echoed across the water—wet, broken sounds that stirred things beneath the surface. Shapes moved in the darkness between the pillars. Limbs that were not human. Eyes that reflected no light.
"You came for the Phase Step." The King's barnacle-crusted face split into something approximating a smile. "They always do."
Kael felt ice form in his stomach. He hadn't told anyone what he sought. The rift had appeared three miles beneath Thornwick's canal district, a wound in the stone that breathed cold air and the smell of brine. He'd followed it down through flooded tunnels, past the bodies of cultists who hadn't survived the descent, and into this place that shouldn't exist.
The Underworld. The realm beneath the realm. Where the Abyss leaked through old fractures and things older than the Orders made their homes.
"Word travels fast in the deep," the King said. He rose from the water, his body unfolding in ways that hurt to watch—bodies, plural, fused together beneath a corroded crown. "Or maybe you're the one they sent. The Reaver who doesn't know what he carries."
A bell rang somewhere in the drowned city. Then another. Then a dozen, echoing from every direction as the shapes in the water began to climb the pillars, the walls, anything that would hold their weight.
Kael counted seven. Eight. Twelve figures emerging from the dark, their movements synchronized in a way that suggested a single mind controlling them all.
"I'm not here to fight you," Kael said.
"No?" The King waded closer, water streaming from robes that had once been silk. "Then why do you carry a blade?"
"I said I'm not here to fight." Kael forced his hand away from the weapon. "I'm here to negotiate."
The King stopped. His head tilted at an angle that would have broken a human neck.
"Negotiate," he repeated, tasting the word like something rotten. "In the Drowned Court. With the dead who drowned the living." His smile widened, showing teeth that were different sizes and shapes, harvested from different mouths. "What could you possibly offer me, little Reaver, that would interest the King of Those Who Did Not Die?"
"Information," Kael said. "About what's happening above. About the cultists who've been using your rifts as shortcuts."
The bell-ringing stopped. The King's terrible smile faltered.
"Speak carefully, Reaver. The dead are sensitive about their territory."
"I'm not lying. Thornwick's been crawling with cultists for months. They've been digging through the old tunnels, using them to move goods, weapons, people. They've been using the rifts."
"Every realm has its scavengers." The King waved a hand that trailed kelp. "We don't concern ourselves with the surface."
"This is different." Kael took a step forward, ignoring the figures closing in around him. "They're not scavengers. They're organized. Someone's coordinating them, giving them access to old tunnels, old maps. Someone who knows about this place."
The King's eyes—the real ones, buried somewhere in that mass of fused faces—narrowed.
"You're trying to frighten me."
"I'm trying to make you curious." Kael reached into his coat and pulled out a rag-wrapped bundle. He unwrapped it slowly, letting the torchlight catch on the symbols etched into a jade tablet. "I found this in a cultist's camp. It's written in Old Sepulcher. The language of the first Soul Reavers."
The silence that followed was absolute. Even the water stopped lapping against the stone.
"Where," the King said, and his voice had dropped an octave, "did you get that."
"Does it matter?" Kael tucked the tablet away. "What matters is that someone out there knows about the old pacts. The agreements your kind made with the original Reavers. And they're using that knowledge to open doors that should stay closed."
A ripple passed through the assembled dead. They exchanged glances—not with each other, but with something unseen. A communication Kael couldn't follow.
"You have sixty seconds," the King said, "to convince me not to feed you to my court. Speak."
Kael took a breath. This was the gamble. Everything depended on the next words.
"The cult isn't trying to free your kind. They're trying to harvest you."
The King's face went blank.
"The old texts describe it," Kael continued, pressing the advantage. "Soul Reavers didn't just harvest individuals. They could harvest entire locations. Lock onto a place's spiritual resonance and drain it dry. That's what the seals really are, isn't it? Not just barriers. They're anchors. Keeping your kind tethered to specific locations so you can't be... Collected."
The King's composure cracked. For a moment, Kael saw something beneath the cruelty and the decay—fear. Old, deep fear.
"You're lying."
"I have no reason to lie. If the cult succeeds, they won't need you anymore. They'll have the power to take what they want directly from the Abyss. No more intermediaries. No more kings."
"Then why are you here?" The King's voice shook. "If you know what they want, why come alone? Why not bring an army?"
Kael met the King's gaze without flinching. "Because I'm not with the cult. I'm not with the Orders. I'm not with anyone but myself. And I need something from this place before everything falls apart."
"What?"
"Phase Step. The technique they buried with your champion."
The King's face twisted. "That ability is not for the living."
"Then it's perfect for me." Kael spread his hands. "I can either take it from your champion's corpse, which I've been told is possible but difficult, or I can negotiate with you for access to it. Your choice."
The dead around them began to murmur. Shapes in the water churned. The pressure in the chamber shifted, building toward something Kael couldn't predict.
Then the King laughed again—but this time it was different. Quieter. Almost... Amused.
"You have spine, Reaver. I'll grant you that. Most who come here are screaming by now."
"Most who come here don't have leverage."
"No." The King's smile returned, but it was colder now. Calculating. "They don't." He turned to his court, exchanging words in a language Kael didn't recognize. The assembled dead nodded, then began to sink back into the water one by one, disappearing beneath the surface until only the King remained.
"I will give you what you seek," the King said. "But not for free."
"What do you want?"
"A service. A promise. When the time comes, and it will come soon, you will help us. Not the cult. Not the Orders. Us. The forgotten dead who have kept the Abyss at bay for centuries while the living forgot we exist."
Kael considered. The words were a trap—he knew it, the King knew it. But the alternative was fighting through a dozen undead guardians and hoping he could harvest the skill from their remains before the King drowned him.
"Define 'help'."
"When the sealed rifts begin to fail—and they will, Reaver, they already are—you will not stand against us. You will not block our passage into the surface world. You will let us rise, and you will let us claim what was promised to us."
"And what's that?"
The King's face twisted. "Everything. The world belongs to the dead. It always has. The living only borrowed it."
"That's not an answer."
"It's the only one you'll get." The King extended a hand that was more kelp than flesh. "Do we have an accord?"
Kael stared at the offered hand. The Drowned King had drowned thousands. He'd pulled ships under, flooded cities, murdered anyone who sailed these waters in the centuries before the Orders consolidated power. Making deals with him was madness.
But the Phase Step was buried here. And Kael needed every advantage he could get.
"Agreed," he said, and clasped the King's hand.
The grip was cold. Wrong. Kael felt something shift in his chest—a resonance, a binding—and then the King released him and turned toward the drowned city.
"Follow. The champion's tomb lies beyond the Bone Gardens. Do not stray from the path, and do not touch anything that grows in the dark."
The King walked into the water. It parted around him like a curtain, revealing a staircase that spiraled downward into deeper darkness.
Kael followed.
The path wound through structures that had once been buildings—towers with windows like eye sockets, bridges made of ribcages, parks where nothing grew but coral and bone. The dead watched from doorways and rooftops, silent figures that tracked his movement with eyes that glowed faintly in the dark.
"Curious place," Kael murmured. "Is all of this... Them?"
"The drowned. The drowned and the drowned-out." The King's voice echoed strangely in the underwater tunnels. "Every ship that sank. Every sailor who went down with the crew. Every city that fell to the rising sea. They all end up here, eventually."
"That's a lot of souls."
"The Abyss takes most. But enough slip through. Enough linger. Enough remember what it was to be alive." The King glanced back at him. "Does that trouble you, Reaver? All that death?"
"No." Kael kept his voice steady. "I'm the one who harvests them. I don't have room to be troubled."
"Honest, at least. The Orders would have lied. Called us monsters. Abominations." The King spat into the water. "While they built their halls on our graves and taught their children we never existed."
They reached the Bone Gardens.
Kael stopped. The garden stretched before him like a nightmare made flesh—trees of human bone, their branches intertwined, forming a canopy that blocked out the faint light from the tunnels above. Flowers made of fingers bloomed in patches along the ground, their petals opening and closing slowly, as if breathing. In the center, a path wound through the grotesque forest toward a structure that looked almost like a temple.
"Do not stray," the King reminded him. "The bone-flowers hunger. And the trees... They remember being human. They don't appreciate visitors."
"Comforting."
The King didn't smile. He led Kael along the path, through the garden of dead things, past a fountain that ran with black water and something else Kael didn't want to identify. The smell was overwhelming—brine and rot and something sweet underneath, like decay flavored with perfume.
They reached the temple. It was small, built from coral and bone and stone that had never seen sunlight. The entrance was guarded by two statues that might have been human once but were now more statue than corpse.
The King pressed his hand to the door. Symbols flared with pale light, and the door swung inward.
"Go. Your prize waits inside."
Kael stepped through. The interior was dim, lit by bioluminescent moss that clung to the walls. A single sarcophagus dominated the center of the chamber—black stone carved with images of waves and drowning and something else, something that hurt to look at directly.
A warrior lay on the sarcophagus. Dead, obviously—the skin was blue-white and waterlogged, the eyes empty sockets filled with pearls. But something remained. A residue. A presence that Kael could feel with his Soul Reaver senses, reaching out to him like a hand from a grave.
This was the champion. The one who had Phase Step, whatever that meant, buried with them in this watery tomb.
Kael approached the sarcophagus. His heart hammered in his chest. This was it. This was what he'd descended into the Underworld to find.
He placed his hand on the champion's cold chest.
[Soul Harvest: Champion of the Drowned Court — nearly all soul coherence detected. Phase Step skill fragment detected. Proceeding with extraction.]
Power flooded into him. Not the usual trickle he felt when harvesting from fresh corpses—this was a torrent, a river of memories and skill compressed into something almost too vast to process.
Images cascaded through his mind: a young man standing on a ship's prow, arms raised as a storm approached. The same man fighting in an arena, his movements blurring between positions. The same man drowning, water filling his lungs, but instead of dying he moved *through* it, his body slipping between moments of reality like a fish through water.
The memories crystallized into understanding.
Phase Step. A technique that allowed the user to slip partially out of reality, becoming untouchable for brief moments. Not invisibility—something deeper. A step into the space between heartbeats, between thoughts, between the present and what comes next.
The extraction completed. The champion's body crumbled, dust and salt and the memory of a man who had once commanded the tides.
[Phase Step acquired. Passive ability: When activated, the user phases 0.a few heartbeats ahead of their current position. Cooldown: a few heartbeats. Active cost: 12 soul-energy per use. Note: Effectiveness increases with user advancement.]
Kael steadied himself against the sarcophagus. His hands shook. His vision swam. But the ability was there, humming in his chest like a second heartbeat.
"Impressive," the King's voice echoed from outside. "Most Reavers can't take anything from my champions. The old pacts protect them."
"The old pacts didn't account for me."
"No." The King appeared in the doorway, his form silhouetted against the dim light. "They didn't."
For a long moment, neither of them moved. Kael's hand drifted toward his blade. The King's eyes glittered with something that might have been respect or might have been hunger.
"The accord stands," the King said finally. "When the time comes, remember what you promised."
"I will."
"Good." The King turned to leave, then paused. "One more thing, Reaver. A gift, from one who respects what you are."
"What?"
"The Whisper King. That is what the cultists call their leader. The one orchestrating all of this." The King's voice dropped. "He was once like you. A Reaver. But he wanted more than power. He wanted to become something else entirely."
The words hit Kael like a physical blow. A former Soul Reaver. Leading the cult. Trying to unseal the rifts.
"Who?"
"I don't know his true name. No one does. But I know what he seeks." The King's face twisted. "He wants to merge with the Abyss itself. To become the 73rd Entity. And when he does, every Reaver who has ever lived will belong to him."
The King left before Kael could respond.
Kael stood alone in the champion's tomb, the Phase Step ability burning in his chest, the weight of new knowledge pressing down on him. A former Reaver. A conspiracy reaching back centuries. A plan to unmake reality itself.
He needed to get back to the surface. He needed to warn someone—the Orders, if they'd listen, or someone else. Anyone.
A sound echoed from the Bone Gardens. Not the King's footsteps. Something else. Something moving through the bone-flowers, disturbing their slow breathing rhythm.
Kael drew his blade and moved toward the entrance.
A figure stood at the temple's threshold. Human-shaped, but wrong—the proportions were off, stretched in ways that made Kael's eyes water. The figure wore robes that had once been fine but were now rotting, and on its head sat a crown of black coral.
The Whisper King.
"Impressive," the figure said, and its voice was like wind through a hollow skull. "You negotiated with the Drowned fool. I underestimated you."
Kael's grip tightened on his blade. "You followed me."
"I have eyes everywhere, Reaver. The cult reports to me. The Orders report to me. Even some of the dead hear my voice." The figure stepped closer, and Kael saw its face—or where a face should have been. Nothing. A void that pulled at his gaze.
"Why are you here?"
"To see you, of course. To see what you've become." The Whisper King tilted its head. "You have potential. More than any Reaver I've encountered in centuries. That's why I need you."
"Need me for what?"
"To serve. To join. Or to die." The void-face twisted into something like a smile. "Choose quickly. My patience ended long ago."
Behind Kael, the champion's remains began to stir. Dust and bone pulling themselves together, reforming into something that would attack him the moment he turned his back.
In front of him, the Whisper King extended a hand made of shadow.
And in the distance, echoing through the drowned tunnels, came the sound of voices. Cultists. Dozens of them, converging on the tomb.
Kael was surrounded.
The Phase Step hummed in his chest, ready to be used. The Whisper King waited for his answer. The cultists drew closer.
And somewhere in the darkness, the Drowned King watched, waiting to see which way the Reaver would fall.
Kael raised his blade. "You want an answer? Here's one."
He activated Phase Step.
The world stuttered. Kael felt himself slip forward, between heartbeats, between moments—a fraction of a second that stretched into eternity as his body moved three-tenths of a second ahead of where he'd been.
He appeared behind the reforming champion, blade arcing down. The creature's skull split open, and Kael harvested its remains in a single motion, tearing out whatever remnant consciousness had animated it.
[Soul Harvest: Remnant Champion — a third soul coherence detected. Memory fragment acquired.]
Fragments of the cult's plans flooded into him. Their numbers. Their targets. The location of their primary sanctum beneath Thornwick's old cathedral.
Kael spun, looking for the Whisper King—but the figure was gone. Only shadow remained, streaming toward the tunnel entrance where the cultists were emerging.
"Run, Reaver," the shadow's voice echoed. "Run back to your surface world. Run and hide. When I rise, there will be nowhere left to run."
The cultists poured into the temple. Kael cut down the first two, harvested their skills in the chaos—nothing useful, just fanatical devotion and a rudimentary control of shadows—and then he ran.
Through the Bone Gardens, where the bone-flowers reached for him with desperate fingers. Past the fountain of black water, where hands emerged to grab his ankles and he had to cut his way free. Into the flooded tunnels, where the Drowned King's servants watched him pass but did not interfere.
The accord held. For now.
Kael surfaced through a crack in Thornwick's canal district, gasping for air that didn't smell of brine and death. The city above was dark, lit by gas lamps and the glow of steam engines. Normal. Peaceful.
Deceptive.
He climbed out of the water, his clothes soaked, his body exhausted, his mind racing with everything he'd learned. The Whisper King was a former Reaver. The cult was organized, sophisticated, centuries in the making. The sealed rifts were failing faster than anyone knew.
And he was the only one who could stop it.
No. Not alone. He couldn't do this alone.
Kael staggered toward the nearest safehouse—a cellar beneath a defunct tannery where he'd stashed supplies. He needed rest. He needed to process the Phase Step ability. He needed to figure out what to do with the information burning in his head.
But first, he needed to know one thing.
He reached into his coat and pulled out the jade tablet, the one written in Old Sepulcher. The symbols were worn, ancient, but he could read them now that the harvest had given him fragments of the original Reaver's knowledge.
The first line translated as: "When the 73rd rises, the 72 will answer."
The second line was worse.
"He who wields the Reaver's power will either become the key or the lock. There is no third choice."
Kael stared at the tablet until his vision blurred.
A key or a lock.
The Whisper King wanted to be the key—unlocking the Abyss, merging with it, becoming the 73rd Entity. That much was clear.
But what did that make him? The lock? Something to be destroyed to prevent the unsealing?
Or something else entirely?
Footsteps echoed in the alley above. Kael moved into shadow, blade ready.
A figure descended the cellar stairs. Female. Hooded. Moving with the careful grace of someone who knew how to fight.
Gwen.
She stopped when she saw him, her hand going to her weapon.
"Kael. You're alive."
"Disappointed?"
"No." She hesitated. "Where have you been?"
"Underground." He didn't elaborate. "What are you doing here?"
"Looking for you. The Shadow Court wants a meeting. They have information about the cult's next target." Her eyes narrowed. "What happened to you? You look like something crawled inside you and died."
"Something like that."
Gwen studied him for a long moment. Whatever she saw made her take a step back.
"You're different. I can feel it. What did you do down there?"
Kael considered lying. Considered keeping his cards close, his secrets closer.
But he was tired. And the world was ending. And maybe—just maybe—he needed allies more than he needed secrecy.
"I made a deal with the dead," he said. "And I learned something that changes everything."
"What?"
Before he could answer, a sound echoed from above. Not footsteps. Something else. A whisper, carried on the wind, sliding through cracks and.
The Whisper King was here.
Or at least, his voice was.
"Run while you can, Reaver. The game has changed. And you just became the most valuable piece on the board."
Kael's hand went to his blade. Gwen drew her weapon. The whisper laughed, then faded.
Above them, in the city that didn't know it was about to be devoured, bells began to ring. Emergency bells. The sound of the Orders mobilizing.
Something was happening.
Kael didn't know what yet.
But he intended to find out.
