The vial shattered against the cobblestones, splashing violet light across Kael's boots. The luminescent liquid pooled in the cracks between the worn stones, hissing as it ate through a layer of soot. The smell hit him a second later—ozone and burnt copper, the unmistakable tang of refined essence scorching the air.
"Thirty seconds." The cloaked figure dissolved into the rolling steam and amber gaslight of Thornwick's Underbelly, leaving nothing behind but the acrid scent of soul-essence burning into the stones. "The Ashfen Market. Come alone."
Kael crouched, fingers hovering over the stain. Heat radiated from the puddle, warming his knuckles. Not a trap—his instincts screamed that much. The hair on his arms stood on end, a cold prickling at the base of his skull. Whoever had delivered this message wanted him to follow, not to spring an ambush. The vial's residue hummed with the particular frequency of Awakened essence, vibrating against his fingertips when he brought them close enough. Expensive and illegal, worth a small fortune on the Veiled Market. No one wasted product like this on a kill contract. No one threw away that kind of currency unless the information mattered more than coin.
He stood and moved.
The Underbelly breathed around him—wet stone underfoot, the rhythmic clank of distant steam-pipes, the ever-present taste of coal dust settling on his tongue. The gas lamps lining the narrow alley flickered in their brass housings, their flames guttering whenever a burst of pressurized steam vented through the grates. His boots found the familiar rhythm of cobblestone, puddle, broken brick. He kept to the shadows where the light didn't reach.
The Ashfen Market operated in the hours between midnight and dawn, when the city's patrols thinned and the steam-gnomes that powered the district's lamps cycled down to their lowest setting. Kael wove through crowds of laborers, merchants, and people whose faces suggested they preferred darkness for reasons of their own. A woman selling bottled moonlight from a pushcart caught his eye and looked away fast. A man with mechanical arm extensions haggled over a pile of rusted cogwork, his voice a low drone beneath the market's ambient murmur. The air hung thick with competing smells—roasting meat from a food stall, the chemical bite of alchemical reagents, the sweeter note of pipe smoke drifting from a cluster of gamblers hunched over a dice game.
He passed through the crowd without touching anyone. A skill he'd perfected. The less contact, the fewer memories brushed, the fewer questions asked.
The booth waited at the market's edge—a canvas pavilion with no sign, no goods on display. A single lantern burned inside, casting long shadows through the fabric walls. The canvas billowed slightly in the breeze, and the light within danced across its surface like something alive and pacing.
"You're either brave or stupid." The voice came from everywhere and nowhere, the acoustic trick of someone who knew how to build a sound chamber. The words seemed to reflect off the cobblestones, bounce against the market stalls behind him, and converge on the pavilion from all sides. "The Shadow Court has eyes in every gutter of this city. You walk through their territory like you're taking a morning stroll."
Kael's hand found the door flap. The canvas was rough under his fingers, starched and weathered. "You wanted to talk. Talk."
The canvas parted.
The woman inside looked like she'd been carved from pale wood and sharp edges. Her cheekbones could cut glass. Her jawline spoke of someone who hadn't eaten well in weeks but hadn't let weakness slow her down. She knelt on a threadbare cushion, her coat pooling around her in dark folds. Her eyes caught the lantern light at wrong angles, the pupils too large, the irises ringed with silver threads that pulsed when she moved. Spiritual sensitivity—the second tier, not Awakened, but not blind either. She could see the residue that clung to him. Could probably taste it in the air between them.
"Sit." She gestured to a cushion opposite her. The fabric was worn, stuffing protruding from one seam. "I have something to show you, Soul Reaver. Something that explains why every cultist in Thornwick wants your head mounted on their wall."
Kael remained standing. His calves burned from the crouch earlier, but he wasn't about to lower himself. Not in a stranger's tent. Not in this city. "Name first."
"Sera." The word came out clipped, professional. She didn't blink. "I work for the Mortician's Guild, but not the part that sells essence to the Arcane Spire. I work for the part that processes the dead. And what I've found in the last six months—" She pulled a leather folio from beneath the cushion, spreading its contents across the floor between them. The papers whispered against each other as they fell. "—suggests that the deaths in Thornwick aren't random."
Kael's gaze dropped to the papers. Death certificates, yes, but the dates told a story. Forty-seven corpses in ninety days, all with the same cause listed: spiritual resonance failure. All Awakened, ranging from Ascendant to low Transcendent. All missing their soul-essence reservoirs entirely. The ink on the certificates had faded unevenly, some written in haste, others in careful script. Different hands. Different district offices. But the same result, over and over.
"The Mortician's Guild harvests essence legally," Sera said. "We take residue, the dregs that dissipate after death. But these bodies—" She tapped a certificate. Her nail made a sharp sound against the stiff paper. "—are bone dry. Someone harvested them before we could reach the bodies. Someone with the ability to pull every drop from a corpse in minutes."
Her silver-threaded eyes fixed on him. The glow intensified, casting faint light across her cheekbones. "Someone like you."
Kael picked up one of the certificates, studying the name at the top. Marcus Ashworth, Order of Dawn, Ascendant stage. Dead three weeks ago in what the official report called a 'routine patrol incident.' The paper felt wrong—too stiff, as though the essence of the person it documented had leeched into the parchment itself. "You're accusing me of—"
"I'm telling you what the Shadow Court believes." Sera's voice hardened. She sat straighter, her shoulders squaring beneath her coat. "They've compiled a dossier on every unusual death in the city for the past two years. Pattern analysis suggests one harvester, possibly two working together. They presented their findings to the Council of Six last month. The conclusion?" She smiled without warmth. The expression didn't reach her eyes. "Soul Reaver activity in Thornwick has increased seven hundred percent. Someone is destabilizing the local Awakened population."
"Or someone is killing Awakened and framing Soul Reavers."
"That's what I told them." Sera gathered the papers back into the folio, her movements precise and practiced. "But they weren't interested in alternatives. The Whisper King has been pushing hard for a unified response to the Soul Reaver threat. He wants authorization to conduct 'sanctioned acquisitions'—their term for harvesting Reavers for research."
Kael felt something cold settle in his stomach. His jaw clenched. The muscles in his forearms tightened beneath his sleeves. "They want to capture me."
"They want to capture any Reaver they can find. There are three confirmed in Thornwick right now. You're one of them." She leaned forward. The lantern flame caught the silver in her eyes again, and for a moment she looked less human and more like something that had crawled out of one of the rifts she was describing. "But here's what the Shadow Court doesn't know, what even the Council doesn't know. I traced those forty-seven deaths backward. The victims weren't random targets. They were all investigating the same thing: the seal network."
The lantern flame flickered, and for a moment the shadows in the pavilion seemed to lean inward, hungry. The canvas walls went taut as the wind shifted. Somewhere outside, a steam-pipe groaned.
"Forty-seven Awakened, all tasked with monitoring the rifts around Thornwick, all dead within three months of each other." Sera's silver-threaded eyes pulsed brighter. "Someone is clearing the field. Someone who knows exactly how the seal network works, who has access to patrol schedules and rotation orders. Someone on the inside."
"You're saying there's a traitor."
"I'm saying there's a conspiracy." She pulled out another paper—this one handwritten, cramped with details. The ink was faded, the margins filled with annotations in a different hand. "The seal network around Thornwick consists of eleven primary anchors and sixty-one secondary nodes. Every Awakened in the city takes a rotation maintaining at least one seal. These forty-seven were all assigned to the outer ring, the least-visited sites, the ones where a death could be explained as a convenient accident."
Kael took the paper. His thumb pressed against the edge, feeling the slight indentation where the writer had borne down too hard. The names meant nothing to him, but the pattern didn't need names to speak clearly. "Why bring this to me?"
"Because you're not part of any Order. Because the Shadow Court is watching everyone who is." Sera's expression softened fractionally. The hard line of her mouth loosened, and something almost human flickered behind the silver of her irises. "And because I know what you are, Kael Ashborn. I know what you can do. Those forty-seven Awakened died with their essence intact. No natural death leaves a body that empty. Whatever killed them wasn't human. And whatever killed them is still out there."
The market sounds outside seemed to fade, replaced by a silence that pressed against the pavilion walls. The lantern flame stilled. Even the steam-pipes in the distance seemed to quiet, as though the city itself was holding its breath.
"I have one more thing." Sera reached into her coat and produced a small glass cylinder, no larger than her thumb. Inside, suspended in preservation fluid, floated a fragment of something that looked like black glass but moved like smoke. The fluid was tinged with amber, and the fragment shifted within it with a slow, writhing motion that made the liquid around it churn. "This was found in the lung cavity of the last victim. The healers couldn't identify it. The researchers at the Mortician's Guild called it a 'spiritual anomaly.' But I know what it is."
She held the cylinder up to the lantern. The black fragment twisted, and for an instant Kael saw something like a face in its depths—a mask, a void, a hunger given form. His pulse jumped. His skin crawled. The instinct to recoil warred with the instinct to look closer, and both instincts came from the same place—the part of him that knew what it meant to take from the dead, that recognized a kindred hunger when it stared back at him.
"Entity residue. Touched by something from beyond the Veil." Sera set the cylinder down between them. The glass clicked against the wooden floorboard beneath the cushion. "Someone in Thornwick is working with an Abyssal Entity. Not the usual cultists who sacrifice goats and pray for power. Someone who has actual contact, actual communication. Someone who knows what they're doing."
Kael stared at the fragment. The thing inside seemed to sense his attention, pressing against the glass from within. The preservation fluid around it darkened, amber bleeding to rust.
"The seal network," he said slowly. The words formed in his mouth before his mind caught up, connection firing ahead of conscious thought. "If the Awakened responsible for maintaining the outer seals are dead—"
"Then the seals weaken." Sera finished the thought. "Weakened seals mean more leak-through. More leak-through means more incidents, more 'accidents,' more excuses to deploy Awakened to the outer ring." Her voice dropped. The words came quieter now, stripped of their earlier professional crispness. "It's a harvesting operation, but not for soul-essence. They're harvesting the people who could stop them."
Outside, a whistle shrieked—the city watch changing shift. The sound cut through the muffled market noise, sharp and close.
"You need to go," Sera said, standing. Her coat fell around her in dark folds. "I can't be seen with you. If the Shadow Court realizes I've been—"
The lantern went out.
Kael moved without thought, his body hitting the canvas wall three feet to the left while his hand found Sera's collar and pulled her down. The fabric tore. Cold air rushed in. A blade punched through the space where they'd been sitting, silver steel catching the faint light from the market beyond. The steel sang as it cut air, and the cushion they'd occupied split open, spilling stuffing across the floor.
"Wrong answer." The voice came from the dark, accompanied by the click of another blade being drawn. Metal on leather, smooth and practiced. "The Soul Reaver stays. The informant leaves. Those are the terms."
Sera's hand found his arm, her grip urgent. Her fingers dug into his forearm, and he could feel the faint tremor in her grip—not fear, but adrenaline. "Back exit. Canvas unsealed at the rear corner."
"I'll hold them."
"You'll die. There are at least four of them."
Kael's fingers closed around the cylinder with the Entity fragment. The glass was cold against his palm, colder than it should have been, and the thing inside pulsed once as his skin made contact. "Then I won't die. Go."
She hesitated for one breath. Then moved—fast and silent, the way of someone who'd learned to survive in Thornwick's worst corners. The canvas rustled, and then she was gone, swallowed by the dark.
Kael turned to face the darkness.
Four figures materialized from the shadows, wearing the mottled gray of professional killers. Their movements carried the economical grace of Awakened training—no wasted motion, no telegraphed intent. The blades in their hands hummed with faint spiritual resonance, a low vibration that Kael felt in his teeth. Stage-two weapons, probably purchased from the Veiled Market or stripped from some fallen Order member. The resonance signature tasted like burnt tin.
"Soul Reaver Ashborn." The speaker stood in the center, larger than the others, his face hidden behind a cloth mask. The mask was plain, unadorned, the kind that said rank didn't need decoration. "The Whisper King sends his regards. He's been watching your progress with great interest. The seal network, the Shadow Court's machinations, the Mortician's Guild's internal politics—all of it traces back to the same source. You understand the game now, don't you?"
"I understand enough." Kael's hand dropped to his belt, finding the knife he kept for close work. The handle was worn smooth, fitted to his grip. "You're Shadow Court. Operating outside your legal authority."
"We're operating under authority you can't see." The leader stepped forward, his blade carving a slow arc in the air. The steel caught the faint light from the market beyond, and the hum of its resonance intensified. "The Whisper King has plans that require a Reaver. Not a dead Reaver—a living one. Cooperative, preferably. But the choice isn't yours."
Kael counted the angles. Four against one in a confined space, no exits except the one behind him. Standard fighting odds for a Sovereign, but the realm meant nothing if they had numbers and coordination. The canvas walls limited movement. The low ceiling forced crouching. Tight quarters, tight angles.
The leader lunged.
Kael moved.
His body flowed through the gap between two attackers, knife finding the soft tissue beneath the third's ribs before the leader's blade completed its arc. The steel sank in, and he felt the resistance of muscle parting, the wet heat of blood flooding over his wrist. Hot blood splashed across his forearm as he twisted, using the dying man's momentum to pull the fourth attacker off balance. The body jerked once, a spasm running through it as the spiritual essence released. The man's blade clattered against the floor.
One down. The leader's eyes widened behind his mask. The cloth stretched across his cheekbones.
"Impressive." The masked man circled, blade weaving a defensive pattern. The steel traced figure-eights in the dark. "They said you were skilled, but this—" He gestured at his fallen comrade. The body was still twitching, essence dissipating into the air. "—this is beyond what the reports suggested."
"Reports lie." Kael wiped the knife on his sleeve, watching the remaining two assassins take positions at the tent's corners. Their boots scuffed against the floor, finding grip. "Your employers sent you to capture, not kill. That means you're holding back."
"Holding back enough to take you alive." The leader smiled beneath his mask. The fabric crinkled at the corners of his mouth. "But not enough to let you escape."
The two corner men attacked simultaneously, blades crossing in a scissor pattern designed to trap and pin. Kael dropped beneath the strike, rolled across the bloody canvas, and came up with the fallen assassin's weapon in his left hand. The second knife felt different—lighter, balanced for thrusting than slashing. He adjusted his grip.
Double steel sang against double steel. Sparks flew when the edges met, brief flashes that illuminated the cramped space in strobing bursts. Kael gave ground, his boots sliding on the blood-slicked floor, then stopped giving ground, his weapons flickering in patterns that hadn't come from any training manual. Something in his blood remembered how to kill—muscle memory from a hundred battles he hadn't fought, skills harvested from corpses scattered across a dozen different conflicts. His body knew the angles before his mind calculated them. Strike. Parry. Redirect. Thrust. The knife in his left hand punched upward through a jaw, severing the tongue, dropping the second attacker. The right-hand blade followed a horizontal arc that opened the third's throat.
The corner men died within seconds of each other, their bodies falling with wet thuds that echoed in the sudden silence. Blood pooled beneath them, dark and spreading.
The leader stood alone, his blade lowered.
"Enough." He stepped backward toward the canvas wall. His boot found the edge of the torn cushion, and he sidestepped it without looking down. "You've made your point. The Whisper King will be informed that negotiations require a different approach."
"Negotiations?" Kael advanced, his weapons still raised. The blood on the blades was cooling, darkening. "You attacked me."
"A test. Nothing more." The leader's voice carried a note of something that might have been respect. His shoulders had relaxed, the tension bleeding out of his stance. "You're exactly what we need. Someone who can navigate the seal network, who can harvest what the cultists leave behind, who isn't bound by Order loyalties or Concordat restrictions." His hand found the canvas behind him. "The Whisper King wants to offer you a position. Not as a pawn—as a player."
"And if I refuse?"
"Then the offer stays open." The leader slipped through the canvas. His voice carried back, muffled by the fabric. "But know this, Soul Reaver: the conspiracy you're starting to see has roots you can't imagine. It's been growing for two hundred years. Every death in Thornwick, every weakening seal, every cultist raid—all of it serves a purpose. The Whisper King can explain everything. When you're ready to listen, find the Old Cathedral at midnight. Three nights from now."
His footsteps faded into the market noise.
Kael stood in the ruined pavilion, surrounded by corpses, and felt the weight of everything he'd learned settle onto his shoulders. His hands trembled—not from fear, but from the harvest instinct clawing at him. The essence in the dead men called to him, a pull that started in his chest and spread outward, demanding release. The Shadow Court, the Whisper King, a conspiracy spanning two centuries—every thread connected to the seal network, to the rifts, to the Entities imprisoned beyond the Veil.
His eyes fell on the fallen assassin's body. Fresh. Still warm. The blood hadn't coagulated yet. The spiritual essence clung to the flesh like morning dew, dissipating slowly, irretrievably, into the cold air.
He knelt.
Kael pressed his palm to the cooling chest, feeling the spiritual residue cling to his fingers. The sensation was immediate—a pull, a draw, a hunger that started in his chest and radiated outward through his arm. The dead man's essence rose to meet him, eager to be consumed. His ability stirred—a hunger he understood now, a gift that came with a cost measured in more than soul-energy. The memories came with it, fragments of a life ending in darkness, flooding his consciousness with images that weren't his.
The harvest began.

The notifications faded, leaving Kael kneeling in the dim light, the assassin's final memories settling into his mind like sediment in still water. The dead man's name surfaced—Eldric, no surname, recruited from the Drowning Districts two years ago. His life had been violent and short. His death, quicker. The memories tasted like ash and desperation, the particular flavor of a man who'd known he was disposable and had served anyway.
The Shadow Court operated in cells—never more than five members per unit, each cell knowing only its direct superior and one adjacent cell. The Whisper King commanded from somewhere in the upper hierarchy, his identity protected behind layers of compartmentalization. Eldric had never seen his face. No one in his cell had. But the assassin's fragment contained a piece of communication, a partial message that had passed through his cell en route to higher authority.
"...the secondary seals will fail within the quarter-moon cycle. Confirm Prime Zarek's readiness for phase two. The Reaver must be acquired before the Window opens, or the entire operation—"
The message ended there. The memory fragmented, dissolving into the chaos of the man's final moments. Kael's fingers tightened on the assassin's collar, knuckles white.
Prime Zarek. The name struck like a blade between his ribs. The name he'd been hunting since his execution, the man who'd framed him for crimes he hadn't committed. His heart hammered. His vision narrowed, the edges going dark. Here, in a dead cultist's final thought, the thread connecting everything became visible.
Zarek wasn't just a cultist. He was part of the conspiracy's leadership. And the conspiracy—the real one, the two-hundred-year-old shadow that moved beneath the Shadow Court's operations and the cultists' raids—was about to trigger something called "phase two."
Kael stood, sheathing both knives. The leather whispered against the steel. The blood on his hands was cooling now, sticky against his skin. Beyond the ruined pavilion, the Ashfen Market continued its midnight commerce, oblivious to how close the world had come to tipping. A vendor laughed. Dice clattered. Steam hissed from a vent somewhere to his left.
Three nights from now. The Old Cathedral. The Whisper King's offer.
He could walk into that meeting and learn everything—every secret, every connection, every hidden truth about the conspiracy that had shaped his life since the moment of his execution. Or he could refuse, and hunt Zarek on his own terms, and trust that his harvest-enhanced instincts would guide him to the truth faster than any shadowy bargain.
The choice gnawed at him. The Whisper King wanted him as a player, but players could be sacrificed when the game demanded it. And Kael had learned long ago that the only advantage worth keeping was the one no one else could take away.
He needed more power. More knowledge. More harvests.
The Old Cathedral waited in three nights. But tonight—
Tonight, he had forty-seven dead Awakened whose seals had been left untended. Forty-seven gaps in the defense network, each one a potential entry point for whatever the conspiracy planned to unleash. If he moved fast enough, if he could reach the outer ring before the seal degradation became catastrophic, he might learn something the Shadow Court didn't know he possessed.
Might learn something about the original Soul Reaver, whose tablets had led him this far.
Kael stepped out of the pavilion and into the steam-thick streets of Thornwick's Underbelly. The air hit him—cold, damp, heavy with coal smoke and the faint sweetness of laudanum drifting from an upper window. His boots splashed through a puddle, the water tinged with something oily that caught the gaslight in iridescent swirls. Above him, the sky glowed with the perpetual smog of the city's industrial heart, and somewhere in that haze, the stars remained invisible—as unreachable as the truth he was beginning to grasp.
The conspiracy had roots. It had a purpose. And in three nights, it would make him an offer designed to be impossible to refuse.
But Kael Ashborn had refused death itself. He could refuse whatever game the Whisper King had prepared.
The question was whether his refusal would come before or after he learned what the conspiracy was building.
And whether he could survive the learning.
Footsteps echoed behind him—too many, too coordinated. Boots on cobblestone, moving in lockstep. The Shadow Court hadn't sent just four assassins. They'd sent a demonstration, a sample of what they'd bring if negotiations failed. The sound bounced off the narrow walls, amplifying, making it impossible to tell if there were six or sixteen.
Kael drew his knives and disappeared into the steam, his form dissolving into the rolling white clouds that poured from the street vents. The gaslight caught the mist at strange angles, casting long shadows that moved like living things. He moved fast, boots silent on the wet stone, his breath controlled and shallow.
Behind him, unseen, something in the black-glass fragment pulsed once—and began to glow.
