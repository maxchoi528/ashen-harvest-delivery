The first shell screamed overhead and Kael counted three heartbeats before impact. The explosion tore through Iron Vanguard lines sixty meters ahead, showering mud and shrapnel in a plume of black smoke.
"Soul Anchor, form up!" Commander Voss roared somewhere to the left, his voice hoarse from hours of shouted orders.
Kael dropped flat as another shell screamed past. The ground trembled. He pressed his palms into cold, wet earth, feeling the vibration through his bones. Three Ascendant warriors from the Vanguard stumbled past his position, their spiritual signatures flaring gold and silver as they reinforced the failing barricade. One of them—a woman with a shattered left arm dangling at her side—locked eyes with him for half a second before she kept moving.
Nobody asked why a Sovereign-ranked prisoner stood in the Order's rear guard. They'd needed every warm body. Voss had looked at the chains on Kael's wrists, looked at the cultist corpses piled behind their lines, and made a calculation.
"Reaver." The Commander's voice cut through the chaos. "Those bodies. Can you do anything with them?"
The question hung in the air between artillery impacts. Kael wiped grime from his face with the back of his hand.
"That what you want me for? Grave-robbing?"
"I want you earning your keep." Voss's jaw tightened. "My soldiers are dying because their spiritual reserves are depleting faster than our supply lines can deliver essence vials. Those cultists you left behind—if there's anything useful in them—"
"There is." Kael pushed himself to his feet. "But harvesting in front of your people? You understand what they'll see."
The Commander's expression didn't change. "I understand we're losing this battle. Move."
Kael climbed out of the shell crater, boots squelching in the churned mud. The smell hit him first—cordite, burning flesh, the copper tang of blood mixing with sulfur from corrupted earth. Ahead, the barricade buckled. A creature that might once have been human lurched through the smoke, its skin the color of curdled milk, eyes replaced by pits of swirling darkness.
An Entity-touched. Not fully transformed, but close.
Kael ran.
His chains rattled against his legs as he closed the distance. The creature's mouth opened too wide, revealing rows of needle teeth, and it shrieked a sound that vibrated in his molars. He dodged left as it swung clawed fingers at his head. The motion carried it off-balance, and Kael drove his palm into its chest.
Skin contact. The Reaver pulse activated.
[Soul Harvest: Entity-touched corpse detected. Extraction available.]
The creature convulsed, then collapsed. Kael kept moving, not looking back as he sprinted toward the barricade where the Vanguard warriors were holding a thin line against a tide of corrupted bodies. Three cultists lay crumpled near a toppled supply wagon. Fresh kills—blood still warm, spiritual residue potent.
He dropped to his knees beside the first corpse. A man in dark robes, face frozen in a rictus of pain, ritual scars covering his throat. Kael pressed both hands against the cooling flesh.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Memory Fragment from the remains. A fragment of the cultist's final ritual experience has been preserved.]
Information flooded his mind—fragmented, disorienting. A cavern. Torches made of blue flame. Voices chanting in a language that felt like broken glass scraping against his thoughts. The sensation of something vast pressing against reality, testing for weaknesses.
The seals. They were testing the seals.
Kael pulled his hands back and moved to the second corpse. Female, middle-aged, bearing the marks of someone who had Awakened recently and poorly—spiritual channels scarred and inflamed. Her death had been violent, a spear of condensed essence having punched through her sternum.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 3 units of degraded soul-essence from the remains. Quality: Poor. Cause: Delayed harvest (12+ hours post-mortem). Awakened potential: Negligible.]
Twelve hours. These cultists had been dead since before dawn, positioned as a forward strike team that the Vanguard had apparently intercepted. The organized nature of it sent cold fingers down Kael's spine. This hadn't been a random assault. Someone had planned this. Someone had known the Vanguard would be here.
A third corpse lay a few meters away, positioned differently from the others—falling backward than forward, a wound in his back suggesting he'd been struck down while fleeing. This one wore officer's insignia, black metal shaped like a serpent eating its own tail.
Kael crouched beside him and placed his hands on the man's chest. The flesh was still warm.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Memory Fragment from the remains. A fragment of the cultist commander's strategic overview has been preserved.]
More information cascaded through him. The layout of the surrounding area—the real layout, not what the maps showed. Tunnel systems running beneath the Ashenmoor Wastes, connecting to a network of caves that the Orders had never mapped. The locations of four more cult strike teams, positioned to cut off retreat routes. And a phrase, repeating like a mantra through the dead man's thoughts: *The seal weakens. The Prime is pleased.*
Zarek. This had to be connected to Zarek.
"Soul Anchor!" Voss's voice cut through his concentration. "We've got a breach on the eastern flank!"
Kael opened his eyes. He'd been kneeling there too long, too deep in the harvest visions. Around him, Vanguard warriors were scrambling to reinforce a section of barricade that had collapsed under a coordinated assault. The Entity-touched were coming through in greater numbers now, drawn by the scent of spiritual conflict like sharks to blood in water.
He grabbed the officer's body by the ankles and dragged it toward the supply wagon, using the wreckage for cover. His hands worked automatically, pressing against flesh, pulling essence from a body that had once held secrets worth dying for.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 15 units of soul-essence from the remains. Quality: Good. Source: Cult Commander (recently deceased). Spiritual residue: Moderate. Estimated value: 1,500 gold sovereigns in the Veiled Market.]
The essence flowed into his personal reserves, not that he needed it—his Soul Reaver abilities drew from the dead directly, leaving his own soul untouched. But having essence stockpiled meant he could trade it, bribe with it, buy information and silence. The world didn't know that Kael operated on a different set of rules than every other Awakened. They feared him for what he took from corpses, but they didn't understand that taking cost him nothing.
That ignorance would get them all killed eventually.
The eastern barricade collapsed with a crash of splintering wood and screaming metal. Kael watched three Vanguard warriors go down beneath a wave of corrupted bodies, their spiritual signatures flaring once, twice, then guttering out like candles in a storm. He could feel them die—their souls departing, their essence dissipating into the ambient spiritual energy of the battlefield. In minutes, there would be nothing left to harvest.
He ran toward the breach.
"Soul Anchor, fall back!" someone shouted—a young soldier, face pale beneath the grime,.
Kael ignored him. The first creature through the gap was a massive thing, eight feet of muscle and corrupted flesh, arms ending in blades that gleamed with oily darkness. It moved faster than anything that size should, closing the distance to the nearest Vanguard defender in two bounds.
Kael's palm struck the creature's side.
[Soul Harvest: Entity-class remains detected. Extraction requires physical contact maintained for a few heartbeats. WARNING: Target is still animate.]
The creature screamed and spun, one blade-arm carving toward Kael's head. He ducked, keeping his hand pressed against its flank, feeling the spiritual energy of the thing's corrupted soul trying to tear itself free from the contact. Pain lanced up his arm—not spiritual cost, not from his own reserves, but the simple physical sensation of touching something that didn't want to be touched.
Nine seconds. The creature clawed at him, connected with his shoulder, shredding fabric and drawing a line of fire across his skin.
Twelve seconds.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Corrupted Strength from the remains. Entity-class ability acquired. WARNING: Corruption risk detected. Use with caution.]
The creature collapsed, its animating force absent, and Kael rolled away from the twitching corpse with blood dripping from his shoulder wound. The Corrupted Strength sat in his mind like a splinter—useful, dangerous, addictive in ways he didn't fully understand. The Orders called Entity-touched abilities the path to damnation. They'd executed Soul Reavers for less.
But the barricade held where he'd stopped the breach, and the Vanguard warriors who witnessed it were staring at him with expressions caught between gratitude and terror.
"Commander Voss." Kael's voice came out steadier than he felt. "We need to talk about those tunnels."
Voss appeared at the edge of the breach a moment later, his uniform torn and his left arm hanging at an angle that suggested a dislocated shoulder. The man hadn't stopped fighting—he'd paused long enough to reposition. His eyes swept over the fallen Entity-touched, cataloging what Kael had done, then returned to Kael's face.
"What tunnels?"
"The cultists came through tunnels. Underneath the Wastes. There's a network—" Kael pressed his hands against his knees, fighting to keep his breathing even. The shoulder wound wasn't serious, but the spiritual feedback from the harvest had left his nerves singing with dissonant frequencies. "—that runs beneath our position. Four more strike teams waiting to cut off retreat."
Voss's expression didn't change, but something shifted behind his eyes. "How do you know this?"
"Harvested it. From their commander." Kael straightened, ignoring the way the world tilted slightly. "We can hold this position, but if those tunnels aren't collapsed or occupied by nightfall, we're going to find ourselves surrounded."
"Collapsing the tunnels would require engineering teams we don't have." Voss's jaw worked. "Occupying them requires soldiers we can't spare."
"Then find another option, Commander. Those are your people dying out there." Kael gestured toward the continuing conflict, where fresh waves of corrupted were pushing against Vanguard lines that had thinned dangerously in the last hour. "I don't work for free, but I also don't particularly want to die in this mud pit. So here's what I can do—I can scout those tunnels. Find what's waiting in them. Give you intelligence that might let you position forces to counter whatever's coming."
"And why would you do that?"
"Because right now, the people trying to kill me are also trying to kill you." Kael shrugged, wincing as the motion pulled at his shoulder. "Hard to harvest anything useful from a battlefield if the battle's lost and I'm buried under a pile of rubble."
Voss studied him for a long moment. Around them, the sounds of combat continued—the clash of spiritual energy against corrupted flesh, the screams of wounded soldiers, the constant percussion of artillery shells screaming overhead. The air smelled like smoke and copper and the sulfurous taint of Entity corruption.
"The tunnels enter three hundred meters north of the main supply depot," the Commander said finally. "There's a collapsed section near the entrance that should provide cover for infiltration. You'll find mapping posts marked in blue chalk every fifty meters—the engineers from the Third Company surveyed what they could before the assault began."
"The Third Company?"
"Wiped out. The cultist assault hit their position first." Voss's voice was flat. "Their survivors made it to our lines before the corruption took them. Most of them died within hours."
Kael absorbed this. The organized nature of the assault, the positioning of strike teams, the tunnels running beneath Order positions—it was too coordinated for random cultist activity. Someone had spent months planning this. Maybe years.
"The Six Orders," he said. "They're fighting the Abyss, aren't they? The 72 rifts, the seal network—it's what keeps the Entities from pouring through into the Material."
Voss's expression flickered—surprise, quickly suppressed. "You didn't know?"
"I knew there were seals. I didn't know there were six Orders maintaining them." Kael rolled his shoulder experimentally, testing the range of motion. The wound burned, but it would hold. "I'm a Reaper. Nobody teaches us world history."
"The Orders have kept that information classified for centuries. Public knowledge about the Abyss, the Entities, the true nature of the seal network—it causes panic. Encourages cult recruitment." Voss glanced toward the battle, then back. "The Concordat exists to prevent inter-Order conflict from weakening those seals. Every major conflict between Orders for the last three hundred years has been about maintaining that balance."
"And the cults? The ones throwing corrupted at your lines right now?"
"The cults want the seals broken. They believe the Entities will reward them for releasing what they call the 'true inheritors of the Abyss.'" Voss's lip curled. "Fanatics. Martyrs. People who think death is a doorway than an ending."
Death is a doorway than an ending. Kael filed that phrase away, knowing it was only half true. For normal people, death was an ending—the soul's energy recycled, personality and memory dissolved back into the spiritual ether. The Orders knew this truth. They'd built their entire hierarchy on managing humanity's relationship with mortality, with the lie of an afterlife serving as social control.
Soul Reavers like him could pull fragments back. Could preserve moments of a dying person's existence. But resurrection was impossible. The dead stayed dead.
"The Shadow Court," he said. "They're one of those Orders? Operating through deniable assets?"
Voss's expression closed off. "The Shadow Court's affiliation is classified. If you're asking whether they monitor Soul Reavers, the answer is yes. They monitor everything."
Of course they did. Kael had known that the moment he'd woken up after his execution with a power that shouldn't exist. Someone had arranged his survival. Someone had placed him in situations where he'd learn to use his abilities. Someone was watching.
He hadn't figured out who, or why.
"Commander." A young soldier ran up, gasping for breath. "New arrivals on the southern ridge. Spiritual signatures consistent with Order identification markers. But they're not responding to our challenge signals."
"Which Order?"
"Unable to confirm. Their spiritual signatures are... Strange. Like they're masking their true resonance."
Voss cursed under his breath. "Could be reinforcements from Iron Vanguard headquarters. Could be another faction trying to establish a presence during the chaos."
"Could be the Shadow Court," Kael said.
The Commander's silence was answer enough.
"Go scout those tunnels," Voss said. "Find what's waiting for us. I'll handle the arrivals on the ridge." He paused, something shifting in his expression. "Soul Anchor. What you did here—stopping that breach—it bought my people time. Time they needed. I don't care what you are, or what the Order of Dawn thinks they know about you. On this field, today, you fought for my soldiers."
"I fought for myself."
"Sometimes those are the same thing." Voss turned to leave, then stopped. "The Mortician's Guild has a field station six kilometers west of here. If you survive the tunnels, you might want to visit it. They have supplies, medical facilities, a direct communication line to Thornwick. And their head medic—her name is Sera. She's been asking questions about you."
Sera. The woman connected to the Mortician's Guild, the one whose sister was supposedly held in a facility somewhere. The plot thread that had been dangling since earlier chapters.
Kael filed the information away. "I'll keep that in mind."
Voss nodded once, then moved off toward the southern ridge, shouting orders to the warriors near him. The battle continued to rage around the barricades, but the immediate crisis had passed—the breach held, the line reinforced, the Vanguard's position marginally more secure.
The tunnels waited.
Kael moved north, keeping to the shell craters and collapsed earth that offered cover from the fighting. The terrain changed as he traveled—the churned mud of the front giving way to cracked stone and vegetation killed by years of spiritual contamination. The Ashenmoor Wastes earned their name. Nothing grew here. Nothing lived here except the things that crawled out of rifts that should have stayed sealed.
He found the tunnel entrance where Voss had described—a collapsed section of rock face revealing a passage that plunged into darkness. Blue chalk marks glowed faintly on the stone, barely visible in the dim light. Kael pulled a chemical light from his pack and snapped it, bathing the area in sickly green illumination.
The tunnel smelled of earth and moisture and something else beneath that—something organic and faintly sweet, like flowers left too long in stagnant water. His skin prickled as he descended. The spiritual pressure here was different than on the surface. Heavier. More focused. The seal network might be holding above ground, but down here, in the deep places, the Abyss pressed closer.
He moved carefully, one hand trailing along the wall, the other holding the chemical light ahead of him. The chalk marks guided his path through a series of branching passages—left at the first fork, straight through the second, right at the third. The engineering teams had been thorough, even as they'd died for their efforts.
Voices echoed from somewhere ahead. Cultist voices, speaking in that same glass-scraping language he'd heard in the harvest visions. Kael killed his light and pressed himself against the tunnel wall, letting his eyes adjust to the darkness. The Sovereign stage gave him advantages beyond raw power—heightened senses, faster reaction time, an intuitive grasp of spiritual flows that made navigating corrupted territory significantly less suicidal.
He crept forward.
The passage opened into a larger chamber, naturally formed but modified by cultist activity. Torches burned in sconces along the walls, their flames that same blue he'd seen in his vision. Thirty or forty cultists moved through the space, most in dark robes marked with that serpent-eating-its-tail insignia he'd seen on the commander's corpse. They were carrying equipment—crates of supplies, coils of what looked like blessed chain, stacks of metal plates etched with symbols that made Kael's eyes water.
And in the center of the chamber, chained to a stone altar that pulsed with sickly light, was a figure.
Human. Female. Barely conscious, her head lolling against the restraints that held her in place. She wore the remnants of an Order uniform—blue and silver, the colors of the Order of Dawn. One of the Order's own people, captured and brought here for what purpose?
Kael's breath caught. Not because of her—strangers died every day in this war—but because of what hovered above the altar. A rift, maybe three feet across, edges flickering with unstable energy. Through it, he could see something vast and dark, a presence that seemed to fill the gap between dimensions with malevolent awareness.
A sealed rift. Partially unsealed.
The cultists weren't just testing the seals. They were breaking them.
One of the cultists noticed the woman stirring and walked over, running a hand along her face with something like tenderness. "Soon," he murmured. "The Prime requires your essence to complete the ritual. Your Order's power, channeled through a vessel of their own faith. It will be beautiful."
The woman spat at him. Blood—her lips were split, her face bruised and swollen. The cultist laughed and walked away.
Kael watched, counting figures, cataloging positions, measuring the distance between himself and the altar. Thirty-seven visible cultists. Unknown number in the passages beyond. One partially unsealed rift. One captive who might be bait, might be a genuine prisoner, might be something else entirely.
His shoulder throbbed. His reserves held essence worth a fortune, abilities harvested from the dead sitting unused in his mind. And somewhere in Thornwick, Zarek was pulling strings that reached into this underground chamber, orchestrating attacks across multiple cities, preparing for something that went beyond simple cult activity.
The Six Orders fighting the Abyss. The 72 sealed rifts. The Shadow Court monitoring everything. The original Soul Reaver's fate—still a mystery, but hints creeping closer with every harvest.
Kael exhaled slowly, calculating his options.
A hand touched his shoulder.
He spun, Reaver instincts flaring, and found himself face-to-face with a woman in black leather armor, her features shadowed by a hood. Her spiritual signature was muted, almost completely masked, but her eyes gleamed with recognition.
"Soul Reaver." Her voice was low, barely above a whisper. "We've been waiting for you."
Behind her, two more figures emerged from the darkness— black armor, masked presence. Shadow Court operatives, come to observe or intervene.
The woman smiled. It wasn't a friendly expression.
"You found the rift chamber. Congratulations. Now you need to decide what you're going to do about it."
The rift pulsed behind her, that vast dark presence pressing against the weakening boundary between worlds. The cultists continued their preparations, oblivious to the confrontation happening in the shadows at the edge of their sacred space.
Kael's hand hovered near his weapon, his mind racing through possibilities. The Shadow Court wanted something from him—he'd known that since before his execution. They wanted to control him, or study him, or use him for purposes he hadn't yet uncovered.
But right now, in this moment, the greater threat wasn't the shadowy organization watching from the darkness.
The greater threat was the rift, the cultists, and the woman chained to an altar who was about to become the catalyst for something that couldn't be undone.
The woman's smile widened. "Tick tock, Reaver. The cultists will complete their ritual in approximately eighteen minutes. The rift will be fully open in twenty-five. And my superiors are very interested to see which choice you make."
She stepped back, melting into the shadows, her companions following.
Kael stood alone at the edge of the light.
Eighteen minutes.
Twenty-five until the world changed.
His hand trembled against the hilt of his blade—not from fear, but from the weight of the decision pressing down on him. Every choice had consequences. Every harvest left scars. And the people watching from the darkness wanted him to act, wanted to see what kind of Reaver he truly was.
The woman on the altar opened her eyes. Through the pain and exhaustion, her gaze found him—recognized him, despite the distance and the darkness.
"Please," she breathed. The word barely carried across the chamber. "Help me."
The cultists kept working. The rift kept pulsing. And somewhere in the depths of the Ashenmoor Wastes, shells continued to fall and soldiers continued to die and the war ground on toward an ending that no one could predict.
Kael drew his blade.
He moved.
