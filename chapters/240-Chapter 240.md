Not a death spasm. Something deliberate. The man's fingers curled around Kael's wrist with surprising strength for a dead thing, and his mouth opened, throat working around words that shouldn't exist without breath.
"Reaver." The voice scraped out like iron on stone. "They know you're here."

Just a corpse, slack and empty, wearing the tattered remains of what had once been a Thornwick city guard uniform.
[System Notification] Soul Harvest available. Target: Unidentified Guard. Corpse age: a few hours. Harvest quality: High.
Two hours. Fresh enough to yield something worthwhile. Kael crouched beside the body, rain plastering his hair against his skull, and studied the man's face. No visible wounds. No signs of struggle. He'd died standing, based on how he'd fallen. Spiritual attack, then. Something that stopped the heart without leaving marks.
Or someone who wanted it to look that way.
"You're playing games with me." Kael's voice cut through the drumming rain. He wasn't speaking to the corpse. The alley offered no answers—just garbage heaps, waterlogged crates, and the distant clang of factory bells marking the third hour past midnight. "Show yourself."
Silence answered. The rain intensified, drumming against the warehouse rooftops overhead, turning the narrow passage into a river of filth. Kael remained motionless, counting heartbeats, waiting for the presence he'd sensed since entering this district.
There. A flicker at the edge of his spiritual perception. Someone standing thirty feet back, behind the collapsed shed that served as cover for the alley's only exit. They weren't moving. Weren't attacking. Just... Watching.
City watch wouldn't wait this long. Bandits would have hit him already. Which meant the observer belonged to one of three groups: Shadow Court operatives tracking his movements, cult members sniffing out competition, or someone else entirely.
Kael made his choice. He pressed both palms flat against the guard's chest, feeling the damp fabric, the cold flesh beneath, and reached inward with the Soul Reaver's hunger.
The harvest took hold.
[System Notification] Soul Harvest initiated...
The guard's memories crashed through Kael's mind like shattered glass—a dozen images overlapping, fragmentary, overwhelming. Darkness. A voice speaking words that tasted like ash and honey. The sensation of something vast and cold pressing against his mind, searching for secrets he didn't know he possessed. And finally, a single clear image: a face.
Not a face. A mask. White porcelain, featureless except for the painted smile that stretched too wide across its surface.
"Find the Reaver," the mask had said. "Bring him to me. Alive, if possible."
The vision shattered. Kael gasped, his own breath abruptly precious, his body drenched in sweat despite the cold rain. The harvest wasn't finished. His fingers still rested on the corpse's chest, and the ability's pull hadn't yet released him.
More came. Skills, embedded in muscle memory and spiritual resonance. The guard had been Awakened—stage one, barely beyond threshold, but Awakened nonetheless. [System Notification] Soul Harvest complete. You extracted: Urban Pursuit (Basic). Memory Fragment acquired. Warning: Incomplete soul data. Information may be unreliable.
The memory fragment. Kael held it up for examination, turning it over in his mind's eye. It felt like a splinter—foreign, uncomfortable, but impossible to ignore. The mask's painted smile lingered behind his eyes. Whoever wore that mask, they'd sent this guard to die just to deliver a message.
Find the Reaver. Bring him to me.
"Well." Kael stood, wiping rain and something else from his hands. "You found me."

City watch ran regular patrols in this district, and a dead body—particularly one killed by spiritual means—would draw investigators from the Orders within the hour.

Three hours later, Kael stood in the basement of an abandoned textile mill, studying the first safe house marked on his map. The location had been wrong when he'd arrived—the building had collapsed decades ago, leaving only rubble and rats. But the map had included a secondary notation: look beneath.
Beneath led to tunnels. Ancient ones, predating Thornwick's founding, carved from stone that shouldn't exist this far from the city's original coastline. Someone had been using them recently—fresh footprints in the accumulated dust, the faint smell of lamp oil, scorch marks on walls that suggested recent combat.
Kael followed the tunnel deeper, one hand trailing along the damp stone, the other resting on his knife. The hidden layer pulsed at the edge of his perception. Something had died here. Something powerful enough to leave spiritual residue that hadn't fully dissipated.
The tunnel opened into a chamber. Circular, thirty feet across, with drainage channels carved into the floor and iron rings set into the walls at regular intervals. Chains hung from several of those rings—some empty, some still attached to skeletal remains that had been picked clean by rats and time.
A torture room. Or a prison. Maybe both.
Kael stepped inside, his boots echoing against the stone. The air tasted of copper and old fear, and the hidden layer twisted around him like smoke, making the walls seem to breathe.
"Impressive, isn't it?" The voice came from everywhere and nowhere. Kael spun, knife drawn, but the chamber remained empty. "The architects who built this place understood something modern builders have forgotten. Space shapes behavior. Confined spaces make people confess. Circular rooms eliminate corners where enemies might hide."
"Show yourself." Kael's voice sounded flat even to his ears. "Or I'll start harvesting these corpses and see if any of them remember your face."
Silence. Then laughter—not mocking, but genuinely amused.
"You would, wouldn't you? That's what makes you interesting, Kael Ashborn. The Reaver who truly uses his abilities." The voice shifted, seeming to coalesce near the chamber's center. "Very well. You've passed the first test."
A figure materialized from the shadows—or maybe it had been there all along, and Kael's senses just hadn't been sharp enough to detect it. Tall, gaunt, wrapped in robes that seemed to drink the light fairly than reflect it. A mask covered the face—white porcelain, painted smile stretching too wide.
The mask from the guard's memories.
"You're the one who sent the guard." Kael's knife remained steady. "The one who wanted me found."

The cult you're investigating, the conspiracy you've stumbled into, the ritual at Rift Alpha-7—these are only symptoms. Surface manifestations of something far older."
"And what would that be?"

Kael's grip tightened on his knife. The hidden layer writhed around them both now, responding to the tension crackling through the air. Something was building—a confrontation that would require more than words to resolve.

Kael emerged from the tunnel network two hours before dawn, surfacing in a drainage culvert that emptied into Thornwick's harbor district. The rain had stopped, leaving the air thick with moisture and the stench of fish processing and industrial runoff. Somewhere across the water, factory horns signaled the start of the morning shift.
He found a crate to sit on, back against a warehouse wall, and allowed himself to breathe. The masked figure's words echoed through his thoughts, each sentence raising more questions than it answered. The Whisper King. A title held by many throughout history. Someone who believed only absolute authority could save humanity from whatever was coming.
The guard's memory fragment pulsed at the back of his mind. The painted smile. The voice like ash and honey. The sense of something vast and cold pressing against the man's thoughts.
Find the Reaver. Bring him to me.
Someone wanted him found. Someone who commanded enough resources to manipulate city watch, to place operatives in Thornwick's Shadow Court networks, to know about Kael's activities before he knew them himself. The masked figure claimed to be warning him, but warnings and manipulation often wore identical faces.
A bell rang somewhere in the harbor district—three clear tones that echoed across the water. Kael's body tensed before his mind registered the sound. That bell meant one thing: vessel arriving.
He stood, abandoning his hiding spot, and made his way toward the docks. The harbor was already stirring with activity—dock workers preparing for the morning shipments, fishermen checking their nets, customs officials yawning through their paperwork. Kael moved through the crowd like smoke, drawing no attention, his unremarkable features and mud-colored clothing rendering him invisible.
The vessel in question was a cargo hauler, its hull scarred by decades of salt water and rough handling. But the crew wore more than simple work clothes. Kael counted three individuals carrying themselves with the coiled readiness of Awakened fighters, their hands never far from concealed weapons. They stood at the gangplank's base, checking cargo manifests against the hold's contents.
And they were checking everyone boarding.
Not city watch. Not customs. Someone else entirely.
Kael pulled back into an alcove between warehouses, his mind racing through options. The ship was the obvious extraction point for whoever had been operating in Thornwick. The safe houses, the tunnels, the masked figure—all of it suggested a network with significant resources and a clear agenda. Being seen near that vessel would connect him to whatever was happening.
But running meant losing access to information. And information was the one currency he couldn't harvest from corpses.
A commotion near the gangplank drew his attention. One of the Awakened crew members had grabbed a dock worker—a young woman carrying a basket of fish, her face pale with fear. The crewman was speaking, low and urgent, but Kael caught fragments: "Shadow Court" and "orders" and "the Reaver."
They were hunting him. Already. Before he could find them.
The woman broke free, dropping her basket, fish scattering across the gangplank. She ran, and the crewman let her go, turning back to his post with a disgusted shake of his head. Not a target. Just a civilian in the wrong place.
Kael watched the scene unfold, calculating distances and angles. The crewman who'd spoken stood twelve feet from the alcove, his back partially turned. Two others covered the gangplank. A fourth—he hadn't noticed the fourth before—stood at the ship's rail, watching the crowd with eyes that seemed to track movement like a predator's.
Stage two. Minimum. The hidden layer shimmered around that one, visible even to Kael's relatively limited perception.
He had seconds to decide. Run and lose his lead, or act and reveal his position.
The fourth crew member's gaze locked onto his alcove.
"Kael Ashborn." The voice carried across the harbor noise like a bell tolling. "We know you're there. The Whisper King requests your presence."
Kael's hand found his knife. The hidden layer writhed around him, responding to the confrontation building like a storm on the horizon. Every instinct screamed to run, to vanish, to let the mystery remain unsolved fairly than risk whatever waited at the end of this rope.
But mysteries had a way of unraveling whether you chased them or not.
He stepped out of the alcove, knife in hand, and faced the vessel's crew.
"Tell your Whisper King I'm listening."
The stage-two crew member smiled—a thin, cold expression that didn't reach his eyes.
"He prefers to speak for himself." He extended one hand toward the gangplank. "If you'll come with us."
Behind Kael, footsteps approached. Not running—walking, deliberate, multiplying as more figures emerged from the warehouse district. He was surrounded. Trapped between the harbor and the approaching unknown.
The painted smile flashed through his memory. Find the Reaver.
They'd found him.
And Kael still had no idea what they truly wanted him for.
