The blade pressed against the vein in his neck, cold enough to leave a mark.
"Don't. Move." The voice belonged to a woman, roughened by smoke and something worse. Kael's fingers curled against the alley stones, feeling the damp seep through his torn gloves. Three hours ago, he'd been sprinting through the undercity. Now he was pinned like a common thief against a wall that reeked of rendered fat and copper.
"Reaver." She spat the word like a curse. "I should cut your throat right now."
Kael stayed still. His Awakened senses traced her outline in the dark—mid-thirties, broad-shouldered, a scar bisecting her left eyebrow. The blade trembled slightly. Exhaustion or hesitation. He couldn't tell which mattered more.
"You won't." He kept his voice flat. "If you wanted me dead, I'd already be bleeding out. You've got questions instead of a grave."
The blade pressed harder. A bead of blood traced down his collar.
"Damn right I do." She kicked his legs apart, searched his pockets with brutal efficiency. His knife, three copper pence, a scrap of paper with an address he didn't recognize—everything went into a pouch at her belt. "The Mortician's Guild has been hunting Reavers for six months. Paying silver for dead ones, gold for live ones. You're worth a hundred gold sovereigns to me."
A hundred gold. Kael felt something cold settle in his chest. The Mortician's Guild wasn't supposed to know he was in Thornwick. Someone had talked.
"I'm not your enemy." He chose each word like stepping through a minefield. "The Guild isn't yours either. You know that."
The blade wavered. He heard her breath catch—once, sharp through the nose.
"Maybe." She stepped back, kept the knife raised. A streetlight guttered overhead, throwing her face into harsh relief. "You've got ten seconds to convince me you're worth more alive than dead. Talk fast."
Kael turned slowly, keeping his hands visible. The woman was younger than he'd thought at first—late twenties at most, with the kind of hollowed cheeks that came from skipping meals or worse. Her coat bore the faded insignia of the Iron Vanguard, though the emblem had been deliberately scratched out.
"You were a soldier." He nodded toward the ruined patch. "Order of Dawn's campaign in the northern marshes. Three years back. The Eighth Cohort."
Her expression didn't change, but the knife dipped an inch. "How do you know that?"
"I can read the formation scars. The way you stand—weight on your back foot, ready to pivot. Vanguard trains their soldiers to fight in tight ranks, shield walls. But you favor your left side. Shrapnel wound, maybe two years old, never healed right." He paused. "You're not with the Guild. You're not with any Order. You're freelance."
"Freelance." She laughed, a sound like breaking glass. "That's a nice word for it. Bounty hunter. Scavenger. Grave robber with a license."
"Same work, different branding." Kael met her eyes. "What's your name?"
"Why should I tell you?"
"Because you're still here. You could have killed me and taken the bounty. Instead you're listening." He spread his hands. "That means you're not just in it for the money. You've got problems of your own."
She stared at him for a long moment. The streetlight flickered again, and somewhere in the distance a steam-whistle shrieked—the seven o'clock shift change at the factories along the river.
"Vex." She lowered the knife, not sheathing it. "My name's Vex. And you're going to tell me exactly why the Mortician's Guild wants you so badly that they've got informants in places they shouldn't be."
Kael exhaled slowly. "That's a long story."
"We'll walk while you talk. There's a safehouse two streets over—abandoned print shop. We'll be found within six hours if we stay in the open." She grabbed his arm, her grip iron-strong despite her lean frame. "Move. And if you try anything, I will gut you and take the bounty anyway."
They moved through Thornwick's underbelly like ghosts, slipping through alleys that reeked of coal smoke and rotting cabbage. Kael counted turns automatically—standard evasion protocol. Vex seemed to know the route by instinct, ducking under low-hanging pipes and stepping over sleeping vagrants with practiced ease.
"The Guild started hunting Reavers eight months ago," Kael said finally, matching her pace. "They claim it's about the soul trade. Soul-essence harvested from corpses, sold on the black market. Reavers undercut their monopoly."
"That's the public story." Vex ducked through a gap in a wooden fence, pulling him after her. "What do *you* think is really going on?"
Kael considered lying. The truth would be dangerous. But something in her voice—a thread of genuine curiosity beneath the hardness—made him decide otherwise.
"The soul trade is just the excuse. The real reason is that the Orders are scared." He kept his voice low as they passed a group of factory workers heading home, their faces gray with exhaustion. "Reavers can harvest abilities directly from the dead. We don't need their rituals, their temples, their blessed essence vials. We don't need *them*."
Vex snorted. "That's not what the propaganda says. The Orders teach that Reavers are abominations. Soul parasites. That you steal power that should return to the Abyss."
"The Orders teach a lot of things." Kael's jaw tightened. "Does it look like I have an endless well of power? Can you sense me drawing on reserves that aren't there?"
She glanced at him sideways. "No. But I can sense... Something. It's like standing next to a cold hearth. The heat's gone but the shape of it remains."
He hadn't expected her to be spiritually sensitive. That changed things—gave him a potential edge, but also a vulnerability. She could sense his abilities. She could report them to someone with better resources than a bounty hunter.
"What are the Six Orders?" he asked, steering the conversation deliberately. "I've heard the names but never understood what they do."
Vex laughed, but there was no humor in it. "You don't know? A Reaver operating in Thornwick, and you don't know about the Orders?"
"I've been... Disconnected from mainstream Awakened society. Learning on the job."
"Learning on the job." She shook her head. "Fine. I'll give you the short version. There are six Orders, each aligned with one of the original Entities that fought in the Abyssal Wars. The Order of Dawn serves the Sun God—light, healing, divine judgment. The Iron Vanguard serves the Mountain King—strength, endurance, military discipline. The Arcane Spire serves the Void Weaver—knowledge, magic, the manipulation of spiritual energy. The Thornheart Circle serves the Green Mother—nature, growth, healing through earth and plant. The Stormborn Covenant serves the Tempest Lord—wind, lightning, freedom and chaos. And the Shadow Court..."
She trailed off.
"The Shadow Court?" Kael prompted.
"Serves the Whisper King." Her voice dropped. "No one knows what the Whisper King represents. The Court specializes in information, espionage, wet work. They operate in the spaces between the other Orders. The Concordat technically forbids them from open conflict, but everyone knows they have fingers in every pie."
Kael filed that away. The Whisper King. He'd heard the name before—whispered in the margins of texts he'd stolen from a dying Archon's study. The name always appeared alongside symbols of deletion, as though someone wanted it forgotten.
"And they all fight the Abyss together?"
"That's the official story." Vex stopped at a corner, scanning the street ahead. "The reality is more complicated. The six Orders maintain a network of seals—the 72 rifts between our world and the Abyss. Each rift contains an Entity, a piece of the original Abyssal force that nearly consumed everything three thousand years ago. The seals keep them contained."
"72 rifts." Kael felt the words settle into his mind like stones into water. "And they're weakening."
"Now you understand." Vex started walking again, faster now. "The seals decay over time. Maintenance requires constant sacrifice—soul-essence, spiritual energy, lives if necessary. The Orders collect these tithes from Awakened and civilians alike. When the seal network fails completely, the Entities break free. That's the apocalypse scenario every child learns about in temple school."
They reached the print shop ten minutes later. The building listed sideways, its windows boarded up, its door hanging by one hinge. Vex pushed inside without hesitation, and Kael followed into a darkness that smelled of old ink and mildew.
Upstairs, she lit a candle. The weak flame revealed a cramped space—a printing press covered in dust, stacks of paper rotting in the corners, a thin mattress on the floor. Someone had been living here recently. Empty bottles clustered near the window.
"It's not much." Vex barred the door with a heavy iron pipe. "But it's invisible. The Guild's informants don't watch abandoned buildings in the undercity. Too much other business to track."
Kael lowered himself onto a crate, feeling exhaustion seep through his bones. The wound on his neck had stopped bleeding, but his head throbbed with the beginnings of a headache. Too much adrenaline, not enough food.
"Why are you helping me?" he asked. "You could have taken the bounty. A hundred gold sovereigns is enough to live on for months."
Vex sat across from him, knife resting on her knee. The candlelight carved deep shadows under her eyes.
"Because I know what the Guild really does." Her voice was barely above a whisper. "I've seen their facilities. The people they take—the Awakened ones especially. They don't just harvest soul-essence through rituals. They *experiment*. They try to force Awakening on civilians. They try to extract abilities from living subjects. They call it 'research.' I've seen what it does to people."
Kael went still. "You've seen their facilities."
"I've seen what comes out." She met his gaze, and he saw something he'd almost missed before—fear, buried deep beneath the cynicism and the hardness. "My brother went through their Awakening ritual three years ago. He was one of the success stories. Recruited into their enforcement arm. Last month, he went missing. I tracked him to a Guild facility in the Ashwick district. I got close enough to see through a window."
She stopped. Her hands clenched on the knife.
"What did you see?" Kael asked quietly.
"Cages. Dozens of cages, stacked like merchandise. Inside each one..." She swallowed hard. "They'd cut him open. Opened his chest like a butcher's carcass. Something was *growing* inside him. Some kind of... Parasite. Feeding on his soul while he was still alive."
The room felt colder. Kael's stomach turned.
"The Guild isn't just hunting Reavers," Vex said. "They're trying to *create* them. Or something like us. Something that can harvest abilities directly. They've been experimenting for years, and lately the pace has picked up." She leaned forward. "That's why they want you. You already have what they're trying to build. They want to understand how it works. They want to take it from you."
Kael sat back, processing. The Mortician's Guild had always been morally questionable—harvesting corpses with "nominal family consent" was a polite fiction that everyone pretended to believe. But this was something else. Systematic experimentation on living subjects. Forced Awakening.
And if they were accelerating their research, something must have changed. A deadline. A threat. A goal they were rushing to meet.
"The seals," he said slowly. "The 72 rifts. You said they're weakening. How fast?"
"Faster than projected. Some of the Order scholars think there's a connection—between the seal decay and the Guild's activities. That the entities pushing against the Veil are influencing human organizations. Encouraging them to prepare." Vex shook her head. "I don't know if that's true. But I know the Guild is scared. Scared enough to do terrible things."
A new sound intruded on the silence—footsteps on the stairs. Vex was on her feet instantly, knife raised, positioning herself between Kael and the door. He rose too, his body tensing for a fight he wasn't sure he could win.
The footsteps stopped. A voice called out, muffled by the wood.
"Vex? It's me. Open up."
She exhaled slowly, lowering her blade. "It's okay. He's a friend."
"A friend who happens to be a Reaver?" The voice from outside carried a note of dark humor.
"A friend who's willing to help us." She moved to the door, unbarred it. "Just don't startle him. He's... Particular."
The door swung open. A man stepped into the candlelight—tall, gaunt, with the kind of weathered face that suggested a lifetime of hard living. His left arm ended at the elbow, the stump wrapped in dirty bandages. He smelled of horse sweat and tobacco.
"Name's Dorian." The man inclined his head toward Kael, a gesture that was almost mocking in its formality. "Dorian Ashford. Retired Iron Vanguard, current freelance troubleshooter, occasional grave robber when the mood strikes." His eyes—gray, hard, assessing—fixed on Kael's face. "You're the one everyone's talking about. The Soul Reaver who walked out of a death sentence."
Kael said nothing. Something about the man felt wrong, though he couldn't pinpoint what.
"Vex says you can get us into the Guild's Ashwick facility," Dorian continued, settling onto a crate with the ease of someone who'd spent years in uncomfortable places. "She's wrong. *We* can get you in. You provide the distraction—Reavers are pretty good at drawing attention—and I'll handle the infiltration. Vex watches our backs."
"Why would you help me?" Kael asked.
Dorian smiled, but it didn't reach his eyes. "Because the Guild took something from me too. Because I'm tired of watching monsters profit from other people's suffering. Because..." He paused, studying Kael's face. "Because I think you're going to change things. The way the world's structured—Orders at the top, Awakened second, everyone else scrambling for scraps—it's not sustainable. The Reavers are proof that power doesn't have to come from Entities or rituals or institutional blessing. Power can be taken. Refined. Used by anyone willing to pay the price."
"The price being what?" Kael kept his voice flat.
"Souls." Dorian spread his hands. "Yours, other people's. Doesn't matter, as long as you're willing to pay it."
"That's not how Soul Reaving works." Kael felt a strange clarity settling over him, as though pieces were finally clicking into place. "The harvesting itself costs nothing. But there's always a cost. Social. Political. The world punishes Reavers because we're a threat to the established order. Because anyone with the ability could theoretically ascend without the Orders' permission. Without their control."
Dorian's smile widened. "Exactly. The real cost isn't spiritual. It's the hatred of everyone who benefits from the current system. The Orders, the Guild, the nobles, the merchants who sell soul-essence at inflated prices. Every harvest you make is a declaration of war against the hierarchy. Most Reavers can't handle that. They break, or they run, or they die. But the ones who survive..."
He leaned forward, and his voice dropped to a whisper.
"The ones who survive become something new. Something the world hasn't seen in three thousand years."
Kael met his gaze. "What happened three thousand years ago?"
"The first Soul Reaver." Dorian's eyes glittered in the candlelight. "The original. They called him the Deathless King. He walked through the Abyssal Wars like a god among mortals, harvesting the power of every being he killed—human, Entity, anything with a soul. The Orders united against him at the end. Not because he was evil, but because he was *free*. Because he answered to no one and no thing. They bound him. Sealed him away. Erased his name from every record they could reach. But the knowledge of what he did..."
He tapped his temple.
"It didn't die. It's been waiting. Passed down in whispers, in forbidden texts, in the nightmares of high priests who know the truth. And now it's waking up again."
Vex moved closer to Kael, her presence a reminder that he wasn't alone. "We should rest," she said. "Dawn's in five hours. The facility shifts guard rotations at first light—that's our best window."
Kael nodded slowly. His mind churned with new information, new possibilities. The Six Orders. The 72 rifts. The original Soul Reaver, the Deathless King. The Mortician's Guild's experiments. Every piece connected to the others, forming a picture he was only beginning to understand.
"There's something I need to do first." He stood, moving toward the window. "In the alley outside. There's a body."
Dorian and Vex exchanged glances.
"What kind of body?" Dorian asked.
"The kind that still has something to give." Kael's hand tingled, that familiar coldness spreading through his fingers. "I need to practice. To understand what I can do. And the dead don't care about politics."
He waited for an objection, for moral outrage, for the familiar condemnation. Instead, Dorian nodded slowly, a grim appreciation in his expression. Vex moved aside.
"Don't get caught," she said. "The streets aren't safe for Reavers after dark. Not anymore."
Kael descended the stairs and slipped through the broken door into the alley beyond. The night air hit him like a slap—cold, damp, carrying the ever-present coal smoke of Thornwick's factories. His Awakened senses expanded outward, reading the spiritual residue of the city.
There. Twenty feet away, slumped against a brick wall. A corpse, fresh enough that the soul-essence hadn't begun to dissipate. A man, judging by the size, though decay had already started its work. The smell hit him a moment later—old blood, human waste, the sweet rot of abandoned flesh.
Kael approached slowly, crouching beside the body. The man's face was a ruin—beaten badly before death, features swollen beyond recognition. His clothes were threadbare, marked with the ash stains of factory work. A laborer. Someone who'd died in the gaps between the world's great powers.
Kael reached out.
His fingers touched cold flesh, and the Soul Reaver stirred.
The sensation was unlike anything else—a pull from somewhere deep inside him, a hunger that had nothing to do with food or air. The man's spiritual residue rose to meet it, fragmentary and fading but still present. Memories brushed against Kael's consciousness: endless shifts, aching muscles, a wife who no longer waited at home, children who'd grown up without him.
Pain lanced through his skull. Not from the harvest itself—the harvesting was effortless, free, exactly as the system claimed. The pain came from the *information*, the echoes of a life he'd never lived flooding into his awareness.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 3 units of spiritual essence from the remains. You acquired: Laborer's Endurance (lesser passive) - Reduces fatigue during repetitive physical tasks.]
Kael withdrew his hand, gasping. The notification hung in his vision, glowing faintly in the darkness.
Laborer's Endurance. A minor ability, barely worth harvesting. But it was *his* now, grafted onto his soul alongside whatever other skills he'd collected from previous harvests. The system didn't care about quality. It only counted quantity.
He sat back on his heels, staring at the corpse. Another nameless death in a city full of them. Another life reduced to raw materials.
But for the first time, he understood what that meant.
The Soul Reaver wasn't just a tool. It was a key. The power to unlock abilities from anyone who'd ever lived, preserving fragments of their souls in his own being. The knowledge of the first Reaver—the Deathless King—still existed somewhere, hidden from the world but not destroyed. The Orders feared Reavers because they represented a path to power that bypassed every gate they'd built.
And somewhere in Thornwick, the Mortician's Guild was trying to replicate that power by force. Experimenting on living subjects. Creating something monstrous in their desperation.
Kael rose to his feet. Above him, a cloud of steam rose from a factory pipe, illuminated by the orange glow of gaslights. The city churned on, indifferent to his revelation.
He had five hours until dawn. Five hours to rest, to plan, to prepare for whatever came next. And then the facility. The cages. The experiments.
The bodies.
He climbed back up the stairs to find Dorian watching him with an unreadable expression. Vex stood by the window, her knife now sheathed at her hip.
"Well?" she asked. "Did you get what you needed?"
"I learned something." Kael moved to the thin mattress, lowering himself onto it with a grunt. "I learned that I don't know nearly enough. About the system. About my abilities. About what's really happening in this city."
Dorian laughed softly. "Welcome to Thornwick. Nobody knows what's happening. We just try to survive long enough to figure it out."
Kael closed his eyes. Sleep wouldn't come easy—he could hear the city breathing around him, a thousand lives lived in parallel, most of them ending in unmarked graves. But exhaustion dragged him under regardless.
He dreamed of seals cracking. Of voices whispering through the Veil. Of a man standing alone against an army of shadows, harvesting power from beings that shouldn't exist.
When he woke, the candle had burned to a stub, and gray light leaked through the boarded windows. Vex was already awake, checking her equipment. Dorian sat by the door, sharpening a blade that gleamed too brightly for the dim light.
"Time to move," Vex said. "The facility is twenty minutes from here. We go in through the service tunnels—Dorian mapped them last week. You stay in front, draw their attention. We slip in behind you. Agreed?"
Kael nodded, rising. His neck still ached where she'd held the knife, but the cut had scabbed over. He'd live.
"Agreed."
They descended into the morning streets, three figures moving against the current of factory workers heading to their shifts. Kael walked in the middle, Vex on his left, Dorian on his right. None of them spoke.
The city swallowed them whole.
Twenty minutes later, they stood at the mouth of a drainage tunnel, dark water sloshing against their boots. The smell was indescribable—rot and chemicals and human waste, layered over each other like geological strata.
"Stay close," Dorian whispered. "The tunnels branch ahead. Left fork leads to the old sewer system. Right fork goes to the Guild's sub-basement. Don't touch anything metal—the guards use electrified tripwires."
They entered single file, Dorian in front, Kael in the middle, Vex guarding the rear. The darkness pressed in immediately, thick enough to taste. Kael activated his Awakened senses, reading the spiritual residue of the walls—decades of waste, countless bodies flushed through the system, and beneath it all, something else. Something that pulsed faintly, like a second heartbeat.
"What is that?" he breathed.
"What is what?" Dorian's voice echoed strangely in the confined space.
Kael didn't answer. The pulse grew stronger as they approached the right fork, a rhythm that matched no human heartbeat he knew. It felt like the Veil itself—thin, fragile, straining against something on the other side.
They reached a junction. A rusted grate blocked the passage ahead, and beyond it, faint light leaked through gaps in the metal. Voices murmured on the other side—guards, by the sound of it, complaining about their shift schedules.
Dorian produced a set of picks, working the lock with practiced speed. Thirty seconds later, the grate swung open on silent hinges.
"This is where we split," Dorian said quietly. "Kael—you go through first. Make noise. Draw them out. Don't engage unless you have to."
"And if they kill me?"
"Then we're no worse off than we were." Dorian's smile was thin and humorless. "But they won't kill you. They want you alive. Use that."
Kael stepped through the grate into a corridor of white stone, lit by phosphorescent panels that cast everything in a sickly green glow. The air changed immediately—cleaner, colder, with an undertaste of something chemical. Antiseptic. The smell of a hospital, or a morgue.
He walked forward, letting his footsteps echo.
The voices ahead fell silent. Then, footsteps. Many of them, moving fast.
They came around the corner in a wall of bodies—five guards in gray uniforms, their faces hidden behind visored helmets. Each one carried a stun baton crackling with spiritual energy, the kind designed to incapacitate Awakened without permanent damage.
"Hold!" The lead guard raised a fist, and his team stopped three feet from Kael. "By order of the Mortician's Guild, you are under—"
Kael moved.
He didn't think, didn't plan. The Soul Reaver responded to instinct, reaching out through his fingertips as he ducked under the first guard's swing. Power flooded into him—combat training, spiritual pressure, the raw edge of violence.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 7 units of spiritual essence from the remains. You acquired: Guard Discipline (lesser passive) - Improves reaction time during coordinated group combat.]
Not all of them. The harvesting required touch, and there were too many targets. But he felt the knowledge graft itself onto his consciousness—the way soldiers moved, the rhythm of their formations, the weakness in their formation patterns.
The second guard swung. Kael twisted, feeling the baton pass inches from his skull. He grabbed the man's wrist, used his momentum to pull him off-balance, then shoved him into the guard behind.
They went down in a tangle of limbs and equipment. Three left.
"Fall back!" The lead guard's voice carried an edge of panic. "Call for reinforcements! We have a Reaver in Sector Seven!"
Kael pressed forward, but the remaining guards had formed a defensive line, their batons raised. He was fast, but not fast enough to close the gap without taking a hit. And spiritual stun batons weren't designed to incapacitate—they were designed to *hurt*.
"Kael!" Vex's voice echoed from somewhere behind him. "Move!"
He threw himself sideways as something sailed over his head—a bottle, filled with liquid, trailing a wisp of smoke. It shattered at the guards' feet, releasing a cloud of acrid gas that sent them staggering, clawing at their eyes.
"Flashbloom," Dorian's voice said grimly. "Works on spiritual energy too. They'll be blind for two minutes, maybe three. Move *now*."
Kael ran.
The corridor branched ahead—left toward the light, right toward deeper shadows. He chose right, following the faint pulse that had drawn him from the tunnels. Behind him, he heard Vex and Dorian breaking through the grate, their footsteps matching his pace.
The passage narrowed, then opened into a vast chamber.
Kael stopped, his breath catching in his throat.
Rows upon rows of cages stretched into the darkness, each one filled with bodies. Men, women, children—some alive, some dead, all of them connected by thin tubes that pumped spiritual energy into a central column of pulsing light. The column throbbed in rhythm with his heartbeat, matching the pulse he'd felt in the tunnels.
"What in the Abyss..." Vex breathed beside him.
The dying lights along the walls flickered. Somewhere in the chamber, a mechanism activated—a grinding of gears, a hiss of released pressure.
Then, a voice. Deep, resonant, speaking words that seemed to come from everywhere at once.
"YOU SHOULD NOT BE HERE, REAVER. BUT I SUSPECTED SOMEONE WOULD COME EVENTUALLY."
Kael's hands went cold. The Soul Reaver in his chest recoiled, as though recognizing something it had encountered before.
"I AM THE KEEPER OF THIS FACILITY. THE GUILD'S SERVANT. THE ONE WHO ENSURES THAT THE GREAT WORK CONTINUES."
The shadows in the corner of the chamber shifted, coalesced, took form. A figure emerged—tall, impossibly thin, wearing robes that seemed to absorb the light around them. Its face was hidden behind a mask of polished bone, and its eyes...
Its eyes burned with the same light as the central column.
"THE SEAL NETWORK WEAKENS," it continued, moving toward them with steps that made no sound. "WITHIN TEN YEARS, THE FIRST RIFT WILL BREAK. THE ENTITIES WILL POUR THROUGH. AND YOUR WORLD WILL BURN."
It stopped ten feet away, studying Kael with an intensity that felt like needles on his skin.
"BUT THE GUILD HAS FOUND ANOTHER WAY. WE DO NOT SEEK TO PREVENT THE UNLEASHING. WE SEEK TO *SURVIVE* IT. TO ASCEND ALONGSIDE THE ENTITIES. TO BECOME SOMETHING... MORE."
Dorian drew his blade, but Vex grabbed his arm. "Wait. Look."
The Keeper raised a hand, and the tubes connecting the cages to the central column pulsed with increased brightness. The bodies inside—the living ones—convulsed silently.
"WE HAVE TRIED TO CREATE REAVERS THROUGH CONVENTIONAL MEANS. HARVESTING SOULS, FORCING AWAKENING, BINDING FRAGMENTS OF ENTITY ESSENCE TO HUMAN VESSELS. ALL FAILED. THE PROCESS REQUIRES SOMETHING... ELSE."
Its burning gaze fixed on Kael.
"IT REQUIRES A LIVING REAVER. ONE WHOSE ABILITIES ARE ALREADY DEVELOPED. ONE WHO CAN TEACH US, OR WHO CAN BE DISMANTLED SO WE MAY STUDY THE MECHANISM DIRECTLY."
The chamber's doors began to close, sealing them in.
"SO. WHICH WILL IT BE?"
Kael felt the Soul Reaver surge inside him, responding to the threat. Power hummed in his veins—harvested strength, stolen abilities, the accumulated might of every soul he'd touched. He could fight. He could probably win.
But the Keeper wasn't alone. Guards were pouring into the chamber from every entrance, their formations professional and deadly. And the thing behind the mask—the thing that wore a human shape but burned with something older—
It wasn't human at all.
"We need to run," Kael whispered. "Now."
He turned, grabbing Vex's arm, pulling Dorian toward the passage they'd come through. Behind him, the Keeper laughed—a sound like cracking ice.
"YOU CANNOT ESCAPE. THE FACILITY IS SEALED. THE CONTAINMENT PROTOCOLS ARE ACTIVE. YOU ARE TRAPPED, LITTLE REAVER, AND I HAVE ALL THE TIME I NEED."
The lights went out.
In the darkness, Kael felt the Soul Reaver pulse again—and this time, it *pulled*. Not toward the corpses in their cages, but toward the Keeper itself.
Toward whatever burned behind that bone mask.
"Kael!" Vex's voice cut through the blackness. "What are you doing?"
He didn't answer. Couldn't answer. The pull was too strong, too urgent, as though the Reaver recognized something ancient and dangerous and *wrong* about the being before him.
"Soul Harvest," he murmured. "Can I—"
He lunged forward, reaching through the darkness toward the Keeper.
The world exploded into light.
