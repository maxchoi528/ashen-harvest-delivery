The ground split open with a sound like tearing iron. Kael dropped his cigarette and stepped back as the crack raced down the cobblestones, splitting the street in two. Dust billowed from the fissure, carrying with it a smell like burnt copper and rotting flowers.
"What in the Sun God's name—" Mira shouted from behind him, her hands already weaving a defensive sigil.
Kael didn't answer. He watched the crack widen, watched darkness pulse at its edges. The spiritual pressure hit him next—not a wave but a presence, something vast and hungry pressing against the thin membrane of reality. His own spiritual sense screamed warnings in frequencies most people couldn't hear.
Sealed rift. Something behind it stirring.
Three blocks away, a church bell began to toll without anyone pulling the rope. People poured from buildings, faces pale, some already running toward the harbor. A woman stumbled past Kael, her child clutched to her chest, and she didn't even look at him. In Thornwick, you learned quickly that some disasters were bigger than fear.
"Orders are mobilizing," Mira said, pulling a brass spyglass from her coat. "I can see Iron Vanguard banners on Seventh Street. They're not usually this fast."
"Because this isn't their territory." Kael kept his eyes on the crack. It pulsed again, and this time, a sound accompanied it—not a voice, not wind. Something between a whisper and a scream. "The Concordat says they can respond to threats in neutral zones. That means Dawn and Spire will be here too. This is going to get crowded fast."
The pressure intensified. Kael felt his Soul Reaver senses react, responding to the proximity of something that wasn't dead but had never been truly alive. The Entities existed in the Abyss, and when rifts opened, fragments of them could bleed through—touches of wrongness that corrupted everything they contacted.
"We need to move," he said. "Away from the rift. Now."
Mira didn't argue. She fell into step beside him as they moved through the gathering chaos, past markets being abandoned mid-trade and carriages stopped in the street. A man in Arcane Spire robes pushed past them, his face tight with concentration, already building some kind of containment array in his mind.
Kael knew that look. He'd seen it on hunters, on zealots, on people who believed they could fix anything if they just built the right spell. The Spire mages were the most arrogant of the Orders, convinced that spiritual power could solve any problem if you just applied enough theory.
They were wrong. The rifts weren't problems to be solved. They were wounds, and wounds didn't heal cleanly.
The crack behind them widened further. Kael glanced back and saw something emerge—not a creature, not exactly, but a shape of darkness that moved like smoke given terrible purpose. It drifted across the street, and wherever it touched cobblestones, those stones turned black and crumbled to powder.
"Cultists," Mira hissed. "They did this. They must have."
"Obviously." Kael's hand found the grip of his blade. "The question is which cult. There are at least four operating in Thornwick, and they don't all want the same thing."
"Some of them want to end the world," Mira said.
"Some of them want to rule what's left of it." Kael pulled her into an alley as another Spire mage rushed past, this one running toward the rift instead of away. Brave or stupid—the line was thin. "Zarek's people would love to destabilize the city enough to force a response. If the Orders commit their full strength here, their other operations get easier."
The shadow-thing drifted closer. Kael watched it through the gap between buildings, tracking its movement. It wasn't fast, but it didn't need to be. It just needed to touch things and corrupt them, spreading wrongness like a disease.
"Soul Reaver," a voice said from behind them.
Kael spun, blade clearing scabbard in one smooth motion. The speaker stood at the alley's far end—a woman in the grey robes of the Mortician's Guild, her face half-hidden by a cloth mask perfumed with herbs. She held no weapon, but her hands were raised in a gesture of peace that didn't reach her eyes.
"Clever eyes you have," Kael said, not lowering his blade. "Most people don't notice."
"Most people can't." She stepped forward carefully, her boots silent on the wet stones. "The Guild has been watching you since you arrived in Thornwick. We've catalogued fourteen harvests in three weeks. You're either very skilled or very lucky."
"Which do you think?"
"Both." She stopped at a careful distance, still watching him. "My name is Thessaly. I represent interests that prefer you alive and operational. The Orders would execute you on principle. The cults would dissect you for power. But there are others who understand what you represent, and what you could become."
"I don't represent anything." Kael kept his voice flat. "I harvest what I need and move on. If your 'interests' want something from me, they can say it plainly."
Thessaly's masked face showed nothing, but something in her posture shifted—appreciation, maybe, or acknowledgment. "The sealed rifts are failing. Not gradually, not the way the Orders predict. Something is accelerating the decay, and it's not natural. We believe cult activity is destabilizing the network faster than anyone realizes. When the major rifts crack—and they will crack—the Entities won't just bleed through. They'll pour through like water through a broken dam."
"Bold prediction." Kael sheathed his blade slowly, letting her see the decision was his. "The Orders have maintained the seals for a thousand years."
"The Orders maintain the illusion of control." Thessaly's voice hardened. "The seal network is a house of cards held together by tradition and desperation. You want proof? Look behind you."
Kael turned. The crack in the street had grown again, and now figures emerged from it—not shadow-creatures but humans, dozens of them, walking out of the darkness as if stepping through a doorway. They moved wrong, their joints bending at angles that hurt to watch, their faces blank and empty.
Cultists. Corrupted, hollowed out, turned into puppets for something else's will.
The Spire mage who'd run toward the rift screamed once, high and terrible, before the hollowed ones swarmed him. His death took less than five seconds.
"Zarek's work," Thessaly said. "He's not just destabilizing rifts. He's using them as transit points. That's why the sealed locations matter—not because of the Entities inside, but because they're anchored to the Abyss. When seals crack, the Abyss becomes an extension of our world."
"That's insane. Opening the Abyss would destroy everything."
"It would destroy the current order of things. But some people see that as a feature, not a bug." Thessaly pulled something from her robes—a small vial filled with dark liquid that seemed to move on its own. "This is soul-essence. High-grade, Awakened source. Consider it a gift, and an invitation. When the war comes—and it will come, Soul Reaver—people like you will decide which side wins. The Orders are too proud to use tools like you. The cults want to own you. But there are those who would... Work alongside you."
She set the vial on a broken crate and stepped back.
"The Mortician's Guild doesn't take sides in Order politics. We deal in death, which belongs to no faction. But we prefer a world where death means something to a world where it doesn't. If you want to know more about the rifts, about what's really happening, find us. The Ember District, behind the old cathedral. Ask for the Pale Table."
Then she was gone, vanished into the alley's shadows as if she'd never existed.
Mira let out a breath. "What the hell was that?"
"Pitch," Kael said. "Everyone wants a piece of the Soul Reaver. The trick is figuring out which pitch has truth in it."
He picked up the vial and examined it. The soul-essence inside was indeed high-grade—he could feel the spiritual resonance even through the glass, could sense the power coiled within. Worth a fortune. Worth more than that, maybe. Awakened soul-essence was contraband in most places, but here, in the chaos of a rift crisis, its value would multiply.
"The hollowed ones are spreading," Mira said. "We need to move or we'll be surrounded."
Kael pocketed the vial and drew his blade again. Through the alley's mouth, he could see the corrupted cultists moving through the streets, their wrong-jointed bodies taking down anyone who couldn't run fast enough. The Spire mage's corpse lay where he'd fallen, his robes stained with something darker than blood.
Fresh. Less than a minute dead.
Kael didn't hesitate. He crossed to the body and knelt beside it, pressing his palm against the cooling flesh. The harvest came automatically, Soul Reaver ability responding to contact, and the familiar notification pulsed in his awareness.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 847 units of spiritual energy from the remains. Memory Fragment acquired: "Spire Combat Doctrine — Tier 2 containment arrays."]
The Spire mage's knowledge flowed into Kael's mind—not complete, not perfect, but enough. He could feel the shapes of containment spells, the theory behind them, the muscle memory of a mage who'd spent decades learning to seal things away. It wouldn't make him a Spire fighter, but it would let him understand what they were doing when they did it.
"Disgusting," Mira said quietly.
"Useful," Kael corrected. "The Spire mages are running containment protocols. Now I'll know what they're trying to do before they do it."
He stood, wiping his hand on his coat. The hollowed ones were getting closer, their blank faces turning toward the alley, their wrong-jointed bodies shuffling forward with horrible purpose.
"We go west," Kael said. "Away from the rift, toward the Iron Vanguard positions. If the Vanguard is here, they have combat formations. We link up with them, we have a chance to get out of this alive."
"Link up with the Vanguard? They hate Soul Reavers more than the cults do."
"They hate me specifically. But they hate Entities more." Kael moved toward the alley's far exit. "In a fight between me and an Abyssal threat, they'll pick me last. That's enough for now."
They ran.
Westbrook Street had become a war zone. Kael emerged from the alley into chaos—crumbling buildings, fires burning out of control, the screams of the wounded and dying filling the air like a chorus of the damned. The hollowed ones had spread throughout the district, their corruption spreading with them. Everything they touched turned black and wrong.
A building collapsed to their left, its foundations undermined by the spreading corruption. Kael pulled Mira down as debris rained overhead, and when he looked up, he saw the Iron Vanguard.
Fifty soldiers in heavy armor, arranged in a shield wall formation at the intersection of Westbrook and Harbor Road. Their commander—a massive woman with a battle-axe that seemed too heavy for her to lift, let alone wield—barked orders in a voice that carried over the chaos.
"Hold the line! Nothing passes!"
The hollowed ones threw themselves at the formation and died against it, breaking on the Vanguard's shields like waves on a cliff. But more kept coming, and Kael could see exhaustion setting in. The soldiers were strong, disciplined, skilled—but they were mortal, and their spiritual reserves weren't infinite.
"The Spire mages need to seal the rift," Mira said. "Without the source, the hollowed ones will keep coming until there's no one left to corrupt."
"Seal the rift." Kael looked at the crack in the distance, still pulsing with darkness. The pressure was building, and now he could see shapes moving in the darkness—not hollowed ones but something worse. Bigger. Hungrier. "That's not going to happen. The Spire can't seal a major rift without preparation, and they don't have time."
"Then what?"
"Then we do what we can." Kael's eyes swept the battlefield, looking for opportunities. The Vanguard was holding, but they'd be overrun within the hour. The Spire mages were scattered, trying to contain individual threats instead of addressing the source. The Dawn Order was conspicuously absent—probably waiting to arrive as heroes once others had bled.
But there, at the edge of the rift, Kael saw something else. Figures in black robes, standing at the edges of the darkness, directing the hollowed ones like shepherds. Cultists. Not corrupted, not hollowed—still themselves, still in control.
Zarek's people.
If Kael could reach them, could disrupt their coordination, the hollowed ones would lose direction. They weren't smart enough to act on their own. They needed orders. They needed someone to point them at targets.
"Stay with the Vanguard," he told Mira. "If I don't come back, find the Mortician's Guild. The Pale Table."
"Kael—"
He was already running.
The streets became a maze of horror. Kael moved through alleys and over rooftops, staying above the corruption spreading below. He saw things that would haunt him—people he'd passed in the market that morning now walking with wrong-jointed steps, their eyes empty, their lives drained to fuel something terrible.
Every corpse he passed, he touched. Every harvest he took.

He reached the rift's edge as the cultists were adjusting their positions. Five of them, standing in a circle around the crack, their hands raised in synchronized gestures. The darkness pulsed with their rhythm, and the hollowed ones responded, moving in patterns that made terrible sense.
Kael counted them. Five cultists, each wearing the same black robes with the same silver symbol—a broken circle, representing the shattered Veil. Zarek's sect. The Separatists, they called themselves. People who believed the Entities should be freed, that humanity had been weak to seal them away, that the Abyss was salvation than damnation.
They were insane. But they were organized, and their insanity had a plan.

"None of you are innocent." Kael withdrew his blade and faced them. "You opened this rift. You hollowed out those people. You turned them into puppets."
"We freed them." A man stepped forward, his eyes blazing with genuine conviction. "The Soul Reaver takes from the dead, but we offer the living to something greater. The Entities will remake this world. Those who serve them will be reborn in glory. Those who resist—"
He lunged.
Kael sidestepped, blade arcing, and opened the man's throat. Blood sprayed, and the cultist fell, his hands clutching at the wound that wouldn't stop bleeding. The others came at Kael together now, abandoning their gestures for direct combat.
Three against one. They had numbers. Kael had the Soul Reaver's edge.
He let the first one come in close, felt the cultist's blade scrape against his armor, and then his own weapon found its mark—under the arm, through the gap in the ribs, into the heart. The cultist's eyes went wide with surprise, and then empty.

A thrust through the sternum. A spin that brought his blade across her throat.
Two left. They broke.

They were still dangerous, still corrupting everything they touched, but they were no longer a directed force.
The Vanguard would hold now. The Spire mages would be able to contain the rift, eventually. The war wasn't won, but this battle might be.

The Dawn Order. Coming to take credit for victories they hadn't earned. Coming to tell the survivors how lucky they were that the Sun God's chosen had arrived.
Kael sheathed his blade and walked toward the rift's edge. The darkness pulsed, and for a moment, he thought he saw something looking back at him—something vast and ancient and utterly alien. It wasn't an Entity, not fully. It was a projection, a touch of awareness from whatever waited on the other side of the seal.
*Soul Reaver*, something whispered in a voice that wasn't a voice. *You wear the mantle of the First. Do you know what he became? Do you know what you will become?*

Kael stood in the middle of it all, covered in blood that wasn't his, holding a blade that had taken more lives in one night than most soldiers saw in a year. Around him, the people of Thornwick began to emerge from their hiding places, their faces pale, their eyes haunted.
The war had come to their city. The sealed rifts were cracking. And the Soul Reaver was still standing.

Kael smiled. He had never been good at front doors.
A narrow maintenance entrance around the side, barely visible unless you knew to look. The lock was old, designed to keep out thieves than Awakened. The door swung open on silent hinges.
