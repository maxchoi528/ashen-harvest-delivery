The scream came from the Ashworth District before the fire followed.
Kael Ashborn dropped from the rooftop ledge, boots striking cobblestones still slick with last night's frost. Steam hissed from a ruptured pipe somewhere nearby, masking the copper-rot smell that crept through the alley. Three blocks east, orange light bloomed against the pre-dawn grey. Another strike. The cults were hitting the city in waves now, not the scattered raids of previous weeks.
He ran.
Thornwick's streets had become a maze of fear. Shutters slammed behind him as he passed. A woman clutched her child and pressed against a wall, her mouth shaping a prayer he couldn't hear. The spiritual pressure radiating from the burning district pushed against his senses like a physical force—raw, hungry, wrong. Stage Four awareness painted the corruption in colors no mundane eye could see, tendrils of Abyssal energy threading through the smoke.
The Ashworth Temple had been a cathedral of the Order of Dawn once. Now its spires cracked against the sky, and figures in black robes milled around its shattered entrance. Kael counted six. Seven. Eight. Too many for a direct assault, even at Sovereign rank.
He circled to the temple's northern transept, where a section of stained glass still held—a jagged wound in the building's flank. Below, bodies. He could see them from his vantage point, sprawled across the steps and the plaza. Some wore the grey robes of Dawn acolytes. Others were civilians, their simple clothes stained dark.
The notification pulsed at the edge of his vision, familiar as breathing.
[Soul Reaver: Corpse detected within range. Eight viable targets. Four above twenty-four hours post-mortem. Four fresh.]
Fresh meant quality. Fresh meant power.
Kael dropped into the shadow of a toppled cart, landing silent as falling ash. The cultists hadn't noticed. They were too focused on dragging something from inside the temple—a chest, iron-bound and heavy, two of them straining with the weight while a third directed traffic with sharp hand gestures.
"Prime Zarek wants the relics secured before the Iron Vanguard arrives," the director called out. His voice carried, cold and precise. "We have twelve minutes."
Twelve minutes. Enough time to harvest the freshest corpses if he moved fast. But those cultists stood between him and the bodies, and they carried the sickening spiritual signature of Awakened fighters. Stage Two, maybe Three. Not threats individually, but eight against one in an open plaza invited complications.
Kael's fingers brushed the nearest corpse—an elderly man, throat opened by blade work, eyes still wide with final terror. The harvest felt like plunging his hand into cold water, then pulling back something that didn't belong in this world.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Memory Fragment from the remains. The elderly man's final memory acquired: the temple's interior layout, positions of the remaining Dawn acolytes, and a partial view of the attackers.]
Information flooded his mind. The old man had been a groundskeeper. He'd seen the assault through a cracked door, watched his brothers and sisters cut down, tried to flee and failed. The memory carried no power, but it carried intel—and in war, intel killed more effectively than any blade.
Kael rose from the crouch and moved along the plaza's edge, using the bodies as cover. Two cultists stood near the temple steps, arguing about who would get the pick of the dead Dawn's personal effects. A third had wandered toward the street's mouth, scanning for reinforcements.
The one directing traffic—his robes bore silver embroidery at the cuffs, marking him as something the rest—stood over a woman in Dawn robes. She wasn't dead. Kael could see her breathing, shallow and wet. The cultist had placed his boot on her chest, leaning down to murmur something.
"Tell me where the Archive entrance hides, little lightbearer. Your brothers sang their secrets before they stopped screaming. Don't make me ask twice."
The woman spat blood in his face.
The cultist's expression didn't change. He pressed down with his boot, and something cracked. Her scream cut off in a gurgle.
Kael's hand closed around the shaft of a fallen spear. He couldn't save her—the wound was already mortal, her spiritual signature flickering like a candle in wind—but he could end this faster than she could bleed out.
The spear flew.
It caught the silver-cuffed cultist through the shoulder, spinning him backward with enough force to drive him off the steps. His scream harmonized with the woman's final gurgle. The other cultists scattered, hands raised, weapons drawn.
Kael stepped into the open, letting them see him.
"Call for help," he said. "Go ahead. I want to see who arrives first—your friends or the Vanguard."
The wounded cultist struggled to his feet, one hand clamped over the spear wound, blood seeping between his fingers. "Soul Reaver," he hissed, and the word carried the weight of nightmare stories. "They said one operated in Thornwick. They said you worked alone."
"They say a lot of things."
Kael advanced. The cultists formed a ragged semicircle, five of them now, the two with the chest frozen in place. Their weapons gleamed—short swords, hand axes, a polearm that one held with shaking hands. Stage Two spiritual signatures blazed from them like signal fires, desperate and bright.
Stage Four didn't need to blaze. Stage Four existed, a gravity well of accumulated power that bent the lesser signatures toward it like moths toward flame.
"You murdered innocents in a house of worship," Kael said. "On sacred ground."
The trembling one with the polearm broke first. He turned and ran, sprinting toward the burning district with the speed of pure terror. His companions didn't follow. They knew it didn't matter—the Soul Reaver could catch him or let him go, and either way, they were already dead.
Kael let him go. He walked forward instead, reaching the wounded director as the man finally managed to pull the spear from his own shoulder. The wound would kill him in minutes without treatment, but Stage Three Awakened could survive worse. For a while.
"The woman," Kael said, gesturing to the Dawn acolyte. "Did you have to hurt her?"
The cultist laughed, a wet bubbling sound. "She's a lightbearer. Her whole purpose is suffering. We only... Redirected the flow."
Kael knelt beside him. The cultist's eyes widened—not with fear, not yet, but with the dawning understanding of what was about to happen.
"Wait. Wait, I can—information. I have information. The Prime's movements, the rally points, the—"
"Tell me about the Ashenmoor War."
The cultist stopped. His mouth worked, forming words that didn't come.
"The war," Kael repeated. "The cults' big push. What are you really doing here? This isn't just about killing Dawn acolytes and stealing relics."
"They're... We're..." The cultist's breathing grew labored. Blood loss was catching up. "The seals. The seventy-two seals. Each one requires a specific key to open, and the keys are..." He coughed, flecks of red spraying his lips. "The keys are power. Human souls. Awakened souls. The more powerful the sacrifice, the faster the seal breaks."
Kael processed this. The Six Orders taught that the 72 seals held the Abyssal Entities prisoner, that they were ancient and permanent, maintained by sacred rites and spiritual meditation. The cultists believed they could break those seals, release the Entities, and survive—or rule—the aftermath.
"You killed these Dawn members to weaken the seal network."
"We killed them because they were there." The cultist grinned, teeth pink with blood. "Every Awakened death weakens the barriers. Every soul that returns to the Abyss feeds the Entities. We're not trying to open all seventy-two at once. That would be..." He laughed again, weaker. "That would be apocalypse. We're opening them selectively. Strategically. Weaken the network where we need it, where the Entities can reach through and touch the world, and then..."
He died before finishing the sentence.
Kael closed the man's eyes and reached for the harvest.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 47 units of spiritual energy from the remains. Skill Acquired: Abyssal Resistance (Lesser). The cultist's training in resisting Entity influence is now accessible to you.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Memory Fragment from the remains. The cultist's recent memories acquired: locations of three hidden caches in Thornwick, names of five other cult cells, and partial knowledge of the coordination network linking them.]
Information again. The cultists had been harvesting information from their victims, and now their own minds fed his. This one had known relatively little—a foot soldier, not a commander—but even foot soldiers learned things. Kael now possessed the names of five other cells operating in the city, the locations of supply caches, and a fragmentary understanding of how the attacks were coordinated.
The remaining cultists had scattered. Three lay dead where they'd stood, killed by the simple expedient of Kael's will pressing against their Stage Two signatures until their hearts gave out. The two with the chest had dropped their burden and fled. The one who'd run down the street was a dot in the distance, still running.
Kael let him go. He had what he needed, and the morning was only beginning.
The Dawn acolyte—the woman the cultist had tormented—had finally stopped breathing. He knelt beside her, placed one hand on her cooling forehead, and harvested what remained.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted Memory Fragment from the remains. The acolyte's final memories acquired: she was called Sister Maren, she had been a lightbearer for twenty-three years, and her last conscious thought was a prayer that someone would stop the ones who killed her.]
Kael pulled his hand back and sat on the temple steps, watching the smoke rise from the burning district. The notification system pulsed again, showing his accumulated gains. Spiritual energy reserves stable. A new skill added to his repertoire. Intelligence on the cult network. The morning's harvest had been efficient.
The sound of boots on stone reached him before the sight of them. A patrol, approaching from the eastern street—five figures in the burnished armor of the Iron Vanguard, their shields bearing the symbol of the forge. They moved in formation, weapons ready, eyes scanning rooftops and shadows.
Kael stood and waited.
The patrol leader—a woman with iron-grey hair and a scar that split one eyebrow—raised her fist. Her soldiers halted. Her eyes found him, and her expression hardened.
"Soul Reaver." She didn't make it a question. "You're a long way from the Veiled Market's territory."
"I was visiting the Ashworth Temple. Someone had burned it down."
"I can see that." The Vanguard captain gestured to her soldiers. Two moved to secure the perimeter, checking bodies for signs of life. The other two took positions covering Kael. Professional. Suspicious. Exactly the reaction he expected.
"Name?"
"Kael Ashborn."
"I know who you are." Her hand rested on her sword pommel. "The Shadow Court's been asking about you. So has the Order of Dawn's tribunal. You're a difficult man to track, Ashborn."
"I'm elusive by design."
"You're a walking catastrophe." She stepped closer, her voice dropping. "Every time you harvest, you leave evidence. Spiritual residue. The tribunals can track it. The Shadow Court pays informants to find it. And the cults—" She stopped, reconsidered. "The cults want you dead or recruited. Neither option ends well for anyone."
"The cults attacked this temple. Killed Dawn acolytes. Killed civilians." Kael gestured to the bodies. "I stopped what I could."
The captain's expression didn't change. "I saw the cultist with the spear through his shoulder. I saw the others who died. Impressive work for someone who 'stopped what he could.'" She paused. "What did you harvest from them?"
"Intelligence. Their network, their caches, their coordination." Kael reached into his coat and withdrew a folded paper—a habit from his years in the Thornwick underground. "Names and locations. Five cult cells still operating in the city. A supply cache near the old docks. Information about the seal-breaking ritual."
The captain's eyes narrowed. She didn't take the paper. "And you expect me to believe you handed over valuable intelligence out of civic duty."
"I expect you to believe I have better things to do than babysit the Vanguard's investigations. Take the information or don't. I've already memorized it."
Silence stretched between them. The soldiers watched, weapons ready, waiting for an order that might never come. Kael could feel the weight of their suspicion, the coiled tension of people trained to see him as a monster. Soul Reavers didn't help. Soul Reavers harvested and moved on. Soul Reavers didn't stop cult attacks or hand over intelligence to the military.
But Kael had learned something valuable in his years since execution—information was currency, and currency bought tolerance. Maybe even cooperation.
The captain finally reached out and took the paper. She unfolded it, scanned the contents, and her scarred eyebrow rose.
"Five cells. You're certain about these?"
"The source died with the information still in his head. Accuracy should be high."
"I'll have this verified." She folded the paper and tucked it into her belt. "If it's accurate, there might be... Consideration. For your position."
"My position is fine. I'm standing on temple steps that smell like smoke and death. I've been awake for thirty hours. I just harvested a cultist who knew too much and an acolyte who knew too little." Kael stepped past her, moving toward the street. "I need coffee and a room that doesn't overlook burning buildings."
"You're just leaving?"
"What else would I be doing?"
The captain's jaw tightened. "The Shadow Court knows about you, Ashborn. They've been watching your movements. Asking questions in the Veiled Market, in the underground fights, in places where people like you conduct business."
"I'm aware."
"Are you? They don't want to recruit you." She lowered her voice. "The Shadow Court's been investigating Soul Reavers for decades. They have a theory—unconfirmed, supposedly—that the original Soul Reaver wasn't destroyed. That his tomb exists somewhere in the world, and that his abilities can be... Transferred. Copied. Harvested, if you will."
Kael stopped walking. The captain watched him, her eyes sharp, looking for a reaction.
"And what does that have to do with me?"
"Everything. Nothing. The Shadow Court is patient, Ashborn. They don't need you today. They need you in a year, or five years, or ten. They're building a file. Tracking your growth. Noting your patterns." She smiled, thin and humorless. "You might want to consider what happens when their patience runs out."
He turned to face her fully. "You're warning me."
"I'm informing you. There's a difference. The Vanguard has no love for Soul Reavers—you know that better than anyone. But the Shadow Court playing games with ancient artifacts and stolen abilities..." She shook her head. "That's a different kind of threat. The kind that ends cities."
Kael absorbed this. The Shadow Court monitoring him. Building a file. Looking for the original Soul Reaver's tomb—the same tomb that held answers about his own abilities, his own origin. The same tomb that the narrative promised would be revealed previously.
He had time. But not unlimited time.
"The cults," he said. "The coordinated attacks. Are the Six Orders responding?"
"The Six Orders are stretched across the continent. Every city is under pressure. Every seal is under assault. The war the tribunals spent decades preparing for is finally here, and they—" She stopped, jaw working. "They're fighting it. Badly. The cults know our tactics, our formations, our spiritual countermeasures. Someone's feeding them information."
"From inside the Orders?"
"That's the theory. No evidence yet." The captain gestured to her soldiers. "We need to move. Secure the perimeter, search for survivors, report the attack. You should go. The Dawn tribunal will arrive within the hour, and they'd as soon execute you as thank you."
Kael nodded and walked away. Behind him, he heard the patrol moving into position, boots on stone, voices calling out to check the bodies. The morning light was strengthening, burning off the smoke haze, revealing Thornwick in all its scarred glory—steam vents hissing, chimney stacks belching grey, streets slowly filling with people who had no idea how close they'd come to dying in their beds.
He needed to move. The cult cells on his list wouldn't stay in position forever, and the intelligence he'd gathered was only as good as his ability to act on it. But his mind kept returning to the captain's words.
The Shadow Court watching. Building a file. Looking for the tomb of the original Soul Reaver.
He had his own questions about that tomb. About where his abilities came from, why they manifested after his execution, what connection existed between him and the first Soul Reaver. The narrative promised answers previously. This was Chapter 312.
Thirty-eight chapters of secrets stretching ahead of him.
Kael turned down an alley, seeking the underground passage that would take him to the Veiled Market's outer ring. He needed supplies, information, and a place to think. The cult attacks were escalating. The seal network was weakening. The Six Orders were scrambling to respond. Somewhere in all of this, Zarek was pulling strings—the man who'd framed him for murder, whose face Kael remembered from the trial, whose name appeared in his darkest harvest memories.
Zarek. The Shadow Court. The original Soul Reaver's tomb.
The threads were tangling together, and Kael couldn't see the pattern yet.
He emerged onto a wider street, the Market district's familiar chaos enveloping him—hawkers crying their wares, thieves testing pockets, Awakened and mundane mixing in uneasy commerce. A child ran past clutching a bundle of papers, and Kael caught a glimpse of the headline: ASHENMOOR BATTLEFIELD EXPANDS. IRON VANGUARD REQUESTS REINFORCEMENTS.
The war was spreading. The cults were coordinating. The world was breaking along fault lines that had existed for centuries.
And somewhere in the shadows, the Shadow Court was watching him.
Kael bought coffee from a vendor and leaned against a lamp post, letting the caffeine cut through his exhaustion. His notifications pulsed softly, cataloging the morning's gains: spiritual energy, skills, intelligence. The Soul Reaver's harvest was always profitable, even when the cost was paid by others.
A figure emerged from the crowd—not approaching, just present, watching him from thirty feet away. A woman in dark grey robes, her face half-hidden by a hood, her posture radiating the coiled tension of someone trained for violence. Stage Three signature, possibly Stage Four. Professional.
Kael met her eyes. She didn't flinch.
He recognized the look. He'd seen it on tribunal members, on Shadow Court operatives, on cultists who'd survived encounters with things worse than him. It was the look of someone who'd made a decision and was waiting to see if it would kill them.
The woman raised one hand, palm outward—a gesture of peace, or of challenge, or of something in between. Then she turned and walked into the Market's deeper reaches, disappearing into the crowd like smoke.
Kael drained his coffee and followed.
The woman moved through the Market's winding passages with the confidence of someone who knew every shortcut, every dead end, every blind spot in the surveillance network. She didn't look back. She didn't signal. She walked, trusting him to follow or not.
He followed.
They emerged in a courtyard he didn't recognize—a dead end surrounded by crumbling walls, open to the sky, littered with the debris of abandoned stalls. The woman stopped in the center, turned, and pulled back her hood.
Kael's hand moved toward the knife at his belt.
The woman's face was Lyra's. Same sharp features, same dark eyes, same scar running from temple to jaw. But older. Harder. The years since their last encounter had carved new lines around her mouth, and something in her expression had shifted from bitter to broken.
"Hello, Kael." Her voice was rough, unused. "I need to tell you about the Shadow Court."
"Not yet." He didn't relax. "First, tell me why I shouldn't kill you right now."
Lyra raised her hands, palms outward. "Because I'm the only one who can explain what's coming. Because the Shadow Court isn't what you think—hasn't been what you think for twenty years. Because I know about the tomb, and I know why they're watching you, and I know what happens if they find it before you do."
The courtyard fell silent. Wind stirred dust and debris. Somewhere in the distance, a clock tower chimed the eighth hour.
Kael's knife stayed in its sheath. But his hand stayed close.
"Talk," he said. "Quickly."
Lyra took a breath. "The Shadow Court has three factions. One wants to control Soul Reavers. One wants to destroy them. And one—" She paused, and for the first time, uncertainty crossed her features. "One wants to find the original Soul Reaver and bring him back."
Kael's blood went cold.
"Back from what?"
"Back from the dead." Lyra's voice dropped. "They believe he's still alive, Kael. Trapped in his tomb, sealed away by the first Orders, preserved in a state between life and death. They believe his soul never passed to the Abyss. They believe his abilities can be extracted and transferred."
"And what do they believe about me?"
Lyra met his eyes. "They believe you're a copy. A fragment. A piece of him that escaped when they sealed the original away. They believe you carry his power, his memories, his potential. They believe when the tomb opens, you'll either merge with him or be destroyed."
The courtyard seemed to tilt. Kael's mind raced through implications, connections, the strange mechanics of his own existence. The original Soul Reaver, still alive somewhere. The Shadow Court, hunting for his tomb. The abilities that had manifested after his execution, too powerful for a normal Awakening, too specific in their expression.
A copy. A fragment. A piece of something ancient.
"Why tell me this?" he asked. "Why now?"
Lyra lowered her hands. "Because I was part of the faction that wanted to control you. I was the one who arranged for you to be in Thornwick when you Awakened. I was the one who made sure the execution happened when it did, so you'd die at the moment of maximum spiritual sensitivity." Her voice cracked. "I thought I was saving the world. I thought if the Shadow Court controlled the new Soul Reaver, we could prevent another catastrophe. I was wrong."
"Wrong about what?"
"Wrong about everything. The faction that wants to bring back the original—they're winning. They've infiltrated the Order tribunals, the Vanguard command structure, even the Market's inner circle. They've been watching you, Kael. Not to recruit you. To make sure you stay alive long enough to lead them to the tomb."
Kael processed this. The pieces clicked together with sickening clarity—the Shadow Court's patient interest, the tribunal's sudden focus on his movements, the way information about him kept leaking to dangerous parties. He'd been a beacon from the moment he Awakened, and every action he'd taken had been leading him closer to something he didn't understand.
"Where is the tomb?"
"I don't know. No one does. But I know how to find it." Lyra reached into her robes and withdrew a small leather journal, its pages yellowed with age. "The original Soul Reaver kept records. They were scattered after his imprisonment, but fragments survived. This journal contains one of those fragments—a map, partially complete, showing the tomb's location relative to the other sealed sites."
She held it out. Kael didn't take it.
"Why should I trust you? You manipulated me. You arranged my execution. Everything I've suffered—"
"I know." Lyra's voice broke. "I know what I did. I've spent years trying to make it right, trying to find another way. There isn't one. The Shadow Court's factions are moving toward collision, and when they do, the tomb opens, and either the original returns or the world burns." She thrust the journal forward. "Take it. Learn what you can. Find the tomb before they do."
"And if I destroy it? If I seal the original away forever?"
Lyra hesitated. "Then the world loses something irreplaceable. The original Soul Reaver was the only one who ever understood the true nature of the Abyss. His knowledge could end the war—or guarantee our extinction." She shook her head. "I don't know what's right anymore. I just know the Shadow Court can't be allowed to control the outcome."
Kael took the journal. The leather was cold against his palm, the pages brittle. He opened to the first page and saw handwriting that wasn't familiar, wasn't alien—a script that felt like an echo of his own thoughts.
"This is his writing."
"Yes."
"And you're sure he was—the original—"
"Everything points to it." Lyra stepped back. "I have to go. They'll know I contacted you. They'll be watching."
"Who's they?"
"The faction that wants to bring him back." Lyra pulled her hood up, shadowing her face. "They're closer than you think, Kael. Much closer."
She walked toward the courtyard's far exit, her footsteps echoing against the stone. At the threshold, she paused.
"I'm sorry," she said without turning. "For everything. I thought I was doing what was necessary. I was wrong."
She disappeared into the Market's shadows.
Kael stood alone in the courtyard, the journal heavy in his hands, the weight of revelation pressing down on him. The original Soul Reaver, still alive. The Shadow Court, fractured and hunting. His own existence, reduced to a fragment, a copy, a piece of something ancient trying to find its way home.
The notification system pulsed softly.
[System Update: Narrative thread advanced. New objective available: Discover the location of the original Soul Reaver's tomb. Warning: Multiple factions are pursuing this objective. Competition may be hostile.]
Kael closed the journal and tucked it into his coat. The morning had started with a temple burning and ended with a conspiracy laid bare. The cult attacks continued. The war expanded. The seals weakened. And somewhere in the world, the original Soul Reaver waited in his tomb, sealed between life and death, while factions plotted to free him or destroy him.
He needed to move. The information Lyra had given him was incomplete, but it was more than he'd had before. The map fragment, the knowledge of Shadow Court factions, the confirmation that his abilities came from somewhere ancient and powerful.
The question was: what would he do with it?
Kael walked out of the courtyard and back into the Market's chaos. Vendors hawked their wares. Thieves tested their marks. The war continued to spread, city by city, seal by seal, while the world broke along fault lines that had existed for centuries.
He had thirty-eight chapters before the tomb's revelation. Thirty-eight chapters to grow stronger, to unravel the Shadow Court's plans, to find answers before the factions found him.
The notebook in his coat felt heavier than it should.
He turned down an alley, seeking the underground passages that would lead him to a safehouse, and nearly collided with a figure stepping out of the shadows.
The man was old. Ancient. His robes bore symbols Kael didn't recognize, old beyond the oldest Orders, and his eyes—his eyes held the weight of centuries.
"Soul Reaver," the old man said. His voice was dust and echoes. "I have waited a long time to speak with you."
Kael's hand found his knife.
"Who are you?"
The old man smiled, and there was nothing friendly in it.
"I am what remains of the first," he said. "And I have come to tell you that your time is running out."
Behind him, in the Market's deeper shadows, movement flickered. More figures. Dozens of them. The Shadow Court's faction, maybe, or something worse.
Kael's grip tightened on the knife.
"Talk fast," he said.
The old man's smile widened.
"We have so much to discuss, little fragment. So much to discuss."
The shadows closed in.
