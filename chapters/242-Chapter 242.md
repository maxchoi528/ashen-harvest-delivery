The corpse twitched beneath Kael's palm. Warmth fled from the skin in degrees, the flush of recent life surrendering to the familiar cold that preceded extraction. He focused, reaching into the fading echo of the dead man, and pulled.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted "Shadowstep" from the remains. Agility acquired.]
The notification burned across his vision in letters of pale gold. Three fresh kills lay scattered across the alley—cultists who'd ambushed him near the Drowning Quarter. Their leader had possessed something worth taking, and Kael had taken it. The dead didn't need skills.
He rose, wiping his hand on his coat. Steam hissed from a ruptured pipe overhead, mingling with the copper reek of blood and coal dust until the air sat heavy on the tongue. Thornwick never slept, but it whispered differently at this hour—distant factory bells, the clatter of night-shift workers trudging home through fog-choked streets, the perpetual groan of the city's bones settling into mud. Gaslight flickered from a lamp at the alley's mouth, its flame guttering in drafts that carried the stench of the river and something older beneath it. Wet brick gleamed. A rat nosed at the boot of the nearest corpse, whiskers twitching, before scuttling away into darkness.
Movement flickered at the alley's mouth. Kael's hand found his knife before his mind caught up, fingers closing around the bone grip with the ease of long practice.
"Impressive form." A woman's voice, dry as old parchment. "Most Reavers fumble their first harvest. You didn't even flinch."
She stepped into the gaslight. Tall, gaunt, dressed in the tattered remnants of an Order uniform—iron-gray, bearing the broken circle of the Iron Vanguard stitched at the shoulder, the threadwork half-picked as though removed in haste. Her eyes held the same void Kael saw in mirrors. The hollow recognition of another predator, another creature that hunted in the spaces between what the city acknowledged and what it feared.
"Not an Order uniform anymore," Kael said. "Deserted, or executed?"
"Option three." She tilted her head, studying him like a specimen pinned to a board. "They threw me to the dogs when they discovered what I was. Same as they'll do to you, if you're careless."
Kael's grip tightened on the knife. The leather of his glove creaked. "You've been following me."
"Watching. There's a difference." She moved closer, each step deliberate, her boots barely disturbing the puddles she stepped through. "The Shadow Court has taken an interest in your activities. Three harvests in Thornwick this week alone. Bold. Stupid, but bold."
"I'm aware of the Court's reach."
"Are you?" Her smile cracked her gaunt features, splitting dry lips. "They don't send scouts for Reavers they intend to destroy. They send them for Reavers they intend to acquire."
A cold weight settled in Kael's gut, the chill spreading outward through his ribs, his limbs, his fingers. The Shadow Court—the Orders' deniable blade, the knife in the dark that no faction would claim. They'd handled the messy problems no institution could officially acknowledge, and they were very good at making people disappear into that acknowledgment's absence.
"And which category am I?"
"That depends." She stopped ten feet away, close enough to strike, far enough to react. "On whether you're as useful as the reports suggest. On whether you can do what needs doing."
"What needs doing?"
She produced a small crystalline vial from her coat. Soul-essence—expensive, military-grade, glowing with captured potential that cast shifting blue light across the wet brick. She tossed it. Kael caught it reflexively, feeling the thrum of concentrated power against his palm, the warmth of it seeping through leather and into bone.
"Payment. For services rendered."
"I haven't rendered any services."
"Not yet." She gestured toward the three corpses at his feet. "Those cultists you killed? They were mine. Preliminary scouts for a larger operation. The real targets are holed up in the undercroft beneath the old Foundry. Forty-seven cultists, three Awakened lieutenants, and something they're trying to wake."
Kael's thoughts circled the information, turning it, examining it from angles. A trap. A test. A genuine opportunity. All three possibilities carried weight.
"Why tell me this?"
"Because you're going to help me clean it up." Her eyes glittered in the gaslight. "And because when we're done, you're going to have questions. Questions about what you are. About where that power came from."
She turned, coat swirling. "The Foundry. Midnight. Don't be late, and don't be stupid. If you're what I think you are, we have a great deal to discuss."
She vanished into the steam and shadows before Kael could respond. One moment she occupied space and light. The next, the alley held only corpses and fog and the fading warmth of the vial in his grip.
He stood alone, the vial heavy in his palm, the bodies cooling around him. Questions multiplied. The woman knew too much. Spoke of his ability with familiarity that transcended intelligence-gathering, as though she reached into the same dark well he drank from each time he harvested.
She'd called him a Reaver.
Kael had assumed that was his own private nightmare—a name he'd invented to describe his cursed gift in the hours after his execution, when he'd woken on the corpse pile with something new coiled inside him. But she'd used it like a title. Like a classification.
Like something that existed before him.
The Foundry district had been dead for thirty years, killed by the same industrial collapse that gutted Thornwick's lower quarters. Hulking skeletons of brick and iron lined the streets, their windows gaping like empty eye sockets in faces forgotten by the living. Fog curled through the ruins, thick with the reek of rust and stagnant water and old ash that still carried the bitter taste of burned coal. Kael's boots crunched on debris—shattered glass, fragments of machinery, bones he chose not to examine. The gas lamps here had been dark for years, their pipes corroded, their glass shattered. He navigated by the faint glow of the vial in his pocket and the wrongness that pulsed ahead like a second heartbeat.
He approached the main structure as the night deepened. The vial sat in his coat pocket, radiating faint warmth against his ribs. He hadn't drunk it. Couldn't trust the woman enough to consume something she'd given him, regardless of payment offered.
But he hadn't left it behind either.
The building's entrance had been deliberately collapsed, bricks scattered in a pattern too deliberate for natural decay. Fresh tracks marred the mud—boot prints, dozens of them, all leading inward through a gap barely wide enough for a man to squeeze through. Kael studied the tracks. Different sizes, different wear patterns. Real cultists, not constructs. He noted the depth of the impressions, the spacing. They'd been moving with purpose, carrying weight. Supplies, maybe. Or sacrifices.
He entered sideways, knife in one hand, spiritual senses flaring. The Transcendent stage granted him perception beyond the physical. He sensed the residual energy signatures of the cultists, felt the thrum of the seal network deep beneath the city, and—distant, wrong, discordant—a presence that felt like looking into a mirror that showed someone else's face.
She was already inside.
Kael moved through corridors choked with debris and shadow. His footsteps made no sound. The Shadowstep ability pulsed in his newly acquired instincts, promising speed and silence when he needed it most. The walls wept moisture. Rust had eaten through old pipes, leaving jagged teeth of metal jutting from brick at odd angles. The air grew thicker as he descended, warmer, carrying the stench of old blood and incense and something else—heavy, electric, the taste of it sitting on the back of his tongue like copper.
Voices echoed from ahead. Raised, frantic. The cadence of ritual chanting that built upon itself in layers, each repetition louder, faster, the syllables blurring together into a wall of sound.
He found a service hatch overlooking the central forge—a vast chamber where massive iron cauldrons once glowed with molten steel. Now the space served darker purposes. Cultists in tattered robes circled a central pit, their chanting building toward crescendo. The sound pressed against Kael's ears, his skull, his teeth. Three figures in ornate armor stood apart, directing the proceedings with sharp gestures.
And in the pit itself, bound with chains of inscribed silver, knelt a figure whose spiritual energy blazed like a dying star.
Awakened. Captured, drained, prepared for sacrifice.
The Orders taught that Awakened were sacred—vessels of humanity's defense against the Abyss. Kael didn't care about sacred doctrine. He cared that the cult was doing something that would draw attention, and attention meant pursuers, and pursuers meant someone might track the spiritual disturbances back to the alley where three corpses still steamed. Still, the figure in the pit was a resource. The thought arrived cold and clean. An Awakened, brimming with energy. If they died, that energy would dissipate unless someone harvested it.
He filed the thought away and focused on the situation at hand.
The woman appeared at the pit's edge, stepping from shadows that shouldn't have contained her. The cultists didn't notice. Their chanting reached fever pitch.
"Stop the ritual," she called out, voice cutting through the noise. "I've brought the seventh."
The lead lieutenant spun. Recognition flickered across his face—then shock as he registered Kael's position on the gallery above.
"Kill them both!" He raised his staff, arcane energy crackling at its tip. "The ritual proceeds regardless!"
Cultists broke from the circle, rushing toward both intruders. Kael activated Shadowstep.
The world blurred. His body flowed between attacks like water through fingers, knife finding throats and hearts with mechanical precision. The ability wasn't just speed—it was absence, a brief dissolution into the spaces between moments that let him pass through danger. A blade swung where he'd stood and found only air. His knife opened a throat. Blood sprayed hot across his knuckles. Another cultist lunged. He stepped through the lunge, felt the passage of the man's arm against his shoulder like a memory, and drove his blade up beneath the ribs.
Three cultists surrounded him. He ducked a blade, hamstrung its wielder, used the dying man's body as a springboard to vault over the second. His knife took the third through the eye socket, the sound of it wet and final. He landed in a crouch, already moving toward the next threat, his breath steady despite the carnage.
Below, the woman fought differently. She moved like smoke, each gesture precise and economical. When she touched a cultist, that cultist stopped—collapsed like a puppet with cut strings. Not dead. Harvested. Drained of everything that made them function while their eyes still tracked, still watched, still understood what had been done to them.
Kael's throat tightened. He harvested corpses, taking what remained after life departed. She harvested the living, stripping them down while they still breathed.
The three lieutenants intervened together. Their combined assault drove the woman back, forcing her into defensive positions around the pit. Kael saw his opening—the ritual continued unchecked, the central figure now screaming as chains glowed red-hot with channeled power. The sound of that screaming cut through the chanting, through the clash of steel, through everything.
He dove.
The fall was long. Shadowstep elongated the moment, let him adjust his trajectory mid-air, land on the pit's edge without breaking anything. The chains blazed against his skin, heat that spoke of spiritual energy compressed and weaponized. The air around the pit shimmered, distorted. His lungs burned.
Kael grabbed the nearest chain. Pulled. The silver resisted, inscribed with binding sigils that fought his touch. But his ability operated on a level that ritual constructs couldn't anticipate. He pushed, and the sigils shattered.
One chain broken. Two remaining.
The lead lieutenant abandoned the woman, hurling a bolt of concentrated force that caught Kael in the shoulder. Pain detonated through his arm, his collarbone, his spine. He staggered but held his position, tearing at the second chain while cultists milled above, too panicked by the woman's approach to remember they outnumbered her.
"Stop him!" The lieutenant's voice cracked with desperation. "The Unbinding proceeds regardless of casualties! If we lose the vessel—"
Kael's fingers found the second chain's weak point. He pulled, feeling the silver stretch, the sigils scream, the binding release. The metal snapped. The young Awakened slumped forward, caught only by Kael's grip on their shoulders.
"Can you walk?"
"I—I don't know. They took so much—"
"Find out."
He hauled the victim toward a service ladder while the lieutenant screamed orders. An arrow zipped past his head, close enough that he felt the wind of its passage. A thrown knife carved a line across his thigh. Blood ran warm down his leg.
The woman appeared beside him, covered in blood that wasn't hers. "Leave the vessel. We need to run."
"I don't—"
"Leave. Them." She grabbed his arm, fingers digging in with bruising force. "You don't understand what's happening. The Unbinding isn't just ritual. It's a beacon. When it completes, it calls things that don't appreciate being summoned."
Above the pit, reality began to tear.
Kael saw it happen—saw the air open like a wound, saw darkness bleed through the gap, saw something vast and terrible press against the boundary between worlds. The Veil, weakening. A rift, forming. The edges of it crackled with energy that made his teeth ache and his eyes water.
The cultists cheered. The lieutenants fell to their knees in supplication.
The woman pulled Kael toward the exit. "Move. We can stop the Unbinding from here, but not if we're inside the blast radius when it tears fully open."
"The victim—"
"Crawling toward the ladder. Look." Her voice hardened. "We get out. We regroup. We deal with the consequences. Or we die here, and the rift swallows half of Thornwick with us."
Kael looked back. The young Awakened had managed to reach the ladder, hauling themselves up rung by agonizing rung. Behind them, the tear in reality widened, darkness whispering promises of annihilation in a language that bypassed the ears and spoke directly to the marrow.
He ran.
They emerged into fog-choked streets, the city's night sounds muffled by distant thunder that wasn't thunder. Behind them, the Foundry groaned. Cracks spiderwebbed across ancient brick. The rift's pull bent light, distorted sound, made the air taste of copper and wrong. Kael's wounds throbbed. His reserves had run dry, the energy he'd channeled through the chains leaving him hollow and shaking.
The woman didn't stop until they'd put three blocks between themselves and the disaster. Kael matched her pace despite his wounds, despite the exhaustion that sat on his shoulders.
"What was that?" he finally demanded.
"The Unbinding. A ritual to crack the Veil without bothering with the seals." She found a relatively stable wall, leaned against it. "Crude, but effective. The cult's been running variations for months. Tonight's attempt was the most powerful."
"The thing in the rift—"
"Didn't fully manifest. The ritual failed before completion. But it left a wound." She met his eyes. "A small rift. The Orders will have felt it. They'll mobilize within hours."
"Then we need to—"
"We need to disappear. Both of us." She pulled a small notebook from her coat, flipped to a marked page. "The Shadow Court wanted the cult disrupted. That's done. What comes next is the hard part."
She handed him the notebook. Inside, sketched in careful detail, was a diagram of the Soul Reaver's extraction process. The same technique he used. The same theory, refined and expanded into annotations that crowded every margin.
"Where did you get this?"
"Same place you got your power." Her smile returned, colder now. "From someone who had it before."
Kael's blood turned to ice water. His fingers went numb around the notebook's spine. "That's not possible. I was born with this ability. It manifested after—"
"After your execution. When your soul should have departed for the Abyss." She reached up, traced a scar along her jawline. Her fingertip came away clean. "Same thing happened to me. Years ago. The Order of Dawn tried to execute me for crimes I didn't commit. I woke up on the corpse pile with something new inside me."
"Who are you?"
"Someone who's been hunting answers longer than you've been alive." She pushed off the wall, steadying herself. "You're not the only one carrying this. And you're not the first."
"That doesn't make sense. I've never met anyone else like—"
"You have now." She extended her hand. "My name is Vesper."
Kael stared at her hand. The implications cascaded through his mind, each one worse than the last. Others. Before him. Carrying the same curse, the same hunger, the same voice that whispered from the place where souls went when they left their bodies.
"Why tell me this?"
"Because the Shadow Court is hunting Reavers. Because the cult is learning to replicate our abilities without paying the price. Because the seals are failing and someone needs to stand in the gap." Her expression shifted, the hard lines around her mouth deepening. "And because the Court doesn't just hunt. They collect. They've taken two that I know of. I don't intend to be the third."
Distant alarms began to wail. The Orders mobilizing.
"The notebook contains what I know about controlling the harvest. How to refine extraction. How to access deeper memories." She stepped back. "Read it. Decide what you want to do. But decide quickly—the next few days will determine whether Thornwick survives what's coming."
She moved to leave, then paused.
"One more thing. The cult didn't just try to open a rift tonight. They were testing something. Someone." Her eyes glittered in the gaslight. "They were testing you, Kael. To see if you were real."
"Real?"
"A true Soul Reaver. Not a copy, not a construct, not a failed experiment." She smiled, and the expression held no warmth. "Congratulations. You passed."
She vanished into the fog before he could respond.
Kael stood alone in the ruined street, notebook clutched in bloodied hands, the distant wail of alarms growing louder. The wound in the sky behind him pulsed with wrongness. The Orders would descend soon—investigators, Inquisitors, hunters trained to track spiritual anomalies. Every cultist corpse in the Foundry would carry traces of his energy, and the energy of the woman who'd vanished.
He opened the notebook. The first page bore a single line, written in ink that shifted between colors as the gaslight caught it:
*"The Abyss does not take. The Abyss only borrows. And debts, my child, always come due."*
The fog swallowed him as he read, as the night deepened, as the consequences of tonight's harvest began to spiral beyond any control he might have exercised. His thigh wound had stopped bleeding, the blood crusting against his trouser leg. His shoulder throbbed where the lieutenant's bolt had struck. His fingers ached from gripping the chains. The vial of soul-essence sat heavy in his pocket, unused, its warmth a constant reminder that the woman had paid him for services he hadn't agreed to render.
And somewhere in the city, she moved through shadows, carrying knowledge of what he was, knowledge she'd chosen to share in fragments—enough to hook him, not enough to free him.
He turned a page. The second bore a diagram he recognized, the extraction process rendered in precise ink strokes. Annotations crowded the margins in handwriting that slanted hard to the right. Notes on timing. On resonance. On what happened when you harvested a soul still clinging to its body. The ink changed color three times across the page, as though written over days or weeks, in different moods, under different lights.
A third page. A fourth. The notebook was dense with information, and Kael's fingers trembled as he turned each one. Not from cold. From recognition.
These were his techniques. His private refinements, the adjustments he'd made through trial and error over years of harvesting, documented by someone else's hand and expanded into territory he hadn't yet explored.
The footsteps came without warning. Measured, deliberate, belonging to neither Order nor cult nor the woman called Vesper. They stopped ten paces behind where Kael stood reading.
The stranger spoke a single word, barely audible over the rising wind:
"Finally."
The footsteps resumed, circling closer.
