The blood was still warm when Kael found the body.
He crouched beside the fallen cultist, fingers hovering above the torn robe. Steam hissed from ruptured pipes overhead, mingling with the copper tang filling the narrow alley. Somewhere distant, a factory whistle screamed its midday note.
"Three minutes," Kael muttered. "Maybe four."
He checked the street behind him. Empty. The blast had drawn everyone away—smart of them. Whatever had detonated here had left more than scorch marks on the cobblestones. A sigil, half-scorched into the stone, pulsed with residual light. Not light. Something else. Something that made his teeth ache and his left eye twitch.
The Soul Reaver stirred.
Not in his chest. Deeper. In whatever passed for his soul these days, that twisted thing he'd been born with or cursed into or both. The Reaver didn't care about philosophy. It cared about what lay before him—the cooling corpse of someone who had died badly and left something valuable behind.
Kael pressed his palm flat against the cultist's chest.
The harvest hit him like ice water flooding his veins. Not painful, exactly. More like the world contracting to a single point of sensation as the Reaver reached out, grabbed hold of what remained, and pulled.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted skill-fragment from the remains. *Shadow Meld* acquired.]
Information poured through him unbidden. A memory surfaced: the cultist, hood up, hands shaking as he drew the sigil. Another: running through these same streets, heart pounding, knowing something was hunting him. And beneath both, a cold certainty—something was coming. Something that would make today's casualties look like rounding errors.
Kael pulled his hand back. His fingers trembled.
"Shadow Meld," he whispered.
The skill sat in his mind like a new muscle, waiting to be used. He could feel its edges—how to flex it, how to direct it, how long it would last before burning out. An infiltration tool. Something for moving unseen, unheard, slipping between the cracks of perception.
"Useful," he admitted.
The sigil on the ground pulsed again. Kael studied it, crouching low. He'd seen similar marks in the ruins outside the city walls—older ones, faded to near-invisibility. The Order of Dawn had dismissed them as "residual corruption." An easy answer. A lazy one.
This was fresh. This was organized.
Kael straightened, scanning the rooftops. Nothing moved except steam and the lazy drift of ash. He needed to move. The cultists who had fled the blast would return, or worse, reinforcements would arrive. Staying in the open was stupidity wearing a death wish.
He chose a direction and started walking.
---
The Underworld had many names among Thornwick's criminal underclass. The Veiled Market called it the Below. The Iron Vanguard's intelligence reports labeled it Substrate Zone C. The common folk—the spiritually inert majority who made up the city's beating heart— called it "down there" and left it at that.
Kael called it useful.
The entrance he'd discovered three weeks ago led through a condemned textile mill, down through the old drainage system, and eventually into a network of tunnels that predated Thornwick's founding by at least three centuries. Stone walls gave way to older stone, then to something that wasn't stone at all—dark material that seemed to drink in his lantern light than reflect it.
He walked for an hour before encountering the first checkpoint.
Two men in leather armor, armed with crossbows that hummed with faint spiritual signatures. Not Awakened—too expensive to arm the rank and file with enhanced weapons—but trained. Professional.
"Halt," one of them ordered. "State your—"
Kael's shadow rippled. Shadow Meld activated without conscious thought, sliding him into the darkness between the lantern's reach and the checkpoint's view. He moved past them in three strides, their conversation continuing as though nothing had passed.
"—told you, the meeting's at the third junction."
"Like I give a damn. I just want my payment and out."
Kael smiled in the darkness. Shadow Meld made him invisible to casual observation, but he could still hear. Still watch. The skill didn't create true invisibility—just a gap in perception that most people couldn't cross without spiritual sensitivity.
He filed away the information and continued deeper.
The tunnel opened into a cavern that shouldn't exist. Massive support columns, carved with symbols that predated the current language, held up a ceiling lost in shadow. Vendors lined the edges—not of goods, but of services. Information. Weapons. Things in jars that pulsed with sickly light.
And at the center, a stage.
Kael found a shadowed alcove and settled in to watch. Shadow Meld let him hold the concealment indefinitely, though he could feel the drain on something else—not spiritual energy, since the Reaver didn't require that, but stamina. Focus. The skill wanted him to stay still, stay quiet, stay unnoticed.
The auctioneer, a gaunt figure in gray robes, struck his podium.
"Lot forty-seven. Former member of the Order of Dawn's auxiliary corps. Abyssal exposure confirmed. Starting bid: five soul vials."
Kael's blood chilled.
A cage was wheeled onto the stage. Inside, a woman hung from chains—young, barely past twenty, with the vacant stare of someone who had seen too much. Spiritual burns covered her arms, the telltale marks of someone who had touched something from beyond the Veil and paid the price.
"Her knowledge of Dawn patrol routes is current," the auctioneer continued. "Her breaking was... Thorough. Starting bid: five soul vials."
Hands rose throughout the cavern. Bids came fast and clinical. This wasn't slavery in the traditional sense—not anymore. These were sources being bought and sold. Minds picked apart for information, then discarded.
Kael's grip tightened until his knuckles ached.
He could intervene. The auction house had no Awakened protection that he could sense. Two dozen armed guards, but none above Awakened stage one. He could kill them all, free the woman, burn this place to the foundations.
But he was one person. And this wasn't the only auction house in the Below.
The woman's gaze drifted across the cavern, passing over his hiding spot without stopping. She was spiritually inert now—whatever the Abyssal exposure had done to her, it had burned away her abilities. She couldn't see him even if she looked directly at him.
Still, something in her expression made him pause.
Recognition? No. Not of him. Of this place. Of what happened here. She knew the score. Knew she was already dead, just waiting for the paperwork to finish.
The bid reached twelve vials. Fifteen. Twenty.
Kael turned away.
He couldn't save her. Couldn't save any of them—not without more power, more resources, more time. The Below was a symptom, not a disease. Cutting off one head only meant two more would grow.
First, he needed information. Then strength. Then a way to strike at the roots instead of the branches.
---
The third junction led to a chamber that smelled of ozone and blood.
Kael emerged from Shadow Meld's concealment as the skill faded, his stamina protesting the extended use. The cavern before him was a workshop of some kind—surgical tables, alchemical apparatus, and rows of glass cylinders containing things he refused to examine closely.
And at the center, a Steam Wraith.
Not the mechanical constructs the Iron Vanguard deployed for heavy labor. This thing was different. Humanoid in shape, but wrong—its body was a shell of metal plates and spinning gears, but within the gaps, steam seethed like trapped clouds. A face, if it could be called that, stared out from the central cavity: a mask of burnished copper with two holes where eyes should be.
The Wraith turned toward him.
Its voice was the sound of a thousand valves releasing at once.
"YOU ARE NOT EXPECTED."
Kael drew his blade. Not the ceremonial trash the city watch carried, but something older—a weapon he'd taken from a cultist's corpse two months ago. The steel hummed with faint spiritual resonance, nothing special, but better than fighting empty-handed.
"You're right," he replied. "I wasn't planning on crashing this party."
"SPEAK NOT. YOU WILL BE PROCESSED."
The Wraith moved with unnatural speed, gears screaming as it crossed the distance between them. Kael sidestepped, blade carving a line across its torso, but the steel sparked against metal plating without penetrating. Inside, the steam churned, undeterred.
He dodged the return strike—Loss. No—Loss wasn't right. Something else.
"SHADOW TOUCHED," the Wraith observed. "YOU CARRY THE REAVER'S STAIN."
Kael rolled behind a surgical table as a gear-studded fist punched through the space where he'd stood. The table exploded into splinters. He came up swinging, targeting the joints where metal met metal, but the Wraith anticipated him. A steam vent opened in its forearm, scalding vapor jetting into his face.
He barely got his arm up in time. The pain was immediate—skin blistering where the steam touched—but he forced through it, drove his blade into the Wraith's knee joint.
The mechanism ground, shrieked, and held.
"IMPRESSIVE," the Wraith admitted. "BUT IRRELEVANT. YOU CANNOT KILL WHAT HAS NO LIFE TO END."
Kael twisted his blade, searching for something vital. The Wraith was an animated construct—no soul to speak of, just spirit energy shaped into a purpose and left to run. Destroying it would require dispersing that energy or finding its core.
"Correct," Kael said. "But I don't need to kill you."
He released his blade and stepped into the Wraith's reach.
The construct's hand closed around his throat, lifting him off the ground. Steam hissed from its joints, building pressure. Another few seconds and it would boil him alive from the inside out.
Instead, Kael reached out with the Soul Reaver.
Not harvesting. Not extracting. Just... Touching. The Reaver didn't care whether its target was living or dead. It cared about spiritual energy, about the essence of being. And the Steam Wraith had plenty of that—enough to fill its metal shell, enough to animate it, enough to give it a kind of pseudo-life.
The connection flared.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted spiritual energy from construct. *Steam Core (depleted)* acquired.]
The Wraith convulsed. Steam vented from every aperture at once, its pressure dropping catastrophically. Kael dropped to the ground, gasping, his throat already bruising. The construct stumbled backward, movements sluggish, joints grinding without the power to smooth them.
"SABotage," the Wraith rasped. "You... You are not... The first to..."
It collapsed.
Kael retrieved his blade, breathing hard. The wound on his arm throbbed, steam burns spreading red inflammation across his skin. He'd need treatment—soon, before infection set in. The Below wasn't known for its medical facilities.
But first, the corpse.
The Wraith was a construct, not a creature. Its body wouldn't yield a skill like a human's would. But, the Soul Reaver had extracted something: Steam Core, a fragment of the energy matrix that powered this thing. Useless to him directly, but potentially valuable. Information, at minimum. Maybe more.
He filed it away and turned to search the workshop.
---
The cultists had been busy.
Kael found their records in a locked cabinet—physically locked, not spiritually. Crude security for information this sensitive. Either they didn't expect anyone to reach this deep into the Below, or they didn't expect anyone who reached this far to be stopped by mere metal.
He flipped through the documents, scanning quickly. Names, dates, locations. Ritual schedules. And underneath it all, a pattern he recognized from his own nightmares.
The seals were failing.
Not gradually. Not at the predicted rate. The data showed acceleration—a massive spike in Abyssal energy detected at multiple monitoring points across Thornwick's district. The cultists weren't waiting for the seals to break naturally. They were actively weakening them.
Kael's stomach dropped.
"How many?" he muttered. "How many rifts are you targeting?"
No answer. The documents didn't say. But the scope was staggering—dozens of sites across the city, all being worked simultaneously. A coordinated campaign to breach the seal network.
The Veiled Market made sense. The auctions. The trafficking in stolen Awakened and their knowledge. It wasn't greed. It was logistics. The cultists were building resources—funding, information, tools—for something massive.
And they were close to ready.
Kael photographed the documents in his mind, memorizing what he could, then replaced them exactly as he'd found them. The cultists would know someone had been here eventually, but that couldn't be helped. The information was too valuable to ignore.
He moved through the workshop's back exit, descending deeper into the tunnel network. The air grew colder as he traveled, the stone walls slick with moisture. No lanterns here. The darkness was absolute.
His eyes adjusted anyway. Ascendant stage granted certain perks, including enhanced low-light vision. Not seeing in true darkness, but close enough to navigate without stumbling into pitfalls.
The tunnel opened into a vast space that his eyes couldn't map.
Kael stopped at the threshold, every instinct screaming warning. Something waited in the darkness ahead. Something big. Something that felt like pressure against his mind, pushing in from all sides.
"You're a long way from home, Reaver."
The voice came from everywhere and nowhere. Female, cultured, edged with amusement.
Kael drew his blade. "Show yourself."
A figure emerged from the darkness—tall, gaunt, wrapped in robes that seemed woven from shadow. A mask covered the lower half of her face, leaving her eyes exposed. They were wrong. Too bright. Too aware.
"The Shadow Court has taken an interest in you," she said. "You're becoming... Inconvenient."
Kael's grip tightened. "Shadow Court?"
"We prefer to work in the margins. Quiet operations, deniable assets, clean extractions. You, But, have developed a habit of disrupting carefully constructed plans."
She stepped closer. Kael tracked her movement, blade ready, but something held him back. An instinct. A warning from the Reaver itself.
"You killed our asset in the textile district," she continued. "You disrupted the auction house—unacceptable, by the way. Three months of preparation, down the drain. And now you've found the workshop." She tilted her head, studying him like a specimen. "Impressive. For someone at your stage."
"I'm flattered."
"You shouldn't be. You've drawn attention from parties far more dangerous than the Shadow Court. Inquisitor Vane, for instance. He's been asking questions about you. Very thorough questions."
Kael's blood chilled. "Vane?"
"The Order of Dawn's chief hunter. Stage three. Specializes in tracking... Unique individuals. Soul Reavers, for instance." The masked woman's lips curved beneath her mask. "He's not the only one. You've attracted more attention in six months than most Reavers collect in a decade. Whatever you're planning, he's determined to stop it."
"And you're telling me this why?"
"Because we're offering you a choice. Join us—formally, this time—and that attention goes away. Refuse, and you're on your own against everyone. The Shadow Court has resources. Connections. We can protect you from Vane, from the other Orders, from whoever ordered the purge of your squad."
Kael went still. "What do you know about my squad?"
"We know everything. The ambush. The survivors. The ones who were supposed to die but didn't." She spread her hands. "Join us, and we'll tell you who gave the order. Refuse, and we make sure Vane finds you before the week is out."
The darkness pressed closer. Kael's blade hummed in his grip, eager for a target, but his mind raced faster than his heart.
The Shadow Court wanted to control him. Use him. Turn the Soul Reaver into a weapon they could aim at their enemies.
But they had information he needed. The identity behind the ambush. The reason his squad had been marked for death.
"Come find me when you've decided," the woman said. "The offer expires in three days. After that..."
She stepped backward into the darkness and vanished.
Kael stood alone in the tunnel, his burns aching, his blade dripping with Wraith residue, and the weight of impossible choices pressing down on his shoulders.
Three days.
He had three days to find another way.
---
The climb back to the surface took two hours. By the time Kael emerged into the condemned textile mill, dawn light was filtering through the cracked roof. His arm throbbed with every movement, the steam burns spreading despite his attempts to clean them.
He needed a healer. A real one, not the back-alley surgeons who served the Below's population.
He needed information. About Vane. About the cult's operations. About whoever had ordered his squad's death.
He needed to move carefully. The Shadow Court's offer might be a trap, but their information was probably accurate. Vane was hunting him. The other Orders would follow once the Inquisitor's Trail heated up.
Kael limped toward the mill's exit, mind churning through options.
Then footsteps echoed from the doorway ahead.
A figure stood silhouetted against the morning light. Tall, broad-shouldered, wearing the crimson and gold of the Order of Dawn. A blade hung at his hip, and his hand rested on the pommel with casual familiarity.
"You're Ashborn," the man said. Not a question. "Kael Ashborn."
Kael stopped. His blade was sheathed, his stance non-threatening, but his body coiled with readiness. "Who's asking?"
The figure stepped forward, letting the light fall across his features. Young—mid-twenties—with a scar running from his left temple to his jaw. Steel-gray eyes, hard and assessing.
"Call me Aldric," he said. "I'm a friend of Gwen's."
The name hit Kael like a physical blow. Gwen. His squad's sergeant. The woman who had died screaming in the ambush, trying to buy time for the rest of them to escape.
Except Aldric said *friend*. Past tense.
"She talked about you," Aldric continued. "Before she died. Said you were stubborn, reckless, and too smart for your own good. Also said if anything ever happened to her, I should find you and make sure you didn't get yourself killed."
Kael's throat tightened. "She didn't mention you."
"We hadn't... We weren't on speaking terms. Not since I left the Order." Aldric's jaw tightened. "I've spent the last six months tracking down who ordered the ambush. Finally got a name. But I can't take them alone."
"Neither can I."
"That's why I'm here." Aldric held out his hand. "Partners?"
Kael looked at the offered hand, then at the man behind it. Former Order of Dawn, if he was telling the truth. Probably capable. Definitely dangerous.
And possibly a trap.
But Gwen had trusted him once. That had to count for something.
"Partners," Kael said, clasping the hand. "But if you betray me, the last thing you see will be your own spine."
Aldric smiled grimly. "Fair enough. Now, we need to move. Inquisitor Vane is two blocks east, and he's bringing a full hunting party."
Kael's blood turned to ice. "Then let's go."
They ran.
Behind them, the city of Thornwick woke to another gray morning, unaware of the war brewing in its shadows. The seals weakened. The cultists gathered. And somewhere in the darkness, the Whisper King smiled at the chaos to come.
Kael Ashborn had three days to find an alliance, unmask a traitor, and survive an Inquisitor's hunt—all while the world itself prepared to crack open.
No pressure.
