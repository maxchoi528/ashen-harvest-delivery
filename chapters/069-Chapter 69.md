"I grabbed what the corpse had left. Memory fragmentation—happens when death comes fast." He rose, ignoring the protest from muscles still humming with combat readiness. "The ritual chamber is somewhere underground. Central district, maybe. I couldn't get specifics."
Lyra studied him for a moment, her expression unreadable. Many people looked at Soul Reavers with horror, with fear, with the particular kind of revulsion reserved for those who violated natural law. Lyra looked at him like he was a weapon she'd decided to carry.
"Abyssal Sense could be useful," she admitted. "If the cult is summoning Entities or using Abyssal artifacts, you'd sense them before they could spring a trap."

They moved through Fogmire District's tangle of alleys and converted warehouses. Fog curled between buildings like something alive, carrying the stench of the river and the ever-present coal smoke from the industrial sectors. Gas lamps cast pools of sickly yellow light at irregular intervals, leaving stretches of shadow between.
Kael concentrated on the new ability, trying to parse what he was sensing. The world looked the same—cobblestones, brick walls, puddles reflecting lamplight—but beneath the surface, something else churned. Faint currents of energy that he hadn't noticed before. Where an Awakened passed, the energy stirred differently than where ordinary people moved. And somewhere to the east, a wrongness that made his newly sensitized nerves itch. Entity energy, maybe. Or something touched by the Abyss.
"Got something," he murmured.
Lyra's hand moved to her blade. "Cultists?"
"Not sure. Could be anything." He tilted his head, trying to narrow the sensation. It felt like standing too close to a fire—heat on one side of his face, nothing on the other. "East. Maybe three blocks."
"Investigation or avoidance?"
Kael considered. The last cultist had been alone, but that didn't mean they operated solo. And if they had a ritual site in central Thornwick, they might be preparing something big. The thought of Zarek—wherever he was—moving forward with whatever design the cult was executing didn't sit well.
But it also didn't sit well to stumble into an unknown situation with an injury still bleeding through his coat.
"First we find somewhere to stop," he said. "I need to check my injuries properly, and I'd not do it in the open. Then we investigate."
Lyra nodded and took point. She navigated the district's maze with the ease of someone who'd memorized every turn. Kael followed, his attention split between watching for threats and cataloging the new sensations flooding his awareness.
A street vendor selling roasted chestnuts—the energy signature of an ordinary person, nothing special. Two men arguing outside a gin house—same baseline humanity, slightly elevated because of the alcohol. A woman in expensive clothing hurrying past with a security detail—Awakened, definitely, her spiritual energy a bright spark compared to the civilians around her.
And then, as they turned down a narrow passage between a defunct textile factory and a tenement building, something else. A pulse of darkness that made Kael stop mid-stride.
"Kael?" Lyra noticed immediately.
He held up a hand, listening to his instincts. The Abyssal Sense painted pictures in his mind now—not visual, but present nonetheless. There was something in this passage. Something that didn't belong. The energy signature felt like looking at a hole cut into reality itself.
"Stay alert," he breathed. "Something's here."
Lyra's blade cleared its sheath in a whisper of oiled steel. They advanced slowly, Kael leading, his newly acquired senses reaching ahead like invisible fingers. The passage narrowed. Brick walls pressed close on either side, slick with condensation and something darker that might have been old blood.
Ten feet ahead, the passage opened into a small courtyard. A single gas lamp flickered overhead, casting more shadow than light. And in the center of that courtyard stood a doorway that Kael was certain hadn't been there when they entered.
The door was black. Not painted black—black like the absence of everything, like looking into a space where light refused to exist. It stood eight feet tall and three feet wide, its edges perfectly straight despite the decay surrounding it. No handle, no hinges, no visible mechanism.
And it was pulling at him. Not physically—spiritually. Something behind that door wanted what he carried.
"Abyssal construction," Kael said quietly. "It's a temporary rift. They must be using artifacts to punch small holes through the Veil."
Lyra's face had gone pale. "The cult is opening rifts in the middle of Thornwick?"
"Not permanent ones. This looks like a shortcut—a way to move through the Abyss without fully crossing into it." He approached the door slowly, his Abyssal Sense screaming warnings with every step. The darkness pulsed, hungry and patient. "It's stable for now, but it won't last. When it collapses, everything on the other side comes here."
"What do we do?"
Kael considered the options. He could ignore it—leave and hope someone else dealt with the problem. He could try to close it, though he had no idea if he possessed the ability. Or he could step through and see what waited on the other side.
The latter option carried obvious risks. But if the cult was using these doorways to move operatives or materials, understanding their construction could prove invaluable.
"I want to look," he said. "Just a glance. I need to understand what we're dealing with."
Lyra grabbed his arm. "If you go through, I'm coming with you."
"I know." He almost smiled. "That's why I'm not going alone."
They approached the door together. The pull intensified as they drew near, a suction that seemed to reach for his soul. Kael could feel the cold radiating from the black surface, a chill that had nothing to do with temperature.
He reached out and touched the door.
The world inverted.
Kael gasped as the courtyard vanished and reappeared twisted, the gas lamp now hanging overhead and below him at the same time. He stood on a platform of black stone that stretched in all directions, surrounded by a sky that wasn't a sky—more like the inside of an infinite wound, bleeding colors that had no names.
Lyra stood beside him, her blade raised, her face a mask of controlled terror.
The Abyssal plane. Kael had heard about it from other Awakened, from texts that warned against direct contact with the Abyss. This was the realm the 72 Entities called home, the dimension that pressed against the Material world like a vast ocean against a seawall.
And standing a hundred feet away, watching them with eyes that burned like dying stars, was something that definitely wasn't human.
The creature rose thirty feet into the non-air, a mass of writhing shadows and angles that shouldn't exist. It had no fixed form—one moment it resembled a great serpent, the next a twisted mockery of human anatomy, the next something with too many limbs and not enough surfaces. Its presence made Kael's teeth ache and his vision blur at the edges.
"A Soul Reaver," the thing said. Its voice came from everywhere and nowhere, vibrating in Kael's chest like a second heartbeat. "I can taste the stolen souls on you. How delicious. How very, very delicious."
Kael's hand found his own blade. The Abyssal Sense told him this creature was nowhere near as powerful as the Entities sealed at the Great Rift, but it was still an Abyssal being, still capable of things that would break a mortal mind.
"I'm just passing through," Kael said. His voice came out steadier than he felt. "We saw your door. Wanted to understand."
"Understand." The creature's laughter was like glass breaking in reverse. "Mortals always want to understand. You build walls to keep us out, then dig tunnels to see what's inside. Tell me, little thief—did the first Soul Reaver understand when he made his bargain?"
The first Soul Reaver. Kael's mind raced. There were stories about the origin of Soul Reaving, but nothing concrete. Most people believed it was an ancient ability, one of the original gifts from the Entities during the Abyssal Wars. Others whispered that a specific person had invented it, had found a way to steal what should never be taken.
"I don't know anything about that," Kael said. It wasn't a lie. "And I don't have time for a history lesson. If this is your territory, we'll leave."
"Leave?" The creature drifted closer. Up close, its features shifted faster, cycling through expressions that belonged to people who had probably died screaming. "You stepped through my door. You taste of stolen death and forbidden harvests. Do you think I would let you leave?"
"I think," Kael said slowly, "that if you wanted to attack us, you would have done it already."
The creature's burning eyes narrowed. "Clever. You are clever, aren't you? You sense my weakness. This place—this little bubble of stolen reality—holds me as much as it holds you. I cannot touch the Material plane directly without anchors, without vessels. The cultists bring me sacrifices, but it is never enough."
Cultists. Sacrifices. Kael filed the information away. This was what the cult was doing—summoning Abyssal entities and feeding them. For what purpose? To weaken the Veil? To gain power? Both?
"The cult serves someone called Zarek," Kael said. "Do you know them?"
The creature laughed again, but this time there was genuine amusement in it. "Zarek is a tool. A puppet dancing on strings held by one far older, far hungrier. You think this is about politics? About territorial control? Child, this is about survival. The seals fail. The Veil tears. And when the 72 wake fully, the Material world becomes ours."

"You should not have come here." The creature's form began to coalesce, pulling itself into something more solid, more dangerous. "Now I know a Soul Reaver walks Thornwick. Now my siblings will know. You have made yourself a target for every Abyssal being in this half of reality."
Lyra moved. Her blade flashed in the non-light, severing one of the creature's reaching appendages before it could touch Kael. The severed piece dissolved into smoke, and the creature screamed—a sound that made reality shudder around them.
"We need to leave," Lyra hissed. "Now."
Kael grabbed her arm and turned toward the doorway they'd entered through. It still stood there, a rectangle of relative sanity in an insane landscape. But as they ran toward it, he could see the edges beginning to fray. The door was collapsing. They had minutes at most.

"There's nothing here but us and that thing."

Forcing his newly acquired awareness through the connection, letting her see through his senses for a few precious seconds. "I can't take from you. But I can show you what I see."

The creature's claw raked across Kael's back, tearing cloth and flesh. Pain exploded along his spine, and he felt blood running down his legs. But they were through. The black void shattered around them, reality rushing back like a wave breaking on rocks.

"I can still sense it," he managed. "The thing. It left a trace on me."

"Stay close," she mouthed.
They descended into the dark.
