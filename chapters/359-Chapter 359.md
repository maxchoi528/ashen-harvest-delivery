The avatar lunged. Kael's blade caught its arm, black ichor spraying across the cobblestones like spilled ink.
"Demigod," the thing hissed, its voice a chorus of drowned screams. "The Reaver who walks among corpses. Do you know how many of my kind have hunted you?"
Kael twisted his sword, driving the avatar back a step. Three cultists circled behind him, their chanting growing louder. The rift behind them pulsed with sickly light. "You've got me confused with someone important."
"No." The avatar smiled with too many teeth. "I have been sent specifically for you."
A scythe of spiritual energy carved through the air. Kael dove left, his boots skidding on wet stone. The attack struck the building behind him, tearing a gash in the brick. Dust and debris rained down.
He landed in a crouch, scanning the alley. Four cultists total, plus the avatar. The rift behind them churned like a wound in reality. Through it, he glimpsed something vast moving in the darkness beyond—something with too many eyes.
The avatar pressed forward, its form flickering between human and nightmare. One moment it wore the face of a young woman, pale and hollow-eyed. The next it became a twisted thing of bone and shadow, limbs jointed in wrong directions.
"You harvest the dead," it said, circling. "You steal what should return to the Abyss. Do you think the Entities will permit this forever?"
"I think you're stalling." Kael adjusted his grip on his sword. The blade hummed, eager. His Soul Reaver senses tingled. The cultists—two Transcendent, two lower. The avatar itself radiated power equivalent to a Sovereign at minimum. "Your friends aren't coming to help, are they?"
The chanting faltered. One cultist's eyes widened.
"They're not your friends," Kael continued, straightening. "They're sacrifices. You needed fresh deaths to stabilize your form here. The chanting, the ritual circle—all theater. You were going to kill them the moment they finished."
The avatar's smile widened. "Clever little Reaver."
The cultists caught on a heartbeat too late. Two screamed as shadows erupted from beneath their feet, dragging them toward the rift. The other two turned to run. The avatar's hand shot out, fingers lengthening into tendrils that pierced their backs and pulled them in.
Kael moved. Not toward the cultists—toward the avatar.
The thing recoiled, caught off guard by his directness. Its scythe-arm swung in a wide arc. Kael ducked under it, driving his blade toward the avatar's center mass. Steel met something harder than bone. The impact shuddered up his arms.
"Not bad," the avatar gurgled. "But I've killed a hundred Demigods."
Kael wrenched his blade free. "And how many of them could harvest you?"
His free hand shot out, fingers closing around the avatar's wrist. The Soul Reaver ability activated on instinct.
Contact.
The notification blazed across his vision:
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 847 units of spiritual energy from the remains. 12 Essence acquired.]
[Soul Harvest: Memory fragment acquired — Entity hunting protocol.]
Kael's mind flooded with alien sensations. Images of endless dark, of hunting through the spaces between worlds, of the satisfaction of consumption. He tasted copper and ash. He heard the whispers of a thousand cultists begging for power.
Then he pulled back, gasping.
The avatar had lost cohesion. Its form wavered, edges blurring like smoke in wind. It shrieked—a sound that cracked windows three streets away.
"What are you?" it demanded, retreating toward the rift. "Reavers cannot harvest Entities. The soul signatures don't match. We're not human."
Kael rolled his shoulder, ignoring the ache. The extracted memories showed him what he needed to know. The avatar was a projection, a fragment of something larger. Killing it here would hurt the Entity but wouldn't stop it. The real threat was the rift behind it—seventy-two feet of tearing reality, growing by the minute.
"Then why," Kael asked, "am I holding part of your soul right now?"
He raised his hand. On his palm, a wisp of black energy coiled like a captured serpent. The avatar screamed again, this time in genuine terror.
"Give that back! That is mine!"
"It's mine now." Kael closed his fist around the fragment. The energy resisted, then surrendered. New information flowed into him—not memories this time, but knowledge. Combat techniques from something that had fought wars before humans learned to make fire. Spatial awareness that let him sense the rift's exact dimensions, its rate of decay, the number of Entities pressing against its edges.
Three. Three Entities were pushing against this single rift.
The cultists' bodies had stopped twitching. The rift pulsed once, twice—then began to close, dragging the avatar's fragmented form backward.
"You've bought yourself nothing," the avatar said, its voice fading as it was pulled into the darkness. "The seals weaken. The Great Rift opens. We will harvest your soul, Reaver, and every other human who stands against us."
"I'll keep that in mind," Kael said, and drove his blade through the closing rift. The steel shrieked as it intersected with the closing barrier. Something on the other side screamed.
The rift sealed. Silence fell.
Kael stood alone in the alley, surrounded by four corpses and the lingering stench of brimstone. His hands wouldn't stop shaking. The extracted combat knowledge settled into his mind like a new muscle, foreign but powerful.
Footsteps echoed from the far end of the alley. Multiple sets, moving with discipline.
"Order of Dawn!" a voice called out. "By authority of the Concordat, you are commanded to surrender!"
Kael looked at the corpses, then at the approaching figures. Six Awakened, all wearing the sunburst insignia of the Order of Dawn. Their leader carried a war-hammer that crackled with dawnfire.
"I just closed your rift," Kael said.
"You also committed four murders." The leader stepped forward, her eyes cold. "We tracked the cultist cell here. Now we find you standing over their bodies with a blade still wet. Explain."
"They were cultists. Abyssal worshippers trying to—"
"We know what they were." She gestured to her squad. "We also know what you are. Soul Reaver Kael Ashborn, also known as the Gravedancer, also known as the Corpse King. You have no legal standing. You have no Order protection. You harvested those cultists, didn't you?"
Kael considered his options. Fighting six Awakened, including what felt like two Sovereigns and four Transcendents, wasn't ideal. His newly acquired knowledge showed him angles of attack, weaknesses he could exploit—but that would mean killing Order members. That crossed lines he tried not to cross.
"They were cultists," he repeated. "Look at their marks. Check their bodies for Entity corruption. I closed the rift. I stopped whatever they were summoning."
"You harvested them." Her voice hardened. "That's illegal. That's—"
"That's effective," Kael cut in. "Your way would have taken hours. They'd have summoned their avatar and torn this district apart. Instead, four cultists are dead, the rift is closed, and zero civilians died. You're welcome."
The leader's jaw tightened. "The Six Orders exist for good reason. The Concordat—"
"Was signed to prevent Order-on-Order warfare. It doesn't cover cultists." Kael sheathed his sword, keeping his hands visible. "I know the law. I lived under it for six years before they framed me for murder and executed me for being different. So unless you're charging me with something specific, I'm going to walk away now."
"You harvested four souls tonight."
"I closed a rift. The souls were going to be devoured by whatever they were summoning anyway." Kael met her gaze. "You want to arrest me for being efficient? Go ahead. But know that the next cultist cell will have three hours more to work before your people arrive. How many deaths is that worth?"
Silence stretched between them.
A younger Dawn knight spoke up. "Captain Vera, the rift signature is completely gone. Not suppressed—closed. That requires power equivalent to a Sovereign's full commitment. He did it alone. He—"
"I know what he did." Captain Vera studied Kael with an unreadable expression. "And I know what he is. A Soul Reaver operating without oversight, harvesting the dead, answering to no one."
"Answering to results," Kael said.
"Results aren't everything." She took a step closer. "The Six Orders have held back the Abyss for a thousand years. We've sacrificed millions to maintain the seals. We've watched our brothers and sisters die to keep the Entities contained. And now you appear—a man who can harvest souls, who can take the power of the fallen, who answers to no code or covenant. Do you understand why we fear you?"
"I understand why you're afraid." Kael's voice softened slightly. "I also understand that three Entities are pushing against that rift right now. It took me four seconds to close it. How long would it have taken your best Sovereign?"
Captain Vera's expression flickered. For a moment, something other than hostility showed through.
"The Shadow Court has been asking about you," she said quietly. "So has the Iron Vanguard. And the Arcane Spire's Research Division has put out feelers. You're becoming known, Reaver. That's not always a good thing."
"Let them ask."
"They're not asking. They're recruiting. Or eliminating. The difference depends on which faction moves first." She glanced at her squad. "We're not arresting you tonight. But not because you closed the rift. Because you're right—those cultists were already dead, spiritually speaking. Harvesting them was..." She paused, choosing her words carefully. "Pragmatic."
Kael nodded slowly. "Pragmatic. I can work with pragmatic."
"Don't." Her eyes hardened again. "Pragmatic gets you through one battle. Principles keep you human through the war. You're standing on a knife's edge, Ashborn. The Abyss wants your soul. The cultists want to use you. The Shadow Court wants to control you. The other Orders want to kill you before you become a bigger threat. You have no allies. No protectors. No one will mourn you when you're gone."
"Plenty of people have tried to kill me already."
"And plenty more will try." She turned away. "Consider aligning with one of the Orders before someone less reasonable than me finds you. The Six exist for a reason. They may be slow, they may be corrupt, but they're the only barrier between humanity and extinction."
She walked away without another word. Her squad followed, casting wary glances back at Kael until they rounded the corner.
Kael stood alone in the alley. The bodies were already attracting rats. In an hour, the city guard would arrive. In two, the Mortician's Guild would claim the corpses for "proper disposal." The entire incident would be recorded, debated in Council meetings, and added to the growing file on the Soul Reaver who operated outside Order authority.
His notification system pinged:
[System Update: Soul Energy reserves increased by 847 units. Current total: 12,453 units.]
[System Update: Memory Fragment (Entity hunting protocol) integrated. New combat technique available: Void Step.]
[System Update: Spatial awareness enhanced. You can now sense rift decay within 500 meters.]
Kael examined the new technique. Void Step allowed short-range teleportation through gaps in reality. Dangerous, energy-intensive, and exactly what he needed for situations like tonight. He could have used it to flank the avatar, to strike from multiple angles, to escape if things went wrong.
He practiced the sensation mentally. A skip, a fold, a blink—and he'd be somewhere else within thirty feet.
Not bad for four corpses.
A whisper brushed against his mind. Not the avatar's voice—something older, more patient.
*Little Reaver. You take so freely. But everything has a cost.*
Kael's hand went to his sword. "Who's there?"
Silence answered. The whisper didn't repeat.
He scanned the alley, the rooftops, the shadows between buildings. Nothing moved. But he felt watched, measured, evaluated by something that operated on scales beyond human comprehension.
The Faceless One's fragment pulsed in his chest. It felt warmer than usual.
Kael left the alley, moving through Thornwick's gaslit streets toward his current base—a condemned warehouse in the industrial district. The steam-powered streetlamps cast orange pools of light. Workers trudged home from the night shift, too tired to notice the blood still staining his coat.
Three blocks from the warehouse, he stopped.
A figure stood in the middle of the street. Tall, gaunt, wrapped in robes that seemed to absorb the lamplight. No insignia marked their affiliation. No weapon was visible. They stood there, waiting.
"Reaver Ashborn," a voice said. Female, cold, precise. "You have a visitor."
Kael's hand drifted to his sword. "You have the wrong person."
"I don't think so." The figure stepped forward, and the streetlamps flickered. "You're the one who closed the Thornwick rift tonight. You're the one the Shadow Court has been watching for months. You're the one carrying a fragment of something ancient and terrible."
"The Shadow Court." Kael kept his stance loose, ready to move. "I was wondering when you'd show up."
"We show up when it serves our purposes." She reached into her robes and withdrew a small vial filled with shimmering liquid. Soul-essence. High quality, if the color was any indication. "A gift. To establish... Rapport."
"I don't accept gifts from people who ambush me in the street."
"This isn't an ambush. An ambush would involve more swords." She tossed the vial. Kael caught it reflexively. "The Shadow Court knows what you are, Ashborn. We know what you can do. We also know you're being hunted by every other faction in Thornwick."
"Word travels fast."
"Word travels at the speed of soul-essence transactions." Her smile didn't reach her eyes. "The Order of Dawn will protect you, but only as long as you serve their interests. The Iron Vanguard will train you, but only if you become their weapon. The Arcane Spire will study you, but only to replicate your abilities. None of them see you as a person. We do."
"Define 'see.'"
"Define 'value.'" She spread her hands. "You harvest souls. You extract power from the dead. You can sense rifts, close them, exploit them. You're a one-man army against the Abyss. But you're also a liability—every Order fears what you might become. We're offering protection. Resources. Information. In exchange, you work with us occasionally. Small tasks. Limited engagements. Nothing that compromises your... Principles."
Kael examined the vial. One hundred gold sovereigns, sitting in his palm. More money than most people saw in a lifetime. "And if I refuse?"
"Then you face the Orders alone. The cultists alone. The Entities alone." She shrugged. "Maybe you're strong enough. Maybe you last another year before something kills you. But you could be stronger. You could have allies. You could understand what you're carrying."
She pointed at his chest. "That fragment in your heart. You don't know what it is. You don't know what it will do. We do. The Shadow Court has records going back to the original Soul Reaver. We know how this story ends. The question is whether you want to hear it."
Kael's grip tightened on the vial. "The original Soul Reaver."
"Every Reaver has an origin. Yours isn't as unique as you think." She began backing away. "Think about our offer. We'll find you again in three days. If you refuse, we'll ensure the Orders know about your... Extracurricular activities. If you accept, you get resources, protection, and answers to questions you're afraid to ask."
She vanished into the shadows between one blink and the next.
Kael stood in the empty street, holding a vial of soul-essence and an ultimatum. The Shadow Court knew about his Soul Reaver abilities. They knew about the Faceless One's fragment. They claimed to have information about the original Reaver.
Everything he'd been trying to learn, handed to him by an organization that operated in shadows and traded in secrets.
Too convenient. Too easy. Too much like a trap.
But traps could be useful. Traps revealed information about the trapper.
He pocketed the vial and continued to the warehouse. Inside, he found his meager belongings untouched. The building's structural damage from last week's Entity attack remained unrepaired. Wind whistled through gaps in the walls, carrying the smell of coal smoke and river water.
Kael sat on a crate, letting the night settle around him. The extracted memories from the avatar's fragments organized themselves in his mind. Combat techniques, spatial awareness, Entity hunting protocols. Knowledge that no human should possess, now living in his head.
His notification system pinged again:
[System Analysis: Spatial awareness detects 3 active rift decay signatures within 500 meters. Locations: Thornwick Docks (primary), Ironmonger's District (secondary), beneath the Cathedral of Dawn (tertiary). All signatures indicate rapid degradation.]
Three rifts. Three points where reality was tearing apart. The seals were weakening faster than the Orders realized.
Kael pulled out the Shadow Court's vial of soul-essence. High-grade. Worth killing for. Worth dying for. The kind of prize that got merchants murdered and nobles corrupted.
Someone had paid a fortune for this. Someone had delivered it to him as a "gift."
The Shadow Court wanted something. They always wanted something.
He set the vial aside and focused on what he'd learned tonight. The Six Orders existed to fight the Abyss. The 72 sealed rifts held back the Entities. The cult activity in Thornwick was organized, not random. And the Shadow Court was watching him, specifically, with interest that bordered on obsession.
The original Soul Reaver. The Shadow Court had records. They knew how his story ended.
Kael pulled out a worn notebook. Inside, he'd been documenting everything—every harvest, every ability, every strange occurrence since his awakening. The pages were filled with questions.
Who created the Soul Reaver ability?
Why was he chosen?
What happened to the previous Reavers?
What did the Faceless One's fragment want?
How many rifts could he close before the Entities adapted?
Tonight added new entries. Void Step. Entity hunting protocols. Knowledge of three weakening rifts. An offer from the Shadow Court that promised answers he desperately wanted.
He wrote quickly, documenting everything while it was fresh. When he finished, exhaustion pressed against his skull. The day's events caught up with him—the fight, the harvest, the confrontation with Captain Vera, the Shadow Court's ambush.
Four corpses converted to power. One avatar wounded and banished. Three rifts identified. One faction recruited him. One faction threatened him. Zero answers to the questions that mattered.
His growth curve pinged:
[Realm: Demigod Stage 5. Progress: 12%. Distance to next stage: 88%.]
Eighteen chapters to breakthrough. Assuming nothing killed him first.
Kael lay back on the crate, staring at the water-stained ceiling. Above him, the city hummed with spiritual energy—some natural, some corrupted, all flowing toward rifts that widened by the hour. The Orders were fighting a holding action. The cultists were accelerating the decay. The Entities pressed against seals that grew thinner every day.
And he stood at the center, a Reaver who could harvest power from the dead, hunted by everyone who feared what he might become.
A whisper brushed against his mind again. Deeper this time. More real.
*Sleep, little one. Dream of what you'll become.*
Kael's eyes snapped open. The warehouse was empty. The shadows held still. But he felt something vast and patient, watching from somewhere between thought and reality.
The Faceless One's fragment pulsed in his chest.
And for just a moment, he glimpsed something—a face made of static and void, eyes like dying stars, a smile that stretched across dimensions.
Then it was gone.
Kael sat up, breathing hard. Sweat soaked his shirt. His hands trembled.
"What are you?" he asked the empty warehouse.
No answer came.
But somewhere in the darkness, something laughed.
He didn't sleep again that night. Instead, he sat in the ruined warehouse, sword across his knees, watching shadows that moved when they shouldn't. The rifts pulsed in his expanded awareness. The Shadow Court's vial sat in his pocket like a bribe he hadn't accepted. The notification system tracked his progress, his harvests, his growth.
At dawn, he made his decision.
Three days until the Shadow Court returned. Three hours until the city's morning traffic filled the streets. Three rifts weakening beneath Thornwick's surface, waiting to tear reality apart.
Kael sheathed his sword and stood. The Demigod realm felt like standing on a cliff's edge—power enough to change things, not enough to control them. The avatar's words echoed in his mind: *We've killed a hundred Demigods.*
But none of those Demigods could harvest the dead.
He moved through the warehouse, gathering supplies. Rope, bandages, a change of clothes. His sword. The vial of soul-essence. The notebook full of questions. Everything he needed to survive, nothing he couldn't afford to lose.
The Faceless One's fragment pulsed once, warm and approving.
Kael stopped. "Are you sentient?"
Silence answered. But the warmth remained.
He shook his head and headed for the door. The Shadow Court wanted to recruit him. The Orders wanted to kill him. The Entities wanted to devour him. And somewhere in Thornwick, three rifts were about to unleash horrors that would consume everything they touched.
Time to make a choice.
Not which faction to join. Not which side to take. Those were political games for people with time to spare.
The choice was simpler: let the rifts tear open and watch Thornwick burn, or close them himself and deal with the consequences.
He chose consequences.
The warehouse door creaked open. Morning light spilled across the threshold. Somewhere across the city, a bell began to toll—Loss or warning, he couldn't tell.
Kael stepped out into the street, toward the first rift signature he'd sensed.
Behind him, in the darkness of the abandoned building, something that had been watching him for a very long time finally smiled.
And far beneath the Cathedral of Dawn, where the oldest seal in Thornwick held back the darkest Entity, something ancient and terrible began to wake up.
The bell tolled again. Louder this time. More urgent.
Kael broke into a run.
