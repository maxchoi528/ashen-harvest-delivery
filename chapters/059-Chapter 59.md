The blade missed Kael's throat by an inch. He twisted sideways, boots scraping against wet cobblestones slick with alley muck and the greasy runoff from the tannery up the street. The near-miss brought a gust of cold air against his neck. He drove his elbow into the cultist's solar plexus. The man doubled over, gasping through rotted teeth. Kael's knee found his chin. The crack echoed off the gaslit walls of the alley, a wet, final sound that bounced between the crumbling brickwork. The cultist crumpled. His skull cracked against the cobbles. He didn't get up.
"Six more coming," Lyra called from behind the overturned cart where she'd taken cover. Her daggers flashed in the amber glow of the streetlamps, catching the sickly light. "They're flanking left."
Kael counted four silhouettes spilling from the shadows near the condemned tannery. His fingers tingled with that familiar hunger—the pull that told him bodies lay somewhere close, warm and waiting. Three weeks in Fogmire District had sharpened his instincts. The stench of old blood mixed with river rot, and beneath it, something fouler. The Abyssal tang that made his teeth ache and his stomach clench. The air tasted like copper and wet ash.
A woman in a blackened robe stepped forward, arms raised, chanting in a language that scraped the inside of Kael's skull. Her eyes held no whites—just swirling darkness.
"Worship of the Veil's dissolution," Kael muttered. He'd read the pamphlets the Order of Dawn distributed, warning citizens about cult activity. They never mentioned what to do when you found twelve of them converging in an alley at midnight. They never mentioned the smell.
The chanting woman thrust her palm toward him. Pressure built in the air. Kael moved without thinking, years of street-fighting reflexes translating into pure survival instinct. A beam of black light seared past his shoulder, punching a hole through the brick wall behind him. The stone fused and bubbled. Heat washed over his back. The scent of scorched minerals filled his nose.
Lyra appeared beside him, blade already singing. She intercepted the second blast meant for his back. The impact sent her skidding three feet, but she kept her feet.
"Your turn," she growled.
Kael dropped low and charged. The cultists scattered, but not fast enough. He caught the nearest one by the collar and threw him into the path of an oncoming comrade. Both went down in a tangle of limbs and robes. His hand found the hilt of his knife—a simple thing, iron-banded, the kind smugglers used to cut rope. It wouldn't stop a charging bull, but it didn't need to.
The chanting woman pointed at him again. He was already moving.
This time the blast caught his arm. Pain lanced through his shoulder like a hot poker. The flesh blackened and peeled. Kael didn't stop. He closed the distance in three heartbeats and buried his blade in the cultist's throat. Blood sprayed across her black robes. Hot and thick, it splashed his knuckles. The chanting died mid-syllable.
[Skill Acquired: Null Ward (Basic) — Passive resistance to spiritual attacks. Duration: Short.]
Kael didn't have time to process the notification. The remaining cultists screamed battle cries and rushed him. He carved through two before the third landed a fist to his ribs. The blow staggered him. A boot caught his knee. He went down on one leg, blade still gripped in white-knuckled fingers.
Lyra's daggers sang. She appeared from his left, a blur of motion and fury, her steel finding gaps in the cultists' disorganized defense. She'd been an assassin before whatever had happened between her and the Shadow Court. Kael had never asked. She fought like someone who understood that every second of hesitation meant death.
The woman went for Lyra next, raising her hand for another attack. Kael threw his knife.
The blade spun end over end and buried itself in the woman's eye socket. She crumpled without a sound.
The remaining cultists hesitated. Their leader dead, their ritual interrupted, they looked at each other with uncertainty. One broke and ran. Two more followed. The last three formed a defensive circle, eyes darting between Kael and Lyra.
Blood dripped from Kael's wounded arm. He retrieved his knife from the corpse, wiping it clean on his sleeve. The pull toward the bodies intensified—that maddening hunger that never fully quieted.
"Cover me," he said.
Lyra nodded, positioning herself to watch the alley's entrances.
Kael knelt beside the chanting woman's body. His fingers brushed her cooling skin. The sensation hit him—the familiar rush of power, the flood of foreign memories, the ghostly whispers of a life he'd never lived. Her childhood. A village near the coast. Salt air and fishing boats. Then darkness. Then the voice that promised everything.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted spiritual essence from the remains. Memory Fragment acquired.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted spiritual essence from the remains. Skill Fragment acquired.]
The notifications scrolled through his mind, each one a small victory against the crushing weight of his own limitations. Three weeks ago, he'd been a death-row prisoner with nothing but the clothes on his back and a power he didn't understand. Now he had skills he'd harvested from the fallen, enemies who'd wanted him dead, and a growing awareness that the world was far more dangerous than the pamphlets suggested.
"You need to stop bleeding," Lyra said. "They'll have reinforcements. The Veiled Court's people don't leave their own hanging."
"They're not Veiled Court." Kael moved to the next corpse—a man with a stubbled jaw and calloused hands that suggested manual labor. "Different sigils on the robes. I saw them when they first attacked."
"Then who?"
Kael touched the second corpse. Another harvest. Another flood of fragmented memories—working in the docks, a wife, three children, a recruiter who promised easy money for simple work, a charismatic leader who spoke of salvation and rebirth. The man's hands had built ships. Now they were cold and stiff, curled into claws.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted spiritual essence from the remains. Memory Fragment acquired.]
"Workers from the docks," Kael said, rising. "Shipbuilders, laborers, men with families. They weren't criminals or mercenaries. They were recruited."
"Recruited for what?"
"The ritual she was trying to complete." He held up his knife, still wet with the woman's blood. "She was chanting in Old Tongue—the language the Orders use for seal maintenance. The same language the first Soul Reaver supposedly wrote in."
Lyra's expression darkened. "You're saying common laborers joined a cult performing seal magic?"
"I'm saying someone convinced dockworkers to bleed for them in the middle of Thornwick's slums, chanting words that should be restricted to the Six Orders." Kael wiped his hands on his trousers. The blood smeared. "That's not random criminal activity. That's organized recruitment on a scale the city guard should have noticed."
A sound echoed from the far end of the alley—footsteps, multiple sets, moving with purpose. Lyra raised her daggers. Kael positioned himself beside her, knife ready, ignoring the throbbing in his arm.
The footsteps stopped. A figure emerged from the gaslight shadows—a woman in a long coat that had seen better days, her hair pulled back in a severe knot. Silver insignias glinted on her collar: the Order of Dawn's sunburst emblem.
"Interesting," the woman said. Her voice carried the clipped tone of someone accustomed to authority. "Twelve cultists dead in a Fogmire alley, and when my scouts report the disturbance, they find only two survivors."
"We were passing through," Lyra said smoothly. "They attacked us."
The woman raised an eyebrow. "Passing through. At midnight. In the district with the highest crime rate in Thornwick." She stepped closer, and Kael noticed the spiritual pressure emanating from her—the unmistakable weight of someone far past the Awakened stage. Ascendant, at least. Maybe higher. The air around her felt heavier. "You're the one they call the Reaver."
Kael's grip tightened on his knife. "Never heard that name."
"Your reputation precedes you." The woman didn't draw a weapon, but her posture shifted subtly, ready for violence. "I am Commander Sera Vance of the Order of Dawn. My organization has been tracking cult activity in this district for months. What you've just interrupted was significant—the largest gathering we've documented in Thornwick."
"Then you should thank us."
"I should arrest you for unlicensed harvesting of spiritual essence, which is a capital offense under the Concordat's third amendment." Vance's gaze dropped to Kael's blood-stained hands. "Or I could overlook certain technical violations in exchange for cooperation."
"What do you want?"
"The cult we just disrupted—they're not acting alone. Someone is coordinating them, directing their operations, feeding them information about seal locations and Order patrol patterns." Vance's eyes hardened. "We suspect a connection to the larger conspiracy you may have heard about—the Whisper King's network."
Kael hadn't heard that name before, but something about the way Vance spoke it made his skin prickle.
"I want to know what you learned from those bodies," Vance continued. "Your abilities let you harvest more than essence—you pull memories, skills, fragments of the dead's final moments. Information that would take my investigators weeks to reconstruct."
"You want me to read them."
"I want you to share what you found. In exchange, I'll ensure your presence here is... Officially unnoted. And I'll provide information about the Six Orders' current operations against the Abyssal resurgence."
Lyra stepped forward, positioning herself between Kael and Vance. "How do we know this isn't a trap? Order of Dawn has hunted Reavers before."
"Which is why I'm offering this deal openly, than arresting you both and shipping you to the Sunken Citadel for trial." Vance's expression didn't change. "I have my own agenda, and it doesn't align with the Council's official policies. The cult activity is accelerating. Three months ago, we caught a handful of isolated worshippers. Now we're finding organized cells performing seal-dissolution rituals."
"Seal-dissolution," Kael repeated. "The seals keeping the Abyss closed."
"Seventy-two seals, each containing an Entity. The seals are weakening faster than projected. Something is working against them from inside." Vance met his gaze. "Someone is teaching these cultists the Old Tongue, providing them with ritual components, directing their attacks at specific seal points. I need to know what you saw in those memories."
Kael considered his options. Vance had more fighters waiting in the shadows—he could feel their spiritual pressure, at least four Awakened positioned at various points in the surrounding streets. Fighting would mean Lyra's death, his capture, and the end of everything he'd built in the past three weeks.
But cooperation meant trusting someone from an Order that officially hunted his kind.
"The woman who led them," Kael said slowly. "She knew the Old Tongue fluently. Not just phrases—full ritual syntax. That level of knowledge doesn't come from pamphlets or underground texts."
"Agreed."
"There was something else in her memories. A symbol she kept returning to—not their robed organization's mark. Something older. A serpent eating its own tail, with eyes like burning coals."
Vance's composure cracked for an instant. Recognition flickered across her features. Her jaw tightened.
"You've seen that symbol before," Kael said. It wasn't a question.
"The Ouroboros Protocol," Vance said quietly. "An ancient conspiracy dating back to the first Abyssal War. The theory was that they were destroyed centuries ago." She paused. "My sources in the Arcane Spire tell me otherwise."
"What do they tell you?"
"That the Protocol has been operating inside the Six Orders for generations. That they have agents in positions of authority across all six factions. That they're working toward something called the Great Unsealing—a simultaneous breach of all seventy-two seals." Vance's jaw tightened. "If they succeed, the Entities will pour through into the Material realm. The world as we know it ends."
Kael processed this information. Seventy-two seals. Seventy-two Entities. The scale of it made his head spin.
"The cultist's memories," he said. "She mentioned a name. Or a title, I think. Someone called the Whisper King."
Vance went very still.
"You know that name," Lyra observed.
"I know enough to be concerned." Vance's voice cooled. "Enough to know we need to move quickly. The cult activity isn't random, isn't isolated—it's a coordinated assault on the seal network. Every seal they weaken brings the Great Unsealing closer."
"How long?"
"We have months, maybe weeks, before the first seal fails completely." Vance stepped back, her expression settling into something resembling hope. "Help me stop them, and I'll give you everything I have on the Whisper King's organization. Names, locations, methods. And protection from Order prosecution."
Kael looked at Lyra. She shrugged—a gesture that said *your decision, your risk*.
He turned back to Vance. "I'll need proof you're not just using me as bait for a larger trap."
"Test me." Vance pulled a folded document from her coat. "This contains the patrol schedules for Order of Dawn forces in the eastern districts. Information that could get my people killed if it reached the wrong hands. I'm giving it to you now, before you've agreed to anything."
Kael took the paper. The seal was genuine—he recognized the wax stamp from the documents he'd stolen during his escape from Coldwater Prison.
"One more thing," Vance said. "The woman whose body you harvested first. Her memories contained details about an upcoming ritual. Tomorrow night, a location called the Drowned Cathedral in the Mirehollow district. They're targeting a seal that guards the forty-seventh Entity."
"You want us to stop them."
"I want you to gather intelligence. Observe, harvest what you can, and report back." Vance smiled thinly. "If you happen to disrupt their ritual, I won't object."
The footsteps resumed—moving away this time. Vance's people withdrawing into the gaslit darkness. Kael counted six distinct spiritual signatures fading into the night.
"We should move," Lyra said. "She gave us one night to decide. I doubt that's a coincidence."
"Probably not." Kael examined his wounded arm. The blast had burned through fabric and skin, leaving an angry red mark that would scar without treatment. "But she wasn't lying about the seal weakening. I felt it in the cultist's memories—a wrongness in the world, a thinning of the barrier between here and... There."
"The Abyss."
"Yeah." He started walking toward the alley's exit. "I also felt something else. Something the cultist didn't fully understand but was afraid of."
"What?"
"The Whisper King isn't just a title. In her memories, he felt more like a presence. Something that's been around for a very long time, watching, waiting." Kael paused at the alley's mouth, scanning the empty street. "She thought he was immortal. And she thought he'd made some kind of deal with one of the Entities themselves."
Lyra caught up with him. "That's not comforting."
"Wasn't meant to be." Kael glanced back at the bodies cooling in the alley. "But at least we know what we're fighting."
"Do we? Six Orders, seventy-two Entities, a Whisper King, and cultists in the thousands." Lyra counted on her fingers. "We have a wounded Reaver, an ex-Shadow Court assassin, and no resources."
"We have each other. And information the Orders don't want us to have."
"And tomorrow night, we have a ritual to crash."
Kael allowed himself a grim smile. "That we do."
They moved through the fog-shrouded streets of Fogmire District, past shuttered windows and flickering gaslights. The city's steam engines rumbled in the distance, a constant heartbeat of industry and expansion. Somewhere in those tall buildings, people slept unaware that their world teetered on the edge of annihilation.
Kael's arm throbbed with each step. His mind churned with fragmented memories—the cultist's life, her recruitment, her fear, her blind faith in a leader she'd never seen. She had three children waiting for her at home. Her husband would wake tomorrow to find her missing.
The weight of it pressed against Kael's chest. He had harvested her soul, taken what remained of her essence, stripped away the last fragments of her existence. By any moral standard, he was a monster.
But monsters survived. Monsters adapted. Monsters harvested power from those who would see them dead and used it to tear down the systems that created them.
Tomorrow, he would need every advantage he could claim. The Drowned Cathedral awaited, and with it, answers about the Whisper King's network and the cult's coordinated assault on the seals.
Kael just hoped they would survive long enough to use those answers.
The fog swallowed them whole as they disappeared into Thornwick's labyrinthine streets, leaving the dead to their silence and the living to their desperate gambles.
Somewhere in the city's depths, a figure watched from a rooftop. The sigil on his cloak—serpent devouring its tail—glowed faintly in the darkness.
"They're moving faster than expected," he murmured to no one. "The Reaver complicates things."
A voice answered from the shadows, thin and reedy. "The King anticipated this. The Order of Dawn's Commander will lead them to the Cathedral. From there, our plan proceeds."
"And if the Reaver interferes?"
The shadow laughed softly. "He already has. Every harvest, every memory he takes—our message spreads further. By the time he realizes what he's done, it won't matter."
The figure in the serpent cloak turned and vanished into the night, leaving only the fog and the distant rumble of the city's engines.
The game had changed. The pieces were moving.
And Kael Ashborn, death-row survivor and Soul Reaver, had just become the most valuable pawn on the board.
