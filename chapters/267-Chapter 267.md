The carriage shuddered over cobblestones, its iron wheels grinding against rusted tracks. Kael Ashborn pressed his palm against the reinforced glass, watching the gaslit streets of Thornwick blur past. Three weeks since the Gallows. Three weeks of hiding, of harvesting corpses from plague pits and murder scenes, of rebuilding his strength in the margins of a city that wanted him dead.
A folded note had arrived at his lodgings that morning, sealed with indigo wax and the symbol of a rising sun.
*"The Order of Dawn requests an audience. Neutral ground. Come alone."*
No signature. No address for reply. Just a time and a place—the abandoned foundry in the Merchant Quarter, where steam whistles once called factory workers to their deaths.
He didn't trust it. But the note had included something that made ignoring it impossible: a strand of silver hair tied with black ribbon. Lyra's hair.
The carriage halted. Through the window, the foundry's skeletal silhouette rose against a bruised sky.
"Mr. Ashborn." The driver—hired muscle, not a servant—leaned through the flap. "I'll be waiting. One hour, then I'm gone."
Kael stepped out, his boots splashing in a puddle of rainwater and soot. The foundry's main doors hung open, revealing darkness within. He could sense the resonance of spiritual energy inside—multiple signatures, none hostile, all suppressed to near-invisibility.
A trap? Almost certainly. But Lyra was involved, and he hadn't survived the Gallows to start making safe choices.
He drew a slow breath, rolled his shoulders, and walked inside.
The foundry's interior had been transformed. Where molten steel once flowed, crystalline lanterns now cast pale blue light across a space fitted with velvet chairs and a polished table bearing wine glasses and a silver tea service. Steam still hissed from dormant pipes overhead, lending the scene an eerie quality—civilization bleeding through industrial decay.
Six figures waited. Four stood against the walls, armored in light leather and bearing the disciplined stance of professional soldiers. Order of Dawn enforcers, judging by the sunburst insignias on their pauldrons.
The fifth figure sat at the table—a woman in her middle years, with steel-gray hair cropped short and a scar that traced her left jaw like a river following terrain. Her eyes were the color of old iron, and they studied Kael with the patience of a predator that had learned to wait.
The sixth figure stood behind her, hands clasped, head bowed.
Lyra.
She looked thin. Paler than he remembered. The black robe she wore bore no insignia, but her posture spoke of someone who'd been taught to be invisible. Her eyes lifted slightly as Kael entered, then dropped again—a flicker of something that might have been guilt.
"Kael Ashborn." The scarred woman gestured to the chair across from her. "Please. Sit."
"Do I know you?"
"Not yet. I am Commander Elara Vance of the Order of Dawn. I lead the Inquisition's External Division." She smiled, though the expression didn't reach her eyes. "In simpler terms, I handle problems that require discretion."
Kael remained standing. "Start with what you want with me."
"As you wish." Vance leaned back, fingers steepled. "You killed three men in the Ashworth District three nights ago. The official report lists it as a gang dispute. I know it was you harvesting a Shadow Court operative and two of his bodyguards."
His stomach tightened. He'd been careful. The corpses had been disposed of through the usual channels, their deaths attributed to a turf war that was already simmering in that district.
"Accusations require proof."
"I have proof. I also have restraint." Vance lifted a wine glass, swirling the contents without drinking. "If I wanted you arrested, you would be in chains right now. Instead, I'm offering you something valuable."
"Which is?"
"Survival. Legitimacy. A place in a world that currently wants you skinned and mounted above every city gate." She set the glass down. "The Order of Dawn has watched your career with great interest, Mr. Ashborn. Your abilities are... Unique. Valuable. The kind of value that should not be wasted on petty crime and street-level murder."
"I'm not a criminal."
"You are a Soul Reaver operating outside Order sanction in a city where the population has been conditioned to fear and hate your kind for three centuries." Vance's voice carried no judgment—only statement of fact. "By any practical definition, you are exactly what the world considers a criminal. The question is whether you want to remain one."
Lyra shifted slightly behind Vance. A small movement, barely perceptible, but Kael caught it. She was trying to warn him about something.
"I'm listening," Kael said carefully.
"Good." Vance gestured, and one of the enforcers approached with a leather folder. She opened it, spreading documents across the table. "The Thornwick Cult. You've encountered them, yes? The fanatics bleeding soul-essence from prisoners in the catacombs beneath the old cathedral?"
Kael's blood chilled. "I've heard rumors."
"Not rumors. Fact. They're accelerating their timetable." Vance slid a map toward him—the city's underground network, marked with red circles. "Seventeen sites have been identified. Each one is a ritual anchor point. Together, they form a seal-breaking configuration."
"Seal-breaking?" The words felt like ice in his mouth.
"The seals that contain the Abyssal Entities. Seventy-two total, scattered across the continent. They're weakening. The cult intends to accelerate that process—to tear open multiple rifts simultaneously." Vance's iron eyes bore into his. "If they succeed, the consequences would dwarf anything your petty executions ever achieved. The Abyssal Wars would seem like a minor skirmish."
Kael studied the map, forcing his mind to work through the implications. He'd known the seals were degrading—the Whisper King's fragments had made that clear. But coordinated mass unsealing was something else entirely.
"Why tell me this?"
"Because you can do something about it." Vance leaned forward. "The cult's ritual sites require specific conditions—blood sacrifice, soul-essence concentration, proximity to ley line intersections. They also require protection. Armed cultists, Abyssal constructs, and at each major site, a high-ranking Prime to oversee the final activation."
"The cult has dozens of Primes?"
"More than you'd think. The Shadow Court has been feeding them resources for years, deliberately weakening the seal network." Vance's expression hardened. "We believe the Shadow Court intends to allow the mass unsealing, then position themselves as humanity's savior by closing the rifts. They would emerge as the dominant power, controlling the Entities or using them to eliminate all opposition."
The pieces clicked into place with sickening clarity. Zarek. The assassination attempts. The elaborate frame job that had sent Kael to the Gallows in the first place. Not just personal vendetta—strategic elimination of a variable.
"So you need someone who can infiltrate cult sites, harvest their Priests, and extract information about their operations." Kael's voice came out flat. "Someone expendable."
"Invaluable," Vance corrected. "Someone willing to dirty their hands in ways the Order cannot. We have the resources, the intelligence, and the political cover. You have the abilities necessary to operate in spaces where normal Awakened cannot survive. Together, we can dismantle the cult's infrastructure before their timetable reaches completion."
"In exchange for what?"
"Official sanction. A position within the Order of Dawn as a contracted specialist. Protection from prosecution. Access to resources and information." Vance spread her hands. "And, of course, the satisfaction of watching the people who tried to kill you burn."
Kael circled the table slowly, studying the documents. The map showed seventeen ritual sites scattered across Thornwick's underground—plague cemeteries, abandoned factories, flooded tunnels, and yes, the old cathedral's catacombs. Each site was marked with dates, most of which had already passed.
The cult was already operating.
"The timeframe," he said. "How much time do we have?"
"Unknown. Intelligence suggests the cult has accelerated their plans multiple times in recent months. Something is driving them—pressure from leadership, maybe, or the perception of opportunity." Vance's jaw tightened. "Our analysts estimate weeks, possibly a month before the first major seal fails."
"And Lyra?"
The question hung in the air. Vance glanced at the woman behind her, then back to Kael.
"Lady Lyra Mercer was recruited six months ago as an asset. She has proven useful in Shadow Court intelligence matters." Vance's tone shifted—subtly, but Kael caught the edge beneath it. "She suggested we approach you."
Lyra still didn't speak. Her eyes remained downcast, hands clasped in that practiced posture of invisibility.
"Is that true?" Kael turned to face her directly.
Slowly, Lyra lifted her gaze. The exhaustion in her face was evident now—dark circles beneath her eyes, a tremor in her fingers she couldn't hide. This wasn't the confident spy he'd known in the Crimson Chalice. This was someone who'd been through something terrible.
"They have my sister," she said quietly. "The Mortician's Guild took her two years ago. I thought she was dead until the Order found records. She's alive, Kael. In one of their facilities. If I help them—if I bring them what they need—they'll get her out."
"So you're trading intel for a hostage extraction."
"I'm trading intel for my sister." Lyra's voice cracked slightly. "I know what they're doing is wrong. I know the cult is going to kill thousands of people if they succeed. But I also know that Sera is all I have left, and I can't—" She stopped, swallowing hard. "I'm sorry. I should have warned you about the meeting. I couldn't risk you not coming."
Vance watched the exchange with clinical interest, measuring the emotional weight of the revelation.
Kael's mind raced. The pieces were rearranging themselves—Lyra's sudden disappearance after the Gallows, her resurfacing as an Order asset, the complex web of obligation and manipulation that had led to this moment. He didn't trust Vance. He didn't fully trust Lyra, either. But the map on the table was real, and the threat was real, and somewhere in this room was the information he needed to survive the conspiracy that had tried to crush him.
"The offer," he said, turning back to Vance. "What exactly are you asking me to do?"
"Harvest them." Vance's answer was blunt, stripped of euphemism. "The cult's Primes carry unique knowledge—ritual configurations, communication protocols, leadership identities. Normal interrogation yields limited results; their training includes resistance techniques. But a Soul Reaver can extract information directly from their souls."
"You're asking me to murder your enemies."
"I'm asking you to harvest cultists who've killed dozens of innocents and intend to kill thousands more." Vance met his gaze without flinching. "Call it what you will. The practical result remains the same."
One of the enforcers shifted uneasily. Kael caught the discomfort in the man's posture—the Order soldiers weren't as comfortable with this conversation as their commander. They followed orders, but this was outside their usual experience.
"What's stopping me from walking out of here and ignoring everything I've learned?"
Vance's expression didn't change. "Nothing. You could disappear. Leave Thornwick, travel to another city, try to build a life away from the conspiracy. But you would still be a Soul Reaver in a world that hates Soul Reavers. The Shadow Court knows what you can do. The cult knows. The Mortician's Guild knows, and they have resources and reach that extend across every major city on the continent." She leaned forward. "You cannot hide from what you are. You can only choose which powers you align with—and which you oppose."
"And if I refuse your offer but keep the information?"
"Then we become problems for each other." Vance's voice hardened. "The Order of Dawn does not tolerate loose ends. I am offering you a path to legitimacy. Refuse, and you become an enemy of the one faction that might have protected you."
The threat was clear. Accept the leash, or face the consequences.
Kael studied the map again, tracing the lines of the underground network with his eyes. Seventeen sites. Cultists at each one. Primes overseeing the critical anchors. And somewhere in this maze of tunnels and secrets, the people who'd framed him, tried to kill him, and destroyed everything he'd built.
He thought of the Gallows. The crowds cheering. The hood over his head and the rope around his neck. He'd died that day, in every way that mattered. What had pulled him back was something darker than hope—something that refused to let his enemies win.
Lyra had betrayed him. The Order of Dawn was manipulating them both. The cult wanted to tear the world apart. And the Shadow Court was playing a game so vast that individual lives were only pieces to be sacrificed.
But Kael had survived worse odds. He'd harvested monsters and kings, dragged power from corpses that should have stayed dead, and built himself into something the Orders feared to name.
"Three conditions," he said.
Vance raised an eyebrow. "You are in no position to negotiate."
"I am exactly in that position. You need me, or you wouldn't be having this conversation." Kael met her iron gaze without flinching. "First: my operations remain independent. I choose targets, methods, and timing. You provide intelligence and support, not commands."
"That is acceptable in principle."
"Second: Lady Mercer's extraction takes priority. You get her sister out before I start harvesting."
Lyra's head snapped up. "Kael—"
"Third: I want access to your archives. Everything you have on the Shadow Court, the cult's structure, and the seal network." He let the words settle. "I've been fighting blind for too long. That ends now."
Vance studied him for a long moment, then smiled—a thin, predatory expression that transformed her scarred face into something almost human.
"Access to archives is... Negotiable. We can provide sanitized copies of relevant materials. The other conditions are acceptable, with the caveat that operational security may require temporary modifications." She extended her hand. "Do we have an accord?"
Kael looked at her hand. At the enforcers with their hands near their weapons. At Lyra, who was watching him with something that might have been hope or might have been another layer of deception.
He thought about the people in the streets above—the ordinary citizens going about their lives, unaware that beneath their feet, fanatics were preparing to tear holes in reality. He thought about the Gallows, and the faces of the crowd, and the cold certainty that the world had systemically tried to erase him.
He thought about the souls waiting in the darkness, ready to be harvested.
Then he reached out and clasped Vance's hand.
"We have an accord."
The sound of a carriage arriving outside echoed through the foundry. Vance released his hand and stood, her posture shifting from negotiator to commander in an instant.
"Your first target is identified." She gestured, and one of the enforcers brought forward a leather tube. Inside was a rolled document. "Prime Maren, codename 'The Architect.' She's been coordinating the ritual site activations. Currently operating from a warehouse in the Dockside District. Two guards, standard cultist training. No Abyssal constructs observed."
"And the timeline?"
"Tonight. Before the midnight bell." Vance moved toward the door, her enforcers falling into formation around her. "You will receive your credentials tomorrow—official documentation that grants you Order authority. Until then, operate carefully. The cult has eyes everywhere."
She paused at the threshold, glancing back at Lyra, who remained motionless in the shadows.
"Lady Mercer will accompany you to the warehouse. She knows the layout." Vance's iron eyes glittered. "Consider it a gesture of good faith. And a reminder that your alliance depends on continued cooperation."
Then she was gone, her footsteps echoing on the foundry floor, leaving Kael alone with the woman who'd sold him out and the mission that might kill them both.
The silence stretched between them.
"You didn't have to agree to that," Lyra said quietly. "The conditions. You could have asked for anything else."
"I wanted information. I wanted Sera saved." Kael pulled the target document from its tube and examined the sketch inside—a severe woman with close-cropped hair and empty eyes. "And I wanted you to know that I don't forget debts."
Lyra flinched. "I never meant—"
"I know what you meant. You made a choice. I made mine." He rolled the document back up and tucked it into his coat. "Tonight, we harvest a Prime. Tomorrow, we start dismantling a conspiracy. And somewhere in the middle of all that, we figure out whether we can still work together."
He walked toward the foundry's main entrance, not waiting to see if she followed.
The gaslit streets of Thornwick stretched before him, full of shadows and secrets and the distant rumble of steam engines. Somewhere in the Dockside District, a woman named Maren was preparing to accelerate the end of the world. The cult had been operating for months, maybe years, building toward a moment of catastrophic revelation.
And Kael had just been handed a knife and pointed at the enemy.
The Harvest system in his mind pulsed with dormant potential—soul fragments waiting to be claimed, abilities ready to be extracted. For the first time since the Gallows, he had a clear target and the resources to pursue it.
He could feel the conspiracy tightening around him. The Whisper King's influence. The Shadow Court's machinations. The cult's fanatical determination. All of it was connected, all of it was moving toward some terrible convergence.
But Kael had died once already. He'd clawed his way back from oblivion through sheer spite and the power to take what he needed from the dead.
Whatever was coming, he would face it. And when the moment came, he would harvest the truth from the corpses of everyone who had tried to destroy him.
Behind him, Lyra's footsteps finally followed.

The Dockside District crawled with activity despite the late hour. Stevedores hauled cargo from warehouse roofs while steam-cranes lifted shipping containers onto iron barges. The air smelled of brine, coal smoke, and the sour tang of water. Kael moved through the crowds with practiced ease, his hood drawn low, eyes scanning for threats.
Lyra walked beside him, silent as a ghost. She'd changed into darker clothes—fitted black that allowed free movement, with small pouches at her belt that probably held tools or weapons. Her training was evident in every step, every glance. Even exhausted and compromised, she was still dangerous.
"The warehouse is three blocks ahead." Her voice was barely audible above the street noise. "Corner building, red door, two stories. There's a basement level, but the ritual preparation happens on the upper floor. Maren prefers height when she works."
"How do you know her?"
"We've crossed paths. The Shadow Court and the cult have... Overlapping interests." Lyra's jaw tightened. "She was at the Gallows. Watching your execution. She wanted to make sure you died."
Something cold moved through Kael's chest.
"You knew?"
"I suspected. The cult had intel on you for months before the frame job. I didn't know they were working with Zarek until after." Lyra's expression tightened with old anger. "If I'd realized sooner, I might have been able to stop it. Instead, I was chasing Mortician's Guild leads while your execution was being prepared."
Kael absorbed the information, filing it away. Another piece of the puzzle. Another connection between the conspiracy's threads.
They reached a narrow alley between two warehouses, the brick walls slick with condensation and covered in moss. The red door was visible at the far end, flanked by shadow.
"Two guards," Lyra said. "Standard cult rotations. They'll change at the quarter-hour. We have a window."
"Or we create our own."
Kael stepped out of the shadows and approached the door. The guards straightened, hands moving toward weapons—but they hesitated when they saw his face. Not recognition. Something else. A sense of wrongness that radiated from his presence like cold from ice.
"You're not supposed to be here," one of them said. His voice trembled slightly.
"No," Kael agreed. "I'm not."
He moved forward, each step deliberate. The guards exchanged glances, uncertainty warring with training. They were cultists, true believers who'd killed innocents in the name of their cause. But they were also just men, and something in Kael's approach made their weapons feel inadequate.
"Kael Ashborn." The second guard's voice cracked. "They said you were dead."
"They said a lot of things."
Kael reached out and touched the first guard's chest. His fingers found the pulse point, pressed, and the Harvest system activated with a whisper of power that only he could perceive.
The soul was thin, weak—a small thing barely worth the effort. But it carried useful fragments: a communication password, the current rotation schedule, and a fragment of Maren's personality that included her paranoia about intruders.
**[Soul Harvest: You extracted a minor fragment from the remains. Security Protocol acquired.]**
The guard collapsed, eyes glassy, breathing shallow. The other spun toward the door, reaching for an alarm bell—
Kael caught him before he could sound it. One hand over the mouth, the other on the chest, and then the Harvest pulled again, gentler this time, a careful extraction that left the man unconscious than empty.
Two down. Minimal noise. The Harvest had cost him nothing but a moment of contact.
He dragged both bodies into the shadows, out of sight from the street. Lyra appeared at his side, helping him stack them behind a pile of rotting crates.
"The alarm won't sound for another ten minutes," she said, her voice tight. "Rotation change is the only scheduled check. We have a window."
Kael wiped his hands on his coat and approached the red door. The lock was spiritual—wards set into the wood that would trigger if approached without the proper resonance. But he'd harvested the password, and more importantly, he'd harvested a fragment of the cult's internal spiritual signature.
He pressed his palm against the wood, let the harvested resonance flow through him, and felt the wards recognize him as friend than foe.
The door clicked open.
Inside, the warehouse was dark except for a circle of blue light emanating from the second floor. Kael could hear chanting—low, rhythmic, the cadence of ritual speech. Multiple voices, harmonizing in a pattern that scraped against his senses like broken glass.
He took the stairs carefully, each footfall silent on the wooden steps. Lyra followed close behind, her breath controlled, her movements precise.
The second floor opened into a vast space filled with ritual implements—silver chalices, iron cauldrons, scrolls covered in symbols that seemed to writhe when viewed directly. At the center of the room, a woman stood within the circle of blue light, her arms raised, her voice rising in invocation.
Maren. The Architect. She was thin to the point of gauntness, her skull visible beneath paper-white skin, her eyes covered by a blindfold embroidered with silver thread. Around her, three lesser cultists knelt in supplication, their chanting forming the harmonic layer beneath her solo.
Kael watched from the shadows, assessing. The guards downstairs had been weak—the Architect herself radiated power. Not Awakened, exactly, but something else. Something that tasted of the Abyss in ways that made his soul recoil.
"She used to be Mortician's Guild," Lyra whispered. "Harvester-class. She developed a taste for Entity-touched essence and went further and further until she crossed over. The cult recruited her after her second meltdown."
"Second meltdown?"
"She killed an entire research team the first time. The Guild tried to rehabilitate her." Lyra's voice carried dark irony. "It didn't take."
The chanting reached a crescendo. Maren's voice soared above the others, syllables building into something that vibrated in Kael's bones. The blue light intensified, and he felt the wards around the room activate—a protective shell that would trigger if anyone attempted to interfere with the ritual.
"Thirty seconds," Lyra said. "The ritual will complete, and she'll be vulnerable for maybe a minute while she comedown. That's our window."
"Or I create my own."
Kael stepped into the light.
The cultists' chanting faltered. Maren's blindfolded face turned toward him, her expression shifting from concentration to shock to something that might have been delight.
"Kael Ashborn." Her voice echoed strangely, layered with harmonics that weren't entirely human. "The dead man walks. I wondered if you would come."
"You wanted me to?"
"I've been waiting." Maren's hands lowered slowly, deliberately. "The Whisper King said you would. Said you would find your way back, no matter how many times we tried to stop you."
The Whisper King. The name landed like a physical blow.
Kael's mind raced. The Whisper King had been mentioned before—in fragments, in hints, in the cryptic messages that had occasionally appeared in his Harvest visions. But hearing it spoken aloud, in this context, by a cult Prime who seemed to expect him...
"How does the Whisper King know me?"
"Ah." Maren's lipless mouth curved into something approximating a smile. "That would be telling. But I suspect you already know, somewhere in that harvested soul of yours. You just haven't put the pieces together yet."
Behind her, the three lesser cultists were scrambling to their feet, reaching for weapons. Kael dismissed them from immediate concern—they were obstacles, not threats. The real danger stood at the center of the fading ritual circle, radiating wrongness like a small sun.
"I can feel your soul," Maren said. "It's fascinating. Broken and remade, over and over. Every harvest adds new facets. You're becoming something new, Kael. Something the world has never seen."
"Is that supposed to scare me?"
"It's supposed to prepare you." Maren spread her arms, and the blue light flared again. "The Whisper King wants you to understand something before the end. The game is larger than your petty vendettas. Larger than the Orders, larger than the cult, larger than anything you've imagined."
She leaned forward, her blindfolded face inches from his.
"The seals will break. The Entities will rise. And when the dust settles, only those who chose correctly will survive. You have a choice to make, Kael Ashborn. Join us now, while there's still time, or be crushed when the world reshapes itself."
The words hung in the air like poison.
Kael felt the Harvest system stirring, hungry for the powerful soul before him. Maren was strong—stronger than anyone he'd faced since his transformation. But she was also talking, which meant she was buying time.
Which meant she was afraid of him.
"I've heard this pitch before," he said. "From Zarek. From the Shadow Court. From everyone who thinks they can use me." He stepped closer. "I'm not interested in joining your apocalypse. I'm interested in taking what I need to stop it."
"Stop it?" Maren laughed—a sound like breaking glass. "You can't stop it. The timeline is accelerating. The seals are failing faster than even we predicted. Something is pushing them—something that wants the Abyss opened wider than anyone imagined."
"What something?"
"The same something that created you, maybe." Maren's blindfold seemed to focus on him with impossible precision. "The Soul Reaver ability didn't emerge from nowhere. It was designed. Placed in the world for a purpose. Do you really think you're an accident, Kael Ashborn? A happy coincidence of broken fate and stolen power?"
The question hit harder than any blow.
Kael's mind flashed to the Sepulcher tablets, the fragments of the first Soul Reaver's knowledge he'd absorbed from the Hollow King. To the cryptic messages that suggested his abilities had a origin beyond mere survival. To the way every harvest seemed to pull him toward something larger, some predestined role he hadn't chosen.
He didn't have time to think about it now. But he filed the implication away, another piece of the puzzle that was slowly forming in his mind.
"Your ritual failed," he said, changing the subject. "I interrupted it. Whatever you were trying to accomplish tonight won't happen."
"Tonight's ritual was a test." Maren lowered her arms. "A demonstration for my superiors. They've been watching you, Kael. Watching your progress. They want to see what you'll do when cornered."
"And what am I doing?"
"Exactly what they predicted." Maren's smile widened. "Coming to harvest us. Because that's what you are. A tool. A weapon. And weapons don't get to choose who wields them."
Behind her, the three cultists had positioned themselves strategically—one near the stairs, one by a side door, one with a hand on a ceremonial blade. They weren't running. They weren't surrendering. They were waiting for something.
Kael realized, with cold certainty, that he'd walked into a trap.
The warehouse shook. Dust fell from the ceiling, and the ritual circle blazed back to life—but this time, the light was red than blue, and the air filled with a sound that wasn't voice, wasn't wind, was something between.
"The cult thanks you for your cooperation," Maren said. "Your soul will make an excellent anchor point."
The cultists charged.
Kael moved.
His hand caught the first cultist's throat before the man could complete his swing. The Harvest pulled—aggressive, desperate, ripping skills and memories from the dying man in a torrent of information. Combat training flooded through him: knife work, grappling techniques, the precise angles for breaking bones. Useful. Valuable. Enough to turn the fight.
**[Soul Harvest: You extracted combat proficiency from the remains. Blade Technique III acquired. Grappling Fundamentals acquired.]**
He threw the empty body into the second cultist, buying himself a heartbeat. The third was already closing, the ceremonial blade rising—
Lyra appeared from the shadows, a thin blade flashing in her hand. She hamstrung the cultist with surgical precision, then drove her knife up through the base of his skull. He collapsed without a sound.
"Maren!" Lyra shouted. "The anchor!"
Kael spun. The Architect had completed some gesture, and the ritual circle was pulsing with concentrated power—a localized tear in reality that was pulling at him with invisible hooks. His soul recoiled from the sensation. This wasn't a simple kidnapping trap. This was something worse.
"Run!" Lyra grabbed his arm. "That will pull you into the rift if you stay inside the circle!"
Maren laughed, the sound layered with that wrongness that scraped against his senses. "Running won't help you. The rift has locked onto your soul signature. It will follow."
Kael felt it—the connection between himself and the flickering tear in space. Maren was right. The pull was sticky, persistent, a hook in his essence that would trail him wherever he went.
Unless he severed it.
The thought crystallized with sudden clarity. The rift was anchored to Maren's soul. If he harvested her, pulled the power that was feeding the connection, the rift would collapse.
But Maren was stronger than anyone he'd faced. The Harvest might not work on someone so deeply touched by the Abyss. And if it failed, he would be pulled into a space between realities with no way out.
He had seconds to decide.
The rift pulsed, its pull strengthening. Lyra was pulling him toward the stairs, but they would never make it—not with the connection dragging at him like chains.
"Maren," Kael said. "Let's end this."
He released Lyra's hand and charged into the circle.
The power hit him like a physical blow. The Abyssal energy wrapped around him, trying to drag him down, trying to pull him apart. He could feel his soul fraying at the edges, his sense of self beginning to dissolve.
Maren's face twisted with triumph. "Fool! You walked right into—"
Kael's hand found her chest.
The Harvest activated with a roar that shook the warehouse. Soul energy flooded through him—not the gentle whisper of previous harvests, but a torrential downpour of raw power that threatened to overwhelm his senses. Maren screamed, her body convulsing, as decades of accumulated knowledge and ability were ripped from her soul.
**[Soul Harvest: You extracted extensive abilities from the remains. Abyssal Resistance IV acquired. Ritual Mastery II acquired. Architect's Knowledge acquired. WARNING: Soul capacity approaching limit.]**
The rift collapsed. The pull vanished. Kael staggered back, gasping, his mind swimming with new information that threatened to drown him.
Maren crumpled to the ground, empty. Her blindfold fell away, revealing eyes that had turned completely black—no whites, no irises, just an endless void that stared at nothing.
"She's still alive," Lyra said, kneeling beside the body. "Breathing. But her soul is gone. You hollowed her out completely."
Kael couldn't respond. The Architect's Knowledge was unfolding in his mind—a comprehensive map of the cult's operations, their timeline, their leadership structure, and most importantly, the location of the primary seal sites.
Including the one that was about to fail.
