The blade missed Kael's throat by a finger's width. He twisted sideways, boots scraping cobblestone, and drove his elbow into the attacker's solar plexus. The cultist doubled over, wheezing. Kael wrenched the knife from nerveless fingers and pressed it flat against the man's neck.
"Who sent you?" Kael's voice came out flat, stripped of everything except the threat underneath.
The cultist spat blood and laughed. Three more figures emerged from the gaslit fog, circling. Kael counted their stances—Iron Vanguard technique, but sloppier. Trained thugs wearing someone else's doctrine.
"Doesn't matter who we are." The speaker carried no weapon, which meant he was the dangerous one. Spiritual pressure rolled off him in waves. "What matters is what comes next."
Kael dropped low as a fist carved through the space his head had occupied. He parried the next strike, then the one after, each impact jarring through his bones. These cultists fought to kill, not to capture. That told him something.
"Shadow Court?" Kael guessed, dancing back from a sweeping kick.
The leader's expression flickered. Confirmation enough.
A carriage rattled past on the adjacent street, its lanterns casting long shadows across the alley mouth. Kael calculated distances, exits, the crowd noise two blocks away. Not enough witnesses to guarantee safety if he went loud.
Then again, safety had stopped being an option the moment they'd chosen him.
He let the next punch graze his shoulder—close enough to sell the stumble, far enough to keep moving. The cultist overextended. Kael pivoted, slamming his forehead into the man's nose. Cartilage crunched. The cultist staggered back, clutching his face.
"Four of you." Kael wiped blood from a split lip. "Four of you against one man in an alley. And you're still losing."
The leader's calm cracked. "Kill him."
They rushed him together.
Kael met the first charge with a brutal knee to the groin, following with a palm strike that snapped the man's head back. The second attacker managed a shallow cut across Kael's forearm before taking a knife between the ribs. The third swung a length of chain. Kael blocked with his forearm, felt bones grind, and drove his knee upward into the man's stomach.
The fourth—fast, trained, and carrying spiritual weight—landed a blow to Kael's kidney that dropped him to one knee. Pain lanced through his side. He rolled away from the finishing strike, came up swinging, and caught the cultist across the jaw with a vicious hook.
All four lay groaning on the ground.
Kael stood over them, chest heaving, blood dripping from multiple wounds. The street remained empty. No gas-lamp patrols in this district after dark. That had been intentional on someone's part.
He searched the bodies with practiced efficiency. No identifying marks. No correspondence. The weapons were quality but unmarked. Professional work. The leader carried a small vial of something that pulsed with sickly green light—Abyssal essence, unrefined. Worth a small fortune on the black market.
Kael pocketed it. Evidence, or payment. Depending on how things played out.
One cultist still breathed steadily. Kael crouched beside him, checking for pulse. Alive, but unconscious. He'd be useful.
Movement at the alley mouth. Kael spun, knife raised, only to find a familiar silhouette against the lamplight.
Sera stepped into the fog. Her coat hung open, revealing the glint of warded armor underneath. She surveyed the carnage with an expression caught somewhere between approval and exasperation.
"You left one alive." She nodded toward the unconscious cultist. "I assume that was intentional."
"They attacked me in a pack." Kael wiped his blade clean. "Shadow Court, if I had to guess. Coming to test how much heat I could take."
"You're not wrong." Sera produced a folded paper from her coat. "Got this twenty minutes ago. Dropped through my window like a love letter from someone who wanted me dead."
Kael unfolded the paper. A single line in elegant script: *The Reaver draws too much attention. Correct this.*
"Not subtle," Kael observed.
"The Shadow Court never is, when they want you to know they mean business." Sera crouched beside the leader's body, studying his face. "These aren't rank-and-file cultists. This one's got surgical modifications along his meridian points. Someone spent serious money turning him into a weapon."
"Iron Vanguard techniques with Shadow Court backing." Kael's jaw tightened. "Someone's combining resources."
"That's the problem." Sera stood, brushing dust from her knees. "The Shadow Court operates as a deniable arm for Orders that want results without fingerprints. But this kind of coordination? Resources flowing between factions that should be competing? That's not proxy warfare. That's a coalition."
Kael handed her the vial of Abyssal essence. Sera's eyebrows rose.
"Pretty gift."
"Came with the ambush."
She pocketed it without ceremony. "Then we have leverage. The question is who to show it to."
The unconscious cultist groaned. Kael pressed his boot against the man's windpipe until the sound stopped.
"We should move him." Sera glanced at the street, scanning for observers. "There's a safehouse two blocks east. Mortician's Guild property—I still have access. We can question him there, away from prying eyes."
"And if someone's watching the safehouse?"
"Then we're walking into a trap." She met his gaze without flinching. "But we're already in one. At least this way, we control the terms."
Kael considered. His wounds throbbed, demanding attention. The blood cooling on his skin would attract spiritual scavengers within the hour. But leaving meant losing the only lead he had on who wanted him eliminated and why.
"The Shadow Court sent four fighters," he said slowly. "Four fighters and a message. They wanted me to know they're watching. They wanted me scared."
"Are you?"
"No." He bent and hauled the unconscious cultist over his shoulder. "But I should be."
They moved through Thornwick's underbelly like ghosts, sticking to service corridors and maintenance tunnels. The city's steam infrastructure provided convenient cover—pipes hissing, valves releasing pressure in rhythmic bursts. Noise meant concealment.
Sera led with the confidence of someone who'd memorized every shortcut. She hadn't spoken since they'd left the alley, but Kael caught her checking her surroundings with the vigilance of prey.
"You've dealt with the Shadow Court before," Kael said quietly as they descended a rusted staircase.
"Everyone in my position has." She didn't elaborate.
"What did it cost you?"
Sera's pace didn't falter. "It cost me someone I trusted. Someone who turned out to be working both sides of a war I didn't know was happening." A pause. "That's how they operate. They find your attachments, your loyalties, and they use them to pry you open."
The staircase ended in a heavy door marked with the Mortician's Guild's symbol—a stylized eye overlaid with crossed bones. Sera produced a key from somewhere and worked the lock with practiced efficiency.
Inside, the safehouse smelled of formaldehyde and old paper. Shelves lined the walls, holding specimen jars and leather-bound ledgers. A examination table dominated the center of the room, its leather surface stained with decades of accumulated fluids.
Kael dumped the cultist onto the table without ceremony. Sera moved to the supply cabinet and returned with bandages, antiseptic, and a syringe filled with clear liquid.
"Pull your shirt up."
Kael complied, wincing as she cleaned the cut on his forearm. The antiseptic burned worse than the original wound.
"That'll leave a scar," Sera observed, almost conversationally.
"Add it to the collection."
She taped a bandage in place and moved on to the deeper cut along his ribs. Her fingers were steady, professional. Nothing about her demeanor suggested she found any of this unusual.
"The Shadow Court message," Kael said. "What does it mean, 'the Reaver draws too much attention'?"
"They're afraid of what you represent." Sera sealed the second wound. "A Soul Reaver who operates openly, who takes from anyone regardless of faction or alignment—that's a threat to every power structure in Thornwick. The Orders, the criminal syndicates, even the Abyssal cults. You don't fit into their neat categories."
"I never claimed to."
"Doesn't matter. You exist. That's enough." She stepped back, surveying her work. "Someone's decided you're more useful dead than alive. The fact that they sent four fighters instead of six or eight suggests they're still testing. Gauging your response. Your strength."
Kael flexed his fingers experimentally. The joint ached where he'd blocked the chain, but nothing felt broken. "And my response?"
"That's why we're here." Sera nodded toward the unconscious cultist. "We find out who gave the order. We find out why. And then we decide whether to kill them or leverage them."
The cultist stirred. His eyes fluttered open—unfocused, then sharpening as he registered his surroundings. He tried to move and found his wrists bound to the table's edges.
"Welcome back." Kael leaned against the wall, arms crossed. "I have questions. You're going to answer them."
The cultist tested his bonds again. Finding them solid, he settled into stillness. His gaze moved from Kael to Sera and back again.
"The Mortician's Guild," he observed. "Convenient."
"We have resources." Sera pulled a chair close and sat, crossing her legs. "So do you. The question is whether you're more afraid of dying here or more afraid of whoever sent you."
A long moment passed. The cultist's breathing remained even, controlled. Not the calm of a fanatic—that would have manifested as defiance or prayer. This was the stillness of someone calculating odds.
"The Shadow Court doesn't give orders," he said finally. "They give opportunities. If someone wanted you dead, you'd be dead. What they want is your attention."
"My attention." Kael's voice carried a dangerous edge. "They sent four men to kill me and take my attention."
"They sent four men to test you. To see how you fight, how you think, what you'd do with an unconscious body instead of a corpse." The cultist's lips curved without humor. "The difference matters. A true enemy would have sent eight. Or sixteen. Or someone from the upper tiers."
"You're saying they wanted me to win."
"I'm saying they wanted to see what you'd do next." The cultist's gaze drifted to Kael's hands. "Most Reavers, when they have a body? They harvest immediately. Instinct. Need. The power calls to them."
Kael said nothing.
"But you didn't." The cultist sounded almost admiring. "You searched the body. You took the essence vial but left the corpse intact. You carried me here instead of stripping me for parts. That tells them something."
"What does it tell them?"
"That you're not like the others. That you're thinking, planning, building toward something." The cultist's smile widened. "The Shadow Court respects that. They want Reavers who can be reasoned with. Controlled."
"Controlled." Sera's voice could have frozen steam. "You're telling us the Shadow Court wants to recruit him?"
"I'm telling you they want to open a dialogue." The cultist's gaze remained fixed on Kael. "The message wasn't a threat. It was an invitation. The attack was the test. Now comes the conversation."
Kael stepped closer. "You said 'the Reaver draws too much attention.' Attention from who?"
"Everyone." The cultist didn't flinch. "The Six Orders are aware of you. The Council of Six has discussed your existence three times in the past month. The Arcane Spire wants to study you. The Iron Vanguard wants to destroy you. The Order of Dawn wants to redeem you. And the Shadow Court wants to use you."
"Use me for what?"
"The seals are failing." The cultist's voice dropped. "All seventy-two. Not at once, but progressively. Each year, more cracks appear. Each decade, more entities break through partial barriers. The Orders are losing ground. They're fighting defensive battles they can't win with resources they don't have."
"And the Shadow Court has a solution?"
"The Shadow Court has an alternative." The cultist's eyes glittered. "Soul Reavers can harvest power directly from the Abyss. They can tap into entities without being consumed. They can do what the seals cannot—contain the bleeding, stabilize the barriers, maybe even reverse the damage."
"You're saying they want me to become a weapon against the Abyss."
"I'm saying they want to talk. What happens after that depends on what you agree to." The cultist tested his bonds once more. "The Shadow Court sent me to deliver a message, not to die for it. If you let me walk out of here, I can arrange a meeting. Someone high-ranking. Someone who can explain what they're offering."
Sera stood abruptly. "Absolutely not."
"It's not your decision." Kael's voice cut through her protest. He studied the cultist for a long moment. "If I let you go, you'll report everything back. You'll tell them I asked questions. That I showed interest."
"Yes."
"That tells them I'm considering their offer."
"Yes."
Kael turned to Sera. "Lock the door behind him. I want ten minutes alone with our guest."
"Kael—"
"Ten minutes. Then you can come back in and decide whether to help me or stop me."
Sera's jaw tightened. She held his gaze for three heartbeats, then nodded once and left. The door closed behind her with a heavy thunk.
Kael waited until the footsteps faded before turning back to the cultist.
"The truth this time." He pulled a chair close and sat, leaning forward with his elbows on his knees. "Not the sales pitch. Not the Shadow Court talking points. The truth."
The cultist studied him. Whatever he saw seemed to satisfy some internal criterion.
"The Shadow Court isn't monolithic. It's a network of cells, each pursuing different objectives under a shared philosophy. Expansion through deniable operations. Power through flexibility. Survival through adaptation." He paused. "Within that network, there are factions. Some want to accelerate the seal failure—they believe chaos creates opportunity. Some want to maintain the status quo—they've built comfortable lives within the current power structure. And some want to find a solution that doesn't involve the Orders' failing methods."
"Which faction sent you?"
"The pragmatic faction. The one that believes Soul Reavers are the key to surviving the next century." The cultist's voice carried something that might have been conviction. "We know what the seals can do. We know what they can't. When they fail completely—and they will—we'll need people who can fight entities directly. Harvest their power. Turn their own nature against them."
"Like the original Soul Reaver."
The cultist went still.
Kael watched the reaction carefully. Surprise. Recognition. Something else—fear?
"The original Soul Reaver," the cultist repeated slowly. "Where did you hear that name?"
"Does it matter?"
"It might." The cultist's composure had cracked slightly. "The original Soul Reaver is a myth. A legend from the Abyssal Wars. Someone the Orders invoke when they want to justify their fear of your kind."
"But you know the legend."
"I know the legend exists." The cultist chose his words with visible care. "I know it involves someone who could harvest directly from entities. Who could consume their power without corruption. Who vanished at the end of the Wars and was never seen again."
"The Sepulcher Tablets mention them."
"You've read the Tablets?" The cultist's surprise was genuine now. "No Awakened below Sovereign stage can access the Tablets. They're locked to—"
"Keep talking."
The silence stretched. Kael let it.
"The original Soul Reaver," the cultist said finally, "is said to have discovered the source of the seal network. The mechanism that holds the Veil together. If the legend is true, they could have done something no one else has done—reinforced the seals permanently. Or destroyed them entirely."
"Which did they choose?"
"No one knows. The legend ends with the Reaver's disappearance. Some say they transcended. Some say they were killed by the Orders who feared their power. Some say they're still out there, waiting." The cultist met Kael's gaze. "The Shadow Court has spent decades trying to find the answer. The location of the original tomb. The contents of the Reaver's final harvest. Anything that might explain how they achieved what they achieved."
"And what have they found?"
"Nothing conclusive." The cultist's expression tightened. "Which is why the emergence of a new Soul Reaver has the Shadow Court very, very interested. Not as a weapon. As a potential key to unlocking secrets that have been buried for centuries."
Kael absorbed this. The cultist had said nothing directly about the Whisper King or the original Reaver's fate—nothing that violated the information boundaries. But he'd confirmed that the Shadow Court was actively searching for answers Kael himself was trying to find.
"The meeting," Kael said. "Where and when?"
"The Ashford warehouse district. Midnight, three days from now. Come alone. You'll be met at the entrance." The cultist paused. "If you don't come, the Shadow Court will assume you're not interested. They'll adjust their approach accordingly."
"Adjust how?"
"More direct methods." The cultist's meaning was clear. "The Shadow Court prefers negotiation. But when negotiation fails, they have other tools."
Kael stood. He crossed to the table and worked at the cultist's bonds, loosening them enough for the man to free himself.
"Three days," Kael said. "I'll think about it."
The cultist rubbed his wrists, watching Kael with an unreadable expression. "The Shadow Court knows more about Soul Reavers than you might expect. We have records. Fragmentary, incomplete, but more than anyone else possesses. If you want to understand what you're becoming, we're the only ones who can help."
"Noted."
"Another thing." The cultist slid off the table, testing his legs. "The woman. Sera. She's connected to this whether she wants to be or not. The Mortician's Guild has been working with Shadow Court interests for longer than she's been alive. If she's offering you protection, ask yourself what she's protecting you from. And what she's protecting them from."
Kael said nothing. The cultist didn't seem to expect a response.
Sera opened the door and gestured curtly. "Out. Now."
The cultist walked past her without a backward glance. Kael listened to his footsteps fade down the corridor, then turned to find Sera watching him with an expression that had shifted from concern to something harder.
"What did he tell you?"
"Enough to be dangerous." Kael moved to the supply cabinet and retrieved a roll of bandages. "The Shadow Court wants to meet. Three days from now."
"I heard." Sera's arms crossed. "And I'm telling you it's a trap."
"Probably. But traps can be useful." He started rewrapping his ribs, hissing at the pull of damaged skin. "He also said the Mortician's Guild has connections to the Shadow Court. That you've been working with their interests."
Sera's expression didn't change, but something behind her eyes shifted. "He's not wrong."
"I need to know what that means."
"It means I've been playing both sides since before I understood there were sides to play." She moved to the window and checked the street below. "My sister was taken by the Guild years ago. I've been doing what I had to do to keep her alive. Sometimes that involved information that helped the Shadow Court. Sometimes it involved information that hurt them."
"The Shadow Court has leverage over you."
"The Shadow Court has leverage over everyone. That's their purpose." She turned back to face him. "The question is whether you're going to hold it against me."
Kael finished securing the bandage. "Your sister. The one in the Mortician's Guild facility."
"Yes."
"The cultist said they're looking for someone. Someone connected to Soul Reaver history." Kael met her gaze. "Could your sister be part of that?"
Sera's silence stretched long enough to become an answer.
"Three days," Kael said. "I need to prepare. And I need you to find out everything the Mortician's Guild knows about the original Soul Reaver. Not legends. Actual records. Who they were. Where they went. What they discovered."
"That's asking a lot."
"I'm asking you to choose sides. That's what this has always been about." Kael crossed to her. "The Shadow Court is moving against me. The Orders are watching. The seals are failing. In a few days, weeks, months—whatever timeline we're operating on—you're going to have to decide whose side you're on. Mine or theirs."
"And if I choose wrong?"
"Then we'll both find out what happens."
The gas-lamp outside flickered, casting shifting shadows across Sera's face. For a moment, Kael saw the calculation behind her eyes—the weighing of risks, benefits, loyalties.
"My sister," Sera said quietly. "If she becomes relevant to whatever you're planning, you protect her. Not as a negotiation. Not as a condition. As a given."
"Agreed."
Sera extended her hand. Kael took it. Her grip was firm, steady, betraying nothing of whatever conflict churned beneath the surface.
"Then we have an understanding." She released his hand and moved toward the door. "I'll start digging tonight. The Guild archives have restricted sections. It might take a day or two to access them without raising flags."
"Be careful."
"Always." She paused at the threshold. "Kael. Whatever the Shadow Court offers you at that meeting—whatever they promise, whatever they reveal—remember that they've been playing this game for centuries. They've manipulated people far more experienced than you. Don't let them make you into a weapon you don't want to become."
She left before he could respond.
Kael stood alone in the safehouse, surrounded by specimen jars and stained leather and the lingering smell of formaldehyde. His wounds throbbed with each heartbeat. His mind churned with fragments of information, half-truths, and dangerous implications.
The Shadow Court wanted him. The Orders feared him. The seals were failing. And somewhere in the depths of Thornwick's history lay secrets about the original Soul Reaver that might explain everything—including what Kael himself was becoming.
He checked the window. Dawn crept across the skyline, painting the steam-belching factories in shades of amber and gray. Somewhere out there, the cultist was reporting back to his handlers. Somewhere else, the Shadow Court was planning their next move. And somewhere in the city's underbelly, seventy-two seals continued their slow decay toward collapse.
Three days until the meeting. Three days to prepare. Three days to decide what kind of weapon he wanted to be—and whether he wanted to be one at all.

"It's me." Dorian's voice, muffled through the wood. "Open up. We have a problem."

"This is Shadow Court work?"
"Worse." Dorian tapped a circled entry. "This cell was supposed to be independent. Thrill-seekers, not ideologues. But look at the resources they accessed two weeks ago." He indicated a list of equipment. "Soul-essence vials. Abyssal anchor points. Ritual components worth a small fortune."

Twenty minutes. The cultist had been gone for thirty. Enough time for Sera to report, for Dorian to arrive, for the Shadow Court to adjust their timeline if they were monitoring communications.
The Shadow Court wanted him scared. Wanted him isolated. Wanted him doubting everyone around him.
They were succeeding.

Somewhere else, the cult cells continued their coordinated assault on the seals. And somewhere in the space between truth and deception, Kael was being slowly encircled by enemies he couldn't identify and allies he couldn't trust.
Three days until the meeting. Three days until everything changed.
He just hoped he survived long enough to see which direction the change went.
