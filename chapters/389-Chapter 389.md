The stolen knowledge bloomed behind his eyes—patterns of combat movement, threat assessment matrices, the dead hunter's professional analysis lodged in Kael's skull like a splinter of glass. His stomach lurched. The essence always settled wrong, foreign instincts occupying space meant for his own.
A second figure lunged from the archway. Kael pivoted, his wounded arm screaming protest, and caught the strike on his bracer. The impact rattled his teeth, jolted through his shoulder socket, numbed his fingers to the elbow. This hunter moved differently—faster, trained, spiritual energy coiling around his blade like serpentine fire, the glow casting dancing shadows that stretched up the curved walls. The air around him crackled, ozone-sharp, carrying the distinct char of channelled essence.
"The Shadow Court sends its regards," the man snarled.
Kael's counter came instinctive, a driving thrust that the hunter deflected. Steel shrieked against steel, sparks showering the darkness between them. They separated, circled, boots scraping grit from the ancient floor. Blood dripped from Kael's fingers onto inscriptions older than the Orders themselves, the symbols pulsing faintly at the contact—feeding, tasting, recognizing something in him.
Thornwick's undercroft stretched beneath the city in arterial passages, forgotten since the Abyssal Wars. Kael had followed whispers here—fragmented accounts of a tomb that predated the Six Orders, a burial site containing answers about his abilities. The first Soul Reaver had walked these tunnels. The legends claimed he died sealing the Great Rift.
Legends, it seemed, required revision.
The air hung thick with the taste of centuries: mineral and bone-dry dust layered over something older, something that pressed against the back of Kael's throat. The walls sweated moisture in patterns that suggested deliberate carving—sigils worn smooth by age but still humming with residual power. Kael's shoulder brushed one as he circled, and cold bit through his sleeve, shot up his neck, set his teeth on edge.
The hunter attacked again. Kael met him blow for blow, each exchange driving them deeper into the chamber. His arm hung useless now, the wound from the first hunter reopened by a deflected strike, blood running hot down his forearm and dripping from his fingertips. Each pulse of his heart pushed more warmth out of him. Pain meant nothing. Pain was information, nothing more. The Soul Reaver's hunger churned beneath his skin, demanding satisfaction, coiling in his gut like something alive.
Not yet. Not from allies who fell.
But this hunter wore no Order insignia. His garb bore no affiliation—plain black, unmarked, the uniform of deniable assets. The fabric was good quality, though. Expensive. Someone with coin funded this operation.
"You killed my partner," the hunter said, circling. His weight shifted to the balls of his feet, blade held low in a guard Kael's borrowed instincts recognized from the Tactical Foresight. "That corpse still steams. You understand what you've become?"
"I understand enough."
"Do you?" The hunter's smile held no warmth. His lips stretched thin across his teeth, and the torchlight caught something in his expression—not fear, not hatred. Pity. "The first Reaver thought he controlled his gift. Thought he served humanity. The Orders erased him for that arrogance. You think your circumstances different?"
Kael said nothing. His eyes tracked the man's footwork, the tension in his shoulders, the tells that preceded each strike—the micro-shift of weight to his left hip, the flick of his wrist before a diagonal cut. The Tactical Foresight from the fallen hunter pulsed through his awareness—tactical assessments he hadn't earned, insights that felt borrowed. Stolen. The dead man's expertise whispering suggestions Kael hadn't asked for.
They crashed together again. Kael's blade locked against the hunter's, faces inches apart. The man's breath was hot, sour, carrying the tang of whatever stimulant kept him fighting through exhaustion. Blood from his split lip spattered Kael's cheek.
"Zarek wants you alive. The Court disagrees. We disagree about many things."
The name landed like a physical blow. Zarek. The architect of Kael's false execution, the man who had framed him for murders committed by other hands. Kael's jaw clenched, molars grinding against each other. His grip on the blade shifted, knuckles white. Chasing ghosts through Thornwick's undercroft, Kael had lost track of how many days passed since his resurrection. The city above him moved through its cycles of light and dark, commerce and prayer, while he crawled through its bones hunting answers that kept slipping further away.
"Zarek sent you?" Kael forced the words through gritted teeth.
"Zarek sent us all. Different employers, same destination." The hunter headbutted him. Stars exploded across Kael's vision, white-hot points that scattered across his peripheral. His skull rang. The taste of copper flooded his mouth. "This tomb holds answers he needs. You're collateral damage."
Kael staggered back, tasting copper and something else—essence residue, the bitter aftertaste of spiritual energy discharged at close range. The hunter pressed forward, spiritual energy flaring brighter around his blade, the serpentine coils intensifying until the weapon keened with audible power. The chamber's ancient torches guttered in phantom wind, shadows leaping and twisting across stone carved with symbols Kael couldn't read. The symbols seemed to recoil from the light, retreating into the grooves as if the carvings themselves flinched from exposure.
Answers Zarek needed. The original Soul Reaver's resting place—Kael had found it without knowing. His investigation into cult activity, the whispers about Thornwick's organized sigils, all leading here. The hunter's presence confirmed what Kael suspected: the cults weren't random. Something coordinated them.
Something with resources, reach, and purpose.
The hunter's next strike opened a gash across Kael's ribs. The blade bit deep, parting leather and flesh in a single vicious draw. Kael retreated two steps, breathing hard, each inhale sending fire through the wound. Blood soaked through his shirt, warm and spreading, making the fabric cling to his skin. His coat grew heavy with it, pulling at his shoulders. The Soul Reaver's mark burned against his chest like a brand pressed to bare flesh—the sigil seared into him during his resurrection, pulsing in time with his heartbeat, pulsing faster now.
"You feel it," the hunter observed, watching Kael's hand press against his ribs. "The hunger. The Court knows what you are, Reaver. Knows what you take from the fallen. That power belongs to the Abyss. Humanity was never meant to wield it."
"Humanity didn't wield it. One man did. A thousand years ago."
"And look what happened to him." The hunter gestured toward the chamber's depths, toward a sarcophagus Kael had noticed but not approached. The stone lid bore carvings that seemed to drink the torchlight, swallowing illumination than reflecting it. The air around it was different—colder, heavier, carrying a pressure that built behind Kael's eyes. "Sealed away by the Orders he served. His own allies feared what he'd become. You think your situation different?"
Kael's fingers tightened on his blade. The leather wrapping groaned under his grip. The hunter advanced, confidence growing, his steps measured and precise. He expected Kael to weaken, to slow, to bleed out before reaching him.
The Tactical Foresight whispered options. Angular attacks, deflection patterns, positioning matrices—the dead hunter's expertise painting probable outcomes across Kael's awareness like an overlay. Kael dismissed them. The stolen ability was borrowed, not earned. He relied on his own instincts, honed through months of survival since waking on the execution platform with the noose still loose around his neck and the hangman's confused face swimming above him.
The hunter lunged.
Kael sidestepped, catching the man's wrist and twisting. Bone cracked. The sound was wet, final. The blade clattered against stone, ringing in the chamber's acoustics. A palm strike to the sternum folded the hunter double, air leaving his lungs in a ragged burst. A rising knee shattered his nose—cartilage giving way with a crunch Kael felt through his kneecap. He collapsed, whimpering, blood streaming between fingers pressed to his face, the red threading between his knuckles and pooling on the ancient stone.
Kael stood over him, blade ready. The hunter's eyes held defiance still, even through pain—the look of a man who expected to die and had made peace with it long before entering this tomb.
"Finish it," the man spat. Blood and saliva threaded from his split lips. "Add me to your collection. The Court will send more. They'll never stop."
"The Shadow Court." Kael's voice came flat. "They monitor me."
"They monitor all Reavers. All anomalies. All threats to the seal network." The hunter laughed, wet and ugly, the sound bubbling in his throat. "You think you're special? The Whisper King marked you the moment you woke. Every Order in Thornwick knows what you are. The only question is who claims you first."
The Whisper King. Another name from legend, another Entity mentioned in fragmented texts Kael had pieced together during his months as a fugitive. The Blood God, the Green Mother, the Whisper King—72 Entities sealed behind the membrane between worlds. Kael had assumed the stories were metaphor, historical references to human conflicts dressed in theological language.
He was revising that assumption too.
The chamber pressed close around him. The walls seemed to lean inward, the ceiling lower than it had been when he entered. The torchlight flickered with a rhythm that matched his pulse—or his pulse matched the flames. In this place, beneath centuries of accumulated stone and silence, the distinction between the physical and the spiritual had begun to blur.
"Why would Zarek want this tomb?" Kael asked.
The hunter's smile turned bloody. His teeth were stained red, gums swelling. "The original Reaver didn't die sealing the Great Rift. He became something else. Something the Orders couldn't destroy, only imprison. Zarek wants that power. The Court wants to ensure he doesn't get it."
"You could have killed me when I entered. Why wait?"
"Hunters need confirmation. Evidence. A dead Reaver tells no tales about what he found here." The hunter's eyes flicked toward the sarcophagus. The movement was brief, instinctive, but Kael caught it. "The Court needs to know what's in that tomb. Whether it should be destroyed or preserved. You were a means to that end."
Kael processed the information. The hunter had let him proceed, watching, cataloging. He'd led them directly to the original Reaver's resting place without knowing. Every step Kael took had been observed, recorded, reported.
The cult activity in Thornwick wasn't random—it was reconnaissance. Organized, methodical, funded by interests that wanted the seal network maintained or disrupted. The Shadow Court, the Orders, Zarek's faction, possibly others. All circling each other, all watching him.
A pawn being moved across a board he couldn't see.
His stomach tightened. The realization settled into his gut like a stone dropped into still water, ripples spreading outward through his understanding. He'd thought himself the hunter. Instead, he was the prey—walked into position, allowed to bleed, kept alive until he served his purpose.
"Tell me about the Whisper King," Kael said.
The hunter laughed again, weaker now. The sound was thin, reedy, a dying man's last amusement. "The Whisper King doesn't concern himself with Reavers. He concerns himself with the Veil, with the seals, with the barrier holding back 72 Entities. You're a ripple in his ocean, Reaver. Nothing more."
"And if he decides I'm a threat?"
"Then you die. Like the first Reaver died. Like every Soul Reaver before you." The hunter's defiance flickered. His voice dropped, the bravado cracking to reveal something underneath. "Some things can't be controlled. Your power proves that. The Orders learned that lesson. The Court learned it. Zarek will learn it too."
Kael studied the man. Wounded, defeated, but still burning with conviction. The hunter believed what he said. Believed the Soul Reaver's gift was corruption waiting to happen, a ticking clock toward inevitable damnation.
Maybe he was right. Kael had felt the hunger after every harvest, the whisper of voices that weren't his own. The abilities he'd stolen from the dead didn't vanish after use. They lodged in his consciousness like splinters, occasionally surfacing unbidden. He woke some nights with memories that didn't belong to him, skills he hadn't practiced, fears from lives he'd ended. A swordsman's terror of falling. A thief's habitual scan of exits. A scholar's obsessive need to categorize, to understand, to name.
The dead didn't stay buried in his mind. They lived there, crowding the space behind his thoughts, pressing against the edges of his identity with fingers that weren't real.
The Soul Reaver marked its bearer. That much was undeniable.
But marked didn't mean conquered. Kael had made choices since his resurrection, hard ones, ugly ones. He'd harvested enemies and walked away. He'd spared allies when the hunger demanded otherwise. He was still himself.
Mostly.
The distinction felt thinner in this place. The walls pressed closer. The air thickened with each breath, carrying a taste that wasn't physical—resonance, accumulated spiritual weight, the residue of whatever had been sealed here a millennium ago. His skin prickled with it.
The hunter groaned, struggling to rise. Kael pressed his blade to the man's throat. The steel settled against stubble and skin, dimpling the flesh. One thrust. One harvest. The hunger surged at the thought, heat spreading through his chest, and Kael's hand trembled before he steadied it.
"Last chance," he said. "The Whisper King. The tomb. Zarek's plans. Talk."
"Or you'll harvest me?" The hunter met his eyes without flinching. His pupils were dilated, the stimulant still burning through his system, keeping him conscious when his body wanted to shut down. "Do it. The Court knows what I know. Killing me changes nothing except adding a Reaver murder to your list of crimes. Zarek's people are coming regardless. The tomb's secrets won't stay buried."
"How long?"
"Zarek's team entered through the eastern passages a short while ago. You'll have company soon." The hunter smiled, blood on his teeth, the red gleaming in the torchlight. "Unless the Whisper King's servants reach you first. They move faster."
Kael's blood chilled. The warmth drained from his extremities, pulled inward toward his core as if his body was preparing for something. The Whisper King's servants. Not hunters, not cultists—Entities themselves, or beings touched by them. The Abyssal Wars had ended a thousand years ago, but the Entities hadn't vanished. They'd been sealed, contained, watched.
Watching worked both directions.
The hunter coughed, tasting his own blood. His head tipped back, and he spoke toward the ceiling as if reciting scripture. "The first Reaver tried to save humanity. Fought the Entities, protected the seals, served the greater good. The Orders killed him for it. Called him corrupted, dangerous, a threat to stability. Sound familiar?"
It did. Kael thought of his own resurrection, the execution that hadn't taken, the Orders' immediate response. Hunt the anomaly. Destroy the threat. Maintain the seal network.
History repeating. Different century, same fears.
The stone beneath his feet was worn smooth in patterns that suggested centuries of foot traffic. Others had stood here, in this exact spot, asking the same questions. The realization was cold and unwelcome. He wasn't the first to reach this chamber seeking answers. He was only the latest.
"I'm not him," Kael said. "I don't serve the Orders."
"No. You're something new." The hunter's voice dropped. The defiance bled out of it, replaced by something quieter. Fear, maybe. Or awe. "The Whisper King doesn't fear the Orders. Doesn't fear their hunters or their seal rituals. He fears Soul Reavers. We're the only thing that can truly kill an Entity, strip away their Abyssal essence, end them permanently. That's why the first Reaver had to die. That's why you'll have to die too."
The weight of the words settled over Kael like a burial shroud. Soul Reavers could kill Entities permanently. The one thing the seal network couldn't accomplish, a human marked by the gift could accomplish. The Orders had known this for a millennium. They'd buried the knowledge, killed the only Reaver who could threaten their carefully maintained barrier.
The six Orders fought the Abyss, maintained the seals, protected humanity. That was the public story. The hidden truth was darker: they fought to maintain a system that kept Entities imprisoned, not destroyed. Killing an Entity permanently wasn't the goal. Control was.
The hunter watched understanding dawn across Kael's face. His swollen eyes tracked the micro-expressions, the tightening around Kael's jaw, the way his blade hand shifted. "Now you see. The war never ended. It just went underground."
A distant rumble shook dust from the chamber ceiling. Fine particles drifted down, catching the torchlight, turning the air hazy. Kael's head snapped toward the archway leading deeper into the tomb. The sound came again—footsteps, multiple sets, approaching from the sarcophagus direction. The rhythm was wrong for a single group. Two sets, maybe three, moving in coordinated formation.
Not from behind him. From ahead.
The hunter laughed, a bubbling, wet sound that ended in a cough. Blood sprayed from his lips. "Zarek's people. They bypassed the traps you triggered. Congratulations, Reaver. You led them right to the prize."
Kael had seconds to decide. The hunter posed no immediate threat—broken nose, crushed wrist, barely conscious. Behind him, the entrance archway offered escape, but that path led through the bodies of the first hunter, through evidence of his crimes. The Orders would find them eventually. His participation in another massacre would be confirmed.
Ahead, the footsteps grew louder. Zarek's team, or the Whisper King's servants, or something worse. The sarcophagus waited beyond them, containing secrets about the original Reaver's fate. Answers Kael needed if he intended to survive whatever was coming.
Survival. The only currency that mattered anymore.
He sheathed his blade and strode toward the deeper chamber. The hunter called after him, something about traps and death and regret, but Kael didn't look back. The words faded behind him, swallowed by the tomb's hungry acoustics. The hunger in his chest pulsed in rhythm with his heartbeat, stronger now, as if the proximity to the original Reaver's remains awakened something dormant. Something that recognized kinship across a thousand years of silence.
The corridor narrowed, walls pressing close. Kael's shoulder brushed ancient stonework carved with symbols that hurt to look at directly. They seemed to shift when viewed peripherally, forming patterns that suggested meaning just beyond comprehension. The Tactical Foresight screamed warnings he ignored. The stolen ability threw threat assessments across his awareness—structural instability, spiritual residue, containment deterioration—but Kael pushed through. Borrowed knowledge was borrowed trust. He trusted his own feet.
The temperature dropped. Each breath misted before him, white vapor curling in the torchlight that flickered ahead—not the guttering flames from the entrance chamber. Someone had lit new torches recently. The air grew colder with each step, biting through his torn coat, raising gooseflesh along his arms. The blood on his shirt cooled and clotted, pulling at his skin with each movement. His ribs screamed with every breath, the gashed flesh parting and re-parting as his lungs expanded and contracted.
The corridor opened into a vaulted chamber.
The ceiling soared above him, lost in darkness, supported by pillars carved with the same shifting symbols. At its center stood the sarcophagus, lid thrown back, empty inside. The stone was black—not quarried black but light-eating black, consuming illumination and returning nothing. The interior of the coffin seemed to breathe, a faint draft rising from within carrying a smell like old dust and something else. Something sweet, organic, preserved.
But that wasn't what stopped Kael's breath.
Figures stood around the sarcophagus. Three of them, robed in gray, hoods drawn. They turned as one when Kael entered. The movement was synchronized in a way that hurt to watch—three bodies moving as a single organism, joints rotating without transition, fabric settling in patterns that didn't follow natural physics.
Their faces were wrong—too smooth, lacking features, like masks carved from pale flesh. No eyes. No nose. No mouth. Just blank expanse of skin-toned nothing that still gave the impression of attention. Of focus. Of being seen.
"Kael Ashborn," one said. Its voice came from everywhere and nowhere, a whisper that filled the chamber. The sound bypassed his ears entirely, forming words in the space behind his skull. "The Whisper King sends his regards."
The Faceless One. Kael's hand found his blade. The Entity had planted fragments of itself in three individuals—the whisper from his resurrection, a presence he'd felt watching him for months. He'd assumed it was metaphorical, a manner of speaking, a symbolic representation of Abyssal attention.
He was revising that assumption too.
The chamber's cold deepened. Frost crept across the flagstones from the Entities' feet, crystalline patterns spreading outward in fractal designs that matched the symbols on the walls. The torches didn't gutter—they dimmed, light itself retreating from whatever these beings radiated.
"You carry our brother's gift," another Faceless One said. "The first Reaver was ours before he was human. His power flows through your veins."
"Brothers." Kael's voice came steadier than he felt. His pulse hammered against his throat, his body's instincts screaming to run, to fight, to do anything but stand and converse. His grip on the blade hilt was crushing. "The Whisper King and the Faceless One. Related."
"The Entities share essence. We were one, before the seal fractured us." The first speaker stepped forward, its movement wrong—too fluid, joints bending in directions human anatomy couldn't accommodate. Its knees flexed backward. Its shoulders rolled in their sockets with a wet sound. "The first Reaver threatened that unity. You threaten it again."
"He sealed the Great Rift," Kael said. "Protected your prison."
"He delayed the inevitable." The third Faceless One hadn't spoken before. Its voice carried harmonics that scraped against Kael's mind, frequencies that shouldn't exist, that pressed against his consciousness like thumbs against eyeballs. "The seal weakens. The Veil tears. Seventy-two will walk the Material realm once more. The Reaver's gift ensures we return stronger than before."
Kael's grip tightened. The Entities wanted him for the same reason the Orders feared him—Soul Reaver abilities could kill them permanently. But these beings spoke of returning, of unity, of the seal's failure. The war wasn't only continuing. It was escalating.
"The tomb," Kael said. "What's really in this tomb?"
The Faceless Ones exchanged glances, or performed whatever passed for communication between them. The first one smiled—a terrible expression on a face that shouldn't have been able to smile, the skin stretching and splitting to approximate the shape of human amusement without understanding it. Blood welled in the cracks, black and sluggish.
"The original Reaver didn't die sealing the Great Rift. He died trying to destroy us. We made certain he failed." The Entity gestured toward the empty sarcophagus. Its hand had too many joints. "His body is here. His power is here. His failure is here. You may claim what remains, Soul Reaver. Whether you use it wisely remains to be seen."
"We offer a choice," the second one continued. "Join us. Become what the first Reaver refused to become. Serve the unity than oppose it. Or die here, as he should have died a thousand years ago."
Kael assessed his options. Three Entities, possibly more in the chamber's shadows. His blade, already notched from recent combat. His wounds, still bleeding freely—the rib gash seeping with each breath, the arm wound throbbing in time with his pulse. The corridor behind him offered retreat, but Zarek's people were coming that direction. The sarcophagus offered answers, but accepting the Entities' offer meant...
What? Becoming like them? Losing whatever remained of himself? The hunger in his chest responded to their presence, surging against his ribs, straining toward them as if recognizing something kindred. The sensation was nauseating.
Footsteps. Different from the Entities' wrong movements. Normal footsteps, human, approaching from a side passage Kael hadn't noticed. The Faceless Ones turned, attention shifting. Their synchronized movement broke for an instant—three heads turning at slightly different speeds, the coordination faltering.
"Interference," one hissed. The whisper sharpened to a sound that made Kael's ears ring. "The Shadow Court's puppet approaches."
Zarek emerged from the shadows, blade drawn, eyes fixed on the Entities than Kael. His face bore fresh burns, spiritual damage from whatever traps he'd bypassed—the skin blistered and weeping along his jaw, one eye swollen half-shut. He moved like a man who'd expected resistance and found worse. His breathing was ragged, his left arm hanging stiff at his side.
"The tomb's sealed," Zarek said. "The Reaver's remains contained. You can't have them."
"We already have what we need," the first Faceless One replied. "The vessel stands before us. What lies in that sarcophagus is only history."
Zarek's eyes found Kael. Something complicated passed between them—enmity, recognition, the acknowledgment of shared circumstances. Zarek had framed Kael for murder. Kael had every reason to kill him on sight. The burned man standing before him bore little resemblance to the polished architect who'd orchestrated his downfall. The traps had taken their toll. Whatever Zarek had expected to find in this tomb, it had cost him to reach it.
But Zarek had also come here hunting the Entities, not serving them.
"Work together?" Zarek asked.
The Faceless Ones laughed—a chorus of whispers that set Kael's teeth on edge, that made his fillings ache and his vision blur at the edges. The sound was pollution, auditory poison, frequencies that scraped against the inside of his skull.
The third one spoke again, its voice carrying weight that pressed against Kael's consciousness. His knees buckled. He locked them, forcing himself upright through will alone.
"The Whisper King offers you power, vessel. The Shadow Court's master offers you survival. Both require service. Both demand payment." The Entity leaned close, its featureless face inches from Kael's own. He could smell it—something like ozone and rotting flowers, sweet and corrosive at once. Its breath, if it had breath, carried the stench of places that had never known light. "What do you offer yourself, Soul Reaver?"
The question hung in the cold air. Kael's wounds pulsed. His ribs leaked. The Entities watched with faces that couldn't watch. Zarek waited with his blade drawn and his burns weeping. The sarcophagus waited, black and empty and breathing.
And somewhere in the darkness beyond the chamber, more footsteps approached—human, numerous, the sound of a force that meant business. Boots on stone, disciplined rhythm, coordinated movement. Not hunters. Soldiers.
The hunter had been right. The tomb's secrets wouldn't stay buried.
Neither would the dead.
Kael drew his blade and stepped forward.
