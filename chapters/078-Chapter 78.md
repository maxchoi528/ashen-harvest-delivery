The fog swallowed the lantern light three streets before Kael heard the patrol.
He flattened against a damp brick wall, breath shallow, fingers pressed to the cold surface. Two figures moved through the mist ahead, their armored boots grinding against cobblestones slick with condensation. Order of Dawn insignia gleamed on their shoulders—crescents crossed by a blade.
"Still no sign of the Reaver." The taller one spoke, voice muffled. "Lieutenant wants the Fogmire District swept by dawn."
"Manpower's stretched thin. We've got cult activity near the old tanneries and that thing feeding in the Undercroft." His companion spat. "Pick your poison."
Kael didn't move until their footsteps faded into the fog. The Order of Dawn operated openly here, emboldened by the seal degradation. They searched for him—a death-row prisoner who had crawled out of his own grave and learned to drink power from corpses.
The system notification hummed at the edge of his consciousness, a familiar whisper:
[Soul Reaver: Awakened Stage. Spiritual reserves: 73%. Status: hunted.]
Thornwick had become hostile territory. Three weeks ago, he'd established a base with Lyra in the condemned tenements of Fogmire District, far from Order eyes. Three weeks of building a fragile sanctuary in the gaslit ruins of a city that wanted him dead.
The streets here told stories in the language of decay. Rusted pipe-work dripped condensation into flooded gutters. Steam vents hissed from broken streetlamps, casting orange glows through the murk. Somewhere distant, a factory whistle screamed the shift change, and the sounds of poverty—drunken arguments, crying children, the clatter of illegal gambling dens—rose like background static.
Kael moved through the fog with practiced silence. He'd mapped three patrol routes, four escape corridors, and two locations where the Order stationed sentries. Tonight's route took him through the Ashpit Passage—a narrow alley carved between collapsed buildings, named for the ash that fell like snow when the foundries worked.
The passage reeked of burnt coal and something fouler underneath. Rotting refuse, urine, the particular smell of a city that had stopped pretending to care for its underclass.
He made it fifteen steps into the passage before the temperature dropped.
Kael stopped. His skin prickled with the familiar wrongness that preceded spiritual manifestation. The fog here thickened, swirling in patterns that defied the wind. Ice crystals formed on the brick walls, spreading outward from a central point.
Something waited in the darkness ahead.
"Little thief," a voice rasped. Child's voice, twisted by something that had forgotten what childhood meant. "You carry death in your hands. I smell it."
Kael's left hand drifted toward the knife at his belt. "Show yourself."
The fog parted like a curtain being drawn. What stood there might have been a boy once—eight years old, maybe nine, wearing the tattered remains of a newsboy cap and a shirt that hung to its knees. But the eyes were wrong. They glowed with pale blue light, revealing a hunger that had calcified into obsession. Frost crawled across its exposed skin in fractal patterns.
[ENTITY DETECTED: Revenant (Street Urchin). Threat Level: Moderate. Spiritual signature: fragmented soul bound by unfinished purpose.]
The system offered assessment, but Kael needed no classification. He'd encountered revenants before—echoes of the dead who couldn't accept their ending. This one reeked of the Abyss, touched by spiritual contamination that turned grief into something predatory.
"What do you want?" Kael kept his voice level.
The revenant tilted its head, moving with the jerky uncoordinated motion of something that had forgotten how bodies worked. "Want? I want what you have. The gift. The power to take and never give." It smiled, and the expression cracked the grey pallor of its face like ice breaking on a winter pond. "I've been watching you, death-thief. You walk through this city like you own it. Taking from the fallen. Growing stronger while the rest of us fade."
"I'm not interested in your approval."
"Never said I wanted yours." The revenant's hands elongated, fingers stretching into that gleamed like black ice. "But you have something I need. And I've learned that when you want something in this city, you take it. Before someone takes it from you."
It lunged.
The attack came faster than Kael expected from something so decayed. The revenant covered the distance between them in a heartbeat, claws raking toward his face. Kael threw himself backward, felt one claw catch his sleeve and tear through fabric to score a line across his forearm. Blood welled up, hot against the cold air.
He rolled, came up with his knife slashing outward. The blade bit into the revenant's side, and the creature shrieked—a sound like glass grinding against stone. Black ichor oozed from the wound instead of blood.
"Physical weapons hurt," it hissed, circling. "Good to know. Most of the Order doesn't figure that out until too late."
Kael wiped his knife on his coat. "You're organized. Most revenants just attack mindlessly."
"Mindless." The revenant laughed, and frost spread across the ground where it stepped. "Is that what you think? I'm not mindless. I'm hungry. There's a difference." It lunged again.
This time Kael was ready. He sidestepped, drove his knee into the revenant's midsection as it passed, felt the impact of bone against something that was no longer entirely solid. The revenant stumbled, and Kael followed with a backhand slash that opened a gash across its throat.
Still, the creature kept coming. Revenants didn't feel pain the way living things did. They operated on spiritual momentum, driven by the obsession that bound them to the mortal plane.
"What binds you?" Kael asked, buying time to think. His Soul Reaver ability required physical contact, but getting close enough while those claws were swinging was dangerous.
"Binds me?" The revenant's eyes narrowed. "You think you can break a revenant's chains with questions? I was nine when they threw me into the river. Nine years old. I stole bread from a merchant's cart. They held me underwater until the bubbles stopped." The words came faster, tumbling over each other. "I didn't even get to see the river. Just hands pushing down. Just water in my lungs. I was so hungry. I just wanted bread."
The confession emerged raw, unfiltered—the obsession that animated this thing laid bare. In the Order's theology, the Abyssal Entities consumed souls. In Kael's experience, the Abyss made appetites more honest.
"You want my power," Kael said. "But you don't understand what you're asking for."
"Enlighten me." The revenant feinted left, struck right.
Kael blocked with his forearm, felt claws sink into flesh, and grabbed the creature's wrist with his bloody hand. The pain was immediate—a bright flare of agony that his body registered before his mind caught up. But pain was information. Pain meant he was close enough.
Soul Reaver activated.
The sensation was unlike anything the natural world offered. Kael's consciousness slid through the boundary of his own skin, extended into the revenant's fractured existence, and what he found there churned his stomach. A child's memory, preserved in spiritual amber: cold water, flailing limbs, the absolute injustice of a death for bread. Layered beneath it, the Abyssal contamination that had twisted grief into hunger.
[Soul Harvest initiated. Contact duration: 0.a few heartbeats. Extraction in progress...]
"Don't," the revenant screamed. It thrashed, trying to pull away, but Kael held fast. "Don't take this from me. It's all I have left."
"You don't have anything," Kael said through gritted teeth. "You died forty years ago. Whatever you think you're holding onto—it's not you."
The harvest pulled fragments free. Kael received them in broken pieces: a name—Tomás—lost to time. A final memory of sunlight on water, glimpsed from a cart before the hands pushed him under. And something else, something that felt like it belonged to the revenant but wasn't native to it.
[Soul Harvest Complete. Extracted: Fragmented Memory (Tomás). Spiritual Residue (Abyssal-touched): 14 units. Acquired Skill: Shadow Blend (Passive).]
The revenant collapsed like a puppet with cut strings. Frost retreated from the cobblestones. The blue glow faded from its eyes, leaving them dark and empty—finally dead, after forty years of unfinished business.
Kael released the corpse and stumbled back, his arm screaming where the claws had torn flesh. Blood dripped onto the ash-covered ground. The wound wasn't deep, but revenant claws carried spiritual contamination. He'd need to cleanse it soon, before infection set in.
[Soul Reaver: Spiritual reserves: 71%. Minor contamination detected. Recommend cleansing within a full day.]
He examined the skill that had transferred. Shadow Blend—passive ability allowing the user to move more quietly in low-light conditions, reducing the range at which movement could be detected by sound-based senses. Useful, given his current lifestyle.
Kael crouched beside the revenant's remains. The body looked smaller now, diminished—the spiritual energy that had animated it dispersed into the fog. In a few hours, nothing would remain but ash and old bones.
"The Order's patrols are getting bolder," he muttered. "Cultists running organized cells. Revenants appearing in places they shouldn't be. Something's changing."
The city felt different than it had three weeks ago. The seal degradation was accelerating, or people were reacting to rumors of it. Either way, Thornwick was entering a period of instability that would make survival harder for someone like him.
He should harvest more thoroughly—the corpse was still fresh enough to yield additional information about the Abyssal contamination. But footsteps echoed from the passage entrance. Multiple sets, moving with military precision.
"Contact reports a spiritual disturbance in Ashpit Passage," someone called out. "Single individual, possibly hostile. Move in formation."
Order of Dawn. They'd detected the revenant's spiritual signature. Kael had minutes at best before they reached him.
He made his decision quickly. The corpse would have to wait—his survival came first. Kael moved toward the far end of the passage, toward the collapsed section that led into the Undercroft. Behind him, lantern light flickered against the fog.
The passage narrowed as he ran. Broken furniture, collapsed crates, and debris from decades of neglect formed a maze that Kael had memorized during his first week in the district. He squeezed through a gap between two fallen beams, heard the scrape of armored boots on brick as the patrol reached the revenant's remains.
"Dead," one of them reported. "Recent. Still warm."
"Examine the wound pattern. Revenant kill, but the termination technique—" The speaker paused. "This looks like Soul Reaver extraction. The Lieutenant needs to know. Fan out. The Reaver can't have gotten far."
Kael didn't wait to hear more. He dropped into the Undercroft through a hidden hatch, landed hard on damp stone, and let the darkness swallow him. Above, boots thundered across the passage. Voices gave orders, fragments of which filtered down through the gaps in the floorboards.
"—search the entire Undercroft section—"
"Request additional units. If the Reaver's gone to ground—"
Kael moved through the Undercroft's familiar gloom, his new Shadow Blend skill making each footfall silent as thought. The underground network stretched beneath Fogmire District like a second city—sewers, forgotten tunnels, the remnants of pre-war infrastructure that the surface dwellers had abandoned. Down here, he was harder to track. Down here, the Order's lanterns couldn't reach.
But they weren't the only ones who used these tunnels.
Twenty minutes of careful navigation brought him to a junction he'd identified during his initial mapping. Three tunnels converged here, leading to different sections of the Undercroft. Water dripped from somewhere above, echoing in the silence. The air smelled of mold, rust, and the particular staleness of places that hadn't seen sunlight in decades.
He stopped. His instincts—sharpened by weeks of survival in hostile territory—registered the wrongness before his eyes confirmed it.
Someone waited in the shadows of the left-hand tunnel.
"You move quietly." The voice was female, cultured, carrying the accent of the upper districts. "The Shadow Blend skill suits you. I wondered what the revenant had been screaming about."
Kael's knife appeared in his hand. "Show yourself."
A figure stepped into the faint light filtering down from a grate above. Tall, lean, dressed in clothing that was both practical and expensive—a contradiction that screamed of wealth that didn't need to prove itself. Her face was angular, handsome in the way of people who'd never had to worry about their next meal. Dark hair pulled back severely. Eyes that reflected light like a cat's.
"Soul Reaver Kael Ashborn." She spoke his name like a fact. "I've been observing your activities in this district for some time. You operate with efficiency. Discretion, when necessary. You're building a base of operations, acquiring resources, establishing patterns." She smiled, thin and knowing. "The Order of Dawn would find this information valuable. So would several other parties."
"Who are you?"
"My name is Sera. I represent interests that prefer to remain anonymous." She raised a hand, showing empty palms—a gesture of peace that didn't reach her eyes. "I mean you no harm. If I did, I wouldn't have announced my presence. You would have disappeared."
Kael didn't lower his knife. "How did you know about the skill I just harvested?"
"The revenant's scream carried spiritual resonance. Certain individuals can interpret such things." Sera tilted her head, studying him. "You're cautious. Good. Caution keeps you alive in cities like Thornwick. But caution also limits your options. You're surviving, Mr. Ashborn, but you're not thriving. You're reacting to threats than anticipating them."
"I don't need lectures from mysterious strangers."
"No. You need allies. Resources. Information." Sera's smile sharpened. "You need to know who your enemies are, what they want, and how they're connected. Right now, you're fighting blind. The Order of Dawn hunts you. Cultists move through this city with increasing boldness. Something is destabilizing the seal network, and when the seals fail—" She spread her hands. "Well. You've seen what a single revenant can do. Imagine seventy-two entities tearing through the fabric of reality."
The number hit Kael like cold water. Seventy-two. The sealed rifts. The Abyssal Entities that the Six Orders had imprisoned during the wars that predated living memory.
"You're talking about the Great Rift," he said slowly. "The seal degradation."
"The Great Rift is one of seventy-two. Each seal is weakening. The Order knows this. The cult knows this. The question is what they're doing about it." Sera stepped closer, and Kael's grip tightened on his knife. She stopped out of arm's reach. "The cult is organized, Mr. Ashborn. What you've seen—random violence, isolated incidents, chaotic ritual—that's the surface. Beneath it, a structure is forming. Cells. Networks. Supply lines. They're preparing for something."
"How do you know this?"
"My organization has resources. Eyes in places the Orders don't monitor. Connections that span the continent." Sera's expression remained unreadable. "We believe you're valuable. Not for what you are, but for what you could become. A Soul Reaver who operates outside the established power structures, who takes from the dead without the moral constraints the Orders impose, who could potentially harvest information that no one else can access—"
"You want to use me."
"I want to offer you a choice." Sera reached into her coat and withdrew something small—a glass vial containing liquid that glowed faintly blue. Soul-essence. A hundred gold sovereigns' worth, at minimum. "One vial. Pure quality. Enough to sustain your spiritual reserves for weeks, or to purchase information, equipment, allies. Consider it a demonstration of good faith."
Kael stared at the vial. His reserves were adequate, but the contamination in his arm would drain them faster as his body fought the infection. Resources mattered. Resources kept him alive.
"What's the cost?"
"Occasional cooperation. When we need something retrieved from the dead—information, items, sometimes the body itself cleaned of evidence—we call on you. In exchange, we provide intelligence, safe houses, resources. You continue to operate independently. We... Coordinate."
"And if I refuse?"
"Then you refuse. No consequences. No retaliation. We move on to other potential assets." Sera pocketed the vial, her expression suggesting the decision was entirely his. "But consider what you're facing. The Order of Dawn hunts you with increasing resources. The cult grows bolder. The seals weaken daily. In six months, Thornwick will be a very different city. Do you want to survive that change alone?"
Kael's mind raced through the implications. Accepting meant entanglement with an unknown organization, obligations he couldn't fully understand, risks he couldn't calculate. Refusing meant walking away from resources that could make the difference between life and death.
The wound on his arm throbbed. The contamination was spreading.
"Information," he said finally. "I want information first."
Sera nodded, unsurprised. "Reasonable. Ask your questions."
"The cult activity in Thornwick. You said they're organized. What do you know about their structure?"
"They operate in cells of three to seven members. Each cell handles a specific function—recruitment, supply, ritual preparation, violence. The cells don't know each other's identities. Communication flows upward through intermediaries." Sera's tone was clinical, detached. "We believe they're building toward a coordinated action. Something requiring multiple ritual sites, synchronized timing, significant spiritual energy. The target appears to be the seal network itself."
"They want to break the seals."
"They want to weaken them. Controlled degradation serves their purposes better than catastrophic failure." Sera studied his face. "The Six Orders maintain the seals through a ritual that requires Awakened participants at each of the seventy-two locations. If the cult can disrupt enough rituals simultaneously, the seals won't collapse—they'll become vulnerable. Penetrable."
"And then the Entities escape."
"Some of them. The ones whose cults have prepared vessels." Sera's expression flickered for the first time—something that might have been genuine concern. "The Abyssal Entities aren't demons, Mr. Ashborn. They're forces. Raw spiritual potential given form and will. Some want destruction. Some want absorption. Some want... Other things. The cult believes they can control which ones emerge, channel them toward goals that align with human ambition."
Kael had seen the Whisper King—or, the entity that wore that title. He'd felt the hunger in that presence, the vast and alien wanting that reduced human concerns to insignificance. The idea of cults deliberately inviting such forces into the world made his stomach turn.
"Why approach me? I'm one person. I can't stop a conspiracy."
"You can harvest from the dead." Sera's voice hardened. "The cult sacrifices. Their rituals leave corpses. Each corpse contains information about the ritual performed, the entity targeted, the purpose served. If you could access that data—if you could harvest knowledge of their plans before they're executed—"
"You're asking me to spy on them through their own dead."
"I'm asking you to use your abilities in service of preventing catastrophe." Sera retrieved the vial, held it between them. "The dead don't lie, Mr. Ashborn. Their final moments preserve truth with perfect accuracy. A Soul Reaver harvesting cultist remains could extract their orders, their timelines, their targets. You could give us warning. You could save lives."
Kael thought of the revenant—Tomás—who had died at nine years old for stealing bread. The cultists who had died for their beliefs probably felt equally justified. The moral calculus wasn't simple. But the world was simpler when it was dying.
"Show me proof," he said. "One successful harvest, one piece of intelligence that helps your organization. Then we'll discuss further cooperation."
Sera smiled, and this time it reached her eyes. "Acceptable. There's a cult ritual scheduled for tomorrow night, in the old foundry district. We have the location. We have the timing. If you attend, harvest the remains, and bring us what you extract, we'll consider it a successful partnership."
"And if I die?"
"Then you die having tried to prevent something terrible. Few can claim more." Sera extended her hand. "Do we have an agreement?"
Kael looked at her hand, at the vial in her other hand, at the tunnel stretching into darkness behind her. His arm ached. His reserves would need replenishment. His knowledge of the conspiracy was fragmentary at best.
The Order of Dawn was hunting him. The cult was growing stronger. The seals were weakening. And somewhere in this chaos, Lyra was maintaining their base, probably worried about his extended absence.
He took Sera's hand. Her grip was firm, professional.
"One harvest," he said. "Then we negotiate."
"Fair terms." Sera pressed the vial into his palm and released his hand. "The contamination in your arm will spread faster now that you've used Soul Reaver. Cleansing agents can be purchased at the Veiled Market, or—" She paused, as if reconsidering. "There's another option. The Mortician's Guild maintains facilities in the Undercroft. They can cleanse contamination, repair wounds, provide medical services to those who can pay. They don't ask questions about how injuries were sustained."
"The Mortician's Guild operates above ground. Why would they have facilities down here?"
"The legitimate operation is above ground. The real business happens below." Sera moved toward the tunnel she'd emerged from. "You have until dawn to decide. The cult ritual occurs at midnight, in the foundry district. If you want to harvest, you'll need to be there." She paused at the tunnel's edge. "Don't underestimate the cult, Mr. Ashborn. They have Awakened among their number. They've harvested abilities from the dead just as you have. Some of them have been doing it longer."
She vanished into the darkness.
Kael stood alone in the junction, the vial heavy in his hand, the wound in his arm pulsing with increasing urgency. Above him, the Order of Dawn searched for a Reaver they believed had killed a revenant. In the foundry district, cultists prepared for a ritual that might weaken the seals further. And somewhere in Thornwick, Lyra waited at their base, unaware of how the night had changed.
He could return to the base, cleanse his wound with supplies they had on hand, and process what he'd learned. He could visit the Mortician's Guild's underground facility and pay for faster, more thorough treatment. He could spend the night planning the cult infiltration, mapping the foundry district, preparing for a harvest that might yield intelligence worth dying for.
The night stretched ahead, full of choices.
Kael pocketed the vial, checked his knife, and began moving toward the surface. The fog above would be thinning by now, the patrol searching in the wrong direction, his trail gone cold in the Undercroft's darkness.
Tomorrow, he would decide about the cult. Tonight, he needed to survive.
He climbed the ladder toward a maintenance hatch that opened into an abandoned bakery three blocks from his base. His foot slipped on the third rung, and he caught himself, felt the wound in his arm tear slightly under the movement. Fresh blood soaked his sleeve.
Somewhere behind him, in the tunnels he'd already left, water dripped in the darkness. The sound was steady, metronomic, like a heart that kept beating in a city full of corpses.
Kael emerged into the bakery, dust swirling in the faint light from the street. The building had been condemned years ago, its ovens cold, its displays empty. But the back room held supplies—a bedroll, preserved food, a change of clothes. A base of operations in hostile territory.
He cleaned his wound as best he could, applied pressure to stop the bleeding, and wrapped it with strips torn from his spare shirt. The contamination would need proper treatment, but he'd bought himself time.
His mind turned to the information Sera had provided. The cult operating in cells. The coordinated plan to weaken the seal network. The Awakened members who had harvested abilities from the dead—Soul Reavers, or something similar, working for purposes Kael didn't yet understand.
The Six Orders maintained the seals. The cult wanted to break them. And somewhere in the middle, Kael moved—hunted, resourceful, carrying a power that let him drink truth from corpses.
A sound reached him from outside. Footsteps on the street, too quiet to be the Order's patrols, too deliberate to be coincidence. Someone had followed him. Someone had tracked him to this location.
Kael extinguished the small lamp he'd been using and moved to the window, pressing himself against the wall to peer through a gap in the boards. The street below was empty, fog-wrapped, silent.
But something felt wrong. The hair on his arms stood up. His instincts screamed warning.
The footsteps stopped directly below the window.
For a long moment, nothing happened. Then a voice drifted up through the boards—muffled, distorted, but recognizable.
"Mr. Ashborn. We know you're in there." A pause. "We'd like to talk. About old times. About shared enemies. About what really happened the night your world ended."
Kael's blood went cold.
The voice belonged to someone he should have killed three weeks ago. Someone who should have been dead.
Someone who had been at his execution.
The footsteps moved away, retreating down the street. A door opened somewhere in the fog, and light spilled out briefly before being extinguished.
"Sleep well, Soul Reaver. Tomorrow, we hunt together."
Kael stood motionless in the darkness, his wound throbbing, his mind racing through impossible possibilities. The Order of Dawn couldn't know about this location. Lyra hadn't been followed. He'd been careful, methodical, thorough.
And yet someone from his past had found him. Someone who knew about his execution. Someone who wanted to hunt with him.
Tomorrow, the cult ritual. Tonight, a new complication that demanded answers he didn't have.
The fog pressed against the windows like something alive, patient, waiting.
Kael didn't sleep.
