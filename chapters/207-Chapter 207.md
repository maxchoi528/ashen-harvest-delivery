The door of the Crossed Antlers tavern didn't open so much as surrender. Splintered wood scattered across cobblestones as Kael Ashborn stumbled through, one hand clutching a bleeding gash across his ribs, the other wrapped around a small leather satchel that held everything he'd stolen from the Sepulcher archives.
Three men followed him into the gaslit street.
"Stop running, Reaver." The tallest one, a scarred veteran with Iron Vanguard insignia tattooed on his neck, leveled a repeating crossbow. "By Order law, you die tonight."
Kael spun, his boots sliding on rain-slicked stone. The wound on his side screamed, but pain was just information. He'd learned to parse it, file it away, keep moving. The six months since his execution had stripped away everything soft from his frame, replacing it with lean muscle and harder edges.
"You Order men always get the wrong idea." Kael's voice came out ragged, tasting copper. "I didn't steal anything that wasn't already buried."
The crossbow bolt took him in the shoulder instead of the heart. Kael staggered backward into a coal cart, sending bags of fuel spilling across the cobbles. The impact rattled his teeth. His vision swam, doubled, then snapped back into terrible clarity.
[SYSTEM NOTIFICATION: Minor wound sustained. Spiritual reserves unaffected. Recommend field treatment within four hours to prevent infection.]
The notification pulsed behind his eyes, unwelcome but useful. The Soul Reaver's connection to his consciousness came with perks and intrusions in equal measure. He couldn't turn off the stream of information that flowed through every moment of his existence.
"Target is stationary," the scarred man announced to someone beyond Kael's line of sight. "Moving to secure."
Two of them approached while the third covered with a palm-sized sigil that glowed with Order-approved light. A binding seal, probably. Kael had seen the Arcane Spire's work before—neat, precise, designed to contain than kill. They wanted to take him alive.
That was a mistake.
Kael let himself fall backward into the coal bags, playing dead. Blood pooled beneath him, his blood, warm and human and absolutely authentic. The three Awakened hesitated at the doorway, reading the scene.
"He took a bolt to the shoulder and one to the ribs," one of them muttered. "Maybe we finished the job already."
"We need proof of harvest." The scarred man moved forward, boots crunching on spilled coal. "Reavers always leave marks. Check for the extraction wounds."
Kael counted their footsteps. One set, approaching from the left. Two sets, hanging back near the door. The third had moved to flank right, trying to get an angle on any last-second escape attempt.
Smart tactics. Too bad they'd never fought someone like him.
When the first man's boot came within range, Kael moved.
He came up from the coal bags with a handful of black dust and slate shards, hurling them into the scarred man's eyes. The veteran flinched, the crossbow swung wild, and Kael was already inside his guard. One elbow to the throat. A knee to the groin. Then Kael's hand closed around the man's wrist and squeezed until something cracked.
The crossbow clattered free. Kael caught it, spun, and fired three bolts in the time it took the other two to react. Two found targets. The third sailed past when someone yanked their companion sideways.
The surviving Awakened had a sigil up now, blazing white light that seared Kael's night-adapted eyes. He threw himself sideways as a lance of ordered energy carved a trench through the cobblestones where he'd stood.
"You can run," the Awakened said, voice tight with pain from the bolt in his shoulder. "But the Council knows about you now. The whole city will know by morning."
Kael didn't answer. He was already running, leaping over a low wall and into the maze of back alleys that comprised Thornwick's underbelly. Behind him, the injured man shouted for reinforcements, his voice echoing off damp brick and leaking gas pipes.
Five minutes later, Kael found a collapsed chimney stack that provided shelter from the rain and the gaslight glare. He pressed himself into the alcove, breathing hard, and finally let himself assess the damage.
The shoulder wound was ugly but clean—entry and exit, no fragments. The ribs were cracked, not broken. The blood loss was the real problem. He needed to stop the bleeding and find somewhere to rest before dawn, when Thornwick's rats emerged to hunt.
[SYSTEM NOTIFICATION: Physical assessment complete. Recommend immediate wound treatment. Second priority: blood replacement. Third priority: secure location for eight-hour rest cycle.]
"Helpful as always," Kael muttered.
He tore a strip from his already ruined coat and fashioned a crude bandage. The fabric was dark enough to hide fresh bloodstains, which mattered more than sterility in the kind of places he frequented now. Infection was a concern, but hunters were a more immediate threat.
As he worked, his mind churned through the night's events. The archives had yielded exactly what he'd suspected—fragmented records of Zarek's movements over the past year. The man had been busy, assembling resources and personnel in preparation for something. The cultist activity in Thornwick wasn't random harassment. It was reconnaissance, positioning assets for a larger operation.
And they'd caught him in the middle of it.
That was the problem with being right. Being right meant people noticed you.
A sound echoed through the alley—footsteps, deliberate and unhurried. Not the heavy tread of a hunting pack, but the measured pace of someone who knew exactly where they were going.
Kael tensed, sliding a knife from his boot. The alley entrance was ten feet away, masked by shadow and rain. If whoever was coming proved hostile, he could be around the corner before they registered his position.
The footsteps stopped.
"Ashborn." A woman's voice, low and controlled. "I know you're injured. I also know you're listening. We need to talk."
Kael recognized the voice but couldn't place it. Frustration gnawed at him as he sorted through recent memories, trying to identify the speaker. Someone from the Archives? The Veiled Market? A contact he'd burned?
"My name is Vane. Order of Dawn, First Circle. I'm not here to capture you." A pause. "Though I probably should be."
Order of Dawn. The paladins of light, guardians of the seals, hunters of corruption. They'd burned Reavers before the practice became officially banned. The fact that this woman hadn't attacked on sight meant she either had questions or had gone rogue.
Neither option made Kael comfortable.
"You have ten seconds to convince me not to disappear," he called back, keeping his body angled away from the alley mouth.
"Zarek's ritual. You know what he's planning. So do I." Another pause, weighted with implication. "We need each other, Ashborn. He's moving soon—days, not weeks. If we don't coordinate, the whole eastern seal network comes down."
The words hit Kael like a physical blow. Days. He'd estimated months of preparation time, enough to gather his own resources, track down the Sepulcher tablets, maybe recruit allies. If Zarek was accelerating the timeline...
"Why should I trust an Order woman?"
"You shouldn't. But you need information I have, and I need someone who can operate outside Concordat restrictions. The Orders can't touch Zarek directly—not without proof that would satisfy the Council. You're not bound by those limitations."
Kael's mind raced through calculations. Trust was a luxury he couldn't afford, but information was currency. And if Zarek was really moving that fast, waiting to build leverage wasn't an option.
Slowly, carefully, he moved to the alley mouth and peered around the corner.
Vane stood twenty feet away, rain streaming down her face and turning her blonde hair to dull gold. She was tall, broad-shouldered, wearing practical traveling clothes that bore no visible insignia. Her hands were empty, raised slightly to indicate non-aggression.
She was also the most dangerous-looking woman he'd ever seen, with a scar that ran from her left temple to her jaw and eyes that tracked his movement with predatory focus.
"Satisfied?" she asked.
"Not remotely. But I'm listening."
Vane lowered her hands, though she didn't put them in her pockets or make any move that might seem casual. "Not here. There are ears everywhere, and not just Order ears. The Shadow Court has people watching you. They've had people watching you since the night you walked out of your own execution."
Kael's blood chilled. The Shadow Court. Deniable operations arm for half the Orders, enemy of anyone who threatened their carefully maintained balance. If they were watching him specifically...
"Why?" he asked.
"That's what I'm trying to find out. Come with me. I have a safehouse two streets over—sanctuary wards, suppression sigils, the works. We can talk there without being overheard."
"Take me there at knifepoint?"
Vane's mouth curved, the first hint of dark humor he'd seen from her. "Would you prefer I knock you unconscious and drag you? I'm strong enough. You're not in shape to resist effectively."
She wasn't wrong. Kael's body was screaming for rest, for food, for the basic maintenance that combat readiness demanded. He'd pushed himself too hard over the past six months, burning reserves faster than he could replenish them.
The Soul Reaver ability was free to use, but it didn't make him invincible. It made him something potentially worse—a target for everyone who feared what he represented.
"Fine," he said. "But if this is a trap, I'm taking you with me."
"Fair enough."
The safehouse turned out to be a basement beneath a shuttered printing shop, accessed through a hidden door behind a false wall of type cases. Inside, the air was dry and cool, filtered by sigils that Kael didn't recognize but could feel pressing against his spiritual senses.
"Sit," Vane ordered, gesturing to a cot in the corner. "Let me see those wounds properly."
"I can bandage my own—"
"Stubborn. The reports mentioned that." She crossed to a cabinet, retrieved supplies, and set them on the small table beside the cot. "The wounds aren't fatal, but they'll slow you down. You need to be functional if we're facing Zarek's people."
Kael wanted to argue, but exhaustion was eroding his resistance. He sat, then let Vane cut away his ruined shirt so she could examine the damage.
Her hands were competent, clinical, treating the wounds with an efficiency that spoke of field medicine experience. Kael watched her work, cataloging details. The way she held instruments, the economy of her movements, the slight tightening around her eyes when she encountered particularly bad damage.
Not cruelty. Not indifference. Just someone who had seen too much suffering to be shocked by it.
"You're Order of Dawn," he said eventually. "Paladins don't usually deal with Reavers."
"Most paladins don't know what I know."
"And what do you know?"
Vane finished stitching his shoulder, tied off the thread, and sat back. "I know that the seal network isn't failing because of age or Entropy. It's being deliberately weakened. I know that cultists aren't operating independently—they're coordinating through someone with access to internal Order communications. And I know that someone tried to have me killed three weeks ago when I got too close to finding out who that someone is."
"Zarek."
"Zarek." She nodded. "He's high-ranking in the Order of Iron, but that's not what concerns me. He answers to someone else. Someone who has been positioning pieces for decades."
The implications settled over Kael like cold water. "A conspiracy within the Orders themselves."
"The highest levels. The Orders teach that the seal network was created by the first Awakened, maintained by cooperation, protected by unity. The truth is uglier. The seals are maintained by constant sacrifice. Blood, soul-essence, spiritual energy from every Awakening. The system works because it was designed to work. But if someone wanted to break it..."
"They'd need to corrupt from inside. Sabotage the maintenance, accelerate the decay, create a crisis that justifies radical action."
"And then what?" Vane's eyes were hard now. "If the seals fail, the 72 Entities come through. Humanity survives, barely, if the Orders can mount a defense. But who controls that defense? Who decides which rifts get sealed and which don't? Who emerges from the chaos with more power than before?"
Kael understood then. This wasn't about Zarek. Zarek was a piece, a tool, a weapon pointed at specific targets. The real game was about what came after—the reshaping of the entire Awakened hierarchy into something new, something controlled by whoever was pulling the strings.
"You're talking about a coup."
"I'm talking about a purge. The Orders have maintained their dominance for centuries by controlling access to spiritual advancement. If the seal network fails, that control evaporates. New powers rise. Old powers fall. But if someone could position themselves as the savior, the one who held everything together when everything was falling apart..."
"They'd become indispensable. More than indispensable—they'd become absolute."
Vane nodded grimly. "The six Orders, unified under a single authority. The Concordat scrapped, rewritten to serve whoever survives the crisis. It's been tried before, during the Abyssal Wars. The founders prevented it by distributing power equally among the Orders. Equal but competitive. Equal but suspicious of each other."
"Until someone found a way to tilt the balance."
"Until someone found a way to make cooperation look like the only option."
Kael's mind raced through implications, connections, possibilities. The cult activity he'd been tracking. The mysterious Shadow Court surveillance. The accelerated timeline. It all fit together into a pattern that made terrible sense.
"The Sepulcher tablets," he said. "The ones I was looking for in the archives. They contain information about the original seal network. About how it was designed to fail if certain conditions were met."
"And you're looking for them because?"
"Because if I know how they can break the seals, I know how to stop them. Maybe."
Vane studied him for a long moment. "You're either very brave or very stupid, Ashborn. Going up against a conspiracy that reaches the highest levels of the Orders, armed with nothing but a Reaver ability and whatever you can steal from corpses."
"I prefer resourceful."
"Desperate might be more accurate." She stood, moving to a desk covered in maps and documents. "I have pieces of the puzzle. You have pieces. Neither of us has the whole picture. But if we work together..."
Kael knew he should refuse. Trust was vulnerability. Vulnerability was death. Every lesson of the past six months screamed at him to walk away, to operate alone, to trust no one.
But Zarek had resources he couldn't match. Information networks he couldn't penetrate. And if Vane was right about the timeline, he didn't have time to build alternatives.
"What do you want from me specifically?"
"Access to places I can't go. The Veiled Market, the cult gathering points, anywhere the Orders can't operate openly. And if it comes to confrontation with Zarek..." She hesitated. "I can't kill him directly. Concordat restrictions. But you can."
"You want me to assassinate a ranking member of the Order of Iron."
"I want you to remove an obstacle. How you do it is your business."
The request should have horrified Kael. Six months ago, it would have. Back when he'd been a death-row prisoner with nothing to lose, before he'd discovered what he could become, before the world had stripped away every comfortable lie he'd believed about justice and consequence.
Now it just felt like another transaction. Another cost calculated against potential benefits.
"One condition," he said. "My methods are my own. You don't get to dictate what I harvest or how. If I need to take something from a corpse to get information, I take it. No interference."
Vane's jaw tightened, but she nodded. "The Orders have spent centuries pretending death is sacred. The truth is messier. If harvesting the dead gives us an advantage, I won't stop you."
"Then we have a deal."
They shook on it, her grip strong and calloused, his still trembling slightly from blood loss. The contact lasted only a moment, but something shifted in the air between them—a fragile alliance forged from mutual need than mutual respect.
"Now," Vane said, releasing his hand, "let's talk about where Zarek is likely to strike next."
The next two hours dissolved into a blur of maps, intelligence reports, and strategic analysis. Vane had compiled extensive notes on cult movements across the eastern territories, tracking patterns that suggested a coordinated operation than scattered incidents. Kael contributed what he'd learned from his own hunting—locations where cultists gathered, names of leaders, the fragmented speech patterns of the partially Awakened zealots he'd encountered.
The picture that emerged was deeply concerning. Zarek's network had been building for years, maybe decades. Assets positioned in every major city. Sleepers embedded in organizations from the Mortician's Guild to the city watch. And at the center, directing everything, a figure whose identity remained frustratingly obscured.
"The Whisper King," Vane said at one point, a name that made Kael's attention sharpen.
"Who?"
"I don't know. That's what concerns me. The name comes up in every intercepted communication, every coded message, every secret meeting. But no one seems to know who the Whisper King is. They just know he speaks for someone higher. Someone who wants the old order burned down."
Kael filed the name away. The Whisper King. Another piece of the puzzle, another layer of conspiracy. He had the uncomfortable feeling that he was only seeing the surface of something much deeper.
"What about the Shadow Court?" he asked. "You said they're watching me. Why?"
"That's the part I can't figure out. The Shadow Court doesn't waste resources on surveillance without reason. They're not ideologically motivated—they sell deniability to anyone who can pay. If they're watching you specifically, someone is paying them to watch."
The implication was clear. Someone with resources and connections had taken an interest in Kael Ashborn. Someone who wanted to know where he went, who he talked to, what he was planning.
Had to be connected to the Soul Reaver ability. Had to be connected to whatever had brought him back from the dead in the first place.
"You have questions," Vane observed. "About yourself. About what you are."
"Everyone has questions."
"Not everyone asks them. You're different, Ashborn. The reports say you were a criminal, a desperate man willing to do anything to survive. But the man I see tonight is calculating, controlled, thinking three moves ahead. Something changed when you came back from the dead. Something made you dangerous in ways you weren't before."
Kael considered how much to reveal. Vane had been forthcoming, but trust was a spectrum. She had given him information that could be used against her. He didn't owe her equivalent vulnerability.
"I'm still figuring that out myself," he said finally.
It wasn't a lie. The Soul Reaver ability had changed him, but he didn't understand the mechanism. He didn't know where it came from, why it had manifested in him, or what its long-term implications might be. All he knew was that it worked, and that it made him valuable to people who valued terrible things.
Vane seemed to accept the non-answer. She gathered her documents, returned them to their hiding place, and turned back to him.
"You should rest. The wounds need time to stabilize, and you'll need your strength for what comes next."
"Define 'what comes next.'"
"Tomorrow night, there's a gathering at the old ironworks. Cultists from three territories, meeting to coordinate their activities. Zarek's people will be there. If we can capture one of them alive, we might be able to extract information about the larger operation."
"And you want me there because?"
"Because cultists recognize Reavers. They have a... Complicated relationship with Soul Reaver mythology. Some worship your kind as avatars of entropy. Others want to harvest you for parts. Either way, you're bait."
Kael laughed, the sound harsh and humorless. "You don't sugarcoat things, do you?"
"Would it help if I did?"
"No."
He lay back on the cot, feeling exhaustion pull at his consciousness. The safehouse was quiet, the sigils humming at the edge of perception, Vane moving around the room with the soft efficiency of someone accustomed to shared spaces and minimal privacy.
"Ashborn," she said after a long silence. "The Order of Dawn officially considers you a threat to be eliminated. If I were following orders, I'd have killed you already."
"Why didn't you?"
"Because orders don't always serve justice. And because I think you're going to be important in what's coming. Whether we like each other or not."
Kael closed his eyes, letting her words settle. Important. The word had different meanings depending on who used it. Important enough to watch. Important enough to recruit. Important enough to eliminate if he became a liability.
"Get some sleep," Vane said. "Tomorrow we hunt."
He drifted into darkness, and for the first time in weeks, the dreams that came were not of the execution scaffold or the cold certainty of death. They were of fire, of seals breaking, of something vast and terrible pressing against the edges of reality.
And standing at the center of it all, a figure whose face he couldn't see, speaking words he couldn't hear.
The Whisper King.
When Kael woke, gray dawn light filtered through cracks in the boarded windows. Vane was gone, but she'd left water, dried meat, and a note written in precise handwriting.
*Out arranging transport. Back by noon. Don't leave the safehouse.*
Kael read the note twice, then burned it in the small oil lamp on the desk. His wounds had closed overnight, the stitches holding firm, the pain reduced to a dull throb that he could ignore if he didn't think about it too hard.
[SYSTEM NOTIFICATION: Physical regeneration progressing. Estimated full recovery: a day and a half. Recommend continued rest.]
He ignored the notification and moved to the window, peering through gaps in the boards. The street outside was quiet, early morning traffic minimal. A coal wagon rattled past, its driver hunched against the chill. Two figures in the uniforms of the city watch walked their beat, speaking in low voices.
And on the rooftop across the street, barely visible against the morning sky, a third figure stood motionless.
Watching.
Kael stepped back from the window, his heart rate spiking. The figure hadn't moved, hadn't reacted to his observation. Either they hadn't seen him, or they didn't care if he knew.
Shadow Court surveillance. Vane's warning from the night before came back to him. They had people watching. They'd had people watching since the night he'd walked out of his own execution.
The question was why.
Kael's mind raced through possibilities. The Soul Reaver ability made him valuable to collectors and researchers. His involvement in the Sepulcher archives made him a threat to whoever was running the conspiracy. His connection to Zarek, But indirect, marked him as a variable that needed tracking.
But surveillance implied intent to use, not just to monitor. The Shadow Court didn't watch people without reason. Someone was paying them to know his every move.
And that someone was probably connected to the Whisper King.
A sound at the hidden door—three soft knocks, then two, then one. Vane's signal. Kael moved to let her in, finding her, her usual controlled composure showing cracks.
"We have a problem," she said, slipping inside and sealing the door behind her. "Zarek moved up his timeline. The gathering at the ironworks isn't tomorrow night—it's tonight. And he won't be there in person. He's sending a proxy with authorization codes that could compromise three seal maintenance sites if they fall into the wrong hands."
"Tonight." Kael processed the implications. "That's less than twelve hours."
"Less than eight,. The meeting starts at midnight, but I got word an hour ago. We have to move now if we're going to intercept."
"Impossible. I'm not healed. You're working alone. And we have no idea what kind of security Zarek will have in place."
"I know." Vane's jaw tightened. "But if that proxy escapes with those authorization codes, we lose three critical sites. Maybe more, if they can use the codes to access others. The entire eastern seal network could be compromised within weeks."
The stakes crystallized in Kael's mind. Not just conspiracy anymore. Not just personal vendetta against Zarek. This was about preventing catastrophic failure of the system that kept the world from drowning in Abyssal corruption.
"I'm in," he said. "But I need food, water, and something better than a knife and a stolen crossbow."
Vane smiled, the first genuine expression he'd seen from her. "I was hoping you'd say that."
She produced a wrapped bundle from her coat—field rations, a waterskin, and a leather roll that unfolded to reveal a collection of nasty-looking weapons. Short blades, garrotes, a pair of throwing axes, and a compact pistol that fired spiritual-imbued rounds.
"I requisitioned these from an Order armory I shouldn't have access to," she said. "Consider it an investment in our partnership."
Kael selected a short sword with good balance and a weighted gauntlet that could serve as a bludgeon. The rest of the weapons he distributed among various pockets, weighting himself down with tools for every contingency.
"The ironworks," he said. "Tell me everything."
The briefing took twenty minutes. Vane had maps, reconnaissance reports, the names of known cultist operatives. The ironworks had been abandoned for decades, its massive furnaces cold, its catwalks rusting, its depths filled with shadows and secrets. Cultists loved symbolic locations, and the ironworks fit—industry fallen to ruin, power transformed into decay, the perfect metaphor for what they wanted to do to the world.
Security would be layered. Outer perimeter to catch intruders. Inner perimeter for trusted operatives. And at the center, whatever proxy Zarek had sent, protected by the best weapons cultist money could buy.
"Entry points?" Kael asked.
"Three main approaches. The main gate, heavily guarded. A service tunnel from the canal, flooded but passable. And the roof, accessible from the adjacent warehouse if you can make the jump."
"Too obvious. I'll take the tunnel. Water won't bother me."
Vane nodded. "I'll draw attention through the main gate. Not directly—I have contacts who can create a distraction. When security focuses on them, you come up through the water and hit whoever's guarding the inner perimeter."
"And if it's a trap?"
"Then I'm buying you time to escape." She checked her pistol, thumbing the safety off and on. "The information I have is too valuable to lose. You need to survive long enough to use it."
The statement carried weight beyond its tactical implications. Vane was volunteering to be the sacrifice, the distraction, the expendable asset. Kael understood the calculation—she was Order of Dawn, bound by rules that limited her options. He was a Reaver with nothing to lose except his life, and he'd already lost that once.
"New plan," he said. "We go in together through the tunnel. You have spiritual senses I don't. I have abilities you don't. We cover each other's weaknesses."
"That's not optimal—"
"Neither is losing you in the first hour. You're useful. I prefer useful allies alive."
Something flickered in Vane's expression, quickly suppressed. "Fine. But if things go wrong, we separate and regroup at the secondary location. Don't try to be a hero."
"Heroism died with my execution. I just don't like waste."
They spent the next hour planning approach vectors, contingency routes, signal patterns. By the time they finished, the gray dawn had brightened to pale morning, and Kael's body had stiffened from rest he hadn't taken.
"Four hours until we move," Vane said. "Try to sleep more. You'll need the energy."
"Can't sleep. Too many variables."
"Then at least eat something. And let me check your stitches."
Kael submitted to her ministrations with bad grace, allowing her to examine the wounds and reapply bandages. Her touch was professional, impersonal, efficient. He found himself watching her face instead of her hands, studying the scar that ran from temple to jaw, wondering what story it told.
"Question," he said as she worked.
"Ask."
"The scar. Who gave it to you?"
Vane's hands paused for a fraction of a second before resuming their work. "A cultist. Three years ago. He'd been possessed by something from the Abyss—corrupted, barely human. I was leading a strike team against a ritual site. He got through my guard."
"Did you kill him?"
"I did. But not before he marked me." She tied off the bandage with unnecessary force. "The Orders teach that scars are lessons, reminders of failures we must overcome. I prefer to think of mine as a promise. Every wound I take, I repay tenfold. The cultist who gave me this—she didn't escape. Her entire cell did, except her. I made sure of that."
Kael recognized the emotion beneath her words. Not cold-blooded vengeance, but controlled fury, directed with precision. He'd felt the same thing in the aftermath of his own execution—the rage that came from being violated, used, discarded.
Different circumstances. Same fundamental wound.
"We're more alike than you'd like to admit," he said.
Vane met his eyes, her gaze hard. "Don't mistake pragmatism for kinship, Ashborn. We work together because we have to. That doesn't make us friends."
"I wasn't offering friendship. Just observation."
She held his gaze for a long moment, then stood, gathering her supplies. "Four hours. Rest if you can. I have final preparations to make."
She left through the hidden door, leaving Kael alone with his thoughts and the weapons she'd provided.
He sat on the cot, turning the short sword over in his hands, feeling the weight of it, the balance. The blade was well-made, Order-forged, probably worth more than everything else he owned combined. Someone had spent considerable resources arming him for this mission.
That level of investment suggested faith in the outcome. Or desperation. Kael wasn't sure which was worse.
Three hours passed in preparation. He cleaned and oiled the weapons. He stretched, testing his body's limits, finding them acceptable if not ideal. He reviewed the maps Vane had provided, memorizing approach routes and exit strategies.
And then, with an hour remaining, he did something he hadn't done in months.
He meditated.
The Soul Reaver ability manifested differently than standard Awakened powers. Where other practitioners drew on accumulated reserves, channeling spiritual energy through trained pathways, Kael harvested from the dead. The energy came from outside himself, absorbed through physical contact with corpses, processed by a system he didn't fully understand.
The meditation wasn't about accessing his own reserves. It was about understanding the foreign energy that now flowed through him—the remnants of other souls, transformed and integrated, powering abilities that shouldn't exist.
He closed his eyes and descended into himself.
The darkness behind his eyelids wasn't empty. It swirled with fragments, echoes of the dead he'd harvested. Battle instincts from soldiers. Tracking senses from hunters. Fragments of memory from Awakened and unawakened alike, all compressed into the engine of his consciousness.
And beneath it all, something older. Something that had been there since his resurrection, waiting.
Kael probed the edges of his awareness, trying to understand what he was feeling. The older presence had no distinct form, no identifiable traits. It was just... There. A foundation beneath the foundation, a bedrock supporting everything else.
The original Soul Reaver.
The thought arrived unbidden, carrying implications he wasn't ready to examine. If the ability had existed before him, if someone else had first carried this power, then where were they now? What had happened to the first Reaver?
Why did he exist at all?
The meditation dissolved into frustration. Questions without answers. Mysteries without solutions. The conspiracy surrounding him was vast, interconnected, reaching into places he couldn't see. And at the center of it all, the Whisper King moved pieces that Kael couldn't identify.
"Come on," he muttered to himself. "Focus."
He couldn't change what he didn't understand. He could only act on what he knew. And what he knew was that tonight, something was happening at the ironworks. Something that could accelerate a catastrophe already in motion.
He had to stop it. Not for justice. Not for the world. Just because it was there, and he could, and the alternative was letting people like Zarek win.
The clock in the corner of the room chimed the hour. Eleven at night. Time to move.
