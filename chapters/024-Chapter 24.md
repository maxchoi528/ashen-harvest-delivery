The thing wearing Sergeant Merrick's face lunged from the shadows.
Kael's blade met serrated steel mid-swing, the impact shuddering through his arm and grinding the bones of his wrist together. The creature's jaw hung wrong, unhinged too far, revealing gums where teeth should be. Black fluid dripped from the corners of its mouth, hissing where it struck the cobblestones. It spoke in a voice like grinding gravel, the sound rattling through a throat no longer designed for speech.
"You shouldn't have come back, Reaver."
He kicked it backward, boot connecting with a ribcage that cracked like wet kindling, buying two steps of space. Blood pounded in his ears. The stench hit him—rotting meat overlaid with chemical preservatives, the Mortician's signature. Three more shapes materialized from the fog rolling through the alley, their footsteps uneven, arrhythmic. Former prisoners, their tags still hanging from their necks, the metal clinking against exposed collarbones.
"The Mortician's Guild sends its regards," another rasped.
Kael counted six. The alley behind him dead-ended at a collapsed wall, rubble fused together by years of industrial grime and neglect. Ahead, the creatures formed a loose semicircle, blocking the only route to the Veiled Market. Brick walls rose on either side, slick with moisture, offering no handholds. His hand drifted to Soul Reaver's hilt. The leather wrapping pressed against his palm, warm despite the chill.
A notification flickered at the edge of his vision, cold and precise as always.
[DANGER ASSESSMENT: 6 hostile entities detected. Threat level: Moderate. Recommended action: Engage or withdraw.]
Withdraw meant backtracking through the tunnel network, risking patrol encounters in tight confines where his blade held no advantage. Engage meant fighting through a group of reanimated corpses with stolen Awakened energy, each one burning through essence that made it faster and stronger than any common thug. Neither option satisfied him. His jaw tightened.
"What's the matter?" the lead creature tilted its head at an angle that made its neck crack, the sound echoing off the brickwork like a snapped branch. "Aren't you supposed to be powerful?"
"Power isn't the same as survival," Kael said.
He moved before the words finished leaving his mouth.
The first creature died properly this time—blade through the eye socket, twisting to destroy whatever remained of the nervous system. Bone splintered under the steel. Kael felt Soul Reaver pulse against his palm, hungry, the vibration crawling up his forearm. He touched the cooling forehead of the corpse before it finished falling, fingers pressing against flesh already losing its borrowed warmth.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 12 units of spiritual energy from the remains. Memory Fragment acquired: Guard Patrol Route Alpha-7.]
The energy flooded into him like ice water, sharp and clean. His reserves climbed, the sensation spreading through his chest. The memory fragment settled into his mind—a patrol schedule, faces of guards, the layout of a corridor he'd never walked. The other five reanimated bodies hesitated, their borrowed instincts recognizing the threat. Whatever animating force drove them processed the death and recalculated.
"Clever trick," the lead creature hissed, its tongue flicking across lipless gums. "But you're still just one man."
"Was."
Kael charged.
The fight lasted ninety seconds. Each corpse fell to a precise strike—joint, throat, temple—and each corpse fed him another fragment of stolen power. The second creature raised an arm to block, and Kael's blade severed it at the elbow. Black fluid sprayed. He drove his knee into its chest, knocking it backward, and thrust through the exposed throat. His fingers found the cooling skull. Harvested. The third came low, tackling at his waist. He rolled with the impact, slammed his elbow down onto the base of its skull, and felt vertebrae separate. Harvested. The fourth and fifth attacked together, coordinated in a way that spoke of shared animating intelligence. Kael pivoted between them, let the fourth's clawed hand slice air where his throat had been, and drove Soul Reaver upward through its chin. The fifth grabbed his coat from behind. He dropped his weight, threw the corpse over his shoulder, and crushed its temple with his boot heel. Harvested. Harvested.
By the time the last body collapsed, Kael stood in a ring of cooling meat, breathing hard but whole. His muscles burned. Sweat cut tracks through the grime on his face.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 8 units of spiritual energy from the remains. Memory Fragment acquired: Mortician's Guild Distribution Schedule.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 15 units of spiritual energy from the remains. Skill Acquired: Basic Shield Reinforcement (Awakened Tier).]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 6 units of spiritual energy from the remains. Memory Fragment acquired: Delivery Route to Thornwick Docks.]
The notifications scrolled past, clinical and useful. Six bodies meant six harvests, and the quality surprised him. These weren't common thieves or beggars—the Mortician's Guild had invested real Awakened essence into animating them. The spiritual residue coating his palate tasted of ozone and copper.
That meant someone wanted him dead.
Or wanted to test him.
Kael crouched beside the lead creature, examining the ritual scarring on its wrists. Standard binding marks, designed to anchor a stolen soul to stolen flesh. Crude work, efficient, the kind of thing you'd learn in any underground market if you had the coin and the stomach for it. The scars were old, healed white before death, then reopened during the reanimation process. Fresh ink overlaid faded lines—corrections to the binding formula, adjustments made post-mortem. Someone had tweaked these puppets after their first activation.
The creature's original identity was gone, consumed by whatever reanimation process the Morticians used. All that remained was a puppet, burning through stolen essence with every movement. They'd paid for the privilege. Whoever they'd been, whatever lives they'd led, reduced to fuel for someone else's errand.
Footsteps echoed from the street beyond the alley. Multiple sets, moving with the coordinated rhythm of trained guards. Lantern light swung across the alley mouth.
Kael sheathed Soul Reaver and moved, scaling the collapsed wall in three pulls, the brick crumbling under his fingers. His boots found the narrow ledge on the far side, and he dropped into the drainage tunnel beyond. His knees absorbed the impact. Darkness swallowed him.
He emerged onto Charcoal Street through a drainage grate, landing in ankle-deep water that stank of industrial runoff. The fog had thickened while he fought, turning the gaslights into diffuse orange halos that floated disconnected in the murk. Steam vented from pipes overhead, hissing against the damp air. The moisture settled on his skin, mixing with the dried blood and sweat. He tasted sulfur on his tongue.
Thornwick's Undercity operated on different rules. Down here, the Concordat's authority dissolved into a patchwork of territory agreements between the Veiled Market's governing council, the Mortician's Guild, and whatever criminal syndicate held power in a given district. Boundaries shifted weekly. A wrong turn could take you from neutral ground into hostile territory without warning. Kael had spent three months learning those rules the hard way—through scars, close calls, and the kind of hunger that gnawed bone. His ribs still showed the bruise from a week ago, purple-yellow where an enforcer's boot had caught him during a retreat through the tannery district.
He needed supplies. Fresh rations, information about the Order movements in the Upper City, and a way into the Archive Vaults beneath the Temple of Dawn. The Sepulcher tablets Sera had mentioned—recorded by the first Soul Reaver—might contain answers about his abilities. Answers the Orders had spent centuries suppressing. The weight of that need pressed against the base of his skull.
But first, he needed to eat. His stomach cramped, a sharp twist that made his vision blur at the edges.
The Rustbucket Saloon squatted at the intersection of three tunnels, its facade constructed from salvaged ship hulls and welded iron. Rivets protruded from the walls like boils. A sign creaked overhead, depicting a corroded stein overflowing with foam, the paint peeling in strips that curled toward the ground. The rusted chain holding it swayed in the tunnel draft, producing a thin metallic whine. Inside, the air tasted of spilled ale and pipe smoke, thick enough to chew. Heat from the pressed-tin radiators clung to the low ceiling, trapping every smell in a dense layer.
Kael pushed through the door. Conversation stuttered, resumed. The patrons—smugglers, information brokers, a few Awakened too desperate or criminal to surface—returned to their drinks with studied disinterest. Everyone in the Rustbucket understood the first rule of survival: mind your own business, and your business might survive.
Marlowe spotted him from behind the bar. The elderly woman had run this establishment for forty years, long enough to remember when the Mortician's Guild buried their dead instead of harvesting them. Her face creased into something between a smile and a grimace, deep lines folding around her mouth.
"You're alive," she said, sliding a tankard across the counter without being asked. The wood was scarred, gouged by knife marks and stained with decades of ring marks. "That'll cost you two shillings."
"I have information worth ten times that."
"Information's cheap. Survival's expensive. Sit."
Kael sat. The ale tasted like rust and regret, but his throat hadn't realized how dry it was until the liquid hit it. He drained half the tankard before coming up for air, wiping his mouth with the back of his hand. The bitter aftertaste coated his tongue.
"The Morticians are making moves," he said. "Six of their puppets came at me in Alchemist's Alley. Good quality animates, real Awakened essence underneath."
Marlowe's expression didn't change, but her hands paused mid-motion while wiping down the counter. The rag twisted between her knuckles. "That's not neighborhood business. That's Guild politics."
"I'm neck-deep in Guild politics whether I want to be or not."
"You're neck-deep in something. Not sure it's politics." She leaned closer, lowering her voice beneath the ambient noise. The smell of pipe tobacco and old gin clung to her collar. "Word came down three days ago. The Mortician's Guild is looking for a specific type of asset. Someone who can harvest without depleting. Someone who doesn't need ritual circles or blood sacrifice."
Kael's fingers tightened around the tankard. The pewter dented under his grip. "They're looking for Soul Reavers."
"They're offering bounty. Fifty sovereigns for information leading to capture. A thousand for a live specimen." Marlowe's eyes met his, ancient and knowing. "The kind of money that makes old friendships very temporary."
"I'm not a specimen."
"No. You're a problem. Problems get solved in this city." She slid a folded paper across the bar. "This came for you. Left by a woman with silver eyes. Said you'd know what it meant."
Kael unfolded the paper. A single symbol marked the center: a broken circle intersected by three lines. Lyra's mark. The message beneath contained only coordinates and a time.
Midnight. Dockside Warehouse 17.
"That's either a trap or an opportunity," Kael said.
"Usually both." Marlowe collected his coins. "You want to survive in this city, boy? Don't go to that meeting."
"Too late for that advice."
He finished the ale, left payment plus extra, and headed for the door. The fog outside had transformed into a fine mist that clung to his coat, weighing the fabric down. Somewhere in the distance, a steamwhistle screamed—the signal for the night shift change at the ironworks.
His stomach reminded him that ale wasn't food.
The food stalls near the market junction served things that might have once been meat, transformed by spice and desperation into something edible. Kael bought two skewers of something labeled "lamb" and a wrapped bundle of fried roots that tasted like salted cardboard. He ate standing up, watching the crowd flow past. Grease dripped from the skewer onto his fingers.
People-watching taught you things. The nervous shuffle of pickpockets casing targets, their eyes never resting on one face. The deliberate gait of guards working their beats, hands resting on truncheons. The subtle tells of Awakened trying to appear normal—hands in pockets to hide trembling from energy expenditure, eyes tracking multiple threats simultaneously, the faint shimmer of activated sigils beneath their collars.
A girl darted past clutching a bundle of stolen fabric. Security enforcers gave chase, boots pounding against cobblestones. Someone's grandmother cursed at the disruption, shaking a fist. Normal Undercity life, continuing despite everything.
Kael finished eating and wiped his hands on his coat. The coordinates Lyra had sent burned in his pocket. Midnight gave him enough time to prepare, not enough to overthink.
He needed to understand what he was facing.
The Cog & Crown library occupied a basement three levels below street level, accessible through a hidden entrance behind a working steam engine. The entrance was a narrow gap between the engine's housing and the tunnel wall, invisible unless you knew where to look. Kael squeezed through, his coat catching on a protruding bolt. The library's founder—a retired Arcane Spire scholar named Thaddeus Coal—had built his collection from banned texts, stolen Order archives, and gifts from patrons who valued knowledge over propriety.
Kael descended the spiral staircase, each step groaning beneath his weight. The air grew cooler as he dropped below ground, carrying the mineral tang of deep water and old paper. Dust motes drifted through the light. Gas lamps flickered at intervals, casting uneven light across shelves that stretched toward distant walls. The silence pressed against his ears, broken only by the distant hum of the steam engine above.
Coal sat at his usual desk, surrounded by stacks of books that defied gravity. The old man's eyes—milky with cataracts but sharper than they appeared—tracked Kael's approach without lifting his head from the document he was examining. A magnifying glass rested in his right hand. His left hand pinned the parchment flat.
"Ashborn." Coal's voice rasped like turning pages. "Your soul signature precedes you. Nasty business, what you're carrying."
"I need information."
"Information costs. What are you offering?"
"About the Cobblestone Crawler."
Coal's hands stilled. The magnifying glass lowered. He looked up, genuine interest replacing habitual suspicion. "Where did you hear that name?"
"It's been surfacing in my harvests. Memory fragments mentioning it in connection with the Mortician's Guild. Something about containment failures and cleanup protocols."
"Containment." Coal snorted. "That's one word for it. The Crawler is a Class-3 Abyssal manifestation. What the Orders call a 'breach entity'—something that slips through weakened seals without being large enough to trigger full alarm protocols."
Kael pulled out a chair, sat. The wood creaked. "Why would the Morticians contain it?"
"Why does anyone contain anything? Leverage. The Crawler feeds on ambient spiritual energy, converting it into physical mass. Drop it in a room full of Awakened, and it becomes a meat grinder in seconds. Very useful for disposing of problems." Coal shuffled through his papers, extracting a yellowed sheet covered in diagrams that Kael recognized as ward configurations. "It escaped containment two weeks ago. Killed a full squad of Mortician enforcers before they recaptured it. The Guild has been scrambling to clean up loose ends ever since."
"And loose ends include me."
"Especially you. A Soul Reaver capable of harvesting the Crawler's essence would be..." Coal paused, choosing his words. "Catastrophic. To their operations. To the balance of power in this city."
The implications settled into Kael's understanding. If the Morticians had been using Abyssal entities as weapons, controlling them through containment and force, then his ability to harvest their essence represented a fundamental threat to that power structure. He could drain their weapons dry. He could turn their tools against them.
He could become the most dangerous person in Thornwick.
"Where is it now?" Kael asked.
Coal's expression flickered with something like concern. The lines around his mouth deepened. "You're planning to hunt it."
"I'm planning to survive. Two different things."
"The Crawler is currently held in a facility beneath the old foundry on Miller's Row. Four stories underground, heavily guarded, warded against spiritual detection. You'd need a small army to breach it."
"I work alone."
"Even alone, you need intelligence. Routes. Guard rotations. Weak points in their wards." Coal slid a second document across the desk. "This will cost you."
"Name your price."
"Three favors. Unspecified. To be called upon at my discretion within the next year."
Kael considered the terms. Favors from a man who knew every secret in Thornwick's Undercity represented a resource more valuable than gold. But favors also represented obligation, and obligation created vulnerability. His thumb traced the edge of the desk.
"Two favors," he countered. "And I reserve the right to refuse anything that endangers my survival."
Coal's papery lips cracked into something resembling a smile. "Acceptable. We have an accord."
They shook hands. The old man's grip felt like gripping a bundle of kindling.
Before midnight, Kael stood outside Warehouse 17, counting guards.
The building squatted at the edge of the dock district, its iron walls streaked with rust and salt corrosion. Ships groaned at anchor in the harbor beyond, their cargo cranes swinging in the wind, chains rattling. The smell of the sea—brine, fish, diesel fuel—filled his nostrils. Waves slapped against the pilings. The wind cut through his coat.
Three guards patrolled the perimeter. Two more stood at the main entrance. Their uniforms bore no insignia, but the quality of their equipment spoke of Guild funding. Mortician's enforcers, or mercenaries working under contract.
Kael circled the building's perimeter, counting windows, measuring distances. A loading dock on the north side offered potential entry—single guard, minimal lighting, crates providing cover. He waited for the patrol pattern to complete, pressing himself flat against the corrugated iron wall. His breath clouded. The guard's footsteps grew, peaked, faded.
Kael moved.
The guard never saw him coming. Kael struck once, precisely, the heel of his palm driving upward into the base of the man's nose. Bone crunched. He caught the man as he slumped, the dead weight pulling at his shoulders. He dragged the unconscious body into the shadows, bound his wrists with the man's own belt, and continued inside. The taste of adrenaline sat bitter on his tongue.
The warehouse interior stretched cavernous and dark, lit only by a cluster of lanterns near the center. Crates lined the walls, stamped with shipping manifests Kael couldn't read in the dim light. His boots made no sound on the concrete floor. The air hung cold and still, carrying the faint chemical smell of preservative fluids.
Lyra waited there, standing beside a table covered with maps and documents. Her silver eyes caught the lantern light, reflecting it back like chips of polished steel. She looked the same as when he'd last seen her—lean, dangerous, radiating the coiled tension of a predator deciding whether to strike or flee. A scar ran along her jaw that he didn't remember.
"You came," she said.
"You knew I would."
"I knew you were too stubborn to avoid a direct challenge." She gestured at the maps. "The Morticians are moving. Whatever you're planning, they're planning faster."
Kael examined the documents spread across the table. Shipping manifests. Guard schedules. A schematic of the facility beneath Miller's Row, including the chamber where they held the Cobblestone Crawler. Someone had annotated the schematic with entry points, ward configurations, shift changes.
"This is Guild-level intelligence," he said.
"Yes."
"Where did you get it?"
Lyra's expression tightened. "From someone inside. Someone who's been feeding me information for months."
"Someone trustworthy?"
"Someone with reasons to want the Guild's power broken." She pulled a small vial from her coat, holding it up to the light. The liquid inside glowed faintly green. "This is concentrated Crawler essence. Highly unstable. The Guild uses it as a component in their reanimation rituals."
"You stole it."
"I liberated it. There's a difference." She set the vial down. "The Crawler isn't just a weapon. It's a lynchpin. If you harvest it—if you can drain its essence—the Guild loses their entire leverage against every other faction in this city. The Orders. The criminal syndicates. The other Guilds. They'd all owe you."
"Or they'd all hunt me."
"Both can be true." Lyra met his gaze. "You asked me once why I kept helping you when it would be easier to walk away. This is why. You're the only person in this city who can do what needs to be done. Not because you're special. Because you're willing to be monstrous."
The word hung between them, heavy with implication.
"I'm not a monster," Kael said.
"No. You're something worse. You're necessary."
A sound echoed from the warehouse entrance—the distinctive click of multiple weapons being cocked. Kael spun, hand dropping to Soul Reaver's hilt. His knuckles whitened around the leather.
Six figures emerged from the shadows, arranged in a semicircle. Their boots scraped against concrete. Their leader stepped forward, revealing a face Kael recognized from Wanted posters and whispered warnings.
Zarek. Prime Cultist. The man who'd framed him for murder, who'd ordered Gwen's squad to die in an ambush, whose name had haunted Kael's every step since his awakening. His features were carved in sharp angles, hollow cheeks casting shadows in the lantern light.
"Hello, Ashborn." Zarek's voice dripped with false warmth. "The Guild sends their regards. So does the Shadow Court. So do the families of everyone you've ever killed." He smiled, thin and cruel. "They all want the same thing. Your head. Your abilities. Your soul."
Kael counted weapons. Six loaded, one reloading. Distance measured in heartbeats.
"The Whisper King sends his regards as well," Zarek continued. "He wants you to know that your path was always intended. That every step you've taken led here. That the Soul Reaver was never an accident."
Those words struck harder than any blade. His chest tightened.
"What do you want?" Kael demanded.
"What everyone wants. Control." Zarek raised his hand, and his companions raised their weapons. "Surrender now, and you live long enough to be useful. Resist, and we take what we need regardless."
Kael's mind raced through options. Six armed opponents, narrow exit routes, Lyra standing beside him with no visible weapon. Against him: Soul Reaver's zero-cost harvesting, whatever abilities he'd accumulated from today's kills, and the desperate cunning of a cornered predator.
He smiled.
"Come and get it."
The first gunshot cracked through the warehouse. Kael moved.
