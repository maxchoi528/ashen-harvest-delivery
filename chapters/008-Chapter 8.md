The corpse at Kael's feet still twitched. A man in merchant's clothes, throat opened by a blade meant for Kael's back. Three more bodies lay in the narrow alley, victims of a negotiation gone wrong over stolen soul-essence vials.
"Should've taken the deal, reaver." The last standing attacker spat blood onto cobblestones. His companions had fled into Thornwick's fog. "The Veiled Market doesn't negotiate."
Kael crouched, pressing his palm flat against the merchant's cooling chest. The Soul Reaver activated without resistance—no spiritual drain, just the familiar cold flow of essence moving through his hand.
[unknown] Soul Harvest: You extracted 12 units of residual spiritual energy from the remains. Memory Fragment acquired.
Fragments of transactions, warehouse locations, names floated through Kael's mind. The merchant had been a middleman. More interesting: a shipment of soul-essence moving through Blackgate Prison within the week. The facility that had executed Kael three weeks ago.
He released the memory, letting it settle into his existing knowledge. The attacker's eyes widened at the visible glow fading from Kael's fingers.
"You're insane," the man whispered. "Harvesting in the open—"
"Watch me." Kael stood, rolling his shoulders. His Awakened senses tracked the fog movement, the distant steam-whistles of the night shift, the heartbeat of a city that wanted him dead. "You can run, or you can answer questions. Your choice."
The man ran.
Kael let him go. Dead witnesses attracted more attention than live ones with damaged pride. He moved deeper into the alley, past steam vents hissing against the cold, through the labyrinth of Thornwick's lower districts where the gaslight barely reached.
Blackgate Prison. The name echoed in his skull. He'd been sentenced there for murder he didn't commit—or murder he'd committed under orders he couldn't remember. The distinction had seemed important once. Now it only mattered that Zarek's frame job had put him in chains, and chains were where his former cellmate had died screaming from wounds that shouldn't have been fatal.
Someone had wanted that cellmate silenced. Someone who knew things about the orders of awakening, about the Six Orders' war against the Abyss, about the 72 sealed rifts that kept reality from unraveling.
Kael needed answers. Blackgate held them.
A shape detached from the shadows ahead. Kael's hand found his knife—
"Easy, dead man." The voice was young, female, edged with the rasp of someone who'd breathed too much coal dust. A child emerged into a patch of gaslight, maybe twelve years old, dressed in layers of scavenged cloth that added bulk without adding warmth. Her eyes caught the light like a rat's. "You're the one they executed last month. Saw the hanging myself. Stepped over your grave dirt to get a better look."
"Congratulations on your observational skills." Kael kept his hand on the knife. "Who are you?"
"Name's Pip. I run messages for the Veiled Market." She tilted her head, studying him with unsettling intensity. "You just harvested old Merchant Vell without paying the toll. That means you owe the Market something now. Bad policy, harvesting in their territory without permission."
"I wasn't aware I needed permission to defend myself."
"Everyone needs permission for something." Pip's thin lips curved. "Lucky for you, I'm not here about that. I'm here because you took something from those dead men that I want."
Kael's eyes narrowed. "You saw what I harvested?"
"I saw enough. Memory fragments about a shipment to Blackgate. Soul-essence going into the prison, not out. That's interesting, isn't it? Prisoners don't usually get fed soul-essence. Unless they're not prisoners."
She knew more than a street child should. Kael filed that away, part of the growing map of Thornwick's hidden layers. "What do you want?"
"Partner." Pip stepped closer, close enough that Kael could smell the rot-gut alcohol on her breath, the copper tang of old blood. "I've been watching you since you crawled out of your own grave. Nobody walks away from Blackgate's gallows. Nobody should be able to. You did. That makes you useful."
"I'm not looking for a partner."
"Good thing I wasn't asking." She grinned, showing missing teeth. "Blackgate's got something I need. A ledger. Names, transactions, proof of who really runs the soul trade in Thornwick. I get that ledger, I burn the people who destroyed my family. You get whatever's in it for you—revenge, answers, I don't care. But I can't get in alone."
"You've tried."
"Three times." No shame in the admission. "Got caught the third time. Lost two fingers." She held up her left hand, the pinky and ring finger reduced to scarred stumps. "Blackgate's Warden Maren runs the tightest prison in the northern provinces. Ex-Iron Vanguard. She doesn't take prisoners—she takes assets. People who know things, people with gifts. They go in, they don't come out. But shipments keep going in, and shipments keep going missing."
The fog curled around them, thick with the stench of the river. Somewhere above, a steam engine groaned through its cycle.
"Why tell me this?" Kael asked.
"Because you're already dead to them." Pip spread her arms wide, encompassing the fog, the shadows, the city that wanted him erased. "What's the worst they can do? Execute you twice?"
A fair point.

Blackgate Prison rose from Thornwick's eastern harbor like a monument to cruelty. Black stone imported from the northern quarries, fitted together without mortar, sweating moisture in the cold. No windows on the lower floors. The upper floors had windows, but they were barred with iron that gleamed faintly in the gaslight—spiritual reinforcement, meant to keep Awakened prisoners from walking through walls.
Kael crouched on a rooftop two blocks away, Pip beside him. The child had procured a stolen spyglass from somewhere, pressing it to her eye as she surveyed the approach.
"Delivery entrance," she said, pointing. "South side. They bring in supplies twice a week—food, materials, soul-essence shipments. Guards check everything, but they don't check the underside of the supply wagons. Too busy checking the cargo."
"You've used this route before."
"Second attempt. First time, the wagon I hid under had a broken axle. Third time, the guards found me before I reached the inner gate." She lowered the spyglass, meeting his eyes. "This time, we do it right. You go in, get the ledger from Warden's office. I stay out, watch for reinforcements. Simple."
Simple. Nothing in Thornwick was simple.
"What's the warden's security?" Kael asked. The question tasted bitter—he should be gathering intelligence through harvest, not asking a child.
"Four Iron Vanguard veterans retired to civilian contract. Plus whatever Awakened she has on staff. Word is she's got at least two serving as interrogators." Pip made a face. "Your old friends from the trial probably. Judge Harlan and that prosecution lawyer. They're not guards—they're special guests. Which means they have access to places regular guards don't."
Judge Harlan. The man who'd pronounced sentence with barely concealed satisfaction. Kael's hands tightened on the rooftop tiles.
"You're angry." Pip watched him with those too-old eyes. "Good. Anger's useful. But anger makes mistakes. So does being dead already. You want to get in, get the ledger, get out without dying again? Listen to me."
She outlined the route. Storage basement, service corridors, administrative wing, warden's office on the third floor. Guards at fixed posts, rotating patrols, shift changes at midnight and four bells. The ledger would be in a locked cabinet behind the warden's desk, warded with spiritual locks that would require either the warden's blood or significant force to bypass.
"And if I trigger an alarm?"
"Then we improvise." Pip's grin held no warmth. "I wasn't planning on leaving you in there if things went wrong."
"How reassuring."
"You wanted reassuring? Find a priest." She handed him a small leather pouch. "Lockpicks. Got them off a guard I befriended before he transferred to the northern wall. Should fit the administrative doors."
Kael took the pouch, weighing it in his palm. "If this is a trap—"
"Then you harvest me and add child-killer to your list of crimes." Pip shrugged. "But it's not. I want those names. You're the only one who can get them. We're both betting on each other, reaver. That's how partnerships work."
She vanished back into the shadows, leaving Kael alone with the prison's looming presence.

The wagon rattled through Blackgate's delivery entrance at half-past ten bells. Kael hung beneath the axle, fingers hooked through the frame's rusted struts, breath held as guards with lanterns swept the underside with light. The wait lasted an eternity. Then the wagon lurched forward, and Kael's arms screamed as he held his weight against the cobblestones, dragging himself through the gap between wheel and ground.
He waited until the guards moved away before dropping into the drainage channel that ran alongside the service road. The smell hit him immediately—rotting food, human waste, the copper undertone of old blood. Blackgate's garbage disposal. He followed it toward the basement access.
The grate lifted with minimal resistance. Kael slipped through into darkness, landing in a foot of stagnant water that soaked through his boots immediately. His Awakened senses expanded outward, mapping the space. Stone walls, vaulted ceiling, shelving units loaded with supplies. A single guard at the far end, asleep at his post with a bottle of something clutched to his chest.
Kael moved past him without hesitation.
The service corridor beyond led upward through a maintenance shaft, the rungs slick with condensation. He climbed blind, relying on touch and the faint spiritual pressure of the prison's wards to navigate. The wards pulsed like a heartbeat—old magic, carefully maintained, designed to contain than kill. Designed to keep things in.
Twelve minutes of careful progress brought him to the administrative level. The corridor here was different—polished floors, gaslight sconces, the distant murmur of voices from behind closed doors. Kael pressed himself into an alcove as footsteps approached. Two guards in Iron Vanguard colors passed without glancing his way.
The warden's office was at the corridor's end. Heavy oak door, spiritual lock crackling faintly in Kael's perception. He tested the lockpicks Pip had provided, working through the mechanism with fingers that remembered better days. The lock clicked. The door swung inward.
The office beyond was sparse. A desk, two chairs, filing cabinets along one wall. A window overlooking the harbor, moonlight cutting silver lines across the floor. Kael moved immediately to the cabinet, searching for the ledger Pip had described.
His hand closed around leather binding. He pulled—
Nothing. The cabinet was empty.
"Looking for something?"
Kael spun. Warden Maren stood in the doorway, arms crossed, her Iron Vanguard scar visible even in the dim light. Behind her, two figures Kael recognized with cold certainty: Judge Harlan and Prosecutor Vance. The men who'd sentenced him. The men who'd known he was innocent and convicted him anyway.
"We wondered when you'd come back," Maren said. "The dead don't usually haunt their executioners. You're either very brave or very stupid."
"Bit of both," Kael replied, his hand drifting toward his knife. "Where's the ledger?"
"Destroyed three days ago. Along with any record of what happens in the lower cells." Maren's smile didn't reach her eyes. "You think this is about the soul trade? Small potatoes, reaver. Blackgate holds things far more valuable than essence."
Judge Harlan stepped forward, his Awakened presence flaring with barely contained power. Ascendant stage, Kael's senses told him. A rank above where Kael stood. "We know what you did. The executions. The harvests. You're a Soul Reaver—everything the Orders said you were."
"And more," Prosecutor Vance added. "A murderer, a thief, a creature that defiles the natural order. We should have killed you properly the first time."
The three of them moved to block the door. Kael's mind raced through options. The window was barred, the warden's presence was too strong to bypass, and backup would arrive within minutes if he triggered an alarm.
He chose the third option.
Kael threw the empty ledger binder at Judge Harlan's face, using the distraction to sprint toward the filing cabinets. His shoulder caught the wood, shattering it inward, and he dove through the gap into darkness—
The lower cells. He'd found them.
The corridor stretched ahead, lined with iron doors, each one emanating the same cold pressure that Blackgate's wards produced. Cells for Awakened prisoners. Cells designed to contain things that shouldn't exist.
Kael ran.
Behind him, Maren's voice echoed: "Seal the wing! He doesn't leave this building!"
Alarms shrieked to life. Gaslight flickered as the prison's spiritual defenses activated, throwing shadows everywhere. Kael found stairs descending deeper—past the cells, into foundations that shouldn't exist according to any building plan he'd seen.
The Rat King Swarm waited in the basement.
They emerged from the darkness like a tide of writhing bodies, dozens of them, each one a grotesque fusion of rat and something else. Bigger than dogs, their skulls stretched too long, teeth like needles, eyes that glowed with stolen spiritual energy. The Abyssal contamination was obvious—the creatures weren't natural, they'd been created or corrupted, feeding on the residual essence that leaked through Blackgate's containment wards.
One of them lunged. Kael's knife opened its throat, black blood spraying across his face. Another bit at his leg, teeth scraping bone before he kicked it away. A third climbed the wall, preparing to drop onto his back—
He let the Soul Reaver take over.
The harvest was visceral. Kael's hand closed around the creature's skull, pressing palm to matted fur, and the ability activated without resistance. Cold flowed upward, carrying fragments of hunger, of memory, of something that had once been many rats but was now one terrible whole.
[unknown] Soul Harvest: You extracted 8 units of Abyssal-touched spiritual energy from the remains. Ability Fragment acquired: Swarm Senses (Lesser).
The notification barely registered. More rats were coming.
Kael moved through the basement like a reaper, harvesting as he fought, taking fragments of abilities he didn't understand and might never use. The creatures fell one by one, their coordinated attacks failing against a foe who could consume their stolen essence and turn it against them.
But there were always more.
The Rat King—because there was always a King—emerged from the deepest darkness at the corridor's end. It stood seven feet tall at the shoulder, a mountain of corrupted flesh, its rat-features almost human in arrangement. Spiritual power radiated from it in waves that made Kael's teeth ache.
"You are not the first," the creature said, its voice a chorus of whispers. "You will not be the last. The Reaver's heir seeks what has already been taken."
Kael's knife felt inadequate against something that size. "What does that mean?"
"It means the Warden knows what you carry." The Rat King gestured with a paw that ended in claws like steel. "It means the ones who framed you still live. It means the ledger you sought was never about commerce."
"Then what was it about?"
"Names." The creature's grin split its elongated face. "The names of everyone who ordered your cellmate dead. The names of everyone who knows what the Soul Reaver truly is." It leaned closer, breath reeking of carrion. "The name of the man who wore your power before you were born."
The door behind Kael exploded inward. Warden Maren's Awakened presence flared as she entered the basement, Judge Harlan and Prosecutor Vance at her flanks. The Rat King shrieked and fled into shadows too deep for even Kael's enhanced senses to track.
Maren's eyes swept the carnage—dozens of rat corpses, black blood pooling on stone, the reek of death and spiritual discharge. Her gaze settled on Kael with something that might have been respect.
"You killed more in ten minutes than most Awakened manage in a year." She raised her hand, spiritual energy coalescing into visible light. "Impressive. But this ends here."
Kael prepared to fight.
Then the ceiling collapsed.
Stone and rubble fell in a cascade, forcing everyone to dive for cover. Through the hole, silhouetted against moonlit clouds, descended a figure wrapped in shadows that defied gravity. The presence that accompanied this arrival made Kael's soul recoil—not hostile, not friendly, vast. An entity that belonged to a category he hadn't encountered before.
Transcendent. The word surfaced from knowledge he didn't know he possessed. Stage three.
The figure landed without sound. When the dust settled, Kael saw a woman in robes that seemed woven from night itself, her face hidden behind a mask of pale porcelain. She surveyed the basement with cool detachment, taking in the rat corpses, the frozen combatants, the carefully maintained chaos.
"Warden Maren." Her voice carried like wind through dead leaves. "The Council of Six has questions about your facility's contents. I am here to answer them."
Maren's spiritual pressure spiked. "This is a matter for the Concordat—"
"The Concordat covers inter-Order conflict. This covers something else entirely." The masked woman turned toward Kael, and he felt her attention like a physical weight. "You are the one they executed. The Soul Reaver who walks out of graves. The Council wants to meet you."
"I'm not interested in meetings."
"Then you'll be interested in this." She produced a small journal from her robes—leather bound, pages thick with ink. "The ledger you sought. Names, transactions, proof of everything the Shadow Court has built in Thornwick. Including the name of the man who ordered your cellmate's death."
Kael's hands tightened. "How do you have that?"
"Because I took it from the Rat King an hour before you arrived." The masked woman extended the journal. "He was never meant to keep it. He was meant to ensure you learned its contents before you died here, in a prison basement, with no one to mourn you."
Maren's voice cut through: "Lady Vesper, this is under Order jurisdiction—"
"Your Order's jurisdiction ended when you imprisoned Awakened without trial and fed them to creatures of the Abyss." Lady Vesper didn't look at the Warden. Her gaze remained fixed on Kael. "The Council will hear of this. The Concordat will be enforced. But first—this journal. This is what you came for, is it not?"
Kael reached for it.
The moment his fingers touched leather, the masked woman moved. Her hand closed around his wrist, spiritual energy flooding through the contact with enough force to stagger an Awakened. But the Soul Reaver inside him responded, not with resistance but with hunger—
Pain. Both of them recoiled.
"Soul Reaver," Lady Vesper breathed. Something shifted in her posture, a fraction of a change that Kael barely caught. "You are... More than I was told."
"What were you told?"
"That you harvested abilities. That you defied execution. That you might be useful." She released his wrist, and the spiritual pressure diminished. "I was not told you could harvest from the living."
The basement fell silent. Even the distant sounds of the prison's alarm seemed to fade.
Kael's mind raced through implications. This woman—Lady Vesper—had information about the Soul Reaver. About its origin. About the names that could lead him to Zarek, to answers, to the truth of what had been done to him.
He could try to take the journal by force. He could try to escape. He could try to learn more.
The Soul Reaver whispered possibilities.
Maren made her choice. "Guards! The intruder is interfering with a lawful detention—"
The masked woman raised one hand. The Warden froze mid-word, spiritual energy locked around her like chains.
"The Council's business takes precedence." Lady Vesper's voice hardened. "You will release the prisoner. You will provide information about the cells below. And you will remember that the Concordat protects all Awakened from unlawful imprisonment—including those the Orders would see silent."
She turned back to Kael, the journal still extended.
"Take it. Read it. Then decide if you want answers badly enough to face what the Six Orders have hidden for three hundred years."
Kael took the journal.
The weight felt heavier than leather and paper should.

Outside Blackgate, fog swallowed the harbor. Kael found Pip waiting at the agreed location, her thin frame shivering in the cold. Her eyes went wide at the journal in his hands.
"You got it." Wonder and something like hope crossed her face. "You got it."
"Your share of the information." Kael opened to the first page, scanning names, dates, transactions. His cellmate's name appeared on the third line. "The people who ordered the deaths—you'll find them listed."
"And you?"
He closed the journal. Inside, he could feel the Soul Reaver stirring with awareness of something it recognized. Something familiar in the names, in the dates, in the patterns of conspiracy that stretched back decades.
"I found more than a ledger." Kael looked back at Blackgate's looming silhouette. Somewhere inside, Lady Vesper was extracting information that would reshape everything he thought he knew. "I found a trail that leads back to whoever created the Soul Reaver. Whoever shaped this power and put it inside me."
Pip studied his face. "What are you going to do?"
The question deserved consideration. But consideration was a luxury Kael couldn't afford. The orders had already mobilized—Maren would send word to her superiors, the Council would debate jurisdiction, and somewhere in the shadows, the people who'd framed him would learn that their convenient scapegoat had survived and was asking dangerous questions.
"They'll come for me," he said. "The Order of Dawn, probably. Maybe others. Anyone who knows what the Soul Reaver can do."
"And when they come?"
Kael's hand found the journal's leather binding, fingers tracing the embossed symbols he didn't recognize. Somewhere in these pages lay the name of the man who'd ordered his cellmate's death. The name of someone connected to the original Soul Reaver. The beginning of a trail that might end in answers or a grave.
The fog thickened around them, hiding the city, hiding the harbor, hiding the prison that still steamed in the distance.
"I run," Kael said. "I learn. I find the people who did this to me." He looked down at Pip, at the hope and hunger warring in her young face. "And when I find them, I harvest everything they know. Then I decide what they deserve."
Pip nodded slowly. "I can get you places. Safe houses, supply caches, information networks the Orders don't control. It'll cost you."
"Cost me what?"
"Access." Her missing fingers flexed. "When you find the people who killed my family, I want to be there. I want them to know why they're dying."
The request sat between them. Kael had rules—he didn't kill children, didn't betray genuine allies. But he also didn't make promises about who he might become when this was over.
"Stay alive until then," he said. "That's the best I can offer."
Pip's grin returned, sharp and knowing. "That's enough."
She led him into the fog, into the hidden paths of Thornwick's underworld, toward whatever came next.
Behind them, Blackgate Prison burned.
Not with fire—with light. Spiritual discharge erupted from the upper floors, cracks spreading through stone walls as containment wards failed in cascade. The Rat King Swarm's scream echoed across the harbor, a sound of freedom and hunger combined.
Something had broken loose.
Kael didn't look back. He'd learned long ago that some doors, once opened, couldn't be closed. The journal in his hands contained names. Lady Vesper contained knowledge. The Soul Reaver contained power that defied everything the Orders taught about spiritual limits.
The trail had begun.
All he had to do was follow it until it ended—on his terms, not theirs.
