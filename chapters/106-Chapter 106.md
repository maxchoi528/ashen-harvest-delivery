"The bodies were wrong."
Kael crouched beside the third corpse, rain streaming down his collar. These cultists hadn't died in ritual sacrifice. They'd been executed—methodically, professionally, a single cut across the throat while they slept.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted minor Shadow Affinity from the remains. Acquired: 3 spiritual essence fragments."
He pulled his hand back, wiping residue on his coat. The notification pulsed behind his eyes, but the information meant nothing. Shadow Affinity wasn't power—it was a liability. The Shadow Court used that ability like a brand.
Someone was framing the cultists.
Someone wanted the Orders to think Abyssal worshippers were dying here.
Footsteps splashed in the alley behind him. Kael spun, hand dropping to the blade at his hip.
"Easy." A woman's voice, rough with smoke. "I saw you enter. Figured you were either a very stupid thief or something worse."
She stepped into the weak light from the gas lamp—tall, gaunt, wearing the mud-brown leathers of someone who didn't want to be noticed. Her eyes caught the light wrong. Spiritual sensitivity, but not Awakened. A scout for one of the Orders.
"Third corpse I've seen this week," she continued. "Different alleys, same kill style. Someone's culling them."
"Culling?"
"The cult's gone quiet. For months, they were recruiting in the lower districts. Then ? Radio silence. Now their people are turning up dead." She crossed her arms. "You're the Soul Reaver everyone's whispering about. The one who walked out of his own execution."
Kael said nothing.
"I'm Sera," she offered. "I work for someone who wants to meet you. Someone who knows what's really happening in Thornwick."
"Why would I care?"
"Because your name is on the next list." She pulled a folded paper from her jacket. "Three days ago, someone in the Mortician's Guild received an order. Standard extraction protocol. Collect the body of Kael Ashborn from Ironpoint Prison before dawn tomorrow."
The rain seemed colder.
"The prison fire was supposed to kill you," Sera said. "It didn't. Now they need to clean up the loose end before—"
A sound. Metal striking stone.
Kael moved before thought caught up. He grabbed Sera's collar and yanked her down as a crossbow bolt hissed through the space where her head had been. It struck the wall behind them with a crack of splintering wood.
Three figures emerged from the shadows at the alley's far end. They wore the gray cloaks of the Mortician's Guild, but their movements were too fluid, too predatory. Guild collectors didn't move like hunters.
"Stay down," Kael muttered.
"How many—"
"Three. Awakened. Stage one, maybe two."
"You're stage two. You can take them."
Kael's hand tightened on his blade. "I can. But you asked who ordered those cultists dead. The answer just arrived."
The lead figure raised a hand. Spiritual energy crackled around his fingers—Order-trained combat techniques, not the corrupted wildness of true cultists.
"Kael Ashborn," he announced. "By authority of the Concordat Tribunal, you are charged with unauthorized spiritual harvesting, trafficking in stolen soul-essence, and conspiracy to commit Abyssal corruption. Surrender your weapons and come with us."
"Charged by whom?"
"The Order of Dawn. The Iron Vanguard. The Arcane Spire." A pause. "All three have evidence linking you to the Sepulcher Tablets."
Sera's breath caught. "That's impossible. The Tablets were destroyed centuries ago."
"The Tablets were never destroyed." The hunter's voice carried the weight of certainty. "They've been hidden. And this man knows where."
Kael's mind raced. The Sepulcher Tablets—the recordings of the first Soul Reaver. He'd heard whispers of them since his Awakening, fragments of information that never connected. But he'd never seen them. Never known their location.
Someone was lying.
"The charges are fabricated," he said flatly. "You're not here for a tribunal."
"Doesn't matter." The hunter smiled. "Dead men don't get trials."
He lunged.
Kael met him halfway, blade singing through the rain. Steel clashed against spiritual-enhanced flesh, the impact jarring through his arm. The hunter's companions split wide, flanking.
The one on the left swung a chain-weighted mace. Kael twisted, letting it graze his shoulder instead of crushing his skull. Pain flared—not his soul's pain, not the spiritual drain that should accompany combat at this level. His reserves stayed full.
Soul Reaver. Zero spiritual cost.
He riposted, driving the chain-wielder back. The third hunter circled behind Sera.
"Run," Kael ordered.
"I'm not leaving you."
"Then die here. Your choice."
She hesitated—just a heartbeat—and that was enough. The hunter behind her raised a blade coated in something that shimmered like oil on water. Nullification poison. One cut would sever her spiritual pathways permanently.
Kael abandoned his third opponent and sprinted.
He reached them in two strides. His hand closed around the hunter's wrist, wrenching the blade aside. Bones ground against each other. The hunter screamed.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted [Iron Vanguard Combat Technique - Pressure Strike] from the remains. Acquired: 1 spiritual essence fragment."
The notification flashed as the body crumpled. Kael didn't stop to analyze it. He threw the poisoned blade at the second hunter, then grabbed Sera and ran.
They burst out of the alley into a wider street, horse-drawn carts rattling past, pedestrians hurrying under umbrellas. Normal city life, oblivious to the violence thirty feet away.
"There." Sera pointed to a narrow gap between two buildings. "Service entrance for the old canal works. They won't follow us there—too many witnesses in the streets around."
"You hope."
"I know. I've been tracking these kills for weeks."
They slipped into the darkness. The canal works smelled of rust and stagnant water. Kael led them through a maze of pipes and abandoned machinery until they reached a dry chamber with a stone floor.
"Talk," he said, turning to face her. "Now. Who are you really working for?"
Sera leaned against a support beam, catching her breath. "I told you. Someone who knows what's happening."
"The Shadow Court."
She went still.
"Your shadow sensitivity. Your knowledge of these kills. The fact that you found me within hours of arriving in this district." Kael shook his head. "You're Court-trained. Which means whoever you're working for is Court-affiliated."
"They're not what you think."
"Every Order is what I think. They use people like tools and throw them away when they break." He stepped closer. "Your people ordered Gwen's squad into that ambush. Forty-seven soldiers died so the Shadow Court could position assets in the southern campaign."
Sera's expression flickered. Recognition. Guilt.
"How do you know that?"
"Because I harvested one of the officers who gave the order. His memories were... Instructive." Kael's voice hardened. "Now you tell me who wants me dead and why. Or I leave you here for those hunters to find."
"The Mortician's Guild has been compromised."
"By whom?"
"By someone inside the Order of Dawn. Someone high-ranking." She swallowed. "The Guild provides legal cover for soul-essence harvesting. They handle the bodies, the paperwork, the transport to Order facilities. For decades, they've been skimming a percentage for the Veiled Market. It's an open secret."
"But?"
"But now it's different. The skimming has stopped. The Guild is following orders exactly as the Orders specify. No extractions, no side sales, no profit margins." She met his eyes. "Someone is using the Guild to track specific individuals. People who might know something about the rifts."
"The sealed rifts."
"Seventy-two gates between the Material and the Abyss. Each one contains an Entity. Each one is sealed by a network maintained by the Six Orders." She paused. "The seals are weakening. Faster than they should. And someone is accelerating the decay."
Kael felt ice crystallize in his chest. The Great Rift—the one he'd encountered during his first weeks in Thornwick. The seal had been cracked then. He'd assumed it was natural deterioration.
It wasn't natural.
"The cultists," he said slowly. "They're not worshipping the Entities. They're working with whoever is weakening the seals."
"It's worse than that." Sera's voice dropped. "The cult isn't worshipping anything. They're scientists. Researchers. They've been studying the seal network for years, mapping its weaknesses, identifying which gates can be opened without triggering a response from the Orders."
"And someone is funding them."
"The Shadow Court isn't just monitoring you, Kael. The Court is monitoring everyone with Soul Reaver abilities. There have been seven documented Reavers in the last century. Six of them disappeared within months of Awakening. The seventh was executed by the Order of Dawn after harvesting from a sealedrift guardian without authorization."
"What's the connection?"
"The Soul Reaver is the only ability that can bypass the seal network. Normal Awakened can't touch the Entities directly—the Abyssal energy corrupts them, drives them mad. But a Reaver can harvest from anything. Including the seals themselves."
Kael stared at her. "You're saying I could open the rifts."
"I'm saying someone believes you can. Or will be able to, once you reach a high enough stage." She stepped closer. "The Whisper King isn't just a cult leader. He's something older. Something that remembers what the world was like before the seal network existed. And he's been waiting for a Reaver he could control."
"And I'm supposed to be that Reaver."
"You've been set up since the day you woke up from execution. The charges, the prison fire, the cultists—it's all designed to push you in a specific direction. Make you paranoid, isolated, desperate. A weapon that trusts no one is easier to aim."
The words hung in the stale air. Kael's mind raced through implications, connections, lies.
"The hunters," he said finally. "The ones who attacked us. They mentioned the Sepulcher Tablets."
"Because someone planted evidence linking you to them. Physical evidence. Documents with your name, your blood, your spiritual signature. Enough for any tribunal to issue a warrant."
"Who?"
"That's what I don't know. The Court knows the evidence exists, but we don't know who created it or why." Sera hesitated. "There's one possibility. The seventh Reaver—the one the Order executed. What if he didn't die? What if the Order kept him, studied him, extracted everything he knew about Soul Reaving before announcing his death?"
Kael's stomach clenched. "You're suggesting the Order has another Soul Reaver."
"I'm suggesting the Orders have been preparing for one of us to appear. For decades. They know what the Reaver can do. They know what we could become." She pulled something from her coat—a small vial filled with swirling gray energy. "This is why I was sent. The Court wants to make you an offer."
"What kind of offer?"
"Protection. Resources. Information about the people who want you dead." She held out the vial. "In exchange, you work with us. Help us identify who is weakening the seals, who is funding the cult, who is building a weapon out of Soul Reaver abilities."
"And if I refuse?"
"The hunters will keep coming. The Orders will keep hunting. The Shadow Court will keep watching." She shrugged. "But at least with us, you have a chance."
Kael looked at the vial. Soul-essence—the currency of power, forbidden by every law, traded in every shadow market. This was maybe a dozen vials' worth, enough to fund a small war.
"Why would the Court spend this much on me?"
"Because the Whisper King has something we want. A record of every seal location, every weakness, every contingency. If he opens even one rift before we're ready, the consequences will be catastrophic." Sera's voice hardened. "And because Dorian Ashford has joined his cause."
The name struck like a physical blow. Dorian—Kael's former mentor, the man who had taught him to survive in the Awakening slums, who had disappeared after Kael's arrest.
"He was the one who testified against me," Kael said slowly. "At my trial."
"He was the one who was paid to testify against you. By someone who knew you'd survive the execution. By someone who needed you to walk free."
"Dorian knew I would survive?"
"No. But he was told to ensure your conviction regardless. He didn't know the details—just that you were meant to die." She paused. "After the execution failed, Dorian went looking for answers. He found the cult. The cult found him."
Kael's vision darkened at the edges. Dorian, selling him out. Dorian, working with the enemy. Dorian, manipulated from the start.
"Where is he now?"
"The cult's primary research facility. An abandoned mine complex outside the city, near the site of the Third Rift." Sera's eyes glittered. "That's where the Sepulcher Tablets might be. Not hidden somewhere safe—actively being used to study the seal network."
A sound echoed through the canal chamber. Distant. Rhythmic.
Kael's hand moved to his blade.
"That's not the hunters," Sera whispered. "That's the Veil responding to spiritual stress. Something is opening."
The air itself seemed to thicken. Gray light pulsed at the edges of his vision—not hallucinations, but the hidden layer of reality that only stage two Awakened could perceive.
Something was tearing through.
"They're summoning it," Sera gasped. "The cult—they're using the Veil's resonance to punch through. An Entity. Here. In the city."
"Then we run."
"We can't. Once a summoning starts, the Veil locks onto the location. We'll be trapped in a resonance cage until the manifestation completes or the Orders intervene."
"How long?"
"Minutes. Maybe less."
Kael grabbed the vial from her hand and shoved it into his coat. Information now. Questions later.
"The hunters," he said. "Are there more?"
"At least a squad in the district. Probably more converging."
"Then we use them." He moved toward the chamber's exit. "The hunters don't know about the summoning. If we lead them into it, the Entity will drain their spiritual energy before they can report back."
"That's suicide."
"It's mathematics. Three hunters versus an Abyssal Entity. Which dies faster?"
Sera grabbed his arm. "Wait. There's another way."
"Talk fast."
"The cult uses specific rituals to anchor a summoning. Physical anchors—soul-essence constructs that must be destroyed to disrupt the flow." She pulled a folded map from her coat. "This shows the anchor points. Three locations, forming a triangle around the city. If we destroy even one, the summoning collapses."
"Which one is closest?"
"Old foundry, west district. Quarter mile from here."
Kael studied the map. Two routes—direct approach through open streets, or circuitous path through the canal system.
"Does the foundry have cultists?"
"Three, maybe four. They won't be Awakened—cult researchers rarely are. But they'll be protected by constructs and wards."
"And the hunters?"
"Will be between us and the foundry."
"Then we move now. Before they organize a proper search."
They left the chamber and entered the canal tunnels. Water dripped from ancient pipes. Somewhere above, the city continued its normal routines—oblivious to the Veil bleeding through reality.
Sera matched his pace, her breath coming in controlled bursts. Scout training, Kael noted. She knew how to move without wasting energy.
"The woman I work for," she said quietly. "Her name is Lyra. She was Shadow Court once. High-ranking. When she discovered what the Court was really doing—arming both sides of the Concordat conflict, manipulating Order politics—she was marked for termination."
"Your point?"
"She survived. And she's been working against the Court ever since. Not for the Orders—against everyone who treats the seal network as a political tool instead of a global defense." Sera glanced at him. "She's the one who ordered Gwen's squad to die. The one who gave that order to the Court operative who passed it to the Guild officer you harvested."
Kael stopped walking.
"You're telling me the woman you're working for is responsible for forty-seven soldiers dying."
"I'm telling you she was given the order and had to choose between following it and getting her entire network killed. She chose survival. She chose to live with the weight." Sera met his eyes. "Sound familiar?"
The words hit harder than any blade. Kael stood motionless in the tunnel, rain echoing in his memory, forty-seven faces that hadn't deserved to die.
"She's not asking for forgiveness," Sera continued. "She's asking for help. Because she knows the Whisper King has the same list of names. The same methodology. The same willingness to sacrifice innocent people for strategic advantage." She paused. "The difference is, she's trying to stop him. Even if she can never undo what she's done."
Kael resumed walking. His jaw ached from clenching.
"After we destroy the anchor," he said finally. "After we survive whatever's coming. Your woman and I have a conversation. In person."
"She'd like that."
"Don't mistake this for alliance. Don't mistake survival for forgiveness." His voice dropped. "If she wants redemption, she earns it by helping me end the Whisper King. Everything else is negotiable."
They emerged from the canal system into an alley behind a row of tenements. The air smelled of coal smoke and cooking grease. In the distance, church bells tolled the hour.
The foundry loomed ahead—a black skeleton of rusted iron and crumbling brick. Lights flickered in the upper windows. Spiritual energy pulsed at the edge of perception.
Kael pulled his blade and checked the edge. Still sharp enough to cut through soul-essence constructs.
"Three cultists," Sera whispered. "Two guards at the main entrance. One inside working the anchor."
"What about the hunters?"
"Moving this direction. Two minutes, maybe less."
Kael nodded. "Stay here. If this goes wrong, you run. Don't look back."
"And if it goes right?"
"Then we have a lot more problems."
He crossed the alley in silence. The foundry's side door hung open—carelessness or invitation. Kael stepped through into darkness.
The anchor dominated the main floor—a crystalline structure three stories tall, suspended by chains of woven light. Gray energy pulsed through its core, matching the rhythm of something vast and patient beyond the Veil.
One cultist sat at a desk nearby, transcribing notes. The other two patrolled the perimeter, carryingStaff weapons crackling with stored spiritual energy.
Kael moved.
The first guard died without making a sound—blade across the throat, body caught before it hit the ground. The second turned, weapon raised, and Kael was already inside his guard. Elbow to the nose, knee to the abdomen, blade sliding between ribs.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted [Cult Researcher - Abyssal Geography] from the remains. Acquired: 7 spiritual essence fragments."
The third cultist—the transcriber—scrambled backward, knocking over her chair. Her mouth opened to scream.
Kael covered the distance and pressed his blade against her throat.
"Where is the anchor's core?"
She blubbered something in a language that wasn't human.
"The core. Now."
"Below—beneath the floor—" Tears streaked her face. "Please—I was just copying texts—"
Kael knocked her unconscious with a precise strike to the temple. He didn't kill researchers. They weren't threats.
He found the floor hatch beneath a workbench—metal, heavy, sealed with a spiritual lock. His blade carved through the lock's structure, disrupting the energy pattern. The hatch groaned open.
Below, a chamber. And at its center, a sphere of compressed soul-essence, pulsing with stolen life force.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted [Anchor Core - Rift Resonance] from the remains. Acquired: 23 spiritual essence fragments, 1 unique ability fragment: [Veil Sight - Basic]."
Kael staggered as information flooded his mind. The anchor wasn't just channeling energy—it was a map. Fragments of seal locations, rift depths, Entity classifications. Information that should have taken years to compile.
Someone had spent decades building this.
A sound above. Shouting. The hunters had arrived.
Kael climbed back through the hatch and sprinted for the side door. Behind him, the anchor shuddered. Cracks spread across its crystalline surface.
The building groaned.
He burst into the alley and grabbed Sera's arm. "Run."
They ran. Blocks away, the foundry collapsed in on itself, crystalline fragments scattering through the night air. The summoning collapsed—the Veil's pressure vanished as if it had never existed.
The hunters wouldn't have survived. Not against that energy release.
Kael slowed to a walk, breathing hard. His coat stank of sweat and canal water. The vial in his pocket felt heavier than it should.
"The anchor," Sera said. "You destroyed it?"
"I harvested it. Including information about seal locations I shouldn't have access to."
"What kind of information?"
"The kind that makes me dangerous to everyone involved." He turned to face her. "Your woman wants a meeting. I want answers. About Dorian. About who ordered the cult to kill those soldiers. About what the Whisper King really is."
"She'll tell you what she knows."
"And if I don't like the answers?"
Sera shrugged. "Then you walk away. But you'll walk away knowing the truth. Isn't that better than the shadows you've been living in?"
A fair question. One Kael didn't have an answer to.
Distant sirens wailed—the city guard responding to the foundry collapse. Time was running out.
"The meeting," he said finally. "Where?"
"Tomorrow night. The old lighthouse at Thornwick Point. Come alone."
"And if I bring friends?"
"Then we have the conversation over your corpses."
Kael almost smiled. Almost.
He turned and walked into the darkness, leaving Sera behind. The information in his mind burned like embers—seal locations, rift depths, Entity signatures. Data that would take weeks to fully process.
One fragment stood out. A name, attached to a seal location.
*The Whisper King was once a man named Aldric Vane.*
Kael stopped walking.
Inquisitor Vane. The hunter who had been pursuing him since Thornwick. The man who had orchestrated his arrest, his false execution, his resurrection.
Vane wasn't hunting the Soul Reaver.
Vane was the Soul Reaver.
The implications cascaded through his mind. Everything the hunter had done—framing him, hunting him, driving him into the open—all of it served a purpose beyond capture.
Vane needed him. Needed him alive or dead, he wasn't sure which.
Kael stood motionless on the empty street, rain dripping from his coat. The lighthouse meeting tomorrow. The Shadow Court. The Whisper King's true identity.
And somewhere in the city, hunters who served a man who had been walking among them for decades, pretending to be righteous while plotting to break the seals.
Footsteps echoed behind him. Multiple sets. Moving fast.
Kael drew his blade and melted into the shadows.
The footsteps stopped. A voice called out—young, uncertain.
"Kael Ashborn? We were told to find you. The woman—Lyra—she sent us."
Three figures emerged from the rain. Awakened, all of them. Stage one, barely trained, armed with mismatched weapons that looked more like trophies than tools.
They weren't hunters.
They were recruits.
"We're deserters," the leader explained. "From the Iron Vanguard. We saw what the Orders are really doing—the corruption, the lies, the way they sacrifice their own people for political gain." He swallowed. "She said you'd know what to do. That you could show us a different path."
Kael studied them. Scared kids, barely out of adolescence, carrying the weight of choices they didn't fully understand.
The rain fell harder.
Tomorrow, the lighthouse. The truth about Vane. The Shadow Court's offer.
But tonight, he had a choice. Turn them away. Use them. Train them. Something in between.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted [???" from the remains. Acquired: incomplete data - subject still breathing."
The notification pulsed. Kael sheathed his blade.
"Follow me," he said. "And don't fall behind."
He walked into the rain, and three young Awakened followed.
Behind him, the footsteps of pursuit grew louder.
And ahead, the lighthouse waited, holding answers he wasn't sure he wanted.
