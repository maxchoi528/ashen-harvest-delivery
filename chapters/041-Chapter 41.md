The iron door screamed on its hinges. Kael's head snapped up from the stained mattress, his muscles coiling before his mind caught up.
Three guards stood in the corridor beyond. Two held pikes with crackling spiritual cores mounted near the spearhead. The third—a broad woman with a scar bisecting her left eyebrow—stepped forward with a rusted keyring the size of a dinner plate.
"Ashborn." She spat his name like venom. "Warden wants a word."
Kael rose from the cot, his bare feet meeting freezing stone. Three days in Blackgate's deepest level. Three days of questions about the bodies they found in his cell after the execution that didn't take. The guards had stopped asking yesterday. The silence felt worse.
He didn't speak. Wouldn't give them the satisfaction.
The scar-faced guard unlocked the inner gate. "Try anything stupid and we fry you where you stand. Spiritual inhibitors cost a fortune, but they'll hold an Awakened."
Her partner snickered. "Should've just let the executioner finish the job."
Kael walked between them, letting the pike points graze his ribs. Pain meant little now. He'd died once already. Whatever remained felt borrowed, provisional—a sketch of a man waiting for better paper.
The corridors of Blackgate wound downward in spirals, each level darker than the last. Prisoners hunched against walls, their eyes tracking his passage with the hollow hunger of men who'd forgotten the sun. Kael catalogued them automatically: spiritually inert, most. A few with the faint glow of awakening, probably criminals who'd stolen powers they couldn't control.
None of them mattered. Not yet.
The Warden waited in a chamber carved from the bedrock itself, lit by phosphorescent moss that cast everything in sickly green. He sat behind a desk cluttered with ledgers and bone-white porcelain figurines. His face belonged on a coin—handsome, weathered, stamped with the authority of the Thornwick Council.
"Ashborn." The Warden gestured to a chair bolted to the floor. "Sit."
Kael remained standing.
"Suit yourself." The Warden's fingers drummed against his desk. "I'll make this simple. Thirty-seven dead in your execution. The executioner. The witnesses. The priests. Thirty-seven corpses, all drained of spiritual essence. And, you walked out of that chamber alive."
"I didn't walk out of anything." Kael's voice came out rusted. Three days without water except the foul dregs they'd tossed into his cell. "They dragged me to the morgue. I woke up on a table."
"Among the corpses, yes." The Warden's eyes narrowed. "With your hands covered in the residue of harvesting. Did you think our spiritual investigators would miss that? Soul Reaver contamination is unmistakable."
Kael felt the first flicker of genuine unease. He'd woken in the morgue, disoriented, surrounded by bodies. He'd touched one to steady himself and felt—something. The sensation of hunger, of potential, of power that didn't belong in his hands. He'd pulled back before the ability fully engaged.
Apparently, he hadn't pulled back far enough.
"The Order of Dawn wants you." The Warden leaned forward. "The Iron Vanguard wants you. The Mortician's Guild is filing formal complaints about interference with their harvest rights. Do you understand what you are, Ashborn? Do you understand what they'll do when they get their hands on you?"
"They'll dissect me." Kael said it flatly. "Or try."
The Warden's lips twitched. "You've got spine, I'll grant you that. The Council's inclined to execute you again—properly this time, with blessed silver through the heart and priests to verify the soul's departure. But First Blade Varek has intervened. He claims you have information about the cult activity in the eastern districts."
Kael's memory surfaced: Zarek's face, twisted with fanatical certainty. The words carved into the walls of that warehouse. The ritual that had opened something that shouldn't have opened.
"Zarek." The name scraped out of him. "Prime Zarek."
"The cult's leader, yes." The Warden studied him. "You know him."
"He framed me for murder." Kael's hands curled at his sides. "He killed my squad and made it look like I defected to the Abyss."
"That's not what his testimony claims."
"His testimony." Kael laughed, and it sounded like gravel in a coffin. "Of course. The Abyssal cultist who's been feeding information to the Orders for years. Trusted asset. Reliable source. Tell me, Warden—how many of those trusted cultists are operating inside Thornwick's walls right now?"
The Warden's expression didn't change, but something shifted behind his eyes. "Careful, Ashborn. Accusations require evidence."
"I'm sitting in a prison cell waiting for a second execution. Evidence is a luxury I can't afford."
A knock echoed through the chamber. The scar-faced guard entered without waiting for acknowledgment, her face pale beneath the green phosphorescence.
"Warden. Problem in Block Seven."
"Not now, Marrek."
"It's the prisoners." She swallowed. "Three dead in their cells. Drained dry. Spiritual residue matches the execution chamber."
The Warden's chair scraped back. He rounded the desk, stopping inches from Kael's face. "You're supposed to be in isolation. No contact with other inmates. No access to—"
"Check your cameras." Kael met his gaze without flinching. "I haven't left that cell since you threw me in it."
"Then how?" The Warden's hand drifted toward a blade at his belt. "How are they dying with the same signature? Who else in this prison carries a Reaver's touch?"
The question hung in the air. Kael felt the weight of it—not just from the Warden, but from the architecture itself. Blackgate had been built on an old ley line nexus, its foundations touching the places where the Veil grew thin. Old prisons accumulated old secrets. Old corpses. Old power.
"I don't know." He told the truth. "But I'd like to find out."
The Warden stepped back. "Marrek. Take him to Block Seven. Let him see what his kind does to this facility."
"Warden—"
"He wants answers? Give him the scene. Let him wallow in it." The Warden's smile held no warmth. "And when the Order of Dawn's investigators arrive, they'll find him standing over the bodies, covered in evidence. One way or another, Ashborn, you're going to hang for this."
Kael said nothing as they led him away.

Block Seven occupied the eastern edge of Blackgate's lowest level, where the stones grew damp with condensation that never dried. The guards who'd escorted him from the Warden's chamber kept their distance now, their spiritual cores humming with barely contained fear.
Soul Reavers were rare. A specter whispered through the Orders' training halls—a warning, a cautionary tale. They harvested the dead. They stole power. They were the reason the Abyssal Wars had nearly ended in humanity's extinction, because the first Reaver had turned his gift against his own kind.
Kael had never considered what it meant to carry that legacy. The ability had awakened during his execution,, born from the moment his heart stopped. He'd felt it then—the hunger that wasn't hunger, the promise of anything, everything, if he just reached out and took it.
He hadn't reached. Not fully.
But someone in Block Seven had.
The cellblock stretched ahead in a corridor of rusted bars and darker shadows. Marrek stopped at the entrance, her hand shaking as she pointed toward the third cell on the left.
"Cadet Morris. Block supervisor before the transfer." Her voice cracked. "He was Awakened. Second stage. We found him like that an hour ago."
Kael approached the bars. Inside, a man slumped against the wall, his face frozen in an expression of absolute terror. His chest cavity gaped open—not physically, but spiritually. The wound showed itself as a discoloration, a bruise of absence where his soul had been.
The signs were unmistakable. The same marks he'd seen in the morgue. The same signature the Warden had identified.
But Kael hadn't done this.
He pressed his palm against the bars, closing his eyes. The Soul Reaver ability stirred at his touch, responding to the residual energy clinging to the metal. He let it reach out, sampling the traces of spiritual contact.
Information flooded through him.
A guard. Entering the cell four hours ago. Spiritual core—registered, the word surfaced from somewhere deep in the connection—spiritual core registered to a man named Dorvan. But the energy signature didn't match. Someone had masked their presence, mimicking another Awakened's resonance.
The guard had used some kind of tool. A vial, cracked open, poured onto the sleeping prisoner. Soul-essence, but wrong—tainted, mixed with something that paralyzed the spiritual system before the harvesting began.
Then the harvest itself. Fast. Efficient. Professional.
Kael pulled back, his hand smoking where it had touched the bars.
"Dorvan." He turned to Marrek. "Who registered that spiritual core?"
"I don't—" She paled. "The weapon records are in the Warden's office. Why would a guard harvest a prisoner? The Mortician's Guild has rights to all—"
"The Guild harvests legally dead." Kael's voice cut through her stammering. "This was murder. Someone's using Blackgate as a private hunting ground."
He moved to the second cell. The corpse here was older—a woman who'd probably died of malnutrition weeks ago, her body a husk barely worth noticing. But someone had harvested her anyway. The technique was different, sloppier, as if practiced on a lesser target before attempting something more ambitious.
Kael touched the bars again.
The traces were old, buried beneath layers of other contacts. Guards who'd cleaned this cell. Prisoners who'd passed through. And beneath it all, a thread of energy that felt familiar in a way he couldn't name—a resonance that tugged at something in his newly awakened soul.
He followed the thread.
Images surfaced. A figure in guard's clothing, face hidden beneath a hood. A vial of glowing liquid, green-gold and wrong. A whispered word that made the air vibrate: "Sepulcher."
Kael wrenched his hand back. His palm burned with residual contact, and for a moment, he saw double—the prison around him overlaid with spiritual traces, each contact point glowing like coals in the darkness.
The Sepulcher.
The word meant nothing to him. Or, it meant too much, a weight pressing against the edges of his memory. Someone had carved it into the walls of Zarek's warehouse. Someone had whispered it during the ritual that had opened the rift.
"Ashborn!" Marrek's shout echoed down the corridor. "We've got movement in the upper levels. Guards reporting something—spiritual disturbance, big one."
Footsteps. Running, multiple sets, growing closer.
Kael ignored them. The third cell held the most recent corpse—a young man, barely past his first Awakening, still carrying the unstable glow of a spiritual system not yet fully stabilized. His death had been recent, maybe an hour old.
The fresher the corpse, the better the harvest.
Kael's hand found the bars. He hesitated.
The Soul Reaver ability demanded contact. Demanded harvest. Demanded satisfaction. Every corpse was an opportunity, every death a door waiting to open.
But the Warden's words echoed in his mind: "When the Order of Dawn's investigators arrive, they'll find him standing over the bodies."
If he harvested now, he'd confirm everything they wanted to believe. Soul Reaver. Monster. Abomination.
If he didn't harvest, the opportunity might pass. The knowledge locked inside this fresh corpse—the memory of whoever had killed him—might dissolve before Kael could access it.
The footsteps grew louder. Torchlight flickered at the far end of the corridor.
Kael made his choice.
He pushed through the bars, his fingers closing around the dead man's wrist. The Soul Reaver ability surged through him—not the trickle he'd felt before, but a flood, a cascade of information that threatened to drown his consciousness.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted spiritual essence from the remains. +15 Essence Points acquired.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted skill fragment from the remains. Memory Fragment: Guard Patrol Routes acquired.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted partial memory from the remains. Image captured: Hooded figure, carrying vial. Name mentioned: Cassus.]
The dead man's final moments played through Kael's mind like a half-remembered dream. The hooded figure entering the cell. The paralysis spreading through limbs that wouldn't obey. The hunger that wasn't hunger, drawing life upward and outward.
And the voice, speaking words that shouldn't have made sense: "The Seal weakens. The Whisper King wakes. Soon, all will serve."
Kael released the wrist. The corpse slumped, truly empty now, its spiritual residue erased.
"Ashborn!" Marrek stood at the end of the corridor, her pike raised, her face twisted with fear. "Step away from the body!"
Behind her, more guards poured into the cellblock. At their head walked a man in the white robes of the Order of Dawn, his spiritual pressure dense enough to make the air tremble.
"By the authority of the Dawn Concordat," the robed man announced, "you are hereby remanded to the custody of the Order of Dawn for crimes against spiritual law. Resist, and we will be forced to—"
"You should be looking for Cassus." Kael's voice cut through the ritual words. "He's the one killing prisoners in this block. Wearing a guard's uniform. Carrying stolen soul-essence."
The Dawn enforcer paused. His eyes—one natural, one replaced with a glowing spiritual core—fixed on Kael with unsettling intensity.
"Cassus. The name of a dead man."
"Not so dead, apparently."
"And how would a death-row prisoner know this?"
"I touched the corpse." Kael held up his hands, showing the faint discoloration where his skin had made contact with spiritual residue. "My kind learns by touching. That's what Soul Reavers do."
A murmur rippled through the guards. Marrek's pike wavered.
"You admit it freely." The enforcer's tone sharpened. "You are a Reaver."
"I admit nothing except that someone in this prison is harvesting prisoners for sport, and if you drag me away before I find them, more will die." Kael stepped forward, past the bars of the cell, putting himself within arm's reach of the enforcer. "You want a monster? Fine. Let me hunt."
The enforcer studied him for a long moment. The spiritual cores in his eyes flickered, processing data Kael couldn't see.
"The Sepulcher." The word dropped like a stone into still water. "Your harvest showed you this word?"
"A figure in shadow mentioned it. While draining a prisoner."
The enforcer's face went pale. He raised one hand, and the guards behind him lowered their weapons.
"Warden Aldric." His voice emerged strained. "Release the prisoner to our custody. Immediately."
The Warden materialized from the crowd, his expression unreadable. "On what authority?"
"The authority of the Six Orders' Concordat of Sealed Knowledge." The enforcer produced a sigil from beneath his robes—a sunburst wrapped in chains. "This man has information about the Sepulcher. The Council must be informed."
"The Council meets in three days. If we wait—"
"There may not be a Council in three days." The enforcer's voice hardened. "The Sepulcher has been dormant for centuries. If someone is speaking its name in Thornwick's dungeons, it means the Rifts are bleeding faster than predicted. It means the Entities are waking."
Kael felt the weight of the words without fully understanding them. The Six Orders. The Rifts. The Entities. Concepts he'd never encountered—or had encountered and forgotten in the chaos of his false execution.
The enforcer turned to him. "You claimed you can identify the killer by touching his victims. Can you track him? Find him?"
"I don't know." Kael flexed his fingers, feeling the residual charge of harvested energy. "I've only done this a few times. Never deliberately."
"Then we test that limitation now." The enforcer gestured toward the corridor. "Show me what you are, Soul Reaver. Prove you're worth more alive than dead."

The next hour passed in a blur of cells and corpses.
Kael touched bodies throughout Block Seven, each contact sending cascades of information through his nervous system. The harvest left him dizzy, disoriented, his thoughts swimming with borrowed memories and fragmented skills he hadn't earned.
But it also worked.
The killer's pattern emerged: Cassus, a former Order of Dawn initiate who'd been declared dead during a Rift-sealing operation fifteen years ago. He'd survived,, and had spent the intervening years accumulating power through illegal harvesting. His current target appeared to be Awakened prisoners with unstable spiritual systems—their essence was easier to extract, more potent when refined.
The location of his next hunt: Block Twelve, three levels up, where the newest intake of prisoners awaited processing.
"Move." The enforcer's command cut through Kael's analysis. "Now."
They climbed through Blackgate's twisting passages, moving faster than protocol allowed. Guards scattered from their path, some pressing against walls to avoid the robed enforcer's spiritual pressure.
Block Twelve opened before them—a long hall of individual cells designed for high-value prisoners. The spiritual cores in the walls hummed with suppression arrays, dampening any Awakened's abilities to near-zero.
It should have been impenetrable.
The smell hit Kael first. Copper and ozone, the signature of spiritual blood. Someone had harvested recently, violently, without the careful technique he'd observed in the lower levels.
The cells along the right side stood empty, their occupants slumped against walls or sprawled across narrow cots. Six prisoners, all drained. All recently dead.
And at the far end, a door stood open. Beyond it, a figure in guard's clothing ran toward an exit that shouldn't exist.
"Cassus!" The enforcer lunged forward, his hand blazing with Dawn-fire. "By order of the Concordat, surrender yourself for—"
The figure turned. Beneath the hood, a face emerged that made Kael's blood freeze.
He recognized those features. That crooked nose, broken once and never properly set. Those eyes, pale as winter ice.
"Dorian." The name tore out of him. "Dorian was supposed to be dead."
His former comrade from the eastern garrison—the man who'd testified that Kael had defected, who'd watched impassively as they dragged him to execution—stood in the doorway of Block Twelve's emergency exit.
Dorian smiled. It was the expression of a man who'd already won.
"Hello, Kael." His voice carried the easy confidence of someone who'd never doubted his own survival. "I see you finally figured out what you are. Took you long enough."
The enforcer's Dawn-fire guttered. "You know this man?"
"He framed me for murder." Kael stepped forward, his hands shaking with a rage he couldn't contain. "He killed my squad. He—"
"He did what he was ordered to do." Dorian's smile widened. "Just like I'm doing now. The Whisper King sends his regards, Soul Reaver. He's been waiting for you for a very long time."
The emergency exit groaned. Something massive pressed against it from the other side, something that radiated cold so deep it burned.
"The rifts are opening." Dorian backed through the doorway. "All 72 of them. And when the Entities pour through, the Whisper King will be there to greet them. With you."
The door burst inward.
Darkness flooded the cellblock—Abyssal darkness, thick and hungry, carrying whispers that clawed at the edges of Kael's mind. Through the void, shapes moved. Not fully formed. Not yet. But coming, soon, drawn by the spiritual energy of the harvested corpses.
The enforcer grabbed Kael's arm. "We need to leave. Now."
Kael pulled free. "Dorian—"
"Escaped through the tunnel." The enforcer's face had gone grey. "The exit leads outside the city walls. We can't follow without—"
"Without what?"
"Without triggering a containment breach." The enforcer's voice cracked. "That tunnel was sealed decades ago because it connects directly to a Rift nexus. If we pursue, we risk destabilizing the membrane. If we don't—"
The whispers grew louder. The darkness pulsed, and in its heart, Kael saw eyes opening—countless eyes, watching, waiting, hungry for the soul-energy that only a Soul Reaver could provide.
"The Sepulcher tablets." He spoke without thinking, the knowledge surfacing from his harvest. "They contain a True Name. Someone's trying to use it."
"The tablets were destroyed." The enforcer grabbed him again, pulling him toward the exit. "Centuries ago. There are no—"
"The Sepulcher tablets." The words came from somewhere else, borrowed from the dead man's memories. "Hidden in the place where death meets judgment. Waiting for one who can read them."
The darkness surged forward.
Kael ran.

"Someone who harvests the dead."

"Can unmake the seals entirely." Kael finished the thought. "Or remake them."
The enforcer nodded slowly. "The Whisper King has been searching for a Reaver who could read the Sepulcher for centuries. He framed you, Kael. Orchestrated your execution. He needed you to awaken your abilities, and he needed you desperate enough to use them."
"The resurrection ritual." Kael's hands clenched. "The one that opened the rift in the warehouse. It wasn't about opening a Rift. It was about killing me and bringing me back as something else."

"And now the Whisper King wants to finish what they started."
"The Whisper King wants to finish what the first Reaver started." The enforcer stepped back. "Find the Sepulcher tablets, Ashborn. Before he does. Or the world burns."
He vanished into the smoke and confusion, leaving Kael alone in the street.
The city screamed around him. Rift alarms wailed. Guards struggled to maintain order against a population that could sense something coming, even if they couldn't name it.
Kael stood in the chaos, feeling the harvested power coiled in his soul. Dorian's face haunted him. The Whisper King's plans. The seventy-two Rifts bleeding into the world.
And beneath it all, a truth he was only beginning to understand:
He wasn't just a Soul Reaver.
He was the key to everything.
The whispers in the darkness hadn't stopped. Now, carried on the wind from somewhere far away, he heard them calling his name.
Kael.
Kael.
Kael.

Somewhere in the city, a door waited to be opened.

Kael stood before those doors now, his hand pressed against metal that hummed with ancient wards. The wards recognized something in his touch. Something that belonged.
The Sepulcher tablets waited inside.
Dorian's trail led here—the harvested memories had confirmed it. The cult had been using the Chapel as a meeting place for decades, hiding in plain sight while the Orders looked elsewhere for their secret headquarters.
Now the doors groaned, responding to a pressure from within.
Someone was trying to open them from the inside.
Kael pushed.
