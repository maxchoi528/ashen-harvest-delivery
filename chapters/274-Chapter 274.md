The blade missed Kael's throat by three inches. He ducked, felt the wind of its passage against his scalp, and rolled beneath a rusted iron table that exploded into shrapnel behind him.
"You're faster than the reports suggested." The voice came from everywhere and nowhere, echoing off the sweat-stained walls of the cellar. A figure emerged from the coal dust, face hidden beneath a hood of black silk. "The Whisper King will be disappointed. He expected you to die in that alley."
Kael rose to his feet, tasting copper. His fingers found the hilt of his sword—a crude thing, forged from scavenged scrap, but it sang when he drew it. "The Whisper King sent you?"
"Sent? No." The figure moved like smoke, circling left. Kael tracked the motion, every muscle coiled. "Invited. There's a difference."
Above them, the floorboards creaked. Other footsteps. Kael counted at least four distinct gaits—heavier than the first, armored.
"Thornwick's Undercroft," Kael said. "I didn't realize it had been occupied."
"Occupied?" A laugh, soft and terrible. "Child. This cellar has belonged to the Shadow Court for sixty years. You walked into our house the moment you took that contract."
The contract. Three days ago, a dead drop in the Merchant Quarter: a sealed envelope, one hundred gold sovereigns, and an address. The message inside had been simple. *Someone is killing our people. Find them. Stop them.*
Kael had been starving. The sovereigns had bought him a week of meals and a room above a gin-house. He hadn't questioned the source.
Stupid.
"The Order of Dawn thinks you're a cultist," the hooded figure continued. "The Iron Vanguard thinks you're a rogue killer-for-hire. The Arcane Spire thinks you're an experiment gone wrong. Everyone is looking for you, Soul Reaver. And yet here you stand, in the one place no one would think to search."
"I could ask how you know what I am."
"You could. But you won't." The figure spread gloved hands. "Because you've already worked it out. You've felt it. That hunger. That pull. Every corpse you've touched has whispered to you, hasn't it? Promised you more. Promised you everything."
Kael's jaw tightened. The first harvest had been an accident—a dying man in an alley, blood on his hands, and knowledge pouring into his skull like liquid fire. The technique had been called Razor Wind, a minor cutting technique used by an Awakened of the Fourth Tier. He'd used it that same night to gut two men who'd tried to rob him.
The second harvest had been deliberate. A cultist, killed by the city guard. Kael had waited until the crowd dispersed, touched the cooling flesh, and pulled. He'd gotten fragments—a map of rifts, a name, a prayer to something that slithered in the dark. The information had kept him alive for a month.
The third, fourth, fifth harvests had followed. Each one easier than the last. Each one more rewarding.
"I know what you are," the figure said. "And I know what you could become. Join us. The Shadow Court has resources, information, protection. The Orders will hunt you to the ends of the earth otherwise. We can offer sanctuary."
"Join you." Kael tested the words. "And do what?"
"What you were born to do. Harvest. Consume. Grow." The figure stepped closer, and Kael caught a glimpse of eyes beneath the hood—pale, pupilless, wrong. "You think you're the only one? There are others like you. Scattered. Hunted. We've been watching you for months, waiting for you to mature."
The footsteps above grew louder. The cellar door groaned as weight pressed against it from above.
"Last offer," the figure said. "Come with us willingly, or be brought."
Kael answered with steel.
The fight lasted nine seconds.
Razor Wind sang through the dusty air, but the hooded figure was already moving—faster than any human had a right to move, faster than Kael's blade could track. A foot caught him in the chest and sent him crashing into a pile of rotting grain sacks. The sword skittered from his grip.
Kael scrambled backward, reaching for the weapon, but a boot came down on his wrist. Bones cracked. He swallowed the scream.
"Impressive," the figure said, standing over him. "You've advanced further than we expected. Sovereign stage at your age. The raw potential is remarkable."
"Let me up."
"Let me think about it." The figure crouched, examining Kael's face like a butcher examining a carcass. "The Sepulcher tablets mention a technique. A way to harvest not just skills and memories, but something deeper. Soul fragments. The essence of what makes a person who they are."
Kael's blood ran cold. "The what?"
"The true harvest. Every Soul Reaver discovers it eventually—the ability to extract the soul itself, not just the echoes left behind. But most die before they learn how. The power required would destroy a lesser being." The figure's pale eyes gleamed. "You wouldn't destroy. You'd consume. Become."
"You're insane."
"I'm patient." The figure rose. "The door is about to break. Decide now."
Kael looked at the cracked wrist, at the sword three feet away, at the four sets of boots now hammering on the cellar door. His options narrowed to a razor's edge.
"Give me a reason," he said.
"Zarek."
The name hit like a physical blow. Kael's chest seized.
"Prime Zarek," the figure continued. "The man who framed you. The man who made you a death-row prisoner in your own city. He's here. In Thornwick. Arrived three days ago, posing as a merchant from the eastern provinces."
"Where?"
"The Concordat requires neutral ground for inter-Order conflicts. But Zarek is not acting as an Order operative. He's here as a private citizen, pursuing private interests." The figure smiled. "Private interests that happen to include you. There's a bounty on your head, Kael Ashborn. Fifty thousand sovereigns. The Shadow Court has declined to collect."
"Why tell me this?"
"Because we want you alive. Zarek wants you dead. Consider it a gesture of goodwill."
The cellar door exploded inward. Four figures in gray leather dropped through the gap, weapons drawn. They fanned out, surrounding Kael and the hooded figure.
"Clear the room," one of them commanded.
The hooded figure raised a hand. "Stand down. He's with us."
Confusion rippled through the team. The commander—tall, scarred, missing two fingers on his left hand—frowned. "Maul, we received orders—"
"Orders change. This one is an asset."
"He's a Reaver."
"Which is why he needs to be controlled, not killed." The figure—Maul—glanced down at Kael. "Get up. Slowly. Your wrist will heal, but not if they put three inches of steel through your spine first."
Kael rose, cradling the broken wrist against his chest. His mind raced. Zarek. Here. The man who'd orchestrated everything—the framing, the execution, the death of everyone Kael had once called friend. Three years of running, hiding, starving, and now the bastard was within reach.
But not alone. Never alone. The cult had protected him, or the Orders, or both. Kael couldn't walk into Thornwick and gut him in the street. Even if he could, the Shadow Court had just made clear that they wanted him alive.
"Why do you care what happens to me?"
Maul began walking toward a tunnel at the back of the cellar, hidden behind a collapsed shelf. "The Whisper King has use for a Reaver of your caliber. Zarek is an obstacle. We remove obstacles."
"By helping me kill him?"
"By helping you take what's his." Maul glanced back. "You haven't harvested an Awakened above the Fourth Tier yet, have you? Never touched a truly powerful corpse. Zarek's security detail includes a former Sentinel of the Iron Vanguard. Ascendant stage. Killed in the Rift Wars, posthumously promoted to Transcendent for his sacrifice."
Kael's breath caught. A Transcendent corpse. The spiritual energy contained within would be staggering. The skills, the techniques, the raw power...
"That kind of harvest would change you," Maul said. "Mark you in ways the Orders would recognize. You'd be declaring war on every faction that benefits from the current order. Are you prepared for that?"
The tunnel yawned before him, dark and cold. Above, Thornwick roared with life—steam whistles, wagon wheels, the endless clamor of a city that didn't know how close it was to annihilation. Somewhere in that chaos, Zarek was plotting. The cult was moving. The seals were weakening.
Kael thought of the corpses he'd harvested. The skills that now lived inside him. The hunger that never went away, no matter how much he consumed.
"The Transcendent," he said. "You can get me access?"
"We can get you close. What you do when you arrive is your choice." Maul stepped into the tunnel. "The Whisper King is patient. He'll wait for you to decide. But Zarek won't. Whatever he's planning, it's happening within the week. After that, he vanishes back to wherever the Order hides its traitors."
"And if I refuse? Walk away?"
"Then you stay a fugitive. The Orders hunt you. The cult hunts you. The Shadow Court withdraws its protection, and you're alone in a city that wants you dead." Maul's voice echoed from the darkness. "The choice is yours, Soul Reaver. It always has been."
Kael looked at his broken wrist, at the sword in the dust, at the armed operatives watching him with barely concealed hostility. He thought of everything he'd lost. The execution. The betrayal. The long, grinding years of running.
He picked up the sword. Sheathed it.
"Lead the way."
---
The tunnel system beneath Thornwick was older than the city itself. Kael followed Maul through passages carved from living rock, past sealed doors covered in script he couldn't read, through chambers filled with supplies—crates of weapons, barrels of alchemical compounds, racks of robes in Shadow Court gray.
"How many of you are there?"
"Enough." Maul didn't slow. "The Shadow Court isn't an army. We're a network. Operatives in every city, every Order, every faction that matters. We don't conquer. We observe. And occasionally, we intervene."
"Like now."
"Like now."
They emerged into a vaulted hall lit by phosphorescent moss. Tables lined the walls, covered in maps, documents, small figurines representing pieces on a board. Kael recognized Thornwick's layout immediately—the Undercroft, the Merchant Quarter, the Order compounds, the seal sites.
"The cult activity in Thornwick," he said. "It's organized."
"Very." Maul indicated a map marked with red pins. "Seventeen confirmed cult cells, operating independently but receiving coordination from a central source. Zarek's arrival coincided with a surge in activity. They're preparing something."
"The seals."
"The seals." Maul nodded. "There are seventy-two rifts on this continent, each sealed after the Abyssal Wars. The seals weaken over time. Maintenance is required. The cult believes they can accelerate the degradation, force a mass unsealing, and ride the chaos to power."
"That's insane."
"That's faith." Maul's voice carried something almost like respect. "The Entities offer rewards for service. The cultists believe they'll be elevated, transformed, made into something greater than human. It's the same promise every religion makes, just with sharper teeth."
Kael studied the map. The seal sites were scattered across the continent, but three in particular drew his attention—locations where the pins clustered thickest, where the threat level was marked in crimson.
"The Great Rift," he said. "The Hollow. The Sunken Barrow."
"The most unstable. The ones most likely to fail first." Maul traced a line between them. "If those three fall simultaneously, the pressure on the remaining seals will increase exponentially. Chain reaction. The entire network could collapse within months."
"And then?"
"And then the Abyss comes flooding through. Seventy-two Entities, each one a god in its own right, each one hungry for the world they were exiled from." Maul met Kael's gaze. "The Orders know. They've known for decades. But they argue about solutions while the cult acts. The Concordat binds their hands. The Shadow Court has no such limitations."
"You're using the threat as leverage."
"We're using every tool available." Maul turned away from the map. "The Whisper King has played this game for two hundred years. He has no intention of losing now. But he needs pieces in position. Pieces like you."
Kael's broken wrist throbbed. He cradled it tighter, watching the red pins pulse like wounds on the map.
"The Transcendent corpse," he said. "Where is Zarek keeping it?"
"In his quarters. The Merchant's Guildhall, upper floors. Guarded constantly, warded against spiritual intrusion." Maul handed him a folded paper. "Schedules. Patrol routes. The layout. Everything you need to get close."
"And if the harvest goes wrong? If I'm discovered?"
"Then you fight your way out or die trying. Either way, you've served a purpose." Maul's pale eyes glittered. "The Shadow Court has invested considerable resources in this operation. We expect a return."
"What kind of return?"
"The Whisper King will explain when the time comes. For now, rest. Heal. Study the information. Tomorrow night, Zarek hosts a private gathering in the Guildhall. His security will be focused outward, watching for threats. The interior will be vulnerable."
Kael unfolded the paper. The layout was precise, annotated in elegant script. Guards marked in blue. Wards in yellow. The Transcendent's location circled in red.
"If I do this," he said slowly, "I'm not doing it for the Whisper King. I'm not doing it for the Shadow Court. I'm doing it because Zarek destroyed my life and someone has to answer for that."
"Naturally." Maul inclined his head. "We never imagined otherwise."
---
They gave him a room. Small, clean, a cot with actual sheets. Kael sat on the edge of the mattress and worked on his wrist, feeling the bones grind as he set them. The pain was familiar now—an old companion, like the hunger that lived in his chest.
He thought about what Maul had said. *The true harvest. Soul fragments. The essence of what makes a person who they are.*
He'd never attempted anything like that. The skills and memories came easily enough, drawn from the residual energy left behind after death. But the soul itself? That was something else. That was crossing a line that couldn't be uncrossed.
The notification appeared in his vision, text burning itself into the air:
[Soul Harvest: You extracted X from the remains. Y acquired.]
No harvest this time. No corpses touched. But the system was there, waiting. Reminding him what he was.
Kael thought about the Transcendent's corpse. The power contained within. The skills and memories that had been earned over a lifetime of struggle and sacrifice. If he could take even a fraction of that...
His stomach clenched. The hunger surged, and for a moment he saw the world through different eyes—saw the spiritual energy flowing through every living thing, saw the dead as pools of potential waiting to be claimed.
*Is this what I am?* he wondered. *A monster wearing a man's face?*
The question had no answer. There was only the next step, and the next, and the next.
---
Evening came. Kael stood before a tarnished mirror, binding his wrist with strips torn from an old shirt. The bone was set, knitting already beginning, but the joint would be weak for days.
Through the room's single window, he could see the city. Thornwick sprawled across the river valley, steam rising from a thousand chimneys, lanterns flickering to life as darkness fell. Somewhere in that maze of streets and alleys, Zarek was preparing for a party. Somewhere, guards were posted, wards were maintained, a Transcendent's corpse lay waiting.
Kael sheathed his sword. Adjusted his coat to hide the weapon's bulk. Checked the folded paper Maul had given him, memorizing the routes, the guard positions, the windows of vulnerability.
*Seventeen cult cells. Seventy-two rifts. The Abyss waiting behind the Veil.*
None of it mattered if Zarek walked away free. None of it mattered if Kael kept running, kept hiding, kept being the prey instead of the predator.
He opened the door.
Maul stood in the corridor, flanked by two operatives. "Ready?"
"No."
"You don't have to be." Maul gestured toward the stairs. "The night is young, and the Guildhall awaits. Do what needs to be done. We'll handle the rest."
Kael walked past him, toward the exit, toward the tunnel that would carry him up into the city's underbelly. His wrist ached. His head throbbed. The hunger in his chest gnawed like a living thing.
But beneath the pain, beneath the fear, something else stirred. Something that had been sleeping since the day they'd put the noose around his neck.
*Vengeance.*
The night swallowed him whole.
---
The Merchant's Guildhall rose from the cobblestones like a monument to excess. Three stories of white marble, brass fixtures, stained glass windows depicting ancient victories. Music drifted from within—strings, pipes, the clink of glasses.
Kael circled the building twice, counting guards, timing their movements. The front entrance was suicide. The side doors were better, but still heavily monitored. The third option was the servant's entrance in the rear, where deliveries were received and garbage was taken out.
He found a dead end alley, climbed a rainspout, and swung himself onto a second-story balcony. The window was unlatched. He slipped inside.
A storage room, filled with crates of wine and preserves. Kael moved silently, pressing his ear to the door. Footsteps passed outside—he waited until they faded, then eased into the corridor beyond.
The layout matched the paper exactly. He moved through servant passages and service corridors, avoiding pools of lamplight, ducking into shadows whenever footsteps approached. The party noise grew louder, then softer, depending on which direction he traveled.
Three floors up, the architecture changed. The servants' quarters gave way to private chambers, the wallpaper thickening, the carpets growing plush. Wards hummed at the edges of his perception—a barrier of spiritual energy designed to alert the wearer to intrusion.
Kael pushed through them. The wards tingled against his skin, then fell silent. The sensation was strange, like walking through cobwebs. He didn't understand how he'd bypassed them, only that he had.
*Part of what you are,* something whispered in the back of his mind. *They recognize their own.*
The Shadow Court's operatives had walked these halls before. Others like him.
Kael found the door marked with a symbol he didn't recognize—a circle intersected by three lines. The paper indicated this was Zarek's private study. The Transcendent's remains should be within.
He tried the handle. Locked.
"Kael Ashborn."
The voice came from behind him. Kael spun, hand going to his sword.
A man stood at the end of the corridor. Tall, gaunt, dressed in robes of deep crimson. His face was lined with age, but his eyes burned with something far younger. Recognition. Anticipation.
"Zarek."
"Three years," Zarek said. "Three years since the noose closed around your throat. I watched the execution from the shadows. Watched them cut you down and dump your body in a pauper's grave." He smiled. "I assumed that was the end. I was wrong."
Kael drew his blade. "You framed me. You killed everyone I—"
"I did what was necessary. You were a loose end. A variable that couldn't be controlled." Zarek raised a hand, and spiritual energy crackled between his fingers. "The cult demanded your death. The Order of Dawn obliged. Case closed."
"The cult."
"The Hollow Communion. My employers, before I became my own employer." Zarek's smile widened. "They've been busy, these past decades. Placing pieces. Weakening seals. Preparing for the grand unsealing that will remake this world."
Kael's grip tightened on the sword. "You're the one coordinating the cells."
"Someone had to. The cultists are fanatics, not strategists. They need guidance. Leadership." Zarek stepped forward. "The Shadow Court thinks they're playing me. The Orders think they've contained the threat. But the game is much larger than any of them understand."
"And what do you want?"
"What I've always wanted. Power. Ascension. A seat at the table when the new world is built." Zarek's eyes narrowed. "You're standing in my house, boy. Surrounded by my people. Armed with a blade that couldn't cut silk at ten paces. How do you imagine this ends?"
Kael lunged.
The fight was brief and brutal. Zarek was faster than he looked, his spiritual energy manifesting as blades of condensed force that deflected Kael's swings and drove him back. Twice Kael felt the wind of those attacks miss his throat by inches. Once a strike caught his shoulder, spinning him into the wall.
But Kael had been fighting for survival since the day he crawled out of his own grave. He knew how to survive.
He let himself stumble. Let his blade clatter from his grip. Zarek pressed forward, confident, raising his hand for the killing blow—
Kael's boot caught Zarek in the knee. Cartilage shattered. Zarek screamed and staggered. Kael caught him by the throat and slammed him against the wall.
"The corpse," he said. "Where?"
Zarek laughed, blood on his teeth. "Behind the painting. The room's warded, but you'll never—"
Kael threw him to the ground and crossed to the wall. The painting was a landscape—mountains, forest, a river winding toward distant peaks. He tore it aside, revealing a door.
The lock was spiritual. Kael pressed his palm against it, felt the resistance, and *pushed*. The lock shattered. The door swung open.
The room beyond was cold, sterile, lit by softly glowing crystals. In the center stood a glass coffin, and within it—
The Transcendent lay in perfect preservation. A man in his middle years, scarred and weathered, wearing the remnants of Iron Vanguard armor. Spiritual energy pulsed beneath his skin, visible even through the glass, waiting to be claimed.
Kael approached. His hand hovered over the coffin.
*Soul fragments,* Maul had said. *The essence of what makes a person who they are.*
He could feel the power radiating from within. The skills, the memories, the raw spiritual energy accumulated over decades of combat and struggle. This was what he'd been hungry for. This was what he'd been missing.
His palm touched the glass.
The notification appeared:
[Soul Harvest available. Target: Transcendent-class Awakened. Estimated yield: 3400 spiritual units. Skills: Iron Sentinel (Legendary), Undying Will (Rare), Void Anchor (Epic). Memory fragments: HIGH. Warning: Extraction at this scale will leave permanent marks on the recipient's soul.]
Kael hesitated.
Permanence. Once he crossed this line, there was no going back. The Orders would know. The cult would know. Every faction that hunted Soul Reavers would have a new reason to want him dead.
But Zarek was still alive. Zarek was still planning. The cult was still moving, the seals were still weakening, and if Kael walked away now, nothing would change.
He pressed his hand through the glass. Touched the corpse.
The harvest began.
Power flooded into him—not the gentle trickle he'd experienced before, but a torrent, a deluge that threatened to sweep away everything he was. He gasped, felt his knees buckle, but he held on, drawing the Transcendent's essence into himself piece by piece.
Skills slotted into place. Iron Sentinel granted him an understanding of armor, weapons, tactical formations—knowledge that felt like it had always been inside him, waiting to emerge. Undying Will hardened his resolve, his ability to push through pain and exhaustion. Void Anchor anchored his spiritual energy, preventing drain and stabilizing his reserves.
And beneath it all, the memories. Fragments of a life lived in service—the battles fought, the comrades lost, the oath sworn to protect the innocent from the darkness beyond the Veil. Kael felt the Transcendent's final moments: the Rift collapse, theEntity rising, the choice to stand and fight when everyone else fled.
*I held the line,* the memory whispered. *I died, but I held the line.*
Kael opened his eyes. The glass coffin was empty. The crystals had dimmed. The body within had crumbled to dust.
[System Notification: Soul Harvest complete. 3400 spiritual units acquired. Skills assimilated: Iron Sentinel (Legendary), Undying Will (Rare), Void Anchor (Epic). Memory fragments integrated. Permanent alteration: Soul density increased by 15%. Warning: Spiritual signature now visible to all Awakened of Fourth Tier or higher.]
He heard footsteps in the corridor. Heavy, armored, moving fast.
Guards. They'd found him.
Kael turned, sword in hand, and faced the door.
The first guard burst through. Kael moved without thinking—Iron Sentinel guided his blade, finding gaps in the armor's protection, striking with precision that felt inhuman. The guard fell. The second came. Kael deflected, riposted, killed.
He fought his way down the corridor. Down the stairs. Through a servant's passage and out into the night. Behind him, shouts rose. Ahead, the city sprawled in darkness.
Kael ran.
He didn't stop until Thornwick was a distant glow behind him, until the road stretched empty in both directions, until his lungs burned and his legs trembled and he could finally think.
He'd done it. The harvest. The Transcendent's power was his now, woven into his very soul. Zarek was still out there—wounded, but alive—but the mission was complete. The Shadow Court had what it wanted.
Or had they?
Kael looked at his hands. The skin seemed the same, but beneath the surface, everything had changed. He could feel the new density of his soul, the increased capacity for spiritual energy. He could sense the ward around the city now, the weak points in Thornwick's spiritual defenses, the places where the Veil thinned and the Abyss pressed close.
*They knew,* he realized. *The Shadow Court. They knew what the harvest would do to me. They wanted me to change.*
The question was: change into what?
He heard something in the darkness behind him. Footsteps. Too quiet to be horses, too heavy to be human.
Kael turned.
Eyes watched him from the treeline. Not human eyes. Not animal. Something older, hungrier, waiting at the edge of perception.
The Entity pressed against the weakening Veil. It sensed his changed soul, his new power. It reached out with something that felt like a whisper, like a promise.
*Come closer,* it seemed to say. *I can give you more. I can give you everything.*
Kael's hand tightened on his sword. The hunger in his chest stirred, answering the call.
Somewhere in the distance, a bell began to toll. Dawn was coming.
And the road ahead stretched endless, dark, and full of monsters.
