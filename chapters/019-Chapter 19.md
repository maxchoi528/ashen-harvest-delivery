The creature burst from the alley wall like flesh tearing through wet paper. Kael dropped into a crouch, fingers scraping against cobblestones slick with evening drizzle. Shadowed limbs twisted at angles human joints could not achieve. Four arms ended in claws that dripped something darker than blood. The thing's face—if the mass of gnashing teeth and pulsing veins qualified—fixed upon him with predatory recognition.
"You." The word emerged from that horror in a voice like grinding glass. "The Veil-touched one. The Prime will be pleased."
Kael's hand found the hilt of his blade. Not the best weapon for this situation, but the only thing he'd had when three cultists had cornered him in this Thornwick alley twenty minutes ago. He'd dealt with them. Their bodies cooled in the shadows behind him, but this new arrival had crawled out of the walls themselves.
"What Prime?" Kael asked. Stalling. Gauging distance. The street behind the creature emptied into the main thoroughfare—too far, too exposed. The alley's narrow walls worked both ways.
The abomination lunged.
Kael moved left, feeling supernatural reflexes surge through limbs that had belonged to a Watercast Awakened named Orlan until three days ago. The creature's claws raked the stone where he'd stood. Sparks scattered. The stench of burning rock reached his nostrils—whatever this thing was, its touch corroded.
"Little reaver." The creature circled, blocking the alley's far end. "The Prime hungers for your kind. The Whisper King remembers what you are. What you could become."
Not a cultist. Something else. Something that spoke of kings and hungers and remembered things that should have stayed buried.
The sound of boots on stone reached him from the street. Multiple sets, moving with military precision.
"Hold position!" A voice, deep and commanding, barked from beyond the alley mouth. "Third squad, flank left. Nobody enters or leaves until I give the word."
The creature's head snapped toward the sound. For the first time, something like hesitation crossed its twisted features.
"Interference." It hissed the word like a curse. "The Six dare to—"
A crossbow bolt punched through its shoulder. Black ichor sprayed. The creature shrieked, a sound that made Kael's teeth ache, and spun back toward him.
Kael didn't wait for it to recover. He closed the distance in three strides, blade rising. The creature's remaining arm caught his wrist, impossibly strong fingers crushing against bone. Pain flared white-hot through his arm. But he'd felt worse. The Orlan whose life he'd harvested had been a Watercast specialist—Kael's skin hardened to stone-gray moisture as the ability activated instinctively.
The creature's grip slipped. Its eyes widened.
"What are you?" it demanded.
Kael's answer came as a knife across its throat.
Not a clean cut. The flesh was wrong, resistant, like cutting through rubber packed with gravel. But it was enough. The abomination stumbled backward, clawing at its ruined neck. Ichor poured between its fingers. Its movements grew sluggish.
"Hold!" The commanding voice again. "Fire on my mark!"
Kael threw himself flat as a volley of bolts hissed through the space he'd occupied. Two found the creature's chest. One caught its knee. The abomination collapsed, still twitching, still trying to regenerate.
A man vaulted into the alley, armor catching what little light filtered through Thornwick's perpetual smog. Steel-grey hair cropped short. A scar that started above his left eye and disappeared into his hairline. He moved like someone who had fought things like this before and survived through skill than luck.
"Stay down," he commanded, raising a device that crackled with spiritual energy.
The creature's remaining eye fixed upon the newcomer. Something like recognition passed through its alien features.
"Commander Gallagher." The abomination's voice had changed, become almost conversational despite the fatal wounds. "The Wolf of Ironmoor. Still playing lapdog for the Council after all these years?"
Commander Rex Gallagher, apparently. The name meant nothing to Kael, but the title carried weight—heavy infantry, siege specialists, the Order most likely to hold a line when everything else collapsed.
"Still speaking, I see." Gallagher's device hummed louder. "Let's fix that."
Spiritual fire erupted from the weapon, washing over the creature in a wave of purifying light. The abomination screamed, thrashed, and finally went still. Its flesh crumbled to ash, leaving only a scorched patch on the cobblestones.
Gallagher turned his attention to Kael. The assessment was quick, professional, dismissive of threat. "You're Awakened. Stage one, maybe stage two." His eyes flicked to the bodies Kael had left behind. "Those yours?"
Kael rose slowly, keeping his hands visible. The blade remained sheathed at his side. "They attacked me."
"Three on one. And you left them intact enough to identify." Gallagher's tone suggested this was noteworthy than damning. "You're either confident or stupid. Which is it?"
"Survival instinct."
One corner of Gallagher's mouth twitched. Not a smile. "The creature called you reaver. Care to explain that?"
Here it was. The moment where Kael decided how much truth to offer. The Commander knew something about abominations and Prime and Whisper King. That knowledge had value. But revealing the Soul Reaver ability meant exposure, meant becoming a target for the Orders that hunted his kind.
Better to test the waters first.
"What was that thing?" Kael asked instead. "It crawled out of a wall. Spoke like it knew things it shouldn't."
Gallagher studied him for a long moment. Whatever he saw, it was enough. "Abyssal construct. Middle-tier possession. Someone's puppet, not an independent entity." He crouched beside the creature's ash pile, touching the scorched stones. "The smell is wrong. Standard possession leaves sulfur and decay. This..." He stood, expression grim. "This smells like the Void.
"We've had three such incidents in the capital this month. All targeting Awakened. All mentioning a Prime." His eyes returned to Kael. "You just became interesting. The Council will want to speak with you."
"I'm not interested in speaking with councils."
"Then you'll be interested in knowing that cultists are hunting Soul Reavers." Gallagher delivered the words flatly, without inflection. "The Whisper King wants you captured alive. The extraction process is supposed to be unpleasant."
Kael kept his face neutral. The name struck like a physical blow—a chill traced down his spine, his hands curling at his sides. The Whisper King. He'd heard whispers in the Veiled Market, fragments of legend, but nothing concrete. Nothing useful.
"And you're telling me this because?"
"Because you're clearly already marked." Gallagher gestured to the scorched patch where the construct had died. "They sent a mid-tier to capture a stage-one Awakened. That's not standard protocol. Someone knows what you can do, and they want it badly enough to spend serious resources."
Soldiers entered the alley, six of them, wearing the interlocking steel plates of the Iron Vanguard. They secured the perimeter with practiced efficiency, taking positions that blocked both exits without seeming threatening. Not a threat, but not freedom either.
"Commander," one of them called out. "The bodies check out. Local syndicate runners, registered to the Copper Coast gang. Nothing on our watchlist."
"Then they were hired." Gallagher accepted a report slate, scanning it with quick jerks of his eyes. "Check the construct's ash. I want spiritual signature analysis run. If there's a tether back to its source, I want to know."
Kael watched the Commander work. The man moved with economy, no wasted motion, every gesture purposeful. He'd dealt with Awakened before—his assessment had been too accurate for coincidence. But he hadn't drawn his weapon, hadn't tried to restrain Kael, hadn't shown the hostility that usually accompanied discovery of what Kael was.
"You're not afraid of me," Kael said.
Gallagher looked up from his slate. "Should I be?"
"I've killed seventeen people in the past month. Harvested their skills, their memories. I'm not a good man."
"No." Gallagher's agreement was immediate. "You're probably not. But I've known plenty of good men who died stupid, and plenty of monsters who kept their word when it mattered." He handed the slate to a subordinate. "The question isn't whether I should fear you. It's whether you're worth the trouble of saving."
"Why would you save me?"
"Because I hate cultists." The words carried old pain, carefully buried. "Because the Whisper King's plans usually involve a lot of dead people. And because—" He paused, choosing his words. "Because I've seen what happens when Soul Reavers are pushed into corners with nothing to lose. The Orders created the problem by treating Reavers like weapons instead of people. I'm not interested in repeating their mistakes."
Kael's hand drifted toward his chest, where the Soul Reaver sigil burned with cold fire. The ability that had marked him since execution, since whatever force had pulled him back from death's door with a fragment of power that shouldn't exist.
The notification flickered unbidden across his vision. Not from the harvest—that required a corpse—but from proximity to fresh death. Gallagher's purifying weapon had left nothing to touch.
"You want something," Kael said. "Nobody offers protection without cost."
"Smart." Gallagher gestured toward the street. "Walk with me. My men will secure this alley and meet us at the garrison. What I have to say isn't for open air."
The Iron Vanguard soldiers parted as Gallagher moved toward them. Kael followed, staying half a step behind, positioning himself to watch the Commander's back while keeping his own exit routes clear. Old habits, or maybe the instincts of someone who'd survived this long by trusting no one.
Thornwick's streets churned with evening traffic—steam-carts hissing past, pedestrians hurrying beneath oiled umbrellas, street vendors calling their wares over the din. Normal life, oblivious to the Abyssal presence that had crawled through their city's walls.
"The Six Orders," Gallagher began without preamble, "were founded to maintain the seal network. Seventy-two rifts between our world and the Abyss, each containing an Entity powerful enough to end civilization if it broke free. The seals hold. They've held for three hundred years. But they're weakening."
"I know about the rifts." Kael kept his tone neutral. The information wasn't new—he'd pieced together fragments from his harvests—but confirming knowledge might earn him more in return.
"Then you know the seals require maintenance. Spiritual energy, fed monthly. Coordination between Orders. And absolute secrecy." Gallagher steered them down a broader avenue, toward a fortress-like structure that dominated the district's skyline. "What you might not know is that the cultists aren't trying to destroy the seals."
"They want to control them."
"Now you're thinking like a commander." Gallagher's approval was grudging. "The Whisper King doesn't want to end the world. He wants to rule what's left of it. The Entities in those rifts, if released, would tear everything apart. But if properly bound, properly enslaved..." He shook his head. "Imagine seventy-two Abyssal Gods answering to a single mortal will. That's his goal. That's what he's been building toward for decades."
They reached the fortress gates. Guards saluted Gallagher as he passed, their eyes lingering on Kael with undisguised curiosity. The Commander led them through a courtyard cluttered with equipment—crossbow racks, armor stands, crates stamped with military seals—and into a stone-walled briefing room.
Maps covered every surface. Some showed Thornwick's districts. Others displayed something Kael didn't recognize—geometric patterns that pulsed with faint light, converging on points scattered across a continental map.
"The seal network." Gallagher traced the patterns with one calloused finger. "Each convergence point is a rift. The cultists have been targeting them individually, testing defenses, probing weaknesses. Three rifts have shown accelerated degradation in the past month. At this rate..."
Kael examined the map. The degraded points glowed with sickly luminescence, their geometric shapes slightly distorted. "How long?"
"If they're not stopped? Six months. Maybe less." Gallagher pulled a vial from his belt—soul-essence, high-grade, worth a small fortune. He set it on the table between them. "That's the cost of maintaining one seal for a year. The Orders spend millions in resources every month just to hold the network together. The cultists are accelerating deterioration while simultaneously destabilizing the political structures that fund the maintenance."
"Why tell me this?"
"Because you're already involved." Gallagher's scarred face hardened. "The construct that attacked you tonight—it knew what you are. Not suspected. Knew. That means information has leaked somewhere. Someone in the Orders, or close to them, has been watching Soul Reavers and reporting to the cultists."
The implication settled heavily in Kael's stomach. A traitor. Someone with access to secrets about Reavers, feeding information to the Whisper King.
"You suspect someone."
"Suspect everyone." Gallagher tapped the vial. "Soul-essence. Currency of the Awakened. This is a month's salary for a mid-tier Iron Vanguard officer. The cultists have been flooding the Veiled Market with it for the past year. Someone's funding their operations, and that someone has access to resources that shouldn't exist outside official channels."
Kael's mind raced through implications. The Mortician's Guild controlled legal soul-essence harvesting. The Orders purchased through intermediaries. A leak could exist anywhere in that chain.
"You think a Reaver is feeding them information."
"I think Reavers are valuable enough to have been noticed by someone with long-term plans." Gallagher met his gaze directly. "The Whisper King is old. Decades old, possibly centuries. He predates the current Order structure. If he was once something else, something that knew what Soul Reavers could become..." He let the sentence hang.
Kael thought of the fragments he'd harvested. The memories that didn't belong to him, skills absorbed from the dead. Were there others like him? Had there been Reavers in the past, and if so, what had happened to them?
"The cultists hunted me specifically tonight," he said slowly. "That construct wasn't random."
"No. It was a message." Gallagher's expression darkened. "The Whisper King knows about you. He's watching. And he's moving faster than we anticipated."
A knock interrupted them. A young officer entered, saluted, and handed Gallagher a folded report. The Commander scanned it, his jaw tightening.
"My analyst found something in the construct's ash." Gallagher held up the report. "Residual spiritual signature. It's not matching anything in our databases, but it matches fragments recovered from three other sites. The Abyssal constructs are connected. They're drawing power from a single source."
"A rift?"
"Worse." Gallagher's voice dropped. "A repository. Somewhere the cultists have been collecting soul-essence, building reserves for whatever they're planning. If we can find it, destroy it, we buy time to track down the leak."
He slid the report across the table. Kael examined it—coordinates, spiritual frequency readings, a rough map of the city's underbelly. The Hollows, from what he could tell. The abandoned mining district that had collapsed two centuries ago, leaving tunnels beneath Thornwick that most people pretended didn't exist.
"You're asking me to go in there."
"I'm offering you a chance to do something useful." Gallagher retrieved the report. "Your ability to harvest from the dead—it's the only way we'll learn what the construct knew before it died. The ash still holds traces. Weak traces, but traces."
Kael's hands clenched. The ability. Always the ability. The thing that made him valuable, dangerous, and utterly alone.
"The ash is cold. Hours old."
"Then you'd need fresher sources." Gallagher moved to another map, this one showing tunnel networks beneath the city. "My scouts reported cultist activity in the lower Hollows two nights ago. We've tracked them since then. If you want answers about who's hunting you and why, the source is down there."
"Answers aren't free."
"No. They're not." Gallagher's scarred face showed no emotion. "The cultists will be performing a ritual at midnight. Based on our intelligence, they're trying to open a temporary rift—small, controlled, just enough to funnel energy to their repository. If we interrupt the ritual, we'll capture cultists. Living cultists with recent knowledge."
"And you'll harvest them."
"I will offer them the opportunity to surrender and face Order justice." The Commander's tone made clear how much he expected that offer to be accepted. "What happens after that isn't my concern. But their memories, their training, their knowledge of the Whisper King's plans—those become yours through whatever means you deem appropriate."
Kael stared at the map. The tunnels wound beneath Thornwick like arteries beneath skin, connecting places that shouldn't be connected. He'd avoided the Hollows since arriving in the city. Too many unknowns, too many places for enemies to hide.
But enemies were already hunting him. The construct's words echoed through his mind: *The Prime hungers for your kind. The Whisper King remembers what you are.*
He needed to know what he was. What he could become. The only way to learn was to take the knowledge from those who already knew.
"Transport to the Hollows," he said. "Extraction point if things go wrong. And immunity from Order prosecution for tonight's activities."
"Granted." No hesitation. "My scouts will guide you to the ritual site. The signal for extraction is two short whistles. If you're not out by dawn, we assume you're dead and move on."
"Generous."
"I don't have the luxury of generosity. I have the luxury of pragmatism." Gallagher extended his hand—a soldier's grip, firm and brief. "Midnight. The ritual site is marked on this map. Don't be late."
Kael took the offered hand, feeling calluses scrape against his palm. The deal was struck. He would descend into the Hollows, witness whatever ritual the cultists were performing, and harvest what knowledge he could from the dead they left behind.
It wasn't a choice, really. Not when the alternative was waiting for the next construct to crawl through a wall and try to capture him. He would face his enemies on his terms than theirs.
The Commander led him to a side chamber where weapons and equipment waited on long tables. "Take what you need. Light armor, if you can wear it. The Hollows are cold and the cultists won't be the only things down there."
Kael selected quickly—a coat lined with spiritual-resistant fabric, a set of knives that felt balanced in his hand, a pack carrying alchemical lights and emergency rations. Nothing he couldn't afford to lose if he had to run.
"One more thing." Gallagher caught his arm as he turned to leave. "The cultists who'll be at that ritual—they're not random zealots. Prime Zarek has been recruiting from the Orders themselves. Former acolytes, disgraced warriors, people who know our methods and our weaknesses. If you encounter anyone wearing Order insignia, don't assume they're friendly."
Zarek. The name struck Kael like a physical blow. He'd been hearing whispers about Zarek since his awakening—a powerful figure in the cultist hierarchy, someone who orchestrated hunts for Reavers. The frame job that had sent him to execution in the first place, all those months ago, bore the hallmarks of Zarek's operations.
"Zarek," Kael heard himself say. "You've dealt with him before?"
The Commander's expression flickered. Something old and painful crossed his scarred features before being buried behind professional neutrality.
"Everyone who's dealt with Zarek has paid for it. The man doesn't exist—not officially. No records, no history, no photographs. He appears when it suits him and vanishes when it doesn't. The Orders have been hunting him for twenty years." Gallagher's voice hardened. "Whatever you're thinking about him, assume it's worse."
Kael filed the information away. Another thread in a tapestry that grew more complex with every passing day. Zarek, the Whisper King, the Soul Reaver origin—all of it connected, all of it leading somewhere he couldn't yet see.
He left the fortress as the city's gaslamps flickered to life, casting orange pools across rain-slicked streets. The Hollows waited beneath him, hungry and dark. Midnight approached.
The journey down took longer than expected. Gallagher's scouts guided him through abandoned maintenance shafts, collapsed mining tunnels, and passages that hadn't seen foot traffic in generations. The air grew colder with each descent, carrying the mineral tang of deep stone and something else—something that tasted like ozone and fear.
"This is as far as we go." The lead scout, a wiry woman with night-sight augments glinting in her pupils, pointed toward a fissure in the rock ahead. "Ritual chamber's through there. We counted twelve cultists on entry, plus whatever they're summoning. Your extraction point is back the way we came, two hundred meters to the staging cavern. Don't miss dawn."
"I won't." Kael checked his equipment one final time—knives secure, alchemical lights ready, the cold weight of the Soul Reaver ability burning steady beneath his sternum.
The scouts vanished into shadows. Kael was alone.
He approached the fissure cautiously, moving without sound, his borrowed Watercast abilities enhancing his control over his own body. Through the gap, he glimpsed the ritual chamber—a natural cavern expanded by carving, its walls inscribed with symbols that pulsed with sickly light.
Twelve hooded figures stood in a circle around a pit filled with churning shadow. At the circle's center, a man in ornate robes gestured while chanting words that made Kael's ears ache. The pit's darkness seemed to breathe, expanding and contracting like a massive lung.
One of the hooded cultists turned slightly, and Kael glimpsed something beneath their robes—a glint of metal, a symbol etched into armor. The symbol of the Order of Dawn.
They had sent him to kill their own.
No. Not sent. Gallagher had offered him a choice. What Kael chose to do with that choice was his own responsibility.
He watched the ritual continue, counting figures, noting positions, memorizing the layout. The chanting grew more intense. The pit's shadow deepened, and something began to coalesce within its depths—a shape, a presence, something that shouldn't exist in the material world.
The ritual was almost complete.
Kael drew his knives and moved.
The first cultist died without knowing he was under attack. Kael's blade found the gap between hood and collar, severing spine and carotid artery in one swift motion. The body crumpled. Kael caught it, lowering it silently, his fingers pressing against cooling flesh.
The Soul Reaver ability activated on contact.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 0.3 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Memory fragment acquired: Ritual structure, binding invocation, the Pit of Whispers.]
Information flooded through Kael's mind—fragmented, incomplete, but valuable. The ritual was a summoning. Not a rift opening, but a summoning—a call for something to answer from the other side. The pit served as a focus, channeling power from whatever Entity it was connected to.
Two cultists noticed their companion's absence. They turned, hands rising to sound alarm, and Kael was already among them.
His knives spoke twice more. Blood sprayed across ancient stone. Two more bodies dropped, and Kael harvested again, pulling knowledge from neural tissue that hadn't yet stopped firing.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 0.2 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Memory fragment acquired: Guard rotation, tunnel access codes, the Prime's location.]
The Prime. Information about Prime Zarek's whereabouts burned through Kael's consciousness. The cultists knew where he operated from. Knew how to reach him.
He filed the knowledge away, attention returning to the ritual.
The remaining cultists had noticed something was wrong. The chanting faltered. The robed figure at the circle's center spun, eyes blazing with spiritual power.
"Intruder!" His voice boomed through the chamber. "The ritual must complete! Kill them!"
Nine cultists broke from their positions, drawing weapons that gleamed with corrupted spiritual energy. Kael counted them, tracked their movements, analyzed their formations. Former Order members, just as Gallagher had warned. They moved with military precision, trying to flank him, to cut off escape routes.
They were competent. Professional.
They weren't prepared for what Kael had become.
He met the first wave with violence. A cultist lunged with a spear blessed by Order magic—Kael sidestepped, drove his knife through the man's wrist, wrenched the weapon free, and reversed it into the cultist's throat. Another harvest, another flood of information.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 0.4 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Combat memory acquired: Order of Dawn defensive stance, weakness in left knee joint.]
The knowledge translated immediately into action. Kael targeted the next cultist's left leg, driving his blade through the joint where armor plating failed to protect. The man fell, screaming. Kael stamped on his throat and moved on.
The chamber became a killing floor. Blood painted ancient stone in abstract patterns. Bodies fell in Kael's wake, each one a source of power, each harvest making him faster, stronger, more precise. He absorbed combat techniques from trained warriors, tactical insights from former officers, and something darker from those who had spent too long serving entities from beyond the Veil.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 0.5 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Combat memory acquired: dual-wielding technique, spiritual blade enhancement.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 0.3 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Memory fragment acquired: Order of Dawn infiltration protocols, Council security rotations.]
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 0.4 units of spiritual essence from the remains. Combat memory acquired: grappling technique, joint lock application.]
The ritual pit pulsed with increased intensity. Whatever was being summoned grew stronger, its presence pressing against Kael's awareness like a physical weight. The robed figure at the center screamed commands, urging his remaining followers to complete the summoning.
Three cultists remained between Kael and the pit. They had abandoned any pretense of capture, fighting now only for survival. One threw an alchemical charge that exploded against Kael's coat, setting the spiritual-resistant fabric smoldering. Another lunged with a blade coated in something that hissed when it touched stone.
Kael endured the burning, closed the distance, and dealt death with mechanical efficiency. His harvested abilities blended together—watercast flexibility, enhanced reflexes, combat memories that guided his blade like a second mind. The three cultists fell in seconds.
The robed figure ran.
Kael let him go. The pit demanded attention.
He approached the churning shadow, feeling its cold breath against his face. The summoning was nearly complete—a shape pressed against the boundary between worlds, trying to force its way through. If it emerged, Kael would face something far worse than cultists.
He reached into the pit.
The cold hit him like a physical blow, burning through his arm, his chest, his soul. The presence within the darkness recoiled from his touch, sensing something it recognized, something that threatened it.
"NOT YET." The voice came from everywhere and nowhere, vibrating through Kael's bones. "YOU ARE NOT READY. THE RITE IS INCOMPLETE."
Kael's fingers closed around something solid—a tendril of shadow, physical despite its nature, a tether connecting this realm to whatever Entity the cultists had tried to summon. The Soul Reaver ability surged, pulling, drawing power from the contact.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 1.2 units of spiritual essence from the tether. Memory fragment acquired: The seal at Rift Seventeen is weakest. The Prime knows. The Prime waits.]
The tether shattered. The pit's darkness dissolved, leaving only empty stone. Whatever had tried to manifest had been forced back, at least temporarily. The ritual had failed.
Kael collapsed against the chamber wall, breathing hard, his arm numb from shoulder to fingertips. The harvest had cost him—not spiritually, since the ability drew from elsewhere, but physically. His body wasn't designed to touch the Abyss directly.
Footsteps echoed from the tunnel. Not the scouts—they would have whistled. Not the cultists—they were dead. Kael forced himself upright, knife finding his hand through muscle memory alone.
Commander Gallagher emerged from the fissure, three soldiers at his back. They took in the scene with professional detachment—the bodies, the blood, the scorch marks, the empty pit.
"You survived." Gallagher's tone was unreadable. "And the ritual?"
"Failed. Something tried to come through. I stopped it."
"With what?" One of the soldiers pointed at Kael's arm, where the skin had turned grey-black from cold burns. "That's not standard Reaver damage."
Kael looked at his arm. The flesh was dead from elbow to wrist, tissue destroyed by contact with whatever dwelled in the pit. It would need treatment, or amputation. Either way, a problem for later.
"The pit was connected to something. Something old." He met Gallagher's eyes. "It spoke to me. Told me I wasn't ready for whatever it's planning."
"The seal at Rift Seventeen." Gallagher's face hardened. "We suspected that location was compromised, but we couldn't confirm it. You've just given us actionable intelligence."
"Information about the Prime," Kael continued. "Where he's operating from. Guard rotations at his compound. Access codes." He paused. "And something else. The thing in the pit—the Entity—it called me 'not ready.' It knows what I am."
Gallagher's expression flickered. For a moment, something like sympathy crossed his scarred features.
"The Whisper King has been searching for a Soul Reaver for decades. The fact that this thing recognized what you are means the connection goes deeper than simple hunting." He gestured to his soldiers. "Help him up. We need to extract before any reinforcements arrive."
Kael accepted the offered hands, letting them pull him to his feet. His numb arm hung useless at his side, but his mind churned with the information he'd harvested. The cultists had been trying to summon something to strengthen their repository. The repository itself held massive reserves of soul-essence, stolen or harvested from sources Kael couldn't yet identify. And Prime Zarek operated from a known location—one Kael now possessed the means to reach.
The extraction took twenty minutes. The scouts guided them through alternative tunnels, avoiding the main routes they'd used on entry. By the time they reached the surface, dawn painted Thornwick's smog-filtered sky with shades of grey and amber.
Gallagher debriefed him in the fortress's medical ward, where order-certified healers tended to his arm with practiced efficiency. The dead flesh would need to be removed and regrown—a painful process that would take weeks. Weeks of vulnerability, weeks of limited mobility.
"The information you extracted," Gallagher said once the healers had finished their initial work. "It's valuable. More valuable than I expected. The Council will want to compensate you."
"Compensation for what? Information I gathered to keep myself alive?"
"Consider it an investment." The Commander settled into a chair across from Kael's bed. "The cultists failed tonight. Their repository remains intact, and they'll know we disrupted their operations. They'll adapt, change tactics, become harder to track. But we've gained ground. We know about Rift Seventeen, about the Prime's location, about their summoning capabilities. That's more than we had yesterday."
Kael flexed his healing fingers, feeling the ache of regeneration, the pull of new tissue knitting itself together. "You mentioned the Whisper King. That he's old. That he predates the current Order structure."
"Yes."
"What do you know about him?"
Gallagher was silent for a long moment. When he spoke, his voice carried the weight of carefully guarded secrets.
"Legend says the first Soul Reaver was executed two hundred years ago. The Orders hunted him down, killed him, scattered his remains across the continent to prevent anyone from replicating his abilities." The Commander's eyes met Kael's. "But legends are written by victors. And the Whisper King appeared shortly after those events. Coincidence, maybe. Or maybe something else."
Kael's blood chilled. The first Soul Reaver. The origin of his power. Something the Orders had supposedly destroyed, except that Kael stood here, alive, carrying abilities that shouldn't exist.
"You're saying the Whisper King might be the original Reaver."
"I'm saying the timing is suspicious." Gallagher rose, moving toward the door. "The cultists have been hunting Reavers for decades, trying to recreate or control whatever the original power was. Now they've found you. Whatever happens next, you're at the center of something that's been building for two centuries."
He paused at the threshold, looking back.
"Rest. Heal. The information you gathered will take time to analyze, and we'll need your input when we plan the assault on the Prime's compound." His scarred face softened slightly. "You've done good work tonight. Try not to die before you can do more."
The door closed behind him. Kael lay in the medical bed, staring at stone ceiling, his newly-regenerated arm throbbing with healing fire. The weight of everything pressed down on him—the cultists hunting him, the Entities stirring in their prisons, the Whisper King's ancient plans finally reaching some kind of climax.
And somewhere in all of it, the truth about his own origin waited to be discovered.
Sleep came eventually, fitful and filled with dreams of shadow-pits and voices calling from beyond the Veil. When Kael woke, the sun had set and risen again, and a new day had begun.
