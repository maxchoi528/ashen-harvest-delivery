The cultist's body hit the cobblestones with a wet thud. Kael wiped black ichor from his blade and crouched beside the corpse, fingers pressing against cold flesh.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted Essence Threading (Fragment) from the remains. Perception +0.3 acquired."
The notification pulsed green at the edge of his vision. Useful, but the fragment was degraded—hours old, maybe more. The cultists had been dying before he found them, torn apart by something far less surgical than his blade.
Three bodies lay scattered across the alley's narrow mouth. Steam hissed from a ruptured pipe, mixing with the copper tang of blood and something else—ozone, like the air before a lightning strike. Thornwick's industrial heart beat, engines thundering somewhere in the distance, but here the world held its breath.
Kael straightened. His Divine senses spread outward like oil on water, tasting the spiritual residue clinging to every surface. The cultists hadn't come alone. Something larger had passed through here, dragging currents of Abyssal energy in its wake.
"The Order of Dawn will want to know about this."
The voice came from the rooftop above. Kael's blade rose before the words finished leaving his mouth.
A figure dropped from the shadows, landing in a crouch ten feet away. Gray cloak, silver trim—Order of Dawn. Her face remained hidden beneath a deep hood, but the spiritual pressure rolling off her form announced her rank. Commander tier, at minimum.
"You've been following me." Kael didn't phrase it as a question.
"Following implies subtlety." She stepped forward, hands visible at her sides. "I've been observing. There's a difference."
"Observing me kill cultists?"
"Observing you harvest them." The word landed like a slap. "The Shadow Court has taken notice of your activities, Soul Reaver. So have we."
Kael's grip tightened on his weapon. The Order of Dawn hunted his kind—everyone knew that. But she hadn't attacked. Hadn't summoned backup. She stood there, patient as stone, waiting for him to make the first move.
"What do you want?"
"Information. There's a rift opening in the Ashwick catacombs beneath the old cathedral. The seals are failing faster than we can repair them. We need—"
Another body slammed into the alley entrance. Kael spun, blade ready, but the corpse belonged to a cultist—throat torn open, chest caved in by something with tremendous force. Fresh. Still bleeding.
The woman from the Order of Dawn moved past him, crouching beside the new corpse. Her hood fell back, revealing sharp features and silver-streaked hair. Older than he'd first assumed. Eyes like winter ice.
"Your hunter made a mistake." She examined the wounds without touching them. "They assumed the prey would run. Instead, it circled back."
"Entity," Kael said. Not a question.
"Greater-class. One of the 72." She stood. "The seals won't hold another week. When they break, the entity escapes. When it escapes, Thornwick dies."
"And you want me to seal it."
"I want you to understand what you're facing." She turned to face him fully. "Soul Reavers are not invincible. I've buried three who thought they were. The entity in those catacombs has consumed Awakened beyond your stage. Beyond mine. Beyond anyone still breathing."
The woman's hand moved to her chest, pressing against something beneath her cloak. A scar, or maybe something else. Her expression remained controlled, but Kael caught the flicker of something raw beneath.
"I've seen what happens when a Greater-class entity fully manifests. Entire city blocks, gone. Not destroyed—unmade. Reality itself folds where they walk." She stepped closer. "You have power, Soul Reaver. Genuine power. But the 72 are not monsters to be slashed. They are forces of nature given will. The only way to stop them is through the seal network."
"The seals are failing."
"Yes." Her jaw tightened. "Which brings me to why I'm here. The Order of Dawn maintains the seals through conventional means—rituals, blood offerings, spiritual energy expenditure. It costs us dozens of Awakened every year. But you're different. Your ability harvests energy without cost. Without depletion."
Kael understood. The Law of Soul Conservation stated that spiritual power couldn't be created—only transferred. Every seal required massive energy input to maintain. The Orders spent centuries building their network, burning through generations of Awakened to keep the Abyss contained.
But Soul Reavers could harvest energy from anywhere. From anything. The rules that bound every other Awakened didn't apply to him.
"You want me to harvest an Abyssal Entity."
"I want you to understand what that would require." The woman reached into her cloak and produced a small vial. Inside, something dark writhed against the glass. "This is crystallized Entity essence. We extracted it from a weakened entity during the Third Incursion. One drop contains more spiritual energy than a hundred Awakened can produce in a lifetime."
She held it out to him.
"Take it. Study it. You'll find that Entity energy operates differently than human or animal essence. It's older. Wilder. When you harvest from something that came from the Abyss, you're not just taking power. You're pulling against the Abyss itself. The entity will know. It will fight back. And if you're not prepared—"
The vial exploded.
Kael threw himself sideways as darkness erupted from the broken glass, a wave of freezing spiritual pressure that sent him tumbling across the cobblestones. The woman staggered, her cloak catching fire from some invisible source.
From the alley's far end, something emerged.
It had no fixed shape—more absence than presence, a wound in reality where something had been carved away. Kael's Divine senses screamed warnings as tendrils of pure Abyssal energy lashed out, each one carrying the weight of centuries spent trapped beyond the Veil.
"SHADOW COURT!" the woman shouted, drawing a blade that blazed with golden light. "We have a host! I repeat, Shadow Court contact in Ashwick district!"
The entity laughed. The sound existed somewhere between Kael's ears, bypassing his ears entirely—a whisper that spoke of endings, of silence, of the moment when all struggle ceases and only void remains.
The creature they'd been hunting had found them first.
Kael rolled to his feet, Soul Reaver blade singing through the air. The entity's formless body split around his strike, reforming seconds later without a scratch. Behind him, the Dawn Commander engaged, her illuminated weapon carving patterns in the darkness, but each wound sealed itself moments after appearing.
"You cannot kill me with steel," the entity said. Its voice carried no malice, only a terrible patience. "I existed before your species learned to walk upright. I will exist long after your civilization crumbles to dust."
"Then why talk?" Kael circled left, keeping the creature in front of him. "If you're so eternal, why bother with words?"
"Because I want you to understand what's coming." The entity's formless mass contracted, gathering into something vaguely humanoid. "The seals are failing. All of them. Not because of age, not because of weakness, but because the one who built them is letting them fail."
The Dawn Commander's blade stopped mid-swing. "You're lying."
"Am I?" The entity turned what might have been its face toward her. "Ask yourself why the Whisper King has spent two centuries positioning his pieces. Ask yourself why the Soul Reaver was created. Ask yourself why you, specifically, were allowed to survive this long."
Kael's blood ran cold.
The Soul Reaver. His power. The ability that had branded him a monster, that had made him a fugitive, that had shaped every moment of his existence since waking on the executioner's table. No one knew where it came from. No one understood how it worked. He'd always assumed it was a curse—something broken, something wrong.
But what if it was intentional?
"The First Reaver was a seal," the entity said, reading his thoughts or maybe his expression. "A failsafe built into the network itself. When the seals finally fail, when the 72 walk freely through your world, only the Reaver can contain us. Only the Reaver can absorb what we're willing to release. But here's the truth you've never been told—"
The Dawn Commander moved. Her blade swept toward the entity's center mass, golden light blazing with concentrated spiritual energy. The entity didn't dodge. It absorbed the strike, the blade sinking into its formless body and vanishing entirely.
The woman's eyes went wide. She tried to pull back, but her weapon was gone, consumed, and the entity's mass was already climbing her arm.
"KAEL!"
He moved without thinking. Soul Reaver blade extended, he charged directly into the entity's form, feeling the cold burn where Abyssal energy touched his skin. His free hand grabbed the Dawn Commander's wrist and pulled.
Power erupted from him—not spiritual energy but something stranger, something that tasted like endings and smelled like graves. The Soul Reaver drank. The entity screamed.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted Entity Fragment (Lesser) from the remains. Abyssal Resistance +5% acquired."
The entity recoiled, its formless body losing cohesion as Kael's touch drained energy from its very essence. The sensation was unlike anything he'd experienced before—human souls felt like water, animal souls like smoke, but this thing was solid, dense, hungry. It fought back with every byte stolen, clawing at his consciousness with borrowed memories of worlds before humanity.
"IMPOSSIBLE!" The entity's voice shattered windows in the surrounding buildings. "You're not strong enough! You're not—"
"I'm Divine," Kael said. "And you're just an appetizer."
He pushed harder. The Soul Reaver responded to his will, channeling power he'd never known he possessed. The entity's mass began to dissolve, its stolen form collapsing inward as energy poured out faster than it could regenerate.
But it wasn't dying. Kael realized this with growing horror. He wasn't killing it—he was feeding it. Each harvest made the entity stronger, filling gaps in its form with stolen essence from his own reserves.
The Dawn Commander's hand found his shoulder. "Release it. Now. Before—"
Too late.
The entity erupted outward in a sphere of pure Abyssal force. Kael flew backward, spine cracking against a brick wall. Pain exploded through his body, but his Divine resilience kept him conscious, kept him breathing.
The creature loomed over him, massive now, its formless body filling the entire alley. The shadows it cast moved independently, reaching toward Kael with hungry intent.
"Divine," it said, almost admiringly. "Yes. This will do. This will do nicely."
It descended.
Kael brought his blade up, but the entity's mass flowed around it, consuming his weapon, consuming his arm, consuming everything. Cold filled him, not physical cold but existential—the temperature of spaces between stars, of voids between realities.
The Last Moment.
He understood now. This was how it ended. Not in glory or vengeance or the completion of some grand purpose, but here, in an alley behind a broken pipe, his body serving as the gateway for something ancient and terrible to enter the world.
The Dawn Commander screamed something. The entity laughed. And Kael—
Kael reached.
Not with his hands. Not with his blade. He reached with the Soul Reaver itself, that strange gift that had defined his existence, and he touched the thing consuming him.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted MEMORY FRAGMENT from the Entity. WARNING: Contents classified. WARNING: Reality distortion detected. WARNING: Proceed with caution."
Information flooded his mind. Not much—the entity's resistance was tremendous, and each fragment cost more than it gave—but enough. Enough to understand.
The seals weren't failing randomly. They were being deactivated from within. The Whisper King had spent centuries placing agents in every Order, in every seal maintenance team, in positions of power that could touch the network without raising suspicion.
And the Soul Reaver—the power that made him special—wasn't a weapon against the entities.
It was a key.
When the seals finally fell, when the 72 emerged and the world burned, the Soul Reaver would absorb the shock. Would contain the initial surge. Would give humanity a single moment to adapt before the next wave arrived.
He was never meant to win. He was meant to delay.
The entity sensed his realization and laughed again, its formless mass surging with renewed hunger. "Now you understand! The Whisper King created your kind to be vessels, to be sacrifices! Every Soul Reaver who has ever lived was bred for this moment! When I consume you, when I take your power, I become complete! I become—"
The alley exploded in light.
Golden fire rained from the sky, each drop carrying the concentrated will of dozens of Awakened. The Dawn Commander stood at the alley's entrance, blood streaming from her nose, both hands raised as she channeled every ounce of spiritual energy she possessed.
"RETREAT!" she screamed. "ALL UNITS RETREAT TO SECONDARY POSITION!"
The entity roared as the golden flames carved into its form, forcing it to release Kael, forcing it to reconstitute against an assault it hadn't anticipated. The window was seconds, maybe less.
Kael rolled away from the creature's grasping tendrils, his Soul Reaver blade reforming in his hand. His left arm hung useless—consumed by the entity, maybe, or overwhelmed—but the rest of him still functioned.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted Entity Fragment (Critical) from the remains. WARNING: Harvest interrupted. WARNING: Quality degraded. WARNING: Partial acquisition only."
The notification flickered, corrupted, dissolved. Something had gone wrong. Something had been lost.
The entity's formless mass contracted, gathering itself for one final assault. The Dawn Commander's golden flames were fading, her reserves nearly exhausted. In seconds, the creature would be free, would be complete, would consume them both and whatever else stood in its path.
Kael could run. The option existed, clear and tempting. He could use his Divine speed to escape, to put distance between himself and the monster, to survive another day while Thornwick burned.
But running meant leaving the Dawn Commander to die. Running meant abandoning the city to something that had already tasted his power and wanted more. Running meant accepting the role the Whisper King had designed for him—sacrifice, delay, eventual consumption.
He was done being a piece on someone else's board.
Kael charged.
The entity's formless mass crashed against him like a wave of frozen needles, each point of contact sending agony screaming through his nervous system. His Divine resilience buckled, cracked, held. His Soul Reaver blade carved channels through the creature's essence, each stroke drawing power that healed wounds, restored energy, pushed him forward.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted ENTITY CORE FRAGMENT from the remains. Unique acquisition detected. New ability forming..."
The entity screamed. Its form destabilized, fragments breaking away and dissolving into the air. Kael's touch burned with cold fire, a paradox that should have destroyed him but instead transformed him, each harvest changing something fundamental about his relationship with the creature.
"You're... You're taking too much!" The entity's voice lost its arrogance, replaced by something like fear. "STOP! I'll give you anything! Knowledge! Power! I'll tell you where the others are hiding! Just stop!"
Kael didn't stop.
His hand pressed against the creature's core—that dense knot of pure Abyssal energy at its center—and he pulled. The Soul Reaver sang in his blood, a song of endings that he was only now beginning to understand.
This was what he was made for. Not sacrifice. Not delay.
Absorption.
"Soul Harvest: You extracted GREATER ENTITY ESSENCE from the remains. Soul Resonance capacity expanded. WARNING: New ability unstable. WARNING: Entity consciousness fragments detected. WARNING: Mental integrity at risk."
The entity collapsed.
Its formless mass fell away like ash, dissipating into the steam and shadows of the alley until nothing remained but a patch of scorched cobblestones and the lingering smell of ozone. Kael stood over the space where the creature had been, his body trembling from exertion and something deeper.
The Dawn Commander approached slowly, her golden flames extinguished, her face pale with exhaustion. She looked at the scorched ground, then at Kael, then at her own hands as if seeing them for the first time.
"You killed it," she said. "You killed a Greater-class entity."
"I absorbed it." Kael flexed his left hand experimentally. The limb was returning, slowly—spiritual energy reconstituting what the entity had consumed. "It's not dead. It's part of me now."
"That should be impossible."
"So is everything else about me."
The woman studied him for a long moment. Whatever she saw made her nod slowly, accepting something she couldn't articulate.
"My name is Commander Elara Voss. Order of Dawn, Third Seal Division." She extended her hand. "I think we need to talk."
Kael took her hand. Her grip was firm, steady despite everything they'd just survived.
"Talk about what?"
"The Whisper King. The seals. The 72." Elara's eyes hardened with determination. "And how we're going to stop his plan before everything burns."
They walked together toward the alley's exit, leaving the scorched cobblestones behind. Behind them, steam hissed from broken pipes, and somewhere in the distance, engines roared their endless mechanical song. Thornwick continued its rhythm, unaware of how close it had come to ending.
But ahead of them, in the shadows of buildings and the spaces between streetlamps, Kael felt other presences watching. Waiting.
The Shadow Court had taken notice.
The 72 were stirring.
And somewhere in the depths of his newly expanded soul, the absorbed entity's consciousness whispered secrets he wasn't ready to hear.
One word repeated like a heartbeat, like a promise, like a threat:
"Soon."
Kael's spine stiffened. The entity was still aware. Still planning. Still—
"Problem?" Elara asked.
"New development." Kael's hand found his blade's hilt. "The thing I absorbed. It left something behind."
"What kind of something?"
"The kind that knows where all the other seals are." Kael turned to face the street ahead, where shadows gathered and reformed in patterns too deliberate to be natural. "The kind that wants us to find them."
Elara's hand moved to her empty scabbard—her weapon still lost inside the entity she'd destroyed. "Can you control it?"
"I don't know." Kael stepped forward, each footfall bringing him closer to the gathered darkness. "But I'm going to find out."
The shadows parted. A figure emerged—cloaked in black, face hidden beneath a mask that showed no features, only the suggestion of eyes watching from somewhere far away.
"Kael Ashborn." The voice carried no malice, no threat. Only acknowledgment. "The Whisper King has been waiting for you."
Behind Kael, Elara drew a knife from somewhere—improvised, desperate, completely inadequate for what they faced. Ahead, the masked figure produced a small object from within its cloak: a vial filled with something that glowed faintly silver.
"A gift," the figure said. "The last piece you'll need to understand your purpose. The Whisper King sends his regards."
The vial landed at Kael's feet. The masked figure dissolved into shadow, leaving only the glowing container and the weight of unanswered questions.
Elara stared at the vial. "What is it?"
Kael knelt, fingers hovering over the glass. His Soul Reaver senses screamed warnings—the contents were alive, aware, dangerous beyond measure.
But also familiar.
"A fragment," he said slowly. "A fragment of the First Soul Reaver."
The notification that appeared in his vision confirmed what his instincts already knew:
"Soul Harvest: Entity consciousness fragment detected. Soul Reaver origin mystery: 12% complete. WARNING: Further investigation may trigger Cascade Event."
Cascade Event.
Whatever that meant, it couldn't be good.
Kael picked up the vial and felt centuries of secrets pressing against his mind. The Shadow Court. The Whisper King. The 72 Sealed Rifts.
Everything was connected.
And he was standing at the center of a web he hadn't known existed until tonight.
