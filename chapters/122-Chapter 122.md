Kael pressed his back against the alley wall, breath shallow. Three shadows moved at the far end of the tunnel—men in ash-gray cloaks, their boots making no sound on the wet cobblestones. Cultists. He'd tracked their courier from the Drowning Swan tavern a few hours ago, through the steam-fogged markets of Lower Thornwick and into this maze of forgotten drainage channels.
The Soul Reaver markings on his forearm pulsed faintly, a cold light that had nothing to do with the dim glow of phosphorescent moss clinging to the brickwork. Those markings would betray him the moment he acted.
"The Order sends its regards," one of the cloaked figures called out. No—two words into the darkness, and they already knew he was there.
Kael's fingers found the hilt of his blade. Not spiritual steel, not blessed by any Order, just good iron with a practical edge. The men approaching were Awakened, probably Stage One or Two, but there were three of them and only one of him. Even with Soul Reaver abilities, numbers mattered.
"The Order of Dawn's Inquisitors don't usually slum it in drainage tunnels," Kael said. His voice echoed off the curved walls. "What's worth harvesting down here?"
One of the cultists laughed—a short, ugly sound. "Harvesting. Is that what you call it?"
The lead figure raised a hand. Faint runes glowed along his wrist, marking him as someone with spiritual sensitivity at minimum. "The Veil is thinning, Reaver. Can't you feel it? The membrane between worlds grows weak with every passing moon. Your kind won't be able to hide much longer."
Kael's stomach tightened. The seal network—the rifts holding back the Abyssal Entities. If those failed, nothing else would matter.
He needed information. That meant he needed one of them alive. Preferably coherent. But alive and coherent were luxuries he couldn't guarantee with three Awakened closing distance in a confined space.
The lead cultist gestured to his companions. "Take him. The Whisper King wants his abilities intact."
Kael lunged before the second sentence ended.
---
The first cultist died with a gurgle, Kael's blade opening his throat before he could raise a defensive ward. Blood sprayed across the tunnel floor, mixing with the runoff from the streets above. The Soul Reaver markings blazed hot as Kael reached out, his palm pressing against the dying man's chest.
A cold rush behind his eyes. Fragments of someone else's life flickering through his consciousness.
A name: Corvin. A face: pale, sharp-featured, wearing the expression of a man who had seen something in the Abyss that changed him. A location: the Hollow Cathedral in the Undercroft, where the cult held their true gatherings. And something else—a symbol, burned into the memory like a brand. A serpent consuming its own tail, but with seven eyes arranged in a circle around its body.
Kael withdrew his hand as the body went slack, already pivoting toward the second cultist. This one had managed to raise a barrier—a shimmering wall of spiritual energy that glowed sickly green in the darkness.
"You shouldn't have done that," the cultist snarled. His companion flanked left, moving to cut off Kael's retreat.
Kael didn't retreat. He charged.
The barrier shattered under a concentrated burst of spiritual energy channeled through his blade. The cultist behind it staggered back, eyes wide with surprise. Kael drove his elbow into the man's solar plexus, following with a knee to the groin. The cultist doubled over, and Kael's blade found his heart.
Another harvest. Another rush of information flooding through his mind. This one carried something different—a skill, a fragment of perception that settled into his awareness like a muscle remembering how to flex.
The world shifted. Where before there had been only darkness and the faint glow of phosphorescent moss, now Kael could see the spiritual energy flowing through the tunnel like blood through veins. The drainage system sat above a ley line intersection—one of the natural conduits that the Orders used to power their strongest wards. And at that intersection, he saw something that made his blood freeze.
A rift. Not fully open, but cracked—a wound in the fabric of reality, bleeding raw Abyssal energy into the city's underbelly. The seal on it was ancient, barely holding, and at its center, something moved. Something vast and patient and waiting.
"Gods preserve us," the third cultist whispered. He had stopped advancing, his eyes fixed on the rift. "It's waking. The Prime said we had another month—"
He didn't finish. Kael closed the distance and drove his blade through the man's neck, severing the spine. The cultist dropped, twitching, hands clawing at wet stone.
This corpse was fresh—still warm, blood pooling beneath him. Kael knelt and pressed his palm to the cooling flesh.
The memories that came with this one were clearer, more detailed. The cultist's name was Garrett Sallow, a former dockworker who had joined the Abyssal worshippers months ago after they promised him power. They had shown him the rift beneath Thornwick—seventy-two seals, and one of them weakening faster than projected. The Whisper King had given the order personally: accelerate the timeline. Something was coming. Something that required the seals broken before the summer solstice.
Kael pulled his hand back, wiping the blood on his coat. The skill fragment from the second harvest settled deeper into his senses, and he let it. Abyssal Resistance, partial but functional. He'd take what he could get.
Seventy-two rifts. The seals holding back the Entities. And someone was trying to break them early.
He needed to move. The spiritual discharge from his harvesting would register on any Awakened's senses within a quarter mile. The Cult had called him "Reaver" openly, which meant they had been watching him, or at least, someone had told them what to look for.
The Underworld. The Hollow Cathedral. The Whisper King.
Kael sheathed his blade and stood. He had a choice: report this to the Orders and risk being identified as a Soul Reaver, or investigate alone and potentially stumble into something far beyond his current abilities.
The answer wasn't really a choice at all.
---
The Hollow Cathedral sat beneath the old merchant quarter, in what had once been a temple to the Sun God before the Great Fire of Thornwick destroyed it three centuries ago. The Orders had built over the ruins instead of clearing them, and the Undercroft had grown in the decades since—a warren of tunnels, abandoned basements, and spaces that no one officially acknowledged.
Kael found the entrance in a condemned warehouse, behind a false wall that crumbled at his touch. The steps beyond descended into darkness, and his borrowed sight showed him the spiritual residue of dozens of passages. Cultists had been coming and going for months. Years, maybe.
He descended carefully, one hand on his blade.
The first chamber was empty—old crates, rotting cloth, the remains of rats. The second held evidence of habitation: bedrolls, empty food tins, scratches on the walls that might have been tally marks or might have been symbols in a language Kael didn't recognize. The third...
The third was the cathedral proper.
The ceiling vaulted upward, lost in shadow, supported by pillars carved with figures that hurt to look at directly. Not the Sun God, not any of the recognized Entities from the Order bestiaries. These were older things, primordial shapes that suggested than depicted. And at the center, where an altar should have stood, a hole in the floor revealed the same rift Kael had seen from the tunnel—a wound in reality, sealed by symbols that pulsed with diminishing light.
Seven cultists knelt before it, their robes inscribed with the serpent-and-seven-eyes symbol he had pulled from the dead man's memory. At their head, a woman in white stood with her arms raised, chanting in a language that made Kael's teeth ache.
He recognized her.
Lyra.
Not the Lyra he had known in the Order of Dawn, the fierce warrior who had once saved his life during a patrol in the Ashlands. This Lyra had dark circles under her eyes, and her spiritual signature had changed—warped, tainted by something that flowed from the rift like poison through groundwater.
"The seals will hold for a few more days," Lyra said. Her voice carried perfectly through the chamber, calm and certain. "After that, the first Entity stirs. The Whisper King has prepared the way."
One of the kneeling cultists spoke up. "The Reaver. They've found one. Stage Two, Ascendant level. He killed three of our scouts in the upper tunnels."
Lyra's expression didn't change. "Then he will come here. They always come here, when they learn enough to be dangerous." She turned, and her eyes found the shadows where Kael stood frozen. "Welcome, Kael Ashborn. The Whisper King has been expecting you."
They emerged from the walls, from the shadows, from alcoves he hadn't noticed. A dozen cultists, maybe more, all of them Awakened, all of them wearing expressions that suggested they had stopped being human a long time ago.
Kael's hand found his blade.
"You have a choice," Lyra said. She descended from the altar, her white robes dragging through dust that had been accumulating for centuries. "Join us, and learn what the Abyss truly offers. Your Soul Reaver abilities would be... Valuable. The Whisper King rewards those who serve willingly."
"Or?"
"Or die here, and we harvest what we need from your corpse." Her smile didn't reach her eyes. "Either way, we get what we want. The only question is whether you suffer first."
Kael calculated his odds. Twelve Awakened, probably Stage One or Two. Lyra herself was harder to read—her spiritual energy had been twisted by prolonged exposure to the rift, corrupted into something that resonated with the Abyss than fighting it. He was Stage Two, Ascendant level, with abilities that they didn't fully understand.
Against a dozen, he might win.
Against a dozen plus Lyra, plus whatever protections they had prepared...
"I have a counter-offer," Kael said.
Lyra tilted her head. "I'm listening."
Kael reached into his coat and withdrew a small bundle wrapped in oilcloth. He had found it weeks ago in the possession of a dead courier, along with instructions for reaching the Hollow Cathedral. He had assumed it was a trap, a lure designed to draw him into exactly this situation.
He hadn't been wrong.
"This is a fragment," he said, keeping his voice level. "One of the old tablets. I found it on one of your couriers. I'm guessing the Whisper King wants it, or wants whoever carries it, or both. Either way, it's the only leverage in this room, and I'd like to use it before things get unpleasant."
Lyra extended her hand. "Show me."
"Not yet." Kael tucked the bundle back into his coat. "I want to meet the Whisper King. If he can convince me your cause is worth joining, you get the tablet and my cooperation."
"And if he can't?"
Kael drew his blade. The steel caught the light from the rift, gleaming with a cold fire. "Then we find out whether twelve Awakened are enough to stop a Soul Reaver who doesn't care about dying."
The cultists murmured among themselves. Kael caught fragments—heretic, fool, impossibly arrogant—but Lyra silenced them with a gesture.
"You would gamble everything on a meeting." She studied him, and something shifted behind her eyes. Not recognition. Something closer to calculation. "Fetch the palanquin. The Whisper King will want to meet this one personally."
Two cultists stepped forward, not to seize him but to indicate directions. "This way, Reaver. To the Inner Sanctum."
Kael sheathed his blade and followed.
---
The Inner Sanctum lay deeper still, in a chamber that seemed to exist outside of space. The walls were lined with mirrors, but his reflection in each showed a different moment in time—the cathedral's construction, the Great Fire, the Order of Dawn's founding, moments from his own past that he recognized but couldn't place.
At the center, seated on a throne carved from what looked like solidified shadow, was a man Kael had never seen before.
The Whisper King was old. Ancient, maybe, though his face showed no wrinkles and his body seemed vital. He wore robes of deepest black, embroidered with threads of silver that formed patterns too complex to follow. His eyes were the most unsettling feature—pale white, like marbles, with no visible pupils.
"Kael Ashborn," the Whisper King said. His voice came from everywhere and nowhere, echoing off the mirrored walls until it seemed like a chorus speaking in perfect unison. "Last of the Soul Reavers. Bearer of the Sepulcher Fragment. Ascendant stage, with potential to rise much higher—if you survive the next few hours."
Kael kept his face neutral. "You're well informed."
"I know everything that happens in my domain." The Whisper King rose from his throne, each movement deliberate and precise. "I know about the three cultists you killed in the tunnels. I know about the rift you witnessed. I know about the Order of Dawn Inquisitor who has been hunting you, and the Shadow Court agent who has been reporting your movements to me."
Kael's blood chilled. "Shadow Court."
"Surprised? They have an interest in the seal network—and in Soul Reavers. The Soul Reaver abilities are not unique to you, Kael. There have been others, over the centuries. Most were killed by the Orders. Some joined the Shadow Court. A few found a different path."
The Whisper King extended his hand, palm up. On it, Kael saw a marking identical to his own—the Soul Reaver sigil that had appeared on his forearm the night he woke after his execution.
Kael's jaw tightened. He said nothing.
"You were supposed to be another tool," the Whisper King continued. "A weapon to be used and discarded. But you proved more interesting than anticipated. You survived. You grew. You learned."
"And now you want me to join you."
"I want you to understand." The Whisper King gestured, and the mirrors around them shifted, showing scenes of devastation—cities in flames, armies of the dead, the sky torn open to reveal an endless darkness beyond. "The seal network is failing. Not because of the cult's efforts, but because it was designed to fail. The original sealers knew that containing the Entities forever was impossible. They built in an expiration date—a countdown that will end with the membrane between worlds dissolving completely."
The images shifted again, showing a world consumed by the Abyss. Kael saw the Entities emerging, vast and terrible, and the human civilization that remained afterward—nothing. Emptiness. Silence.
"The goal is not destruction," the entity said. "It is preparation. The Entities will emerge eventually. The question is whether humanity meets them as prey or as equals. The seal network's failure can be managed, channeled. The Entities can be bound to willing hosts, their power controlled than unleashed."
"You want to turn people into vessels."
"I want to give humanity a choice. The Orders would have you believe that the seals are eternal, that the Entities can be contained indefinitely. They lie. The truth is that the seal network will fail, and when it does, there will be no warning, no preparation, no chance for survival. I offer a different future—one where humanity adapts and endures."
Kael stared at the images, at the visions of apocalypse and the possibility of salvation. Part of him recognized the appeal of a plan, of certainty, of someone offering answers to questions he had been asking for months.
But there was something wrong. Something in the way the Whisper King spoke, in the certainty of his tone, in the way the cultists had gathered at the rift, preparing to wake something that had been sleeping for millennia.
"The Orders know about the seal degradation," Kael said slowly. "That's why Inquisitor Vane is in Thornwick. That's why the Shadow Court is monitoring me. They're all preparing for the same eventuality."
"Correct." The Whisper King's smile was cold. "The only question is which preparation succeeds. The Orders believe they can reinforce the seals indefinitely, using soul-essence harvested from Awakened and entities alike. The Shadow Court believes they can negotiate with the Entities, trading worship for peace. I believe in transformation—the merging of human will with Abyssal power."
"And where do Soul Reavers fit?"
"At the center. The Soul Reaver ability is unique—it can touch the dead, harvest their essence, their memories, their very fate. The original Soul Reaver discovered something in the Abyss, something that made him powerful enough to help create the seal network. That knowledge is encoded in the Sepulcher tablets. Whoever controls the tablets controls the seal network—and whoever controls the seal network controls the Entities."
The Whisper King extended his hand again, this time reaching for the bundle in Kael's coat. "Join us. Complete the tablet fragment you carry. Learn what the first Soul Reaver learned. Help humanity survive the coming change."
Kael stepped back, putting distance between himself and the ancient being. "And if I refuse?"
"Then you die here, and the fragment is taken from your corpse. Your abilities will be preserved—extracted through methods that are less elegant but equally effective. The outcome is the same. The only variable is your suffering."
The chamber seemed to contract around Kael, the mirrored walls closing in. He could feel the weight of the Whisper King's attention, the pressure of spiritual energy building toward release. One wrong word, and he would be dead.
But Kael had been dead before. It hadn't stopped him.
He reached into his coat, but not for the tablet fragment. His fingers found the small vial he always carried—an emergency supply of soul-essence, purchased at great cost from a Veiled Market dealer who had not asked questions. He crushed it between his fingers, feeling the energy flood into his reserves.
The Soul Reaver markings on his arm blazed bright enough to cast shadows.
"You want to know what a Soul Reaver can do?" Kael said. "Let me show you."
He struck the nearest mirror, shattering it into a thousand fragments. Each fragment became a window, and through those windows, Kael poured his harvested essence—memories of the dead, skills pulled from corpses, fragments of lives ended. The mirrors reflected not the present but the accumulated power of everyone he had ever reaved.
The Whisper King staggered back, his composure cracking for the first time.
"Impossible," he breathed. "You shouldn't be able to—"
Kael didn't let him finish. He poured everything he had into the mirrors, using his abilities to their full extent for the first time. The reflected power became real, becoming solid constructs of spiritual energy that tore through the chamber like a hurricane.
Cultists screamed as they were caught in the backlash. Lyra raised a barrier, but it shattered under the onslaught. The throne room collapsed in on itself, the Whisper King's domain unraveling as the foundation of his power proved less stable than he had believed.
The Whisper King's pale eyes burned with fury. "You think you understand? You think your handful of harvested souls compares to centuries of knowledge, of planning, of—"
Kael was already moving. He seized the nearest cultist by the throat and harvested him mid-stride, drinking deep. The man's body crumpled, and Kael tossed it into the path of two others who had raised wards. The corpse hit the barrier and went through it, the residual Soul Reaver energy disrupting the spiritual matrix like acid through silk.
He took the second cultist from behind, blade through the spine, palm on the skull before the body hit the ground. More energy. More power flooding into his reserves. The third and fourth came at him together, and Kael met them with everything he had reaved that night.
The first fell with his chest cavity caved in, the harvested strength of a dozen dead men behind the blow. The second managed to land a cut on Kael's shoulder before Kael caught his wrist and took everything—the man's life, his spiritual energy, the meager skills he had spent years developing. The cultist's eyes went blank, and Kael dropped him like a sack of grain.
Lyra was screaming something. Orders, maybe. Pleas. Kael couldn't hear her over the roar of power in his ears, the cold fire of accumulated souls burning through his veins.
Five cultists dead in the span of a few heartbeats. The rest fled, their discipline shattered, their faith in the Whisper King's protection proving weaker than their instinct to survive.
The Whisper King himself stood amid the ruins of his throne room, untouched. The mirrors had gone dark, the reflected power spent. His pale eyes tracked Kael with something that wasn't fear—something older and colder than fear.
"You would destroy everything I've built," the Whisper King said. "For what? The Orders? The seals? A world that hunted you down and executed you?"
Kael's breathing was ragged. The power was fading, the borrowed strength bleeding out of him as fast as it had come. He had minutes, maybe less, before the crash left him vulnerable.
"I don't do this for the Orders," Kael said. "I don't do it for the seals. I do it because you tried to use me, and that was a mistake."
He threw the tablet fragment—not at the Whisper King, but into the collapsing rift at the center of the chamber. The fragment sank into the raw Abyssal energy and vanished, the seals around the rift flaring once, bright and hot, before dimming to a steady pulse.
The Whisper King's expression twisted. "You fool. You have no idea what you've—"
Kael ran.
He didn't look back as the cathedral crumbled behind him. He didn't stop as he burst through the warehouse entrance into the cold night air of Thornwick's streets. He kept running until the sounds of pursuit faded, until the gaslit avenues gave way to the quieter passages of the artisan quarter, until he found himself standing before the door of a boarding house that had seen better days.
Sera looked up from her position by the window, her expression guarded. Behind her, the small room held maps, documents, evidence of the investigation she had been conducting into the Mortician's Guild and the soul trade that supported the Orders' seal-maintenance efforts.
"You look like hell," she said.
"The cult knows about me," Kael said. "The Whisper King knows. The Shadow Court has been watching. And the seals are failing faster than anyone admitted."
Sera's eyes widened. "What happened?"
Kael collapsed into a chair, exhaustion finally catching up with him. The crash was coming—he could feel it at the edges of his vision, the borrowed power draining away and leaving nothing but hollow fatigue. "I met the enemy. Or the ally. I'm not sure which anymore." He looked at the empty space in his coat where the tablet fragment had been, the knowledge it had contained still burning in his mind. "We need to talk. All of us. There's a war coming, and I'm not sure any of the sides are what they claim to be."
Sera crossed the room and knelt beside the chair, her hand finding his. Her fingers were cold. "You're shaking."
He was. The adrenaline was leaving, and in its place came the memories of every soul he had harvested that night—Corvin, Garrett Sallow, the five cultists in the throne room whose names he hadn't bothered to learn. Their lives flickered at the edges of his consciousness, fragments of who they had been before the Abyss had consumed them.
"I'm fine," he said. Neither of them believed it.
Outside, in the darkness of Thornwick's night, something stirred. Not the cultists Kael had scattered, not the Whisper King's diminished forces. Something else—something that had been waiting for the cathedral's collapse, for the moment when the seals weakened and the membrane between worlds grew thin enough to breach.
The first of the Entities was waking.
And it was very, very hungry.
