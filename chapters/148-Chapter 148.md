"Get down!"
The blade whistled past Kael's ear as he dove, rolling beneath a wrought-iron streetlamp. Three figures materialized from the fog-choked alley—shadowcloaked, moving with the synchronized precision of Awakened fighters. Kael's hand found the cobblestones, cold and slick with condensation from the steam vents.
"Soul Reaver," one hissed. Female voice, cultured. "The Court sends its regards."
Kael counted heartbeats. Three opponents, stage two at minimum, armed with spiritual blades drawn in the traditional Dawnguard stance. They bracketed the alley's mouth, cutting off the only exit toward the main thoroughfare. Behind him, a wall of blackened brick. Above, nothing but fog and the distant glow of Thornwick's industrial heart.
He rose slowly, keeping his hands visible—or as visible as hands clutching two short blades could be.
"Tell your Court I'm flattered by the attention." Kael's voice came out steadier than his pulse felt. "But really, a formal invitation would have sufficed."
The lead figure laughed. "You think this is an assassination? We're recruiters, Reaver. The Court wants to talk."
"Then why the weapons?"
"Insurance." She stepped forward, and the fog curled around her boots like something alive. "You're harder to corner than expected. The reports undersold you."
Kael's eyes swept the alley. Three meters to his left, a refuse bin. Six meters to the right, a storm drain grate. Behind him, nothing but brick and the distant rumble of the transit rail. Direct combat against three Awakened would be costly, possibly fatal. Running wasn't an option—they'd pursued him twice already tonight, herding him deeper into the Undercroft district where the steam pipes ran hot and the shadows grew thick.
"Talk fast," Kael said.
"First, proof of good faith." She raised her blade—not to strike, but to expose her wrist. A brand, visible for only a moment: an eye overlaid on a crescent moon. "Shadow Court sigil. I am Veil, and I speak for the Keeper."
Something cold slithered through Kael's chest. The Keeper. Not a name but a title, bandied about in whispers among those who tracked cult activity. The unseen hand that moved pieces across the board while lesser players sacrificed pawns.
"Zarek's superior," Kael said. Not a question.
"Among other things." Veil's smile was audible. "Zarek is a useful asset, nothing more. The Court has grander ambitions than frame jobs and petty vendettas."
The two flanking fighters hadn't moved, but their stance had shifted—less aggressive, more watchful. Kael noted the change. Whatever recruitment pitch was coming, they were prepared for rejection.
"I'm going to disappoint you," Kael said. "I don't join organizations."
"We don't want you to join. We want you to listen."
She reached into her cloak and produced something small, metallic—a coin bearing the same eye-and-crescent symbol. She flicked it. It spun through the fog, catching what little light filtered from the distant streetlamps, and landed at Kael's feet.
"Pick it up."
Kael didn't move. "Why?"
"Because it contains information worth more than gold. Because the alternative is Inquisitor Vane finding you first, and she won't offer conversation." Veil tilted her head, studying him with the detached interest of a collector examining a specimen. "You're stalling. Calculating your odds. Let me save you the trouble—you have no viable escape route, and engaging us would drain reserves you can't afford to lose. The Court knows your capabilities. We know about the Hollow Knights you've harvested, the abilities you've accumulated. We know you've been tracking cult activity in the Ashworth Quarter, following the same thread we've been pulling for months."
Kael's jaw tightened. "You have eyes on me."
"We have eyes everywhere. It's the Court's specialty." She gestured at the coin. "Take it. Your survival depends on what it contains."
Kael crouched, picked up the coin. The metal was cold, almost numbingly so, and it thrummed with a faint spiritual resonance that made his teeth ache. He closed his fingers around it.
"Soul-anchored memory storage," he said. "Class five, minimum."
"Class seven. The knowledge inside represents decades of investigation. Rift locations. Cult hierarchy. The identities of the 72." Veil spread her hands. "All yours, if you agree to meet with the Keeper. One conversation. Then you're free—join us, refuse us, run—we don't care. But you'll have the information."
"What's the catch?"
"The catch is that the Keeper believes you hold a key to something ancient. Something that predates the Orders, predates the Concordat, predates the Abyssal Wars themselves." Her voice dropped. "The original Soul Reaver. His tomb. His legacy."
Kael's grip on the coin tightened until the edges bit into his palm. The original Soul Reaver. The first. The one who had supposedly founded the practice, whose Sepulcher Tablets contained secrets that made the Orders tremble. Kael had been chasing that ghost for months, following fragments of information that dissolved the moment he got close.
"You know where the tomb is," he said.
"We know where to start looking." Veil's smile sharpened. "But we need a Reaver to access what lies within. The Keeper cannot do it alone. None of us can. That's why he wants you alive, Reaver. Not as a soldier. As a key."
The fog seemed to thicken, pressing against Kael's shoulders like hands. He thought of the Hollow Knights he'd harvested—their fragmented memories, their desperate last moments clinging to existence. He thought of the cultists he'd killed in Ashworth, their bodies dissolving into spiritual ash before he could touch them. He thought of Zarek's face, twisted with fanatical certainty, and the conspiracy that had framed him for murder.
The coin burned in his palm.
"If I refuse?"
"Then you refuse, and we part ways. But Inquisitor Vane is three blocks north, closing fast. She has a tracker trained on your spiritual signature." Veil stepped back, and her companions mirrored the movement. "That coin contains a map to a safehouse. Coordinates for three dead drops containing intelligence files. And a single name—the name of the person who ordered Gwen's squad into that ambush."
Kael's breath caught. Gwen. His former partner, dead eighteen months now, her squad slaughtered in what the Orders called a "cultist raid gone wrong." He'd always known it was murder. He'd never known who gave the order.
"Why?" he asked. "Why tell me?"
"Because the person who killed Gwen works for the same people we oppose. The Court may operate in shadows, Reaver, but we don't waste resources on grudges." Veil turned, her cloak flaring. "The Keeper's offer stands. Find us when you're ready—or don't. But take the coin. Knowledge is the only currency that appreciates in value."
The three figures melted into the fog, their footsteps fading until Kael stood alone in the alley, the steam vents hissing their endless breath into the night air.
He looked down at the coin. The eye-and-crescent symbol gleamed like a wound in the metal.
*Three blocks north.*
Kael ran.

The rooftops of Thornwick's Undercroft district were a maze of copper pipes, steam vents, and makeshift walkways bolted to warehouse walls by generations of desperate residents. Kael moved through them like a ghost, his Ascendant-stage reflexes letting him leap gaps that would have killed a lesser Awakened, his body hugging the shadows where the industrial glare couldn't reach.
Below him, the city churned. Steam engines pumped their endless breath into the atmosphere. Smokestacks vomited ash and cinder into skies that hadn't seen clear blue in decades. Somewhere in the distance, a transit rail shrieked, the sound bouncing off the canyon walls of the industrial quarter like a wounded animal.
Vane was close. He could feel her—a pressure against his spiritual senses, cold and focused like the edge of a surgical blade. She'd been hunting him for weeks now, following breadcrumbs he'd left deliberately, always staying one step ahead through a combination of preparation and luck. But luck ran out.
The coin pulsed against his chest, tucked beneath his shirt. A Class seven soul-anchored storage device represented wealth beyond measure—thousands of gold sovereigns on the black market, enough to buy a dozen Awakened fighters or a small army of commoner mercenaries. And Veil had given it away like a calling card.
What did the Court want badly enough to spend that kind of currency?
Kael reached the edge of a drainage gutter, paused, and scanned the street below. Two figures in the uniform of the Order of Dawn's enforcement arm—standard patrol, nothing he hadn't seen before. But beyond them, in the mouth of an alley that led to the Ashworth Quarter, a third figure stood motionless.
Tall. Gaunt. Eyes that caught the lamplight and threw it back like chips of winter ice.
Inquisitor Vane.
She didn't move, didn't gesture, didn't acknowledge his presence. She stood there, watching, as if she knew exactly where he was and was waiting for him to make the next move.
Kael's hand drifted to the knife at his hip. He could fight her—maybe. She was stage three, three full tiers above him, and Inquisitor training meant she'd faced worse threats than a rogue Ascendant with stolen abilities. But running meant leading her to whatever sanctuary he'd been planning, and that compromised future options.
She smiled. Raised one hand, palm outward.
*Stop.*
Kael froze.
"You've led me on the chase," Vane called up, her voice carrying easily through the night air. "I have to admit, I underestimated you. The reports suggested you were a one-trick scavenger, harvesting corpses for power. They failed to mention you had a brain."
"What do you want, Inquisitor?"
"Want?" She laughed, a dry sound like rustling paper. "I want to know why the Shadow Court sent envoys to negotiate with you. I want to know what you have that they want badly enough to break their own protocols. And I want to know who inside my Order is feeding them information about your movements."
Kael said nothing.
"Three days ago, a patrol reported sighting you in the Ashworth Quarter. Two days ago, the same location, same time. I positioned myself accordingly. Tonight, I waited at the third possible approach vector." Vane spread her hands. "Tell me, Reaver—how did they know where you would be?"
*Someone in the Order is compromised.*
The realization hit Kael like a physical blow. He'd been careful, changing patterns, avoiding predictable routes. But if the Shadow Court knew his movements, they had to have access to more than street-level observation. They had eyes inside the Order.
"Does it matter?" Kael called back.
"It matters because an Inquisitor's job is finding corruption. If there's a leak in my Order, I need to know." Vane's eyes narrowed. "The Shadow Court approached you tonight. That means they're interested, which means you're valuable. I need to understand why before someone else does."
Kael weighed his options. Vane was a threat—he'd known that from the beginning. But she was also an opportunity. If she was investigating Order corruption, she might be an enemy of his enemies.
"The Court offered me information," he said slowly. "Location data. Cult hierarchy. The names of the 72."
"Lies," Vane said flatly. "The Court doesn't share intelligence. They use it as bait."
"This was different. They mentioned the original Soul Reaver. His tomb."
Vane went still. For a long moment, the only sound was the hiss of steam and the distant rumble of industry.
"That's impossible," she said finally. "The original tomb was destroyed during the Sundering. All records—"
"Records can be falsified. Tombs can be hidden." Kael gripped the gutter, feeling the metal bend under his fingers. "The Court thinks I can access something. Some kind of legacy."
"You're a scavenger with a stolen ability. What makes you qualified to—"
"I didn't say they were right." Kael dropped from the gutter, landing in a crouch on the rooftop below. "But I said I was curious."
He ran.

The safehouse was a cellar beneath a condemned textile mill, accessible through a drainage tunnel that exited near the river docks. Kael had found it three weeks ago, during a job that required him to disappear for a few days. Now it served as his base of operations—a place to think, to plan, to avoid the constant pressure of pursuit.
He descended the ladder, pulled the hatch shut behind him, and let his eyes adjust to the darkness. The cellar smelled of rust and stagnant water. A single oil lamp sat on an overturned crate, its flame guttering in the draft that crept through the tunnel.
Kael lit the lamp, set it on a makeshift table, and pulled out the coin.
The eye-and-crescent symbol seemed to pulse in the lamplight. He could feel the soul-anchor resonating, waiting for the proper key. His own spiritual signature, if he was reading the construction correctly. Class seven storage devices were designed for single-user access—once he opened it, the information inside would imprint on his consciousness, impossible to transfer or duplicate.
He pressed the coin to his forehead and *pushed*.
The world dissolved.

*Kael stood in a room he had never seen.*
*Stone walls, slick with moisture. Torches guttered in brackets, casting shadows that moved wrong—too slow, too deliberate, as if they were watching him than fleeing his presence. A table dominated the center of the room, covered in papers, maps, and a collection of small glass vials containing liquid that glowed faintly purple.*
*"You're late."*
*The voice came from behind him. Kael turned, hand going to his knife, and found himself facing a man he had never seen before—old, ancient, his face a map of scars and wrinkles, his eyes the flat black of someone who had stared into the Abyss too long. He wore robes that might have been ceremonial once but now hung in tatters, and around his neck hung a pendant shaped like a crescent moon.*
*"Who are you?" Kael asked.*
*"I'm what's left of someone who made the same choices you're making." The old man gestured at the table. "Sit. We don't have much time."*
*Kael didn't sit. "This is a memory. You're not real."*
*"I'm real enough to give you what you need." The old man picked up one of the vials and held it to the torchlight. "Soul-essence. Harvested from an Awakened third-tier, condensed through rituals that haven't been performed in three hundred years. Do you know what it does?"*
*"Fuels spiritual advancement."*
*"It does more than that." The old man set the vial down. "It preserves. The soul inside doesn't dissipate—it remains, conscious, aware, capable of communicating with whoever opens the container." He met Kael's eyes. "That's what the Court has been doing for decades. Collecting the powerful. Preserving them. Waiting."*
*"Waiting for what?"*
*"For someone like you." The old man moved around the table, circling Kael like a predator. "The original Soul Reaver—the one whose tomb you're chasing—figured out how to harvest not just abilities, but entire souls. Compressed, stored, preserved. A library of power accumulated over millennia." He stopped, standing close enough that Kael could smell the grave-rot on his robes. "The Court has been searching for that tomb since before the Orders existed. They believe whoever controls the Reaver's legacy controls the world."*
*"And you believe them?"*
*"I believe that the legacy exists. I believe that what the original Reaver discovered was not meant for humanity—too dangerous, too corrupting, too much power concentrated in too few hands." The old man's black eyes burned. "I believe that if the Court finds that tomb, they will use what's inside to shatter the seals and let the 72 walk free."*
*"Why are you telling me this?"*
*"Because you're going to find it anyway. You're going to open doors that should stay closed. And when you do—" The old man reached out, pressed one finger to Kael's forehead. "—remember that power without purpose is just another form of death."*
*The room began to dissolve, the torches guttering out one by one.*
*"Wait!" Kael lunged forward. "The name—Gwen's squad—who gave the order—"*
*"Find the Sepulcher Tablets." The old man's voice came from everywhere and nowhere. "Find the truth. And when you do—"*
*Darkness.*
---
Kael gasped, jerking back to consciousness. The coin crumbled in his fingers, its purpose spent, fragments of ancient metal dissolving into ash. He was on his knees in the cellar, the oil lamp flickering, his hands shaking.
The memory was gone—not the information, but the experience. Already it felt like a dream, fading at the edges, leaving only impressions: the old man's black eyes, the purple glow of the soul-essence vials, the terrible weight of centuries-old purpose pressing against his mind.
But the information remained.
Kael pulled himself upright, using the table for support. His thoughts raced, sorting through what he'd learned. The Shadow Court had been collecting souls for decades—not just harvesting abilities, but preserving consciousness. Waiting. The original Soul Reaver's tomb contained something so dangerous that even the mention of it made the old man in the memory speak of apocalypse.
And somewhere in that maze of secrets, the name of Gwen's killer waited to be found.
The coin's data unfolded in his mind like a map. Three dead drops, locations in the Ashworth Quarter, the Ironheights industrial district, and beneath the old cathedral in the city's center. Safe house coordinates for a meeting point if he chose to respond to the Keeper's invitation. And the tomb's location—not exact, but a starting point. A region in the Thornback Mountains, a place the original Reaver had supposedly died defending.
Kael's hands stopped shaking.
He thought about the Hollow Knights he'd harvested. The abilities he'd accumulated. The body count he'd accumulated in his climb from death row to Ascendant stage. He thought about the people he'd killed, the lines he'd crossed, the moral compromises that had let him survive when better people had died.
He thought about Gwen, her laugh, her stubborn faith in the system that had betrayed them both.
The Shadow Court wanted to use him. Vane wanted to control him. The Orders wanted him dead or enslaved. And somewhere in the mountains, a tomb waited that might contain the keys to everything—or the seeds of his destruction.
Kael stood. Walked to the cellar's entrance. Paused with his hand on the ladder.
Three floors up, in the condemned mill above, the floorboards creaked.
Kael's hand went to his knife. His spiritual senses extended outward, brushing against the presence above—faint, deliberate, trying to mask itself but failing. Someone had followed him. Someone who knew about this safehouse, which meant someone who had access to information about his movements.
The creaking stopped.
Silence pressed against his ears, broken only by the distant drip of water and the whisper of wind through the drainage tunnel behind him.
Kael didn't draw his knife. He didn't call out. He stood there, at the base of the ladder, waiting for the figure above to make the first move.
The figure above made no move at all.
And in that silence, in that held breath between action and consequence, Kael understood: the Shadow Court hadn't just sent envoys. They'd sent an observer. Someone to watch how he reacted, what he chose, whether he would take the bait or spit it back in their faces.
The game had changed. The pieces were moving. And somewhere in the darkness above him, eyes that belonged to no one he trusted waited to see what he would do next.
Kael's fingers tightened on the ladder's first rung.
The question was: what would he do?
