Kael dropped from the rooftop, boots striking the cobblestones with a sound like a cracking whip. Three bodies sprawled across the alley floor, their blood still wet enough to shimmer under the gaslight. He'd tracked the cultists for six hours through Thornwick's underbelly, and now he had what he needed.
"Circle of Radiance," he muttered, crouching beside the nearest corpse. The symbol branded into the dead man's forehead matched the sigil Kael had seen carved into a dozen crime scenes across the city. Not random violence. Organized sacrifice.
He pressed his palm against the cooling flesh. The Soul Reaver ability stirred, that familiar cold draw spreading from his fingertips into the corpse.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted 3 days of residual memories from the remains. Fragmented tactical knowledge acquired.]
The memories flooded through him in stuttering images. A warehouse near the docks. Figures in dark robes. The phrase "the tomb must open before the seal breaks" repeated like a prayer. And underneath it all, a name that made Kael's stomach clench: Zarek.
The frame-up artist. The man who'd planted evidence in Kael's cell after his execution, ensuring the Orders would hunt him even as a dead man walking. Zarek wasn't just a criminal mastermind—he was connected to whatever the Circle of Radiance was planning.
Kael pulled his hand back, wiping residue on his coat. The corpse's spiritual energy had been nearly spent; three days meant these cultists had been active far longer than anyone suspected.
The alley's shadows deepened. Kael's Demigod senses prickled. Someone else was here, watching from the darkness between buildings. He spun, blade half-drawn, but the rooftop where he'd sensed the presence was empty.
Just paranoia. Or the Shadow Court's ever-present surveillance. He'd grown used to those invisible eyes.
The cultists' leader—according to the harvested memories—met his contacts at a warehouse called the Broken Anchor every third night. Tonight would be that night. Kael had maybe two hours to prepare.
He moved toward the alley's mouth, then stopped.
A mark on the wall. Fresh paint, still tacky, in a color that shouldn't exist in this district's grim palette: silver-white with threads of gold. Kael's blood turned to ice water.
The Soul Reaver's Mark. Not the crude imitations he'd seen cultists use, but something ancient, something that resonated with the power coiled in his own chest. He'd found references to this symbol in forbidden texts—the signature of the original Soul Reaver, the one who'd existed a thousand years ago during the Abyssal Wars.
Someone wanted him to find this. Someone who knew what he was.
Kael's fingers hovered over the mark, not touching. The silver light seemed to pulse in invitation, or warning. His mind raced through implications: if the original Soul Reaver had left markers, there might be a tomb. A repository of knowledge the Orders had spent centuries trying to suppress.
The Soul Reaver Origin obligation—setup in chapter one, payoff expected around chapter three-fifty. This was it. The trail he'd been searching for without knowing he was searching.
He touched the mark.
Images exploded through his consciousness. A man in ancient armor standing before a rift in reality, wielding a blade that drank souls. The same man sealing the Great Rift with blood and will. The same man dying in an empty tomb, his last act to hide everything he'd learned about the Abyss, the Entities, and the power that could destroy them.
Kael gasped, staggering backward. His nose was bleeding. The mark had demanded more than he'd expected—his spiritual reserves dipped slightly before stabilizing.
The tomb existed. Somewhere beneath Thornwick's streets, the original Soul Reaver's final resting place waited. And someone had just revealed its location to him.
A folded paper lay at his feet where none had existed before. Kael picked it up with trembling hands. The handwriting was elegant, archaic: "Three blocks east. Through the iron door. The tomb awaits its heir."
He looked up. The watcher on the rooftop had returned—Kael could feel those eyes now, weighing him with ancient patience. But the figure made no move to attack or flee. watched.
"You're either helping me or setting me up," Kael said to the darkness. No response.
The warehouse meeting would have to wait. The tomb called louder.
Kael ran. The gaslit streets blurred past as he followed the invisible thread the mark had planted in his mind. Three blocks east. Through the iron door. The tomb.
He found the door in a basement beneath a collapsed tenement. Iron, indeed—black iron covered in verdigris, ancient beyond measure. The Soul Reaver Mark was carved into its surface, glowing faintly with residual power.
Kael's hand closed around the handle. The metal burned cold. The lock—what should have been a lock—seemed to recognize something in his touch. Mechanisms clicked. Gears that hadn't moved in centuries ground themselves into motion.
The door swung inward on silent hinges.
Darkness exhaled from the opening, carrying the scent of old stone and older death. Kael's Demigod senses stretched forward, probing the space beyond. Empty. No traps, no guards, no ambush waiting in the black.
Only the promise of answers.
He stepped through.
The door sealed behind him with the finality of a coffin lid.
Kael spun, but the iron surface had become seamless wall. No handle. No hinges. He'd been sealed in, and the only way forward was deeper into whatever this place was.
Light bloomed from his palm—crude spiritual energy shaped into illumination. The chamber revealed itself: a narrow passage carved from living rock, walls inscribed with symbols that made his eyes water. Not any language he recognized, yet comprehensible.
"The seal network weakens," he read. "The 72 rifts hunger. The Entities stir."
The original Soul Reaver had written this. A warning, preserved across centuries.
Kael pressed forward. The passage wound downward, and the air grew thick with the weight of accumulated time. His footsteps echoed against stone that had witnessed no living sound since the tomb's creator had walked this same path.
The passage opened into a circular chamber. Kael's breath caught.
Bones. Hundreds of bones, arranged in concentric circles around a central pedestal. Not burial—dedication. These were the remains of everyone who'd tried to access this tomb over the centuries. Failed attempts, their spiritual energy long dissipated but their bones remaining as testament.
And at the center, the pedestal held a single object: a stone tablet covered in the same script as the walls. The original Soul Reaver's journal. The complete record of how the power worked, where it came from, what it truly meant to carry the ability to harvest souls.
Kael approached the pedestal. His hand extended, fingers trembling.
A voice echoed through the chamber, speaking words he understood despite their foreignness: "Heir of the Reaver. You who walk the path of death. Before you claim the legacy, know this: you are not the first. You will not be the last. The lineage continues until the final rift seals or opens forever."
The tablet glowed, awaiting his touch.
"Touch it and learn everything," the voice continued. "Or leave now and remain ignorant. The choice shapes more than you know."
Kael's hand hovered. The bones of past seekers surrounded him—all those who'd tried and failed, their ambition and desperation calcified into memorial. He'd faced choices before. This was just another.
His fingers made contact.
The world shattered.
Kael stood in a memory that wasn't his. A man—tall, dark-haired, bearing features that echoed his own—stood before the Council of Six a thousand years ago. The same council still governed Thornwick's Order representatives, though every face had changed a hundred times over.
"The Soul Reaver power is not a gift," the man said. "It is a covenant. Sealed in blood when the first Entity touched our world. We are not harvesters. We are vessels."
One of the council members—a woman with silver eyes—spoke: "And if the vessel breaks?"
"Then the covenant transfers to another. The lineage protects itself."
Another vision. The same man sealing the Great Rift, pouring his own soul into the barrier. Not dying—transforming. Becoming something that existed between life and death, the ultimate sacrifice that maintained the seal for a millennium.
Another vision. The man in his tomb, recording everything. The names of the 72 Entities. The locations of all sealed rifts. The techniques to harvest abilities without corruption. The terrible truth about the afterlife—how souls were recycled, how personality was erased at death, how everything the Orders taught about heaven and paradise was elaborate fiction.
Kael's mind expanded, accepting information faster than he could process it. The original Soul Reaver had spent decades compiling this knowledge, and now it flooded into Kael's consciousness like water through a broken dam.
The tomb shuddered. Dust rained from the ceiling. The bones rattled in their circles.
"The seal weakens," the voice said. "Your realm—Demigod—is sufficient. The tablet's knowledge unlocks. But know this: the Shadow Court watches. The Faceless One has marked you. Others hunt what you now possess. Leave quickly."
Kael's eyes snapped open. The chamber was the same, but everything had changed. He knew things now that the Orders had killed to suppress. He understood his power's origin—not mutation, not curse, but covenant. Chosen lineage, hidden from the world.
The tablet in his hands crumbled to dust. Its purpose fulfilled.
A grinding sound echoed from the passage behind him. Something was coming—something that didn't belong in this place of death and secrets.
Kael ran. The walls blurred past as his Demigod-enhanced speed carried him toward whatever exit the tomb provided. Behind him, the grinding grew louder. Footsteps that weren't footsteps. A presence that reeked of Abyssal energy.
The iron door materialized ahead, no longer sealed. Kael threw himself through the opening and into the tenement basement. Fresh air flooded his lungs.
He didn't stop moving. The city swallowed him as he emerged into Thornwick's predawn streets, weaving through alleys and shadows until his pursuer—if it had truly been pursuing—lost his trail.
Dawn painted the horizon orange and red. Kael leaned against a wall in a courtyard three miles from the tomb, catching his breath. His mind churned with new knowledge, new questions, new dangers.
The original Soul Reaver had existed. Had sealed the Great Rift. Had hidden everything about the Abyss, the Entities, and the covenant that bound Soul Reavers across centuries. And now Kael carried that legacy, along with the target it painted on his back.
The Shadow Court knew about him. The cultists operated with disturbing coordination. Zarek's involvement suggested the conspiracy reached higher than he'd imagined. And the footsteps in the tomb—the presence that had pursued him—could only be one thing.
An Entity's avatar. Something from beyond the Veil, manifesting in Thornwick's streets.
The Faceless One had warned him. The other Entities would come.
A folded paper appeared in his hand—when had he picked it up?—bearing coordinates he didn't recognize and a single line in handwriting that matched the note from the tomb: "The true tomb lies elsewhere. This was only the entrance. Seek the Sepulcher Tablets if you wish to understand what you are."
Kael crumpled the paper, then smoothed it again. Information was power. Even cryptic information.
The Soul Reaver Origin obligation wasn't paid off—not entirely. He'd learned the lineage existed, learned his power was covenant than accident, learned the original had sacrificed himself to seal the Great Rift. But the tomb's location remained hidden, its full secrets protected by mechanisms he hadn't triggered or couldn't access.
The war the Orders fought against the Abyss wasn't just ideological or spiritual. It was existential. And he stood at its center.
Kael looked toward the sunrise. The Broken Anchor warehouse waited. The cultists' meeting would proceed with or without his interruption. And somewhere in Thornwick's shadows, Zarek plotted with forces Kael was only beginning to comprehend.
The Soul Reaver's Mark had led him here. Someone or something wanted him to follow this path. Wanted him to discover the truth about his lineage.
The question was whether he was walking willingly into a trap or claiming his inheritance by design.
Footsteps echoed in the courtyard behind him. Multiple sets. Approaching.
Kael's blade materialized in his hand.
The morning light revealed three figures entering the courtyard from the opposite end. Not cultists. Not Shadow Court operatives.
Order representatives. The symbols on their coats marked them as Iron Vanguard—Thornwick's enforcement arm for the Orders' will. The woman in front wore a captain's insignia and carried herself like someone who'd killed more Awakened than she'd had breakfast.
"Kael Ashborn," she said. Not a question. "You're a hard man to find for a dead person."
Kael's grip tightened on his blade. "I'm guessing this isn't a social call."
"The Order of Dawn filed a formal inquiry about you. The Arcane Spire seconded it. Something about stolen knowledge, unauthorized harvesting, threats to the seal network." The captain's smile held no warmth. "My orders are to bring you in. Alive is preferred. But the paperwork is simpler if you're breathing."
Behind her, her two companions fanned out, cutting off his escape routes. All three were Transcendent—lower than his Demigod realm but still dangerous in numbers.
Kael assessed angles. Direct combat would work, but word would spread. Killing Order representatives—even in self-defense—would make him a fugitive from all six factions. His careful balance of information and leverage would shatter.
The captain raised her hand, signaling restraint. "We're not here to fight. The Council wants to talk. Specifically, they want to know about the tomb you just left."
Ice crystallized in Kael's veins. "How do you know about the tomb?"
"Because we've been watching cultists use Soul Reaver markers for months, waiting for someone to lead us to its location. You walked right through our perimeter." She gestured at the surrounding rooftops. Kael noticed them now—the shadows that weren't shadows, the watchers he'd dismissed as Shadow Court surveillance.
Iron Vanguard operatives. Dozens of them, positioned across the district.
"You're surrounded," the captain continued. "Your choice. Come quietly, answer some questions, maybe walk away with your secrets intact. Or draw that blade and become a martyr for whatever cause you've convinced yourself serves."
Kael's mind raced. The Order knew about the tomb. Had known for months. Had used cultists as bait, waiting for someone to trigger the discovery mechanism.
His discovery. His blood on the mark. His navigation of the passage.
They'd been herding him from the start.
The captain's eyes narrowed. "You understand now. You walked into something bigger than yourself. The question is whether you're smart enough to walk out."
Behind Kael, more footsteps. The courtyard's other exit sealed with more Iron Vanguard soldiers. He was surrounded completely, and his Demigod power—while formidable—couldn't fight an entire battalion without eventual exhaustion.
The Soul Reaver abilities hummed in his chest. Zero spiritual cost for harvesting, but he couldn't harvest living people. And these soldiers were definitely alive, their spiritual signatures burning bright in his perception.
"I have questions," Kael said slowly. "Answers to those might make cooperation worthwhile."
The captain tilted her head. "Try me."
"The original Soul Reaver. The one from a thousand years ago. What do your records say about him?"
Something flickered across her face—surprise, quickly masked. "That's not the usual opening."
"I'm not the usual criminal."
"No," she agreed. "You're something else entirely. Someone who knows to ask about the First Reaver instead of the current power structure. Someone who's been doing research beyond what a death-row fugitive could access."
She stepped closer, studying him with new interest. "The First Reaver died sealing the Great Rift. His body was never recovered. The Orders have debated his fate for centuries—was he saint or monster? Hero or cautionary tale? The records were sealed by unanimous vote a hundred years after his death. Even the Council doesn't have complete access."
"And the tomb I just left?"
"According to our research, it's a decoy. A failsafe the First Reaver created to identify worthy successors without revealing the true repository." She smiled thinly. "You passed the test. Which means you might be able to access what we're looking for."
Kael's blood chilled. "What you're looking for?"
"The Sepulcher Tablets. The First Reaver's complete record of the Abyss, the Entities, and the techniques required to fight them. The Orders have fragments, but the full set was hidden a millennium ago. If you're truly his heir, only you can find them."
The same name from the cryptic note. The same knowledge the original tomb had promised but couldn't deliver.
"Why tell me this?" Kael asked. "Why not just capture me and extract the information?"
"Because extraction doesn't work on Soul Reavers." The captain's expression darkened. "We've tried. The power doesn't transfer through torture or ritual. It passes through bloodline and worthiness, neither of which we can manufacture. You have to choose to help us."
She extended her hand—not in greeting, but in offer. "Work with the Orders. Help us find the Sepulcher Tablets before the cultists do. In exchange, we drop all charges, grant you official status as an independent contractor, and share whatever knowledge we have about your lineage."
Kael studied her face for deception. Found none—she believed what she was saying. Whether that belief was accurate remained to be seen.
"And if I refuse?"
"Then we lock you away where you can't interfere with the war. Not execution. Something more permanent. Soul anchoring—the technique we use on the most dangerous Entity-touched criminals. You'd be conscious and aware forever, trapped in a prison of your own soul."
She wasn't bluffing. Kael could sense the truth in her spiritual signature.
The courtyard fell silent. The Iron Vanguard soldiers watched, weapons ready but not raised. Waiting for his decision.
"Information first," Kael said finally. "The cultists' leader. Zarek. What's his connection to the Circle of Radiance?"
The captain's eyebrows rose. "You know about Zarek?"
"I know he framed me. I know he's connected to the cultists. I want to know why."
"Zarek is a merchant. Legitimate business, shipping and trade. He's also the Circle of Radiance's primary financial backer, though we can't prove it." She paused. "And he's the one who originally suggested the Orders investigate Soul Reaver markers in Thornwick. He led us right to you."
Kael's jaw tightened. Zarek had orchestrated everything—the frame-up, the cultist trail, even his own "discovery" of the tomb.
"The cultist leader calls himself the Whisper King," the captain continued. "We've never identified him. But based on intelligence, he's been preparing for something big. The seal network's degradation has accelerated in the past year. He believes the Great Rift will open within months."
"And when it opens?"
"Then the Entities pour through. The Abyss consumes reality. Everything ends." She smiled grimly. "The Orders have been fighting this war for a thousand years. We'd prefer to keep fighting than lose."
Kael looked at the soldiers surrounding him. At the captain's extended hand. At the dawn light painting Thornwick's rooftops in shades of gold and blood.
He had the knowledge now. The lineage. The covenant. Everything the First Reaver had left behind, waiting for someone worthy to claim it.
But worthiness wasn't passive. The First Reaver had acted, had sacrificed everything, had sealed the Great Rift through will and blood. Kael could do the same—or he could refuse, hide, try to survive alone.
The Orders were corrupt. Politically divided. Often more interested in maintaining power than fighting the Abyss. But they were also the only organization with the resources to mount a real defense.
"I want to meet the Council," Kael said. "All six Orders. Simultaneously. Public venue."
The captain's smile faded. "That's... Complicated."
"The Six Orders signed the Concordat to contain conflict, not enable it. If you're asking me to help fight a war, I want witnesses to whatever agreements we make. No private negotiations. No hidden clauses."
"You're asking for political exposure. The kind that makes deals impossible."
"I'm asking for accountability. The kind that keeps me alive."
She considered him for a long moment. "The Council of Six meets in three days. Annual session, public gallery. I can get you a seat if you're willing to wait."
"And my charges?"
"Suspended during negotiation period. You're not cleared, but you're not arrested either. Think of it as supervised probation."
Kael took her hand. The grip was firm, professional. Sealed.
The captain stepped back, gesturing to her soldiers. "Escort Mr. Ashborn to the Iron Citadel. Comfortable quarters. Full access to our archives on the First Reaver. And someone keep an eye on him—carefully."
One of the soldiers—a young woman with nervous eyes—stepped forward. "This way, sir."
Kael followed her from the courtyard. The other soldiers parted, watching him pass with expressions ranging from curiosity to outright hostility. He was a prisoner of sorts, but one who'd negotiated better terms than most ever received.
The Iron Citadel loomed ahead—a fortress of black stone and spiritual barriers, the Iron Vanguard's headquarters in Thornwick. Inside its walls, archives waited. Records about the First Reaver, the Original Soul Reaver's tomb, the covenant that bound him to this ancient lineage.
The captain fell into step beside him. "You asked about the First Reaver's fate. I'll tell you what we know."
Kael glanced at her.
"He didn't just die sealing the Great Rift. He transcended. Became something more than human—a living seal, part of the barrier itself. Some believe he's still conscious, still fighting, still holding the Abyss at bay after a thousand years."
She paused. "Some believe he'll return when the seal finally breaks. That the First Reaver will walk the world again, reclaim his power, and finish what he started."
Kael's mind raced. The original Soul Reaver wasn't dead. Not entirely. His consciousness persisted in the seal network, bound to the Great Rift, waiting for the moment when his power would be needed most.
And if Kael was truly his heir—truly the next vessel for the covenant—then maybe he wasn't just walking his own path.
Maybe he was walking the First Reaver's path as well.
The Iron Citadel's gates opened before them, ancient iron screaming as mechanisms that hadn't moved in decades ground themselves into motion.
Kael stepped through into a new phase of the war.
Behind him, the morning light faded as clouds rolled across the sun. In the distance, barely audible, something that might have been thunder rumbled across a cloudless sky.
The Great Rift's seal weakened another fraction.
The clock continued counting down.
