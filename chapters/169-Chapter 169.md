The door to the Cauldron exploded inward, wooden splinters raining across the smoke-filled room like black teeth. Kael rolled beneath a table as two figures in iron masks crashed through, their boots shattering bottles and scattering coin across the grimy floor.
"You've got thirty seconds before the Iron Vanguard cordon locks the street," Mira's voice cut through the chaos, her pale eyes visible only as slits through the broken window. She perched on the windowsill like a pale moth, one hand pressed against the cracked glass. "The Mortician's delegation just arrived. They're asking about you by name."
Kael rose from the wreckage, dust coating his black coat. The system notification hung in his vision, unbidden: [Alert: Multiple hostile signatures detected. Recommend immediate extraction or concealment.]
Not helpful. Nothing about Soul Reaving ever came with an instruction manual.
"I didn't come here to run." He brushed glass from his sleeve. "The Cinderfang Wyrm. Where?"
Mira's expression flickered—Loss? Fear? Interest? Her kind made a living off hiding all three. "Under the old foundry. Sewers run deep there. But the thing hasn't surfaced in months. Not since—"
She stopped. Looked at him with something approaching pity.
"Not since it ate the last three Awakened who tried to hunt it."
Kael moved through the narrow corridors of the Veiled Market, past stalls draped in corpse-silk and offerings to entities whose names were forbidden. The air hung thick with incense and the copper tang of blood—not the clean industrial scent of the Mortician's Guild, but something older. Rawer.
He found the Undercroft's entrance behind a false wall in a gambling den that catered to those who bet more than coin. The guard was a woman with spiritual written in the way she tracked his movements—not Awakened herself, but close enough to taste his power.
"The Underlord isn't seeing visitors."
Kael placed a soul-essence vial on the table between them. The liquid inside pulsed with sickly green light, harvested from something that had been Entity-touched before it died. "He's seeing me."
The woman's eyes widened. Even in the Veiled Market, Entity-touched essence was contraband—unusable by conventional means, valuable beyond measure to those who knew how to refine it.
She pocketed the vial and gestured to a side passage. "Third door on the left. Don't say I didn't warn you."
The Underlord's chamber was a study in controlled darkness. Candles burned in iron sconces, casting long shadows across walls covered in maps and ledgers. The man himself sat behind a desk carved from something that had once been alive—Kael recognized the bone structure. A predator. Probably something from before the sealing.
"Kael Ashborn." The Underlord's voice carried no particular emotion. "The Soul Reaver. I wondered when you'd come crawling to my territory."
"I don't crawl." Kael kept his hands visible, though his fingers ached to be closer to his blades. "I want in on the Cinderfang hunt."
Silence stretched between them. The Underlord was old—not in body, but in presence. Someone who had survived long enough in the underworld to understand that every gift came with chains.
"The Cinderfang is worth three thousand gold sovereigns to the city for the pelt alone. The glands are worth twice that to the right buyers. But it has killed eleven hunters in the past year." He pulled a ledger from his desk, flipping pages with practiced efficiency. "Seven Awakened. Four Ascended. One Transcendent who thought his flame-attuned abilities made him special."
"Which Transcendent?"
"Called himself Emberfall. Thought he could burn his way through." The Underlord's thin smile held no warmth. "The wyrm was hungry that night."
Kael studied the maps on the walls. Industrial districts, sewer networks, abandoned foundries. The Cinderfang Wyrm's territory covered nearly a quarter mile of underground infrastructure. "I don't need to kill it alone. I need information. Patrol routes. Entry points. Dead drops for supplies."
"And in exchange?"
"Twelve percent of whatever I harvest from the corpse. First refusal on any abilities I extract."
The Underlord leaned back in his chair. Behind him, a curtain stirred—there was someone else in the room, someone Kael hadn't sensed until now. Hidden layer, maybe. Or something worse.
"Ten percent. And you work with my extraction team. No solo runs. No cutting us out when the creature falls."
Kael's jaw tightened. Solo operations were safer. Cleaner. But the Underlord had resources he didn't—sappers who could collapse tunnels, medics who knew how to handle wyrm poisoning, buyers willing to take product that would get a straightforward hunter killed.
"Fifteen percent. And my cut of any kills during the hunt beyond the wyrm itself."
"You drive a hard bargain for a dead man."
"I died once. It didn't take."
The deal was struck with blood and sealing wax. Kael left the Underlord's chamber with coordinates for a safehouse, access codes for supply caches, and a growing list of enemies he'd just made by cutting deals in their territory.
---
The foundry's bones rose from the earth like the skeleton of some massive beast. Steam vents poured white clouds into the night air, obscuring the moon. Kael stood at the edge of the main entrance, where rusted iron doors hung open like a wound that never healed.
Mira materialized from the shadows beside him. She wore leathers now instead of silks, and twin daggers hung at her hips. "The extraction team is in position. Three blocks east, in the old pump station. They won't engage unless you signal."
"You're not coming."
"Underlord's orders." She produced a small crystal from her sleeve, its surface etched with protective sigils. "Communing stone. Press it three times if you're dying. Four times if you've killed the wyrm. Anything else and they'll assume it's a trap."
Kael took the stone. It was cold in his palm, humming with barely contained energy. "What do you get out of this?"
Mira's smile was sharp enough to cut. "The Cinderfang has been disrupting supply routes for months. We've lost three shipments of soul-essence to its territory. It's not just about the bounty anymore—it's about making a point."
"What point?"
"That the underworld doesn't bow to anything with scales and fire." She turned to leave, then paused. "My sister was among the hunters it took. Whatever you pull from her remains... I'd like to know she suffered."
Kael said nothing. There was nothing to say. The dead rarely got to choose their endings.
He descended into the darkness.
---
The sewers stank of rot and industrial waste. Pipes groaned overhead, pressure building in systems that hadn't been maintained in decades. Kael moved by the light of a small spirit-flame cupped in his palm—barely enough to see three feet ahead, but the darkness didn't bother him. Soul Reavers learned to navigate by senses other than sight.
His boots splashed through ankle-deep water. Somewhere ahead, something large moved—he could hear it in the way the pipes shook, the way the air pressure changed. The Cinderfang Wyrm was active tonight.
System notifications flickered at the edge of his vision: [Warning: Elevated spiritual activity detected. Source: Large serpentine entity, approximately 40 meters ahead. Classification: Apex predator, flame-aspect. Threat level: Severe.]
Not helpful. Everything in the Abyss-adjacent zones registered as severe.
He found the first body after another hundred meters. A woman in leather armor, her face frozen in an expression of absolute terror. She had died reaching for something—a brass key hanging from a chain around her neck. Kael crouched beside her, placed his palm against her cooling skin, and felt the familiar pull.
[Soul Harvest: You extracted combat memory fragment (Ember Step technique) from the remains. Fragment integrity: 34%. Full technique unavailable.]
Not enough. Corpses this old—days, maybe a week—yielded only scraps. The wyrm had been feeding well.
He took the key and moved on.
The tunnel opened into a vast chamber—the old foundry's basement, long since flooded. Moonlight filtered through cracks in the ceiling, painting silver lines across water that stood six feet deep. And there, coiled on a platform of collapsed machinery, the Cinderfang Wyrm waited.
It was massive. Forty feet from snout to tail-tip, its scales black as iron and edged with ember-glow that pulsed like a heartbeat. Its eyes—four of them, arranged in a diamond pattern—reflected the faint light with predatory intelligence.
"You smell like the others." The voice came from everywhere and nowhere, resonating through Kael's bones. "But you taste different. Darker."
Kael drew his blades. "You're stalling. Why?"
The wyrm's laugh was a rumble that shook water from the ceiling. "Stalling? Little thing, I am deciding whether you are worth the effort of eating. Your predecessors fought. Screamed. Begged. You... You reek of something I recognize."
Its nostrils flared, tasting his spiritual signature.
"Soul Reaver. The others harvested what they could from my victims. But you... You could take everything. The meat. The essence. The memories." The ember-glow intensified. "That makes you valuable."
Kael understood then. The wyrm wasn't hunting him—it was hunting prey that could replenish its own losses. Soul Reavers were rare. A creature smart enough to recognize that rarity would want to capture than kill.
The first fireball exploded against his left flank. Kael twisted, blade arcing upward, and felt the heat sear past his ear. The water around him boiled where the flames touched it.
He moved.
The fight became a blur of water and flame. The Cinderfang attacked in sweeping arcs, each strike leaving geysers of superheated steam. Kael deflected what he could, dodged what he couldn't, and kept moving—always forward, always closer to the platform where the wyrm coiled.
[Alert: Thermal damage accumulating. Recommend tactical repositioning.]
The system wasn't wrong. His coat smoldered at the edges. His skin burned where flames had kissed it. But retreat wasn't an option. Not with the extraction team waiting. Not with the Underlord measuring his worth by this kill.
He found an opening when the wyrm reared back for another strike. Kael dove beneath the surface of the water, blade extended, and felt it bite into soft flesh just behind the jaw.
The wyrm screamed.
Kael surfaced behind the creature's head, both blades buried in the gap between scales. Blood—hot and thick—fountained around his hands. The ember-glow flickered wildly, and for a moment, the creature's spiritual signature destabilized.
"Now," he hissed. "Now, while you're—"
The tail caught him across the chest. Kael flew backward, crashing through a wall of rusted machinery. Pain lanced through his ribs. Something cracked.
The wyrm turned, one eye fixed on him with ancient hatred. "Clever little morsel. But you forget—I have killed your kind before."
It lunged.
Kael didn't have time to think. He moved, rolling beneath the strike, and felt his hand close around something smooth and cold—a gear, fallen from the machinery, the size of his fist. He threw it with every ounce of strength he had left.
The gear struck the wyrm's eye. Not hard enough to blind it, but hard enough to distract. Hard enough to give Kael the second he needed to reach the platform.
He climbed.
The wyrm thrashed, trying to dislodge him, but Kael found handholds in the collapsed framework. Up and up, until he stood on the highest point of the wreckage, thirty feet above the water, the creature's bleeding head swaying before him.
"The old Reavers," the wyrm growled, "they knew things. Knew how to speak to us. Knew the old treaties." Its ember-glow dimmed, then flared. "What do you know, little death-taker?"
Kael thought of the Sepulcher tablets. The fragments of knowledge left behind by the first Soul Reaver. The truth that the Orders had buried for centuries.
"I know you're not just an animal." He let his blades drop, clattering into the water below. "You're a remnant. Something left behind from before the sealing."
The wyrm's laugh shook dust from the ceiling. "Oh, the Orders have told you stories. The Abyssal Entities, the sealed rifts, the great war. But they never mention what came after. What happened to the creatures that refused to fight. What we became when humanity decided we were too dangerous to live."
"You were the first victims."
"We were the first survivors." The wyrm's head lowered, bringing its eye level with Kael's. Up close, the organ was vast—a window into something older than human civilization. "The Seals trap us, little death-taker. They bind our power, rot our minds, until all that remains is hunger and rage. I have been fighting my nature for three hundred years."
Kael understood, then. The wyrm wasn't evil. It was imprisoned in its own body, corrupted by seals that had been designed to contain than kill. Every hunt, every kill—it was fighting the part of itself that wanted to devour everything.
"You're dying."
"I have been dying since before your great-grandparents were born." The ember-glow steadied. "But you... You could end it. Take what I have left. Give me peace."
Kael hesitated. This wasn't a combat encounter anymore. This was something else—a choice that would define what kind of Soul Reaver he became.
"If I harvest you, I take everything. Your memories. Your power. Whatever's left of who you were."
"Yes."
"The Underlord wanted the pelt. The glands. Payment for his resources."
"I care nothing for gold." The wyrm's voice softened to something almost gentle. "I care only for silence. For the end of the fire that never stops burning."
Kael reached out and placed his palm against the creature's snout. The scales were hot—impossibly hot—but beneath them, he felt the thrum of a fading heartbeat.
[Soul Harvest initiated: Target = Cinderfang Wyrm. Classification: Remnant Entity, flame-aspected, seal-corrupted. Extraction: Complete. Acquired: Ember Breath (legendary), Thermal Adaptation (rare), Ancient Memory Fragment (???).]
Power flooded into him. Not just the abilities—though those came too, burning in his chest like captured stars—but the memories. Three centuries of existence, compressed into fragments that his mind could barely process.
He saw the world before the sealing. Saw cities that floated on spiritual energy, saw humans and Entities living in fragile peace. Saw the war that changed everything—the moment when the Seals went up, trapping creatures on both sides of the Veil in endless torment.
And he saw the original Soul Reaver. A figure cloaked in shadow, standing at the center of the seal network, weaving power that should have been impossible.
[System notification: Soul Reaver ability refined. New technique unlocked: Memory Reading (requires physical contact, consumes no spiritual energy, emotional backlash possible).]
The wyrm's body went still. Its ember-glow faded to nothing, leaving only cooling scales and the fading echo of something ancient.
Kael sat on the platform for a long time, processing what he'd taken. The creature had been right—its mind had been rotting for centuries, sealed power corrupting the vessel until nothing remained but hunger and pain. Death had been a mercy.
But mercy didn't pay the Underlord.
He worked through the night, harvesting what he could from the corpse. The pelt yielded easily—he cut it free in sections, careful not to damage the scales. The glands took more time; they were buried deep in the creature's chest, pulsing with residual heat even after death.
By the time he signaled the extraction team, Kael had enough product to pay the Underlord three times over and still keep enough for himself. The Ember Breath ability pulsed in his core, waiting to be tested. The Ancient Memory Fragment hummed with secrets he wasn't ready to uncover.

The Underlord's reaction to the delivery was... Unexpected.
"This is all of it?" He turned the largest scale in his hands, watching light play across its surface. "The entire pelt? The glands intact?"
"The creature chose to die." Kael kept his voice neutral. "I gave it that option."
The Underlord set the scale down. His expression had changed—something calculating had replaced the earlier skepticism. "You harvested a remnant Entity. Voluntarily."
"Yes."
"Do you understand what that means?"
Kael had begun to, yes. The memories the wyrm left behind had included context—information about the seal network, about the Abyssal Entities, about the true nature of the conflict between humanity and the things beyond the Veil.
The Six Orders existed to maintain the seals. But some creatures were sealed as punishment, not protection. Some Entities had chosen humanity's side and been betrayed by the very seals they helped create. The wyrm had been one of those—original name lost to corruption, but purpose clear. A guardian that became a prisoner.
"It means the orders are going to come for me."
"The orders are going to come for anyone who can harvest remnant Entities." The Underlord stood, moving to a cabinet in the corner. "Soul Reavers were bad enough when they could only take from humans. But if you can ease the suffering of sealed creatures... If you can extract their essence without triggering the alarm wards..."
He produced a small book, its cover worn and unmarked.
"The Shadow Court has been looking for you. Not to kill you—to recruit you. They have questions about what the original Soul Reaver did. About the seal network. About what happens when enough Remnants are harvested."
Kael took the book. It was lighter than it should have been, its pages blank.
"There are names inside. People who know things. People who might be willing to talk to you, if you approach them correctly." The Underlord's eyes met his. "I'm calling in a favor, Kael Ashborn. Not for the underworld. Not for profit. Because I knew the Cinderfang. Before it became what it was."
"You were friends?"
"We were allies. Both of us trapped by choices we made three centuries ago." The Underlord's thin smile returned. "It asked me once to find someone who could give it peace. I told it that Soul Reavers were all dead or hunted. It didn't believe me."
"And now?"
"Now you prove it wrong."
---
Kael left the Underlord's chamber with the book, three vials of wyrm-blood essence, and a new understanding of the game he'd been playing.
The Shadow Court knew about him. The Orders would soon follow. And somewhere in the city, people were gathering information about the original Soul Reaver—the figure at the center of the seal network, the one whose actions had shaped the world for millennia.
The system notification flickered: [Objective updated: Investigate Shadow Court contact. Threat level: Elevated.]
He had work to do.
The door to the corridor opened, and for a moment, Kael smelled something strange on the air. Ozone and copper. The scent of spiritual combat, or the aftermath of it.
A figure stood at the end of the hall. Tall. Hooded. Hands clasped behind their back.
"You're harder to find than the rumors suggested." The voice was soft, almost pleasant. "The Shadow Court has been patient. We thought it best to let you establish yourself before making contact."
Kael's hand drifted toward his blade. The wyrm's power hummed in his chest, ready to be released.
"Who are you?"
The figure lowered their hood. The face beneath was familiar—too familiar. Kael had seen that face before, in the memories he'd harvested, in the fragments of ancient history that shouldn't have survived.
The face of the original Soul Reaver.
"My name," the figure said, "is Dorian. And we need to talk about what you did tonight. About what the seals really are. And about what happens next."
Footsteps echoed from the corridor behind Kael. More of them. At least four, maybe five. The passage was narrowing, the shadows deepening.
[Alert: Hostile encirclement detected. Recommend immediate extraction.]
Dorian smiled. "I'd stay for the conversation, if I were you. The alternative is... Unpleasant."
Kael looked at the exits. Two. One behind him, one past Dorian. Neither clear. The footsteps were getting closer, and something in the air told him these weren't ordinary thugs.
"Threat level?"
[Unknown. Spiritual signatures masked. Classification: Shadow Court operative cadre.]
The Shadow Court. The hidden hand that moved pieces across the board. The organization that existed between and beyond the Six Orders, doing what the Concordat forbade and the world needed.
And they wanted him.
"I'm listening," Kael said.
Dorian's smile widened. "That's the first smart thing you've said since arriving in Thornwick. Let's discuss the future of the seal network, shall we? I think you'll find our proposal... Illuminating."
Behind him, the footsteps stopped. A circle of shadows formed, cutting off retreat.
Kael had walked into a trap. The only question now was whether he could turn it to his advantage.
The Ember Breath ability pulsed in his chest, waiting for release. The Memory Reading technique hummed at the edge of his consciousness, ready to extract secrets from anyone foolish enough to touch him.
And somewhere in the depths of his mind, the Ancient Memory Fragment stirred—the last gift from a dying wyrm, carrying answers to questions that hadn't been asked yet.
[Chapter objective updated: Survive. Extract information. Understand the Shadow Court's true purpose.]
Kael let his hand fall away from his blade.
"Talk," he said.
The shadows closed around him, and Dorian began to speak.
